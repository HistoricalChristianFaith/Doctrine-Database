The New Testament on Matthias

Dates: c. 30 (the election) · c. 62–90 (the writing of Acts) · Wikipedia
Summary: Matthias appears in one passage only, Acts 1:15–26, and is the single apostle whose calling the New Testament narrates in full. Between the Ascension and Pentecost the Eleven, on Peter’s motion, filled the place left empty by Judas: the candidate had to have followed Jesus through the whole of the public ministry and to be a witness of the resurrection; two men qualified; the lot fell on Matthias. He is not named again — but neither are most of the Twelve.

Claims & evidence

Peter proposes to fill Judas’s place

Luke sets the scene in the upper room, in the ten days between the Ascension and Pentecost, before some hundred and twenty believers. Peter rises and reads Judas’s fall as the fulfilment of Scripture, then states the vacancy to be filled:

And in those days Peter stood up in the midst of the disciples, and said, (the number of names together were about an hundred and twenty,) Men and brethren, this scripture must needs have been fulfilled, which the Holy Ghost by the mouth of David spake before concerning Judas, which was guide to them that took Jesus… For he was numbered with us, and had obtained part of this ministry.

— Acts 1:15–17. Peter grounds the replacement in the Psalms — “Let his habitation be desolate… and his bishoprick (episkopē) let another take” (v. 20, citing Psalm 69:25 and 109:8) — so that filling the Twelve is presented not as human tidiness but as the fulfilment of prophecy.

The qualification: an eyewitness from the baptism of John to the Ascension

The requirement Peter lays down is exact, and it is the hinge of every later question about Matthias (and about Paul):

Wherefore of these men which have companied with us all the time that the Lord Jesus went in and out among us, Beginning from the baptism of John, unto that same day that he was taken up from us, must one be ordained to be a witness with us of his resurrection.

— Acts 1:21–22. The twelfth man must be an eyewitness of the entire arc — from John’s baptism of Jesus at the outset to the Ascension at the close — and above all a witness of the resurrection. This is what an apostle of the Twelve is, on Luke’s account: not merely a called man but a guarantor of the whole story from firsthand knowledge.

Two candidates, prayer, and the lot

Two men met the test, and the choice between them was referred to God by lot — the last time in the New Testament that lots are cast, and the last before the Spirit comes at Pentecost:

And they appointed two, Joseph called Barsabas, who was surnamed Justus, and Matthias. And they prayed, and said, Thou, Lord, which knowest the hearts of all men, shew whether of these two thou hast chosen, That he may take part of this ministry and apostleship, from which Judas by transgression fell, that he might go to his own place. And they gave forth their lots; and the lot fell upon Matthias; and he was numbered with the eleven apostles.

— Acts 1:23–26. The prayer treats the lot not as chance but as the way to learn a choice already made in the heart of the Lord “which knowest the hearts of all men” — the standard biblical understanding of the sacred lot (Proverbs 16:33). With verse 26 Matthias is “numbered with the eleven apostles,” completing the Twelve for Pentecost, and the New Testament’s account of him is over.

What the New Testament does not say

It gives Matthias no saying, no deed after the election, no mission, no death. It does not say the choice was mistaken, nor that Paul would later take his place; Paul, on the contrary, hands on the primitive creed that the risen Christ “was seen of Cephas, then of the twelve” (1 Corinthians 15:5), counting the number Matthias had restored as intact. Matthias’s later obscurity is real but unremarkable: of the Eleven listed in Acts 1:13, most are never heard from again either.

Notes

  • The one full call-narrative. Matthias is the only apostle whose appointment the New Testament describes step by step. Ironically, the best-documented calling belongs to the least-documented apostle.
  • The qualification excludes Paul. The demand for a companion “from the baptism of John” is precisely what Paul — who never followed the earthly Jesus — does not satisfy; it is the crux of the argument over whether Paul was the “true” twelfth, weighed on its own page.
  • Joseph Barsabbas Justus. The rejected candidate bears three names; a separate strand of tradition would later confuse him — or Matthias — with Barnabas or with Zacchaeus, identifications weighed on the identity page.
