The apostle-lists and the Golden Legend on Matthias

Dates: c. 6th–13th century (apostle-lists; Golden Legend c. 1260) · Catholic Encyclopedia, “St. Matthias”
Summary: The mission and the death the New Testament withholds are supplied only by the late catalogues — and they do not agree. The Synopsis ascribed to Dorotheus and to Nicephorus sends Matthias to “Ethiopia,” by which they mean Colchis on the eastern Black Sea, among barbarians and man-eaters, and bury him at Sebastopolis. On how he died they splinter: stoned and beheaded at Jerusalem, crucified in Colchis, or dead of old age. The medieval West kept the stoned-and-beheaded story, gave him the axe as his emblem, and located his relics at Trier — the only claimed apostolic tomb north of the Alps.

Claims & evidence

The mission field: “Ethiopia,” that is, Colchis

The list-literature places Matthias in the Caucasus. In the tradition transmitted under the name of Dorotheus of Tyre and echoed by Nicephorus, he “preached the Gospel to barbarians and man-eaters in the interior of Ethiopia, at the harbour of the sea of Hyssus, at the mouth of the river Phasis. He died at Sebastopolis, and was buried there, near the Temple of the Sun.” The “Ethiopia” meant is not the African kingdom but the old idea of a Colchian “Ethiopia” east of the Black Sea — the region of modern Georgia, the Phasis being the Rioni, Sebastopolis modern Sukhumi. The man-eaters here are plainly the same motif that drives the cannibal-city romance: one legend feeding another, and neither resting on any early witness.

The death: three or four incompatible ends

On how Matthias died the sources cannot be reconciled. The most widespread Western tradition holds that he was stoned by the Jews at Jerusalem and then beheaded with an axe — “stoned, and… afterwards beheaded” — which is why medieval and later art puts an axe or halberd in his hand as the instrument of his death. The Greek Synopsis, keeping him in Colchis, has him crucified there. Pseudo-Hippolytus’s On the Twelve Apostles, by contrast, gives him no martyrdom at all: “Matthias… fell asleep” — died of old age at Jerusalem and was buried there. Stoned-and-beheaded, crucified, or peaceful: the traditions offer a menu, and no way to choose among them, because none is early.

The relics: Trier, Rome, and a fortress in Georgia

The relic-cult multiplied his graves. The great Western claim is Trier, where the Benedictine abbey of St. Matthias holds what it presents as the apostle’s tomb — the only claimed burial of an apostle north of the Alps — his remains said to have been carried there by the empress Helena, mother of Constantine. A portion was also claimed at Santa Maria Maggiore in Rome; another burial-tradition keeps his grave at the Gonio fortress on the Georgian Black-Sea coast, consistent with the Colchis mission. The competing claims cannot all be true, and the Bollandists long ago raised a sharper doubt: that the “Matthias” relics venerated at Rome might belong not to the apostle but to a later Matthias, bishop of Jerusalem (d. c. 120) — the same kind of name-collision that runs through the whole tradition.

Notes

  • Church memory, not history. These catalogues are late, mutually dependent, and critically judged worthless as biography; their value is as evidence for how the church came to remember and commemorate Matthias. The shared treatment of the apostle-lists is set out on the apostles hub.
  • The axe. Matthias’s iconographic emblem — an axe or halberd — is a picture of the stoned-and-beheaded tradition, not independent evidence for it. The emblem hardened the story in Western memory, as the saw did for Simon.
  • “Ethiopia” misread. Later readers who took the mission-field as African Ethiopia compounded the confusion; the Caucasian “Ethiopia”/Colchis of the Synopsis is a distinct old geography. Either way the placement is legend, unattested before the list-literature.
