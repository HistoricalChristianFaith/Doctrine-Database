Eusebius on Matthias

Dates: c. 325 (Eusebius of Caesarea, Ecclesiastical History) · Church History
Summary: Eusebius gives Matthias two short notices that point in opposite directions. In Book 1 he records — as the first author known to do so — the tradition that Matthias (and the rejected candidate Barsabbas Justus) had first been among the Seventy, which conveniently satisfies the demand that the twelfth apostle be an eyewitness of the whole ministry. In Book 3, listing the church’s rejected books, he files a “Gospel of Matthias” among the heretics’ forgeries.

Claims & evidence

Matthias among the Seventy

Discussing the disciples of Jesus beyond the Twelve, Eusebius notes that no proper catalogue of the Seventy survives, then relays what tradition preserved — including a placement of Matthias:

Matthias, also, who was numbered with the apostles in the place of Judas, and the one who was honored by being made a candidate with him, are likewise said to have been deemed worthy of the same calling with the seventy.

— Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History 1.12.3. The notice does real work: Peter’s requirement in Acts 1 was that the replacement have “companied with us all the time that the Lord Jesus went in and out among us” — and if Matthias had been one of the Seventy whom Jesus himself sent out (Luke 10:1), his qualification is secured from the tradition rather than merely assumed. Eusebius reports it carefully as something “said,” not as documented fact; it is, so far as we know, first recorded here, and later writers (Epiphanius, Nicephorus) repeat it.

The “Gospel of Matthias” among the rejected books

In his famous sorting of the New Testament writings into accepted, disputed, and rejected, Eusebius reaches a last class — the forgeries circulated under apostolic names — and Matthias’s name is on the list:

…those that are cited by the heretics under the name of the apostles, including, for instance, such books as the Gospels of Peter, of Thomas, of Matthias, or of any others besides them, and the Acts of Andrew and John and the other apostles… the character of the style is at variance with apostolic usage, and both the thoughts and the purpose of the things that are related in them are so completely out of accord with true orthodoxy that they clearly show themselves to be the fictions of heretics. Wherefore they are not to be placed even among the rejected writings, but are all of them to be cast aside as absurd and impious.

— Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History 3.25.6–7. This is almost certainly the same Matthias-literature Clement had quoted a century earlier — the Traditions claimed by the Basilidians (see the Clement page). By Eusebius’s day the wider church had made up its mind: a safely obscure apostle’s name had been borrowed to lend authority to sectarian teaching, and the whole was to be discarded.

Notes

  • Two verdicts on one name. Eusebius honours the man (a disciple from among the Seventy) and condemns the book (a heretical forgery) in the same work — a neat measure of how Matthias’s name had split into an honourable memory and a discredited literature.
  • Why the Seventy? The claim that Matthias belonged to the Seventy is best read as tradition solving a problem: it retro-fits the eyewitness qualification of Acts 1:21–22 onto a man of whom nothing else was known. It is early enough to be worth recording, late enough not to be firsthand.
  • Not the only rejected apostolic Gospel. Matthias’s name sits here beside Peter’s and Thomas’s — a reminder that borrowing an apostle’s authority for a sectarian book was a common second-century move, and that the church’s rejection of these works is itself old and firm.
