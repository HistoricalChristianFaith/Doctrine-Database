Clement of Alexandria on Matthias

Dates: c. 150–215 (Clement of Alexandria) · Stromata
Summary: Clement, writing around A.D. 200, is the first author after the New Testament to mention Matthias — and he does so by quoting a book that bore the apostle’s name, the Traditions of Matthias, a collection of ascetic sayings he cites with respect but not as Scripture. In the same Stromateis Clement drops a second, much-debated notice that some identified the tax-collector Zacchaeus with “Matthias” — the seed of a later legend, and tangled in a manuscript variant that also reads “Matthew.”

Claims & evidence

The Traditions of Matthias: “Wonder at what is before you”

Three times Clement quotes a work he calls the Traditions (Paradōseis) of Matthias. The first is an epistemological maxim he sets at the head of knowledge itself:

The beginning of knowledge is wondering at objects, as Plato says is in his Theaetetus; and Matthew exhorting in the Traditions, says, “Wonder at what is before you;” laying this down first as the foundation of further knowledge.

— Stromateis 2.9. (The Ante-Nicene Fathers translation prints “Matthew” here, but the work Clement names is the Traditions of Matthias — the same Matthias/Matthew slippage that dogs the whole tradition; see the note below.)

The ascetic teaching: fight and abuse the flesh

The second citation gives the Traditions their characteristic ascetic note. Clement is contrasting the genuine self-mastery of Nicolaus with the licence the Nicolaitan heretics falsely claimed from him, and adduces Matthias to the same effect:

It is said that Matthias also taught that one should fight the flesh and abuse it, never allowing it to give way to licentious pleasure, so that the soul might grow by faith and knowledge.

— Stromateis 3.4. This is discipline, not dualism: the flesh is to be mastered so that “the soul might grow by faith and knowledge” — the same gnostic-in-Clement’s-good-sense ideal that runs through the Stromateis. Clement cites Matthias approvingly; but he also notes that the Basilidian heretics claimed Matthias as a secret authority, and it is that claim, not the sober sayings, that later put a “Gospel of Matthias” under suspicion.

The Zacchaeus notice — and the “Matthias / Matthew” variant

In Book 4 Clement retells the story of the repentant chief tax-collector who promised fourfold restitution, and names him with a striking aside:

It is said, therefore, that Zaccheus, or, according to some, Matthew, the chief of the publicans, on hearing that the Lord had deigned to come to him, said, “Lord, and if I have taken anything by false accusation, I restore him fourfold;” on which the Saviour said, “The Son of man, on coming to-day, has found that which was lost.”

— Stromateis 4.6. The Ante-Nicene Fathers translation reads “Matthew,” but the Greek text runs Zakchaion toinun, hoi de Matthian phasi — “Zacchaeus, or, as some say, Matthias.” So the sentence is the earliest witness to a tradition that the apostle chosen by lot was really the publican Zacchaeus of Jericho under a second name. Whether that identification can stand — and how much weight one line of “some say,” itself unstable between two apostolic names, can bear — is weighed on the identity page.

Notes

  • A name attached to a book, not a life. What Clement securely attests is that by c. 200 a body of ascetic sayings circulated under Matthias’s name and was quotable in respectable company. Whether the apostle said any of it is another matter; the Traditions are generally judged second-century pseudepigrapha (see the Eusebius page on the rejected “Gospel of Matthias”).
  • The recurring Matthias/Matthew slip. Twice in these passages the name wobbles — “Matthew” in the ANF at 2.9, “Matthew” for the Greek “Matthias” at 4.6. The two apostolic names, close in Greek and both borne by tax-collectors of a sort, were forever collapsing into one another, in Clement as later in the cannibal-city romance.
  • Cited, not canonised. Clement quotes many books he does not treat as Scripture. The Traditions of Matthias are one such: useful ascetic counsel in his eyes, but a text the Basilidians had also claimed, and the wider church would soon reject outright.
