The Acts of Andrew and Matthias

Dates: c. 2nd–4th century (apocryphal romance) · Acts of Andrew and Matthias (ANF vol. 8)
Summary: An apocryphal adventure — Acts of Andrew and Matthias among the Anthropophagi, “the man-eaters” — sends Matthias by lot to a city of cannibals who blind and fatten their captives to eat them. Imprisoned and blinded, Matthias is rescued by Andrew, whom Christ dispatches across the sea. It is heroic legend of no biographical value, and it cannot even keep its hero’s name distinct from Matthew’s — the very confusion that shadows the whole Matthias tradition.

Claims & evidence

The lot, and the city of the man-eaters

The romance opens where Acts 1 left off — with the apostles casting lots for their fields — and turns the sober scene into the hook of an adventure story:

About that time all the apostles had come together to the same place, and shared among themselves the countries, casting lots, in order that each might go away into the part that had fallen to him. By lot, then, it fell to Matthias to set out to the country of the man-eaters. And the men of that city used neither to eat bread nor drink wine; but they ate the flesh of men, and drank their blood. Every man, therefore, who came into their city they laid hold of, and digging they thrust out his eyes, and gave him a drug to drink, prepared by sorcery and magic; and from drinking the drug his heart was altered and his mind deranged.

— Acts of Andrew and Matthias 1 (tr. Alexander Walker, Ante-Nicene Fathers vol. 8). Matthias arrives, is seized and blinded — though the drug fails to derange him — and is thrown into the prison-pen to be fattened for slaughter; a light and a voice restore his sight and bid him wait until Andrew comes. The Greek text leaves the city unnamed — simply the country of the man-eaters — while the Latin tradition and the Anglo-Saxon Andreas call it “Myrmidonia” (with a swarm of variant spellings).

Andrew’s rescue

Christ appears to Andrew, preaching far off in Achaia, and orders him to sail to Matthias’s aid; the voyage is made in a boat Christ himself pilots in disguise. Andrew enters the city unseen, opens the prison, and heals the captives:

And when he came to the prison, he saw seven warders standing at the gate guarding, and he prayed within himself, and they fell down and expired; and he marked the gate with the sign of the cross, and it opened of its own accord. And having gone in with his disciples, he found Matthias sitting and singing… and Andrew laid his hands upon the faces of the blind men who were in the prison, and straightway they all received their sight.

— Acts of Andrew and Matthias 20–22. A run of wonders follows — a persecuting mob, a flood loosed from a stone statue, Andrew’s own torture and vindication — and the city is at last converted and baptized. Matthias, having set the plot in motion, drops out of it: the story is really Andrew’s, and Matthias is the captive to be saved, not the hero who saves.

The name that will not stay put

The most telling feature of the text, for a life of Matthias, is that it is not securely about Matthias at all. Across the manuscript traditions the hero is called now “Matthias,” now “Matthew” — the far better-known evangelist — and the Anglo-Saxon poem Andreas, built on this very story, makes the rescued apostle Matthew outright. The two Greek names (Matthias, Matthaios) are near-twins, and a scribe with the famous evangelist in mind slid easily from the obscure apostle to him. The confusion is not a marginal slip but a structural fact of the tradition: the one narrative that gives Matthias a starring role keeps forgetting which apostle it means.

Notes

  • Romance, not history. The Acts of Andrew and Matthias belongs to the apocryphal “Acts” literature — adventure-hagiography of low biographical worth, valuable as a witness to how the apostles were venerated and imagined, never as a record of where they actually went. See the shared treatment on the apostles hub.
  • “Ethiopia” and the man-eaters. The cannibal-land motif here later fused with the apostle-lists’ placement of Matthias among “barbarians and man-eaters” in Colchian “Ethiopia” — one legendary strand feeding another. That geography is traced on the apostle-lists page.
  • Matthias or Matthew — again. The same slippage runs through Clement’s Stromateis (the ANF prints “Matthew” where the Greek names Matthias) — see the Clement page. Outside the single verse of Acts 1, the tradition could not reliably distinguish the two.
