The Old Testament Canon (Is the Deuterocanon Scripture?)

Is Tobit — and with it Judith, Wisdom, Sirach (Ecclesiasticus), Baruch, and 1–2 Maccabees, the books Catholics call the deuterocanon and Protestants the Apocrypha — part of the Christian Old Testament? For the first fifteen centuries there was no single fixed list to settle it, and the answer was given differently across time. The Greek Septuagint that the early church inherited included these books; many Fathers who knew the Hebrew (Melito, Origen, Cyril, Jerome) counted a narrower, Hebrew-sized canon and ranked the extra books below it; the African councils, Rome, Florence, Trent, and the Eastern Synod of Jerusalem affirmed them; the Reformers and the Reformed confessions excluded them. This page traces those YES / NO verdicts across the centuries, earliest first, each footnoted to its source.

The question ties into two neighboring doctrines. Because the disputed books reached the church through the Greek Bible, the case turns partly on the standing of that translation — the Origin and Inspiration of the Septuagint — and on the Septuagint-versus-Hebrew quarrel gathered on the Rabbinic Corruption of Scripture page. (The companion Biblical Canon timeline treats the parallel question for the New Testament.) And it matters doctrinally: two of the disputed books carry proof-texts the Reformation contested — Tobit's "almsgiving saves from death" (Tobit 12:9) and 2 Maccabees' prayer for the dead (2 Macc 12:43–45), weighed in the Arguments below as they are added.

Timeline

The Septuagint-to-Reformation timeline is in progress; it runs from the LXX (c. 132 BC) toward the councils and confessions and the Synod of Jerusalem (1672). It opens with a framing note on the test case — Tobit — before the witnesses proper.

The test case: dating Tobit (composed c. 200 BC)

The dispute turns on a handful of books, and Tobit is the convenient bellwether, named on nearly every list below. Its own story is set in the eighth century BC: Tobit is a pious Israelite carried off to Nineveh in the Assyrian deportation that followed the fall of the northern kingdom (c. 722 BC).1 But scholars date the book itself far later — to roughly 200 BC (a range of about 225–175 BC), on the absence of any trace of the Maccabean persecution and on geographical slips that betray an author writing centuries after the events he narrates.2 That gap between narrative setting and date of writing is not peculiar to a "disputed" book: it is the very gap critical scholarship posits for canonical Daniel, whose sixth-century-BC court tales are widely held to reach their final form only in the Hellenistic age (c. 165 BC).3 So the deuterocanon's lateness, by itself, does not set it apart from every protocanonical book — which is why the canon question is settled below not by date of composition but by reception, list by list.

The Septuagint (c. 132 BC): Yes — inclusion

The timeline opens not with a verdict but with a fact: the Greek Old Testament the early church inherited — the Septuagint — already contained Tobit, together with Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch, and 1–2 Maccabees. The three great fourth- and fifth-century Christian Bibles (Codices Vaticanus, Sinaiticus, and Alexandrinus) all carry Tobit among the Old Testament books, interleaved with the protocanonical ones rather than set apart.4 This Greek Bible was "the most frequently used version of the Old Testament in the early Church,"5 and the New Testament's own scriptural citations follow it overwhelmingly — Archer and Chirichigno count 340 quotations agreeing with the Septuagint against only 33 with the (later-named) Masoretic Hebrew.6 So the disputed books were inside the church's working Bible from the outset: the YES / NO verdicts below are arguments over whether to keep what the Greek Scriptures already held, not whether to add something new. (How that Greek Bible came to be reckoned authoritative is the subject of the companion Origin and Inspiration of the Septuagint timeline; it is not re-argued here.)

The "Council of Jamnia" (c. 90 AD, Judaism): No — but no council

The Jewish "No" to the deuterocanon is real but harder to pin to a date than the old story allowed. That story is the Council of Jamnia: a hypothesis Heinrich Graetz floated in 1871, that a rabbinic council sat at Yavneh about 70–90 AD and there closed the Hebrew canon, shutting out the Greek extras in reaction to the rise of Christianity.7 Twentieth-century scholarship has largely abandoned it: Jack P. Lewis's "What Do We Mean by Jabneh?" (1964) showed that "canonicity was not the issue and the debate was not connected with Jabneh… No book is ever mentioned in the sources as being excluded from the canon at Jabneh," and F. F. Bruce judged it "probably unwise to talk as if there were a Council or Synod of Jamnia."8 Strip the legend away and a genuine uncertainty remains — "there is no scholarly consensus as to when the canon of the Hebrew Bible… was fixed," with proposals ranging from the Hasmonean period (c. 140–40 BC) to the second century AD and later.9 What is not in doubt is the result: the Pharisaic, proto-rabbinic Scriptures that hardened into the later Jewish canon never carried Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch, or 1–2 Maccabees.10 So the verdict is double-edged: the rabbinic canon excluded these books, but the sharp conciliar exclusion often cited against them did not happen as described. (The broader Septuagint-versus-Hebrew quarrel is gathered on the Rabbinic Corruption of Scripture page and not re-argued here.)

Melito of Sardis (c. 170 AD): No

The earliest Christian list of the Old Testament books comes from Melito, bishop of Sardis, who travelled East "to the place where these things were preached" to learn "accurately the books of the Old Testament" and sent the catalogue to his friend Onesimus; Eusebius preserved it verbatim. It is a Hebrew-sized canon: Tobit, Judith, Sirach, Baruch, and 1–2 Maccabees are all absent (so too Esther), and it closes at "Esdras."11 The one word that might name a deuterocanonical book — "Wisdom," set right after "the Proverbs of Solomon" — is on the usual reading a second ancient title for Proverbs itself (the "all-virtuous Wisdom"), not the deuterocanonical Book of Wisdom, so the list most likely carries no disputed book at all.12 Melito heads the long line of Hebrew-minded Fathers (Origen, Cyril, Athanasius, Jerome) who counted a narrower canon than the Greek Septuagint the church read in worship.

The Muratorian Fragment (c. 170 AD): Yes — Wisdom only

The same years yield a Western witness pointing the other way. The Muratorian Fragment — the oldest surviving list of New Testament books — counts, in the middle of its New-Testament reckoning, "the [book of] Wisdom, written by the friends of Solomon," ranking the deuterocanonical Wisdom of Solomon among the received writings.13 That makes it the earliest Western witness that a disputed Old-Testament book was received as Scripture — but it is a cross-over witness, not an OT-list: it names only Wisdom (not Tobit, Judith, Sirach, Baruch, or Maccabees), and it files the book oddly among the New Testament, a sign of how unsettled the line between the Testaments still was.14 Set beside Melito's contemporary list — whose "Wisdom" is most likely a second name for Proverbs — the two witnesses reach opposite verdicts on the very same word.

Origen of Alexandria (c. 240 AD): No — but keeps the Greek

The greatest biblical scholar of the early church returns the timeline to a Hebrew-sized canon. In his Commentary on Psalm 1, quoted by Eusebius, Origen lists the Old Testament books "as the Hebrews have handed them down" as twenty-two, the count of the Hebrew letters — Tobit, Judith, Sirach, and the Wisdom of Solomon all absent, and the Maccabees named only to be set "besides these."15 Yet the same Origen will not surrender the longer Greek text the churches actually read: answering Julius Africanus, who challenged the story of Susanna as no genuine part of Daniel, he refuses to "reject as spurious the copies in use in our Churches" and appeals to the Providence that gave those Scriptures to "all the Churches of Christ."16 So his verdict is double, and honestly so: a "No" by the Hebrew enumeration, a working "keep" in the church's practice — the very distinction between canonical and read in the churches that Origen hands forward to Athanasius, Rufinus, and Jerome.17

The Council of Nicaea (325 AD): No recorded ruling — but a Judith report

The first ecumenical council published no canon-list. Its twenty surviving canons and synodal letter concern the Arian and Melitian schisms, the lapsed, the date of Easter, and clerical discipline — not the books of Scripture.18 It belongs on this timeline for one reason: it is the earliest council to which a verdict on a deuterocanonical book has ever been attributed. Eighty years later Jerome reports, in his Preface to Judith, that the council "is found… to have been counted" the deuterocanonical book of Judith "among the number of the Sacred Scriptures," and gives that as his reason for translating a book he otherwise ranked below the canon.19 But the report will not carry a Nicene canon-decree: Jerome himself, in the very same prefaces, rules Judith out of the canon, and Athanasius — who was present at Nicaea — still placed Judith among the books "not… included in the Canon, but appointed by the Fathers to be read" in 367.20 The full adversarial weighing of the claim is set out on its own argument page — assessed weak for the decree-reading, while the modest reading (that Nicaea merely cited Judith as Scripture in argument) is plausible.21

Cyril of Jerusalem (c. 350 AD): No — but Baruch kept

Catechizing the candidates for baptism at Jerusalem, Cyril recites an Eastern Old-Testament list of twenty-two books, the Hebrew-letter count, and draws the line sharply: "have nothing to do with the apocryphal writings… study earnestly these only which we read openly in the Church." Tobit, Judith, Sirach, the Wisdom of Solomon, and 1–2 Maccabees are all absent.22 Yet the list is not the bare Hebrew thirty-nine: Cyril's single book of Jeremiah is reckoned "including Baruch and Lamentations and the Epistle" — two short deuterocanonical works folded in as appendices, exactly as the Greek manuscripts carried them — and he credits the whole Hebrew-sized canon to the Septuagint's seventy-two translators.23 So his "No" to the disputed books keeps Baruch inside a canonical book, the same posture — rank by the Hebrew count, read from the Greek — that Origen had already shown and that Athanasius and Epiphanius will repeat.

Athanasius of Alexandria (367 AD): No — but Baruch kept, in three tiers

The most influential Eastern voice of the century sorts the books into a famous three-tier scheme. In his thirty-ninth Festal Letter Athanasius first lists the Old-Testament canon as the Hebrew twenty-two — Tobit, Judith, Sirach, the Wisdom of Solomon, and the Maccabees all absent — though his Jeremiah, like Origen's and Cyril's, carries Baruch and the Epistle of Jeremiah folded in.24 Below the canon he sets a second rank — books "not indeed included in the Canon, but appointed by the Fathers to be read… for instruction in the word of godliness" — and there he files the deuterocanon proper: the Wisdom of Solomon, Sirach, Judith, Tobit, and (strikingly) even the protocanonical Esther; below that again stand the "apocryphal writings… an invention of heretics."25 So his "No" is graded, not flat: the disputed books are not Scripture, yet not heretical either — "appointed by the Fathers to be read," the very distinction Origen had drawn, now made an explicit list that Rufinus and Jerome will carry forward. (The same letter gives the first New-Testament catalogue identical to the present twenty-seven, treated on his New Testament canon page.)

The Council of Rome (382 AD): Yes — but on a sixth-century document

The first Latin council-list to run out the full deuterocanon is ascribed to the Roman synod that met under Pope Damasus I in 382. Its canon-catalogue — the so-called "Damasine list" — gives the longer Septuagint Old Testament, filing Tobit, Judith, and 1–2 Maccabees among "the Histories" and Wisdom and Ecclesiasticus (Sirach) among the books of Solomon: the whole disputed deuterocanon received as Scripture, the same canon the African councils would enact a decade later.26 But the verdict comes with a sharp caveat about its act: the list survives in no fourth-century text, only as a chapter of the Decretum Gelasianum, a compilation critical scholarship dates to the sixth century (this site c. 500). A real synod did meet at Rome in 382, and the list reflects the canon the late-fourth-century West received; but the claim that the canon was decreed there rests on a single later document and an eighteenth-century attribution, and Jerome — present at Rome, foremost on the canon — nowhere mentions such a decree. The documentary basis is weighed adversarially on its own argument page — assessed weak for the "fixed at Rome in 382" framing, while the modest reading (that Damasus's Rome simply received the deuterocanon, as the rest of the West did) is plausible.27

Epiphanius of Salamis (c. 385 AD): No — but Wisdom & Sirach "useful," Baruch kept

The great heresy-hunter of Salamis returns the Eastern verdict once more. In both the Panarion and On Weights and Measures, Epiphanius gives the Hebrew-sized canon — twenty-seven "recognized canonical books, but twenty-two when counted according to the letters of the alphabet of the Hebrews" — running them out in four "pentateuchs" plus Ezra and Esther, with Tobit, Judith, and the Maccabees all absent.28 But his "No" is graded, like Athanasius's before it: he names the two great Wisdom books — the Wisdom of Solomon and the Wisdom of Sirach — and rules them "of disputed canonicity," yet "helpful and useful," books that "were not kept in the chest, that is, in the ark of the covenant"; and his single Jeremiah carries Baruch and the Epistle of Jeremiah folded in, exactly as Origen, Cyril, and Athanasius had kept them — though he frankly grants "the epistles are not in use among the Hebrews."29

Jerome (c. 390 AD): No — the hardest line, even Baruch dropped

The man who gave the Latin West its Bible draws the sharpest "No" on the timeline. In the Prologus Galeatus — the "helmeted" preface to his translation from the Hebrew — Jerome fixes the Old Testament at the Hebrew twenty-two and rules that "whatever is outside of these is set aside among the apocrypha. Therefore, Wisdom… and the book of Jesus son of Sirach, and Judith and Tobias, and The Shepherd are not in the canon."30 He is harder even than the Eastern Fathers before him: where Cyril, Athanasius, and Epiphanius all folded Baruch into the canonical Jeremiah, Jerome omits it as "neither read nor found among the Hebrews," and it is he who supplies the very word — apocrypha — the later Protestant Bibles will use.30 Yet his "No" is not flat: at his bishops' command he translated Tobit and Judith anyway — Tobit "contrary to their canon," he grants, choosing "to be subject to the commands of bishops" over "the opinion of the Pharisees" — and he ranked Wisdom and Sirach, with Judith, Tobit, and the Maccabees, as books the Church may "read… for the strengthening of the people, but not for confirming the authority of ecclesiastical dogmas."31 That formula — read for edification, not to prove doctrine — is the one the Thirty-Nine Articles and the Westminster Confession will borrow, citing Jerome by name. (His Judith preface also carries the report that the Council of Nicaea "counted" Judith among the Scriptures — weighed, and found wanting as a decree, on its own argument page.32)

The African councils — Hippo & Carthage (393–419 AD): Yes — the deuterocanon in conciliar form

For the first time the boundary is drawn by a council. The North-African synods gathered around Augustine and Aurelius of Carthage — Hippo in 393 (canon 36, the earliest canon-list any church council promulgated), re-enacted at Carthage in 397 and reaffirmed in 419 — each enact the rule "that besides the Canonical Scriptures nothing be read in church under the name of divine Scripture," and then list the books. Their Old Testament is the longer Septuagint canon: Tobit, Judith, and the two books of Maccabees named outright among the Scriptures, and the deuterocanonical Wisdom of Solomon and Sirach carried in "the five books of Solomon."33 Where Athanasius a generation earlier had ranked exactly these books a tier below — "read" but "not in the canon" — the African lists level them up, placing them among the canonical books. The theological hand behind the lists is Augustine, whose On Christian Doctrine (2.8.13, c. 397) matches the conciliar canon book for book and states its criterion — to "prefer those that are received by all the catholic churches to those which some do not receive."34 These are regional synods ratifying a reception already in hand, not ecumenical decrees that created it — the Western counterpart to Athanasius's Eastern list, and the seed of the later Old-Testament dispute that runs to Florence, Trent, and the Reformation. (The same councils' New-Testament list is treated on the companion Biblical Canon page.)

Augustine of Hippo (c. 397): Yes — the full canon, on the breadth of its reception

The theological hand behind those councils sets out the same verdict under his own name. In On Christian Doctrine Augustine runs out a forty-four-book Old Testament that files Tobias, Judith, the two books of Maccabees, and the two of Ezra among the histories, and reckons Wisdom and Ecclesiasticus among the prophetical books — the longer Septuagint canon, with no second tier.35 What carries the disputed books in is not a Hebrew enumeration but a rule of reception: "to prefer those that are received by all the catholic churches to those which some do not receive," weighing a book by the breadth and antiquity of its use.36 That is the exact opposite test from Jerome's Hebraica veritas, and the disagreement between these two contemporaries is the fault line that runs to Trent and the Reformation. Late in life, in The City of God, Augustine meets the objection the Reformers would later press — that the Maccabees are not in the Hebrew Scriptures — and concedes it, only to turn it: those books "are held as canonical, not by the Jews, but by the Church, on account of the extreme and wonderful sufferings of certain martyrs."37 His canon rests not on the Jewish reckoning but on the church's reception — the Western, Septuagint-based canon that Trent would later fix as dogma.

Rufinus of Aquileia (c. 404): No — but "Ecclesiastical," read though not doctrinal

Rufinus gives the cleanest, most systematic version of the middle position. In his Commentary on the Apostles' Creed he sorts the books into three named ranks. The Canonical Old Testament he lists is the Hebrew-sized canon — Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, and the Maccabees all absent, and Jeremiah named without Baruch.38 Below it he sets a second tier, the books "our fathers call not 'Canonical' but 'Ecclesiastical'": the Wisdom of Solomon, the Wisdom of the Son of Sirach (Ecclesiasticus), Tobit, Judith, and the Maccabees — "all of which they would have read in the Churches, but not appealed to for the confirmation of doctrine"; and below that the Apocrypha, which "they would not have read in the Churches" at all.39 His operative formula — read in church, but not to prove doctrine — is almost word-for-word Jerome's, and the rank Athanasius had called "appointed by the Fathers to be read." On the canon these two famous antagonists agree: the real fault line of the age is not Rufinus-versus-Jerome but both of them against Augustine and the African councils, who level the disputed books up into the canon. Rufinus's three-fold scheme — Canonical, Ecclesiastical, Apocrypha — supplies the vocabulary the Thirty-Nine Articles and the Westminster Confession will later borrow.

Innocent I (c. 405): Yes — the deuterocanon received from the see of Rome

A sitting bishop of Rome restates the Western verdict. Answering a query from Exuperius, bishop of Toulouse, on which books "really are received in the canon," Innocent I appends a canon-list whose Old Testament is the longer Septuagint form: Tobit, Judith, and the two books of Maccabees named outright among "the historical books," and the deuterocanonical Wisdom and Ecclesiasticus (Sirach) carried in "the five books of Solomon."40 He files these books among the canon, with none of the "read but not canonical" qualification that Athanasius, Jerome, and Rufinus had attached to the very same titles — the Augustinian, reception-based posture rather than the Hebrew reckoning.41 Coming a decade after the African councils of Hippo and Carthage had drawn the same line, it is the clearest papal-level reception of the deuterocanon before Florence and Trent — a witness to a canon already in hand, not a decree that created it. (The same letter's New-Testament list, and its condemnation of the forged apostolic gospels, are treated on the companion Biblical Canon page.)

The Masoretic Text (c. 1000 AD, Judaism): No

The medieval Jewish standard text returns the Hebrew "No" in its definitive form. The Masoretic Text — the work of the Masoretes, scribal scholars of the seventh through tenth centuries who fixed the Hebrew Bible letter by letter, surviving in the tenth-century Aleppo Codex and the 1008/1009 Leningrad Codex — is "the authoritative Hebrew and Aramaic text of the 24 books of the Hebrew Bible (Tanakh) in Rabbinic Judaism," carrying the protocanonical books alone: Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch, and the Maccabees have no place in it.42 It did not decide the canon — it preserved the boundary the rabbinic stream had long since drawn (the "Council of Jamnia" marker) — but it gave that Hebrew-sized Scripture its permanent written form, and that matters for the Christian question because it is the text "most Protestant translations of the Old Testament" render: when the Reformers returned to the Hebrew against the Vulgate's longer canon, the Hebrew they returned to was the Masoretic Text, and its twenty-four-book table became the thirty-nine-book Protestant Old Testament.42 Its scribes' fidelity, long famed, was largely vindicated by the manuscript finds — "most of the Qumran fragments… closer to the Masoretic Text than to any other text group," and the En-Gedi Leviticus scroll (c. 3rd–4th century AD) found "completely identical with the consonantal framework of the Masoretic Text" — though faithful transmission of the Hebrew settles only what that tradition contained, not whether the church's canon should be measured by the Hebrew table or by the Greek Bible it read.43 (The Septuagint-versus-Hebrew quarrel itself is gathered on the Rabbinic Corruption of Scripture and Origin and Inspiration of the Septuagint pages and not re-argued here.)

The Council of Florence (1442): Yes — the first ecumenical enumeration of the full canon

After the long medieval lull the timeline reaches its first ecumenical verdict. In the bull Cantate Domino, the Bull of Union with the Copts, the Council of Florence professes that "one and the same God is the author of the old and the new Testament" and lists the books it "accepts and venerates" — and there, threaded into one undifferentiated list beside Genesis, Isaiah, and the Gospels, stand Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Ecclesiasticus (Sirach), Baruch, and the two books of Maccabees.44 It is the Augustinian, reception-based canon of the African councils and Innocent I — the disputed books levelled into the canon with none of the "read but not canonical" tier of Athanasius, Jerome, and Rufinus — now raised for the first time to the level of a general council. The canon-list sits inside a reunion profession, not a stand-alone canon-decree, and the union itself failed; but the verb is the receptive one the Council of Trent will repeat a century later (suscipit ac veneratur, "accepts and venerates"), so the Catholic answer to the charge that the deuterocanon was "added at Trent" rests here: Florence professed in 1442 the canon Trent would fix as dogma in 1546.45

Martin Luther (1534, Protestant): No — "not equal to Holy Scripture, yet useful and good to read"

The Reformation reopens the question Florence thought closed, and answers it with Jerome's "No" set in print. In his complete German Bible of 1534 Luther lifted Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch, and 1–2 Maccabees out of the Old Testament and gathered them — all but 1–2 Esdras, which he dropped — into a separate section between the Testaments under the heading "Apocrypha — that is, books which are not held equal to the Holy Scriptures, and yet are profitable and good to read."46 The layout is the verdict, and it is the graded "No" of the Hebrew-minded Fathers made visible on the page: in his prefaces he commends Judith as "a fine, good, holy, useful book, well worth reading by us Christians" and reads Tobit as edifying fiction of married life, while denying both the canonical rank — and in the Table Talk he files them by genre, Judith "a tragedy, setting forth what the end of tyrants is… like that of Homer about Troy," Tobit "a comedy concerning women, an example for house-government."47 Luther keeps the books in the Bible but out of the canon — the same posture as Rufinus and Athanasius, read for edification, not for proving doctrine — and his heading furnishes the very words the Thirty-Nine Articles and the Westminster Confession will take up. (His return to the Hebrew canon was not antiquarian: the demoted books carried the proof-texts — 2 Maccabees 12 for the dead, Tobit 12 for almsgiving — weighed in the Arguments below.)48

The Council of Trent (1546, Roman Catholic): Yes — the deuterocanon defined as canonical, under anathema

The Counter-Reformation answers Luther by closing the question as dogma. In its fourth session, the first to take up doctrine, the Council of Trent declares that it "receives and venerates with an equal affection of piety, and reverence, all the books both of the Old and of the New Testament," and prints the list — Tobias, Judith, Wisdom, Ecclesiasticus (Sirach), Baruch, and the two books of Maccabees threaded among the rest with no second tier — the same receptive verb (suscipit ac veneratur) the Council of Florence had used a century before.49 What sets Trent apart from every earlier "Yes" is the force: it anathematizes anyone who "receive not, as sacred and canonical, the said books entire with all their parts, as… contained in the old Latin vulgate edition" — the first time the reception running from the Septuagint through Augustine to Florence is made a binding test of communion, and "entire with all their parts" deliberately covers the Greek additions to Daniel and Esther.50 The Catholic answer to the charge that the deuterocanon was "added at Trent" rests on this distinction: Trent did not add the books but defined a canon long received. Yet even as it raised the seven disputed books to dogma, its list quietly dropped three texts the medieval Vulgate had carried — 3 and 4 Esdras and the Prayer of Manasses — which the Clementine Vulgate of 1592 then relegated to a non-canonical appendix.51

The Thirty-Nine Articles (1571, Church of England): No — read "for example of life," not to establish doctrine

The English Reformation answers Trent not with Luther's heading but with Jerome's rule, cited by name. Article VI of the Articles of Religion defines canonical Scripture as "those canonical Books of the Old and New Testament, of whose authority was never any doubt in the Church," lists the Hebrew-sized Old Testament book by book, and then files the deuterocanon in a separate rank: "the other Books (as Hierome saith) the Church doth read for example of life and instruction of manners; but yet doth it not apply them to establish any doctrine."52 The list of those "other Books" is wider than Trent's: it brackets Tobias, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch, and 1–2 Maccabees together with the Greek additions to Esther and Daniel and the three texts (3–4 Esdras and the Prayer of Manasses) that Trent itself had dropped — all alike read for edification, none admissible to prove doctrine.53 It is the soft, graded "No" of Jerome, Rufinus, and Luther — keep the books, refuse them as doctrine — the moderate Anglican line that the Westminster Confession would shortly harden into flat exclusion. (The cord it cuts is doctrinal: the disputed books carried the proof-texts — 2 Maccabees 12 for the dead, Tobit 12 for almsgiving — weighed in the Arguments below.)

The Westminster Confession of Faith (1647, Reformed): No — the hardest line: "of no authority… than other human writings"

The Reformed answer hardens the Anglican "No" into a flat exclusion. Chapter 1 of the Westminster Confession names the canon by inspiration — "all the books of the Old and New Testaments… All which are given by inspiration of God, to be the rule of faith and life" — and lists the Hebrew-sized thirty-nine Old Testament books (Genesis through Malachi) with the twenty-seven of the New.54 The next section then rules on the disputed books with a force no earlier Protestant confession had used: "The books commonly called Apocrypha, not being of divine inspiration, are no part of the canon of the Scripture; and therefore are of no authority in the Church of God, nor to be any otherwise approved, or made use of, than other human writings."54 Where Jerome, Rufinus, Luther, and the Thirty-Nine Articles had all kept the deuterocanon as edifying reading — read in church "for example of life," profitable "but not for confirming… doctrine" — Westminster removes that middle rank entirely, leveling the Apocrypha down to ordinary "human writings," the mirror image of Trent's leveling them up into the canon under anathema.55 It is the sharpest Protestant verdict on the timeline, and the one that shaped later Apocrypha-free Protestant Bibles. (The cord it cuts is again doctrinal: a book that may not be "made use of" otherwise than other human writings cannot found the contested doctrines its proof-texts were adduced for — 2 Maccabees 12 for the dead, Tobit 12 for almsgiving — weighed in the Arguments below.)

The Synod of Jerusalem (1672, Eastern Orthodox): Yes — the Eastern counterpart to Trent

The timeline closes in the East. A generation after Westminster, the Synod of Jerusalem — convened at Bethlehem by Patriarch Dositheus II to repudiate a Calvinizing confession that had circulated under the name of Cyril Lucaris, Patriarch of Constantinople — gave the Eastern Orthodox church its most authoritative modern ruling on the canon. The Confession of Dositheus names the books Lucaris had set aside as "Apocrypha" — the Wisdom of Solomon, Judith, Tobit, Bel and the Dragon, Susanna, the Maccabees, and Sirach — and declares them "genuine parts of Scripture," on a level "with the other genuine Books of Divine Scripture."56 Its ground is not the Hebrew table but the delivering tradition of the church — Augustine's reception-criterion pressed to a hard edge: the same church "which hath delivered to us as genuine the Sacred Gospels… hath undoubtedly delivered these also as parts of Scripture, and the denial of these is the rejection of those." The Confession candidly concedes the unevenness this timeline documents — "not always have all of these been considered on the same level as the rest" — and overrules it by the breadth of synodal and patristic use.57 It is the exact mirror of Westminster twenty-five years before: the two latest witnesses, both reacting to the same Reformation rupture, level the same books in opposite directions — down to "human writings" in the Reformed West, up to canonical Scripture in the Orthodox East — and the Eastern "Yes" is the counterpart to Trent's, reached by the same reception-over-Hebrew route. (Later Orthodox teaching softens the affirmation, distinguishing these anaginoskomena — "things that are read" — as a profitable class below the protocanonical books; but the 1672 decree remains the high-water mark of the Eastern reception, and it keeps the Maccabees' prayer for the dead and Tobit's almsgiving inside the Eastern canon — weighed in the Arguments below.)

Arguments

Sub-claims that cut across the timeline rather than sitting at one date — each weighed adversarially on its own page, with an assessment of the interpretation it serves. Two of the disputed books carry proof-texts the Reformation made the doctrinal ground of its "No," and they are weighed here.

Does Tobit 12:9 ("alms… shall purge away all sin") teach a works-righteousness that bars it from the canon? (assessment: weak)

Does Tobit 12:9 teach a works-righteousness that bars the book from the canon? — the reading, urged by Luther in seed and crisply by Kenneth Collins and Jerry Walls (Roman but Not Catholic, 2017), that "alms doth deliver from death, and shall purge away all sin" is a sub-Christian works-salvation contradicting Paul (Eph 2:8–9; Rom 3:28), so that Tobit preaches "another gospel" and cannot be Scripture. It does not hold on its best case: the verse is the ordinary biblical almsgiving idiom — "righteousness delivereth from death" is verbatim Proverbs 10:2 and 11:4, "shewing mercy to the poor" purges iniquity in Daniel 4:27, "charity shall cover the multitude of sins" in 1 Peter 4:8 — one node in a canonical arc (Gary Anderson: Daniel 4:27 → Tobit → Sirach → the Synoptics), so the charge, pressed consistently, condemns the protocanon and the dominical teaching with it; and the faith-and-works tension it raises stands already in James 2:24, at which Luther aimed the identical objection ("a right strawy epistle") without ejecting James. What survives is the narrower, true point that Tobit presses almsgiving's atoning efficacy harder than any single protocanonical verse and became the proof-text for the Catholic doctrines of satisfaction and merit the Reformation denied — a divergence of systematic theology, not a contradiction of the gospel that unbooks it.58

Does 2 Maccabees 12:43–45 teach prayer and atoning offering for the dead? (assessment: contested)

Does 2 Maccabees 12:43–45 teach prayer and atoning offering for the dead? — the deuterocanonical proof-text the Reformation made a doctrinal stake in the canon dispute, affirmed by the Council of Trent and the Catechism, contested by Luther, Calvin, and the Westminster Confession. It is strong on its plainest leg and overstated on its furthest. As a warrant for prayer and offering for the dead the reading is plain and conceded even by the Reformers — Judas Maccabeus sends a sin offering so the fallen "might be delivered from sin," called "an holy and good thought" — and the practice it warrants was the near-universal custom of the ancient Church (weighed sound on the purgatory timeline), while the very move it makes — arguing from a practice for the dead to the resurrection — recurs in protocanonical 1 Corinthians 15:29 ("baptized for the dead"). But the leap from prayer-for-the-dead to purgatory outruns a text that shows intercession grounded in resurrection hope, not a purgative fire; the men died wearing idolatrous amulets (12:40), grave matter from which purgatory by its own definition does not deliver, so pressed as a strict purgatory proof-text it proves the wrong thing; and its doctrinal force depends on the very canonicity in dispute — strong by reception, weak by the Protestant canon-criteria (the Hebrew canon, the author's closing disclaimer at 15:38, the book's self-description as an abridgment of Jason of Cyrene). No clear winner.59
