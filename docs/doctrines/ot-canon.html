The Old Testament Canon (Is the Deuterocanon Scripture?)

Is Tobit — and with it Judith, Wisdom, Sirach (Ecclesiasticus), Baruch, and 1–2 Maccabees, the books Catholics call the deuterocanon and Protestants the Apocrypha — part of the Christian Old Testament? For the first fifteen centuries there was no single fixed list to settle it, and the answer was given differently across time. The Greek Septuagint that the early church inherited included these books; many Fathers who knew the Hebrew (Melito, Origen, Cyril, Jerome) counted a narrower, Hebrew-sized canon and ranked the extra books below it; the African councils, Rome, Florence, Trent, and the Eastern Synod of Jerusalem affirmed them; the Reformers and the Reformed confessions excluded them. This page traces those YES / NO verdicts across the centuries, earliest first, each footnoted to its source.

The question ties into two neighboring doctrines. Because the disputed books reached the church through the Greek Bible, the case turns partly on the standing of that translation — the Origin and Inspiration of the Septuagint — and on the Septuagint-versus-Hebrew quarrel gathered on the Rabbinic Corruption of Scripture page. (The companion Biblical Canon timeline treats the parallel question for the New Testament.) And it matters doctrinally: two of the disputed books carry proof-texts the Reformation contested — Tobit's "almsgiving saves from death" (Tobit 12:9) and 2 Maccabees' prayer for the dead (2 Macc 12:43–45), weighed in the Arguments below as they are added.

Timeline

The Septuagint-to-Reformation timeline is in progress; it runs from the LXX (c. 132 BC) toward the councils and confessions and the Synod of Jerusalem (1672). It opens with a framing note on the test case — Tobit — before the witnesses proper.

The test case: dating Tobit (composed c. 200 BC)

The dispute turns on a handful of books, and Tobit is the convenient bellwether, named on nearly every list below. Its own story is set in the eighth century BC: Tobit is a pious Israelite carried off to Nineveh in the Assyrian deportation that followed the fall of the northern kingdom (c. 722 BC).1 But scholars date the book itself far later — to roughly 200 BC (a range of about 225–175 BC), on the absence of any trace of the Maccabean persecution and on geographical slips that betray an author writing centuries after the events he narrates.2 That gap between narrative setting and date of writing is not peculiar to a "disputed" book: it is the very gap critical scholarship posits for canonical Daniel, whose sixth-century-BC court tales are widely held to reach their final form only in the Hellenistic age (c. 165 BC).3 So the deuterocanon's lateness, by itself, does not set it apart from every protocanonical book — which is why the canon question is settled below not by date of composition but by reception, list by list.

The Septuagint (c. 132 BC): Yes — inclusion

The timeline opens not with a verdict but with a fact: the Greek Old Testament the early church inherited — the Septuagint — already contained Tobit, together with Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch, and 1–2 Maccabees. The three great fourth- and fifth-century Christian Bibles (Codices Vaticanus, Sinaiticus, and Alexandrinus) all carry Tobit among the Old Testament books, interleaved with the protocanonical ones rather than set apart.4 This Greek Bible was "the most frequently used version of the Old Testament in the early Church,"5 and the New Testament's own scriptural citations follow it overwhelmingly — Archer and Chirichigno count 340 quotations agreeing with the Septuagint against only 33 with the (later-named) Masoretic Hebrew.6 So the disputed books were inside the church's working Bible from the outset: the YES / NO verdicts below are arguments over whether to keep what the Greek Scriptures already held, not whether to add something new. (How that Greek Bible came to be reckoned authoritative is the subject of the companion Origin and Inspiration of the Septuagint timeline; it is not re-argued here.)

The "Council of Jamnia" (c. 90 AD, Judaism): No — but no council

The Jewish "No" to the deuterocanon is real but harder to pin to a date than the old story allowed. That story is the Council of Jamnia: a hypothesis Heinrich Graetz floated in 1871, that a rabbinic council sat at Yavneh about 70–90 AD and there closed the Hebrew canon, shutting out the Greek extras in reaction to the rise of Christianity.7 Twentieth-century scholarship has largely abandoned it: Jack P. Lewis's "What Do We Mean by Jabneh?" (1964) showed that "canonicity was not the issue and the debate was not connected with Jabneh… No book is ever mentioned in the sources as being excluded from the canon at Jabneh," and F. F. Bruce judged it "probably unwise to talk as if there were a Council or Synod of Jamnia."8 Strip the legend away and a genuine uncertainty remains — "there is no scholarly consensus as to when the canon of the Hebrew Bible… was fixed," with proposals ranging from the Hasmonean period (c. 140–40 BC) to the second century AD and later.9 What is not in doubt is the result: the Pharisaic, proto-rabbinic Scriptures that hardened into the later Jewish canon never carried Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch, or 1–2 Maccabees.10 So the verdict is double-edged: the rabbinic canon excluded these books, but the sharp conciliar exclusion often cited against them did not happen as described. (The broader Septuagint-versus-Hebrew quarrel is gathered on the Rabbinic Corruption of Scripture page and not re-argued here.)

Josephus (c. AD 93, Judaism): No — a closed twenty-two-book Hebrew canon

The single most-cited Jewish witness for a settled Hebrew canon writes within a few years of the supposed “Council of Jamnia.” Defending the antiquity of his nation against the grammarian Apion, the historian Josephus (c. AD 37 – c. 100) states that the Jews hold not “an innumerable multitude of books… disagreeing from and contradicting one another,” but “only twenty-two books, which contain the records of all the past times” and “are justly believed to be divine” — five of Moses, thirteen of “the prophets, who were after Moses,” and four of “hymns to God, and precepts for the conduct of human life.”11 The reign of Artaxerxes (mid-fifth century BC) is the cutoff: the prophetic record runs “from the death of Moses till the reign of Artaxerxes,” and everything written “since Artaxerxes” he reckons has “not been esteemed of the like authority with the former… because there hath not been an exact succession of prophets since that time” — the very window in which the Greek-only books fall.12 So fixed is this body of writings, he adds, that “no one has been so bold as either to add any thing to them, to take any thing from them, or to make any change in them,” and Jews would “willingly… die for them.”13 That a Greek-writing Jew who himself drew on the Septuagint should bound the inspired books at Artaxerxes is the backbone of the case for a closed, deuterocanon-excluding Hebrew canon already fixed in the first century — weighed on its own argument page (assessment: contested) in the Arguments below. Two cautions ride with it: his “twenty-two” is the Greek-and-patristic count, not the rabbinic “twenty-four” of the Talmud (usually explained as the same books folded differently — Ruth into Judges, Lamentations into Jeremiah — to match the twenty-two letters of the Hebrew alphabet), and he names no titles, so which books fill his thirteen and four is inferred, not stated — “it is impossible to know exactly which books he included, or how he counted them.”14

Clement of Rome (c. 96 AD): Yes — earliest practical use

Before any Christian canon-list exists, the disputed books are already at work in the church's own argument. Writing from Rome to quell a schism at Corinth, Clement — whose letter, 1 Clement, is among the earliest post-apostolic writings, usually dated to about 95–96 AD15 — weaves a line of the deuterocanonical Wisdom of Solomon into his appeal in the same breath as the Gospel and the Psalms: "Who shall say unto Him, What hast thou done? or, Who shall resist the power of His strength?" — the words of Wisdom 12:12 and 11:21, set beside Matthew 24:35 and Psalm 19 with no mark of distinction.16 Later he holds up "the blessed Judith," who, "exposing herself to danger," went out to the enemy camp for her besieged people, paired with Esther as a pattern of faith.17 The two citations differ in force — Wisdom is woven in as authority (though with no "it is written" formula), Judith adduced as a historical example — yet together they make 1 Clement the earliest Christian witness to the disputed books functioning as edifying, quotable Scripture, generations before Melito drew up the first Christian Old-Testament list. No canon-verdict is rendered here — the letter enumerates no books; it simply uses them — which is exactly why it bears on the question, weighed in the Arguments below, of whether the primitive church reckoned these books Scripture.

The Didache (c. 100 AD): Yes — an early practical use

Between Clement and the canon-lists stands an anonymous document witness: the Didache, or Teaching of the Twelve Apostles, the primitive church-order manual usually dated near AD 100 (estimates run c. 50–150).18 In its catalogue of moral duties it embeds a line of the deuterocanonical Sirach (Ecclesiasticus): "Be not a stretcher forth of the hands to receive and a drawer of them back to give" — the words of Sirach 4:31 — set in the same chain of commandments as a paraphrase of the apostolic charge to raise one's children "in the fear of God" (cf. Ephesians 6:4), with no mark of distinction.19 Like Clement and Polycarp, it renders no canon-list and prefaces the maxim with no "it is written"; the Sirach line is woven in as authoritative counsel, a third early datapoint of practical use before Melito drew up the first Christian Old-Testament list. One caveat weighs the force down: the moral section is a Christian recension of the Jewish "Two Ways" tradition (shared with the Epistle of Barnabas), and the Sirach verse stands inside that inherited block — so the echo may travel as common ethical tradition rather than a deliberate citation, a qualification carried into the Arguments below.

Polycarp of Smyrna (c. 125 AD): Yes — an early practical use

A generation after Clement, and still before any Christian canon-list, the disputed books surface again as edifying maxims in the church's own exhortation. Writing from Smyrna to the church at Philippi — a letter usually dated c. 110–140 AD20 — Polycarp urges, "When you can do good, defer it not, because alms delivers from death" — the saying of the deuterocanonical Tobit (Tob 4:10; 12:9), set in a chain of apostolic and prophetic phrases (1 Peter 2:12, 17 and 5:5; Isaiah 52:5) with no mark of distinction.21 The force should not be overstated: Polycarp renders no canon-list and prefaces the maxim with no "it is written"; the Tobit line is woven in as authoritative counsel, not adduced as a proof-text, and the chapter survives only in a later Latin version. Yet the seamless weave — a deuterocanonical maxim braided with 1 Peter and Isaiah — makes Polycarp a second early witness, beside Clement, to the disputed books functioning as quotable, edifying Scripture generations before Melito drew up the first Christian Old-Testament list — the practical "use" datapoint weighed in the Arguments below.

Melito of Sardis (c. 170 AD): No

The earliest Christian list of the Old Testament books comes from Melito, bishop of Sardis, who travelled East "to the place where these things were preached" to learn "accurately the books of the Old Testament" and sent the catalogue to his friend Onesimus; Eusebius preserved it verbatim. It is a Hebrew-sized canon: Tobit, Judith, Sirach, Baruch, and 1–2 Maccabees are all absent (so too Esther), and it closes at "Esdras."22 The one word that might name a deuterocanonical book — "Wisdom," set right after "the Proverbs of Solomon" — is on the usual reading a second ancient title for Proverbs itself (the "all-virtuous Wisdom"), not the deuterocanonical Book of Wisdom, so the list most likely carries no disputed book at all.23 Melito heads the long line of Hebrew-minded Fathers (Origen, Cyril, Athanasius, Jerome) who counted a narrower canon than the Greek Septuagint the church read in worship.

The Muratorian Fragment (c. 170 AD): Yes — Wisdom only

The same years yield a Western witness pointing the other way. The Muratorian Fragment — the oldest surviving list of New Testament books — counts, in the middle of its New-Testament reckoning, "the [book of] Wisdom, written by the friends of Solomon," ranking the deuterocanonical Wisdom of Solomon among the received writings.24 That makes it the earliest Western witness that a disputed Old-Testament book was received as Scripture — but it is a cross-over witness, not an OT-list: it names only Wisdom (not Tobit, Judith, Sirach, Baruch, or Maccabees), and it files the book oddly among the New Testament, a sign of how unsettled the line between the Testaments still was.25 Set beside Melito's contemporary list — whose "Wisdom" is most likely a second name for Proverbs — the two witnesses reach opposite verdicts on the very same word.

Athenagoras of Athens (c. 177 AD): Yes — Baruch quoted as the inspired voice of the prophets

A few years before Irenaeus, an Athenian philosopher-convert presses a disputed book into service as prophecy. Defending Christians against the charge of atheism in his Plea for the Christians (c. 177), addressed to the emperors Marcus Aurelius and Commodus, Athenagoras calls the Hebrew prophets — "Moses or… Isaiah and Jeremiah, and the other prophets," who "lifted in ecstasy… by the impulses of the Divine Spirit, uttered the things with which they were inspired" — and strings together their utterances. The first he quotes, "The Lord is our God; no other can be compared with Him," is not protocanonical but the deuterocanonical Baruch (Baruch 3:35–36), set among the inspired words of the prophets beside three sayings of Isaiah (44:6; 43:10–11; 66:1).26 He does not name Baruch — he gathers the chain under "the prophets," and Baruch was reckoned in the Greek tradition an appendix to Jeremiah, whom he does name — so this is firmer than a woven maxim (the line is expressly classed among words spoken "by the… Divine Spirit") yet short of an explicit canon-list, a notch below Irenaeus's named "Jeremiah the prophet… saying" formula for the same book.27 A contemporary of Melito's narrower list, he renders no canon-verdict; he simply uses a disputed book as the inspired voice of the prophets — the datapoint weighed in the Arguments below.

Irenaeus of Lyons (c. 180 AD): Yes — the disputed books cited as prophetic Scripture

A decade after Melito's narrower list, and from the other direction, the great anti-Gnostic bishop of Lyons quotes the disputed books not as edifying maxims but as the words of named prophets. In Irenaeus's Against Heresies (c. 175–189) the Greek addition to Daniel — the story of Susanna — is cited as words "to be found in Daniel the prophet": "O thou seed of Canaan, and not of Judah, beauty hath deceived thee…" (Susanna 56, 52).28 And a long passage of the deuterocanonical Baruch (Baruch 4:36–5:9) is adduced as genuine prophecy — "Jeremiah the prophet has pointed out… saying, 'Look around Jerusalem towards the east…'" — Baruch being reckoned in the Greek and Latin tradition an appendix to Jeremiah and routinely cited under his name.29 Where Clement, the Didache, and Polycarp braid the disputed books in without a citation formula, Irenaeus — a hearer of Polycarp, himself a disciple of the apostle John — gives them the explicit "the prophet" attribution, the firmest second-century use of the disputed books as Scripture before a Christian Old-Testament list. He renders no canon-verdict (his work enumerates no books); he simply uses them as prophecy — the datapoint weighed in the Arguments below, on whether the primitive church reckoned these books Scripture.

Clement of Alexandria (c. 200 AD): Yes — the disputed books cited with the "Scripture" formula

With the head of the Alexandrian catechetical school the practical use hardens into an explicit citation formula. A generation after Irenaeus, Clement (c. 150–215; his Paedagogus and Stromateis written c. 198–203) quotes four of the disputed books the way he quotes Moses or the Gospel — introduced as “Scripture.” He cites Sirach 21:6 as “the Scripture which says, ‘And he that feareth the Lord will turn to his heart’”; Tobit 12:8 as “the Scripture which says, ‘Fasting with prayer is a good thing’”; Wisdom 2:12 as “Scripture… when it says, ‘Let us remove the righteous man from us’”; and a long passage of Baruch (3:16–19) as the words of “the Divine Scripture.”30 He also strings Judith 8:27 (“he that is near the Lord is full of stripes”) into a chain with Galatians 3:24, Isaiah 5:21, and Proverbs 1:7, a use closer to Clement of Rome's woven maxims than to the explicit-formula citations.31 Where Irenaeus ascribed Susanna and Baruch to named prophets, Clement goes a step further in form — the bare word “Scripture” / “the Divine Scripture,” the same label he reserves for the protocanonical books — making him the firmest pre-list witness yet to the disputed books reckoned as Scripture, weighed in the Arguments below. He renders no canon-list; he simply cites these books as Scripture, a working “Yes” alongside the narrower Hebrew lists of Melito and (a generation on) Origen, his own successor at Alexandria.

The Babylonian Talmud (c. 200 AD, Judaism): Two-edged — a Hebrew-sized enumerated canon ending in Chronicles, yet Sirach cited as Writings

The fullest early Jewish articulation of the canon — and a witness that cuts both ways. A baraita preserved in the Babylonian Talmud, Bava Batra 14b, enumerates the Prophets and then the Writings — "… Daniel and the Scroll of Esther; and Ezra and Chronicles" — closing the whole of Scripture with Chronicles and listing none of the Greek extras. It is the single clearest ancient Jewish attestation of a Hebrew-sized, Genesis-to-Chronicles arrangement (the order on which the "from Abel to Zechariah" argument leans), and because a baraita is tannaitic material it is comparatively early, though it stands almost alone for a millennium.32 Yet the same compilation, at Bava Kamma 92b, quotes Ben Sira (Sirach 13) as a verse of the Writings — "triplicated in the Writings, as it is written…" — with the same "as it is written" formula it gives Torah and Prophets, so the rabbinic stream that supplies the Chronicles-last order did not draw an airtight line around the deuterocanon.33 The baraita is conventionally placed c. 200 (the Mishnaic horizon) and slotted here by that earlier date, though the Talmud's redaction runs c. 500–600; both passages are weighed in the Arguments below.34

Hippolytus of Rome (c. 205 AD): Yes — the disputed books read as prophecy of Christ

In the same decades, and from the West, the most learned teacher of the Roman church puts two of the disputed books to work as prophetic Scripture. Hippolytus (c. 170–235) opens his Expository Treatise Against the Jews by adducing — against his Jewish interlocutor — “the prophecy of Solomon, which speaketh of Christ”: the deuterocanonical Wisdom of Solomon (2:1, 12–20), the foretelling of the plot against the righteous one who “calleth himself the Child of God,” introduced with “the prophet says” and read as a Solomonic prophecy of the Passion.35 And in Against Noetus he expounds the deuterocanonical Baruch (3:35–37) — “This is our God… afterward did He show Himself upon earth, and conversed with men” — as a genuine prophecy of the incarnation; the “Thus it is written” formula is on the lips of his Noetian opponents, but Hippolytus does not contest the citation’s scriptural standing, granting it (“That said he rightly”) and disputing only whom it speaks of.36 Where Irenaeus ascribed Susanna and Baruch to named prophets and Clement labelled the books “Scripture,” Hippolytus reads Wisdom as the very “prophecy of Solomon” concerning Christ — a Western witness, between Clement and Origen, on the “using” side of the divide. He renders no canon-verdict; he simply uses the disputed books as prophecy — the datapoint weighed in the Arguments below.

Tertullian (c. 210 AD): Yes — the disputed books used, as prophecy and as example

The first great Latin theologian, writing from Carthage, puts the disputed books to work in the same African church that would later enshrine them in conciliar form. Tertullian (c. 155 – c. 220) quotes the deuterocanonical Letter of Jeremiah (Baruch 6:3) as the very “words of Jeremias,” set beside Daniel 3 in his exhortation to martyrdom: the three youths “remembered also the words of Jeremias writing to those over whom that captivity was impending… Beware, therefore, that ye also do not be altogether like the foreigners… but say in your mind, Our duty is to worship Thee, O Lord.”37 He ranks the Wisdom of Solomon among “our Christian authorities,” reading of God as “the searcher and witness of the heart” (Wisdom 1:6) in a chain with Proverbs, Matthew, the Psalms, Paul, and 1 John — though that particular citation is disputed: Roger Beckwith reads the phrase as Psalm 139:23 rather than Wisdom, and Tertullian’s firmer, undisputed use of the book comes in the Prescription against Heretics, where “the porch of Solomon” is said to have “taught that ‘the Lord should be sought in simplicity of heart’” — the opening words of Wisdom 1:1.38 And he twice adduces deuterocanonical figures as patterns of holiness: “a Judith, daughter of Merari” stands among “so many other examples of saints” beside Isaac and John,39 and Susanna’s modest unveiling at her trial is set beside Rebecca’s veil — though these are historical examples, not cited authorities.40 The force is graded: Baruch is quoted under the prophet’s name as the others quote “Jeremiah,” Wisdom adduced (with one disputed text) as a “Christian authority,” Judith and Susanna only as saintly examples. Like Irenaeus and Hippolytus he renders no canon-list; he simply uses the disputed books — the earliest Latin witness to that practice, a generation before the Greek-side “No” of Origen, and the datapoint weighed in the Arguments below.

Origen of Alexandria (c. 240 AD): No — but keeps the Greek

The greatest biblical scholar of the early church returns the timeline to a Hebrew-sized canon. In his Commentary on Psalm 1, quoted by Eusebius, Origen lists the Old Testament books "as the Hebrews have handed them down" as twenty-two, the count of the Hebrew letters — Tobit, Judith, Sirach, and the Wisdom of Solomon all absent, and the Maccabees named only to be set "besides these."41 Yet the same Origen will not surrender the longer Greek text the churches actually read: answering Julius Africanus, who challenged the story of Susanna as no genuine part of Daniel, he refuses to "reject as spurious the copies in use in our Churches" and appeals to the Providence that gave those Scriptures to "all the Churches of Christ."42 So his verdict is double, and honestly so: a "No" by the Hebrew enumeration, a working "keep" in the church's practice — and more than a keep, for across his homilies and treatises Origen quotes Tobit, Sirach, Baruch, the Maccabees, Wisdom, Judith, and Susanna outright as "Scripture," "holy Scripture," or what "is written." It is the very distinction between canonical and read in the churches that Origen hands forward to Athanasius, Rufinus, and Jerome.43

Cyprian of Carthage (c. 253 AD): Yes — the disputed books cited as “Holy Scripture”

From the Latin West, and with the fullest formulae yet, the bishop and martyr of Carthage cites the disputed books exactly as he cites the Law and the Gospel. A generation after his fellow Carthaginian Tertullian, and a contemporary of Origen in the East, Cyprian (c. 200–258, bishop of Carthage from c. 248, martyred under Valerian) quotes Tobit 12:8 as what “Holy Scripture instructs us, saying” — “Prayer is good with fasting and almsgiving”44 Sirach 2:1, 4–5 as what “Holy Scripture teaches and forewarns, saying,”45 and the Wisdom of Solomon 3:4–8 — the martyrs “tried… as gold in the furnace” — as “the sacred Scripture.”46 He gathers 1 Maccabees 2:62–63 under “Holy Scripture meets and warns us, saying,”47 ascribes the deuterocanonical Letter of Jeremiah (Baruch 6:6) to “the Holy Spirit… by Jeremiah,”48 and quotes the Greek addition to Daniel (Bel and the Dragon 14:5) as the inspired word of Daniel “filled with the Holy Spirit.”49 He draws up no canon-list, so he renders no canon-verdict; but these are the same labels he reserves for Moses, the Psalms, and the Gospel, making him the strongest pre-Nicene Western witness to the disputed books read outright as Scripture — in the very African church whose councils would later enshrine the longer canon, the Latin counterpart to Origen’s Greek-side practice. The datapoint is weighed in the Arguments below.

Dionysius of Alexandria (c. 264 AD): Yes — the disputed books used as inspired Scripture

The Greek East supplies a near-contemporary of Cyprian from the very school that produced the era's most careful canon-list. Dionysius (c. 190–264), pupil and successor of Origen as head of the Alexandrian catechetical school and bishop of Alexandria from c. 248, twice presses a disputed book into service as inspired Scripture. In his treatise On Nature, arguing against the Epicurean doctrine that the ordered cosmos arose by chance from random atoms, he answers the philosophers by appeal to Sirach: “listen to the divine oracles: ‘The works of the Lord are in judgment…’” (Sirach 16:26–27) — the deuterocanonical wisdom book introduced as ta theia logia, the very category of inspired utterance.50 And in his doctrinal Epistle to Dionysius, Bishop of Rome, defending the Son's eternity against Sabellianism, he glosses the deity of Christ by reading the Wisdom of Solomon's “He is the breath of God's power” (Wisdom 7:25) alongside John 4:24, “God is a Spirit,” to ground the title “Breath” for Christ — the Wisdom text handled exactly as the Johannine one beside it.51 Where his master Origen rendered a double verdict — a Hebrew-sized canon by enumeration, a working “keep” of the books read in the churches — the pupil supplies a clean instance of that second, “using” half: the next Alexandrian generation still citing the disputed books as the inspired word, with no list set against it. He renders no canon-verdict; he simply uses them — the Greek-East datapoint, beside Cyprian's Latin one, weighed in the Arguments below.

Methodius of Olympus (c. 290 AD): Yes — Wisdom & Sirach handled as Scripture

A second Greek witness, from outside the Alexandrian school, joins Dionysius on the “using” side. Methodius (c. 290; martyred c. 311), bishop of Olympus in Lycia and a pointed early critic of Origen, twice handles a disputed book as Scripture. In his major work, the Banquet of the Ten Virgins, debating the divine ordering of every birth, he brings forward Wisdom of Solomon 3:16 with the bare formula “the Scripture which says”: “As for the children of the adulterers, they shall not come to their perfection” — the deuterocanonical text introduced under the unqualified label he reserves for the protocanon.52 And the Oration on Simeon and Anna — a work transmitted under his name but of contested authenticity — ranges Sirach 16:6, “Amongst the disobedient people shall the fire burn,” under “as it is written,” in the same breath as Psalm 18:8.53 That his anti-Origenist pen reaches so naturally for the disputed books shows the “using” practice was no Alexandrian peculiarity but the common Greek instinct, even among Origen’s critics. He draws up no canon-list, so renders no canon-verdict; the datapoint is weighed in the Arguments below.

Lactantius (c. 310 AD): Yes — Sirach cited as “Solomon’s” testimony to the Word of God

On the eve of Nicaea, the first Latin apologist for the faith presses a disputed book into christological service. Lactantius (c. 250 – c. 325), the rhetorician Constantine would set over his son’s education, builds in his Divine Institutes (c. 303–311) a case for the eternal Word made flesh and calls “Solomon” as its Old-Testament witness: “I came forth out of the mouth of the Most High before all creatures… and covered the whole earth with a cloud” — the words of the deuterocanonical Sirach (Ecclesiasticus 24:3–4), the self-speech of personified Wisdom — set immediately beside John 1:1, “In the beginning was the Word.”54 The ascription to “Solomon” is itself a tell: Sirach, written by Jesus ben Sira, circulated among the “books of Solomon” and was cited under his name, so the mistake confirms rather than weakens the witness — Lactantius reaches for the text precisely because he reckons it inspired Solomonic testimony, fit to gloss the Gospel’s prologue. The use is doctrinal, not merely edifying: the deuterocanonical verse carries probative weight for the deity and creative agency of the Word, in the manner of Hippolytus reading Wisdom as “the prophecy of Solomon which speaketh of Christ.” Like the witnesses before him he renders no canon-list; he simply uses a disputed book as prophecy of Christ — a Western, Constantinian-era datapoint, beside the Greek-East Dionysius, weighed in the Arguments below.

Eusebius of Caesarea (c. 320 AD): Both at once — transmits the Hebrew lists, yet cites the disputed books as Scripture

The single figure who most embodies the era's tension stands on the eve of Nicaea. Eusebius (c. 260–339), bishop of Caesarea and father of church history, is the very channel through which the two great Hebrew-sized lists reach us — he preserves Melito's catalogue (Ecclesiastical History iv.26) and Origen's "twenty-two, as the Hebrews have handed them down" (vi.25), the documentary backbone of the narrower-canon case.55 Yet in his own apologetic works he uses the disputed books as Scripture. In the Praeparatio Evangelica (c. 313–320), having quoted the deuterocanonical Wisdom of Solomon — "she reacheth from end to end with full strength, and ordereth all things graciously" (Wisdom 8:1) — among Solomon's words, he caps the citation "Thus the Scripture speaks," then sets Philo's lesser clarity beside it: the bare label "the Scripture" for a disputed book, the same he gives the protocanon.56 In the same work he adduces the Greek addition to Daniel as proof "from Scripture" of God's foreknowledge — "the description by Susanna of God as knowing all things before they come to pass" (Susanna 42) — though here he is transcribing, with approval, a long extract from Origen rather than writing in his own voice.57 And in the Demonstratio Evangelica (c. 318–323), expounding the incarnation, he quotes the deuterocanonical Baruch (3:29–37) — "Afterward did he show himself upon earth, and conversed with men" — and pronounces: "I need add nothing to these inspired words, which so clearly support my argument," reading Baruch exactly as Hippolytus had, as genuine prophecy of Christ.58 He compiles no Old-Testament canon-list of his own, so renders no personal verdict; but the contemporary who hands the church its sharpest Hebrew-canon evidence is, in his own practice, on the "using" side of the divide — the canonical-versus-read-in-the-churches tension of his master Origen embodied in one man, and the datapoint weighed in the Arguments below.

Alexander of Alexandria (c. 321 AD): Yes — Sirach chained with Paul, Genesis & Isaiah as scriptural testimony

The bishop who opened the Arian controversy — Alexander of Alexandria (d. 326), Athanasius’s mentor and predecessor — weaves the deuterocanonical Sirach into the texture of his christology. In his encyclical against Arius (the letter to Alexander of Byzantium, c. 319–324), arguing that the eternal generation of the Word lies beyond inquiry, he chains in one breath Sirach 3:21 — “Seek not out the things that are too hard for you, neither search the things that are above your strength,” adduced as binding authority one is impious “not listening to” — with the Apostle Paul (“Eye has not seen…,” 1 Corinthians 2:9), God’s word to Abraham that he could not number the stars (Genesis 15:5), Sirach 1:2 (“Who can number the sand of the sea, and the drops of rain”), and the prophet’s “Who shall declare his generation?” (Isaiah 53:8).59 He does not label the Sirach lines “Scripture” or “it is written”; he simply uses them — undistinguished from the canonical texts they stand among — as testimony in a doctrinal argument. His witness is therefore softer than an explicit Scripture-formula: it rests on juxtaposition and on the authority-language he applies to Sirach 3:21. Like the witnesses before him he renders no canon-list; he attests early-fourth-century Alexandrian practice, a datapoint — with that caveat — weighed in the Arguments below.

The Council of Nicaea (325 AD): No recorded ruling — but a Judith report

The first ecumenical council published no canon-list. Its twenty surviving canons and synodal letter concern the Arian and Melitian schisms, the lapsed, the date of Easter, and clerical discipline — not the books of Scripture.60 It belongs on this timeline for one reason: it is the earliest council to which a verdict on a deuterocanonical book has ever been attributed. Eighty years later Jerome reports, in his Preface to Judith, that the council "is found… to have been counted" the deuterocanonical book of Judith "among the number of the Sacred Scriptures," and gives that as his reason for translating a book he otherwise ranked below the canon.61 But the report will not carry a Nicene canon-decree: Jerome himself, in the very same prefaces, rules Judith out of the canon, and Athanasius — who was present at Nicaea — still placed Judith among the books "not… included in the Canon, but appointed by the Fathers to be read" in 367.62 The full adversarial weighing of the claim is set out on its own argument page — assessed weak for the decree-reading, while the modest reading (that Nicaea merely cited Judith as Scripture in argument) is plausible.63

Aphrahat the Persian Sage (c. 337 AD): Yes — Maccabean history read as fulfilled prophecy

The timeline reaches the Syriac East, beyond the Roman frontier. Aphrahat (c. 270–345), “the Persian Sage,” a bishop in the Sasanian empire whose Demonstrations are the earliest substantial body of Syriac theology, treats the Maccabean history as authoritative, prophecy-fulfilling Scripture. In Demonstration 5 (Of Wars, dated by its author to AD 337) he declares Psalm 79 “fulfilled… when the venerable and aged Eleazar was slain, and the sons of the blessed Samuna, seven in number” — the martyrs of 2 Maccabees 6:18–7:42 — alongside “Judas (Maccabeus) and his brethren.”64 And, expounding Daniel 7, he reads the worm-eaten death of Antiochus — “he became sick with a grievous and evil sickness… for worms were crawling and falling from him and eating his flesh” — the death narrated in 2 Maccabees 9:5–12, as the heaven-sent judgment Daniel foretold (“the judgment was set… until the end of the kingdom,” Daniel 7:26), with Isaiah’s “worm Jacob” (Isaiah 41:14) supplying the retribution: the worms devour him “because he oppressed the worm Jacob.”65 He prefaces the Maccabean material with no “it is written” formula and draws up no canon-list, so the witness is the softer, historical-and-typological kind — like Clement of Rome with “the blessed Judith” — but it weaves the deuterocanonical narrative seamlessly into Daniel and the Psalms as sacred history come true, and it adds the Syriac/Eastern tradition, geographically distinct from the Greek and Latin witnesses, to the “using” side of the divide weighed in the Arguments below.

Cyril of Jerusalem (c. 350 AD): No — but Baruch kept

Catechizing the candidates for baptism at Jerusalem, Cyril recites an Eastern Old-Testament list of twenty-two books, the Hebrew-letter count, and draws the line sharply: "have nothing to do with the apocryphal writings… study earnestly these only which we read openly in the Church." Tobit, Judith, Sirach, the Wisdom of Solomon, and 1–2 Maccabees are all absent.66 Yet the list is not the bare Hebrew thirty-nine: Cyril's single book of Jeremiah is reckoned "including Baruch and Lamentations and the Epistle" — two short deuterocanonical works folded in as appendices, exactly as the Greek manuscripts carried them — and he credits the whole Hebrew-sized canon to the Septuagint's seventy-two translators.67 So his "No" to the disputed books keeps Baruch inside a canonical book, the same posture — rank by the Hebrew count, read from the Greek — that Origen had already shown and that Athanasius and Epiphanius will repeat.

Hilary of Poitiers (c. 360 AD): No

The first Latin Father to set out a canon-list returns the Eastern verdict in the West. In the prologue to his Tractatus super Psalmos (Expositions of the Psalms) Hilary reckons the Old Testament at twenty-two books, "according to the number of the [Hebrew] letters" — Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, and the Maccabees absent from the count — recording only that "to this some add Tobit and Judith to make twenty-four books." A Westerner who had spent his exile among the Greek bishops, he carries the Hebrew enumeration of Origen and Cyril into Latin a generation before Jerome would make that count the West's standard — though Hilary, like the others, still quotes the disputed books in his own argument, ranking by the Hebrew list while reading from the wider Greek Bible.68

The Cheltenham List (c. 360 AD): Yes — an early African list with the deuterocanon

From the same years, and the other direction, comes an anonymous Latin catalogue — the Cheltenham (or Mommsen) List, a stichometric table of the canonical books with their line-counts, most likely North African. Its Old Testament runs out the longer canon: the two books of Maccabees, Tobit, and Judith are enumerated among the Scriptures with the rest, and the whole is sealed by the Apocalypse's "four and twenty elders."69 It is a Western witness to the deuterocanon a full generation before the African councils of Hippo and Carthage gave that same canon conciliar form — the reception Augustine would later state as a rule.

Athanasius of Alexandria (367 AD): No — but Baruch kept, in three tiers

The most influential Eastern voice of the century sorts the books into a famous three-tier scheme. In his thirty-ninth Festal Letter Athanasius first lists the Old-Testament canon as the Hebrew twenty-two — Tobit, Judith, Sirach, the Wisdom of Solomon, and the Maccabees all absent — though his Jeremiah, like Origen's and Cyril's, carries Baruch and the Epistle of Jeremiah folded in.70 Below the canon he sets a second rank — books "not indeed included in the Canon, but appointed by the Fathers to be read… for instruction in the word of godliness" — and there he files the deuterocanon proper: the Wisdom of Solomon, Sirach, Judith, Tobit, and (strikingly) even the protocanonical Esther; below that again stand the "apocryphal writings… an invention of heretics."71 So his "No" is graded, not flat: the disputed books are not Scripture, yet not heretical either — "appointed by the Fathers to be read," the very distinction Origen had drawn, now made an explicit list that Rufinus and Jerome will carry forward. (The same letter gives the first New-Testament catalogue identical to the present twenty-seven, treated on his New Testament canon page.)

Basil of Caesarea (c. 379 AD): Yes — the disputed books cited as Scripture, though he leaves no list

The eldest of the three Cappadocian Fathers stands on the "using" side of the divide his own circle is famous for narrowing. Basil (c. 330–379), bishop of Caesarea, draws up no Old-Testament canon-list, but across his works he quotes the disputed books as authority. In the Hexaemeron he clinches a lesson on the soul's instability with the bare formula reserved for the protocanon — "What Scripture says is very true, 'As for a fool he changeth as the moon'" — the words of the deuterocanonical Sirach (27:11); and a consoling letter grounds divine providence in the Wisdom of Solomon: the Lord "ordereth 'all things in measure and weight'" (Wisdom 11:20), set beside 1 Corinthians 10:13.72 In On the Holy Spirit he settles points of doctrine by naming the authors of two more disputed books — Baruch, who "exhibit[s] the immutability… of the Divine mode of existence… 'For thou sittest for ever and we perish utterly'" (Baruch 3:3), and Judith, "'Thou hast thought, and what things thou didst determine were ready at hand'" (Judith 9:5–6) — and holds up the Maccabean mother of the seven sons as a martyr-model.73 He is sometimes counted among the Fathers who "rejected" the deuterocanon (so James White lists "Basil the Great"), but that rests on the Cappadocian company, not on Basil: the Hebrew-sized lists belong to his friends Gregory of Nazianzus and Amphilochius, while Basil's own surviving practice — Sirach called "Scripture," Baruch and Judith named to prove doctrine — runs the other way. He renders no canon-verdict; he simply uses these books, the purest "usage" half of the list-versus-usage split seen in Athanasius, Cyril, and Hilary — the datapoint weighed in the Arguments below.

Gregory of Nyssa (c. 380 AD): Yes — the disputed books cited as "the prophetical writing," as Baruch "delivered… by the Holy Scriptures," and as "the Scripture"

The third Cappadocian joins his brother Basil on the "using" side of the divide. Gregory of Nyssa (c. 335–395), bishop of Nyssa from 372, draws up no canon-list, but across his doctrinal and ascetic works he quotes three of the disputed books as inspired authority. In On the Making of Man (16.14, 379) he grounds the divine foreknowledge in the Greek addition to Daniel — the prayer of Susanna, "knoweth all things before they be" (Susanna 42 / Daniel 13:42) — introduced as the word of "the prophetical writing."74 In Against Eunomius (c. 380–383) he twice presses the deuterocanonical Baruch (3:37) into service as the prophetic proof of the Incarnation — "the God who was in the beginning, 'afterwards,' as Baruch says, 'was seen upon the earth, and conversed with men'" — the verse named both as Baruch's and as words "delivered to us by the Holy Scriptures" (and, in Book VI.4, "as the prophet says"), turning the heretic's own argument against him.75 And in On Virginity (15, c. 371) he clinches an exhortation with the deuterocanonical Wisdom of Solomon (1:4) under the bare label "the Scripture": "into the malicious soul Wisdom cannot come."76 Where his brother Basil called Sirach "Scripture" and cited Baruch, Judith, and Wisdom to settle doctrine, Gregory does the same — the two Cappadocian brothers standing together on the "using" side while the Hebrew-sized lists of the circle belong to their friends Gregory of Nazianzus and Amphilochius. He renders no canon-verdict; he simply uses the disputed books — the datapoint weighed in the Arguments below.

Amphilochius of Iconium (c. 380 AD): No

An Eastern bishop, friend of the Cappadocians, casts the canon into verse. In his Iambics for Seleucus Amphilochius warns that "not every book is safe which has acquired the venerable name of Scripture" — some are "spurious and utterly unsafe, like counterfeit… coins" — and then recites a Hebrew-sized Old Testament that omits Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, and the Maccabees, and gives even Esther only as a book "some approve."77 It is the Hebrew-canon posture of his fellow Cappadocian Gregory of Nazianzus, set to meter for memorizing.

The Apostolic Canons (c. 380 AD): Yes — Judith, three Maccabees, Sirach commended

The Eastern church's own canon-law collection returns a "Yes" of singular shape. Canon 85, the closing canon of the Antiochene Apostolic Canons appended to the Apostolic Constitutions, bids "the following books be esteemed venerable and holy by all of you" and lists, among the Old Testament, "[one of Judith;] three of the Maccabees," and urges the young to learn "the Wisdom of the learned Sirach."78 Its canon runs wider than the later Catholic deuterocanon — it receives three books of Maccabees, not two — yet it names neither Tobit nor the Wisdom of Solomon: a reminder that the Eastern "longer" canon was never a single fixed list. (The same canon's New-Testament reckoning omits Revelation and adds 1–2 Clement.)

The Council of Rome (382 AD): Yes — but on a sixth-century document

The first Latin council-list to run out the full deuterocanon is ascribed to the Roman synod that met under Pope Damasus I in 382. Its canon-catalogue — the so-called "Damasine list" — gives the longer Septuagint Old Testament, filing Tobit, Judith, and 1–2 Maccabees among "the Histories" and Wisdom and Ecclesiasticus (Sirach) among the books of Solomon: the whole disputed deuterocanon received as Scripture, the same canon the African councils would enact a decade later.79 But the verdict comes with a sharp caveat about its act: the list survives in no fourth-century text, only as a chapter of the Decretum Gelasianum, a compilation critical scholarship dates to the sixth century (this site c. 500). A real synod did meet at Rome in 382, and the list reflects the canon the late-fourth-century West received; but the claim that the canon was decreed there rests on a single later document and an eighteenth-century attribution, and Jerome — present at Rome, foremost on the canon — nowhere mentions such a decree. The documentary basis is weighed adversarially on its own argument page — assessed weak for the "fixed at Rome in 382" framing, while the modest reading (that Damasus's Rome simply received the deuterocanon, as the rest of the West did) is plausible.80

Epiphanius of Salamis (c. 385 AD): No — but Wisdom & Sirach "useful," Baruch kept

The great heresy-hunter of Salamis returns the Eastern verdict once more. In both the Panarion and On Weights and Measures, Epiphanius gives the Hebrew-sized canon — twenty-seven "recognized canonical books, but twenty-two when counted according to the letters of the alphabet of the Hebrews" — running them out in four "pentateuchs" plus Ezra and Esther, with Tobit, Judith, and the Maccabees all absent.81 But his "No" is graded, like Athanasius's before it: he names the two great Wisdom books — the Wisdom of Solomon and the Wisdom of Sirach — and rules them "of disputed canonicity," yet "helpful and useful," books that "were not kept in the chest, that is, in the ark of the covenant"; and his single Jeremiah carries Baruch and the Epistle of Jeremiah folded in, exactly as Origen, Cyril, and Athanasius had kept them — though he frankly grants "the epistles are not in use among the Hebrews."82

Ambrose of Milan (c. 387 AD): Yes — the disputed books cited as "Scripture" and "it is written," the bishop who shaped Augustine

The great bishop of Milan supplies the weightiest Western "using" witness of the century — the man who catechized and baptized Augustine (at Easter 387), whose reception-canon would carry the day in the West. Ambrose (c. 339–397, bishop of Milan from 374) draws up no canon-list, but across his works he quotes the disputed books with the plain formulae he reserves for the protocanon. He grounds his grief for his brother in the deuterocanonical Wisdom of Solomon — "as Scripture says concerning him: 'He was taken away, lest that wickedness should alter his understanding'" (Wisdom 4:11); reads the deuterocanonical Baruch as the very speech of "the Lord," who "saying: 'Put off, O Jerusalem, the garment of your mourning…'" bids penitent Jerusalem rise (Baruch 5:1); and tells the story of Susanna — the Greek addition to Daniel — under "it is written: 'The Lord heard her voice, when she was being led to be put to death'" (Daniel 13:44).83 He presses the books doctrinally too: against Arius he turns the deuterocanonical Sirach into a divine rebuke — "it was said even to Arius and to all men: 'Seek not that which is too deep for you'" (Sirach 3:22).84 And he adduces deuterocanonical figures as moral examples — Tobit sending "Raphael the archangel" (Tobit 9:3) beside the angel sent to Balaam, "Judith adorned herself that she might please an adulterer" for religion's sake (beside Rahab), and the Maccabean mother who "looked on the corpses of her children as so many trophies" (2 Maccabees 7:20) beside the mothers of Bethlehem.85 Like Cyprian and Tertullian before him he renders no canon-verdict; he simply uses the disputed books — the strongest Latin "using" witness between Cyprian and the African councils, and the immediate teacher of the Augustine whose reception-criterion would carry the longer canon into the Western church. The datapoint is weighed in the Arguments below.

Gregory of Nazianzus (c. 389 AD): No — the narrowest list, even Esther dropped

The great Cappadocian theologian draws the Hebrew line hardest of all. His verse catalogue Concerning the Genuine Books of Divinely Inspired Scripture warns "let not extraneous books seduce your mind, for many malignant writings have been disseminated," and counts "twenty-two of the ancient books, corresponding to the number of the Hebrew letters."86 Not only are Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, and the Maccabees absent — Gregory's list is narrower even than his neighbours', omitting Esther as well, the same omission Athanasius had hesitated over. The poem became a standard Eastern measure of the canon, carried into the medieval Greek canon-law collections. Yet even this strictest of the Fathers quotes the disputed books in his orations — Judith as "the Scripture," the Wisdom of Solomon "as it is written," and Baruch pressed as a proof-text for the Incarnation against the Arians — the sharpest single case of ranking by the Hebrew list while reading from the wider Greek Bible.86

Jerome (c. 390 AD): No — the hardest line, even Baruch dropped

The man who gave the Latin West its Bible draws the sharpest "No" on the timeline. In the Prologus Galeatus — the "helmeted" preface to his translation from the Hebrew — Jerome fixes the Old Testament at the Hebrew twenty-two and rules that "whatever is outside of these is set aside among the apocrypha. Therefore, Wisdom… and the book of Jesus son of Sirach, and Judith and Tobias, and The Shepherd are not in the canon."87 He is harder even than the Eastern Fathers before him: where Cyril, Athanasius, and Epiphanius all folded Baruch into the canonical Jeremiah, Jerome omits it as "neither read nor found among the Hebrews," and it is he who supplies the very word — apocrypha — the later Protestant Bibles will use.87 Yet his "No" is not flat: at his bishops' command he translated Tobit and Judith anyway — Tobit "contrary to their canon," he grants, choosing "to be subject to the commands of bishops" over "the opinion of the Pharisees" — and he ranked Wisdom and Sirach, with Judith, Tobit, and the Maccabees, as books the Church may "read… for the strengthening of the people, but not for confirming the authority of ecclesiastical dogmas." And away from the prefaces his own pen occasionally goes further still — quoting Sirach with "Holy scripture says," the Wisdom of Solomon under "it is written," and ranging Baruch among "the trumpets of the prophets" — loose usage that complicates the proponents' clean appeal to him without unsettling his Hebrew-canon verdict.88 That formula — read for edification, not to prove doctrine — is the one the Thirty-Nine Articles and the Westminster Confession will borrow, citing Jerome by name. (His Judith preface also carries the report that the Council of Nicaea "counted" Judith among the Scriptures — weighed, and found wanting as a decree, on its own argument page.89)

The African councils — Hippo & Carthage (393–419 AD): Yes — the deuterocanon in conciliar form

For the first time the boundary is drawn by a council. The North-African synods gathered around Augustine and Aurelius of Carthage — Hippo in 393 (canon 36, the earliest canon-list any church council promulgated), re-enacted at Carthage in 397 and reaffirmed in 419 — each enact the rule "that besides the Canonical Scriptures nothing be read in church under the name of divine Scripture," and then list the books. Their Old Testament is the longer Septuagint canon: Tobit, Judith, and the two books of Maccabees named outright among the Scriptures, and the deuterocanonical Wisdom of Solomon and Sirach carried in "the five books of Solomon."90 Where Athanasius a generation earlier had ranked exactly these books a tier below — "read" but "not in the canon" — the African lists level them up, placing them among the canonical books. The theological hand behind the lists is Augustine, whose On Christian Doctrine (2.8.13, c. 397) matches the conciliar canon book for book and states its criterion — to "prefer those that are received by all the catholic churches to those which some do not receive."91 These are regional synods ratifying a reception already in hand, not ecumenical decrees that created it — the Western counterpart to Athanasius's Eastern list, and the seed of the later Old-Testament dispute that runs to Florence, Trent, and the Reformation. (The same councils' New-Testament list is treated on the companion Biblical Canon page.)

John Chrysostom (c. 395 AD): Yes — an Antiochene preacher citing the disputed books as Scripture

The greatest preacher of Antioch — the school usually invoked for the narrower, Hebrew-sized canon — reaches for the deuterocanon in the pulpit exactly as he reaches for Moses and Paul. Chrysostom adduces Sirach (4:1) "from the Divine Scriptures," cites Wisdom (11:23) under "it is written," ranks the Greek Susanna (Daniel 13:52) beside two Psalms as a scriptural instance, and sets Tobit (12:9) beside Paul and the Psalter on almsgiving.92939495 The use rises to doctrine: against the Marcionite denial of a real Incarnation he presses Baruch (3:35–37) — "in the book of Jeremiah" — as a prophecy that "this is our God… He appeared upon the earth, and held converse with men."96 He leaves no canon-list, so he casts no formal verdict — but his practice puts the foremost Antiochene on the "using" side of the divide, the same list-versus-usage gap that runs through Basil and the two Gregories, and cuts against any tidy map that aligns the longer canon with Alexandria and the shorter with Antioch.

Augustine of Hippo (c. 397): Yes — the full canon, on the breadth of its reception

The theological hand behind those councils sets out the same verdict under his own name. In On Christian Doctrine Augustine runs out a forty-four-book Old Testament that files Tobias, Judith, the two books of Maccabees, and the two of Ezra among the histories, and reckons Wisdom and Ecclesiasticus among the prophetical books — the longer Septuagint canon, with no second tier.97 What carries the disputed books in is not a Hebrew enumeration but a rule of reception: "to prefer those that are received by all the catholic churches to those which some do not receive," weighing a book by the breadth and antiquity of its use.98 That is the exact opposite test from Jerome's Hebraica veritas, and the disagreement between these two contemporaries is the fault line that runs to Trent and the Reformation. Late in life, in The City of God, Augustine meets the objection the Reformers would later press — that the Maccabees are not in the Hebrew Scriptures — and concedes it, only to turn it: those books "are held as canonical, not by the Jews, but by the Church, on account of the extreme and wonderful sufferings of certain martyrs."99 His canon rests not on the Jewish reckoning but on the church's reception — the Western, Septuagint-based canon that Trent would later fix as dogma.

Sulpicius Severus (c. 403): Yes — Judith narrated within his epitome of "the sacred Scriptures"

A Gallic aristocrat-turned-ascetic, friend and biographer of Martin of Tours, supplies a quieter Western datapoint: not a canon-list and not a proof-text, but a history. In his Sacred History (Chronica, c. 403) Sulpicius Severus sets out to "give a condensed account of those things which are set forth in the sacred Scriptures from the beginning of the world… down to" his own day — an epitome of the biblical narrative whose contents are the canon he is summarizing.100 Into that survey, alongside Esther and the post-exilic books, he folds Judith: "To this series of events it will be right that I should append an account of the doings of Judith; for she is related to have lived after the captivity" — the deuterocanonical narrative narrated as a stretch of the same sacred history.101 The witness is graded, and honestly so: Severus also flags the book's historical difficulty — "the sacred history has not revealed who was king of the Persians in her day" — and works to reconcile its "Nebuchadnezzar" with the Persian chronology, so he handles Judith as genuine but chronologically puzzling sacred history rather than reciting it under an "it is written." Like Clement of Rome with "the blessed Judith" and Aphrahat with the Maccabean martyrs, he renders no canon-list; he simply uses the disputed book — weaving it, without seam, into his epitome of "the sacred Scriptures" — the Western, turn-of-the-fifth-century datapoint weighed in the Arguments below.

Rufinus of Aquileia (c. 404): No — but "Ecclesiastical," read though not doctrinal

Rufinus gives the cleanest, most systematic version of the middle position. In his Commentary on the Apostles' Creed he sorts the books into three named ranks. The Canonical Old Testament he lists is the Hebrew-sized canon — Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, and the Maccabees all absent, and Jeremiah named without Baruch.102 Below it he sets a second tier, the books "our fathers call not 'Canonical' but 'Ecclesiastical'": the Wisdom of Solomon, the Wisdom of the Son of Sirach (Ecclesiasticus), Tobit, Judith, and the Maccabees — "all of which they would have read in the Churches, but not appealed to for the confirmation of doctrine"; and below that the Apocrypha, which "they would not have read in the Churches" at all.103 His operative formula — read in church, but not to prove doctrine — is almost word-for-word Jerome's, and the rank Athanasius had called "appointed by the Fathers to be read." On the canon these two famous antagonists agree: the real fault line of the age is not Rufinus-versus-Jerome but both of them against Augustine and the African councils, who level the disputed books up into the canon. Rufinus's three-fold scheme — Canonical, Ecclesiastical, Apocrypha — supplies the vocabulary the Thirty-Nine Articles and the Westminster Confession will later borrow. Yet Rufinus the exegete strains the line Rufinus the cataloguer draws: expounding the blessing of Gad in his Commentary on the Blessings of the Patriarchs, he introduces Sirach 34 with "ita enim Scriptura dicit" ("for thus the Scripture says") and leans on it to settle a point of moral doctrine — the very appeal his "Ecclesiastical" rank forbids.103

Innocent I (c. 405): Yes — the deuterocanon received from the see of Rome

A sitting bishop of Rome restates the Western verdict. Answering a query from Exuperius, bishop of Toulouse, on which books "really are received in the canon," Innocent I appends a canon-list whose Old Testament is the longer Septuagint form: Tobit, Judith, and the two books of Maccabees named outright among "the historical books," and the deuterocanonical Wisdom and Ecclesiasticus (Sirach) carried in "the five books of Solomon."104 He files these books among the canon, with none of the "read but not canonical" qualification that Athanasius, Jerome, and Rufinus had attached to the very same titles — the Augustinian, reception-based posture rather than the Hebrew reckoning.105 Coming a decade after the African councils of Hippo and Carthage had drawn the same line, it is the clearest papal-level reception of the deuterocanon before Florence and Trent — a witness to a canon already in hand, not a decree that created it. (The same letter's New-Testament list, and its condemnation of the forged apostolic gospels, are treated on the companion Biblical Canon page.)

The Catalogue of Codex Claromontanus (c. 400 AD): Yes

A stichometric book-list, copied into the sixth-century Greek–Latin Codex Claromontanus but reflecting an older (c. 300–400) table, gives the longer canon with line-counts: Tobit, Judith, the Wisdom of Solomon, the Wisdom of Jesus (Sirach), and the books of Maccabees all stand among the Old Testament books.106 The list marks several disputed titles with a small dash — but those obeli fall on contested New-Testament books, not on the Old-Testament deuterocanon, which it carries without qualification: an early-Western "Yes" preserved, like the Damasine list, in a later codex.

John Cassian (c. 426 AD): Yes — Wisdom cited as "Scripture itself testifies," Baruch as "the prophet"

The father of Western monasticism, carrying the desert tradition of Egypt into Gaul, cites the disputed books as Scripture and prophecy in the very work that shaped Latin monastic life. John Cassian (c. 360 – c. 435), whose Conferences (Collationes, c. 426–429) report the teaching of the Egyptian abbots to the monks of Marseilles, introduces the deuterocanonical Wisdom of Solomon with the bare formula he reserves for Scripture: "as Scripture itself testifies: 'For God made not death, neither rejoices in the destruction of the living'" (Wisdom 1:13).107 He quotes the deuterocanonical Baruch as the word of "the prophet" — "nor hear the words of the prophet: Why are you grown old in a strange country?" (Baruch 3:11) — the same book Athenagoras and Irenaeus had cited under the prophets' name.108 And expounding the remedies for sin he sets Sirach beside the apostles — "As water extinguishes fire, so does almsgiving extinguish sin" (Sirach 3:33) — in one chain with 1 Peter 4:8, Matthew 6:14, and James 5:20.109 He draws up no canon-list, so renders no canon-verdict; he simply uses the disputed books as Scripture — a Western, monastic datapoint of the 420s, contemporary with the close of the African councils, on the "using" side weighed in the Arguments below.

Vincent of Lérins (c. 434 AD): Yes — Sirach quoted among "the divine Oracles," bracketed with Proverbs and Ecclesiastes

At the very hinge of the West's classic rule for telling catholic truth from heresy, a disputed book is named among "the divine Oracles." In his Commonitory (AD 434) Vincent of Lérins (c. 400 – c. 445), the Gallic monk whose "what has been believed everywhere, always, and by all" became the touchstone of orthodoxy, clinches his thesis that the faith may never be added to by stringing three wisdom-sayings together under one formula — "the divine Oracles cry aloud, 'Remove not the landmarks, which thy fathers have set,' and 'Go not to law with a Judge,' and 'Whoso breaketh through a fence a serpent shall bite him'" — and the middle of the three is the deuterocanonical Sirach (8:14), set without distinction between Proverbs 22:28 and Ecclesiastes 10:8.110 He draws up no canon-list, so renders no canon-verdict; he simply uses the disputed book as Scripture, on a par with the two undisputed wisdom books beside it — a Western, monastic datapoint of the 430s, a near-contemporary of John Cassian on the same Gallic coast, on the "using" side weighed in the Arguments below.

The Synopsis of Sacred Scripture (c. 550 AD): No — but the deuterocanon "read," in Athanasius's tier

A handbook transmitted under Athanasius's name but later in date, the Synopsis Scripturae Sacrae repeats his graded verdict almost exactly. Its canonical Old Testament is the Hebrew-sized list, the disputed books absent; but it then sets out a second rank — books "not… canonical, but only read" to the catechumens — and there it files the deuterocanon: the Wisdom of Solomon, the Wisdom of Sirach, Esther, Judith, and Tobit.111 It is Athanasius's "appointed by the Fathers to be read" preserved into the sixth century — the disputed books neither canonized nor cast out, but kept as a middle, "read" rank.

Gregory the Great (c. 590 AD): Yes, graded — quotes Wisdom, Tobit & Sirach as Scripture, yet calls 1 Maccabees "not canonical"

The single sharpest "graded standing" datapoint on the timeline: a reigning bishop of Rome who cites the deuterocanon as Scripture with one hand and, of one book, withholds the word canonical with the other. In his Register III.53 Gregory the Great (c. 540–604) adduces the Wisdom of Solomon (1:11) as what "the Truth says through His Scripture" — "The mouth that lies slays the soul";112 and in the Book of Pastoral Rule (III.21) he ranges Tobit (4:17) and Sirach (12:4) together under "what is written," beside Luke and Matthew.113 Yet in his great Morals on the Book of Job (XIX, §34), retelling Eleazar's death from 1 Maccabees 6, he pauses to qualify the source: "we are not acting irregularly, if from the books, though not Canonical, yet brought out for the edifying of the Church, we bring forward testimony."114 The two postures are the same graded standing the Hebrew-minded Fathers held — read, used, and cited, but not reckoned canonical on a par with the Hebrew table — here in the mouth of the pope whose Morals became the West's standard commentary, and a leading single witness in the Arguments below for an early church that used these books while tiering one of them.

Isidore of Seville (c. 625 AD): No — Jerome's count, with a "fourth class" read

The great encyclopedist of the Latin West restates Jerome's Hebrew canon. In the Etymologiae (VI.9) Isidore divides the Old Testament into the three Hebrew classes — Law, Prophets, Sacred Writings — totalling twenty-two books (some, he notes, add Ruth and Lamentations to make twenty-four), with the deuterocanon outside the count.115 But he then names a further class the Church "honors and proclaims" though it stands outside the Hebrew canon — Wisdom, Ecclesiasticus (Sirach), Tobit, Judith, and the Maccabees — the same graded posture, canonical-by-the-Hebrews / read-by-the-Church, that Rufinus and Jerome had handed him, carried into the medieval Latin schoolbooks.

Codex Amiatinus (c. 700 AD): Yes — the whole canon bound in one book

The oldest surviving complete Latin Vulgate in a single volume — a pandect made c. 700 at the Wearmouth–Jarrow monastery in Northumbria — embodies the longer canon physically: Codex Amiatinus carries Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, and the two books of Maccabees among its Old Testament books.116 Yet the same manuscript prints the disagreement in its own front matter: its opening leaves diagram three rival canon-divisions — Jerome's Hebrew-sized twenty-two, the Septuagint order, and Augustine's longer list — so the very codex that hands the West the full deuterocanon also preserves, on its first pages, the Hebrew-sized schemes that disputed it.

John of Damascus (c. 730 AD): No — the Hebrew canon, yet cites Wisdom as "the divine Scripture"

The last of the Greek Fathers gives the Eastern Hebrew canon its closing classic statement. In An Exposition of the Orthodox Faith (IV.17) John of Damascus arranges the twenty-two books in four "pentateuchs" plus two — the deuterocanon outside the count — and names the Wisdom of Solomon and the Wisdom of Sirach only to rule them "virtuous and noble, but… not counted nor… placed in the ark."117 It is the verdict of Epiphanius three centuries on, repeated almost word for word — the Eastern "No" still graded, still keeping the disputed books as honoured but uncanonical, two centuries before the Masoretic Text fixed the Hebrew table for good. Yet the witness is double, and the two halves stand only two chapters apart in the one work: arguing in IV.15 that the holy dead live and may rightly be honoured, the same John quotes the very book his list excludes — "the divine Scripture likewise says that the souls of the just are in God's hand and death cannot lay hold of them," the Wisdom of Solomon 3:1 adduced under the bare formula "the divine Scripture."118 The Hebrew-sized canon-list and the working use of the deuterocanon as Scripture coexist in one volume — the Origen/Eusebius doubleness carried into the eighth century, and the datapoint weighed in the Arguments below.

The Stichometry of Nicephorus (c. 810 AD): No — the deuterocanon ranked "disputed"

A line-count catalogue attached to the Chronography of Nicephorus, Patriarch of Constantinople, gives the last great Eastern list before the medieval Hebrew codices. Its canonical Old Testament is the Hebrew-sized protocanon (Baruch folded into Jeremiah); the deuterocanon is not omitted in silence but sorted into a named lower rank of antilegomena — "those spoken against" — where the books of Maccabees, the Wisdom of Solomon, Sirach, Esther, Judith, Susanna, and Tobit are each listed with their line-counts.119 It is the Synopsis's graded "No" made into a formal three-tier inventory — canonical, disputed, apocryphal — the Eastern position carried to the threshold of the Byzantine Middle Ages.

The Masoretic Text (c. 1000 AD, Judaism): No

The medieval Jewish standard text returns the Hebrew "No" in its definitive form. The Masoretic Text — the work of the Masoretes, scribal scholars of the seventh through tenth centuries who fixed the Hebrew Bible letter by letter, surviving in the tenth-century Aleppo Codex and the 1008/1009 Leningrad Codex — is "the authoritative Hebrew and Aramaic text of the 24 books of the Hebrew Bible (Tanakh) in Rabbinic Judaism," carrying the protocanonical books alone: Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch, and the Maccabees have no place in it.120 It did not decide the canon — it preserved the boundary the rabbinic stream had long since drawn (the "Council of Jamnia" marker) — but it gave that Hebrew-sized Scripture its permanent written form, and that matters for the Christian question because it is the text "most Protestant translations of the Old Testament" render: when the Reformers returned to the Hebrew against the Vulgate's longer canon, the Hebrew they returned to was the Masoretic Text, and its twenty-four-book table became the thirty-nine-book Protestant Old Testament.120 Its scribes' fidelity, long famed, was largely vindicated by the manuscript finds — "most of the Qumran fragments… closer to the Masoretic Text than to any other text group," and the En-Gedi Leviticus scroll (c. 3rd–4th century AD) found "completely identical with the consonantal framework of the Masoretic Text" — though faithful transmission of the Hebrew settles only what that tradition contained, not whether the church's canon should be measured by the Hebrew table or by the Greek Bible it read.121 (The Septuagint-versus-Hebrew quarrel itself is gathered on the Rabbinic Corruption of Scripture and Origin and Inspiration of the Septuagint pages and not re-argued here.)

The Council of Florence (1442): Yes — the first ecumenical enumeration of the full canon

After the long medieval lull the timeline reaches its first ecumenical verdict. In the bull Cantate Domino, the Bull of Union with the Copts, the Council of Florence professes that "one and the same God is the author of the old and the new Testament" and lists the books it "accepts and venerates" — and there, threaded into one undifferentiated list beside Genesis, Isaiah, and the Gospels, stand Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Ecclesiasticus (Sirach), Baruch, and the two books of Maccabees.122 It is the Augustinian, reception-based canon of the African councils and Innocent I — the disputed books levelled into the canon with none of the "read but not canonical" tier of Athanasius, Jerome, and Rufinus — now raised for the first time to the level of a general council. The canon-list sits inside a reunion profession, not a stand-alone canon-decree, and the union itself failed; but the verb is the receptive one the Council of Trent will repeat a century later (suscipit ac veneratur, "accepts and venerates"), so the Catholic answer to the charge that the deuterocanon was "added at Trent" rests here: Florence professed in 1442 the canon Trent would fix as dogma in 1546.123

Martin Luther (1534, Protestant): No — "not equal to Holy Scripture, yet useful and good to read"

The Reformation reopens the question Florence thought closed, and answers it with Jerome's "No" set in print. In his complete German Bible of 1534 Luther lifted Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch, and 1–2 Maccabees out of the Old Testament and gathered them — all but 1–2 Esdras, which he dropped — into a separate section between the Testaments under the heading "Apocrypha — that is, books which are not held equal to the Holy Scriptures, and yet are profitable and good to read."124 The layout is the verdict, and it is the graded "No" of the Hebrew-minded Fathers made visible on the page: in his prefaces he commends Judith as "a fine, good, holy, useful book, well worth reading by us Christians" and reads Tobit as edifying fiction of married life, while denying both the canonical rank — and in the Table Talk he files them by genre, Judith "a tragedy, setting forth what the end of tyrants is… like that of Homer about Troy," Tobit "a comedy concerning women, an example for house-government."125 Luther keeps the books in the Bible but out of the canon — the same posture as Rufinus and Athanasius, read for edification, not for proving doctrine — and his heading furnishes the very words the Thirty-Nine Articles and the Westminster Confession will take up. (His return to the Hebrew canon was not antiquarian: the demoted books carried the proof-texts — 2 Maccabees 12 for the dead, Tobit 12 for almsgiving — weighed in the Arguments below.)126

The Council of Trent (1546, Roman Catholic): Yes — the deuterocanon defined as canonical, under anathema

The Counter-Reformation answers Luther by closing the question as dogma. In its fourth session, the first to take up doctrine, the Council of Trent declares that it "receives and venerates with an equal affection of piety, and reverence, all the books both of the Old and of the New Testament," and prints the list — Tobias, Judith, Wisdom, Ecclesiasticus (Sirach), Baruch, and the two books of Maccabees threaded among the rest with no second tier — the same receptive verb (suscipit ac veneratur) the Council of Florence had used a century before.127 What sets Trent apart from every earlier "Yes" is the force: it anathematizes anyone who "receive not, as sacred and canonical, the said books entire with all their parts, as… contained in the old Latin vulgate edition" — the first time the reception running from the Septuagint through Augustine to Florence is made a binding test of communion, and "entire with all their parts" deliberately covers the Greek additions to Daniel and Esther.128 The Catholic answer to the charge that the deuterocanon was "added at Trent" rests on this distinction: Trent did not add the books but defined a canon long received. Yet even as it raised the seven disputed books to dogma, its list quietly dropped three texts the medieval Vulgate had carried — 3 and 4 Esdras and the Prayer of Manasses — which the Clementine Vulgate of 1592 then relegated to a non-canonical appendix.129

The Thirty-Nine Articles (1571, Church of England): No — read "for example of life," not to establish doctrine

The English Reformation answers Trent not with Luther's heading but with Jerome's rule, cited by name. Article VI of the Articles of Religion defines canonical Scripture as "those canonical Books of the Old and New Testament, of whose authority was never any doubt in the Church," lists the Hebrew-sized Old Testament book by book, and then files the deuterocanon in a separate rank: "the other Books (as Hierome saith) the Church doth read for example of life and instruction of manners; but yet doth it not apply them to establish any doctrine."130 The list of those "other Books" is wider than Trent's: it brackets Tobias, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch, and 1–2 Maccabees together with the Greek additions to Esther and Daniel and the three texts (3–4 Esdras and the Prayer of Manasses) that Trent itself had dropped — all alike read for edification, none admissible to prove doctrine.131 It is the soft, graded "No" of Jerome, Rufinus, and Luther — keep the books, refuse them as doctrine — the moderate Anglican line that the Westminster Confession would shortly harden into flat exclusion. (The cord it cuts is doctrinal: the disputed books carried the proof-texts — 2 Maccabees 12 for the dead, Tobit 12 for almsgiving — weighed in the Arguments below.)

The Westminster Confession of Faith (1647, Reformed): No — the hardest line: "of no authority… than other human writings"

The Reformed answer hardens the Anglican "No" into a flat exclusion. Chapter 1 of the Westminster Confession names the canon by inspiration — "all the books of the Old and New Testaments… All which are given by inspiration of God, to be the rule of faith and life" — and lists the Hebrew-sized thirty-nine Old Testament books (Genesis through Malachi) with the twenty-seven of the New.132 The next section then rules on the disputed books with a force no earlier Protestant confession had used: "The books commonly called Apocrypha, not being of divine inspiration, are no part of the canon of the Scripture; and therefore are of no authority in the Church of God, nor to be any otherwise approved, or made use of, than other human writings."132 Where Jerome, Rufinus, Luther, and the Thirty-Nine Articles had all kept the deuterocanon as edifying reading — read in church "for example of life," profitable "but not for confirming… doctrine" — Westminster removes that middle rank entirely, leveling the Apocrypha down to ordinary "human writings," the mirror image of Trent's leveling them up into the canon under anathema.133 It is the sharpest Protestant verdict on the timeline, and the one that shaped later Apocrypha-free Protestant Bibles. (The cord it cuts is again doctrinal: a book that may not be "made use of" otherwise than other human writings cannot found the contested doctrines its proof-texts were adduced for — 2 Maccabees 12 for the dead, Tobit 12 for almsgiving — weighed in the Arguments below.)

The Synod of Jerusalem (1672, Eastern Orthodox): Yes — the Eastern counterpart to Trent

The timeline closes in the East. A generation after Westminster, the Synod of Jerusalem — convened at Bethlehem by Patriarch Dositheus II to repudiate a Calvinizing confession that had circulated under the name of Cyril Lucaris, Patriarch of Constantinople — gave the Eastern Orthodox church its most authoritative modern ruling on the canon. The Confession of Dositheus names the books Lucaris had set aside as "Apocrypha" — the Wisdom of Solomon, Judith, Tobit, Bel and the Dragon, Susanna, the Maccabees, and Sirach — and declares them "genuine parts of Scripture," on a level "with the other genuine Books of Divine Scripture."134 Its ground is not the Hebrew table but the delivering tradition of the church — Augustine's reception-criterion pressed to a hard edge: the same church "which hath delivered to us as genuine the Sacred Gospels… hath undoubtedly delivered these also as parts of Scripture, and the denial of these is the rejection of those." The Confession candidly concedes the unevenness this timeline documents — "not always have all of these been considered on the same level as the rest" — and overrules it by the breadth of synodal and patristic use.135 It is the exact mirror of Westminster twenty-five years before: the two latest witnesses, both reacting to the same Reformation rupture, level the same books in opposite directions — down to "human writings" in the Reformed West, up to canonical Scripture in the Orthodox East — and the Eastern "Yes" is the counterpart to Trent's, reached by the same reception-over-Hebrew route. (Later Orthodox teaching softens the affirmation, distinguishing these anaginoskomena — "things that are read" — as a profitable class below the protocanonical books; but the 1672 decree remains the high-water mark of the Eastern reception, and it keeps the Maccabees' prayer for the dead and Tobit's almsgiving inside the Eastern canon — weighed in the Arguments below.)

Arguments

Sub-claims that cut across the timeline rather than sitting at one date — each weighed adversarially on its own page, with an assessment of the interpretation it serves. A pair of related arguments press the witness of the earliest church that the disputed books were no Scripture, and a converse argument tests whether the apostles' own demonstrable Greek Bible — the Septuagint — makes the deuterocanon their Scripture; another pair turn on the Gospel saying "from the blood of Abel to the blood of Zechariah" (Luke 11:51 // Matt 23:35) — whether it delimits the canon, and which Zechariah it names; a quartet weighs the New Testament's "strange quotations" — places (Ephesians 5:14, 1 Corinthians 2:9, John 7:38, Luke 11:49) where it cites, with a Scripture-formula, words found in no Old-Testament book; a further pair try to neutralize Augustine's "Yes" by reconstructing the man — that he was "no biblical expert" and read no Hebrew unlike Jerome, and that he kept the books only because he wrongly thought they stood in the Hebrew canon; another presses the Jewish people's custody of the Old Testament (Romans 3:2) toward the Hebrew canon, its umbrella asks whether the Jews in fact held a closed, Hebrew-only canon that rejected the deuterocanon, and a careful form relocates that claim to the Pharisaic stream Jesus and Paul belonged to; a pair press the charge that Tobit teaches error — that its almsgiving "purges away all sin" and that its fish-organ remedy "commands magic" — and a third weighs 2 Maccabees, the prayer-for-the-dead proof-text the Reformation made a doctrinal ground of its "No"; another asks whether the books disclaim their own inspiration; one weighs the meta-question of where the burden of proof lies, given the deuterocanon's long place in the Vulgate; and an umbrella meta-argument asks whether the canon dispute itself, by leaving the table of contents unsettled, undermines sola scriptura. All are weighed here.

Related arguments — the primitive church's witness

Three Protestant removal arguments from antiquity: that the earliest church did not reckon the deuterocanon Scripture, so its later inclusion is an accretion. Two press the silence of the apostles — that the New Testament never quotes the books as Scripture, and that Jesus and the apostles are never recorded disputing the canon's extent with the Jews; the third presses the Hebrew-sized canon-lists of the Fathers (Melito to Jerome). They share one hinge — whether the earliest stratum of the church excluded these books, or whether a learned minority tiered books the wider church read and used. Best read together.

Does the New Testament's never quoting the deuterocanon as Scripture prove it is not canonical? (assessment: weak)

Does the New Testament's never quoting the deuterocanon as Scripture prove it is not canonical? — the stock Protestant argument from silence (scholarly form: Luther in instinct, Roger Beckwith in print): the New Testament cites most of the protocanon as Scripture with formulae like "it is written," but never a deuterocanonical book that way, so the apostles did not reckon these books inspired. The observation is sound — the citation asymmetry is real — but the inference to exclusion does not hold on its best case. It proves too much: the New Testament likewise never quotes Esther, Ecclesiastes, the Song of Songs, Ezra–Nehemiah, Obadiah, or Nahum, which no one drops. It is false to the data: the New Testament alludes to the deuterocanon repeatedly — Hebrews 11:35 ("tortured, not accepting deliverance … a better resurrection") to the martyrs of 2 Maccabees 7, Romans 1 to Wisdom 13–14, Romans 9:21 to Wisdom 15:7, Matthew 27:43 to Wisdom 2, James 1:19 to Sirach 5:11 — allusions the Nestle–Aland critical Greek New Testament itself registers. And "quotation as Scripture" is a fuzzy test the New Testament applies inconsistently, quoting 1 Enoch as prophecy (Jude 14–15) and pagan poets (Acts 17:28; Titus 1:12; 1 Cor 15:33) without thereby canonizing them. What survives is the narrower, true point that the apostles cite the protocanon as Scripture and the deuterocanon only by allusion — evidence for the graded standing the Hebrew-minded Fathers assigned these books, not for the flat exclusion the syllogism wants.136

Does the absence of a recorded canon dispute between Jesus and the Jews prove a settled, shared Hebrew canon? (assessment: weak)

Does the absence of a recorded canon dispute between Jesus and the Jews prove a settled, shared Hebrew canon? — the companion argument from silence (James White, Mike Winger): the Gospels record no exchange in which Jesus and his Jewish opponents fight over which books are Scripture — "would we not have Jesus correcting them?" if they had wrongly cast these books out? — so Jesus and the Jews must have shared one settled, Hebrew-sized canon. The observation is true but the inference does not hold. It is an argument from silence that fits an unsettled canon as well as a settled one, and it begs the question by assuming the very fixed Jewish canon the leading scholarship (Sundberg, Lim, McDonald) says did not yet exist in the early first century. Its premise that the debates were "about this issue" is false: they turned on the sabbath, divorce, the resurrection, tradition, and Jesus' own authority — never on the contested books, which no controversy touched. And the Gospels are not silent about a scriptural-extent disagreement: facing the Sadducees' denial of the resurrection, Jesus reasons from the burning bush in "the book of Moses" (Matt 22:31–32 // Mark 12:26) rather than the plainer texts of the Prophets — meeting a narrower scriptural ground on its own terms, the opposite of the canon-correcting rebuke the argument predicts. What survives is only the modest, true point that Jesus and his interlocutors shared a large common Scripture and argued within it — evidence neutral between the canons, not a verdict for the shorter one.137

Did the early church reject the deuterocanon as Scripture, making its later inclusion an accretion? (assessment: contested)

Did the early church reject the deuterocanon as Scripture, making its later inclusion an accretion? — the Protestant removal argument from the patristic canon-lists (Luther in instinct, Roger Beckwith in print): the earliest Christian lists — Melito, Origen, Cyril, Athanasius, Epiphanius, Jerome, Rufinus — are Hebrew-sized, omitting or tiering the disputed books, so the primitive church did not hold them Scripture. The early witness is genuinely divided, and the verdict turns on the form of the claim. The narrow, graded thesis is strong: a learned, Hebrew-canon-conscious stream did rank these books below a canon measured by the Hebrew table — the backbone of the Reformation's case. But the flat, universal claim does not hold: the church's working Bible was the Septuagint, which carried the books; the great codices interleave them with the protocanon; a parallel, equally early Western stream (the African councils, Augustine, Innocent I) received them among the Scriptures; and even the "excluders" read and cited them, tiering rather than rejecting (Origen's "copies in use in our Churches," Rufinus's "Ecclesiastical" books). So the deuterocanon is not a late addition but a long-disputed inheritance — tiered by a minority, used by the majority, as the Synod of Jerusalem itself conceded ("not always have all of these been considered on the same level as the rest"). No clear winner.138

Related arguments — Abel to Zechariah

Three arguments on a single Gospel saying — Jesus' "from the blood of Abel … to the blood of Zechariah" (Luke 11:51 // Matt 23:35). The first asks whether the saying delimits the canon (Genesis to Chronicles, excluding the Apocrypha); the other two test the premises on which the first depends — which Zechariah Jesus names (the "bookends" reading needs the Chronicles figure, son of Jehoiada, to be the closing "Z"), and whether the Genesis-to-Chronicles book-order the argument assumes was even established and dominant in Jesus' day. Best read together.

Does "from the blood of Abel to the blood of Zechariah" delimit the Hebrew OT canon, excluding the Apocrypha? (assessment: weak)

Does "from the blood of Abel to the blood of Zechariah" delimit the Hebrew Old Testament canon, excluding the Apocrypha? — the popular Protestant "A-to-Z" or "bookends" argument (scholarly form Roger Beckwith; popularly F. F. Bruce, the Reformation's Hebrew-canon instinct, Doug Wilson): because Chronicles stands last in the Hebrew order and records a Zechariah's murder, Jesus' phrase "from the blood of Abel … to the blood of Zechariah" (Luke 11:51 // Matt 23:35) traces the limits of the canon — Genesis to Chronicles — and so excludes Maccabees and the rest of the Apocrypha. That Jesus names the first and last martyrs of Old-Testament history, summing up the generation's guilt in a beginning-to-end merism, is sound and is how the whole church read the saying. But the canon-demarcation inference does not hold on its best case, failing on three independent counts. Reception: no interpreter on record read the saying as a canon-boundary until Eichhorn in 1780 — the unbroken tradition (Tertullian, Origen, Chrysostom, Jerome, Calvin, Lightfoot) read it of the first-to-last martyr, and Jerome, the great Hebrew-canon champion, never enlisted it for the canon. Book-order: Genesis-to-Chronicles is not securely the order Jesus knew — across the first-millennium canon-lists no Chronicles-last sequence is dominant (it standardizes only after 1207), and its one early Talmudic witness (Bava Batra 14b) sits beside material (Bava Kamma) that counts Sirach among the Writings. Identity: Matthew's "Zechariah son of Berechiah" does not fit the Chronicles figure (son of Jehoiada) without positing a scribal error or a name-conflation — a difficulty even proponents (Wilson, citing Gleason Archer) concede. What survives is the modest, universally-held martyr-merism, carrying at most a faint, order-dependent presumption toward a Hebrew-shaped canon — far short of the claim that the Apocrypha is here excluded; the Hebrew-canon case stands on stronger grounds elsewhere.139

Which Zechariah does Jesus name — is he the Chronicles figure the bookends argument needs? (assessment: contested)

Which Zechariah does Jesus name in "from the blood of Abel to the blood of Zechariah" — and is he the Chronicles figure the "bookends" argument needs? — the load-bearing sub-claim beneath the bookends argument: that the Zechariah Jesus names is Zechariah son of Jehoiada (2 Chron 24:20–22), the priest stoned in the temple court and so the "last martyr in the last book." The identification is the most defensible reading and probably right: his is the only temple-court martyrdom in inspired Scripture, it matches "between the sanctuary and the altar" exactly, and the most exacting interpreters (Jerome, Calvin, Lightfoot, Michaelis) settle there. But it is genuinely contested. Matthew's "son of Berechiah" (Matt 23:35) fits the priest (son of Jehoiada) only by an unattested second name, a textual emendation against every critical edition, an over-loose "descendant," or a name-conflation device whose use in Jesus' day is unproven; and a strong patristic stream (Origen, Basil, Gregory of Nyssa, Cyril of Alexandria) read the figure as the father of John the Baptist instead, with the prophet of Zechariah 1:1 and Josephus' son of Baruch as further candidates. So the sub-claim cannot simply be assumed; and even granted in full, identifying the Chronicles Zechariah shows only that Jesus named a martyr who stands in the last book of one Hebrew order — not that he was tracing the canon's edge. The likeliest identification, established, still does not carry the demarcation inference it was enlisted to support.140

Did the Hebrew canon end with Chronicles in Jesus' day — was a Genesis-to-Chronicles order established and dominant before the first century? (assessment: weak)

Did the Hebrew canon end with Chronicles in Jesus' day — was a Genesis-to-Chronicles order established and dominant before the first century? — the historical premise the bookends argument rests on (Roger Beckwith in print, leaning on the Talmud; Doug Wilson popularly: "the order … Jesus would have been familiar with"): that the Jews of Jesus' day arranged their Bible Genesis-to-Chronicles, Chronicles standing last, so that "Abel … to Zechariah" spans first book to last. Surveyed against the record, the strong form of that premise does not hold. Across the first-millennium canon-lists and codices no Chronicles-last order is dominant; the great majority close on Daniel, Ezra, Esther, or Maccabees (Melito, Origen, Cyril, Athanasius, Epiphanius, Jerome, Augustine, and the great codices among them), and the authoritative Tiberian Hebrew manuscripts — the Aleppo and Leningrad codices — put Chronicles first in the Writings, not last. The Chronicles-last arrangement becomes common only after the Adath Deborim (1207) and standard with print — and the Adath Deborim itself reports that Chronicles-first is the "right and true" order and that "the Babylonians have altered this order," moving Chronicles to the end. The one clearly early Jewish witness to a Chronicles-last order, the baraita at Bava Batra 14b, is genuine and is the steelman's proper foundation — but it stands nearly alone for a millennium and sits in a context (Bava Kamma citing Sirach as Writings; Epiphanius reporting Jews still disputing Sirach and Wisdom) that does not yield the clean Apocrypha-excluding canon the argument needs; and the other early Chronicles-last list, Innocent I's (405), includes the deuterocanon. To carry the premise one must hold that Bava Batra preserves a pre-Christian order Jesus knew, lost to history for a thousand years and recovered only after 1207 — possible, but unevidenced, and against the grain of the data. What survives is the modest point that a Chronicles-last order existed in some rabbinic stream; what fails is the load-bearing premise that it was the familiar shape of the Bible in Jesus' day.141

Related arguments — New-Testament quotations with no Old-Testament source

Four arguments on the New Testament's "strange quotations" — the places where it introduces, with a Scripture-citation formula, words found in no Old-Testament book. The centerpiece is Ephesians 5:14 ("Awake thou that sleepest…," prefaced by dió légei); the siblings are 1 Corinthians 2:9 ("Eye hath not seen…," "as it is written"), John 7:38 ("out of his belly shall flow rivers of living water," "as the scripture hath said"), and Luke 11:49 ("said the wisdom of God…"). All four ask the one question — whether the apostolic category of "Scripture" reached beyond a closed Hebrew canon — and the phenomenon they rest on is real; but each citation has a better explanation than the quotation of a non-canonical writing (a hymn, a paraphrase of Isaiah, a composite canonical allusion, Christ speaking as Wisdom). Best read together.

Does the New Testament quote, as Scripture, words found in no Old Testament book? (Ephesians 5:14) (assessment: contested)

Does the New Testament quote, as Scripture, words found in no Old Testament book? — the companion to the deuterocanon-non-quotation argument, run the other way: where that one asks why the apostles never quote the disputed Old-Testament books, this asks what to make of the places where they cite, with a Scripture-formula, words found in no Old-Testament book at all. The centerpiece is Ephesians 5:14 ("Awake thou that sleepest…," prefaced by dió légei, "wherefore he saith"), with a small cluster of siblings (1 Corinthians 2:9, John 7:38, Luke 11:49). The bare phenomenon is sound and undisputed — Jerome himself, scouring "all the editions of the ancient Scriptures and the texts of the Hebrews themselves," could not find Ephesians 5:14 — but the interpretation it is pressed to serve, that the apostles thereby quoted a non-canonical writing as Scripture, is contested. For the centerpiece the stronger reading is an early Christian baptismal hymn, not an apocryphon or lost prophet; the one verse a Father pins to the Apocalypse of Elijah (1 Cor 2:9, via Origen and Ambrosiaster) is disputed by Jerome, who tied it to Isaiah 64:4, and rests on a source that postdates the New Testament and lacks the words, so the borrowing may run the other way; and Epiphanius's primary text says only that the words stand "in Elijah," not naming the Apocalypse, with Holl proposing to emend even that to "Isaiah." A citation formula marks a quotation, not a canon-verdict — Jude quotes 1 Enoch as prophecy without canonizing it. What survives is the modest, true point that apostolic "Scripture" had soft outer edges, which tells against any cleanly-closed-Hebrew-canon premise — not the wider claim that the apostles canonized an apocryphon. No clear winner.142

"Eye hath not seen…" (1 Corinthians 2:9): does Paul quote, as Scripture, an apocryphon outside the Old Testament canon? (assessment: weak)

"Eye hath not seen…" (1 Corinthians 2:9): does Paul quote, as Scripture, an apocryphon outside the Old Testament canon? — the firmest sibling for the wider-canon reading, because here a named Father actually pins the words to a specific apocryphal book: Origen (and Ambrosiaster after him) traces Paul's "Eye hath not seen, nor ear heard…," introduced "as it is written," to the lost Apocalypse (Secrets) of Elijah. Yet the reading is weak on its best case. Jerome, who read the Hebrew, dismissed the apocryphal ascription as "the ravings of the apocryphal books" and located the words in a free paraphrase of Isaiah 64:4 — a canonical source, on the exact paraphrastic practice he documents across a dozen apostolic citations. The apocryphal anchor is itself divided (Ambrosiaster couples it with Isaiah; Jerome denies it), and the surviving Apocalypse of Elijah is a late Coptic work that postdates Paul and does not contain the phrase, so any borrowing runs the other way. A canonical source is available and probably right; a citation formula does not canonize what it cites. The verse shows the looseness of apostolic quotation, not a wider canon.143

"Out of his belly shall flow rivers of living water" (John 7:38): does Jesus cite, as Scripture, words found in no Old Testament book? (assessment: weak)

"Out of his belly shall flow rivers of living water" (John 7:38): does Jesus cite, as Scripture, words found in no Old Testament book? — the weakest sibling. "As the scripture hath said" introduces words with no verbatim Old-Testament locus, but unlike 1 Corinthians 2:9 no Father or modern scholar proposes a non-canonical writing here at all. The candidates are all canonical "living-water" texts — Jerome to Proverbs, others to Zechariah 14:8 (fitting John's Tabernacles setting), Isaiah, Ezekiel, the wilderness rock — best read as a free, composite, fulfillment-shaped allusion to "the Scripture" as a whole. And Chrysostom dissolves even that by repunctuating, attaching "as the scripture hath said" to believing rather than to a quotation ("where does the Scripture say this? No where"). On every serious reading the reference stays inside the canon or is no quotation; as evidence for an open canon it barely registers.144

"Therefore also said the wisdom of God" (Luke 11:49): does Jesus quote a written source outside the Old Testament canon? (assessment: weak)

"Therefore also said the wisdom of God" (Luke 11:49): does Jesus quote a written source outside the Old Testament canon? — the sibling whose source is least recoverable, and so the one where the puzzle is most genuine: a saying ("I will send them prophets and apostles…") is ascribed to "the wisdom of God" with no Old-Testament locus. But the inference to a quoted non-canonical writing is still weak, because the leading readings need no book. The patristic reading (Ambrose, Bede) takes "the wisdom of God" to be Christ speaking in his own person — confirmed by the parallel at Matthew 23:34, where Jesus says "I send" — and the dominant modern reading takes it as personified Wisdom (Sophia) speaking, redacted onto Jesus' lips by Matthew. Whether a lost wisdom-text or a free sapiential composition lies behind the words is conceded to be uncertain, but both leading options identify a speaker, not a quoted writing. The hard-to-place source leaves a soft-boundary point standing, not the wider-canon claim.145

Related arguments — Augustine's canon witness

Three arguments that try to neutralize Augustine's "Yes" to the longer canon by reconstructing the man behind it — or his real view. One discounts his competence — he read no Hebrew and was "no biblical expert," unlike the Hebraist Jerome, so where they disagree Jerome's "No" should win and the African councils that enacted his "Yes" should be discounted as merely regional; another discounts his knowledge — he kept the disputed books only because he wrongly thought they stood in the Hebrew canon; the third re-reads his view — that he retracted his high opinion of these books and ranked them below the canonical Scriptures, so his "Yes" was really a soft "No." They share one root, and largely fail by it: Augustine's canon ran on the breadth of churchly reception, not the Hebrew table, and on that test he listed the disputed books inside the canon and cited them as Scripture to the end — so neither his Hebrew, nor his grasp of the Jewish list, nor a retraction he never made is what his verdict turned on. Best read together.

Should Augustine's canon witness be discounted because he was "no biblical expert" and could not read Hebrew, unlike Jerome? (assessment: weak)

Should Augustine's canon witness be discounted because he was "no biblical expert" and could not read Hebrew, unlike Jerome? — CARM's comparative-competence move (with James White's sharper "Augustine could not read Hebrew"): the councils that carried the longer canon "were influenced heavily by Augustine, who was no Biblical expert, compared to the scholar Jerome," and "were not universal Church councils," so where the two contemporaries disagree the Hebraist's "No" should outrank the non-Hebraist's "Yes." The factual scaffolding is true — Jerome was the superior philologist, Augustine read no Hebrew, and he did shape the African synods — but the discounting inference is weak. It impeaches a competence Augustine's argument never invoked: his canon ran on breadth of churchly reception (On Christian Doctrine II.8.12), not Hebrew enumeration, a test for which reading Hebrew is not decisive. "No biblical expert" holds only by redefining the phrase as "Hebraist," begging the question; Augustine — whose On Christian Doctrine is itself a manual of biblical interpretation — was no such thing. And the longer canon was no one man's product: it was in Western use before and beyond him (Cyprian, the Cheltenham List, his own bishop Ambrose, the Roman Damasine list, Innocent I) and rests on the Septuagint the whole church read, so Hippo and Carthage ratified a reception already in hand. The "not universal" rider is true of those regional synods but misfires on the ecumenical Florence (1442) CARM lumps with them. What survives is the real fault line this timeline traces — reception against Jerome's Hebraica veritas — a dispute over the criterion, which the men's relative command of Hebrew does not settle.146

Did Augustine accept the deuterocanon only because he mistakenly thought it stood in the Hebrew canon? (assessment: weak)

Did Augustine accept the deuterocanon only because he mistakenly thought it stood in the Hebrew canon? — James White's bid to make Augustine a witness for the Protestant side: "though he accepted the Apocryphal books, he did so because he thought they were part of the Hebrew canon. If he had known what the Hebrew canon was, he would have taken a different position." The true core survives — Augustine read no Hebrew and held the Septuagint inspired (City of God XVIII.43), so his canon does follow the Greek Bible — but the counterfactual built on it does not hold, because Augustine contradicts its factual premise in his own late words. City of God XVII.20 (c. 425) flags Wisdom and Sirach as "not written in the canon of the Jews," and XVIII.36 grants the Maccabees are "held as canonical, not by the Jews, but by the Church" — he knew the disputed books stood outside the Hebrew table and kept them on churchly reception, the very criterion of On Christian Doctrine II.8.12, which never measured the canon by the Hebrew enumeration in the first place (so "had he known the Hebrew canon" changes nothing his test runs on). He had pressed Jerome not to translate "out of the Hebrew" precisely because the two canons diverge, and to the end cited the deuterocanon as Scripture (On Rebuke and Grace, c. 426) — no late drift toward Jerome. What is left is not a recruited Protestant witness but the genuine Augustine-versus-Jerome dispute over the criterion — churchly reception against the Hebrew measure — in which Augustine's longer canon is a considered verdict, not a mistake.147

Did Augustine retract his view and rank the deuterocanon below the canonical Scriptures? (Archibald Alexander) (assessment: weak)

Did Augustine retract his view and rank the deuterocanon below the canonical Scriptures? — the Old-School Presbyterian Archibald Alexander (The Canon of the Old and New Testaments Ascertained, 1826) reads Augustine against the Catholic use of him on three counts: that he said Ecclesiasticus "deserves to be received among the prophetical books" "but this opinion he retracted afterwards"; that he reports a custom by which the apocrypha, though "read in some of the churches," were "not read as Holy Scripture, nor put on a level with the canonical books… but from a lower place"; and so that Augustine "did not put them on a level with the genuine canonical books." It does not hold on its best case. The retraction is real but misread: Augustine's Retractations II.4 corrects only the authorship of the Wisdom of Solomon — that he can no longer affirm Jesus son of Sirach wrote it — not the book's canonical standing, which the same review leaves untouched. The "lower place / not as Holy Scripture" custom is not Augustine's at all: that canonical-versus-"ecclesiastical" gradation (books read for edification, not for doctrine) belongs to Jerome and Rufinus, and Alexander projects it onto Augustine, who instead lists the disputed books inside his forty-four-book canon, states the reception-criterion that carries them there (On Christian Doctrine II.8.12–13), and cites Wisdom, Sirach, and 2 Maccabees with "Holy Scripture says" to the end of his life. What survives is a real but smaller kernel — Augustine's criterion grades authority by breadth of reception, so books received by fewer churches rank lower on that scale — but that is a gradation within Scripture, not the categorical demotion (canonical vs. merely ecclesiastical) Alexander needs. Flag on Alexander's framing the overread retraction and the misattributed custom.148

Related arguments — the Trent claim

Four arguments over the one event the popular case leans hardest on — the Council of Trent's definition of the canon in 1546. They escalate: that Trent added the seven books; that it added them in order to manufacture support for purgatory and prayer for the dead; that the books were a late novelty held below Scripture for a thousand years until Trent; and the careful cousin, that Trent was the first universal, binding affirmation. They share one root and largely fail by it: the deuterocanon was inside the church's Bible — Septuagint, the great codices, the Vulgate, the African and Roman lists, and the ecumenical Florence (1442) — for a millennium before Trent, so what 1546 supplied was a dogmatic close under anathema, new in force, not the books themselves or their use. Best read together.

Did the Council of Trent add the deuterocanonical books to the Bible in 1546? (assessment: weak)

Did the Council of Trent add the deuterocanonical books to the Bible in 1546? — the headline popular-Protestant trope (Mark Driscoll, Mike Winger, Todd Friel, Bible.org, downstream of the Reformation's Hebrew-canon instinct): that Trent added — "inserted," "voted into the Bible" — the seven deuterocanonical books in 1546, so that before Trent they were no part of Scripture. As a description of what happened the slogan does not hold on its best case: it confuses defining a book's status with adding the book, and a dispute over rank ("on the same level") with one over presence ("now it's in the Bible"). The books were inside the Septuagint the church inherited, the great uncial codices, the Latin Vulgate of the West, the African and Roman conciliar lists, and the ecumenical profession of the Council of Florence (Cantate Domino, 1442) for a millennium and more before Trent, and were cited as Scripture by a long line of Fathers; Trent's own decree describes itself as "following the examples of the orthodox Fathers" in "receiving" books "used to be read in the Catholic Church" — a definition of a reception, not the creation of new Scripture — and even the careful proponents (Allen Parr) concede "these books were in the Bible" before 1546. What survives, and is sound, is a narrower and different claim — that Trent's dogmatic close under anathema was an innovation in force (the first binding ecumenical definition of the canon's edge) — taken up by the companion Trent-novelty argument. Flag on the proponents' framing the slide from "not on the same level / disputed" (true) to "added / inserted new books" (false).149

Did Trent add the deuterocanon in order to manufacture support for purgatory and prayer for the dead? (assessment: weak)

Did Trent add the deuterocanon in order to manufacture support for purgatory and prayer for the dead? — the sharpened, motive form of the trope (Todd Friel: the church "added those to the canon in order to support their doctrines that had actually no biblical support"; Mike Winger; Bible.org's "29 years after Luther"): not just that Trent added the books in 1546 but that it added them deliberately to supply proof-texts for purgatory, prayer for the dead, and almsgiving-merit. It does not hold, and for the reason the bare trope shares but this version sharpens — it is anachronistic. The books it says were inserted to rescue the doctrine had stood in the Septuagint, the uncial codices, the Vulgate, the African and Roman lists, and the ecumenical profession of Florence (1442) for more than a thousand years before purgatory was a Reformation controversy, and 2 Maccabees 12 was read as the warrant for prayer for the dead from the patristic era (Tertullian, Cyprian, Cyril of Jerusalem, Augustine — the practice traced on the purgatory timeline), so the doctrine grew up alongside a book already received rather than the book being inserted to save the doctrine. Motive and merit stay separate: a polemically-timed definition is not thereby false — the genetic move would equally discredit the Reformers' own sixteenth-century removal — and a canonical book does not by itself prove the doctrine, the proof-text strength being weighed on the 2 Maccabees and Tobit pages. What is granted is the narrower point this overshoots: Trent defined the canon in deliberate answer to the Reformation, so the definition's timing and emphasis were polemically shaped — but a definition's timing is not the books' origin.150

Was the deuterocanon a late novelty — held below Scripture for a thousand years until Trent canonized it in 1546? (assessment: weak)

Was the deuterocanon a late novelty — held below Scripture for a thousand years until Trent canonized it in 1546? — the reception-history form of the Tridentine-novelty case (Allen Parr in print and on video; downstream of the Reformation's Hebrew-canon instinct; answered by Gary Michuta and Trent Horn): that the deuterocanon's full inspired standing begins at Trent in 1546, that the majority of Catholic scholars held the books below the canon for the thousand years before, and that so long a doubt — closed into dogma only as a reaction to the Reformation — is itself evidence the books are not Scripture. Two things are sound and conceded: a learned, Hebrew-canon-conscious stream really did tier the disputed books below the protocanon across the whole period — from Jerome through Gregory the Great, Hugh of St. Victor, Nicholas of Lyra, and Cardinal Cajetan — and Trent's dogmatic close under anathema genuinely was the first binding ecumenical definition of the canon's edge. But the inference is weak, failing four ways. The lateness of a definition measures when a dispute was juridically closed, not whether the thing defined is inspired: the same 1546 decree defined the New Testament canon under the same anathema, which no one treats as evidence the Gospels are uninspired (as Nicaea's homoousios in 325 did not invent the Son's deity). "The majority… for a thousand years" overstates a divided record — graded by a learned minority but read and received by the wider church (Septuagint, the great codices, African councils, Augustine, Innocent I, Florence). The causal story misattributes the doubt to perceived errors, when it rested on the Hebrew canon (Jerome's Hebraica veritas), the historical-anomalies charge being a separate and weak line. And "novelty" cuts both ways, the Reformation's removal of the books being equally a sixteenth-century act. What survives is a real but modest contribution to the books' graded standing, not a proof that the deuterocanon is no Scripture.151

Was Trent the first universal (ecumenical), binding affirmation of the deuterocanon? (assessment: contested)

Was the Council of Trent the first universal (ecumenical), binding affirmation of the deuterocanon? — the careful cousin of the "added at Trent" trope (James White, in his 2004 debate with Gary Michuta; Matt Whitman of The Ten Minute Bible Hour; Carm.org): conceding the books were used and even listed before 1546, it moves to authority — no council of the whole church ever bindingly affirmed them until Trent, the earlier councils being regional, non-penal, or (it is claimed) not truly ecumenical, so the prior receptions "don't count." The claim splits at the seam between force and scope. As a claim about force it is sound and conceded across this timeline: Trent's dogmatic close under anathema genuinely was the first binding definition of the canon's edge — the African councils were regional, the Roman list and Innocent I were receptions, and even ecumenical Florence (1442) enumerated the books without penalty (White's careful "first ecumenical dogmatic binding definition" lands here). But as a claim about scope — "first universal/ecumenical affirmation," "council of the entire church" — it is overstated: the ecumenical Council of Florence professed and enumerated the identical canon 104 years before Trent, so Carm's lumping of Florence with the "local" synods is a factual error; and Whitman's "all of Christendom getting together to sign off" is a standard no canon (the Protestant sixty-six included) has ever met — one that, applied to the East's actual verdict at the Synod of Jerusalem (1672), tells for the books. Trent was first in juridical force, not first in conciliar scope; the deuterocanon's case rests on continuous reception, of which the anathema is the late close, not the origin. Flag the slide from "first binding definition" (true) to "first universal affirmation" (false, given Florence).152

Related arguments — Luther and the deuterocanon

Three forms of the charge that Martin Luther reshaped the Old Testament canon, most defensible first: that his demotion-and-segregation of the seven deuterocanonical books paved the way for their later removal by others; that, more bluntly, he removed the books himself; and that he rejected 2 Maccabees in order to defeat the purgatory proof-text. They rest on one agreed set of facts — Luther's 1534 Bible kept the books but gathered them into a separate, sub-canonical "Apocrypha" section, and the books themselves left the common Protestant Bible only three centuries later, in the Bible societies' decisions of the 1820s. The "paved the way" form, restricted to its modest causal core, is the most defensible of the three (plausible); the bare "removed" and the "in order to" forms over-read those facts and fail (both weak). The mirror from the opposite confessional side is the Trent claim group — "Luther removed them" and "Trent added them" the same sixteenth-century dispute told from each side.

Did Luther's demotion of the deuterocanon pave the way for its later removal? (assessment: plausible)

Did Luther's demotion pave the way for the deuterocanon's removal? — the retreating, causal form of the "Luther removed the books" trope: granting that Luther's own 1534 Bible kept the deuterocanon, the claim is that by demoting it from canon and gathering it into a separate "Apocrypha" appendix he set in motion the process that ended, three centuries on, in the books' removal from the common Protestant Bible. It is the most defensible of the Luther charges. The modest core is well-grounded and even conceded by a Catholic defender of the deuterocanon, Gary Michuta crediting Luther with "beginning the process" that produced the smaller Protestant Bibles: Luther originated the two-tier layout — books segregated, labelled "not equal to the Holy Scriptures" — and that layout became the template every later vernacular Protestant Bible inherited (the Swedish 1541, Danish 1550, Geneva, and 1611 King James included), so that the British and Foreign Bible Society's 1826 decision to drop the "Apocrypha" operated on a section Luther had created and a sub-canonical rank the Reformed confessions (the Thirty-Nine Articles, the Westminster Confession) had hardened. What overstates is the "naturally/inevitably led" force the slogan trades on: the books stood bound in the Protestant Bibles — even mandated, by Archbishop Abbot's 1615 order on pain of imprisonment — for nearly three centuries; the actual removal turned on distinct nineteenth-century drivers Luther neither willed nor foresaw (a Reformed-Puritan campaign and the plain printing economy of the mass Bible societies); and Luther's own Lutheran tradition kept the Apocrypha section, so the removal was the work of a particular later stream, not the natural sequel of 1534. A necessary precondition and originating step, then — not a sufficient or inevitable cause. Flag on the popular framing only the slide from "Luther took the first step" (true) to "the removal followed naturally and inevitably from it" (overstated).153

Did Martin Luther remove the deuterocanonical books from the Bible? (assessment: weak)

Did Martin Luther remove the deuterocanonical books from the Bible? — the headline Catholic-apologetic trope and the exact mirror of the Protestant "Trent added the books in 1546" slogan (Jimmy Akin: the books "were taken out of the Old Testament by Martin Luther," "cut out," "remove that book from the Bible and appendicize it"): that Luther removed — took out, cut out — the seven deuterocanonical books from Scripture. As a description of what he did it does not hold on its best case, and for the same reason its twin fails: it confuses demotion-and-relocation with excision. Luther's 1534 German Bible contains the deuterocanon — gathered into a separate inter-testamental section headed "Apocrypha… not held equal to the Holy Scriptures, and yet… profitable and good to read," each book prefaced and commended to read — so the books were demoted and set apart, not taken out; they then stood in the Lutheran and wider Protestant Bibles (the King James included, by Archbishop Abbot's 1615 order on pain of imprisonment) for nearly three centuries, their actual disappearance being the nineteenth-century work of the British and Foreign Bible Society's 1826 resolution, not Luther's act. Even a Catholic defender of the deuterocanon, Gary Michuta, corrects his own side: "Catholic apologists sometimes claim that Martin Luther removed the deuterocanonical books from Scripture. This is not entirely true. Luther's German translation of the Scriptures included the deuterocanon." What survives, and is sound, is the narrower, different claim — that Luther denied the books canonical, doctrine-establishing status and physically segregated them, a real change in how a printed Bible presented the canon — but that is demotion, not removal. Flag on the proponents (Akin) the slide from "demoted from canon and set apart" (true) to "removed / cut out of the Bible" (false).154

Did Luther reject 2 Maccabees in order to defeat the purgatory proof-text? (assessment: weak)

Did Luther reject 2 Maccabees in order to defeat the purgatory proof-text? — the sharpened motive form of the "Luther removed the books" trope, and the mirror of the Trent-motive charge from the other side (Jimmy Akin: "Since Luther chose to reject… purgatory… he had to remove that book"; Gary Michuta in the "1518 acceptance → 1519 reversal" form): that Luther demoted 2 Maccabees because it taught prayer for the dead, rejecting the book to escape the doctrine. The strong causal form does not hold. Luther's own printed answer to the proof-text (the 1521 Defense and Explanation of All the Articles) is multi-pronged — Jerome and the Hebrew canon, 2 Maccabees' contradiction of 1 Maccabees, and, decisively, that "even were the book authoritative… at least one passage out of the chief books should support it," i.e. purgatory fails even if Maccabees counts — so rejecting the book was not the mechanism by which he meant to defeat the doctrine. He also still believed in purgatory in 1519 and for some years after (so the rejection cannot have been "in order to" defeat a doctrine not yet rejected), and he judged the whole deuterocanon by the same Hebrew-canon method before and after the dispute, citing Sirach and Tobit identically to the end of his life. What survives, and is granted, is the narrower point: the purgatory controversy at Leipzig (1519) was the occasion that crystallized his long-held Hebrew-canon conviction — the dispute shaped his timing and emphasis (a firmer point than its Trent-side mirror, since Luther's act really was occasioned by the controversy) — but not the strong "he had to remove the book to escape the verse." Flag on the proponents (Akin, Michuta) the slide from "the dispute shaped his emphasis" (true) to "he rejected the book solely in order to defeat the doctrine" (false, and against Luther's own stated argument).155

Related arguments — Athanasius's three tiers

Two questions about one document — Athanasius's thirty-ninth Festal Letter (367), which sorts the Old Testament into a famous three tiers: the canon proper, a middle rank of books "appointed by the Fathers to be read… for instruction," and the heretical "apocrypha." The first asks whether the list makes him a witness for the Protestant canon (contested); the second asks the next question down — granting the three tiers, why are the top two split, and is the dividing line function rather than inspiration? (plausible). Read together they are the single-Father case and its sequel.

Did Athanasius reject the deuterocanon — is his Festal Letter 39 a witness for the Protestant canon? (assessment: contested)

Did Athanasius reject the deuterocanon — is his Festal Letter 39 a witness for the Protestant Old Testament canon? — the single-Father, named-proponent form of the wider patristic-lists case (Archibald Alexander in print, James White and Steve Christie and the Reformation's Hebrew-canon instinct popularly, Gavin Ortlund in the careful Eastern form; answered by Gary Michuta): that Athanasius's thirty-ninth Festal Letter (367), which fixes a twenty-two-book Hebrew-sized canon and files Wisdom, Sirach, Judith, and Tobit below it, makes him an early witness for the Protestant canon and against the deuterocanon. The verdict splits by sense. As a claim about canonical status the argument is sound — Athanasius really does place the freestanding deuterocanon below the canon proper, a genuine fourth-century Eastern witness against its canonicity. But the fuller claim that he held "the Protestant canon" and treated these books as the Reformation's Apocrypha is overstated: it collapses his three tiers into two, mistaking books "appointed by the Fathers to be read… for instruction" — prescribed for catechumens, sharply set against the heretical forgeries — for the rejected Apocrypha; his canon is not even the Protestant thirty-nine (he drops Esther and keeps Baruch and the Epistle inside Jeremiah); and across his polemical and pastoral works he cites Wisdom, Sirach, and Tobit in his own voice under Scripture formulae to settle doctrine before bishops and an emperor. The oldest version of the flat claim (Alexander's) rests, moreover, on the Synopsis Scripturae Sacrae — a work now judged pseudo-Athanasian. Strong against the books' canonicity, overstated as a witness for the Protestant canon; the same divided picture weighed for the Fathers as a class on the early-church-rejected page. No clear winner on the headline.156

What divides Athanasius's "canon" and "read" tiers — function (evangelizing Jews vs. catechizing converts), not inspiration? (assessment: plausible)

What divides Athanasius's "canon" and "read" tiers — function, not inspiration? — the narrower companion to the question above: granting the three tiers, the interpretation (in the line of Gary Michuta) that the line between the canon proper and the "read for instruction" tier is one of function, not inspiration — since Athanasius cites the lower-tier Wisdom, Sirach, and Tobit in his own voice as Scripture — with the canon tier proposed as the list usable for evangelizing Jews ("fountains of salvation," grounded in §6 on Jesus shaming the Sadducees and reproving the Jews) and the read tier as catechesis for new converts. The premise is secure and the catechesis half is probable: the read tier is catechetical by Athanasius's own words and by Origen's milk-and-solid-food Homily on Numbers, which names the very same books (Esther, Judith, Tobit, Wisdom) as beginners' reading; and Esther's demotion fits a book whose Jewish standing was still contested (Melito drops it; the rabbis dispute it at b. Megillah 7a). But the canon-tier half overreaches: §6 argues the canon's sufficiency and authority, not a selection-criterion, and the list fits "what the Jews received" only roughly — Athanasius keeps Baruch (no Jewish canon held it) and drops Esther (which it kept). The line is function, not inspiration — secure; the read tier is catechesis — probable; the canon tier is "for evangelizing Jews" — a plausible conjecture beyond the letter.157

Related arguments — Tobit's alleged false teachings

Two Protestant removal arguments from the charge that the book of Tobit teaches doctrinal error, so it cannot be Scripture: that its almsgiving "purges away all sin" (12:9) is a works-righteousness foreign to the gospel, and that it commands magic (the fish-organ remedy of chapter 6 — fumigation to drive off the demon Asmodeus, gall to cure blindness). Both are weighed here and found weak, and both turn on the same move — screening the canon by a doctrinal objection — tested directly in the sola scriptura argument below. Best read together.

Does Tobit 12:9 ("alms… shall purge away all sin") teach a works-righteousness that bars it from the canon? (assessment: weak)

Does Tobit 12:9 teach a works-righteousness that bars the book from the canon? — the reading, urged by Luther in seed and crisply by Kenneth Collins and Jerry Walls (Roman but Not Catholic, 2017), that "alms doth deliver from death, and shall purge away all sin" is a sub-Christian works-salvation contradicting Paul (Eph 2:8–9; Rom 3:28), so that Tobit preaches "another gospel" and cannot be Scripture. It does not hold on its best case: the verse is the ordinary biblical almsgiving idiom — "righteousness delivereth from death" is verbatim Proverbs 10:2 and 11:4, "shewing mercy to the poor" purges iniquity in Daniel 4:27, "charity shall cover the multitude of sins" in 1 Peter 4:8 — one node in a canonical arc (Gary Anderson: Daniel 4:27 → Tobit → Sirach → the Synoptics), so the charge, pressed consistently, condemns the protocanon and the dominical teaching with it; and the faith-and-works tension it raises stands already in James 2:24, at which Luther aimed the identical objection ("a right strawy epistle") without ejecting James. What survives is the narrower, true point that Tobit presses almsgiving's atoning efficacy harder than any single protocanonical verse and became the proof-text for the Catholic doctrines of satisfaction and merit the Reformation denied — a divergence of systematic theology, not a contradiction of the gospel that unbooks it.158

Does Tobit's fish-organ remedy (Tobit 6) prescribe occult magic, barring the book from the canon? (assessment: weak)

Does Tobit's fish-organ remedy prescribe occult magic? — the charge, urged popularly by Matt Slick (CARM) and standard in the Reformation-era "the Apocrypha teaches error, so reject it" objection, that the angel Raphael's prescription in Tobit 6 — burn the fish's heart and liver to drive the demon Asmodeus from Sarah, smear its gall on old Tobit's eyes to cure his blindness — is occult magic forbidden by the Law (Deut 18:10–11), so the book teaches error and cannot be inspired. It does not hold on its best case: Scripture itself heals and delivers through material instruments all over the canon — clay of spittle on a blind man's eyes (John 9:6), handkerchiefs from Paul's body that put out diseases and evil spirits (Acts 19:11–12), washing in the Jordan (2 Kings 5:14), the bronze serpent (Num 21:8–9), blood on the doorposts that turns the destroyer aside (Exod 12:13), a coin in a fish's mouth (Matt 17:27) — so the charge, pressed consistently, would convict the Gospels and the Pentateuch with it. And it equivocates on "magic": the Torah bans autonomous occult power — divination, necromancy, consulting familiar spirits — whereas Tobit's remedy is angel-commanded and God-credited, the demon not obeyed but repelled and "bound by the angel" (8:3); the Church that read the book allegorized the scene as a type of Christ and of baptism (Bede) and presupposed it wholesome (Ambrose), not as sorcery. What survives is the narrower, true point of genre — Tobit displays a Second-Temple world of named demons and folk pharmacology (apotropaic fumigation, fish-gall ophthalmology) more vividly than any protocanonical book — texture some find strange, but milieu, not a command to practice magic that unbooks it.159

Does the deuterocanon dispute undermine sola scriptura — can Scripture settle its own table of contents? (assessment: contested)

Does the deuterocanon dispute undermine sola scriptura — can Scripture alone settle its own table of contents? — the umbrella meta-argument over the whole deuterocanon-removal case (Catholic apologists Devin Rose and Dave Armstrong in print; answered by R. C. Sproul and Michael Kruger): because Scripture contains no inspired list of its own books and the canon's edge is contested — this very timeline's fifteen-century dispute, with the Reformers removing seven books long bound in the Western Bible — a Protestant cannot, on Scripture alone, settle whether a doctrine (purgatory, say) is "biblical," so "the whole Protestant experiment fails." The underlying tension is real and serious: there is no inspired table of contents, the canon cannot be derived from Scripture, and thoughtful Protestants concede it (Sproul's "fallible collection of infallible books"). But the demolition overreaches. Marginal uncertainty over seven books does not dissolve the canon's common core or render doctrines undecidable — purgatory's deuterocanonical proof-text is weighed on its merits regardless; the Catholic alternative faces the same recognition-regress one level up and the awkward fact that its canon was dogmatically fixed only at Trent (1546), after the Reformation it answers; "removed" begs the Hebrew-canon question on which the Reformers claimed to be restoring; and the argument mistakes a claim about Scripture's authority for a claim that the canon is derived from Scripture, which no careful defender holds (the Spirit's witness and the church's ministerial reception recognize, not confer, the canon — Michael Kruger's self-authenticating model). The genuine, contested question is how the canon is recognized; the "experiment fails" leap is conjecture beyond it.160

Does 2 Maccabees 12:43–45 teach prayer and atoning offering for the dead? (assessment: contested)

Does 2 Maccabees 12:43–45 teach prayer and atoning offering for the dead? — the deuterocanonical proof-text the Reformation made a doctrinal stake in the canon dispute, affirmed by the Council of Trent and the Catechism, contested by Luther, Calvin, and the Westminster Confession. It is strong on its plainest leg and overstated on its furthest. As a warrant for prayer and offering for the dead the reading is plain and conceded even by the Reformers — Judas Maccabeus sends a sin offering so the fallen "might be delivered from sin," called "an holy and good thought" — and the practice it warrants was the near-universal custom of the ancient Church (weighed sound on the purgatory timeline), while the very move it makes — arguing from a practice for the dead to the resurrection — recurs in protocanonical 1 Corinthians 15:29 ("baptized for the dead"). But the leap from prayer-for-the-dead to purgatory outruns a text that shows intercession grounded in resurrection hope, not a purgative fire; the men died wearing idolatrous amulets (12:40), grave matter from which purgatory by its own definition does not deliver, so pressed as a strict purgatory proof-text it proves the wrong thing; and its doctrinal force depends on the very canonicity in dispute — strong by reception, weak by the Protestant canon-criteria (the Hebrew canon, the author's closing disclaimer at 15:38, the book's self-description as an abridgment of Jason of Cyrene). No clear winner.161

Does the deuterocanon's thousand years in the Vulgate put the burden of proof on those who would remove it? (assessment: weak)

Does the deuterocanon's thousand-year place in the Vulgate make inclusion the default and shift the burden of proof onto those who would remove it? — the apologetic move that, because the disputed books were bound in the Bible of Latin Christendom from c. 400 to the Reformation, inclusion is the status quo and the whole onus falls on the remover. The historical premise is sound: the Vulgate really was the Western Bible for a millennium and really did carry the deuterocanon, given conciliar and liturgical form by the African councils, Innocent I, Augustine, and Florence. But the burden-of-proof conclusion does not hold on its best case: it is a dialectical framing, not evidence, and cannot conclude for inclusion; "status quo" is itself the disputed point, since Luther and the Reformers claimed to be restoring an older Hebrew canon; the Vulgate's own prologues — Jerome's — flagged these very books as apocrypha, read but "not for confirming … ecclesiastical dogmas," and travelled with the text through the Middle Ages; binding books in one codex never meant equal canonicity (the "Ecclesiastical"/Canonical tiering of Rufinus and Athanasius); and the premise is Western-only, the Greek East never having had "the Vulgate." What survives is the real but modest presumptive weight of long reception — the criterion this timeline already traces — not a burden-shift that settles the canon by default.162

Do the deuterocanonical books disclaim their own inspiration, excluding themselves from the canon? (assessment: weak)

Do the deuterocanonical books disclaim their own inspiration, excluding themselves from the canon? — the Protestant removal argument from the books' own words (Luther and Calvin in seed): several deuterocanonical books say things about themselves that, the argument holds, amount to a disavowal of inspired status — 2 Maccabees ends with the author's apology ("if … slenderly and meanly, it is that which I could attain unto," 15:38) and opens by calling itself a one-volume abridgment of Jason of Cyrene's five (2:23–28); the Sirach prologue has the grandson-translator apologize for rendering imperfections; and 1 Maccabees thrice laments that prophecy had ceased (9:27; 4:46; 14:41). It does not hold as a disqualifier on its best case: none of the passages is a statement about inspiration. The colophon and preface are a conventional authorial modesty formula and an editor's note on his method — the same self-conscious preface that heads Luke 1:1–4, which no one unbooks; the Sirach apology concerns the difficulty of Greek translation while ranking the book "for learning and wisdom"; and the "prophecy had ceased" laments are historical observations about the intertestamental gap — the identical lament protocanonical Psalm 74:9 makes from inside the canon ("there is no more any prophet"). Pressed as a disqualifier the criterion proves too much, threatening Luke, the Psalter, and the compiled protocanonical histories. What survives is the narrower, true point that no protocanonical book ends with an apology like 2 Maccabees 15:38 and that the cessation-of-prophecy laments cohere with the Hebrew-canon dating — a contributing datum to the graded standing the Hebrew-minded Fathers assigned these books, not a self-exclusion that unbooks them.163

Were the Jews "entrusted with the oracles of God" (Romans 3:2), so the church should follow the Hebrew canon? (assessment: contested)

Were the Jews "entrusted with the oracles of God" (Romans 3:2), so the church should follow the Hebrew canon? — the Protestant removal argument from Paul's word that the Jews "were entrusted with the oracles of God" (Luther and the Reformed confessions in instinct, James R. White in print): the Jewish people are the appointed custodians of the Old Testament, so the church should defer to the canon they recognized — the Hebrew table that excludes the deuterocanon. As a proof-text it is weak: "oracles of God" (Greek logia) means God's utterances — the Law and the covenant promises — not a catalogue of inspired books, as the word's New-Testament usage shows (Acts 7:38, the Law as "the lively oracles"; Hebrews 5:12; 1 Peter 4:11), and the unbroken patristic exposition of the verse (Origen, Chrysostom, Theophylact, Aquinas) takes it of Israel's privilege and ingratitude, never as a ruling on the canon; there was no single closed Jewish canon in the apostolic age to defer to (the Septuagint diaspora read the longer Greek Bible, and the rabbinic table hardened only later, via the discarded "Jamnia" legend and the medieval Masoretic Text); and deferring to that post-Christian rabbinic ruling begs the question Augustine answered — the Maccabees are canonical "not by the Jews, but by the Church." But the broader interpretation the verse is enlisted to serve — that the Old Testament should be measured by the Hebrew canon — is no weak position: it is Jerome's Hebraica veritas, the backbone of the Reformation's case, set against Augustine's reception-criterion in the very dispute this timeline traces with no clear winner. The prop fails while the reading it points to stands.164

Do the deuterocanon's historical errors (the book of Judith) disqualify it from the canon? (assessment: weak)

Do the deuterocanon's historical errors — the book of Judith above all — disqualify it from the canon? — the Protestant removal argument from demonstrable error (the Reformation's Hebrew-canon instinct popularly; James White, Mike Winger, Allen Parr in print and on video; answered by Gary Michuta and Trent Horn): the deuterocanonical books contain historical errors, and a book with historical errors cannot be inspired Scripture, so the deuterocanon is excluded — the flagship exhibit being Judith, which makes Nebuchadnezzar "king of the Assyrians" reigning at Nineveh (Judith 1:1, 1:7) when he was king of Babylon and Nineveh had fallen c. 612 BC, set over a post-exilic Judea with the temple freshly re-sanctified (4:3). The verdict splits cleanly. The factual observation is sound: read as plain history, Judith does contain conspicuous anomalies, and they should be conceded, not explained away. But the disqualifying inference is weak, failing four ways. It begs the question — inerrancy is a corollary of inspiration, not a test applied before it, so in a book granted inspired an unresolved difficulty is read as apparent, awaiting context or manuscript, not as proven error (Geisler and Howe's rule for the protocanon, switched off for Judith alone). It applies a standard the protocanon would fail: Mark 2:26 (Abiathar for Ahimelech) and Matthew 23:35 ("Zechariah son of Berechiah") carry difficulties of the same kind, which Protestants harmonize rather than use to unbook Mark or Matthew. It overrides without engaging the live, mainstream literary judgment — across Catholic and critical scholarship — that Judith's impossibilities are deliberate signals of a didactic novella, not blunders. And it over-reaches: a single book's anomalies cannot disqualify the other six deuterocanonical books, which would each need a demonstrated error (1 Maccabees being, by contrast, sober Hellenistic history). What survives is a modest contribution to the books' graded standing — Judith reads as edifying story, not chronicle — not a proof that the deuterocanon is no Scripture.165

Was Jerome "wrong for his reasons" — did later evidence undercut his case for a Hebrew-only canon? (assessment: contested)

Was Jerome "wrong for his reasons"? Did later evidence undercut his case for a Hebrew-only Old Testament canon? — not "was the Hebrew-canon conclusion right?" (the dispute the whole timeline traces) but the sharper charge that Jerome's specific grounds were mistaken, so his "No" was rightly-sounding but wrongly reasoned. His reasons split, and so does the verdict. His philological judgments — Tobit a "Chaldean" (Aramaic) book, 1 Maccabees Hebrew but 2 Maccabees Greek, Wisdom a Greek composition "never among the Hebrews," Sirach a book he had himself "found in Hebrew" — were not embarrassed by the twentieth century's manuscript finds but confirmed by them: the Aramaic and Hebrew Tobit of Qumran (4Q196–200), the Hebrew Ben Sira of the Cairo Geniza, Masada, and Qumran, and the modern consensus that 1 Maccabees is Hebrew, 2 Maccabees and Wisdom Greek. So the popular form of the charge (that the Dead Sea Scrolls caught Jerome out on the languages) is itself mistaken — and he never sorted canonical from apocryphal by Greekness in the first place. Where his reasoning is genuinely undercut is his historical premise: that a fixed, authoritative Jewish canon already stood for the apostolic church to defer to. There was no closed first-century canon, the canon-closing "Council of Jamnia" is a discarded hypothesis, and the Hebrew-and-Aramaic Jewish library at Qumran kept Tobit and Sirach — books the later rabbinic table excluded — so "the Hebrew the Jews kept" was never the same as "the rabbinic canon." Yet the genuine flaw does not dispose of his case: the rabbinic exclusion is real even without a council, a dated premise does not settle whether the church should measure the Old Testament by the Hebrew, and the reading Jerome served — set against Augustine's reception-criterion — remains the serious, live position. A real blow to one premise, a miss on the others, the conclusion left standing.166

Was the apostles' Bible the Greek Septuagint — and does that make the deuterocanon canonical? (assessment: contested)

Was the apostles' Bible the Greek Septuagint — and does that make the deuterocanon canonical? — the standard Catholic/Orthodox answer to the "never quoted" silence: never mind the silence on those particular books, the whole Bible the apostles quoted was the Greek one whose later manuscripts carried them, so the apostolic Scripture was the longer canon (Catholic Answers; the patristic root, Irenaeus, "the apostles … agree with this aforesaid translation [of the Septuagint]"). The verdict splits by discipline. By textual and reception history the core is sound: the apostles' Old Testament was a Greek one, and the New Testament's quotations follow its readings against the proto-Masoretic Hebrew hundreds of times — Archer & Chirichigno's 340-to-33 — sometimes with the argument riding on the Greek (Acts 15:16–17 // Amos 9:11–12, "that the residue of men might seek" for the Hebrew "possess the remnant of Edom"; Hebrews 1:6 // Deuteronomy 32:43, a whole angels-clause the Masoretic lacks; Hebrews 10:5 // Psalm 40:6, "a body" for "mine ears"). But by canon history the inference it is enlisted to carry is overstated: there was no closed, deuterocanon-inclusive Greek canon in the first century to inherit (Sundberg's 1964 demolition of the "Alexandrian canon"), and the books' presence in "the Septuagint" is a fourth-century codex fact, not a feature of the scrolls Jesus read. Every strong proof-case is a protocanonical book, so the divergences fix the apostles' text-form, not their canon-extent. The honest residue is genuine but graded — powerful evidence that the apostolic and early church received a Greek Bible in whose world these books lived and were used, converging with the longer-canon stream this timeline traces, but not a clean apostolic close on the wider list. The mirror image of the silence argument, landing in the same place from the opposite side: a long-disputed inheritance, neither apostolically canonized nor excluded.167

Did the Jews reject the deuterocanon — was the first-century Jewish canon closed and Hebrew-only? (assessment: weak)

Did the Jews reject the deuterocanon — was the first-century Jewish canon closed and Hebrew-only? — the headline popular case for the Protestant Old Testament, with a large modern-apologist roster (F. F. Bruce in scholarly form; CARM, Allen Parr, Mike Winger, James White, GotQuestions, Focus on the Family popularly): the Jews, to whom the oracles were committed (Romans 3:2), recognized a closed, Hebrew-only canon that rejected the deuterocanon, so the church should exclude it too. The umbrella over the Romans 3:2 major premise and the props (Josephus's twenty-two books, Philo's silence, the cessation of prophecy, the "Jamnia" legend) each weighed elsewhere. The minor premise it asserts does not hold on its best case: there was no fixed, closed Jewish canon in the apostolic age to reject the books from — the leading scholarship (Sundberg, Lim, McDonald) holds none was yet settled in the early first century; the Writings were still disputed inside Judaism into the second (the Mishnah's quarrels over Ecclesiastes and the Song); "the Jews" were a plurality of communities, some — the Hebrew-reading sectarians of Qumran among them — keeping Tobit and Sirach; and the Hebrew-only table is a post-70 rabbinic settlement, fixed after and partly against the church's Greek Bible, so deferring to it begs the question Augustine answered ("not by the Jews, but by the Church"). What survives is the narrower, true point that a major Jewish stream — Josephus, and the Pharisaic-rabbinic tradition that became normative — measured Scripture by a shorter, Hebrew table: a contributing datum to the books' graded standing and the Hebrew-measure reading weighed (contested) on the Romans 3:2 page, not the flat "the Jews rejected it" the popular slogans assert. Flag on the proponents the slide from a Hebrew-measured stream that existed to a settled first-century verdict that did not.168

Was there a formal Pharisaic canon — the Protestant Old Testament — that Jesus and Paul accepted? (assessment: contested)

Was there a formal Pharisaic canon — the Protestant Old Testament — that Jesus and Paul accepted? — the careful, strongest form of the umbrella above (Steve Christie, Why Protestant Bibles Are Smaller; in scholarly dress Roger Beckwith and R. Laird Harris): conceding that no single canon was agreed on by all first-century Jews, it relocates the claim to the Pharisaic stream Jesus and Paul belonged to — arguing that "the Law and the Prophets," on the lips of Paul the Pharisee (Romans 3:21) and of Jesus addressing the Pharisees (Luke 16:14–16), named the Hebrew table, content-identical to the Protestant thirty-nine, which excludes the deuterocanon. The relocation is the argument's real strength: the Pharisaic-rabbinic stream is the one Paul belonged to and Jesus disputed within, it became normative Judaism, and its table never numbered the deuterocanon — the very residue the umbrella grants, here put on its best footing, with serious early-closure scholarship (Beckwith, Harris) behind it. But it is genuinely contested. The leading canon historians (Sundberg, Lim, McDonald, Barton) hold the Writings were not yet a formally closed category in the early first century; the Pharisees' own successors still disputed the canon's edge (Ecclesiastes, the Song) into the second (Mishnah Yadayim 3:5) — though over subtracting protocanonical books, never adding deuterocanonical ones; and "the Law and the Prophets" is a bipartite phrase that does not by itself fix the third division, read by the open-canon camp as a sign that division was still open. What is sound is the relocation and the Pharisaic exclusion of the deuterocanon; what is overstated, and to be flagged on the proponent, is Christie's "they would have understood it to be limited to only those writings found in the Protestant Old Testaments today" — a closed, Protestant-identical list, consciously delimited, projected back into Jesus' day. Strong by the early-closure scholarship's lights, short of proof by the open-canon mainstream's: no clear winner.169

Does Josephus's twenty-two-book count (Against Apion 1.8) prove a closed, deuterocanon-excluding Hebrew canon? (assessment: contested)

Does Josephus's twenty-two-book count (Against Apion 1.8) prove a closed, deuterocanon-excluding Hebrew canon? — the strongest single plank of the umbrella above, weighed on its own (James White, Mike Winger, Allen Parr, CARM, Bible.org popularly; F. F. Bruce and John Piper in the careful form; answered by Gary Michuta and Trent Horn). Writing c. AD 93, Josephus gives the earliest datable Jewish reckoning of "only twenty-two books … justly believed to be divine," with the prophetic record running only "from the death of Moses till the reign of Artaxerxes," because since then "there hath not been an exact succession of prophets" — leaving the Greek-only books on the wrong side of the line. The verdict splits. The bare datum is sound: Josephus is a real, early, Hebrew-measured witness who did not number the deuterocanon among the inspired, and the firmest the Protestant case has on the Jewish side. But the strong inference the proponents draw — that the count proves a settled, pan-Jewish, deuterocanon-excluding canon centuries old and binding on the church — outruns it on three counts: it is one witness to one (priestly–proto-rabbinic) stream when the leading scholarship holds no Jewish canon was yet fixed; the count is title-less and the "twenty-two" maps imperfectly onto the Protestant thirty-nine; and Josephus himself drew on 1 Esdras and 1 Maccabees as histories. Strong as testimony, short as proof; flag on the proponents the slide from his count to a church-binding canon, and Bible.org's lean on the discredited "Jamnia" council.170

Did prophecy cease after Malachi — does that disqualify the deuterocanon from the canon? (assessment: weak)

Did prophecy cease after Malachi — does that disqualify the deuterocanon from the canon? — a recurring prop under the umbrella above, in scholarly form Roger Beckwith's, popularly James White's and CARM's (Matt Slick, crediting Geisler & MacKenzie): Jewish tradition held that prophetic inspiration ceased with the last prophets Haggai, Zechariah, and Malachi (b. Yoma 9b; 21b); the deuterocanon was written after that; therefore the Jews did not, and the church should not, reckon it inspired. The premise is sound and well-attested — 1 Maccabees itself records the absence of prophets (9:27; 4:46; 14:41) — but the inference fails at two joints. Cessation of prophecy is not closure of the canon: the very Talmud that preserves the cessation tradition cites Ben Sira as a verse of the Writings (Bava Kamma 92b) and still debated Qohelet and the Song into the second century (Mishnah Yadayim 3:5); the deuterocanon is a witness to the cessation idea, not a violator of it; and the late-rabbinic ban on "everything written after" Sirach would, on its makers' own terms, exclude the Gospels too. What survives is the narrower, true point — a major Jewish stream graded the post-Malachi writings below the prophetic books — a contributing datum to the deuterocanon's graded standing, not a decisive disqualifier. Flag on the proponents the leap from "prophecy ceased" to "the canon was shut," and that CARM's own proof-texts are themselves deuterocanonical verses.171

Were there “400 silent years” — no prophet at all between Malachi and Christ — closing the canon? (assessment: weak)

Were there “400 silent years” — no prophet at all between Malachi and Christ — closing the canon? — the popular “four hundred silent years” form of the cessation prop (Allen Parr most flatly: Malachi “the last prophet inspired by God,” “no other prophetic voices” in the 400 years; CARM likewise), distinct from the companion argument in that it tests whether the silence was real at all. The narrower premise is sound — a recognized tradition ended the line of classical, writing prophets with Haggai, Zechariah, and Malachi — but the flat “no prophetic voices at all” is refuted by the proponents' own anchor witness. Josephus, whose Artaxerxes cutoff is the whole basis, reports prophetic figures after Artaxerxes (Hyrcanus with “the gift of prophecy,” War I.2.8; Menahem the Essene foretelling Herod's reign, Antiquities XV.10.5; Jesus son of Ananus, War VI.5.3) and claims a prophetic gift for himself; the New Testament places Anna (Luke 2:36), John the Baptist (Matt 11:9), and a prophet-acclaimed Jesus (John 9:17) in the supposed silence; and Justin Martyr runs the prophetic succession unbroken “until John” and Christ (Dialogue 51–52, 82). Nor would even the narrower premise seal the canon: prophetic authorship is not the Hebrew canon's criterion (Agur, “the prophecy,” Prov 30:1; Lemuel's mother, Prov 31:1), and cessation of prophecy is not closure of the canon. What survives is the modest, true point that a major Jewish stream graded the post-Malachi writings below the prophetic books. Flag on the proponents (Parr) the slide from a recognized prophetic terminus to a literal four-century silence their own sources deny.172

Did the Council of Jamnia (c. AD 90) close or affirm the Hebrew Old Testament canon? (assessment: weak)

Did the Council of Jamnia (c. AD 90) close or affirm the Hebrew Old Testament canon? — a recurring prop under the umbrella above, in its popular form Bible.org's and Christianity Today's: that a formal “Council of Jamnia” (Yavneh, c. AD 90, sometimes paired with 118) officially closed or gave final affirmation to the thirty-nine-book Hebrew canon, dating a fixed, deuterocanon-excluding Jewish canon to the first century. It does not hold. The “council” is a nineteenth-century reconstruction (Heinrich Graetz, 1871), not an event the sources report, and the field has substantially abandoned it (Jack P. Lewis, Albert Sundberg, Sid Z. Leiman, F. F. Bruce). The rabbinic discussions assigned to Yavneh debated only whether two already-canonical books, the Song of Songs and Ecclesiastes, “defile the hands” (Mishnah Yadayim 3:5) — a question within a settled Scripture, still live into the second century, the opposite of a closure; no source records a book excluded at Jabneh, and the deuterocanon never came up at all. So discredited is the thesis that Protestant apologists themselves disown it (Answers in Genesis: “Jamnia actually wasn't a council, it certainly didn't pronounce on the Jewish canon”). What survives is the different, narrower point — that the Pharisaic–rabbinic stream which became normative measured Scripture by a shorter, Hebrew table, a settlement that hardened gradually rather than at a datable council. Flag on the proponents (Bible.org, Christianity Today) the presentation of a discarded hypothesis as established conciliar fact, and Bible.org's yoking of it to Josephus to project a recognized first-century canon.173

Does Hebrews 11:35's allusion to 2 Maccabees 7 show the New Testament treats the deuterocanon as Scripture? (assessment: weak)

Does Hebrews 11:35's allusion to 2 Maccabees 7 show the New Testament treats the deuterocanon as Scripture? — the affirmative Catholic mirror of the "never quoted" silence argument and a companion to the apostolic-Septuagint case (Jimmy Akin, Defending the Deuterocanonicals): that Hebrews 11:35 ("others were tortured, not accepting deliverance; that they might obtain a better resurrection") has no protocanonical referent and tracks the seven brothers of 2 Maccabees 7, so an inspired New-Testament writer drew on the book as Scripture — which (Akin adds) is also why Luther demoted Hebrews. The verdict splits. The allusion is sound: it is granted across confessions (the Nestle–Aland apparatus and Protestant reference works register it), and Akin's survey premise holds — the nearest protocanonical case, the fiery furnace of Daniel 3, ends in deliverance, not martyrdom-for-resurrection. But the inference to canonicity is weak: a literary echo is not a citation with a Scripture-formula; the very same roll-call alludes two verses later to the martyrdom of Isaiah (11:37, "sawn asunder") from the pseudepigraphal Ascension of Isaiah, so the test would canonize a pseudepigraphon; and the New Testament's allusion-net catches 1 Enoch and the pagan poets, whom no one receives as canon. Akin's added claim that Luther demoted Hebrews "because it cites 2 Maccabees" is contradicted by Luther's own Preface to Hebrews (1522), whose reasons are the book's non-apostolic authorship and its denial of post-baptismal repentance (Heb 6, 10, 12:17), not the allusion. What survives is the true, modest residue the timeline traces — the deuterocanon was known, valued, and used, evidence of reception, not of a closed apostolic canon. Flag on the proponent (Akin) the slide from "alluded to" to "cites … as Scripture," and the unfounded Luther-Hebrews motive.174

Did the deuterocanon confirm doctrine in conciliar argument before Trent — the Wisdom of Solomon behind Nicaea's "Light from Light"? (assessment: plausible)

Did the deuterocanon confirm doctrine in conciliar argument before Trent — the Wisdom of Solomon behind Nicaea's "Light from Light"? — the Catholic continuity-of-use answer to the novelty case (Gary Michuta, council-by-council; Catholic Answers, Jimmy Akin): that the disputed books did doctrine-confirming work in the church's conciliar argument long before Trent (1546) — the Wisdom of Solomon's "brightness of the everlasting light… effluence of the glory of the Almighty" (Wis 7:25–26) standing behind the radiance-theology the fathers of Nicaea (325) used to articulate the Son's consubstantiality, the Creed's "Light from Light." The verdict splits by form. The modest core is sound and already on this timeline: a deuterocanonical book supplied the radiance/effluence imagery of the Nicene definition, and Athanasius, defending the council in De Decretis, both grounds its language in that theology and names a Wisdom-derived formula (Theognostus's "an effluence of the Father's essence," from Wisdom 7:25) as its received pedigree — while citing Wisdom, Sirach, and Tobit in his own voice to settle doctrine; so Jerome's rule that these books serve "not… for confirming… ecclesiastical dogmas" describes his restriction, not the church's practice. But the maximal conciliar form overstates: "Light from Light" is no verbatim quotation of Wisdom (its nearer proof-text is the Wisdom-derived Hebrews 1:3); the evidence is Athanasius's testimony to the council's reasoning, not a conciliar act of citing or canonizing Wisdom; and the auxiliary "Nicaea listed Judith as canonical" rests on Jerome's contested second-hand report (legitur), a seedbed of the "Nicaea fixed the canon" myth. The deuterocanon did do doctrinal work at the church's foundational council — that it did so as a formal conciliar proof-text is more than the record shows.175
