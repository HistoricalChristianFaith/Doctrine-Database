The Old Testament Canon (Is the Deuterocanon Scripture?)

Is Tobit — and with it Judith, Wisdom, Sirach (Ecclesiasticus), Baruch, and 1–2 Maccabees, the books Catholics call the deuterocanon and Protestants the Apocrypha — part of the Christian Old Testament? For the first fifteen centuries there was no single fixed list to settle it, and the answer was given differently across time. The Greek Septuagint that the early church inherited included these books; many Fathers who knew the Hebrew (Melito, Origen, Cyril, Jerome) counted a narrower, Hebrew-sized canon and ranked the extra books below it; the African councils, Rome, Florence, Trent, and the Eastern Synod of Jerusalem affirmed them; the Reformers and the Reformed confessions excluded them. This page traces those YES / NO verdicts across the centuries, earliest first, each footnoted to its source.

The question ties into two neighboring doctrines. Because the disputed books reached the church through the Greek Bible, the case turns partly on the standing of that translation — the Origin and Inspiration of the Septuagint — and on the Septuagint-versus-Hebrew quarrel gathered on the Rabbinic Corruption of Scripture page. (The companion Biblical Canon timeline treats the parallel question for the New Testament.) And it matters doctrinally: two of the disputed books carry proof-texts the Reformation contested — Tobit's "almsgiving saves from death" (Tobit 12:9) and 2 Maccabees' prayer for the dead (2 Macc 12:43–45), weighed in the Arguments below as they are added.

Timeline

The Septuagint-to-Reformation timeline is in progress; it runs from the LXX (c. 132 BC) toward the councils and confessions and the Synod of Jerusalem (1672). It opens with a framing note on the test case — Tobit — before the witnesses proper.

The test case: dating Tobit (composed c. 200 BC)

The dispute turns on a handful of books, and Tobit is the convenient bellwether, named on nearly every list below. Its own story is set in the eighth century BC: Tobit is a pious Israelite carried off to Nineveh in the Assyrian deportation that followed the fall of the northern kingdom (c. 722 BC).1 But scholars date the book itself far later — to roughly 200 BC (a range of about 225–175 BC), on the absence of any trace of the Maccabean persecution and on geographical slips that betray an author writing centuries after the events he narrates.2 That gap between narrative setting and date of writing is not peculiar to a "disputed" book: it is the very gap critical scholarship posits for canonical Daniel, whose sixth-century-BC court tales are widely held to reach their final form only in the Hellenistic age (c. 165 BC).3 So the deuterocanon's lateness, by itself, does not set it apart from every protocanonical book — which is why the canon question is settled below not by date of composition but by reception, list by list.

The Septuagint (c. 132 BC): Yes — inclusion

The timeline opens not with a verdict but with a fact: the Greek Old Testament the early church inherited — the Septuagint — already contained Tobit, together with Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch, and 1–2 Maccabees. The three great fourth- and fifth-century Christian Bibles (Codices Vaticanus, Sinaiticus, and Alexandrinus) all carry Tobit among the Old Testament books, interleaved with the protocanonical ones rather than set apart.4 This Greek Bible was "the most frequently used version of the Old Testament in the early Church,"5 and the New Testament's own scriptural citations follow it overwhelmingly — Archer and Chirichigno count 340 quotations agreeing with the Septuagint against only 33 with the (later-named) Masoretic Hebrew.6 So the disputed books were inside the church's working Bible from the outset: the YES / NO verdicts below are arguments over whether to keep what the Greek Scriptures already held, not whether to add something new. (How that Greek Bible came to be reckoned authoritative is the subject of the companion Origin and Inspiration of the Septuagint timeline; it is not re-argued here.)

The "Council of Jamnia" (c. 90 AD, Judaism): No — but no council

The Jewish "No" to the deuterocanon is real but harder to pin to a date than the old story allowed. That story is the Council of Jamnia: a hypothesis Heinrich Graetz floated in 1871, that a rabbinic council sat at Yavneh about 70–90 AD and there closed the Hebrew canon, shutting out the Greek extras in reaction to the rise of Christianity.7 Twentieth-century scholarship has largely abandoned it: Jack P. Lewis's "What Do We Mean by Jabneh?" (1964) showed that "canonicity was not the issue and the debate was not connected with Jabneh… No book is ever mentioned in the sources as being excluded from the canon at Jabneh," and F. F. Bruce judged it "probably unwise to talk as if there were a Council or Synod of Jamnia."8 Strip the legend away and a genuine uncertainty remains — "there is no scholarly consensus as to when the canon of the Hebrew Bible… was fixed," with proposals ranging from the Hasmonean period (c. 140–40 BC) to the second century AD and later.9 What is not in doubt is the result: the Pharisaic, proto-rabbinic Scriptures that hardened into the later Jewish canon never carried Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch, or 1–2 Maccabees.10 So the verdict is double-edged: the rabbinic canon excluded these books, but the sharp conciliar exclusion often cited against them did not happen as described. (The broader Septuagint-versus-Hebrew quarrel is gathered on the Rabbinic Corruption of Scripture page and not re-argued here.)

Clement of Rome (c. 96 AD): Yes — earliest practical use

Before any Christian canon-list exists, the disputed books are already at work in the church's own argument. Writing from Rome to quell a schism at Corinth, Clement — whose letter, 1 Clement, is among the earliest post-apostolic writings, usually dated to about 95–96 AD11 — weaves a line of the deuterocanonical Wisdom of Solomon into his appeal in the same breath as the Gospel and the Psalms: "Who shall say unto Him, What hast thou done? or, Who shall resist the power of His strength?" — the words of Wisdom 12:12 and 11:21, set beside Matthew 24:35 and Psalm 19 with no mark of distinction.12 Later he holds up "the blessed Judith," who, "exposing herself to danger," went out to the enemy camp for her besieged people, paired with Esther as a pattern of faith.13 The two citations differ in force — Wisdom is woven in as authority (though with no "it is written" formula), Judith adduced as a historical example — yet together they make 1 Clement the earliest Christian witness to the disputed books functioning as edifying, quotable Scripture, generations before Melito drew up the first Christian Old-Testament list. No canon-verdict is rendered here — the letter enumerates no books; it simply uses them — which is exactly why it bears on the question, weighed in the Arguments below, of whether the primitive church reckoned these books Scripture.

The Didache (c. 100 AD): Yes — an early practical use

Between Clement and the canon-lists stands an anonymous document witness: the Didache, or Teaching of the Twelve Apostles, the primitive church-order manual usually dated near AD 100 (estimates run c. 50–150).14 In its catalogue of moral duties it embeds a line of the deuterocanonical Sirach (Ecclesiasticus): "Be not a stretcher forth of the hands to receive and a drawer of them back to give" — the words of Sirach 4:31 — set in the same chain of commandments as a paraphrase of the apostolic charge to raise one's children "in the fear of God" (cf. Ephesians 6:4), with no mark of distinction.15 Like Clement and Polycarp, it renders no canon-list and prefaces the maxim with no "it is written"; the Sirach line is woven in as authoritative counsel, a third early datapoint of practical use before Melito drew up the first Christian Old-Testament list. One caveat weighs the force down: the moral section is a Christian recension of the Jewish "Two Ways" tradition (shared with the Epistle of Barnabas), and the Sirach verse stands inside that inherited block — so the echo may travel as common ethical tradition rather than a deliberate citation, a qualification carried into the Arguments below.

Polycarp of Smyrna (c. 125 AD): Yes — an early practical use

A generation after Clement, and still before any Christian canon-list, the disputed books surface again as edifying maxims in the church's own exhortation. Writing from Smyrna to the church at Philippi — a letter usually dated c. 110–140 AD16 — Polycarp urges, "When you can do good, defer it not, because alms delivers from death" — the saying of the deuterocanonical Tobit (Tob 4:10; 12:9), set in a chain of apostolic and prophetic phrases (1 Peter 2:12, 17 and 5:5; Isaiah 52:5) with no mark of distinction.17 The force should not be overstated: Polycarp renders no canon-list and prefaces the maxim with no "it is written"; the Tobit line is woven in as authoritative counsel, not adduced as a proof-text, and the chapter survives only in a later Latin version. Yet the seamless weave — a deuterocanonical maxim braided with 1 Peter and Isaiah — makes Polycarp a second early witness, beside Clement, to the disputed books functioning as quotable, edifying Scripture generations before Melito drew up the first Christian Old-Testament list — the practical "use" datapoint weighed in the Arguments below.

Melito of Sardis (c. 170 AD): No

The earliest Christian list of the Old Testament books comes from Melito, bishop of Sardis, who travelled East "to the place where these things were preached" to learn "accurately the books of the Old Testament" and sent the catalogue to his friend Onesimus; Eusebius preserved it verbatim. It is a Hebrew-sized canon: Tobit, Judith, Sirach, Baruch, and 1–2 Maccabees are all absent (so too Esther), and it closes at "Esdras."18 The one word that might name a deuterocanonical book — "Wisdom," set right after "the Proverbs of Solomon" — is on the usual reading a second ancient title for Proverbs itself (the "all-virtuous Wisdom"), not the deuterocanonical Book of Wisdom, so the list most likely carries no disputed book at all.19 Melito heads the long line of Hebrew-minded Fathers (Origen, Cyril, Athanasius, Jerome) who counted a narrower canon than the Greek Septuagint the church read in worship.

The Muratorian Fragment (c. 170 AD): Yes — Wisdom only

The same years yield a Western witness pointing the other way. The Muratorian Fragment — the oldest surviving list of New Testament books — counts, in the middle of its New-Testament reckoning, "the [book of] Wisdom, written by the friends of Solomon," ranking the deuterocanonical Wisdom of Solomon among the received writings.20 That makes it the earliest Western witness that a disputed Old-Testament book was received as Scripture — but it is a cross-over witness, not an OT-list: it names only Wisdom (not Tobit, Judith, Sirach, Baruch, or Maccabees), and it files the book oddly among the New Testament, a sign of how unsettled the line between the Testaments still was.21 Set beside Melito's contemporary list — whose "Wisdom" is most likely a second name for Proverbs — the two witnesses reach opposite verdicts on the very same word.

Athenagoras of Athens (c. 177 AD): Yes — Baruch quoted as the inspired voice of the prophets

A few years before Irenaeus, an Athenian philosopher-convert presses a disputed book into service as prophecy. Defending Christians against the charge of atheism in his Plea for the Christians (c. 177), addressed to the emperors Marcus Aurelius and Commodus, Athenagoras calls the Hebrew prophets — "Moses or… Isaiah and Jeremiah, and the other prophets," who "lifted in ecstasy… by the impulses of the Divine Spirit, uttered the things with which they were inspired" — and strings together their utterances. The first he quotes, "The Lord is our God; no other can be compared with Him," is not protocanonical but the deuterocanonical Baruch (Baruch 3:35–36), set among the inspired words of the prophets beside three sayings of Isaiah (44:6; 43:10–11; 66:1).22 He does not name Baruch — he gathers the chain under "the prophets," and Baruch was reckoned in the Greek tradition an appendix to Jeremiah, whom he does name — so this is firmer than a woven maxim (the line is expressly classed among words spoken "by the… Divine Spirit") yet short of an explicit canon-list, a notch below Irenaeus's named "Jeremiah the prophet… saying" formula for the same book.23 A contemporary of Melito's narrower list, he renders no canon-verdict; he simply uses a disputed book as the inspired voice of the prophets — the datapoint weighed in the Arguments below.

Irenaeus of Lyons (c. 180 AD): Yes — the disputed books cited as prophetic Scripture

A decade after Melito's narrower list, and from the other direction, the great anti-Gnostic bishop of Lyons quotes the disputed books not as edifying maxims but as the words of named prophets. In Irenaeus's Against Heresies (c. 175–189) the Greek addition to Daniel — the story of Susanna — is cited as words "to be found in Daniel the prophet": "O thou seed of Canaan, and not of Judah, beauty hath deceived thee…" (Susanna 56, 52).24 And a long passage of the deuterocanonical Baruch (Baruch 4:36–5:9) is adduced as genuine prophecy — "Jeremiah the prophet has pointed out… saying, 'Look around Jerusalem towards the east…'" — Baruch being reckoned in the Greek and Latin tradition an appendix to Jeremiah and routinely cited under his name.25 Where Clement, the Didache, and Polycarp braid the disputed books in without a citation formula, Irenaeus — a hearer of Polycarp, himself a disciple of the apostle John — gives them the explicit "the prophet" attribution, the firmest second-century use of the disputed books as Scripture before a Christian Old-Testament list. He renders no canon-verdict (his work enumerates no books); he simply uses them as prophecy — the datapoint weighed in the Arguments below, on whether the primitive church reckoned these books Scripture.

Clement of Alexandria (c. 200 AD): Yes — the disputed books cited with the "Scripture" formula

With the head of the Alexandrian catechetical school the practical use hardens into an explicit citation formula. A generation after Irenaeus, Clement (c. 150–215; his Paedagogus and Stromateis written c. 198–203) quotes four of the disputed books the way he quotes Moses or the Gospel — introduced as “Scripture.” He cites Sirach 21:6 as “the Scripture which says, ‘And he that feareth the Lord will turn to his heart’”; Tobit 12:8 as “the Scripture which says, ‘Fasting with prayer is a good thing’”; Wisdom 2:12 as “Scripture… when it says, ‘Let us remove the righteous man from us’”; and a long passage of Baruch (3:16–19) as the words of “the Divine Scripture.”26 He also strings Judith 8:27 (“he that is near the Lord is full of stripes”) into a chain with Galatians 3:24, Isaiah 5:21, and Proverbs 1:7, a use closer to Clement of Rome's woven maxims than to the explicit-formula citations.27 Where Irenaeus ascribed Susanna and Baruch to named prophets, Clement goes a step further in form — the bare word “Scripture” / “the Divine Scripture,” the same label he reserves for the protocanonical books — making him the firmest pre-list witness yet to the disputed books reckoned as Scripture, weighed in the Arguments below. He renders no canon-list; he simply cites these books as Scripture, a working “Yes” alongside the narrower Hebrew lists of Melito and (a generation on) Origen, his own successor at Alexandria.

Hippolytus of Rome (c. 205 AD): Yes — the disputed books read as prophecy of Christ

In the same decades, and from the West, the most learned teacher of the Roman church puts two of the disputed books to work as prophetic Scripture. Hippolytus (c. 170–235) opens his Expository Treatise Against the Jews by adducing — against his Jewish interlocutor — “the prophecy of Solomon, which speaketh of Christ”: the deuterocanonical Wisdom of Solomon (2:1, 12–20), the foretelling of the plot against the righteous one who “calleth himself the Child of God,” introduced with “the prophet says” and read as a Solomonic prophecy of the Passion.28 And in Against Noetus he expounds the deuterocanonical Baruch (3:35–37) — “This is our God… afterward did He show Himself upon earth, and conversed with men” — as a genuine prophecy of the incarnation; the “Thus it is written” formula is on the lips of his Noetian opponents, but Hippolytus does not contest the citation’s scriptural standing, granting it (“That said he rightly”) and disputing only whom it speaks of.29 Where Irenaeus ascribed Susanna and Baruch to named prophets and Clement labelled the books “Scripture,” Hippolytus reads Wisdom as the very “prophecy of Solomon” concerning Christ — a Western witness, between Clement and Origen, on the “using” side of the divide. He renders no canon-verdict; he simply uses the disputed books as prophecy — the datapoint weighed in the Arguments below.

Tertullian (c. 210 AD): Yes — the disputed books used, as prophecy and as example

The first great Latin theologian, writing from Carthage, puts the disputed books to work in the same African church that would later enshrine them in conciliar form. Tertullian (c. 155 – c. 220) quotes the deuterocanonical Letter of Jeremiah (Baruch 6:3) as the very “words of Jeremias,” set beside Daniel 3 in his exhortation to martyrdom: the three youths “remembered also the words of Jeremias writing to those over whom that captivity was impending… Beware, therefore, that ye also do not be altogether like the foreigners… but say in your mind, Our duty is to worship Thee, O Lord.”30 He ranks the Wisdom of Solomon among “our Christian authorities,” reading of God as “the searcher and witness of the heart” (Wisdom 1:6) in a chain with Proverbs, Matthew, the Psalms, Paul, and 1 John — though that particular citation is disputed: Roger Beckwith reads the phrase as Psalm 139:23 rather than Wisdom, and Tertullian’s firmer, undisputed use of the book comes in the Prescription against Heretics, where “the porch of Solomon” is said to have “taught that ‘the Lord should be sought in simplicity of heart’” — the opening words of Wisdom 1:1.31 And he twice adduces deuterocanonical figures as patterns of holiness: “a Judith, daughter of Merari” stands among “so many other examples of saints” beside Isaac and John,32 and Susanna’s modest unveiling at her trial is set beside Rebecca’s veil — though these are historical examples, not cited authorities.33 The force is graded: Baruch is quoted under the prophet’s name as the others quote “Jeremiah,” Wisdom adduced (with one disputed text) as a “Christian authority,” Judith and Susanna only as saintly examples. Like Irenaeus and Hippolytus he renders no canon-list; he simply uses the disputed books — the earliest Latin witness to that practice, a generation before the Greek-side “No” of Origen, and the datapoint weighed in the Arguments below.

Origen of Alexandria (c. 240 AD): No — but keeps the Greek

The greatest biblical scholar of the early church returns the timeline to a Hebrew-sized canon. In his Commentary on Psalm 1, quoted by Eusebius, Origen lists the Old Testament books "as the Hebrews have handed them down" as twenty-two, the count of the Hebrew letters — Tobit, Judith, Sirach, and the Wisdom of Solomon all absent, and the Maccabees named only to be set "besides these."34 Yet the same Origen will not surrender the longer Greek text the churches actually read: answering Julius Africanus, who challenged the story of Susanna as no genuine part of Daniel, he refuses to "reject as spurious the copies in use in our Churches" and appeals to the Providence that gave those Scriptures to "all the Churches of Christ."35 So his verdict is double, and honestly so: a "No" by the Hebrew enumeration, a working "keep" in the church's practice — and more than a keep, for across his homilies and treatises Origen quotes Tobit, Sirach, Baruch, the Maccabees, Wisdom, Judith, and Susanna outright as "Scripture," "holy Scripture," or what "is written." It is the very distinction between canonical and read in the churches that Origen hands forward to Athanasius, Rufinus, and Jerome.36

Cyprian of Carthage (c. 253 AD): Yes — the disputed books cited as “Holy Scripture”

From the Latin West, and with the fullest formulae yet, the bishop and martyr of Carthage cites the disputed books exactly as he cites the Law and the Gospel. A generation after his fellow Carthaginian Tertullian, and a contemporary of Origen in the East, Cyprian (c. 200–258, bishop of Carthage from c. 248, martyred under Valerian) quotes Tobit 12:8 as what “Holy Scripture instructs us, saying” — “Prayer is good with fasting and almsgiving”37 Sirach 2:1, 4–5 as what “Holy Scripture teaches and forewarns, saying,”38 and the Wisdom of Solomon 3:4–8 — the martyrs “tried… as gold in the furnace” — as “the sacred Scripture.”39 He gathers 1 Maccabees 2:62–63 under “Holy Scripture meets and warns us, saying,”40 ascribes the deuterocanonical Letter of Jeremiah (Baruch 6:6) to “the Holy Spirit… by Jeremiah,”41 and quotes the Greek addition to Daniel (Bel and the Dragon 14:5) as the inspired word of Daniel “filled with the Holy Spirit.”42 He draws up no canon-list, so he renders no canon-verdict; but these are the same labels he reserves for Moses, the Psalms, and the Gospel, making him the strongest pre-Nicene Western witness to the disputed books read outright as Scripture — in the very African church whose councils would later enshrine the longer canon, the Latin counterpart to Origen’s Greek-side practice. The datapoint is weighed in the Arguments below.

Dionysius of Alexandria (c. 264 AD): Yes — the disputed books used as inspired Scripture

The Greek East supplies a near-contemporary of Cyprian from the very school that produced the era's most careful canon-list. Dionysius (c. 190–264), pupil and successor of Origen as head of the Alexandrian catechetical school and bishop of Alexandria from c. 248, twice presses a disputed book into service as inspired Scripture. In his treatise On Nature, arguing against the Epicurean doctrine that the ordered cosmos arose by chance from random atoms, he answers the philosophers by appeal to Sirach: “listen to the divine oracles: ‘The works of the Lord are in judgment…’” (Sirach 16:26–27) — the deuterocanonical wisdom book introduced as ta theia logia, the very category of inspired utterance.43 And in his doctrinal Epistle to Dionysius, Bishop of Rome, defending the Son's eternity against Sabellianism, he glosses the deity of Christ by reading the Wisdom of Solomon's “He is the breath of God's power” (Wisdom 7:25) alongside John 4:24, “God is a Spirit,” to ground the title “Breath” for Christ — the Wisdom text handled exactly as the Johannine one beside it.44 Where his master Origen rendered a double verdict — a Hebrew-sized canon by enumeration, a working “keep” of the books read in the churches — the pupil supplies a clean instance of that second, “using” half: the next Alexandrian generation still citing the disputed books as the inspired word, with no list set against it. He renders no canon-verdict; he simply uses them — the Greek-East datapoint, beside Cyprian's Latin one, weighed in the Arguments below.

Methodius of Olympus (c. 290 AD): Yes — Wisdom & Sirach handled as Scripture

A second Greek witness, from outside the Alexandrian school, joins Dionysius on the “using” side. Methodius (c. 290; martyred c. 311), bishop of Olympus in Lycia and a pointed early critic of Origen, twice handles a disputed book as Scripture. In his major work, the Banquet of the Ten Virgins, debating the divine ordering of every birth, he brings forward Wisdom of Solomon 3:16 with the bare formula “the Scripture which says”: “As for the children of the adulterers, they shall not come to their perfection” — the deuterocanonical text introduced under the unqualified label he reserves for the protocanon.45 And the Oration on Simeon and Anna — a work transmitted under his name but of contested authenticity — ranges Sirach 16:6, “Amongst the disobedient people shall the fire burn,” under “as it is written,” in the same breath as Psalm 18:8.46 That his anti-Origenist pen reaches so naturally for the disputed books shows the “using” practice was no Alexandrian peculiarity but the common Greek instinct, even among Origen’s critics. He draws up no canon-list, so renders no canon-verdict; the datapoint is weighed in the Arguments below.

Lactantius (c. 310 AD): Yes — Sirach cited as “Solomon’s” testimony to the Word of God

On the eve of Nicaea, the first Latin apologist for the faith presses a disputed book into christological service. Lactantius (c. 250 – c. 325), the rhetorician Constantine would set over his son’s education, builds in his Divine Institutes (c. 303–311) a case for the eternal Word made flesh and calls “Solomon” as its Old-Testament witness: “I came forth out of the mouth of the Most High before all creatures… and covered the whole earth with a cloud” — the words of the deuterocanonical Sirach (Ecclesiasticus 24:3–4), the self-speech of personified Wisdom — set immediately beside John 1:1, “In the beginning was the Word.”47 The ascription to “Solomon” is itself a tell: Sirach, written by Jesus ben Sira, circulated among the “books of Solomon” and was cited under his name, so the mistake confirms rather than weakens the witness — Lactantius reaches for the text precisely because he reckons it inspired Solomonic testimony, fit to gloss the Gospel’s prologue. The use is doctrinal, not merely edifying: the deuterocanonical verse carries probative weight for the deity and creative agency of the Word, in the manner of Hippolytus reading Wisdom as “the prophecy of Solomon which speaketh of Christ.” Like the witnesses before him he renders no canon-list; he simply uses a disputed book as prophecy of Christ — a Western, Constantinian-era datapoint, beside the Greek-East Dionysius, weighed in the Arguments below.

Eusebius of Caesarea (c. 320 AD): Both at once — transmits the Hebrew lists, yet cites the disputed books as Scripture

The single figure who most embodies the era's tension stands on the eve of Nicaea. Eusebius (c. 260–339), bishop of Caesarea and father of church history, is the very channel through which the two great Hebrew-sized lists reach us — he preserves Melito's catalogue (Ecclesiastical History iv.26) and Origen's "twenty-two, as the Hebrews have handed them down" (vi.25), the documentary backbone of the narrower-canon case.48 Yet in his own apologetic works he uses the disputed books as Scripture. In the Praeparatio Evangelica (c. 313–320), having quoted the deuterocanonical Wisdom of Solomon — "she reacheth from end to end with full strength, and ordereth all things graciously" (Wisdom 8:1) — among Solomon's words, he caps the citation "Thus the Scripture speaks," then sets Philo's lesser clarity beside it: the bare label "the Scripture" for a disputed book, the same he gives the protocanon.49 In the same work he adduces the Greek addition to Daniel as proof "from Scripture" of God's foreknowledge — "the description by Susanna of God as knowing all things before they come to pass" (Susanna 42) — though here he is transcribing, with approval, a long extract from Origen rather than writing in his own voice.50 And in the Demonstratio Evangelica (c. 318–323), expounding the incarnation, he quotes the deuterocanonical Baruch (3:29–37) — "Afterward did he show himself upon earth, and conversed with men" — and pronounces: "I need add nothing to these inspired words, which so clearly support my argument," reading Baruch exactly as Hippolytus had, as genuine prophecy of Christ.51 He compiles no Old-Testament canon-list of his own, so renders no personal verdict; but the contemporary who hands the church its sharpest Hebrew-canon evidence is, in his own practice, on the "using" side of the divide — the canonical-versus-read-in-the-churches tension of his master Origen embodied in one man, and the datapoint weighed in the Arguments below.

The Council of Nicaea (325 AD): No recorded ruling — but a Judith report

The first ecumenical council published no canon-list. Its twenty surviving canons and synodal letter concern the Arian and Melitian schisms, the lapsed, the date of Easter, and clerical discipline — not the books of Scripture.52 It belongs on this timeline for one reason: it is the earliest council to which a verdict on a deuterocanonical book has ever been attributed. Eighty years later Jerome reports, in his Preface to Judith, that the council "is found… to have been counted" the deuterocanonical book of Judith "among the number of the Sacred Scriptures," and gives that as his reason for translating a book he otherwise ranked below the canon.53 But the report will not carry a Nicene canon-decree: Jerome himself, in the very same prefaces, rules Judith out of the canon, and Athanasius — who was present at Nicaea — still placed Judith among the books "not… included in the Canon, but appointed by the Fathers to be read" in 367.54 The full adversarial weighing of the claim is set out on its own argument page — assessed weak for the decree-reading, while the modest reading (that Nicaea merely cited Judith as Scripture in argument) is plausible.55

Cyril of Jerusalem (c. 350 AD): No — but Baruch kept

Catechizing the candidates for baptism at Jerusalem, Cyril recites an Eastern Old-Testament list of twenty-two books, the Hebrew-letter count, and draws the line sharply: "have nothing to do with the apocryphal writings… study earnestly these only which we read openly in the Church." Tobit, Judith, Sirach, the Wisdom of Solomon, and 1–2 Maccabees are all absent.56 Yet the list is not the bare Hebrew thirty-nine: Cyril's single book of Jeremiah is reckoned "including Baruch and Lamentations and the Epistle" — two short deuterocanonical works folded in as appendices, exactly as the Greek manuscripts carried them — and he credits the whole Hebrew-sized canon to the Septuagint's seventy-two translators.57 So his "No" to the disputed books keeps Baruch inside a canonical book, the same posture — rank by the Hebrew count, read from the Greek — that Origen had already shown and that Athanasius and Epiphanius will repeat.

Hilary of Poitiers (c. 360 AD): No

The first Latin Father to set out a canon-list returns the Eastern verdict in the West. In the prologue to his Tractatus super Psalmos (Expositions of the Psalms) Hilary reckons the Old Testament at twenty-two books, "according to the number of the [Hebrew] letters" — Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, and the Maccabees absent from the count — recording only that "to this some add Tobit and Judith to make twenty-four books." A Westerner who had spent his exile among the Greek bishops, he carries the Hebrew enumeration of Origen and Cyril into Latin a generation before Jerome would make that count the West's standard — though Hilary, like the others, still quotes the disputed books in his own argument, ranking by the Hebrew list while reading from the wider Greek Bible.58

The Cheltenham List (c. 360 AD): Yes — an early African list with the deuterocanon

From the same years, and the other direction, comes an anonymous Latin catalogue — the Cheltenham (or Mommsen) List, a stichometric table of the canonical books with their line-counts, most likely North African. Its Old Testament runs out the longer canon: the two books of Maccabees, Tobit, and Judith are enumerated among the Scriptures with the rest, and the whole is sealed by the Apocalypse's "four and twenty elders."59 It is a Western witness to the deuterocanon a full generation before the African councils of Hippo and Carthage gave that same canon conciliar form — the reception Augustine would later state as a rule.

Athanasius of Alexandria (367 AD): No — but Baruch kept, in three tiers

The most influential Eastern voice of the century sorts the books into a famous three-tier scheme. In his thirty-ninth Festal Letter Athanasius first lists the Old-Testament canon as the Hebrew twenty-two — Tobit, Judith, Sirach, the Wisdom of Solomon, and the Maccabees all absent — though his Jeremiah, like Origen's and Cyril's, carries Baruch and the Epistle of Jeremiah folded in.60 Below the canon he sets a second rank — books "not indeed included in the Canon, but appointed by the Fathers to be read… for instruction in the word of godliness" — and there he files the deuterocanon proper: the Wisdom of Solomon, Sirach, Judith, Tobit, and (strikingly) even the protocanonical Esther; below that again stand the "apocryphal writings… an invention of heretics."61 So his "No" is graded, not flat: the disputed books are not Scripture, yet not heretical either — "appointed by the Fathers to be read," the very distinction Origen had drawn, now made an explicit list that Rufinus and Jerome will carry forward. (The same letter gives the first New-Testament catalogue identical to the present twenty-seven, treated on his New Testament canon page.)

Amphilochius of Iconium (c. 380 AD): No

An Eastern bishop, friend of the Cappadocians, casts the canon into verse. In his Iambics for Seleucus Amphilochius warns that "not every book is safe which has acquired the venerable name of Scripture" — some are "spurious and utterly unsafe, like counterfeit… coins" — and then recites a Hebrew-sized Old Testament that omits Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, and the Maccabees, and gives even Esther only as a book "some approve."62 It is the Hebrew-canon posture of his fellow Cappadocian Gregory of Nazianzus, set to meter for memorizing.

The Apostolic Canons (c. 380 AD): Yes — Judith, three Maccabees, Sirach commended

The Eastern church's own canon-law collection returns a "Yes" of singular shape. Canon 85, the closing canon of the Antiochene Apostolic Canons appended to the Apostolic Constitutions, bids "the following books be esteemed venerable and holy by all of you" and lists, among the Old Testament, "[one of Judith;] three of the Maccabees," and urges the young to learn "the Wisdom of the learned Sirach."63 Its canon runs wider than the later Catholic deuterocanon — it receives three books of Maccabees, not two — yet it names neither Tobit nor the Wisdom of Solomon: a reminder that the Eastern "longer" canon was never a single fixed list. (The same canon's New-Testament reckoning omits Revelation and adds 1–2 Clement.)

The Council of Rome (382 AD): Yes — but on a sixth-century document

The first Latin council-list to run out the full deuterocanon is ascribed to the Roman synod that met under Pope Damasus I in 382. Its canon-catalogue — the so-called "Damasine list" — gives the longer Septuagint Old Testament, filing Tobit, Judith, and 1–2 Maccabees among "the Histories" and Wisdom and Ecclesiasticus (Sirach) among the books of Solomon: the whole disputed deuterocanon received as Scripture, the same canon the African councils would enact a decade later.64 But the verdict comes with a sharp caveat about its act: the list survives in no fourth-century text, only as a chapter of the Decretum Gelasianum, a compilation critical scholarship dates to the sixth century (this site c. 500). A real synod did meet at Rome in 382, and the list reflects the canon the late-fourth-century West received; but the claim that the canon was decreed there rests on a single later document and an eighteenth-century attribution, and Jerome — present at Rome, foremost on the canon — nowhere mentions such a decree. The documentary basis is weighed adversarially on its own argument page — assessed weak for the "fixed at Rome in 382" framing, while the modest reading (that Damasus's Rome simply received the deuterocanon, as the rest of the West did) is plausible.65

Epiphanius of Salamis (c. 385 AD): No — but Wisdom & Sirach "useful," Baruch kept

The great heresy-hunter of Salamis returns the Eastern verdict once more. In both the Panarion and On Weights and Measures, Epiphanius gives the Hebrew-sized canon — twenty-seven "recognized canonical books, but twenty-two when counted according to the letters of the alphabet of the Hebrews" — running them out in four "pentateuchs" plus Ezra and Esther, with Tobit, Judith, and the Maccabees all absent.66 But his "No" is graded, like Athanasius's before it: he names the two great Wisdom books — the Wisdom of Solomon and the Wisdom of Sirach — and rules them "of disputed canonicity," yet "helpful and useful," books that "were not kept in the chest, that is, in the ark of the covenant"; and his single Jeremiah carries Baruch and the Epistle of Jeremiah folded in, exactly as Origen, Cyril, and Athanasius had kept them — though he frankly grants "the epistles are not in use among the Hebrews."67

Gregory of Nazianzus (c. 389 AD): No — the narrowest list, even Esther dropped

The great Cappadocian theologian draws the Hebrew line hardest of all. His verse catalogue Concerning the Genuine Books of Divinely Inspired Scripture warns "let not extraneous books seduce your mind, for many malignant writings have been disseminated," and counts "twenty-two of the ancient books, corresponding to the number of the Hebrew letters."68 Not only are Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, and the Maccabees absent — Gregory's list is narrower even than his neighbours', omitting Esther as well, the same omission Athanasius had hesitated over. The poem became a standard Eastern measure of the canon, carried into the medieval Greek canon-law collections.

Jerome (c. 390 AD): No — the hardest line, even Baruch dropped

The man who gave the Latin West its Bible draws the sharpest "No" on the timeline. In the Prologus Galeatus — the "helmeted" preface to his translation from the Hebrew — Jerome fixes the Old Testament at the Hebrew twenty-two and rules that "whatever is outside of these is set aside among the apocrypha. Therefore, Wisdom… and the book of Jesus son of Sirach, and Judith and Tobias, and The Shepherd are not in the canon."69 He is harder even than the Eastern Fathers before him: where Cyril, Athanasius, and Epiphanius all folded Baruch into the canonical Jeremiah, Jerome omits it as "neither read nor found among the Hebrews," and it is he who supplies the very word — apocrypha — the later Protestant Bibles will use.69 Yet his "No" is not flat: at his bishops' command he translated Tobit and Judith anyway — Tobit "contrary to their canon," he grants, choosing "to be subject to the commands of bishops" over "the opinion of the Pharisees" — and he ranked Wisdom and Sirach, with Judith, Tobit, and the Maccabees, as books the Church may "read… for the strengthening of the people, but not for confirming the authority of ecclesiastical dogmas."70 That formula — read for edification, not to prove doctrine — is the one the Thirty-Nine Articles and the Westminster Confession will borrow, citing Jerome by name. (His Judith preface also carries the report that the Council of Nicaea "counted" Judith among the Scriptures — weighed, and found wanting as a decree, on its own argument page.71)

The African councils — Hippo & Carthage (393–419 AD): Yes — the deuterocanon in conciliar form

For the first time the boundary is drawn by a council. The North-African synods gathered around Augustine and Aurelius of Carthage — Hippo in 393 (canon 36, the earliest canon-list any church council promulgated), re-enacted at Carthage in 397 and reaffirmed in 419 — each enact the rule "that besides the Canonical Scriptures nothing be read in church under the name of divine Scripture," and then list the books. Their Old Testament is the longer Septuagint canon: Tobit, Judith, and the two books of Maccabees named outright among the Scriptures, and the deuterocanonical Wisdom of Solomon and Sirach carried in "the five books of Solomon."72 Where Athanasius a generation earlier had ranked exactly these books a tier below — "read" but "not in the canon" — the African lists level them up, placing them among the canonical books. The theological hand behind the lists is Augustine, whose On Christian Doctrine (2.8.13, c. 397) matches the conciliar canon book for book and states its criterion — to "prefer those that are received by all the catholic churches to those which some do not receive."73 These are regional synods ratifying a reception already in hand, not ecumenical decrees that created it — the Western counterpart to Athanasius's Eastern list, and the seed of the later Old-Testament dispute that runs to Florence, Trent, and the Reformation. (The same councils' New-Testament list is treated on the companion Biblical Canon page.)

Augustine of Hippo (c. 397): Yes — the full canon, on the breadth of its reception

The theological hand behind those councils sets out the same verdict under his own name. In On Christian Doctrine Augustine runs out a forty-four-book Old Testament that files Tobias, Judith, the two books of Maccabees, and the two of Ezra among the histories, and reckons Wisdom and Ecclesiasticus among the prophetical books — the longer Septuagint canon, with no second tier.74 What carries the disputed books in is not a Hebrew enumeration but a rule of reception: "to prefer those that are received by all the catholic churches to those which some do not receive," weighing a book by the breadth and antiquity of its use.75 That is the exact opposite test from Jerome's Hebraica veritas, and the disagreement between these two contemporaries is the fault line that runs to Trent and the Reformation. Late in life, in The City of God, Augustine meets the objection the Reformers would later press — that the Maccabees are not in the Hebrew Scriptures — and concedes it, only to turn it: those books "are held as canonical, not by the Jews, but by the Church, on account of the extreme and wonderful sufferings of certain martyrs."76 His canon rests not on the Jewish reckoning but on the church's reception — the Western, Septuagint-based canon that Trent would later fix as dogma.

Rufinus of Aquileia (c. 404): No — but "Ecclesiastical," read though not doctrinal

Rufinus gives the cleanest, most systematic version of the middle position. In his Commentary on the Apostles' Creed he sorts the books into three named ranks. The Canonical Old Testament he lists is the Hebrew-sized canon — Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, and the Maccabees all absent, and Jeremiah named without Baruch.77 Below it he sets a second tier, the books "our fathers call not 'Canonical' but 'Ecclesiastical'": the Wisdom of Solomon, the Wisdom of the Son of Sirach (Ecclesiasticus), Tobit, Judith, and the Maccabees — "all of which they would have read in the Churches, but not appealed to for the confirmation of doctrine"; and below that the Apocrypha, which "they would not have read in the Churches" at all.78 His operative formula — read in church, but not to prove doctrine — is almost word-for-word Jerome's, and the rank Athanasius had called "appointed by the Fathers to be read." On the canon these two famous antagonists agree: the real fault line of the age is not Rufinus-versus-Jerome but both of them against Augustine and the African councils, who level the disputed books up into the canon. Rufinus's three-fold scheme — Canonical, Ecclesiastical, Apocrypha — supplies the vocabulary the Thirty-Nine Articles and the Westminster Confession will later borrow.

Innocent I (c. 405): Yes — the deuterocanon received from the see of Rome

A sitting bishop of Rome restates the Western verdict. Answering a query from Exuperius, bishop of Toulouse, on which books "really are received in the canon," Innocent I appends a canon-list whose Old Testament is the longer Septuagint form: Tobit, Judith, and the two books of Maccabees named outright among "the historical books," and the deuterocanonical Wisdom and Ecclesiasticus (Sirach) carried in "the five books of Solomon."79 He files these books among the canon, with none of the "read but not canonical" qualification that Athanasius, Jerome, and Rufinus had attached to the very same titles — the Augustinian, reception-based posture rather than the Hebrew reckoning.80 Coming a decade after the African councils of Hippo and Carthage had drawn the same line, it is the clearest papal-level reception of the deuterocanon before Florence and Trent — a witness to a canon already in hand, not a decree that created it. (The same letter's New-Testament list, and its condemnation of the forged apostolic gospels, are treated on the companion Biblical Canon page.)

The Catalogue of Codex Claromontanus (c. 400 AD): Yes

A stichometric book-list, copied into the sixth-century Greek–Latin Codex Claromontanus but reflecting an older (c. 300–400) table, gives the longer canon with line-counts: Tobit, Judith, the Wisdom of Solomon, the Wisdom of Jesus (Sirach), and the books of Maccabees all stand among the Old Testament books.81 The list marks several disputed titles with a small dash — but those obeli fall on contested New-Testament books, not on the Old-Testament deuterocanon, which it carries without qualification: an early-Western "Yes" preserved, like the Damasine list, in a later codex.

The Synopsis of Sacred Scripture (c. 550 AD): No — but the deuterocanon "read," in Athanasius's tier

A handbook transmitted under Athanasius's name but later in date, the Synopsis Scripturae Sacrae repeats his graded verdict almost exactly. Its canonical Old Testament is the Hebrew-sized list, the disputed books absent; but it then sets out a second rank — books "not… canonical, but only read" to the catechumens — and there it files the deuterocanon: the Wisdom of Solomon, the Wisdom of Sirach, Esther, Judith, and Tobit.82 It is Athanasius's "appointed by the Fathers to be read" preserved into the sixth century — the disputed books neither canonized nor cast out, but kept as a middle, "read" rank.

Isidore of Seville (c. 625 AD): No — Jerome's count, with a "fourth class" read

The great encyclopedist of the Latin West restates Jerome's Hebrew canon. In the Etymologiae (VI.9) Isidore divides the Old Testament into the three Hebrew classes — Law, Prophets, Sacred Writings — totalling twenty-two books (some, he notes, add Ruth and Lamentations to make twenty-four), with the deuterocanon outside the count.83 But he then names a further class the Church "honors and proclaims" though it stands outside the Hebrew canon — Wisdom, Ecclesiasticus (Sirach), Tobit, Judith, and the Maccabees — the same graded posture, canonical-by-the-Hebrews / read-by-the-Church, that Rufinus and Jerome had handed him, carried into the medieval Latin schoolbooks.

Codex Amiatinus (c. 700 AD): Yes — the whole canon bound in one book

The oldest surviving complete Latin Vulgate in a single volume — a pandect made c. 700 at the Wearmouth–Jarrow monastery in Northumbria — embodies the longer canon physically: Codex Amiatinus carries Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, and the two books of Maccabees among its Old Testament books.84 Yet the same manuscript prints the disagreement in its own front matter: its opening leaves diagram three rival canon-divisions — Jerome's Hebrew-sized twenty-two, the Septuagint order, and Augustine's longer list — so the very codex that hands the West the full deuterocanon also preserves, on its first pages, the Hebrew-sized schemes that disputed it.

John of Damascus (c. 730 AD): No — "not counted… nor placed in the ark"

The last of the Greek Fathers gives the Eastern Hebrew canon its closing classic statement. In An Exposition of the Orthodox Faith (IV.17) John of Damascus arranges the twenty-two books in four "pentateuchs" plus two — the deuterocanon outside the count — and names the Wisdom of Solomon and the Wisdom of Sirach only to rule them "virtuous and noble, but… not counted nor… placed in the ark."85 It is the verdict of Epiphanius three centuries on, repeated almost word for word — the Eastern "No" still graded, still keeping the disputed books as honoured but uncanonical, two centuries before the Masoretic Text fixed the Hebrew table for good.

The Stichometry of Nicephorus (c. 810 AD): No — the deuterocanon ranked "disputed"

A line-count catalogue attached to the Chronography of Nicephorus, Patriarch of Constantinople, gives the last great Eastern list before the medieval Hebrew codices. Its canonical Old Testament is the Hebrew-sized protocanon (Baruch folded into Jeremiah); the deuterocanon is not omitted in silence but sorted into a named lower rank of antilegomena — "those spoken against" — where the books of Maccabees, the Wisdom of Solomon, Sirach, Esther, Judith, Susanna, and Tobit are each listed with their line-counts.86 It is the Synopsis's graded "No" made into a formal three-tier inventory — canonical, disputed, apocryphal — the Eastern position carried to the threshold of the Byzantine Middle Ages.

The Masoretic Text (c. 1000 AD, Judaism): No

The medieval Jewish standard text returns the Hebrew "No" in its definitive form. The Masoretic Text — the work of the Masoretes, scribal scholars of the seventh through tenth centuries who fixed the Hebrew Bible letter by letter, surviving in the tenth-century Aleppo Codex and the 1008/1009 Leningrad Codex — is "the authoritative Hebrew and Aramaic text of the 24 books of the Hebrew Bible (Tanakh) in Rabbinic Judaism," carrying the protocanonical books alone: Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch, and the Maccabees have no place in it.87 It did not decide the canon — it preserved the boundary the rabbinic stream had long since drawn (the "Council of Jamnia" marker) — but it gave that Hebrew-sized Scripture its permanent written form, and that matters for the Christian question because it is the text "most Protestant translations of the Old Testament" render: when the Reformers returned to the Hebrew against the Vulgate's longer canon, the Hebrew they returned to was the Masoretic Text, and its twenty-four-book table became the thirty-nine-book Protestant Old Testament.87 Its scribes' fidelity, long famed, was largely vindicated by the manuscript finds — "most of the Qumran fragments… closer to the Masoretic Text than to any other text group," and the En-Gedi Leviticus scroll (c. 3rd–4th century AD) found "completely identical with the consonantal framework of the Masoretic Text" — though faithful transmission of the Hebrew settles only what that tradition contained, not whether the church's canon should be measured by the Hebrew table or by the Greek Bible it read.88 (The Septuagint-versus-Hebrew quarrel itself is gathered on the Rabbinic Corruption of Scripture and Origin and Inspiration of the Septuagint pages and not re-argued here.)

The Council of Florence (1442): Yes — the first ecumenical enumeration of the full canon

After the long medieval lull the timeline reaches its first ecumenical verdict. In the bull Cantate Domino, the Bull of Union with the Copts, the Council of Florence professes that "one and the same God is the author of the old and the new Testament" and lists the books it "accepts and venerates" — and there, threaded into one undifferentiated list beside Genesis, Isaiah, and the Gospels, stand Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Ecclesiasticus (Sirach), Baruch, and the two books of Maccabees.89 It is the Augustinian, reception-based canon of the African councils and Innocent I — the disputed books levelled into the canon with none of the "read but not canonical" tier of Athanasius, Jerome, and Rufinus — now raised for the first time to the level of a general council. The canon-list sits inside a reunion profession, not a stand-alone canon-decree, and the union itself failed; but the verb is the receptive one the Council of Trent will repeat a century later (suscipit ac veneratur, "accepts and venerates"), so the Catholic answer to the charge that the deuterocanon was "added at Trent" rests here: Florence professed in 1442 the canon Trent would fix as dogma in 1546.90

Martin Luther (1534, Protestant): No — "not equal to Holy Scripture, yet useful and good to read"

The Reformation reopens the question Florence thought closed, and answers it with Jerome's "No" set in print. In his complete German Bible of 1534 Luther lifted Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch, and 1–2 Maccabees out of the Old Testament and gathered them — all but 1–2 Esdras, which he dropped — into a separate section between the Testaments under the heading "Apocrypha — that is, books which are not held equal to the Holy Scriptures, and yet are profitable and good to read."91 The layout is the verdict, and it is the graded "No" of the Hebrew-minded Fathers made visible on the page: in his prefaces he commends Judith as "a fine, good, holy, useful book, well worth reading by us Christians" and reads Tobit as edifying fiction of married life, while denying both the canonical rank — and in the Table Talk he files them by genre, Judith "a tragedy, setting forth what the end of tyrants is… like that of Homer about Troy," Tobit "a comedy concerning women, an example for house-government."92 Luther keeps the books in the Bible but out of the canon — the same posture as Rufinus and Athanasius, read for edification, not for proving doctrine — and his heading furnishes the very words the Thirty-Nine Articles and the Westminster Confession will take up. (His return to the Hebrew canon was not antiquarian: the demoted books carried the proof-texts — 2 Maccabees 12 for the dead, Tobit 12 for almsgiving — weighed in the Arguments below.)93

The Council of Trent (1546, Roman Catholic): Yes — the deuterocanon defined as canonical, under anathema

The Counter-Reformation answers Luther by closing the question as dogma. In its fourth session, the first to take up doctrine, the Council of Trent declares that it "receives and venerates with an equal affection of piety, and reverence, all the books both of the Old and of the New Testament," and prints the list — Tobias, Judith, Wisdom, Ecclesiasticus (Sirach), Baruch, and the two books of Maccabees threaded among the rest with no second tier — the same receptive verb (suscipit ac veneratur) the Council of Florence had used a century before.94 What sets Trent apart from every earlier "Yes" is the force: it anathematizes anyone who "receive not, as sacred and canonical, the said books entire with all their parts, as… contained in the old Latin vulgate edition" — the first time the reception running from the Septuagint through Augustine to Florence is made a binding test of communion, and "entire with all their parts" deliberately covers the Greek additions to Daniel and Esther.95 The Catholic answer to the charge that the deuterocanon was "added at Trent" rests on this distinction: Trent did not add the books but defined a canon long received. Yet even as it raised the seven disputed books to dogma, its list quietly dropped three texts the medieval Vulgate had carried — 3 and 4 Esdras and the Prayer of Manasses — which the Clementine Vulgate of 1592 then relegated to a non-canonical appendix.96

The Thirty-Nine Articles (1571, Church of England): No — read "for example of life," not to establish doctrine

The English Reformation answers Trent not with Luther's heading but with Jerome's rule, cited by name. Article VI of the Articles of Religion defines canonical Scripture as "those canonical Books of the Old and New Testament, of whose authority was never any doubt in the Church," lists the Hebrew-sized Old Testament book by book, and then files the deuterocanon in a separate rank: "the other Books (as Hierome saith) the Church doth read for example of life and instruction of manners; but yet doth it not apply them to establish any doctrine."97 The list of those "other Books" is wider than Trent's: it brackets Tobias, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch, and 1–2 Maccabees together with the Greek additions to Esther and Daniel and the three texts (3–4 Esdras and the Prayer of Manasses) that Trent itself had dropped — all alike read for edification, none admissible to prove doctrine.98 It is the soft, graded "No" of Jerome, Rufinus, and Luther — keep the books, refuse them as doctrine — the moderate Anglican line that the Westminster Confession would shortly harden into flat exclusion. (The cord it cuts is doctrinal: the disputed books carried the proof-texts — 2 Maccabees 12 for the dead, Tobit 12 for almsgiving — weighed in the Arguments below.)

The Westminster Confession of Faith (1647, Reformed): No — the hardest line: "of no authority… than other human writings"

The Reformed answer hardens the Anglican "No" into a flat exclusion. Chapter 1 of the Westminster Confession names the canon by inspiration — "all the books of the Old and New Testaments… All which are given by inspiration of God, to be the rule of faith and life" — and lists the Hebrew-sized thirty-nine Old Testament books (Genesis through Malachi) with the twenty-seven of the New.99 The next section then rules on the disputed books with a force no earlier Protestant confession had used: "The books commonly called Apocrypha, not being of divine inspiration, are no part of the canon of the Scripture; and therefore are of no authority in the Church of God, nor to be any otherwise approved, or made use of, than other human writings."99 Where Jerome, Rufinus, Luther, and the Thirty-Nine Articles had all kept the deuterocanon as edifying reading — read in church "for example of life," profitable "but not for confirming… doctrine" — Westminster removes that middle rank entirely, leveling the Apocrypha down to ordinary "human writings," the mirror image of Trent's leveling them up into the canon under anathema.100 It is the sharpest Protestant verdict on the timeline, and the one that shaped later Apocrypha-free Protestant Bibles. (The cord it cuts is again doctrinal: a book that may not be "made use of" otherwise than other human writings cannot found the contested doctrines its proof-texts were adduced for — 2 Maccabees 12 for the dead, Tobit 12 for almsgiving — weighed in the Arguments below.)

The Synod of Jerusalem (1672, Eastern Orthodox): Yes — the Eastern counterpart to Trent

The timeline closes in the East. A generation after Westminster, the Synod of Jerusalem — convened at Bethlehem by Patriarch Dositheus II to repudiate a Calvinizing confession that had circulated under the name of Cyril Lucaris, Patriarch of Constantinople — gave the Eastern Orthodox church its most authoritative modern ruling on the canon. The Confession of Dositheus names the books Lucaris had set aside as "Apocrypha" — the Wisdom of Solomon, Judith, Tobit, Bel and the Dragon, Susanna, the Maccabees, and Sirach — and declares them "genuine parts of Scripture," on a level "with the other genuine Books of Divine Scripture."101 Its ground is not the Hebrew table but the delivering tradition of the church — Augustine's reception-criterion pressed to a hard edge: the same church "which hath delivered to us as genuine the Sacred Gospels… hath undoubtedly delivered these also as parts of Scripture, and the denial of these is the rejection of those." The Confession candidly concedes the unevenness this timeline documents — "not always have all of these been considered on the same level as the rest" — and overrules it by the breadth of synodal and patristic use.102 It is the exact mirror of Westminster twenty-five years before: the two latest witnesses, both reacting to the same Reformation rupture, level the same books in opposite directions — down to "human writings" in the Reformed West, up to canonical Scripture in the Orthodox East — and the Eastern "Yes" is the counterpart to Trent's, reached by the same reception-over-Hebrew route. (Later Orthodox teaching softens the affirmation, distinguishing these anaginoskomena — "things that are read" — as a profitable class below the protocanonical books; but the 1672 decree remains the high-water mark of the Eastern reception, and it keeps the Maccabees' prayer for the dead and Tobit's almsgiving inside the Eastern canon — weighed in the Arguments below.)

Arguments

Sub-claims that cut across the timeline rather than sitting at one date — each weighed adversarially on its own page, with an assessment of the interpretation it serves. A pair of related arguments press the witness of the earliest church that the disputed books were no Scripture; another pair turn on the Gospel saying "from the blood of Abel to the blood of Zechariah" (Luke 11:51 // Matt 23:35) — whether it delimits the canon, and which Zechariah it names; another presses the Jewish people's custody of the Old Testament (Romans 3:2) toward the Hebrew canon; two more weigh the proof-texts the Reformation made the doctrinal ground of its "No"; another asks whether the books disclaim their own inspiration; one weighs the meta-question of where the burden of proof lies, given the deuterocanon's long place in the Vulgate; and an umbrella meta-argument asks whether the canon dispute itself, by leaving the table of contents unsettled, undermines sola scriptura. All are weighed here.

Related arguments — the primitive church's witness

Two Protestant removal arguments from antiquity: that the earliest church did not reckon the deuterocanon Scripture, so its later inclusion is an accretion. One presses the silence of the apostles (the New Testament never quotes the books as Scripture); the other presses the Hebrew-sized canon-lists of the Fathers (Melito to Jerome). They share one hinge — whether the earliest stratum of the church excluded these books, or whether a learned minority tiered books the wider church read and used. Best read together.

Does the New Testament's never quoting the deuterocanon as Scripture prove it is not canonical? (assessment: weak)

Does the New Testament's never quoting the deuterocanon as Scripture prove it is not canonical? — the stock Protestant argument from silence (scholarly form: Luther in instinct, Roger Beckwith in print): the New Testament cites most of the protocanon as Scripture with formulae like "it is written," but never a deuterocanonical book that way, so the apostles did not reckon these books inspired. The observation is sound — the citation asymmetry is real — but the inference to exclusion does not hold on its best case. It proves too much: the New Testament likewise never quotes Esther, Ecclesiastes, the Song of Songs, Ezra–Nehemiah, Obadiah, or Nahum, which no one drops. It is false to the data: the New Testament alludes to the deuterocanon repeatedly — Hebrews 11:35 ("tortured, not accepting deliverance … a better resurrection") to the martyrs of 2 Maccabees 7, Romans 1 to Wisdom 13–14, Romans 9:21 to Wisdom 15:7, Matthew 27:43 to Wisdom 2, James 1:19 to Sirach 5:11 — allusions the Nestle–Aland critical Greek New Testament itself registers. And "quotation as Scripture" is a fuzzy test the New Testament applies inconsistently, quoting 1 Enoch as prophecy (Jude 14–15) and pagan poets (Acts 17:28; Titus 1:12; 1 Cor 15:33) without thereby canonizing them. What survives is the narrower, true point that the apostles cite the protocanon as Scripture and the deuterocanon only by allusion — evidence for the graded standing the Hebrew-minded Fathers assigned these books, not for the flat exclusion the syllogism wants.103

Did the early church reject the deuterocanon as Scripture, making its later inclusion an accretion? (assessment: contested)

Did the early church reject the deuterocanon as Scripture, making its later inclusion an accretion? — the Protestant removal argument from the patristic canon-lists (Luther in instinct, Roger Beckwith in print): the earliest Christian lists — Melito, Origen, Cyril, Athanasius, Epiphanius, Jerome, Rufinus — are Hebrew-sized, omitting or tiering the disputed books, so the primitive church did not hold them Scripture. The early witness is genuinely divided, and the verdict turns on the form of the claim. The narrow, graded thesis is strong: a learned, Hebrew-canon-conscious stream did rank these books below a canon measured by the Hebrew table — the backbone of the Reformation's case. But the flat, universal claim does not hold: the church's working Bible was the Septuagint, which carried the books; the great codices interleave them with the protocanon; a parallel, equally early Western stream (the African councils, Augustine, Innocent I) received them among the Scriptures; and even the "excluders" read and cited them, tiering rather than rejecting (Origen's "copies in use in our Churches," Rufinus's "Ecclesiastical" books). So the deuterocanon is not a late addition but a long-disputed inheritance — tiered by a minority, used by the majority, as the Synod of Jerusalem itself conceded ("not always have all of these been considered on the same level as the rest"). No clear winner.104

Related arguments — Abel to Zechariah

Three arguments on a single Gospel saying — Jesus' "from the blood of Abel … to the blood of Zechariah" (Luke 11:51 // Matt 23:35). The first asks whether the saying delimits the canon (Genesis to Chronicles, excluding the Apocrypha); the other two test the premises on which the first depends — which Zechariah Jesus names (the "bookends" reading needs the Chronicles figure, son of Jehoiada, to be the closing "Z"), and whether the Genesis-to-Chronicles book-order the argument assumes was even established and dominant in Jesus' day. Best read together.

Does "from the blood of Abel to the blood of Zechariah" delimit the Hebrew OT canon, excluding the Apocrypha? (assessment: weak)

Does "from the blood of Abel to the blood of Zechariah" delimit the Hebrew Old Testament canon, excluding the Apocrypha? — the popular Protestant "A-to-Z" or "bookends" argument (scholarly form Roger Beckwith; popularly F. F. Bruce, the Reformation's Hebrew-canon instinct, Doug Wilson): because Chronicles stands last in the Hebrew order and records a Zechariah's murder, Jesus' phrase "from the blood of Abel … to the blood of Zechariah" (Luke 11:51 // Matt 23:35) traces the limits of the canon — Genesis to Chronicles — and so excludes Maccabees and the rest of the Apocrypha. That Jesus names the first and last martyrs of Old-Testament history, summing up the generation's guilt in a beginning-to-end merism, is sound and is how the whole church read the saying. But the canon-demarcation inference does not hold on its best case, failing on three independent counts. Reception: no interpreter on record read the saying as a canon-boundary until Eichhorn in 1780 — the unbroken tradition (Tertullian, Origen, Chrysostom, Jerome, Calvin, Lightfoot) read it of the first-to-last martyr, and Jerome, the great Hebrew-canon champion, never enlisted it for the canon. Book-order: Genesis-to-Chronicles is not securely the order Jesus knew — across the first-millennium canon-lists no Chronicles-last sequence is dominant (it standardizes only after 1207), and its one early Talmudic witness (Bava Batra 14b) sits beside material (Bava Kamma) that counts Sirach among the Writings. Identity: Matthew's "Zechariah son of Berechiah" does not fit the Chronicles figure (son of Jehoiada) without positing a scribal error or a name-conflation — a difficulty even proponents (Wilson, citing Gleason Archer) concede. What survives is the modest, universally-held martyr-merism, carrying at most a faint, order-dependent presumption toward a Hebrew-shaped canon — far short of the claim that the Apocrypha is here excluded; the Hebrew-canon case stands on stronger grounds elsewhere.105

Which Zechariah does Jesus name — is he the Chronicles figure the bookends argument needs? (assessment: contested)

Which Zechariah does Jesus name in "from the blood of Abel to the blood of Zechariah" — and is he the Chronicles figure the "bookends" argument needs? — the load-bearing sub-claim beneath the bookends argument: that the Zechariah Jesus names is Zechariah son of Jehoiada (2 Chron 24:20–22), the priest stoned in the temple court and so the "last martyr in the last book." The identification is the most defensible reading and probably right: his is the only temple-court martyrdom in inspired Scripture, it matches "between the sanctuary and the altar" exactly, and the most exacting interpreters (Jerome, Calvin, Lightfoot, Michaelis) settle there. But it is genuinely contested. Matthew's "son of Berechiah" (Matt 23:35) fits the priest (son of Jehoiada) only by an unattested second name, a textual emendation against every critical edition, an over-loose "descendant," or a name-conflation device whose use in Jesus' day is unproven; and a strong patristic stream (Origen, Basil, Gregory of Nyssa, Cyril of Alexandria) read the figure as the father of John the Baptist instead, with the prophet of Zechariah 1:1 and Josephus' son of Baruch as further candidates. So the sub-claim cannot simply be assumed; and even granted in full, identifying the Chronicles Zechariah shows only that Jesus named a martyr who stands in the last book of one Hebrew order — not that he was tracing the canon's edge. The likeliest identification, established, still does not carry the demarcation inference it was enlisted to support.106

Did the Hebrew canon end with Chronicles in Jesus' day — was a Genesis-to-Chronicles order established and dominant before the first century? (assessment: weak)

Did the Hebrew canon end with Chronicles in Jesus' day — was a Genesis-to-Chronicles order established and dominant before the first century? — the historical premise the bookends argument rests on (Roger Beckwith in print, leaning on the Talmud; Doug Wilson popularly: "the order … Jesus would have been familiar with"): that the Jews of Jesus' day arranged their Bible Genesis-to-Chronicles, Chronicles standing last, so that "Abel … to Zechariah" spans first book to last. Surveyed against the record, the strong form of that premise does not hold. Across the first-millennium canon-lists and codices no Chronicles-last order is dominant; the great majority close on Daniel, Ezra, Esther, or Maccabees (Melito, Origen, Cyril, Athanasius, Epiphanius, Jerome, Augustine, and the great codices among them), and the authoritative Tiberian Hebrew manuscripts — the Aleppo and Leningrad codices — put Chronicles first in the Writings, not last. The Chronicles-last arrangement becomes common only after the Adath Deborim (1207) and standard with print — and the Adath Deborim itself reports that Chronicles-first is the "right and true" order and that "the Babylonians have altered this order," moving Chronicles to the end. The one clearly early Jewish witness to a Chronicles-last order, the baraita at Bava Batra 14b, is genuine and is the steelman's proper foundation — but it stands nearly alone for a millennium and sits in a context (Bava Kamma citing Sirach as Writings; Epiphanius reporting Jews still disputing Sirach and Wisdom) that does not yield the clean Apocrypha-excluding canon the argument needs; and the other early Chronicles-last list, Innocent I's (405), includes the deuterocanon. To carry the premise one must hold that Bava Batra preserves a pre-Christian order Jesus knew, lost to history for a thousand years and recovered only after 1207 — possible, but unevidenced, and against the grain of the data. What survives is the modest point that a Chronicles-last order existed in some rabbinic stream; what fails is the load-bearing premise that it was the familiar shape of the Bible in Jesus' day.107

Does the deuterocanon dispute undermine sola scriptura — can Scripture settle its own table of contents? (assessment: contested)

Does the deuterocanon dispute undermine sola scriptura — can Scripture alone settle its own table of contents? — the umbrella meta-argument over the whole deuterocanon-removal case (Catholic apologists Devin Rose and Dave Armstrong in print; answered by R. C. Sproul and Michael Kruger): because Scripture contains no inspired list of its own books and the canon's edge is contested — this very timeline's fifteen-century dispute, with the Reformers removing seven books long bound in the Western Bible — a Protestant cannot, on Scripture alone, settle whether a doctrine (purgatory, say) is "biblical," so "the whole Protestant experiment fails." The underlying tension is real and serious: there is no inspired table of contents, the canon cannot be derived from Scripture, and thoughtful Protestants concede it (Sproul's "fallible collection of infallible books"). But the demolition overreaches. Marginal uncertainty over seven books does not dissolve the canon's common core or render doctrines undecidable — purgatory's deuterocanonical proof-text is weighed on its merits regardless; the Catholic alternative faces the same recognition-regress one level up and the awkward fact that its canon was dogmatically fixed only at Trent (1546), after the Reformation it answers; "removed" begs the Hebrew-canon question on which the Reformers claimed to be restoring; and the argument mistakes a claim about Scripture's authority for a claim that the canon is derived from Scripture, which no careful defender holds (the Spirit's witness and the church's ministerial reception recognize, not confer, the canon — Michael Kruger's self-authenticating model). The genuine, contested question is how the canon is recognized; the "experiment fails" leap is conjecture beyond it.108

Does Tobit 12:9 ("alms… shall purge away all sin") teach a works-righteousness that bars it from the canon? (assessment: weak)

Does Tobit 12:9 teach a works-righteousness that bars the book from the canon? — the reading, urged by Luther in seed and crisply by Kenneth Collins and Jerry Walls (Roman but Not Catholic, 2017), that "alms doth deliver from death, and shall purge away all sin" is a sub-Christian works-salvation contradicting Paul (Eph 2:8–9; Rom 3:28), so that Tobit preaches "another gospel" and cannot be Scripture. It does not hold on its best case: the verse is the ordinary biblical almsgiving idiom — "righteousness delivereth from death" is verbatim Proverbs 10:2 and 11:4, "shewing mercy to the poor" purges iniquity in Daniel 4:27, "charity shall cover the multitude of sins" in 1 Peter 4:8 — one node in a canonical arc (Gary Anderson: Daniel 4:27 → Tobit → Sirach → the Synoptics), so the charge, pressed consistently, condemns the protocanon and the dominical teaching with it; and the faith-and-works tension it raises stands already in James 2:24, at which Luther aimed the identical objection ("a right strawy epistle") without ejecting James. What survives is the narrower, true point that Tobit presses almsgiving's atoning efficacy harder than any single protocanonical verse and became the proof-text for the Catholic doctrines of satisfaction and merit the Reformation denied — a divergence of systematic theology, not a contradiction of the gospel that unbooks it.109

Does 2 Maccabees 12:43–45 teach prayer and atoning offering for the dead? (assessment: contested)

Does 2 Maccabees 12:43–45 teach prayer and atoning offering for the dead? — the deuterocanonical proof-text the Reformation made a doctrinal stake in the canon dispute, affirmed by the Council of Trent and the Catechism, contested by Luther, Calvin, and the Westminster Confession. It is strong on its plainest leg and overstated on its furthest. As a warrant for prayer and offering for the dead the reading is plain and conceded even by the Reformers — Judas Maccabeus sends a sin offering so the fallen "might be delivered from sin," called "an holy and good thought" — and the practice it warrants was the near-universal custom of the ancient Church (weighed sound on the purgatory timeline), while the very move it makes — arguing from a practice for the dead to the resurrection — recurs in protocanonical 1 Corinthians 15:29 ("baptized for the dead"). But the leap from prayer-for-the-dead to purgatory outruns a text that shows intercession grounded in resurrection hope, not a purgative fire; the men died wearing idolatrous amulets (12:40), grave matter from which purgatory by its own definition does not deliver, so pressed as a strict purgatory proof-text it proves the wrong thing; and its doctrinal force depends on the very canonicity in dispute — strong by reception, weak by the Protestant canon-criteria (the Hebrew canon, the author's closing disclaimer at 15:38, the book's self-description as an abridgment of Jason of Cyrene). No clear winner.110

Does the deuterocanon's thousand years in the Vulgate put the burden of proof on those who would remove it? (assessment: weak)

Does the deuterocanon's thousand-year place in the Vulgate make inclusion the default and shift the burden of proof onto those who would remove it? — the apologetic move that, because the disputed books were bound in the Bible of Latin Christendom from c. 400 to the Reformation, inclusion is the status quo and the whole onus falls on the remover. The historical premise is sound: the Vulgate really was the Western Bible for a millennium and really did carry the deuterocanon, given conciliar and liturgical form by the African councils, Innocent I, Augustine, and Florence. But the burden-of-proof conclusion does not hold on its best case: it is a dialectical framing, not evidence, and cannot conclude for inclusion; "status quo" is itself the disputed point, since Luther and the Reformers claimed to be restoring an older Hebrew canon; the Vulgate's own prologues — Jerome's — flagged these very books as apocrypha, read but "not for confirming … ecclesiastical dogmas," and travelled with the text through the Middle Ages; binding books in one codex never meant equal canonicity (the "Ecclesiastical"/Canonical tiering of Rufinus and Athanasius); and the premise is Western-only, the Greek East never having had "the Vulgate." What survives is the real but modest presumptive weight of long reception — the criterion this timeline already traces — not a burden-shift that settles the canon by default.111

Do the deuterocanonical books disclaim their own inspiration, excluding themselves from the canon? (assessment: weak)

Do the deuterocanonical books disclaim their own inspiration, excluding themselves from the canon? — the Protestant removal argument from the books' own words (Luther and Calvin in seed): several deuterocanonical books say things about themselves that, the argument holds, amount to a disavowal of inspired status — 2 Maccabees ends with the author's apology ("if … slenderly and meanly, it is that which I could attain unto," 15:38) and opens by calling itself a one-volume abridgment of Jason of Cyrene's five (2:23–28); the Sirach prologue has the grandson-translator apologize for rendering imperfections; and 1 Maccabees thrice laments that prophecy had ceased (9:27; 4:46; 14:41). It does not hold as a disqualifier on its best case: none of the passages is a statement about inspiration. The colophon and preface are a conventional authorial modesty formula and an editor's note on his method — the same self-conscious preface that heads Luke 1:1–4, which no one unbooks; the Sirach apology concerns the difficulty of Greek translation while ranking the book "for learning and wisdom"; and the "prophecy had ceased" laments are historical observations about the intertestamental gap — the identical lament protocanonical Psalm 74:9 makes from inside the canon ("there is no more any prophet"). Pressed as a disqualifier the criterion proves too much, threatening Luke, the Psalter, and the compiled protocanonical histories. What survives is the narrower, true point that no protocanonical book ends with an apology like 2 Maccabees 15:38 and that the cessation-of-prophecy laments cohere with the Hebrew-canon dating — a contributing datum to the graded standing the Hebrew-minded Fathers assigned these books, not a self-exclusion that unbooks them.112

Were the Jews "entrusted with the oracles of God" (Romans 3:2), so the church should follow the Hebrew canon? (assessment: contested)

Were the Jews "entrusted with the oracles of God" (Romans 3:2), so the church should follow the Hebrew canon? — the Protestant removal argument from Paul's word that the Jews "were entrusted with the oracles of God" (Luther and the Reformed confessions in instinct, James R. White in print): the Jewish people are the appointed custodians of the Old Testament, so the church should defer to the canon they recognized — the Hebrew table that excludes the deuterocanon. As a proof-text it is weak: "oracles of God" (Greek logia) means God's utterances — the Law and the covenant promises — not a catalogue of inspired books, as the word's New-Testament usage shows (Acts 7:38, the Law as "the lively oracles"; Hebrews 5:12; 1 Peter 4:11), and the unbroken patristic exposition of the verse (Origen, Chrysostom, Theophylact, Aquinas) takes it of Israel's privilege and ingratitude, never as a ruling on the canon; there was no single closed Jewish canon in the apostolic age to defer to (the Septuagint diaspora read the longer Greek Bible, and the rabbinic table hardened only later, via the discarded "Jamnia" legend and the medieval Masoretic Text); and deferring to that post-Christian rabbinic ruling begs the question Augustine answered — the Maccabees are canonical "not by the Jews, but by the Church." But the broader interpretation the verse is enlisted to serve — that the Old Testament should be measured by the Hebrew canon — is no weak position: it is Jerome's Hebraica veritas, the backbone of the Reformation's case, set against Augustine's reception-criterion in the very dispute this timeline traces with no clear winner. The prop fails while the reading it points to stands.113
