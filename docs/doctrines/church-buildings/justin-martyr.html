Justin Martyr on the Place of Christian Worship

Dates: c. 165 · Wikipedia
Summary: Tried at Rome under the prefect Rusticus (c. 165) and pressed to disclose where the Christians assembled, Justin gave the earliest datable testimony to the question this page traces: he named no dedicated meeting-place at all, only the lodging where he stayed — "above one Martinus, at the Timiotinian Bath" — and professed to know "any other meeting than his." His refusal first turns theological ("the God of the Christians is not circumscribed by place"), then resolves into the plain fact that the mid-second-century Roman church met in a private dwelling, not a church building.

Claims & evidence

Asked where the Christians assemble, Justin names no church — only a private lodging

Rusticus the prefect said, "Where do you assemble?" Justin said, "Where each one chooses and can: for do you fancy that we all meet in the very same place? Not so; because the God of the Christians is not circumscribed by place; but being invisible, fills heaven and earth, and everywhere is worshipped and glorified by the faithful." Rusticus the prefect said, "Tell me where you assemble, or into what place do you collect your followers?" Justin said, "I live above one Martinus, at the Timiotinian Bath; and during the whole time (and I am now living in Rome for the second time) I am unaware of any other meeting than his. And if any one wished to come to me, I communicated to him the doctrines of truth."

— The Martyrdom of the Holy Martyrs (the Acts of Justin), ch. 2, source (cf. New Advent).1 The exchange carries two distinct points. The first answer is theological: the prefect assumes a single fixed cult-site, and Justin denies the premise — the Christians' God "is not circumscribed by place," so there is no one shrine where the community must gather, the same de-localizing of worship the apologists pressed against the pagan temple-cult. The second answer is factual: pinned down to a location, Justin can name only the private dwelling he lodged in, "above one Martinus, at the Timiotinian Bath," and knows of no other meeting. The church of Rome in c. 165 is meeting in a rented upper room, not a building set apart for worship.

Notes

  • This is the earliest seated witness on the Place of Christian Worship timeline, and the clearest narrative datum behind the "necessity, not principle" thesis: a generation before the first physically attested Christian buildings, the church in the imperial capital still met in a private home. J. B. Lightfoot glossed the scene — "still the disciples, as of old, meet in an upper room, for fear, not now of the Jews, but of the Gentiles."2
  • Justin's testimony cuts both ways on the disputed question, and so does not by itself settle it. His first answer voices the theological strand (God "is not circumscribed by place"); his second records only the circumstance (he can name no dedicated assembly). The scene shows the absence of church buildings but not its cause — whether conviction or the position of an illegal cult — which is why the weighing belongs on the argument page, not here.
