Does the New Testament prescribe the home as the place of worship, or only describe the early circumstance?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — the New Testament's "church in the house" greetings describe where the first believers met (private homes were the ordinary venue of an unrecognized movement), but no text prescribes the home as the divinely-appointed place of worship; and the one passage that treats the question at length — 1 Corinthians 11 — cuts the other way, distinguishing the private meal "at home" from the gathering "as a church." So the popular reading that Scripture mandates home-only worship does not hold on its best case. What is sound is the bare descriptive observation that homes were the early norm — which establishes a circumstance, not a command · the strong reading tested here sits behind the popular question "why aren't we breaking bread in homes anymore?"; even its sympathetic teachers (GotQuestions, John Piper) stop short of it

Related arguments: the place of worship: principle or circumstance? — see also Was the early church's lack of dedicated buildings a principle of the faith, or a necessity of their position?

The reader's question driving this doctrine — "Why aren't we breaking bread in homes anymore — why is it only in church?" — presupposes an answer: that the first Christians met in homes by rule, so that the later move to dedicated buildings abandoned a New Testament prescription. The presupposition rests on a short list of proof-texts. This page asks whether those texts actually command the home as the place of worship, or only record it as the early circumstance.

The claim

"Churches in the New Testament era were indeed small assemblies that met in homes (Acts 2:46; 20:20). … Home churches are 'biblical' in the sense that there is precedent in Scripture, but there is no biblical obligation to attend a home church."

— GotQuestions, "Is a home church a true biblical church?", gotquestions.org. The careful proponents state the fact plainly and then decline the strong inference. The popular reading behind the doctrine's driving question goes further: it treats the house-church precedent as a pattern to be restored — that Scripture sets the home as the proper place of the "breaking of bread," so that meeting "only in church" is a declension. The descriptive datum is not in dispute; the disputed move is from precedent to prescription. The proof-texts come in two bundles: the house-church greetings adduced for the home as the place of worship, and the passages that point the other way.

Who advances it

The strong reading is rarely defended in print under its own name; it lives as a popular intuition — the sense that the New Testament's house churches are a lost ideal and the basilica a corruption. Where it is examined by those most sympathetic to house churches, the conclusion is consistently the weaker one. GotQuestions grants the precedent but holds "there is no biblical obligation to attend a home church" and that "there is nothing unbiblical about Christians gathering regularly in houses or large buildings or any other venue." John Piper, asked directly "Should we meet in house churches?", likewise treats the size and setting of the assembly as a matter of Christian freedom and wisdom, not apostolic command. So the page tests the inference the popular question presupposes, not a thesis these teachers actually hold.1

The evidence for

Homes were the ordinary venue, and the household carried real weight. The proof-texts are genuine and not isolated: Paul four times greets "the church that is in their house" — Prisca and Aquila's (Romans 16:5; 1 Corinthians 16:19), Nympha's (Colossians 4:15), and that in Philemon's house (Philemon 2) — and Acts reports the first believers "breaking bread from house to house" (Acts 2:46). Chrysostom, expounding the greetings, makes the household itself a thing of honor: Paul "was not in the habit of calling any houses Churches, save where there was much piety," and Prisca and Aquila "had been so estimable as even to make their house a Church." On its strongest footing, then, the house-church reading is not a quibble about real estate: it observes that for the first generations the household was the unit of Christian assembly, and gives that fact a positive theological coloring. The strongest version of the claim is the norm-of-necessity one — that the home was the settled, blessed shape of early worship, not a stopgap.2

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The descriptive core is simply true. Before there were buildings set apart for worship, Christians met where they could, and the well-appointed private house was the natural place; the "church in the house" greetings and the breaking of bread "from house to house" are exactly what one expects of an unlicensed movement without corporate property. Any account of early Christian worship that denied the centrality of the home would be false to the evidence.

The texts record the home; they never command it. Not one of the proof-texts is in the imperative. Paul greets the church in a house; Luke narrates the breaking of bread in houses. To read a greeting or a narrative as a statute is to supply the very thing the texts withhold — a command confining worship to a home. And the locus classicus undercuts the home-only reading on its face: Acts 2:46 names two venues, not one — the believers were "continuing daily with one accord in the temple, and breaking bread from house to house." Chrysostom reads the scene precisely so: the converts, still Jews, "did nothing else great or small, than assiduously attend at the temple," and broke bread in houses besides — "for at present they disturbed none of the Jewish observances." The household meal was something done in addition to temple worship, not the whole of it; the earliest Christians had not yet withdrawn into the home at all.3

The decisive counter: 1 Corinthians 11 drives a wedge between "at home" and "as a church." The one passage that legislates about the assembly does the opposite of confining worship to the home: it pulls the private meal out of the gathering and sends it back home. Paul rebukes the Corinthians for turning the supper into a private feast "when ye come together in the church … into one place" (11:18, 20), demanding: "have ye not houses to eat and to drink in? or despise ye the church of God?" (11:22), and concluding: "if any man hunger, let him eat at home; that ye come not together unto condemnation" (11:34). Here the home and the assembly are two distinct things — the ordinary meal belongs to the one, the Lord's Supper to the other. Chrysostom catches the distinction exactly: the offenders err by "using the Church as a house. For it was made a Church, not that we who come together might be divided, but that they who are divided might be joined"; and on the command to eat at home, "he brings him out of the church and sends him to his house." Aquinas presses the same separation of "the church of God" from "your own homes." (Both fathers, reading from a later age, take "the church" here for a dedicated sacred building — anachronistic, since in Paul ekklēsia still means the gathered congregation, not a structure; but the home-versus-assembly distinction they rightly hear in the text stands whichever way "church" is taken.) Far from prescribing home-only worship, the New Testament's most deliberate word on the subject already distinguishes the corporate assembly from the household and its private meals.4

Paul's own practice ranged over many venues. The Acts narrative never confines the apostolic mission to homes. Paul taught "publickly, and from house to house" (Acts 20:20) — Chrysostom takes the pairing as a mark of tireless labor in both settings — and elsewhere he is found in the temple courts and Solomon's portico, the synagogues, a hired lecture hall (the school of Tyrannus), and a riverside place of prayer. The home was one venue among several the first Christians used as opportunity allowed; the pattern is set by what was available, not by a rule confining worship to a house.5

Assessment

Weak. The interpretation the home-church argument serves — that the New Testament prescribes the home as the place of worship, so that dedicated buildings are a fall from an apostolic rule — does not hold on its best available case. Its one solid foundation is descriptive: homes really were the ordinary early venue, and the household assembly had genuine dignity. But the texts greet and narrate; they never command. Acts 2:46 pairs the temple with the houses; Acts 20:20 pairs public teaching with house-to-house; and 1 Corinthians 11 — the nearest thing to legislation on the gathering — expressly distinguishes the private meal "at home" from the assembly "as a church." Trust the observation that homes were the early norm. Flag the leap from that norm to a divine mandate: nothing in the New Testament forbids a dedicated assembly, and one passage already separates home from church. This is the scriptural half of the same finding the "necessity, not principle" thesis reaches on historical grounds — the home was the early Christians' circumstance, not their creed.
