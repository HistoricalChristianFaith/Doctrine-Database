Was the early church's lack of dedicated buildings a principle of the faith, or a necessity of their position?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: sound — the early absence of buildings set apart for worship was principally a necessity of the Christians' position (an unrecognized corporation could hold no corporate property, and prudence forbade any public display that "might arouse the fury of the populace"), not a principle of their faith; the proof is that the moment the Church was tolerated it acquired and built dedicated churches with no recorded objection of principle — which tells decisively against the popular claim that the New Testament mandates meeting in homes. What should be flagged is only the overstatement that the absence was purely circumstantial: a real theological strand (God "dwelleth not in temples made with hands") colored it — though that strand is aimed at pagan temple-cult and the localizing of God, not at the existence of an assembly hall, and so does not rescue the house-church-mandate reading · advanced by J. B. Lightfoot (Historical Essays, 1895)

Related arguments: the place of worship: principle or circumstance? — see also Does the New Testament prescribe the home as the place of worship, or only describe the early circumstance?

The reader's question driving the doctrine is "Why aren't we breaking bread in homes anymore — why is it only in church?" Behind it sits the popular claim that the first Christians met in homes on principle, so that the later move to church buildings is a fall from a New Testament pattern. Lightfoot's thesis is the historian's reply: the absence of buildings was a circumstance, not a creed.

The claim

"From these notices it is evident that during the early centuries the ritual of the Christians was very simple. One point at least seems clear, that they were not yet in the habit of erecting buildings devoted solely to divine worship. This, however, was not a principle of their faith, but rather a necessity of their position. As a corporation they were not recognized by the law; it was therefore impossible for them to hold corporate property. Moreover, common prudence would deter them from any display which might arouse the fury of the populace, or invite the repression of the magistrates."

— J. B. Lightfoot, Historical Essays, "The Christian Life in the Second and Third Centuries," pp. 52–53, full text at archive.org. The assertion has two parts: a negative point (the early church had no buildings set apart for worship before roughly the close of the second century) and an explanatory point (the cause was legal and prudential, not theological). Lightfoot presses the explanation again a few pages on, calling the plainness of early Christian worship "not a deliberate choice, but an enforced abstention," and indeed "a necessary discipline, a providential design, in the early education of the Church" — so that the later elaboration of architecture, music, and ritual is no "perversion of the simplicity of the Gospel" but a development from circumstances now changed.1

Who advances it

J. B. Lightfoot (1828–1889), bishop of Durham and one of the foremost patristic scholars of the nineteenth century, advanced the thesis in a lecture later printed in his posthumous Historical Essays (1895). It runs against a popular, often anti-institutional reading — heard in the question "the early church met in homes; why don't we?" — which treats the New Testament house churches as a prescription: that Scripture mandates meeting in homes, or at least enshrines a principle against sacred buildings, so that the basilica is a declension from apostolic practice. Lightfoot grants the fact the popular reading rests on (the first Christians did meet in houses and built nothing) but denies the inference (that this was a matter of conviction). The modern archaeological and social history of early Christian architecture — Graydon Snyder's Ante Pacem and L. Michael White's Building God's House in the Roman World — has filled in and broadly confirmed Lightfoot's framework.2

The evidence for

The legal disability was real. Christianity was an unlicensed association; Christians as a body could not securely hold corporate property, and at intervals were actively persecuted. The point is confirmed from the other end: when the emperor Gallienus restored peace (c. 260–261), his rescript ordered that the Christians' "places of worship" be restored to the bishops — Eusebius preserves it — and in doing so recognized the corpus Christianorum as a body able to hold property. That such a restoration was needed, and that it had to await imperial recognition, is exactly Lightfoot's "as a corporation they were not recognized by the law."3

The decisive datum: the moment they safely could, they built — without controversy. The earliest physically identified Christian building, the house-church at Dura-Europos, is a private house remodeled for worship around 233, while Christianity was still illegal — a community converting domestic space to dedicated cultic use the instant it had the means. Once Gallienus's peace held, Eusebius reports, the old buildings no longer sufficed and Christians "raised from the foundations in all the cities churches spacious in plan," a building boom that ran until Diocletian ordered those very churches leveled in 303; after Constantine came the great basilicas. White traces the unbroken trajectory — house church → domus ecclesiae (a house adapted) → aula ecclesiae (a purpose-built hall) → basilica. Nowhere in this rapid, eager development is there a recorded Father protesting that dedicated buildings betray a principle. A community that built as soon as it was free, and argued about how to build rather than whether, did not abstain on principle.4

Lightfoot's own test case points the same way. His earliest literary notice of a church building is the "temple of the Church of the Christians" at Edessa, recorded as destroyed in a flood of 201/202 — and he stresses that Edessa lay beyond the Empire, under kings who had embraced Christianity, "where … no such restraints would be imposed upon the Christians." A church building appears first precisely where the legal and prudential constraints were absent: the variable that predicts where buildings appear is circumstance, not creed.5

The earliest literary witness shows the bare circumstance. When the prefect Rusticus interrogated Justin Martyr at Rome (c. 165) and demanded to know where the Christians assembled, Justin could name no church — only the private lodging where he stayed, "above one Martinus, at the Timiotinian Bath," adding that he knew of "any other meeting than his." A generation before the first physically attested Christian building, the church in the imperial capital was still meeting in a rented dwelling. The scene records the absence of dedicated buildings as plain fact; it does not by itself decide the cause — Justin's first answer ("the God of the Christians is not circumscribed by place") voices the theological strand, while his second simply reports the circumstance.6

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The explanatory core is well-grounded and is the mainstream account. The absence of dedicated buildings in the first two centuries is sufficiently explained by illegality, the threat of persecution, small numbers, and poverty; and the swift, uncontested raising of churches once the Church was tolerated shows there was no doctrinal bar against them. That last fact also disposes of the strong popular reading: the New Testament does not mandate meeting in homes. The homes of Romans 16 and 1 Corinthians 16 were one venue among several the first Christians used as opportunity allowed — the temple courts and Solomon's portico, the synagogues, a hired lecture hall (the school of Tyrannus), a riverside — a descriptive pattern set by what was available, not a prescriptive rule. No apostolic command confines worship to a house.7

What is overstated. Pressed to its strongest wording — that the absence was purely a necessity of position, with no theological dimension at all — Lightfoot's formula understates a genuine strand of early Christian thought. When pagans taunted Christians for having "no temples, no altars, no images," the apologists did not merely plead illegality; they answered theologically: God "dwelleth not in temples made with hands" (Acts 7:48; 17:24), the whole world cannot contain Him, and the truest temple is the worshipper's own mind. Minucius Felix makes the boast a point of pride, and Origen says Christians decline "to build lifeless temples to the Giver of all life." Templelessness, for these writers, carried a positive theological coloring, not bare necessity.8

Why the overstatement does not unseat the thesis. That theological strand is aimed at the pagan idol-temple and at the localizing of deity in a sacred precinct — not at the bare existence of a hall in which to assemble. It is fully consistent with the same communities building dedicated churches as soon as they safely could, which is exactly what they did; "the Most High dwelleth not in temples made with hands" was no obstacle to a domus ecclesiae. So the two readings are not exclusive: the absence of buildings was principally circumstantial and was given a theological gloss when it had to be defended — and neither amounts to a divine mandate to meet in homes. The honest refinement is therefore to drop "purely": the necessity was primary, the theological motif real but narrowly aimed, and the house-church-as-principle reading unsupported either way.

Assessment

Sound. The interpretation Lightfoot's thesis serves — that the early church's lack of buildings set apart for worship was principally a necessity of its position as an unrecognized, persecuted body, not a principle of the faith, and certainly not a New Testament mandate to worship in homes — is well-grounded and close to the scholarly consensus (Snyder, White). The legal disability is documented and its lifting is documented; the instant, uncontroversial building once the Church was free shows there was no principle to overcome; and the house-church pattern of the New Testament is descriptive circumstance, not prescriptive command. Trust the explanatory core and the verdict that the "homes-by-mandate" reading is weak. Flag only the strongest wording of the thesis — that the absence was purely circumstantial: a genuine de-sacralization motif ("God dwelleth not in temples made with hands") did color early Christian self-understanding, but it targets pagan temple-cult and the localizing of God, not the existence of an assembly building, and so neither overturns the necessity explanation nor revives the house-church principle.
