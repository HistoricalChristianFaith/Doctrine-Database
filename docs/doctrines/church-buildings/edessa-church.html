The "temple of the church of the Christians" at Edessa

Dates: c. 202 (the flood is dated to the year 513 of the Greeks [Seleucid era] = A.D. 202 in the Chronicle; most modern scholars place it in November A.D. 201) · Wikipedia
Summary: The earliest literary notice of a building set apart for Christian worship. The Chronicle of Edessa, listing the edifices swept away when the river Daysan inundated the city in the reign of Abgar the Great, names among them "the temple of the church of the Christians" — a church building of some standing in a kingdom that, lying beyond the Roman frontier, imposed none of the legal restraints that kept Christians inside the Empire from owning corporate property.

Claims & evidence

The Chronicle of Edessa names a Christian church among the buildings the flood destroyed

In the year 513, in the reign of Severus, and in the reign of king Abgar … the fountains of the waters of the great river which is called Daisan were stirred up. … And the waters overthrew the great and beautiful palace of our lord the king, and they carried away everything that was found before them, the desirable and beautiful edifices of the city, and whatsoever was near the river … And they destroyed the temple of the church of the Christians. And there were killed by that occurrence more than two thousand men.

— The Chronicle of Edessa, §1, trans. B. H. Cowper, Journal of Sacred Literature, New Series vol. 5 (1864), tertullian.org.1 Two features make the notice valuable. First, the dating: the Chronicle is the earliest dated Syriac document, and its flood account — placed at the head of the work — opens with the precise Seleucid year (513 = A.D. 202; modern reckoning of the November date usually gives A.D. 201). Second, the wording: the building is called a temple (Syriac hayklā), and the community is already called the church — the congregation monopolizes the word "church," which is not yet transferred to the building it meets in. A church substantial enough to be itemized alongside the royal palace among the city's "desirable and beautiful edifices" is no mere house, and the phrasing gives no reason to think it was either the first or the only such building in Edessa.2

Notes

  • Edessa, capital of the kingdom of Osrhoene, lay beyond the Roman frontier under kings (the Abgarids) who had early embraced Christianity, so the legal and prudential restraints that prevented Christians inside the Empire from holding corporate property or raising conspicuous buildings did not apply. This is precisely why J. B. Lightfoot adduced the Edessene church as his earliest datable notice of a Christian building — a building appears first exactly where the constraints are absent, which is the load-bearing point of the "necessity, not principle" thesis. By contrast, Lightfoot found "not … any explicit notice of a church erected either at Rome or in the provinces before the close of the second century."3
  • The Chronicle is a sixth-century compilation, but its flood entry draws on the Edessene royal archives, which the chronicler cites; scholars regard the notice as historically reliable. A caution on its weight: it attests a building in early-third-century Edessa, a Christian frontier kingdom — it does not establish purpose-built churches inside the Empire at that date, where the material record begins a generation later with the converted house at Dura-Europos.4
