The Edict of Milan (313) — confiscated meeting-places restored, churches rebuilt

Dates: 313 (the agreement of Constantine and Licinius at Milan, promulgated as Licinius' edict at Nicomedia in June 313) · Wikipedia
Summary: The settlement that ended the persecutions for good and ordered every confiscated Christian meeting-place and corporate property returned without charge. Preserved in full by Lactantius (in Latin) and by Eusebius (in Greek), its building-relevant core is the restitution clause: the places "where the Christians had been accustomed to assemble," and the property belonging "to their society in general, that is, to their churches," were to be handed back at once — after which, Lactantius reports, Licinius "exhorted the Christians to rebuild their religious edifices." Once the legal disability was gone, the churches multiplied.

Claims & evidence

The edict orders confiscated places of worship and church property restored without payment

Moreover, with respect to the Christians, we formerly gave certain orders concerning the places appropriated for their religious assemblies; but now we will that all persons who have purchased such places, either from our exchequer or from any one else, do restore them to the Christians, without money demanded or price claimed, and that this be performed peremptorily and unambiguously … All those places are, by your intervention, to be immediately restored to the Christians. And because it appears that, besides the places appropriated to religious worship, the Christians did possess other places, which belonged not to individuals, but to their society in general, that is, to their churches, we comprehend all such within the regulation aforesaid, and we will that you cause them all to be restored to the society or churches, and that without hesitation or controversy …

— Lactantius, Of the Manner in Which the Persecutors Died (De mortibus persecutorum) 48, trans. W. Fletcher, Ante-Nicene Fathers vol. 7.1 The same instrument grants "to the Christians and others full liberty to observe that religion which each preferred" and, as the passage above shows, twice orders restitution: first of the buildings used for assembly, then of all other property held by the churches as corporate bodies — the explicit recognition of corporate church property that the rescript of Gallienus had begun half a century earlier.

Licinius then urges the Christians to rebuild their churches

Licinius having issued this ordinance, made an harangue, in which he exhorted the Christians to rebuild their religious edifices. And thus, from the overthrow of the Church until its restoration, there was a space of ten years and about four months.

— Lactantius, De mortibus persecutorum 48.2 The building of churches is treated not as a novelty to be permitted but as a restoration of what the Diocletianic persecution had destroyed — the "overthrow of the Church" measured, tellingly, by the demolition of its buildings. Eusebius preserves the Greek text of the same edict, with the same restitution clauses, in his Church History; the two independent transmissions secure the document.3

Notes

  • The restitution clauses presuppose, again, that the Church already owned meeting-places and corporate property before 313 — confiscated in Diocletian's persecution (303), now ordered returned. The edict does not invent church property; it restores it. This is the load-bearing point for the "necessity, not principle" thesis: the moment the legal disability lifted, the Christians rebuilt and multiplied their churches without any recorded objection that dedicated buildings betrayed a principle of the faith. The variable that predicts when and where churches appear is legal circumstance, not a change of conviction.4
  • "Edict of Milan" is the traditional name for the policy agreed by Constantine and Licinius at Milan in early 313 and published as Licinius' edict at Nicomedia that June; some historians prefer to call it the agreement or the rescript of Licinius, since no edict was issued at Milan itself. The dispute over the name does not touch the building-relevant substance — the universal grant of religious liberty and the order to restore confiscated Christian property — which is preserved verbatim by both Lactantius and Eusebius.5
