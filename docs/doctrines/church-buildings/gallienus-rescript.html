The rescript of Gallienus — restoring the Christians' places of worship

Dates: c. 260 (the rescript followed the capture of Valerian by the Persians in 260; often dated 260–261) · Wikipedia
Summary: The first imperial act to recognize the Christian body's right to hold its own places of worship. When Gallienus became sole emperor he ended his father Valerian's persecution and, by a rescript preserved in Eusebius, directed that the bishops' "places of religious worship" be restored to them and that the Christians could repossess their cemeteries — presupposing that the Church already had recognized meeting-places to be given back, and removing the legal obstacle that had kept such property precarious. It is the documented hinge behind the "necessity, not principle" reading of the early church's lack of buildings.

Claims & evidence

Gallienus' rescript restores the Christians' places of worship and cemeteries

Shortly after this Valerian was reduced to slavery by the barbarians, and his son having become sole ruler, conducted the government more prudently. He immediately restrained the persecution against us by public proclamations, and directed the bishops to perform in freedom their customary duties, in a rescript which ran as follows: "The Emperor Caesar Publius Licinius Gallienus, Pius, Felix, Augustus, to Dionysius, Pinnas, Demetrius, and the other bishops. I have ordered the bounty of my gift to be declared through all the world, that they may depart from the places of religious worship. And for this purpose you may use this copy of my rescript, that no one may molest you. And this which you are now enabled lawfully to do, has already for a long time been conceded by me. Therefore Aurelius Cyrenius, who is the chief administrator of affairs, will observe this ordinance which I have given." I have given this in a translation from the Latin, that it may be more readily understood. Another decree of his is extant addressed to other bishops, permitting them to take possession again of the so-called cemeteries.

— Eusebius, Church History 7.13, trans. A. C. McGiffert, Nicene and Post-Nicene Fathers, 2nd ser., vol. 1.1 The chapter is headed "The Peace Under Gallienus." The obscure clause "that they may depart from the places of religious worship" renders a Greek text whose sense is that the confiscated places of worship be given up — that is, restored — to the Christians; Lightfoot accordingly paraphrases the rescript as "expressly restoring to them their 'places of worship,'" and the parallel decree restoring the cemeteries confirms that property already held by the Church is being returned, not merely toleration declared.2

Notes

  • The rescript is addressed to the bishops as a body and orders the restitution of property held by them collectively — which is why historians read it as the first imperial recognition that the corpus Christianorum could lawfully hold corporate property. That such a restoration was needed, and that it had to await an emperor's decree, is exactly the disability J. B. Lightfoot identified: "as a corporation they were not recognized by the law; it was therefore impossible for them to hold corporate property." The rescript supplies the documented turning point in the "necessity, not principle" thesis — that the early absence of church buildings tracked legal circumstance, not conviction.3
  • The detail that there were already "places of worship" and "cemeteries" to be restored shows that by the mid-third century the Church inside the Empire did hold real property used for assembly and burial, precariously and subject to confiscation in persecution — consistent with the converted house-church at Dura-Europos (c. 233) and a step beyond it. Once Gallienus' peace held, Eusebius reports, the old buildings no longer sufficed and Christians "raised from the foundations in all the cities churches spacious in plan" — a building boom that ran until Diocletian ordered those very churches leveled in 303, and that resumed after the Edict of Milan (313).4
