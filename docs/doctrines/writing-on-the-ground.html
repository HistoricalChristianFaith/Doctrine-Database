What Jesus Wrote on the Ground (John 8:6)

When the scribes and Pharisees set the woman taken in adultery before Jesus, twice the Evangelist reports that he stooped and wrote with his finger on the ground (John 8:6, 8) — and twice he omits what Jesus wrote. The text withholds the content on purpose, and every reading below is a reconstruction from the setting, not a report of the words. Two things frame the whole history of interpretation. First, guessing at the content is, in antiquity, an almost exclusively Latin habit. The surviving Greek commentary tradition barely engages the episode at all: Chrysostom's homilies on John jump straight from 7:52 to 8:12; the earliest Greek writer to retell the story, Didymus the Blind (c. 380), knows a form of it with no writing on the ground at all; and the one Greek commentator to expound the Johannine passage, Euthymius Zigabenus (c. 1118), reaches it only to record that the accurate copies obelize it or lack it altogether.1 Second, some scribes found the silence intolerable and wrote an answer directly into their manuscripts (see below). What follows are the theories that were actually advanced, earliest first — from the fathers who guessed, through the scribes and schoolmen who catalogued the guesses, to the modern reconstructions and the modern refusal to reconstruct.

Timeline

The witnesses are listed earliest first. None claims to know the words; each infers them — or declines to — from the scene, the Law, and the prophets.

Didymus the Blind (c. 380)

The earliest Greek witness comes with a twist: he records the scene but not the writing. Didymus, head of the catechetical school at Alexandria, retells the story in his Commentary on Ecclesiastes — "in certain gospels," a woman "condemned by the Jews for a sin" is brought to be stoned, and Jesus turns the accusers back with "He who has not sinned, let him take a stone and cast it," so that, conscious of their own guilt, "no one dared." There is no finger, no dust, no inscription. The oldest Greek form of the episode has Jesus write nothing at all — itself evidence that the ground-writing of John 8:6, 8 may belong to a later, fuller stage of the tradition.2

Ambrose of Milan (c. 388)

Ambrose, weighing in two companion letters whether a Christian judge may lawfully pass a death sentence, is the first to guess at the content — and gives two different answers. In the letter to Studius he has Jesus write a prophetic oracle disowning the accusers: "What did He write except the prophetic saying: 'Earth, earth, write that these men have been disowned,'" a line Ambrose refers to Jeremiah's oracle against the doomed king Jechoniah, and develops into an earth/heaven contrast — the accusers' names written on the ground, the names of the faithful written in heaven.3 In the companion letter to Irenaeus he reads the gesture differently: Jesus wrote with the same finger that had written the Law, and wrote a second time to signify that the Jews stood condemned by both Testaments.4 His first reading seeds the long Western "earth accuses earth" tradition.

Jerome (c. 415)

Jerome supplies the answer that would prove the most durable — and the most popular ever since: Jesus wrote the accusers' own sins. Against the Pelagians he writes that Jesus, stooping, "began to write with his finger on the ground the sins, to be sure, of those who were making the accusation, as well as the sins of all mortal beings, according to what is written in the prophet, 'Those who depart from you shall be written in the earth'" (Jeremiah 17:13).5 This is the fountainhead of the enduring "he wrote their sins" reading.

Augustine of Hippo (c. 417)

Augustine, by contrast, pointedly declines to name the words and reads the act itself as sign. In the Tractates on John the writing marks the passage from law to grace: the Law "was written with the finger of God; but written on stone because of the hard-hearted. The Lord now wrote on the ground, because He was seeking fruit" — grace sown on fruitful soil against law graven on barren stone. His scene ends not with a decoded message but with a famous image: the two left alone, "the wretched woman and Mercy."6 The most influential Latin father on the passage is the one who refused to say what was written.

Bede (c. 730)

Bede folds two readings together. The writing shows that this was the very one "who once wrote the Ten Commandments of the law on stone with his finger, that is, by the action of the Holy Spirit"; and the second stooping he reads practically — "by directing his look elsewhere he might give them the freedom to go away," foreseeing that the accusers, astounded, "would be more inclined to depart quickly than to ask him more questions." He adds the moral: that Jesus wrote both before and after his verdict teaches us to examine ourselves before and after we correct a neighbour.7

Alcuin of York (c. 800)

Alcuin turns the gesture wholly moral and allegorical, dropping the question of content altogether: "The ground denotes the human heart, which yieldeth the fruit either of good or of bad actions: the finger jointed and flexible, discretion." The scene instructs us, seeing faults in our neighbours, "not immediately and rashly to condemn them, but after searching our own hearts to begin with, to examine them attentively with the finger of discretion."8

The scribes: "the sins of every one of them" (9th century onward)

Some copyists resolved the silence by writing the answer into the text. In a cluster of (mostly Byzantine) witnesses — the uncial U (Nanianus) and a string of minuscules (73, 95, 331, 364, 413, 658, 700, 782, 1592), with 264 placing it at the first writing (8:6) rather than the second, 652 adding it only in the margin, and some Armenian copies — the verse gains after "the ground" the words ἐνὸς ἑκάστου αὐτῶν τὰς ἁμαρτίας — "the sins of every one of them" — so that it now states outright that Jesus wrote "the sins of each one of them." (Metzger's own list also cites the uncial Π, but that manuscript is in fact lacunose right through John 8:6–39 and so cannot witness the gloss at all.) Metzger judges the addition a later expansion made "in order to satisfy pious curiosity concerning what it was that Jesus wrote upon the ground": Jerome's reading crystallized, three or four centuries on, into Scripture itself.9

Euthymius Zigabenus (c. 1118)

The one Byzantine commentator to reach the passage reaches it only to bracket it. Euthymius records that everything from John 7:53 to 8:12 is, "in the accurate copies, either not found or marked with an obelus, because these things seem spurious and added," and then expounds the story as edifying — but never asks what Jesus wrote. He is the terminus Metzger has in mind in the line that "no Greek Church Father prior to Euthymius Zigabenus" comments on the passage; the Greek East's characteristic response to the scene is text-critical doubt, not reconstruction — though his note, in a commentary largely compiled from earlier exegetes, is almost certainly older than he is.10

Bonaventure (c. 1257)

By the thirteenth century the answers were being catalogued as a set. Bonaventure lays them out: "Augustine responds that He wrote what He spoke. But Ambrose says that He wrote: 'Earth, earth, write down the rejected men,' and this is drawn from Jeremiah chapter twenty-two. The Interlinear Gloss says that He was writing 'their sins.' Others say that He was writing letters in which each one could read his own sins." He registers the Greek puzzle too — "some say that it was not found in the ancient Greek books, because John added it after the Gospel was completed" — and, asking why Jesus wrote with his finger, gives the deflating literal answer alongside the mystical one: "The reason is literal, because I believe He did not have another pen."11

Thomas Aquinas (c. 1270)

Aquinas gives the same menu and, unusually among the ancients and medievals, weighs it: "Some say that he wrote the words of Jeremiah: 'O earth, earth, listen — write down this man as sterile.' According to others, and this is the better opinion, Jesus wrote down the very words he spoke... However, neither of these opinions is certain." He then reads the choice of the ground itself three ways: that those testing him "would be written on the earth" (Jeremiah 17:13) while the just are "written in heaven"; that writing signs on earth figures the miracles wrought in the flesh he assumed; and that the soft earth, against the stone tablets of the old Law, shows "the sweetness and the softness of the New Law that he gave to us."12

John Calvin (1553)

Calvin closes the speculative era by refusing it. Those who "conjecture that he wrote this or the other thing," he holds, "do not understand his meaning" — and he declines even Augustine's ingenious law-and-grace reading of the gesture. The stooping and writing showed the accusers only how "unworthy they were of being heard," as a man addressed by another might "draw lines on the wall, or ... turn his back ... to show ... that he was not attending to what was said." The content is not the point, and the search for it is misdirected.13

The modern reconstructions (19th–20th century)

A learned minority has nonetheless kept reconstructing, now on legal and ritual rather than allegorical grounds. T. W. Manson (1953) proposed that Jesus was miming Roman criminal procedure, in which a magistrate wrote out his sentence before reading it aloud — going through the form of a verdict worded so that it could not be executed, and so escaping the trap as he escaped the tribute question; F. F. Bruce followed him, taking the written words to be those of verse 7 itself.14 J. D. M. Derrett (1963) argued instead that Jesus wrote words of the Torah against false witnesses — from Exodus 23, "put not thine hand with the wicked to be an unrighteous witness" (23:1b) and "keep thee far from a false matter" (23:7a) — turning the accusers' own Law against them; Leon Morris inclined to this reading.15 Others, reaching back to John Lightfoot in the seventeenth century and J. W. Burgon in the nineteenth, and revived lately by James F. McGrath, have connected the writing-in-dust to the ordeal of the suspected adulteress in Numbers 5 — the one biblical rite joining an accused woman, a written curse, and the dust of the sanctuary floor — though John supplies none of the ordeal's water or drinking.16

The modern consensus (20th–21st century)

Most modern reference works judge the content unrecoverable and left blank by the Evangelist's own design. C. K. Barrett finds no point in asking after the inscription's content, since the writing itself enacts Jesus' refusal to sit as judge; Barnabas Lindars and George Beasley-Murray likewise read the gesture as a sign of unwillingness to engage, indifferent to any words; Gail O'Day puts the pole most sharply — the very act of writing is what matters, and readings that try to recover its content miss the point of Jesus' nonverbal response; and Craig Keener dismisses even the ingenious guess that Jesus wrote the commandment against coveting (turning it on the accusers) as, at best, speculation the narrator never licenses. Raymond Brown surveys the proposals, rejects Manson's, and commits to none. The learned guessing has itself become an object of study — Chris Keith's monograph catalogues some three dozen distinct proposals, and Jennifer Knust and Tommy Wasserman trace how the "sins of the accusers" answer grew from Jerome through the scribal gloss into popular certainty — so that the surest thing to say about what Jesus wrote is how variously, and how confidently, later readers have said it.17

Arguments

The timeline above records who guessed what. This section weighs individual readings on their own merits — the modern legal and ritual reconstructions as a set, and the most popular ancient guess.

Related arguments — the modern reconstructions

Three post-patristic attempts to recover the withheld content on legal or ritual rather than allegorical grounds: a mimed Roman verdict, an inscription of the Torah's law of witnesses, and an allusion to the Numbers 5 ordeal of the suspected adulteress. Each is weighed against the Evangelist's deliberate silence, and they are best read together.

Did Jesus mime a Roman magistrate's verdict? (assessment: weak)

The Roman-verdict theory — T. W. Manson's proposal (followed by F. F. Bruce) that Jesus mimed the Roman judge who writes his sentence before reading it, framing an unexecutable verdict to slip the trap. It coheres with the well-grounded reading of the scene as a Roman-vs.-Moses dilemma, but transfers a Roman provincial-governor's procedure into a Jewish Temple court and casts Jesus as a sentencing judge when the text makes him the one being tested — and the host trap-reading survives without it.18

Did Jesus write Torah verses against the false witnesses? (assessment: weak)

The Torah against false witnesses — J. D. M. Derrett's reconstruction (Leon Morris inclining to it) that Jesus wrote Exodus 23:1b then 23:7a, fixing the content by a self-estimated sixteen-character writing limit. The theme it serves — that the accusers, not the woman, are the ones the law of witnesses arraigns — is sound and carried by Jesus' spoken invocation of the first-stone rule (Deut 17:7); but the specific inscription is an over-precise conjecture the Evangelist twice declines to supply.19

Does the writing allude to the Numbers 5 ordeal of the suspected adulteress? (assessment: plausible)

The Numbers 5 ordeal — the reading (John Lightfoot, Burgon; revived by James F. McGrath and Daniel McClellan) that the dust-writing evokes the sotah rite, the one scene in the Law joining an accused woman, a written curse, and sanctuary dust, with a real hinge in the rule that the ordeal is void unless the accuser is himself innocent. The strongest of the three on thematic grounds, but the rite's defining mechanics — curse-water, drinking, the ordeal verdict, the priestly officiant — are all absent from John.20

Standalone argument

  • Did Jesus write the accusers' sins on the ground? (Jerome's "he wrote their sins," anchored to Jeremiah 17:13) — contested
