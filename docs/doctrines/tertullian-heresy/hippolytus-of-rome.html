Hippolytus of Rome on the Montanists: a heresy-catalogue with no Tertullian in it

Dates: c. 225 (the Refutation of All Heresies, c. 222–235); Hippolytus c. 170–235 · Wikipedia
Summary: Hippolytus is a witness to Tertullian on the “silence” side of the ledger. Writing at Rome in Tertullian’s own generation, he compiled the most thorough heresy-catalogue the third century produced, and he handled the Phrygian New Prophecy at length — naming Montanus, Priscilla and Maximilla, setting out their errors, dismissing their books. And he says not a word about Tertullian, the one Latin of real stature the movement ever won. The century’s foremost heresy-hunter did not count Tertullian a heresiarch; that figure is not yet on the map. This page covers Hippolytus’s share of the timeline of the verdict passed on Tertullian.

Claims & evidence

The Phrygians catalogued: Montanus, Priscilla, Maximilla — and no Tertullian (Refutation VIII.12)

But there are others who themselves are even more heretical in nature (than the foregoing), and are Phrygians by birth. These have been rendered victims of error from being previously captivated by (two) wretched women, called a certain Priscilla and Maximilla, whom they supposed (to be) prophetesses. And they assert that into these the Paraclete Spirit had departed; and antecedently to them, they in like manner consider Montanus as a prophet… they magnify these wretched women above the Apostles and every gift of Grace, so that some of them presume to assert that there is in them a something superior to Christ.

— Hippolytus, The Refutation of All Heresies, Book VIII, ch. 12, source. Hippolytus grants that the Phrygians “acknowledge God to be the Father of the universe, and Creator of all things, similarly with the Church, and (receive) as many things as the Gospel testifies concerning Christ” — the error, on his telling, lies in exalting the prophets and their new fasts above Scripture, and in a modalist wing that “assent to the heresy of the Noetians.” Throughout the notice he names the founders and the errors and never mentions Tertullian.1

The epitome repeats the roster, still without him (Refutation X.21)

The Phrygians, however, derive the principles of their heresy from a certain Montanus, and Priscilla, and Maximilla, and regard these wretched women as prophetesses, and Montanus as a prophet.

— Hippolytus, The Refutation of All Heresies, Book X, ch. 21, source. Book X is Hippolytus’s own recapitulation, a compact roll-call of every sect he has treated. When he comes to the Phrygians he lists the same three names and no fourth. The recapitulation is the natural place a Latin adherent of the stature of Tertullian would surface if Hippolytus knew of one as a heresiarch; the omission is consistent.2

Notes

  • The value of this witness is an argument from silence, and is weighed as such: it proves that Tertullian was not yet a catalogued heresiarch in his own century, not that he was in good standing. Hippolytus wrote at Rome and may simply not have known the Carthaginian adherent; but he did know the movement in detail, and the omission is of a piece with the wider third-century blank — no synod, no rival’s treatise, no heresy-list names Tertullian. See the summary timeline, third-century block.
  • The earliest Latin heresy-list to cover the group — the anonymous Against All Heresies that comes down appended to Tertullian’s own works (and long circulated under his name) — divides the “Cataphrygians” into the followers of Proclus and of Aeschines and likewise knows no “Tertullianist” branch. Scholars trace it to Hippolytus’s lost Syntagma, so it is very nearly a second voice of the same witness, and it too passes Tertullian over. It is quoted on the summary timeline.
  • Hippolytus appears across this wiki as a witness in his own right — on astrology (Book IV of the same Refutation), the intermediate state, infant baptism, the ministerial priesthood, the Old Testament canon, and prayer to the saints. He is himself a pointed comparandum on this timeline: a schismatic on the usual reconstruction — set up as a rival bishop of Rome — who was nonetheless reconciled, martyred, and venerated as a saint, where Tertullian was quietly left uncanonized.
