Augustine on Tertullian: the entry that made him a heresiarch

Dates: c. 354–430 · Wikipedia
Summary: Near the end of his life, cataloguing the heresies for the deacon Quodvultdeus of Carthage, Augustine gave Tertullian something no earlier writer had: his own numbered entry among the heresies, no. 86 of the De haeresibus (c. 428). This is the moment Tertullian becomes a catalogued heresiarch in the Latin West — and the single piece of ancient evidence that there was ever a distinct Tertullianist sect, whose last members, Augustine says, handed their Carthaginian basilica back to the catholic church in his own day. The entry is also strikingly careful: Augustine reports Tertullian’s doctrine that the soul, and even God, is a body — and then refuses to make that the ground of the heresy. What condemned him, in Augustine’s telling, was rigorism: joining the Cataphrygians he had once demolished, damning second marriage as fornication, and finally breaking even from them to found congregations of his own. This page covers Augustine’s share of the timeline of the verdict passed on Tertullian. Elsewhere Augustine handles him far more gently — pressing his corporealism while half-sharing his instinct on the soul, and naming him, against the Manichees, as a fixed point of the church’s earlier age.

The entry: De haeresibus 86 (c. 428)

About 428, at the request of Quodvultdeus — then a deacon, later bishop of Carthage — Augustine compiled a handbook of the heresies that had arisen since Christ, eighty-odd of them, each in a short notice. The Cataphrygians, that is the Montanists of Montanus, Prisca, and Maximilla, are no. 26. Tertullian gets a separate entry of his own, no. 86 — which is itself the first fact worth marking: Augustine does not fold him into the Montanists but treats the Tertullianistae as a distinct group.1 The notice opens not with doctrine but with an ending — a sect dwindling to nothing in living memory, in Augustine’s own city:

Tertullianistae a Tertulliano, cuius multa leguntur opuscula eloquentissime scripta, usque ad nostrum tempus paulatim deficientes, in extremis reliquiis durare potuerunt in urbe Carthaginensi: me autem ibi posito ante aliquot annos, quod etiam te meminisse arbitror, omni ex parte consumpti sunt. Paucissimi enim qui remanserant, in Catholicam transierunt, suamque basilicam, quae nunc etiam notissima est, Catholicae tradiderunt.

— Augustine, De haeresibus 86, Latin text: “The Tertullianistae take their name from Tertullian, many of whose works — most eloquently written — are read; dwindling little by little down to our own time, they managed to last on in their final remnants in the city of Carthage. But when I was there some years ago — as I think you too remember — they were consumed utterly. For the very few who remained went over into the catholic church and handed their basilica, which is even now very well known, over to the catholic church.” (The English rendering here is our own from the Latin; a full scholarly translation is L. G. Muller’s of 1956.)2 On any reading, this is a single congregation with one building in one city, absorbed back into the church without a fight in Augustine’s own episcopate — a slender basis for the two-centuries-old heresiarch the West would build on it.

“Not for this he became a heretic”: the corporealism Augustine will not condemn

What comes next is the most interesting turn in the notice, because Augustine states the doctrinal charge and then declines to hang the heresy on it. Tertullian, he reports, held that the soul is immortal but a body — and not the soul only, but God himself:

Tertullianus ergo, sicut scripta eius indicant, animam dicit immortalem quidem, sed eam corpus esse contendit: neque hanc tantum, sed ipsum etiam Deum. Nec tamen hinc haereticus dicitur factus.

— De haeresibus 86: “Tertullian, then, as his writings show, says the soul is indeed immortal, but contends that it is a body — and not this only, but God himself as well. Yet he is not on this account said to have been made a heretic.” Augustine goes on to construct a charitable sense in which calling God a “body” need not be impious — not a body with larger and smaller parts, but a nature that is “nowhere partitioned through the spaces of place… yet in its own nature and substance remains unchangeably” — and grants that Tertullian thought “something of this kind” even about the soul. This is the same corporealism Augustine attacks sharply on the origin of the soul; here, weighing it as a heresy, he lets it stand. The judgement is deliberate, and he repeats it.3

The real ground of the verdict, when Augustine finally states it, is discipline, not metaphysics — and his three-part indictment doubles as a compressed history of the sect:

Non ergo ideo est Tertullianus factus haereticus; sed quia transiens ad Cataphrygas, quos ante destruxerat, coepit etiam secundas nuptias contra apostolicam doctrinam tanquam stupra damnare, et postmodum etiam ab ipsis divisus, sua conventicula propagavit. Dicit sane etiam ipse animas hominum pessimas post mortem in daemones verti.

— De haeresibus 86: “It is not, therefore, for this that Tertullian was made a heretic; but because, going over to the Cataphrygians — whom he had earlier destroyed — he began also to condemn second marriages, against apostolic teaching, as though they were fornication; and afterwards, divided even from them, he propagated his own little assemblies. He does indeed also say that the very worst souls of men are turned into demons after death.” Three moves make the heretic: the turn to the Cataphrygians he had once refuted, the rigorist condemnation of remarriage (against 1 Timothy 4:3), and the final schism — ab ipsis divisus, sua conventicula propagavit, the one clause on which the whole idea of a Tertullian-founded sect rests. The demon-souls are added almost as an afterthought (sane etiam).4

What the notice does — and does not — establish

Two things follow, and they pull against each other. First, this is the earliest and effectively the only ancient testimony to a distinct Tertullianist sect; every later report descends from it (the anonymous Praedestinatus, c. 435, simply copies Augustine and expands). If the sect existed as Augustine describes it — founded by Tertullian, outliving him by two centuries in one Carthaginian congregation — he is the source we have. Second, Augustine wrote about two hundred years after Tertullian, with no cited chain of evidence for the founding beyond “his writings show” (sicut scripta eius indicant) — and Tertullian’s surviving writings nowhere announce a sect, a name, or a second schism from the Montanists. Whether the Tertullianistae were really a body Tertullian himself founded by breaking from the Cataphrygians, or a later Carthaginian rigorist group who took his name, or simply the local North-African New Prophecy congregation relabelled, is genuinely disputed: Augustine plainly believed the first, while a strong strand of modern scholarship (Douglas Powell, William Tabbernee) reads the African circle as never having left the church at all, an ecclesiola in ecclesia rather than a breakaway. That question is weighed in its own right; here it is enough to mark that the whole edifice rests on this one late notice, and that Augustine’s confident a Tertulliano is an inference from books, not a documented succession.5

The other Augustine: pressing the corporealism, half-sharing the instinct

Set beside the catalogue, Augustine’s ordinary handling of Tertullian is far warmer — and more entangled. On the origin of the soul he is Tertullian’s sharpest critic exactly where the De haeresibus lets him off: disputing the young enthusiast Vincentius Victor, who made the soul a corporeal exhalation of God, Augustine turns the corporealism into an insult:

In that he believes God to be truly incorporeal, I congratulate him that herein, at all events, he has kept himself uninfluenced by the ravings of Tertullian. For he insisted, that as the soul is corporeal, so likewise is God.

— On the Soul and Its Origin II.9 [V.], source. He then twists the knife with a line that reads oddly against Jerome’s biography down the timeline — marvelling at a “wonderful learning… which contrives to gain for its disciples men of advanced years, and even presbyters” — the very word, presbyter, that Jerome had fastened on Tertullian himself. Yet the entanglement runs deeper than polemic. Augustine could never bring himself to reject traducianism outright, because it alone explained how Adam’s guilt reaches a newborn; and on the reading of his late letter to Optatus he is reported to have edged toward a spiritual generationism precisely while “he remembers Tertullian, and fears being compelled, like him, to affirm the corporeality of the soul.” The man he files under heresy-adjacent corporealism is the man whose mechanism he half-wants and cannot metaphysically afford.6

And where the enemy is the Manichee rather than the creationist, Augustine reaches for Tertullian as an authority. Answering Faustus, who dressed Mani in the title “apostle of Jesus Christ,” Augustine dates the Manichaean fraud by the church’s own calendar: “it is well known that this heresy began not only after Tertullian, but after Cyprian.” The pairing is telling. Tertullian and Cyprian stand together as the fixed early markers of the true tradition against which a real heresy’s lateness is measured — the same two names, and nearly the same reverence, that run through this whole timeline.7

Notes

  • The double attitude this page traces is the timeline’s recurring one, now sharpened. In the handbook Tertullian is a numbered heresiarch; on the facing pages of Augustine’s working theology he is “the master”-adjacent — quarried, dated-by, argued-with as a peer. Augustine even builds the catalogue verdict so as to exclude the doctrine (corporealism) he elsewhere fights, and rest it on the discipline (rigorist schism) instead.
  • Open question, flagged not settled: whether a Tertullian-founded sect ever existed, or whether the Tertullianistae were the African New Prophecy under another name. The whole case for a distinct sect is this one notice (and its copyists); the contrary reading — an ecclesiola in ecclesia that never broke communion — is set out on the summary timeline under Tertullian’s own testimony and the third-century block.
