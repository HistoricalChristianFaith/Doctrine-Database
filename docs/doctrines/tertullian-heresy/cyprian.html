Cyprian of Carthage on Tertullian: the master read daily and never named

Dates: c. 253 · Wikipedia — Cyprian, c. 200–258; bishop of Carthage from c. 248, martyr under Valerian, the leading Latin churchman of the mid-third century
Summary: Cyprian is the great third-century witness to Tertullian’s standing precisely by what he does not do. In the generation after Tertullian’s turn to the New Prophecy, the most orthodox hand in his own city leaned on his writings throughout — and never once named him. Two things reach us: a much later anecdote, that Cyprian read Tertullian every day and called for him as “the master”; and the plain, unacknowledged dependence of Cyprian’s own treatises on Tertullian’s. The datum is silent, constant use — two-edged, and set down here as such. This page covers Cyprian’s share of the timeline of the verdict passed on Tertullian.

Claims & evidence

“Give me the master”: Cyprian read Tertullian every day (as Jerome reports)

I myself have seen a certain Paul an old man of Concordia, a town of Italy, who, while he himself was a very young man had been secretary to the blessed Cyprian who was already advanced in age. He said that he himself had seen how Cyprian was accustomed never to pass a day without reading Tertullian, and that he frequently said to him, “Give me the master,” meaning by this, Tertullian.

— Jerome, Lives of Illustrious Men (De viris illustribus) 53, source. The anecdote is not Cyprian’s own word but Jerome’s, written about 393 — roughly a century and a half after Cyprian — and reported at second hand through a chain Jerome names: the aged Paul of Concordia, who as a youth had been secretary to the aged Cyprian. It should be weighed as such a chain deserves. But it does not stand alone: the daily reading it describes is corroborated, independently of the anecdote, by the plain dependence of Cyprian’s own text on Tertullian’s (below).1

The silent dependence: Cyprian’s treatises rework Tertullian’s, unacknowledged

Cyprian never names Tertullian in his surviving works, yet the debt is written all through them. The framework of his treatise on the Lord’s Prayer (De dominica oratione) follows Tertullian’s On Prayer clause by clause; his book on the good of patience (De bono patientiae) reworks Tertullian’s On Patience; his tract on the dress of virgins (De habitu virginum) draws on the On the Apparel of Women — each recast into a smoother, more “literary” Latin, and in each the source goes unnamed. Jerome, who knew the fact, states it flatly a century and a half later: in Letter 84 he adduces Cyprian’s use of Tertullian as the standing precedent for reading a heretic’s genius without his error.

The blessed Cyprian takes Tertullian for his master, as his writings prove; yet, delighted as he is with the ability of this learned and zealous writer he does not join him in following Montanus and Maximilla.

— Jerome, Letter 84 (To Pammachius and Oceanus), §2, source. “As his writings prove” (ut eius scripta declarant): Jerome does not rest the point on the anecdote but on the visible textual dependence, which modern study of the two writers confirms.2

Why the silence matters — and why it is two-edged

The bare datum is that the most orthodox churchman of Tertullian’s own city, one generation on, read him constantly and built his own treatises on him — and never spoke his name. The natural reading is that Tertullian’s turn to the New Prophecy had put his name under enough of a cloud to make open citation awkward even for a devoted reader; that is plausible and often urged. But it is an inference we supply, not something Cyprian says. Read the other way, the very fact that so orthodox a bishop read him daily tells against any picture of Tertullian as a notorious, formally-condemned heretic in mid-third-century Carthage: there was as yet no verdict to cite, only a name it was more graceful to leave unspoken. Either way, no contemporary calls Tertullian a heretic; the century after him mines him and does not name him, and that continued, unacknowledged use is itself a witness — the ground on which every later verdict was built, and every one of those verdicts is later.3

Notes

  • Cyprian is a “witness by silence” here: he asserts nothing about Tertullian in his own hand. What this page records is the fact of his silent, constant use and the later anecdote that names it. An argument from silence proves little on its own; it is set down as silence, not smuggled in as either acquittal or verdict — see the summary timeline, third-century block.
  • The da magistrum anecdote and the Roman-clergy motive both reach us only through Jerome’s De viris illustribus 53; his reliability as this timeline’s chief biographer is weighed there. Jerome himself practised the “read the genius, refuse the error” principle he credits to Cyprian — it is the whole logic by which the church went on reading both Tertullian and Origen.
  • The same book-list that later declined to receive Tertullian’s works — the anonymous Decretum Gelasianum — also set Cyprian’s own writings among the rejected, the martyr-bishop who had read Tertullian every day keeping his master company on the rejected shelf (further down the timeline).
  • Cyprian appears across this wiki as a witness in his own right — on purgatory, the ministerial priesthood, infant baptism, the Old Testament canon, the Eucharist, prayer to the saints, and the gravity of sins — several of them working out precisely the disciplinary questions (post-baptismal penance, the unity of the church) on which he inherited and softened Tertullian’s rigorism.
