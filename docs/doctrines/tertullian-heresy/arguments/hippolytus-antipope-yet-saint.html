Hippolytus, the antipope who became a saint: was the verdict on Tertullian uneven?

Related arguments: the comparative test — see also Cyprian, wrong on rebaptism yet a saint, Novatian, whose shared rigorism became a schism, and the umbrella question, is it coherent to keep his theology while withholding his name?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline, a comparative test) · Assessment: contested — on the usual reconstruction Hippolytus of Rome led a rival Roman community against three legitimate bishops — Callistus, Urban, and Pontian — for the better part of two decades, an antipope, and attacked Callistus’s theology and his penitential laxity in the Refutation of All Heresies. Yet he reconciled, was exiled and died with Pope Pontian, was venerated as a saint and martyr, and has an ancient seated statue in Rome. The interpretation weighed here is that the contrast isolates a variable: a man whose offense was jurisdiction and church order — a rival altar in Rome itself — is a saint, while Tertullian, who never set up a rival see and (arguably) never formally left, is the byword for a heretic, so the West’s verdict on a Father tracks the manner of his death and the fortunes of his name more than the gravity of his fault. That much is a strong and instructive observation, and it corroborates the finding of the capstone question: no court ever weighed these men against each other, and what separated their fates was martyrdom and reconciliation, not a careful reckoning of guilt. But as a charge that the Tertullian verdict was unfair it is weaker. The criterion the comparison exposes — that blood-witness and return to communion cover a multitude of sins — is a principled one the church openly avows, not caprice; the crispest form of the comparison, Hippolytus the “first antipope,” rests on a reconstruction the specialists (Nautin, Brent) now doubt; and Hippolytus broke with Callistus partly to defend Trinitarian orthodoxy against a bishop he judged a modalist, so his schism is not the bare jurisdictional offense the neat contrast needs. Strong as an isolation of the variable, disputed as a verdict of unfairness, and hinging on how much weight the contested antipope story will bear. Contested.

This is not a count any ancient source ever laid against Tertullian: no synod, no heresy-catalogue, and not Augustine’s notice among the heresies, faults him by comparison with Hippolytus. It belongs, then, not to the reckoning of the counts but to the prior question the whole timeline of the verdict keeps returning to: was that verdict fair? The comparative method is the one Vincent of Lérins himself invited when he paired Tertullian with Origen as the Latin and Greek types of the learned teacher undone by his gifts; here a third case is set beside Tertullian’s — a contemporary Roman who did what Tertullian is most often accused of, and worse, and was canonized for it.1

Hippolytus’s offense: a rival altar in Rome

On the reconstruction that has organized the field since Döllinger, Hippolytus was the most learned Roman churchman of his day and the loser of a bitter contest for the Roman see. When Callistus was elected bishop about 217, Hippolytus — who had already clashed with him under Pope Zephyrinus — refused his communion and, in the Catholic Encyclopedia’s words, “had himself elected antipope by his small band of followers,” continuing “in opposition as antipope throughout the reigns of the two immediate successors of Callistus, Urban … and Pontius.” If that is right, it is the gravest breach of church order there is: a second bishop, a second altar, a rival succession maintained in the city of Rome itself for some fifteen years, against not one legitimate bishop but three.2

And the surviving weapon of that schism is a book whose polemic runs strikingly parallel to Tertullian’s. In Book IX of the Refutation of All Heresies Hippolytus arraigns Callistus on two counts at once. The first is doctrinal: Callistus, he charges, has fallen into the modalism of Noetus, teaching “that the Logos Himself is Son, and that Himself is Father; and that though denominated by a different title, yet that in reality He is one indivisible spirit,” and saying, “I will not profess belief in two Gods, Father and Son, but in one.” The second is disciplinary, and it is almost the mirror of the edict Tertullian attacks in On Modesty: Callistus “first invented the device of conniving with men in regard of their indulgence in sensual pleasures, saying that all had their sins forgiven by himself,” propounded “that, if a bishop was guilty of any sin, if even a sin unto death, he ought not to be deposed,” and let twice- and thrice-married men remain among the clergy. Where Tertullian mocked the “Pontifex Maximus — that is, the bishop of bishops” for remitting the sins of adultery and fornication, Hippolytus assails the very same pope (Callistus is the standard identification of Tertullian’s “bishop of bishops” too) on the very same laxity — the difference being that Hippolytus pressed his protest to the length of a rival episcopate, and Tertullian did not.3

Yet he is a saint and martyr

The schism ended not in condemnation but in reconciliation and a shared crown. In the persecution of Maximinus Thrax, Hippolytus and Pope Pontian were both deported in 235 to the mines of Sardinia, the “unhealthful island” (insula nociva), and there both died. The Catholic Encyclopedia draws the inference the Roman Church itself drew: “Both were equally revered as martyrs by the Roman Church: certain proof that Hippolytus had made his peace with that Church before his death.” His remains were brought back and honoured; his feast is kept on 13 August; and a large marble statue of him seated in a chair — discovered in 1551, its sides engraved with his paschal cycle and a list of his works — stands today in the Vatican. Eusebius, writing a century before Jerome, already knew him as a churchman of the first rank, “who presided over another church,” author of “many … treatises” including the paschal work whose sixteen-year table the statue preserves. A schismatic on the usual reading, he became the rarest of things: an antipope in the calendar.4

What it took to make him one is itself telling. The memory of the schism was awkward enough that the tradition had to work to dissolve it. Pope Damasus, composing a verse epigram for Hippolytus’s grave in the later fourth century, could reconcile the saint with the schismatic only by casting him as a repentant heretic: Hippolytus “the presbyter” is reported to have adhered to the schism of Novatus (Novatian), but, on his way to martyrdom, told his followers who asked where they should go that they must all return to the catholic faith — a story Prudentius elaborated a generation later (“flee from the accursed schism of Novatus; restore yourselves to the Catholic people”). The Novatian label is a plain anachronism — Novatian’s schism postdates Hippolytus’s death — and Damasus flags his own source as hearsay in the line Haec audita refert Damasus, “Damasus reports these things as heard.” The details are confused; but the shape is exactly the one the argument predicts. Sainthood required that the schism be undone — by reconciliation and by blood — before the name could be received.5

The comparison

Set the two side by side and the variable stands out. On the church’s own reckoning schism is no lighter than heresy — Cyprian, the Carthaginian martyr who read Tertullian daily, taught that the man who “forsakes the Church of Christ” and “sets up a false throne” is cut off from the promises, that not even martyrdom washes out the sin of dividing the Church. By that standard Hippolytus committed the graver and more public offense: not a rigorist party inside his own congregation but a rival succession in the capital, maintained against three popes. Tertullian, by contrast, is faulted for prophecy and for discipline; whether he ever separated at all is the disputed load-bearing question of his timeline, and on the revisionist reading he led an ecclesiola in ecclesia and never set up a rival altar. Yet Hippolytus is a canonized saint and martyr, and Tertullian is the standing Latin example of the heretic. The verdicts run opposite to the gravity of the offenses — which is evidence that they were not fixed by the gravity of the offenses at all, but by the endings: Hippolytus reconciled and was martyred; Tertullian, so far as any source records, did neither. That is the same finding the capstone reaches from the other side, and Augustine states the governing rule outright when he says Cyprian’s error over rebaptism was “cleared away by the pruning-hook of martyrdom.”6

Adversarial assessment

What holds. As an isolation of the variable, the comparison is strong. It is a real and awkward fact that a man remembered as Rome’s first antipope stands in the calendar while Tertullian, whose worst is arguably less, does not; and the best explanation is not that the church weighed the two faults and found schism the lighter, but that the fates were decided elsewhere — by martyrdom and by a return to communion that the Damasan legend had to supply even where the history was cloudy. The verdict on a Father, this case shows, tracks the shape of his death and the after-life of his name at least as much as the content of his errors. That corroborates, from a fresh angle, the timeline’s recurring finding that Tertullian’s uncanonized-yet-uncondemned middle state is an accretion, not a sentence — the residue of what he lacked (a martyr’s death, a documented reconciliation), not the output of a court that judged his faults graver than a schismatic’s.

But the criterion it exposes is principled, not capricious. The unevenness is real; that it is unfair does not follow. Martyrdom and reconciliation are not arbitrary favouritism but a coherent theology the church has always avowed: blood-witness is the perfect confession, and return to the Church’s communion is exactly what repentance of a schism consists in. A verdict that turns on whether a man died for Christ and died in the Church’s peace is tracking something other than the bare gravity of his fault, but it is not tracking it wrongly. So the comparison establishes its narrow point — the verdicts do not track offense-gravity — without establishing the broad one that they are therefore unjust; the church could answer, with no incoherence, that Hippolytus is a saint because he reconciled and was martyred, and Tertullian is not because (on the reports) he did neither.7

And the antipope reconstruction is itself disputed. The crispest form of the comparison — “the first antipope is a saint” — leans on a reconstruction the specialists no longer hold with confidence. Pierre Nautin argued in 1947 that the works under Hippolytus’s name cannot be by one hand, splitting the corpus between two authors; Allen Brent, in the standard study of 1995, relocates both to Rome as members of a single “school” and reframes the quarrel: a Roman church that had not yet developed a single monarch-bishop was divided between communities in tension, not by a canonical election of a rival pope — so that “first antipope” is anachronistic, since one cannot be “anti” a pope before there is a monarch-bishop to oppose. Eusebius, the earliest witness, never names Hippolytus’s see at all, calling it only “another church.” If Brent is right the comparison softens — Hippolytus led a rival community, not a formal counter-papacy — but it does not collapse: a rival community in sustained tension with the bishop’s is still a breach of order graver in kind than anything charged against Tertullian, and the sainthood still followed the martyrdom. The reconstruction bends the sharpest edge of the argument without dissolving it.8

And Hippolytus’s cause was partly a contention for orthodoxy. The neat contrast — “jurisdiction pardoned, discipline condemned” — blurs on the one point that most helps Hippolytus. Tertullian broke toward a rigorist novelty; Hippolytus broke, in part, to defend the received doctrine of the Trinity against a pope he judged to have lapsed into modalism. By his own lights, and by the later church’s, he was on the orthodox side of a real doctrinal quarrel — which is precisely the kind of thing that later ages found it easier to forgive as zeal misdirected than to condemn as heresy. So the two men are not offenders of one kind unevenly rewarded; the schismatic who contended for the creed and the theologian who pressed a disciplinary rigour past the apostles are not clean comparanda, and the difference in their fates is not the pure product of the martyrdom-variable the argument wants to isolate.9

Assessment

contested. The Hippolytus comparandum is a strong and honest observation and a weak proof. It is strong in what it isolates: the Western verdict on a Father does not track the gravity of his fault but the manner of his death and the fortunes of his name, and a schismatic remembered as Rome’s first antipope, canonized after reconciliation and martyrdom, is the sharpest available demonstration — the same lesson the capstone draws, and the reason Tertullian, lacking both a martyr’s death and a documented return, was left in the middle. It is weak as a charge of injustice: the criterion it exposes — that martyrdom and reconciliation cover a multitude of sins — is a principled one the church openly holds, not caprice; the crisp “first antipope” framing rests on a reconstruction the specialists (Nautin, Brent) now doubt, with even the see unnamed by the earliest source; and Hippolytus’s break was partly a defence of orthodoxy, not the bare jurisdictional offense the contrast needs. Strong by the light of what it reveals about how such verdicts form, weaker by the light of the fairness it is meant to impugn, and turning on how much weight the disputed antipope story can bear. Contested.
