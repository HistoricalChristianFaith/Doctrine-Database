Is it coherent to keep Tertullian’s theology while withholding his name?

Type: synthesizing argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — the interpretation weighed here is that the church’s handling of Tertullian is fair on its own terms: it took his arguments — the rule-of-faith polemic, the Trinitarian vocabulary (trinitas, persona, substantia), the defence of the flesh and the resurrection — and gave him neither sainthood nor a formal condemnation, and that this refusal to do either is a precise judgement, not an evasion. Two halves of the claim come apart. The principle — that a writer’s teaching may be received where his authority is not — is not merely coherent but the church’s own settled hermeneutic, stated by Tertullian himself, applied by Cyprian, and defended in as many words by Jerome; on that the interpretation holds. And the double refusal does track the actual shape of his case: no doctrinal heresy was ever proven against him (Augustine expressly declines to found the charge on his doctrine), so no anathema; but a turn to the New Prophecy, rigorist novelties against apostolic permission, and — on the later reports — a schism, so no canonization. Where the claim overreaches is the word precise judgement: no court ever sat, so the posture was never a ruling at all but an accretion of a heresy-catalogue entry, a private book-list, and a lament; and the comparanda cut against precision, since Hippolytus, a schismatic on the usual reconstruction, is a saint because he was reconciled and martyred, Cyprian’s rebaptism error cost him nothing because he died in communion and a martyr, and Origen drew anathemas Tertullian never did for reasons partly political — so martyrdom, reconciliation, and the accidents of transmission, as much as any weighing of Tertullian’s faults, explain why he landed in the middle. A defensible outcome reached by drift, not a calibrated verdict: strong both ways. Contested.

This page is the umbrella of the case, and it presupposes the others. Elsewhere the timeline asks whether Tertullian ever left the church, what he was actually faulted for, whether the biography the West repeats is even his, and whether the counts that stuck — the denial of second repentance, the ban on remarriage, the rule against flight — were defensible readings of an unsettled discipline. This one steps back from the counts to the shape of the whole: given all of that, is the verdict the church actually reached — read him, quote him, learn from him, and never canonize or anathematize him — a fair one? It is the exact mirror, on the Latin side, of the question asked of the Greek East in Origen’s case, where the church condemned the man three centuries on while its greatest readers went on mining him — and it is worth asking plainly, because the treatment looks, at first glance, like having things both ways.

The claim

I have praised the commentator but not the theologian, the man of intellect but not the believer, the philosopher but not the apostle… The blessed Cyprian takes Tertullian for his master, as his writings prove; yet, delighted as he is with the ability of this learned and zealous writer, he does not join him in following Montanus and Maximilla.

— Jerome, Letter 84.2, to Pammachius and Oceanus (c. 400), NPNF2 vol. 6. Jerome is defending his own reading of Origen, and he reaches for exactly the distinction this page tests: a heretic’s learning can be received without his error, his arguments taken up without his authority, and the model he cites for it is the very pairing at issue here — Cyprian, the martyr-bishop and saint, reading Tertullian daily and calling him “the master” (da magistrum) while declining to follow him into the New Prophecy. The interpretation weighed here holds that this is not a dodge but a principle, and that the church applied it to Tertullian consistently: it kept what he argued and withheld what would have ratified him — a canonization it never granted, and, equally, a condemnation it never pronounced.1

Who advances it

The distinction is not a modern apologetic construction; it is the church’s own, and it was Tertullian who stated it most sharply. Answering the objection that trusted teachers sometimes defect, he had written in the Prescription that a later lapse leaves an earlier truth untouched: “Do we prove the faith by the persons, or the persons by the faith?” That rule — test the teaching, not the teacher — is the one the church went on to apply to him. Jerome made it explicit in Letter 84 and again in his biographical notice, where Cyprian’s reading of “the master” is the very model of quarrying a heretic’s genius without his creed; Vincent of Lérins built a whole chapter on it, praising Tertullian’s learning to the skies and then mourning, not anathematizing, his fall; and modern reference works describe his status in just these double terms — the father of Latin theology whom the church never made a saint. René Braun’s study of his theological Latin, Deus Christianorum, traces how completely the vocabulary the church kept — trinitas, persona, substantia, satisfactio, sacramentum — is his coinage, received on its merits and detached entirely from the question of his authority.2

The evidence for

The teaching-vs-teacher distinction is coherent, and it is the church’s settled practice. Receiving an argument commits you to the argument, not to the person who framed it; the two can come apart without contradiction. The church does this routinely — it reads Origen for his exegesis while condemning his speculations, it keeps Cyprian’s ecclesiology while rejecting his rebaptism — and it does it because the alternative is absurd: to bind the truth of a proposition to the sanctity of whoever first advanced it is exactly the error (“prove the faith by the persons”) Tertullian himself refuted. That he happens to be the writer to whom the principle was later applied is a fitting irony, not an incoherence.3

What the church kept is genuinely his, and genuinely central. This is not a case of a few salvageable scraps. The West states its central mystery in words Tertullian coined; it argued against heretics for centuries on the model of his praescriptio; it defended the resurrection of the flesh and the reality of Christ’s humanity along lines he drew. If the reception were a polite fiction — a way of quietly keeping a condemned man’s ideas while disowning him — one would expect the debt to be marginal and the borrowings furtive. Instead the debt is structural and, in the vocabulary, total; the arguments were received on their merits, in the open, by writers who knew whose they were. The distinction is doing real work, not covering an embarrassment.4

The double refusal corresponds to the double shape of his case. An anathema is the church’s verdict on a doctrinal heresiarch; a canonization is its verdict on a saint. Tertullian fits neither slot, and the refusal of both is what fitting neither slot looks like. On the doctrinal side the charge was never made to stick: Augustine, cataloguing him among the heresies, names the corporealism of soul and God and then expressly refuses to rest the heresy on it — “yet he is not on this account said to have been made a heretic” — grounding it instead on the turn to the Cataphrygians, the ban on second marriage, and schism. Those are disciplinary and jurisdictional faults, real enough to forfeit sainthood but not the stuff of an anathema. So the church withheld the honour his lapse forfeited and withheld the condemnation his orthodoxy did not earn. That is not indecision; it is two accurate refusals.5

And no genuine act ever named him — which is the point. The spine of the whole timeline is a negative finding: no synod tried Tertullian, no council condemned him, and no authentic papal act carries his name. The one document that looks like an official Roman censure — the placing of his books among the “apocrypha” in the so-called Decretum Gelasianum — turns out on inspection not to be papal at all but a private sixth-century scholar’s handbook, later mistaken for a decree (as von Dobschütz’s edition and Burkitt’s summary established). The church precisely did not do to Tertullian what it did to a doctrinal heresiarch. That restraint, on this reading, is not an oversight but a judgement: it declined to receive his books and left him uncanonized, and it declined to summon, try, or anathematize a man it had not convicted of heresy.6

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The principle is sound and the outcome is defensible. Nothing in reason bars receiving a teaching while withholding endorsement of the teacher, the church has always done exactly that, and the shape of Tertullian’s case — brilliant and orthodox in its arguments, faulty in its discipline and (on the reports) schismatic in its end — maps onto the double refusal cleanly. To the extent the claim says the treatment is coherent, and not a case of wanting things both ways, it is right.

But “a precise judgement” overstates a thing that was never adjudicated. A judgement is an act; here there was no act. As this timeline’s own finding puts it, the condemnation of Tertullian is a thing “assembled from catalogues and laments rather than pronounced from a chair.” The heresy-catalogue entry is Augustine’s, two centuries late; the book-list is anonymous and not papal; Vincent’s chapter is a teaching example, not a sentence. To call the sum of these a precise judgement is to read design into drift. The church did not weigh Tertullian and place him, with care, between the saint and the heresiarch; it never convened to weigh him at all, and the middle position is where a man ends up when no one ever brings the case. The outcome may be defensible, but it was not decided, and calling a non-decision a precise judgement flatters it.7

And the comparanda show the outcome turning on accident as much as on desert. If the placement tracked a calibrated verdict on his faults, men with comparable faults should have fared comparably. They did not. Hippolytus of Rome — on the usual reconstruction an antipope, the leader of a Roman schism far graver than anything charged to Tertullian — is a canonized saint, because he was reconciled before the end and died a martyr in the mines. Cyprian held and pressed, against the bishop of Rome, a doctrine of rebaptism the church would formally reject at Arles and Nicaea; it cost him nothing, and Augustine, defending his memory against the Donatists, argued that the martyr’s error was purged by the martyr’s blood — the very leniency Tertullian, who was not martyred, never received. Origen, whose orthodoxy was at least as defensible as Tertullian’s and by some lights more so, drew a string of anathemas three centuries on, in a controversy everyone grants was partly political. The variable that best predicts these outcomes is not the gravity of the doctrinal error but whether the man died reconciled and a martyr, and how the later centuries happened to use or fear his name. Tertullian was neither reconciled in memory nor martyred, and his books nearly vanished; that he was left in the middle owes as much to those facts as to any precise reckoning of what he taught.8

Yet “evasion” is too strong the other way. To fold the counterweight honestly: an evasion is a dodge of a duty owed, and it is not clear the church owed Tertullian any verdict it failed to give. No one brought a heresy case because, on the doctrine, there may have been no case to bring; no one canonized him because the grounds for canonization were absent. A defensible outcome reached without deliberation is still a defensible outcome — the drift did not land him anywhere the evidence forbids. So the truth sits between the claim and its denial: the treatment is neither the precise judgement its defenders call it nor the evasion its critics suspect, but a defensible posture arrived at by omission, which the accidents of martyrdom and transmission would have shaped the same way whatever a tribunal decided. That is enough to acquit it of incoherence, not enough to credit it with precision.

Assessment

contested. The load-bearing half of the interpretation is sound: the church may receive a writer’s arguments while withholding his authority without contradiction, this is its own long-standing hermeneutic — Tertullian’s own rule, Cyprian’s practice, Jerome’s defence — and the double refusal of both sainthood and anathema does correspond to the real shape of his case, in which the arguments were orthodox and central while the faults that stuck were disciplinary and schismatic. To that extent the treatment is fair on its own terms and is no case of having it both ways. What the claim overreaches is the framing of that outcome as a precise judgement rather than an evasion. It was not a judgement in any deliberate sense: no court sat, and the verdict is an accretion of a late catalogue entry, an anonymous book-list wrongly thought papal, and a lament — design read into drift. And it was not, on the evidence, a calibrated placement of Tertullian between saint and heresiarch, since the comparanda — Hippolytus sainted for his martyrdom, Cyprian’s error covered by his, Origen anathematized in a partly political quarrel — show the outcome turning heavily on whether a man died reconciled and a martyr and on how his name was later used, variables that ran against Tertullian for reasons extraneous to his faults. Defensible as an outcome, overstated as a judgement, and neither the precise verdict its defenders claim nor the evasion its critics allege: strong pulls both ways, and no clear winner. The same paradox, and the same tension between a defensible result and an indefensible process, is weighed on the Greek side in Origen’s case — where Rome is asked whether it condemned the books only and not the man, and whether the whole proceeding was driven by politics rather than doctrine. Contested.
