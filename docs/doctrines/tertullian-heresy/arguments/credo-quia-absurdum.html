Did Tertullian teach fideism — “I believe because it is absurd”?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: sound — the interpretation weighed here is that Tertullian was no fideist: the tag by which he is best remembered, credo quia absurdum (“I believe because it is absurd”), is a sentence he never wrote, and the passage behind it — On the Flesh of Christ 5, “the fact is certain, because it is impossible” — is not a renunciation of reason but a tightly-argued reductio against Marcion’s docetism that turns on a recognised rhetorical figure, the Aristotelian topos that a claim too improbable for anyone to have invented is credible for that very reason. That much is near-consensus among the specialists (Moffatt, Sider, Osborn, Harrison), and the misquotation’s pedigree is now traced in detail: the maxim was assembled in early-modern England and sharpened by Voltaire into a weapon of Enlightenment anti-clericalism, centuries after Tertullian, whose own corpus is a monument of Stoic logic and Roman legal reasoning. So the fideism charge, as popularly grounded on the misquotation and a misread genre, does not hold. Two honest qualifications keep this from being a clean acquittal of every charge in the neighbourhood, and are folded in below: the real On the Prescription of Heretics 7 (“what has Athens to do with Jerusalem?”) does press a severe claim that philosophy breeds heresy, and Tertullian’s later appeal to the Paraclete’s oracles is a genuine subordination of argument to a claimed authority. Neither, however, resurrects the spurious maxim or makes him the anti-rationalist of legend; both qualify the verdict rather than overturn it. On its best case the interpretation holds. Sound.

This page stands a little apart from the doctrinal and disciplinary counts weighed elsewhere in the case against Tertullian. Those ask whether particular things he taught — the corporeal soul, the subordination of the Son, the ban on second marriage — were errors. This one asks about his reputation for a cast of mind: the single most-quoted line ever hung on him is one he did not write, and the popular picture of Tertullian as the father of “faith against reason” rests on it. A verdict built partly on a fabricated quotation is exactly the kind of thing a timeline of how a reputation forms ought to show, which is why it earns a page here even though no synod, and no ancient writer, ever charged him with fideism at all.

The claim

The Son of God was crucified; I am not ashamed because men must needs be ashamed of it. And the Son of God died; it is by all means to be believed, because it is absurd. And He was buried, and rose again; the fact is certain, because it is impossible.

— Tertullian, On the Flesh of Christ (De carne Christi) 5, ANF vol. 3. The Latin runs: Crucifixus est Dei Filius; non pudet, quia pudendum est. Et mortuus est Dei Filius; prorsus credibile est, quia ineptum est. Et sepultus resurrexit; certum est, quia impossibile. The famous slogan — credo quia absurdum, “I believe because it is absurd” — is nowhere in it, nor anywhere else in Tertullian. What he actually wrote is credibile est, quia ineptum est (“it is to be believed, because it is unfitting”) and certum est, quia impossibile (“it is certain, because it is impossible”) — not “I believe,” not “absurd,” and not a bare avowal of irrationality but the punch-line of a chapter-long argument. The interpretation weighed here reads the sentence in its place: it is an argument, in a treatise out to prove — against Marcion’s claim that Christ’s flesh was a phantom — that Christ’s birth, death, and burial were real precisely because they are the sort of shameful, “unfitting” things no one would fabricate for a God. Read so, the words presuppose a correlation of faith and reason, not a rejection of it.1

Who advances it

The reading is the settled position of the students of the text. James Moffatt, in a much-cited note of 1916, found the key by observing that here Tertullian “follows in the footsteps of that cool philosopher Aristotle”: in Rhetoric 2.23.22 Aristotle sets out a recognised topos of proof — that an argument can be drawn from the very improbability of a story, since some things are so unlikely to be invented that their improbability is itself a reason to credit them. Robert D. Sider, taking this up in his 1980 article “Credo Quia Absurdum?”, stresses that “the difference between the imputed and actual words is striking and important,” and that so read the passage is, far from an anti-rational manifesto, “a manifesto on behalf of reason in religious faith,” fitting a treatise whose aim is to demonstrate that Christ’s flesh was exactly what the senses reported. Eric Osborn’s study of Tertullian presses the same point at book length; and Peter Harrison’s 2017 history of the phrase, while it grants the paradox its bite, documents that the anti-rationalist reading is a later construction laid over a text that will not bear it. Behind them stands T. D. Barnes’s portrait of a Tertullian trained in rhetoric and law, at home in Stoic logic.2

The evidence for

The slogan is not his, and the real words say something else. No text of Tertullian contains credo quia absurdum. The nearest thing is the trio of clauses in De carne Christi 5, and their force is comparative, not confessional: Tertullian is not reporting his own psychology (“I believe because…”) but arguing that the shamefulness and impossibility of the incarnation and resurrection tell for their reality against a heretic who would explain them away. Even the English “absurd” is a translator’s liberty: ineptum means “unfitting,” “in poor taste,” the very “foolishness” (1 Cor. 1) that God chose to confound the wise, which Tertullian has been expounding for two chapters. To lift the line out of that frame and recast it as “I believe because it is absurd” is to change both the words and the sense.3

The passage is a step in a reductio, and the logic is Aristotle’s. The chapter answers Marcion clause by clause: was God really crucified? then he really died; and having died, really rose. If Christ’s sufferings were a phantom’s, then Paul preached falsely, “false, therefore, is our faith also,” and the whole hope is void. Against that, Tertullian argues that the reality of the flesh is secured by the very things Marcion finds unworthy of God — birth, shame, death. The “certain because impossible” is the pivot of a proof a fortiori: no one inventing a God would invent one so humiliated, so the humiliation authenticates the history. That is exactly Aristotle’s topos of the incredible-yet-true, deployed by a writer who elsewhere reasons like the advocate he was.4

His corpus is saturated with reasoned argument, not fideist retreat. The man of the “faith against reason” caricature wrote the most closely-argued Latin of his age. The Prescription of Heretics is built on a point of Roman procedural law (the praescriptio that bars a suit before its merits); A Treatise on the Soul opens by promising to refute Plato by argument and marshals the Stoics syllogism by syllogism; Against Hermogenes and Against Praxeas are exercises in dialectic; the Apology argues the Christian case on the pagans’ own legal and rational ground. Whatever else Tertullian was, he was a relentless arguer, and modern study finds his thought shaped through and through by Stoic logic and the categories of the courtroom — the opposite of a mind that despised reason.5

The misquotation has a datable history, and it is not ancient. The paradox entered wide circulation only when Tertullian’s works began to be printed, in the sixteenth century. Sir Thomas Browne, in Religio Medici (1643), was the first prominent author to quote it — and he quoted the accurate form: “I can answer all the objections of Satan and my rebellious reason with that odd resolution I learned of Tertullian, Certum est quia impossibile est.” From there a “credo” version — “I believe because it is impossible” — became common in seventeenth-century England, and it was the Enlightenment philosophe Voltaire who added the “absurdity condition” and gave the modern world “I believe because it is absurd,” deployed to cast religion as an epistemic vice. The tag that indicts Tertullian, in short, is a product of the anti-clerical polemic of the 1600s and 1700s, projected back onto a second-century apologist who argued for a living.6

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The core of the interpretation is not merely defensible but, at this point, the consensus of the field. Credo quia absurdum is a phrase Tertullian never wrote; the sentence behind it is a compressed argument, not a boast of irrationality; its logic is a standard rhetorical topos of the improbable-yet-credible; and the writer who supposedly enshrined faith against reason left behind one of the most tightly-reasoned bodies of work in early Christian Latin. The popular picture of Tertullian the fideist rests on a misquotation and a misreading of genre, and on the evidence it fails. To that extent the claim is sound.

But “Athens and Jerusalem” is a real and severe thesis, not another misquote. The debunking of the false tag must not be allowed to launder a genuine one. In The Prescription of Heretics 7, Tertullian does write, in his own words and at length, that philosophy is “the material of the world’s wisdom, the rash interpreter of the nature and dispensation of God,” that “heresies are themselves instigated by philosophy” — Valentinus from Plato’s school, Marcion’s god from the Stoics — and then: “What indeed has Athens to do with Jerusalem? What concord is there between the Academy and the Church?… Away with all attempts to produce a mottled Christianity of Stoic, Platonic, and dialectic composition!” This is a real and pointed hostility to philosophy as a source of doctrine, and it is his settled view, not a rhetorical slip. Two things keep it from making him a fideist even so: the passage is itself an argument (that heresy is philosophy’s bastard), reasoned from named examples; and its target is speculative philosophy as a rival authority to revelation, not reason as such — the same Tertullian borrows Stoic logic without embarrassment when it serves the faith. But the interpretation overreaches if it uses the death of the slogan to paint him a friend of the philosophers. He was not; he thought their schools bred heretics.7

And the later Tertullian does subordinate argument to a claimed authority. The clean picture of a pure rationalist also has to reckon with the Montanist works, where the decisive move in a dispute is no longer a syllogism but an oracle of the Paraclete. He forbids second marriage not finally by exegesis but because “the Paraclete… has abolished” the apostle’s concession; he forbids flight in persecution on the strength of a New-Prophecy utterance (“seek not to die… but to die the martyr’s death”); he grounds the whole progressive tightening of discipline on the Spirit’s leading men “into all truth.” This is a genuine subordination of argument to authority — not the caricatured “absurdity” fideism, but a real appeal past reasoning to revelation, and it belongs in an honest verdict. It is weighed on its own where it bites hardest, in the ban on second marriage and the rule against flight. It shows that Tertullian was no rationalist without remainder; it does nothing to rehabilitate the sentence he never wrote.8

Assessment

sound. The interpretation is right on the thing it actually claims. Credo quia absurdum is not Tertullian’s; the words behind it, certum est, quia impossibile, are the hinge of a reasoned argument against Marcion, turning on a recognised topos of the credible-because-improbable rather than on any contempt for reason; and the writer to whom the slogan is fastened was, in fact, among the most rigorously argumentative theologians the early church produced, trained in rhetoric and law and steeped in Stoic logic. The pedigree of the misquotation — assembled in Reformation-era and Enlightenment polemic, finished by Voltaire — confirms that the fideist Tertullian is a modern invention, not an ancient fact. This part of the case is at or near scholarly consensus, and it holds. What the interpretation must not be stretched to say is that Tertullian was a friend of reason without qualification: his own Prescription 7 makes philosophy the mother of heresies, and his later works do subordinate argument to the Paraclete’s oracles. Those are real, and they are folded into the verdict as its limits — but they qualify a rating rather than defeat it, since neither restores the spurious maxim or the caricature it feeds. Judged on its own claim, the reading is well-grounded and unrefuted. Sound.
