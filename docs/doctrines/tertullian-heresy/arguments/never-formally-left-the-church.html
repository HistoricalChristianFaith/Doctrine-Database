Did Tertullian ever formally leave the catholic church?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — on the interpretation weighed here, Tertullian never formally separated from the church of Carthage: he ended his life not in a breakaway sect but as the voice of a rigorist current inside the congregation — an ecclesiola in ecclesia — and the “Montanist schism” and the later “Tertullianist sect” alike are largely constructions of fourth- and fifth-century heresiology reading a later category back onto a third-century situation. The positive evidence is real and has convinced much of the modern field: his own late works record no synod, no excommunication, and no departure; they place the New Prophecy’s visionary inside the ordinary Sunday assembly; they insist the quarrel touched discipline, not the creed; and they argue to fellow Christians as an insider, marking himself off from the “heretics” even as he scolds the “Psychics.” But the reading does not carry cleanly. On Modesty sets a “church of the Spirit” against “the church which consists of a number of bishops”; the Psychici polemic is separatist in tone; and Augustine, two centuries later, reports an actual Tertullianist body with its own basilica in Carthage that survived into his day — a datum the silence-based case cannot dissolve. The question is genuinely disputed, and is left disputed here.

Related arguments: the fairness of the verdict — see also Was the New Prophecy heretical in doctrine, or only in discipline and church order? · Was his denial of church forgiveness for adultery and apostasy a defensible reading of early discipline, or a Montanist novelty? · Was his ban on second marriage a severe reading of Paul, or a false doctrine?

This is the load-bearing question of the “Was Tertullian a Heretic?” timeline: nearly every later verdict presupposes an answer to it. The two contested claims of Jerome’s notice (whether Tertullian was ordained a presbyter, and whether resentment of the Roman clergy drove his turn to the New Prophecy) bear on it, and the reality of the sect Augustine names turns on it.

The claim

…the African adherents of the New Prophecy formed, not a schismatic body, but an ecclesiola in ecclesia.

— Douglas Powell, “Tertullianists and Cataphrygians,” Vigiliae Christianae 29 (1975), link.1 The interpretation weighed here is the revisionist thesis that has organized the field since 1971: that Tertullian, for all his embrace of the Paraclete and his war on the “soulish” Christians, never in fact walked out of the catholic church of Carthage. On this reading he was a rigorist agitating from within, not a schismatic founding a rival communion; the “schism” is something later reporters — Jerome, then Augustine — supplied, reading the sharper fourth- and fifth-century sense of “Montanism” and a documented Carthaginian sect back onto a man whose own writings announce neither.

Who advances it

The turn is T. D. Barnes’s Tertullian (1971), which stripped Jerome’s notice of its load-bearing “facts,” and above all Douglas Powell’s 1975 article “Tertullianists and Cataphrygians,” which supplied the governing phrase — the African New Prophecy as an ecclesiola in ecclesia, a rigorist “church within the church” that was neither excommunicated nor excommunicating. David Rankin’s Tertullian and the Church (1995) argued the case at book length from Tertullian’s own ecclesiology; Geoffrey Dunn’s Tertullian (2004) made it the working assumption of the standard English handbook; and David Wilhite’s Tertullian the African (2007) pressed it furthest — a layman who never left. David F. Wright had already put the thesis in a sentence: the Tertullianists “remained a Holy Club within the catholic church, ecclesiola in ecclesia, neither excommunicated nor excommunicating.”2 It is worth marking that Barnes, the movement’s founder, is also its most cautious member: he demolished Jerome’s biography without committing to the strong claim that Tertullian died inside the church, and the positive thesis is Powell’s and Wilhite’s more than his.

The evidence for

His own writings record no exit. The single most striking feature of Tertullian’s late, openly New-Prophecy works is what they do not contain: no synod that tried him, no excommunication he suffered or pronounced, no notice of a congregation he left or founded. He announces the reception of the Paraclete and a withdrawal from the “carnally-minded” — “We indeed, on our part, subsequently withdrew from the carnally-minded on our acknowledgment and maintenance of the Paraclete” — but this reads, in context, as a party’s self-separation in spirit within the one church, not a departure from its communion; no institutional rupture is anywhere described.3

The visionary operates inside the ordinary Sunday assembly. The clearest single datum is On the Soul 9, where the New Prophecy’s gifts are exercised not in a breakaway conventicle but in the regular liturgy of the catholic congregation: “We have now amongst us a sister whose lot it has been to be favoured with sundry gifts of revelation, which she experiences in the Spirit by ecstatic vision amidst the sacred rites of the Lord’s day in the church… After the people are dismissed at the conclusion of the sacred services, she is in the regular habit of reporting to us whatever things she may have seen in vision.” The prophesying happens amidst the sacred rites of the Lord’s day in the church, after which “the people are dismissed” — the setting is the normal Sunday assembly, and the prophet reports to that assembly’s ministers.4

By his own account the quarrel is over discipline, not the creed. In On Fasting he states plainly that the New Prophecies are rejected “not that Montanus and Priscilla and Maximilla preach another God, nor that they disjoin Jesus Christ (from God), nor that they overturn any particular rule of faith or hope, but that they plainly teach more frequent fasting than marrying.” A dispute over fasting and remarriage is a dispute over rigour, not doctrine — the kind of quarrel a rigorist party has with its own church, not the kind that founds a heresy. And Against Praxeas, the very treatise that gives the Latin West the word “Trinity,” is an openly New-Prophecy work: the vocabulary the church kept and the allegiance it later faulted issue from the same pen in the same years, which is hard to square with a man who had by then placed himself outside the church whose faith he was defining.5

He argues to fellow Christians as an insider, not at them as an outsider. Tertullian marks himself off sharply from actual heretics even while scolding the ordinary Catholics. On Monogamy opens by placing three groups on a scale — “Heretics do away with marriages; Psychics accumulate them” — with his own “Spiritual” party as the mean between them: the heretics are the outsiders, the Psychici are erring brethren he is trying to correct, and he stands with neither extreme. The whole rhetorical posture of the late works is intramural: he writes as a member of the church disputing his fellow-members’ laxity, appealing to the Paraclete’s discipline for a body he still counts his own, not as a sectary addressing a rival communion from outside.6

No contemporary calls him a schismatic, and the next generation used him. The argument from silence is set out at length on the timeline and only summarized here: no third-century source names Tertullian a heretic; the Montanism-hunters of his own century (Hippolytus, the anonymous Against All Heresies) catalogue the sect without him; and Cyprian of Carthage — on Jerome’s report, a daily reader who called him “the master” — built whole treatises on him a generation later. A man remembered as the founder of a Carthaginian schism would be an odd figure for the next bishop of Carthage to quarry so freely.7

Adversarial assessment

“The church of the Spirit” against “the church of bishops.” The hardest text for the revisionist reading is Tertullian’s own. Answering a bishop’s edict relaxing the penance for adultery, On Modesty 21 denies that the power of the keys belongs to the hierarchy at all: the church “will forgive sins: but (it will be) the Church of the Spirit, by means of a spiritual man; not the Church which consists of a number of bishops.” That is not the language of loyal opposition within one communion; it opposes a true church (of the Spirit, of the spiritual man) to a merely institutional one (of the bishops), and comes as close as any ancient text to setting a “spiritual” church over against the visible one. A rigorist who still counts the bishops’ church his own does not ordinarily deny that it can bind and loose. The ecclesiola reading has to treat this as heated rhetoric rather than a claim about where the church really is — a possible reading, but a strained one.8

The Psychici language is separatist in tone. To divide Christendom into “Spiritual” and “soulish” by their reception of the Paraclete, and to say of the great body of the church that “the things which are the Spirit’s are not pleasing” to them, is to draw a party line through the congregation. The revisionist can answer that a line drawn through a church is not yet a wall built around a sect — and that is fair as far as it goes. But the vocabulary is the vocabulary of two peoples, not one, and it is at least as compatible with a group on its way out as with a caucus content to stay in.9

Augustine reports an actual sect with a building. The gravest difficulty is not a matter of tone but of fact. Augustine, writing at Carthage two centuries later, records a distinct Tertullianistae — a body that dwindled “little by little down to our own time,” whose last few members, in his own day, “went over into the catholic church and handed their basilica… over to the catholic church.” A sect with a name, a building, and a documented reconciliation is not nothing; and Augustine derives it “from Tertullian” (sicut scripta eius indicant). The revisionist reply — that Augustine infers the founder from the books, that the group may be a later Carthaginian rigorist circle that took the name, or the local New Prophecy congregation relabelled — is reasonable, and the succession from Tertullian himself is genuinely unproven. But it cannot make the basilica disappear: by the late fourth century there really was a separate Tertullianist body in Carthage outside catholic communion, whatever its exact descent, and the burden is on the “he never left” thesis to explain how a man who never separated came to head a separated church that bore his name.10

The silence cuts both ways, and Jerome is a witness too. The absence of a contemporary “schismatic” verdict is the ground of the revisionist case, but silence is not testimony: that no one in the third century called him a schismatic no more proves he stayed in than Cyprian’s refusal to name him proves he was out. And the earliest connected report we have — Jerome, c. 392 — says flatly that he “lapsed to the doctrine of Montanus” and wrote “directly against the church” (proprie adversus ecclesiam). Jerome is late, dependent, and demonstrably careless with Tertullian’s biography, so his verb lapsus est settles nothing on its own — but it is positive evidence for the traditional reading, and it cannot simply be subtracted from the ledger and the silence counted as though it stood alone.11

Assessment

contested. The revisionist reading is strong where it is negative and weaker where it turns positive. That Tertullian records no synod, no excommunication, and no formal exit; that his New Prophecy operated inside the Sunday assembly; that he insisted the quarrel touched discipline, not the creed; that he argued as an insider and was mined without apology by the next generation — all of this is real, and it is decisive against the crude picture of a clean break into a fully-formed heretical sect. To that extent the mainstream since Barnes and Powell is right, and “Tertullian the schismatic heresiarch” is largely a construction of later heresiology. But the strong form — that he never in any sense separated — overreaches the evidence. On Modesty 21 opposes the church of the Spirit to the church of the bishops in terms hard to read as loyal opposition; the Psychici polemic draws a line through the congregation that a departing party would also draw; and Augustine’s Tertullianist basilica is a hard historical datum, however contestable its descent, that the silence-based case cannot dissolve. What survives is the moderate reading: during his lifetime Tertullian most probably remained, in the technical sense, within the Carthaginian church, agitating as a rigorist faction rather than a rival communion — but the materials for a later separation were in his own rhetoric, a separated body bearing his name did exist by Augustine’s day, and whether the man himself ever crossed the line is exactly the point the sources leave open. It is a genuine dispute, strong scholars on both sides, and no verdict the evidence compels.
