Was Tertullian’s subordination of the Son one of order and origin — not of nature?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Related arguments: his theology — see also the summary’s grouped treatment and its sibling, was Tertullian’s corporeal soul crude materialism, or a defensible anti-Platonism? · Assessment: contested — the anachronism at the core is real. Tertullian wrote a full century before Nicaea, in the shared idiom of every ante-Nicene theologian, and against a modalist who had collapsed the persons into one; his gradation of Father, Son, and Spirit is a subordination of order and derivation within a single undivided substance, the exact opposite of the Arian claim that the Son is of another substance and made from nothing. He gives the West una substantia and insists the Son is “God of God,” drawn from the Father’s own being — the germ of the homoousion. But two things keep the defence from carrying the whole way. His portio language — the Father “the entire substance,” the Son “a derivation and portion of the whole” — measures a gradation within the Godhead that the later definition of full equality refused; and in Against Hermogenes he says outright that “there was … a time … nor the Son,” which no reading squares cleanly with the eternal generation Nicaea required. The narrow claim survives — his subordination is one of origin, not of inequality of nature, and to brand him an Arian is to judge a pre-Nicene man by a post-Nicene rule — but the sweeping claim, that the subordination is a merely verbal accident later readers invented, overreaches. Compare the parallel defence made for Origen’s subordination of the Son, where the same anachronism is at stake for the Greek East.

The claim

The charge answered here is that the father of the Latin word trinitas was himself a subordinationist — that alongside the formula Latin orthodoxy would keep he ranks the Son below the Father in a way the Church would later declare heretical. The texts cited for it are genuine. In Against Praxeas the Son is not the Father’s equal but His measured part:

the Father is the entire substance, but the Son is a derivation and portion of the whole, as He Himself acknowledges: “My Father is greater than I.”

— Against Praxeas 9.1 The persons stand “in their order” — “three … not in condition, but in degree; not in substance, but in form” — the Son “the second degree” and the Spirit “the third … by reason of the order observed in the Economy.”2 And in Against Hermogenes, arguing that God was not always “Lord” and “Father” because there was not always something to be Lord and Father of, he says the thing an Arian could quote unaltered: “There was, however, a time when neither sin existed with Him, nor the Son; the former of which was to constitute the Lord a Judge, and the latter a Father.”3

The interpretation weighed here is the defence against that charge, in its advocated form: that this language is subordination of order and origin — the Son second because derived from the Father, as a ray from the sun or a river from its spring — and not of nature; that Tertullian insists throughout on the Son’s unity of substance with the Father, which is precisely what Nicaea would assert; and that to read him through the precision the fourth century forged, and file him with Arius, is an anachronism.

Who advances it

It is close to the standing verdict of patristic scholarship on Tertullian’s Trinity. The nineteenth-century editor of the standard English text noted at this very passage that Tertullian “uses expressions which in aftertimes, when controversy had introduced greater precision of language, were studiously avoided by the orthodox … After Arius, the language of theology received greater precision; but as it is, there is no doubt of the orthodoxy of Tertullian’s doctrine.”4 The judgement has held: René Braun’s standard study of his theological vocabulary, and after it J. N. D. Kelly and Eric Osborn, read the una substantia as Tertullian’s genuine anticipation of the homoousion and his subordination as the common ante-Nicene grammar of derivation — not a first draft of Arianism.5 The defence has a fourth-century warrant of its own: the treatise that carries the subordinationist lines is the West’s first great treatise against a confusion of the persons, and its whole labour is to keep the Son really distinct from the Father while really one God with Him.

The evidence for

The subordination is one of origin, and the substance is one and undivided. Tertullian’s “second” is the second of a source and its issue, not of a greater and a lesser god. His controlling images are all of undivided derivation: “the root and the tree … the fountain and the river … the sun and the ray are two forms, but coherent ones,” and “everything which proceeds from something else must needs be second to that from which it proceeds, without being on that account separated.”6 The Apology makes the point in the very language the charge wants: the Son is “God of God, as light is kindled of light … there is no division of substance, but merely an extension”; He “is made a second in manner of existence — in position, not in nature; and He did not withdraw from the original source, but went forth.”7 In gradu, non in statu — in degree, not in state — is the whole of it: a rank of order, expressly denied of substance.

He supplies the formula Nicene orthodoxy would keep. Against Praxeas he sets down the words the Latin West has confessed ever since: the dispensation “distributes the Unity into a Trinity, placing in their order the three Persons … three, however, not in condition, but in degree; not in substance, but in form; not in power, but in aspect; yet of one substance, and of one condition, and of one power.”8 Una substantia, tres personae is his, and “of one substance” is the sense the Greek homoousion would later carry. A man who first taught the West to say that the three are one substance cannot be the ancestor of the party that denied it.

His subordination is the opposite of Arius’s. The mark of Arianism is that the Son is ex ouk ontōn, made out of nothing, and heteroousios, of a substance other than the Father’s. Tertullian asserts the reverse at every turn: the Son is drawn from the Father’s own substance (“God of God”), a portio of it and not a creature made beside it; the ray is “still part of the parent mass, … the sun will still be in the ray.” Whatever the portio idiom lacks in later precision, it places the Son firmly on the Father’s side of the line Nicaea would draw between the begotten and the made.

The idiom is pre-Nicene and shared. The vocabulary the charge fastens on — the Son as “second,” ranked “in degree,” derived and lesser in order — is not peculiar to Tertullian. It is the common speech of every theologian before the Arian crisis forced the questions to precision: Justin’s Son “subordinate to the Maker of all things,” Origen’s “second God,” Novatian and Hippolytus in the same key. The co-equality and the technical sorting of “begotten” from “made” that would divide the orthodox from the Arian did not yet exist as tests; the language that fails them fails everywhere in the second and third centuries, and no historian of doctrine calls Justin or Novatian an Arian on that account. To single Tertullian out is to apply to one man a rule that would condemn the whole ante-Nicene church.

Adversarial assessment

The portio is more than economy, and it measures the Godhead. The steelman has to concede what the excerpts show. To call the Father “the entire substance” and the Son “a derivation and portion of the whole” is not merely to order the persons in their work ad extra; it grades the divine substance itself, treating it as a quantity of which the Father has the whole and the Son a part. The homoousion of 325 asserts one identical and undivided Godhead in which the Son is whole God of whole God, equal, nothing withheld; “portio” and the citation of “My Father is greater than I” as a statement about the divine natures, not the incarnate economy, run the other way. The editor who vouched for his orthodoxy conceded the same breath that his expressions were “studiously avoided by the orthodox” after Arius. Anachronism explains why he wrote so; it does not turn a graded substance into an equal one. Dating a difficulty is not dissolving it.

“There was a time … nor the Son” resists eternal generation. The Hermogenes sentence is the hardest datum for the defence, and the softening available to it is real but partial. Tertullian holds, with the apologists, that the Reason (ratio) was eternally in God and was “made perfect Son” only when uttered as Word (sermo) for the work of creation; so the defender can say the Word is co-eternal and only the title “Son” is dated. But this two-stage generation is itself the thing Nicaea excluded: it makes the Son’s being as Son begin, and ties His generation to the creation, where the council’s “begotten before all ages, … there was no ‘when’ He was not” makes the generation eternal and independent of the world. Origen, a subordinationist himself, is the one who supplied eternal generation; Tertullian did not, and on this point falls short of Nicaea rather than merely lacking its vocabulary.

The retrojection cuts both ways. If it is anachronistic to read Tertullian as an Arian, it is equally anachronistic to enlist him as a finished Nicene. His corpus contains a real consubstantiality and a real gradation, a real distinction of persons and a Son whose sonship begins; the honest description is not “Tertullian was Nicene and later readers misread him,” but “Tertullian was pre-Nicene, and both the homoousion and the subordination are genuinely in him.” The defence is entitled to the second sentence; it overreaches when it slides to the first and makes the subordination a verbal illusion.

“Not yet defined” is not “not mistaken.” As with the parallel defence of Origen, the exculpatory move slips between two claims. That Nicaea post-dates Tertullian by a century bears on his culpability — he cannot have transgressed a rule not yet made, and no synod ever charged him with a Trinitarian error — and not on whether “the Father is the entire substance, the Son a portion” is, by the later reckoning, exact. The council did not invent in 325 a truth that was false in 210; on the Church’s own account it declared what had always been the case. Tertullian is exculpable for using the idiom of his age; the idiom can still be, by the settled measure, imprecise.

Assessment

contested. By the lights of the history of doctrine the argument is strong: Tertullian wrote a century before Nicaea, in the shared ante-Nicene idiom of derivation, against a modalism that had erased the persons altogether; his subordination is of order and origin within one undivided substance, expressly “in position, not in nature,” and he is a genuine source of the una substantia the West would confess — so to file him with Arius, whose Son is of another substance and made from nothing, is a retrojection that would equally condemn Justin, Hippolytus, and Novatian. On that the defence holds, and it holds firmly. By the lights of systematic theology it falls short of clearing him: the portio language grades the one Godhead into whole and part in a way the homoousion refused, the appeal to “My Father is greater than I” is taken of the divine natures, and “there was … a time … nor the Son” ties the Son’s generation to the creation where Nicaea made it eternal — and dating these does not baptize them. The reading that survives the counter-evidence is the modest one: Tertullian’s subordination is one of origin and not of inequality of nature, and cannot be indicted as Arianism by a rule defined after his death — but it is a real subordination, not an optical illusion of later readers, and “anachronism” ends the trial for heresy without making his Trinity the finished orthodoxy of Nicaea. Fittingly, no council ever tried it: this is a shortfall the tradition quietly outgrew, not a charge on which its first Latin theologian was ever arraigned.
