Was Tertullian’s tradux peccati — sin inherited with the propagated soul — heterodox, or the germ of original sin?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Related arguments: his theology — see also the summary’s grouped treatment and its siblings, was Tertullian’s subordination of the Son one of order and origin, not of nature? and was Tertullian’s corporeal soul crude materialism, or a defensible anti-Platonism? · Assessment: contested — on the interpretation weighed here, Tertullian does not merely propagate the soul from Adam (the doctrine his corporeal-soul page adjudicates); he propagates the fault with it — an antecedent, “in a certain sense natural, evil which arises from its corrupt origin,” a vitium originis carried physically in the soul-seed and washed away only in the second birth. Read for its doctrinal substance, this is not an error but the germ of the church’s own teaching: Augustine built his mature doctrine of inherited sin on exactly this Tertullianic tradux, was never condemned for it, and could never give up the traducianism that carried it — so the fault-inheritance is, in orthodox hands, proto-Augustinian, and what the later verdicts against traducianism actually reach is the propagation of the soul (Aquinas: “heretical to say that the intellectual soul is transmitted with the semen”), not the transmission of the fault. That much holds, and it cleanly separates this charge from the corporeality beside it. But Tertullian’s framing over-reaches the doctrine it anticipates: he hardens the fault into “another nature having a god and father of its own,” a quasi-substance borne in the material soul-stuff — nearer the second-nature of the Manichees Augustine spent his life refuting than the privation-and-guilt of a wounded good nature the church settled into. He stops short of dualism (the God-given good, he insists, is “obscured” but never “extinguished”), but the reifying of sin as a rival natura is a real deficiency. Defensible in substance as the anticipation of original sin, over-reaching in its materialist framing of the fault as a second nature — strong by the light of doctrinal continuity, weak by the light of the later metaphysics of sin, with serious readers on both sides.

This charge lies one layer beneath the corporeal soul, and is distinct from it. That page asks whether Tertullian’s doctrine that the soul — and even God — is a body is defensible; this one asks about a further thing his De anima teaches in its closing chapters: that the soul carries down from Adam not only its substance but its guilt. The two are joined — it is because the soul is propagated (and, for Tertullian, corporeally propagated) that the fault can travel with it — but they are separable claims, and later readers who called traducianism heretical were mostly reaching for the propagation, not the inherited fault, which orthodoxy would keep. The doctrine’s full setting is on Tertullian’s page on the origin of the soul, where the inheritance of character does the arguing; here the inheritance of sin is weighed on its own.

The claim

Every soul, then, by reason of its birth, has its nature in Adam until it is born again in Christ; moreover, it is unclean all the while that it remains without this regeneration; and because unclean, it is actively sinful, and suffuses even the flesh (by reason of their conjunction) with its own shame.

There is, then, besides the evil which supervenes on the soul from the intervention of the evil spirit, an antecedent, and in a certain sense natural, evil which arises from its corrupt origin. For, as we have said before, the corruption of our nature is another nature having a god and father of its own, namely the author of (that) corruption.

— Tertullian, A Treatise on the Soul (De anima) 40–41, ANF vol. 3. The chapter that states it the editors of the translation head, in so many words, “Notwithstanding the Depravity of Man’s Soul by Original Sin…” The interpretation weighed here reads these chapters as a genuine, if early and roughly-cut, doctrine of original sin: the soul inherits from Adam a real antecedent corruption, prior to any actual sin and prior to the devil’s tempting, transmitted with the soul itself at generation and remitted only in the “second birth by water and the power from above.” It holds that this fault-inheritance is the church’s later doctrine in embryo, not the heterodoxy for which traducianism was faulted — and that Tertullian’s error, where he errs, is not in teaching an inherited fault but in the substance he makes of it.1

Who advances it

The reading is advanced, first, by the shape of Tertullian’s own text, which does not argue the inherited fault as a reason for traducianism but simply asserts it as doctrine, teaching that every soul is “in Adam” and unclean until regeneration and that its corruption is “antecedent” and “natural.” It is taken up, second and decisively, by Augustine, whose mature doctrine of the massa damnata — the whole race guilty in Adam and justly condemned apart from grace — leans on exactly this transmission of the fault, and who found traducianism attractive because it carried original sin so cleanly: he could never bring himself to creationism, on his own testimony, precisely because he could not see how a soul freshly made by God contracts Adam’s guilt. And it is the standing judgement of the historians of the doctrine, who trace the Western doctrine of original sin back through Augustine to Tertullian’s Carthage — the first place in Christian Latin where the fault is said to descend with the nature. So read, Tertullian is not the heresiarch of the handbooks but the first rough draftsman of a doctrine the church went on to make its own.2

The evidence for

Tertullian teaches an antecedent, inherited fault, not merely acquired sin. The force of De anima 41 is in the word “antecedent.” Tertullian carefully distinguishes two evils in the soul: the one “which supervenes… from the intervention of the evil spirit” — the sin the demon tempts a person into, which he had described in chapter 39 as fastening on the soul “from the very portal of their birth” — and, beneath it, “an antecedent, and in a certain sense natural, evil which arises from its corrupt origin.” This second evil is prior to any act, prior even to the tempter; it is the condition the soul is born in. That is precisely the structure of original sin as the church would later define it: a fault contracted by origin, distinct from and underlying every personal sin. Tertullian states it a full two centuries before the Pelagian controversy forced the doctrine into precision.3

The doctrine is remitted by regeneration — the mark of a genuine doctrine of inherited guilt. Tertullian does not leave the inherited fault as a bare pessimism; he ties it to baptism exactly as the later doctrine does. The unclean soul is sinful “until it is born again in Christ”; when it “embraces the faith, being renewed in its second birth by water and the power from above, then the veil of its former corruption being taken away, it beholds the light in all its brightness.” A fault that is washed away in baptism is a fault the soul had before baptism and did not commit — which is the whole logic on which the church baptizes infants for the remission of sin. Tertullian’s own hesitation about infant baptism elsewhere does not touch the doctrine here: the theology of an inherited stain that regeneration removes is fully present.4

Augustine built the mainstream doctrine on this very stone — and was never condemned for it. The strongest evidence that the fault-inheritance is not the heresy is that its greatest heir is a doctor of the church. Augustine’s doctrine of original sin transmitted from Adam to the whole race is the direct descendant of Tertullian’s tradux, and Augustine knew it: he leaned toward a propagated soul for precisely the reason Tertullian’s scheme supplied, that a soul which descends from Adam inherits his guilt as a branch inherits the disease of the root, whereas a soul freshly created by God does not. The difficulty is weighed on its own page; the point here is narrower. If the transmission of the fault with the soul were itself the heterodox thing, the theologian who made it the centre of Western anthropology could not have escaped the charge. He did. The doctrine Tertullian drafts is the doctrine the church keeps.5

The later condemnation of traducianism reaches the propagated soul, not the propagated fault. When the tradition finally hardens into a flat verdict — “Tertullian… and a few other heretics advocate Traducianism” (the Catholic Encyclopedia), “heretical to say that the intellectual soul is transmitted with the semen” (Aquinas) — what it condemns is the generation of the soul itself from the parents, which was thought to make the soul non-subsistent and mortal, and, behind it, the corporealism the propagation seemed to require. It is the mechanism, not the inheritance of guilt, that draws the anathema; Pope Anastasius II is likewise reported to have condemned in effect the propagation of souls, not the doctrine of original sin. The fault-transmission rides on the propagation as a passenger, and when the church threw out the vehicle it kept the passenger and re-seated it in a creationist frame. So the tradux peccati is not the root of the “traducianism is heretical” verdict; that root is the tradux animae.6

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The substance of the doctrine is proto-orthodox and genuinely separable from the corporealism. Tertullian teaches, before anyone else in Latin, an antecedent fault inherited with the nature from Adam and remitted by regeneration — the church’s own doctrine of original sin in its first rough form. Augustine made it foundational and was never faulted for it; the verdicts that later called traducianism heretical were aimed at the transmission of the soul, not of the fault. To that extent the interpretation is right, and the handbook framing that files the whole complex under “the traducianist heresy” blurs a distinction that matters: the church condemned the vehicle and kept the cargo.

But the framing reifies sin into a second nature. Where Tertullian goes beyond the doctrine he anticipates is the metaphysics of the fault. He does not say only that the soul is wounded or deprived of an original good; he says “the corruption of our nature is another nature having a god and father of its own, namely the author of (that) corruption.” That is to make sin a positive natura — a second nature, with the devil for its author — carried as a real ingredient in the material soul-stuff his corporealism supplies. Later orthodoxy, and Augustine above all, would insist against the Manichees on the exact opposite: that evil is no nature and no substance, but the privation and corruption of a good nature, so that original sin is a defect and a debt of guilt, not a thing with a being of its own. Tertullian’s language sits on the wrong side of the line the church would later draw against dualism, and it does so because his materialism lets it: a fault that travels physically in a corporeal soul is all too easily pictured as a stuff.7

Yet he stops short of dualism, and the overreach is of framing, not of thesis. The charge must not be overstated in its turn. Tertullian is not a Manichee: in the same breath he insists that the God-given good in the soul is only “obscured” by the corruption, never “extinguished” — “it can be obscured, indeed, because it is not God; extinguished, however, it cannot be, because it comes from God” — and that “even in the worst there is something good.” The corruption’s “god and father” is the devil as the author of the fall, not a rival creator of an eternal evil principle; the good nature remains the deeper and indestructible one. So the “another nature” is loose and dangerous language, not a two-principles cosmology, and the substance of what he teaches — an inherited corruption of a nature that is still God’s — is recoverable within orthodoxy. The deficiency is real but bounded: it is that Tertullian lacked the later vocabulary of privation and stated an orthodox instinct in terms his own corporealism made hazardous, not that he held sin to be a co-eternal substance.8

Assessment

Contested. The interpretation is right where it matters most and overstated where it matters least. It is right that Tertullian’s tradux peccati is the germ of the church’s own doctrine of original sin, not the ground of the traducianist heresy: he teaches an antecedent, inherited, baptism-remitted fault two centuries early, Augustine built the mainstream doctrine on it without censure, and the later anathemas fell on the propagation of the soul and its attendant corporealism, not on the inheritance of the guilt. That distinction is real and is too often lost. But the reading over-reaches if it makes Tertullian’s version simply orthodox in advance. His framing hardens the fault into “another nature” with the devil for its father, carried as a quasi-substance in the corporeal soul — the very reification of evil that Augustine and the church would refuse in the fight with the Manichees, for whom evil was a nature. He stops short of dualism, balancing the corruption against a God-given good that can be obscured but never extinguished, so the fault is a matter of framing rather than of a two-principles thesis; but a framing that puts sin on the wrong side of the nature/privation line is a genuine deficiency, and it is inseparable from the materialism the church rejected. Defensible in its doctrinal substance as the anticipation of original sin, deficient in its materialist metaphysics of the fault — strong by the light of doctrinal continuity, weak by the light of the later account of evil as privation, with serious readers able to weigh it either way. Contested.
