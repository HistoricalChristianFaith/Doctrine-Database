Was Tertullian a presbyter, a lawyer, and a convert in middle life?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — the interpretation weighed here is that the familiar life of Tertullian — that he was an ordained presbyter, that he was the Roman jurist “Tertullianus” whose opinions survive in the Digest, and that he stayed in the church until middle life before lapsing out of resentment at the Roman clergy — is largely a late reconstruction that does not survive scrutiny, since almost every load-bearing detail descends from a single paragraph Jerome wrote about 172 years after Tertullian died, and Tertullian’s own works repeatedly class him among the laity, never mention holy orders, a jurist’s career, a father, or a Roman grievance. Two of the three parts hold up well. The identification with the Digest’s jurist has been abandoned by the specialists (Barnes), and the “presbyter” sits against Tertullian’s own apparent self-classing among the laity. But the sweeping form — that little of the biography survives — overreaches: a real residuum stands (an educated African who converted as an adult and wrote with unmistakable forensic training), the layman reading turns on texts that can be read as rhetorical solidarity with a lay audience rather than a disavowal of orders, and the whole revisionist case is, at bottom, an argument from Tertullian’s silence about himself — a man who volunteered little autobiography of any kind. Strong scholars stand on both sides of the presbyterate, and the evidence compels no verdict. Contested.

This question stands a little apart from the doctrinal and disciplinary counts weighed elsewhere in the case against Tertullian. It is the foundational one: before asking whether “Tertullian the lapsed presbyter” was fairly condemned, it asks whether he was a presbyter at all — and so whether that frame, which the West has repeated since Jerome supplied it, describes what actually happened. Its nearest neighbour is the parallel dispute over whether Tertullian ever formally left the catholic church: both turn on the same body of silent evidence, and both have been reorganized by the same revisionist reading since Barnes.

The claim

Tertullian the presbyter, now regarded as chief of the Latin writers after Victor and Apollonius, was from the city of Carthage in the province of Africa, and was the son of a proconsul or Centurion… He was presbyter of the church until middle life, afterwards driven by the envy and abuse of the clergy of the Roman church, he lapsed to the doctrine of Montanus.

— Jerome, On Illustrious Men 53 (c. 392), NPNF2 vol. 3. This one notice is the fountainhead of the standard biography — the presbyterate, the soldier-father, the conversion “in middle life,” and the Roman-clergy motive — to which later ages added the identification of the writer with the jurist “Tertullianus” cited in Justinian’s Digest. The interpretation weighed here is the revisionist one: that little of this construction holds once it is set against Tertullian’s own words.

Who advances it

The modern deconstruction is the work of T. D. Barnes, whose Tertullian: A Historical and Literary Study (1971) opened by showing how thin the independent support is for nearly every element of the received Life; it has since become the mainstream, carried forward by Geoffrey Dunn and pressed furthest by David Wilhite, who reads Tertullian as a layman who never left the Carthaginian church. Against the sweeping form of it, David Rankin, Tertullian and the Church (1995), argues that Tertullian understood himself in strongly clerical terms even if he held no ordained office — a caution the assessment folds in below. The proponent whose framing prompted this page presses the strong thesis that “little of it survives scrutiny”; the assessment distinguishes the parts that do from the part that does not.

The evidence for

The presbyterate. Tertullian is free with the first person and quick to specify his standing when it serves an argument, yet he nowhere claims holy orders — and in two places he appears to class himself among the laity. Arguing in On Exhortation to Chastity that a layman who remarries forfeits a priestly prerogative, he writes: “Vain shall we be if we think that what is not lawful for priests is lawful for laics. Are not even we laics priests?… where there is no joint session of the ecclesiastical Order, you offer, and baptize, and are priest, alone for yourself. But where three are, a church is, albeit they be laics.” The whole argument rests on his standing with his readers inside the lay estate. He does the same in On Monogamy, grounding clerical monogamy on the discipline of “laics, from whose ranks the Ecclesiastical Order proceeds,” and in On Baptism he expounds the layman’s right to baptize in necessity as a rule that binds “laymen” — the class in which he reasons.1 The presbyterate, the “proconsul or centurion” father, and the Roman-clergy grievance are unattested anywhere but in Jerome, who wrote from Bethlehem about 172 years after Tertullian’s death with no continuous Carthaginian tradition behind him; Barnes reads the presbyterate and the father as inferences without independent support and the “envy and abuse” motive as Jerome’s own novelistic touch.2

The jurist. Justinian’s Digest preserves opinions of a Roman jurist named Tertullianus (author of libri quaestionum), and from the sixteenth century onward it was widely assumed that this was the Christian writer. The equation was always fragile — the legal Tertullianus is never given a Christian character, and his floruit does not sit easily with the theologian’s — and Barnes withdrew it: Tertullian’s undoubted command of legal language and metaphor (the praescriptio that gives The Prescription Against Heretics its whole strategy; the brief To Scapula addressed to a sitting proconsul) “does not demonstrably exceed what could be expected” of any well-schooled Roman rhetor, and proves a good forensic education, not a jurist’s career.3

The convert “in middle life.” That Tertullian was a convert, not a cradle Christian, is his own testimony — “Christians are made, not born” (fiunt, non nascuntur Christiani), he tells the magistrates in the Apology, and he writes throughout as a man who came to the faith as an adult from the pagan learning he knew from the inside. But the further specification — that he was a presbyter until middle life and lapsed only then — again rests on Jerome alone, and Tertullian’s openly New-Prophecy works cannot in fact be cleanly walled off into a late “lapsed” period: the “Catholic” and “Montanist” treatises interleave, and he defends the Paraclete across a stretch of years, not at a single mid-life break.4

Adversarial assessment

What holds. Two of the three parts are well-grounded. The jurist identification really has been abandoned by the specialists, and rightly: it rested on a shared cognomen and a modern guess, not on any evidence that the Digest’s Tertullianus was a Christian, and Tertullian’s legal fluency is fully accounted for by a rhetor’s education. And the presbyterate genuinely sits against Tertullian’s own habit of speech: a man who elsewhere specifies his standing to win an argument, and who twice reasons from within the lay estate, is not the obvious candidate for an unmentioned ordination. That so much of the connected Life — the father, the Roman grievance, the tidy mid-life hinge — hangs on one late paragraph, written by an interested author who mined earlier catalogues and filled the gaps, is simply true, and it is the durable core of the revisionist case.

What is overstated or imprecise. The sweeping claim that little of the biography survives overreaches at three points. First, a real residuum does survive, and it is not trivial: an educated Carthaginian who converted as an adult and wrote with unmistakable forensic and rhetorical training — a portrait drawn from Tertullian’s own works and independently confirmed by Eusebius, who knew him a century before Jerome as a man “well versed in the laws of the Romans… and one of those especially distinguished in Rome.”5 The Eusebian notice, earlier and independent of Jerome, corroborates the legal reputation the revisionist case is at pains to deflate — it deflates the jurist identification, not the legal learning. Second, the layman reading is strong but not decisive: “are not even we laics priests?” is deployed in the service of a thesis about lay priestly power, so the “we” can be read as an orator’s solidarity with his lay readers rather than a flat disavowal of orders, and Rankin has shown that Tertullian conceives himself in markedly clerical terms whether or not he was ordained. Third — and this cuts against the revisionists’ own method — the whole case is finally an argument from silence: it turns on what Tertullian does not say about himself, and he says very little about himself of any kind, so his silence about a presbyterate is weaker evidence than it first appears. The revisionist reading is the better-supported one and now holds the field, but it proves the standard biography doubtful, not false, and it cannot positively establish the layman in place of the presbyter.6

Assessment

Contested. The received Life is genuinely shaky where the revisionists press hardest: the jurist identification is rightly abandoned, and the presbyterate stands against Tertullian’s own apparent self-classing among the laity, so “Tertullian the lapsed presbyter” — the frame Jerome supplied and the West has repeated — may well misdescribe a layman who led a rigorist party inside his own church. But the strong thesis that little of the biography survives overreaches: an educated African convert with real forensic training is a solid residuum, independently attested by Eusebius; the layman texts admit a rhetorical reading and cannot by themselves install the layman in the presbyter’s place; and the case rests, in the end, on the silence of a man who volunteered almost no autobiography. Trust the deflation of the jurist and the doubt cast on the presbyterate; treat “converted in middle life” and the Roman-clergy motive as Jerome’s uncorroborated framing; and flag, on the proponent’s side, the leap from “unproven” to “disproven.” Strong scholars divide over the presbyterate, and the evidence compels no verdict.
