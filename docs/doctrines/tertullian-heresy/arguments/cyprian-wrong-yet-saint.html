Cyprian was wrong on rebaptism yet a saint: did the church make for him the move it denied Tertullian?

Related arguments: the comparative test — see also Hippolytus, the antipope who became a saint, Novatian, whose shared rigorism became a schism, and the umbrella question, is it coherent to keep his theology while withholding his name?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline, a comparative test) · Assessment: contested — Cyprian of Carthage taught that the baptism of heretics is void and that converts from heresy must be baptized afresh; he pressed the doctrine against Pope Stephen in a bitter quarrel in which Stephen threatened to break off communion, and convened African councils that ruled against Rome. The later church judged the doctrine wrong — Rome’s “no rebaptism” prevailed, was settled against the African practice at Arles, and became the rule the Donatists were condemned for defying. Yet Cyprian is a saint, a martyr, and a Father whom Tertullian’s own reader-heir venerates. The interpretation weighed here is that Augustine, refuting the Donatists’ appeal to Cyprian in On Baptism, performs on Cyprian precisely the operation the church declines to perform on Tertullian — keep the doctrine we can use, refute what we cannot, and excuse the sainted man — so the Cyprian case supplies the decisive instance of the church actually making the move, and its differential verdict on the two Africans tracks the manner of a man’s death and the fortunes of his name more than the gravity of his fault. As an isolation of the variable, and as documentation that the move is real and named, this is strong, and it corroborates the capstone from the inside. As a charge that the difference from Tertullian is unprincipled, it is weaker: Augustine states an intelligible rule — that martyrdom and a death in catholic communion cover an error — and the disanalogies run deep. Cyprian erred on a question the church had not yet settled, held it without ever breaking the bond of peace, and died in the Church a martyr; Tertullian pressed a rigorist novelty against apostolic permission and (on the reports) ended outside. Strong as an isolation of the variable and as proof that the church owns the move, disputed as a verdict of unfairness. Contested.

This is not a count any ancient source laid against Tertullian: no synod, no heresy-catalogue, and not Augustine’s notice among the heresies faults him by comparison with Cyprian. It belongs, like the Hippolytus comparandum, not to the reckoning of the counts but to the prior question the whole timeline of the verdict keeps returning to: was that verdict fair? Here a second case is set beside Tertullian’s — his own city’s martyr-bishop, who read “the master” every day, held a doctrine the church would reject, and was canonized all the same. And this case has an advantage the Hippolytus one lacks: the church did not merely tolerate the outcome, it wrote out the reasoning. Augustine’s On Baptism is the “keep the doctrine, excuse the man” move performed in the open, with its principle stated.1

Cyprian’s error: rebaptism, pressed against Rome

The doctrine is unambiguous, and Cyprian never softened it. Because there is no true baptism outside the one Church, he held, whatever heretics and schismatics perform is void, and a convert from them has never been baptized at all; he must receive “the lawful, and true, and only baptism of the Church.” Opening the council he summoned to settle the question, Cyprian put the ruling flatly: “heretics who come to the Church must be baptized and sanctified by the baptism of the Church.” Eighty-odd African bishops in turn voted the same way. It was an old African custom, ratified under Agrippinus a generation before, and Cyprian took it for the plain entailment of the Church’s unity.2

Rome held the contrary, and the collision was head-on. Stephen, bishop of Rome, stood on custom and the tradition — converts from heresy were to be received by the laying on of hands in penance, not rebaptized — and laid down the rule in the words Cyprian sent on to Pompeius to expose: “If any one, therefore, come to you from any heresy whatever, let nothing be innovated (or done) which has not been handed down, to wit, that hands be imposed on him for repentance.” Cyprian read this as Stephen “endeavouring to maintain the cause of heretics against Christians, and against the Church of God.” Stephen went further than argument: on the report of Firmilian of Caesarea, Cyprian’s eastern ally, he moved to cut the dissenters out of communion altogether — and Firmilian turned the threat back on him with real acerbity: “how great sin have you heaped up for yourself, when you cut yourself off from so many flocks! For it is yourself that you have cut off… while you think that all may be excommunicated by you, you have excommunicated yourself alone from all.” The quarrel was still open, and Cyprian still on the losing side of what would become the rule, when the persecution of Valerian overtook him.3

Two things in the dossier sharpen the parallel with Tertullian rather than soften it. The first is a verbal echo. The edict Tertullian had mocked in On Modesty — “the Pontifex Maximus — that is, the bishop of bishops” presuming to remit the graver sins — names the same Roman pretension Cyprian refused; and Cyprian, opening his council, disavowed the title in almost Tertullian’s words: “For neither does any of us set himself up as a bishop of bishops, nor by tyrannical terror does any compel his colleague to the necessity of obedience.” The African resistance to a Roman “bishop of bishops” is one attitude running through both men. The second is an irony: the very letter that carries Cyprian’s cause against Stephen also condemns Tertullian’s movement by name. Firmilian, defending the African rebaptizers, ranks “those who are called Cataphrygians, and endeavour to claim to themselves new prophecies” among the heretics who “can have neither the Father, nor the Son, nor the Holy Spirit,” for “they preach Him who sent the Spirit that speaks by Montanus and Prisca.” The same rigorist African front that defied Rome over baptism reckoned the New Prophecy no church at all — which is a caution against reading Cyprian and Tertullian as simply two versions of one dissent.4

Yet he is a saint — and Augustine writes out the move

The doctrine lost. Rome’s practice prevailed, was imposed on the African rebaptizers at the Council of Arles in 314, and became the settled rule of the whole Church; and when the Donatists made Cyprian their patron — he rebaptized, so rebaptism is catholic — the appeal had to be answered. Augustine answered it in the seven books On Baptism, Against the Donatists, and the answer is the “keep the doctrine we can use, excuse the man” operation performed in full view. He refutes the rebaptism doctrine outright; he keeps and turns against the Donatists everything in Cyprian he can use — above all Cyprian’s theology of the Church’s unity, and his refusal to break communion with those who differed; and he excuses the sainted man himself on stated grounds.5

The refutation rests on a principle Augustine makes explicit, and it is the same principle the capstone turns on: a Father’s authority is not Scripture’s. The canon “stands so absolutely in a superior position to all later letters of the bishops, that about it we can hold no manner of doubt or disputation,” while “all the letters of bishops which have been written, or are being written, since the closing of the canon, are liable to be refuted if there be anything contained in them which strays from the truth.” Cyprian’s letters are weighed and, on baptism, found wanting; his teaching is received where it is true and set aside where it is not. That is exactly what the Church did with Tertullian’s books — kept the rule-of-faith polemic and the Trinitarian vocabulary, let the rest fall — and Augustine here supplies the rationale in as many words.6

The excusing of the man is the crux, and it is the passage the lead comparandum turns on. Cyprian erred, Augustine grants, but the error never became a heresy, because two things covered it: he never broke the peace, and he died a martyr. “His error,” Augustine writes, “was compensated by his remaining in catholic unity, and by the abundance of his charity; and finally it was cleared away by the pruning-hook of martyrdom”; and if “any cloud had crept over the clearness of his intellect from his infirmity as man, it might be dispelled by the glorious brightness of his blood.” The charity half Augustine grounds in Cyprian’s own conduct at the council, where he had refused to unchurch anyone who disagreed — “Judging no one, nor depriving any one of the right of communion if he differ from us” — the precise opposite of the schismatic temper the Donatists had inherited. So the martyr-bishop who held a doctrine the Church rejected is kept, cleansed, and crowned; the theologian who taught the Church its own word for God, and (on the reports) ended in a rigorist splinter, is kept, quoted, and left uncanonized. The move is one; the outcomes diverge.7

The comparison

Set the two Africans side by side and the variable stands out as sharply as with Hippolytus. On the doctrinal ledger Cyprian is arguably the worse case: he held and pressed, against the bishop of Rome, a positive error the whole Church would formally reject, and he held it to the end — where Tertullian’s gravest charge, on the doctrine-or-discipline question, is that his rigorism touched the closure of the apostolic deposit, and Augustine himself declined to found the heresy on any doctrine at all. Yet Cyprian is a saint and Tertullian the standing Latin example of the heretic. The verdicts run against the doctrinal gravity, which is evidence that they were not fixed by it. What fixed them, on this reading, was the endings: Cyprian died reconciled — indeed never estranged — and a martyr, so his error was “cleared away by the pruning-hook of martyrdom”; Tertullian, so far as any source records, died neither martyred nor formally returned, and his books nearly vanished. That is the same finding the capstone reaches and the Hippolytus case isolates from the side of schism — and the Cyprian case adds what those cannot: the church’s own draftsman, writing the leniency down and naming its grounds.8

Adversarial assessment

What holds. As an isolation of the variable the comparison is strong, and stronger than the Hippolytus one at the single point where that one is weakest — its documentation. There is no disputed reconstruction to lean on here: the rebaptism quarrel is attested in Cyprian’s own letters and councils, the doctrine’s later rejection is a matter of record, and the excusing of the man is not an inference from silence but Augustine’s own written argument, with its rule stated. The church did make the “keep the doctrine, excuse the man” move; it made it for a Father who was doctrinally in the wrong; and it made it, by Augustine’s own account, because of martyrdom and a death in communion. That corroborates the timeline’s recurring finding that Tertullian’s uncanonized-yet-uncondemned middle state is an accretion shaped by what he lacked — a martyr’s death, a documented reconciliation, a protected name — rather than the output of a court that weighed his faults against a rebaptizer’s and found them graver.

But the criterion Augustine names is principled, not caprice. The unevenness is real; that it is unfair does not follow. Augustine does not say Cyprian’s blood makes rebaptism true, nor that martyrdom licenses any error; he says a martyr’s death in the Church’s peace covers an error held without breach of charity, on a question not yet settled. That is a coherent theology — blood-witness is the perfect confession, and Cyprian’s refusal to divide the Church over his own view is exactly the disposition that keeps an error from hardening into heresy. A verdict that turns on whether a man died for Christ, in communion, over an open question is tracking something other than the bare content of his mistake, but it is not tracking it wrongly. So the comparison establishes its narrow point — the verdicts do not track doctrinal gravity — without establishing the broad one that they are therefore arbitrary.9

And the disanalogies with Tertullian are deep. Three of them cut against reading the two cases as one fault unevenly rewarded. First, the status of the question: when Cyprian held rebaptism it was genuinely open — an old African custom, argued by whole councils, not yet ruled on by any authority Augustine will grant was binding; Tertullian’s remarriage-ban and denial of second repentance ran against permissions the church already read in the apostle. To err on an unsettled question, in good faith, is not what the church means by heresy, and Augustine says so. Second, communion: Cyprian’s charity was not merely a virtue beside his error but touched its very character — he never separated, never set up against Rome a rival altar or a rival rule, and died inside the Church he had defied; whether Tertullian ever formally left is the disputed load-bearing question of his timeline, and on the hostile reading he ended in a splinter of his own. Third, martyrdom itself, the one thing the church has always said covers a multitude of sins, Cyprian had and Tertullian lacked. Take the three together and the church can answer, with no incoherence, that it treats the two differently because they are different: a martyr who erred in charity on an open question is not a rigorist who pressed novelties against apostolic permission and (on the reports) broke the peace.10

Where the comparison keeps its force. Grant all that, and a residue survives that the disanalogies do not reach. The point of the comparison was never that Cyprian and Tertullian committed the same fault; it is that the church’s honour and dishonour track variables largely outside the fault — and every disanalogy just canvassed is such a variable. “He died a martyr,” “he never broke communion,” “his question was still open”: each is a fact about the man’s circumstances and death, not about the truth of what he taught, and each ran in Cyprian’s favour and against Tertullian. So even the church’s best answer concedes the comparison’s core — that sanctity here is decided by martyrdom, communion, and the fortunes of a name — while denying only that this is arbitrary. That is why the finding is neither the “the church was simply inconsistent” the sharp form wants nor the clean vindication the church’s answer wants: the criterion is principled, but it is a criterion about endings, and Tertullian’s ending, not his doctrine, is most of why he stands where he stands.11

Assessment

contested. The Cyprian comparandum is the best-documented member of the comparative test and a weak proof of injustice. It is strong — stronger than Hippolytus — in what it establishes: not merely that a doctrinally-erring Father was canonized while Tertullian was not, but that the church wrote out the reasoning, Augustine’s On Baptism keeping and refuting Cyprian’s doctrine while excusing the man “by the pruning-hook of martyrdom” and the “abundance of his charity.” That is the “keep-the-doctrine, excuse-the-man” move named and dated, and it corroborates the capstone’s finding that Tertullian’s middle state is an accretion of what he lacked. It is weak as a charge of unfairness: the criterion Augustine states — martyrdom and a death in catholic communion covering an error held in charity on an unsettled question — is principled, and the disanalogies with Tertullian (an open question versus a novelty against apostolic permission; unbroken communion versus a disputed schism; a martyr’s crown versus none) are real. Yet its core survives even the church’s best answer, since every one of those disanalogies is a variable about the man’s ending rather than the truth of his teaching — which is exactly the comparison’s point. Strong by the light of what it reveals about how such verdicts form, weaker by the light of the fairness it is meant to impugn. Contested.
