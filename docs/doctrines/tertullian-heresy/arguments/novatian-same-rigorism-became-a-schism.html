Novatian held Tertullian’s own rigorism yet gave a schism its name: was it the doctrine or the church order the church condemned?

Related arguments: the comparative test — see also Hippolytus, the antipope who became a saint, Cyprian, wrong on rebaptism yet a saint, and the umbrella question, is it coherent to keep his theology while withholding his name?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline, a comparative test) · Assessment: contested — a generation after Tertullian, Novatian of Rome held the very rigorism for which Tertullian is faulted: that the church has no power to grant peace to the lapsed, the apostates of the Decian persecution, who must be left to God’s judgement rather than readmitted to communion — substantively the position of On Modesty, that the church cannot remit adultery and apostasy, transposed from the second-repentance question to the lapsed. Yet where Tertullian is a “condemnation” assembled after the fact, Novatian became the eponym of a named heresy of discipline — the Novatianists, the Cathari (“the pure”) — excommunicated by a Roman synod, refuted by Cyprian, and legislated against as late as Nicaea. The interpretation weighed here is that the case sharpens two things at once. First, what drew the formal brand was not the penitential rigorism as such but the schism: for Novatian did what Tertullian (on the disputed reading of his own timeline) never did — he had himself ordained a rival bishop of Rome against the lawful Cornelius and set up a counter-church, and it is there, in the breach of unity, that Cyprian locates the heresy. Second, even so, the church quietly kept Novatian’s orthodox theology — his On the Trinity, “a sort of epitome of the work of Tertullian” — while condemning the man, and received his clergy at Nicaea without re-ordination: the same man/doctrine split the capstone turns on, now from the condemned side rather than the sainted one. As an isolation of that variable the comparison is strong and well-documented. As a verdict on Tertullian it cuts both ways, and the cut is the whole difficulty: it lightens the doctrinal charge — his rigorism alone did not make a heresy, the schism did, and his schism is contested — while confirming that the church did judge the rigorist doctrine itself false and made it a heresy’s name. Contested.

This is not a count any ancient source laid against Tertullian: no synod, no heresy-catalogue, and not Augustine’s notice among the heresies faults him by comparison with Novatian. It belongs, like the Hippolytus and Cyprian comparanda, not to the reckoning of the counts but to the prior question the whole timeline of the verdict keeps returning to: what, exactly, was he faulted for, and was the verdict even? Here a case is set beside Tertullian’s that runs the other way from the two saints — not a man honoured despite a graver fault, but a man condemned for a fault that begins in Tertullian’s own. Novatian is the rigorism carried to its institutional end: what in Tertullian stayed (on the disputed reading) a party inside the church became, in Novatian, a church of its own.1

The same rigorism, one generation on

The doctrine is Tertullian’s, moved to a new occasion. Tertullian, in On Modesty, had denied that the church could reconcile the capital sinner — the adulterer, the apostate — reserving to “the Church of the Spirit” a power he refused to “the Church which consists of a number of bishops.” Novatian, faced after the Decian persecution of 250–251 with the flood of the lapsi who had sacrificed or bought certificates and now sought readmission, drew the same line at apostasy: the church has no power to grant them peace; let them repent, but their reconciliation belongs to God alone, not to the bishop. Cornelius, in the letter Eusebius preserves, sets down the charge at its sharpest — Novatian was “lifted up with arrogance… as if there was no longer for them a hope of salvation, even if they should do all things pertaining to a genuine and pure conversion,” and his followers, “in the pride of their imagination, call themselves Cathari,” the pure.2

Cyprian, writing to Antonianus to explain “wherein consists the Novatian heresy,” answers the rigorism in the very terms he might have used against Tertullian, and the parallel did not escape his own later editors. To withhold reconciliation from the penitent, he argues, is “to exhort to the repentance of atonement, and to take away the healing from the atonement; to say to our brethren, ‘Mourn and shed tears… but, after all these things, you shall die without the pale of the Church… none of that peace which you seek shall you receive!’ Who would not perish at once? Who would not fall away, from very desperation?” And he presses the point Tertullian had refused: a time of repentance and the hope of pardon are granted “even” to adulterers, the graver case, “so that no one is to be restrained from the fruit of satisfaction, and the hope of peace.” It is precisely the mercy Cyprian extends to the lapsed and the adulterer that Tertullian and Novatian alike deny; the Ante-Nicene editors of Cyprian’s letter mark the spot with a note inviting the reader to “compare Cyprian, in all this, with his less reasonable ‘master’ Tertullian.”3

That the two positions are one doctrine on two occasions is not a modern construction but the church’s own reading of its own history. The second-repentance argument already traces the single rigorist current — from the Shepherd of Hermas’s one-and-only penance, through Tertullian’s hardening of it into a permanent bar, to the Novatianists who “feared the capital sins beyond the church’s reach” — and notes that “the church settled the same question against Novatian.” Novatian is the doctrine’s afterlife, and its defeat: the rigorism Tertullian pressed against a Roman bishop’s edict is the rigorism the Roman church, one generation on, formally rejected in Novatian’s person.4

But Novatian founded a rival church

Here the cases part, and the parting is the argument’s hinge. Tertullian pressed his rigorism as a writer and (on the reports) a partisan; whether he ever formally left the church is the disputed, load-bearing question of his timeline, and his own works record no rival altar and no counter-ordination. Novatian left nothing to inference. Ambitious for the see when Cornelius was lawfully elected, he — in the account Cornelius sends and Eusebius keeps — summoned three bishops from “a small and insignificant corner of Italy,” “rustic and very simple men,” and “by the tenth hour, when they had become drunk and sick, he compelled them by force to confer on him the episcopate through a counterfeit and vain imposition of hands.” He bound those to whom he then gave the eucharist by an oath, holding a man’s hands in both his own and not releasing them until he swore “by the body and blood of our Lord Jesus Christ” never to forsake him for Cornelius. This is a rival bishop of Rome and a counter-church, complete with its own hierarchy and its own communion.5

And it is the schism, not the penitential doctrine on its own, that Cyprian makes the seat of the heresy. Asked what error Novatian had introduced, he answers first that the question is almost beside the point: “we ought not even to be inquisitive as to what he teaches, so long as he teaches out of the pale of unity. Whoever he may be… he who is not in the Church of Christ is not a Christian.” Novatian is one who, “when a bishop has been made in the Church by sixteen co-bishops, strives by bribery to be made an adulterous and extraneous bishop by the hands of deserters” and “is sending his new apostles through very many cities.” The fault Cyprian names is the breach of unity and the counterfeit episcopate; the rigorism about the lapsed follows as its content, refuted second and as the sect’s badge, not as the ground of the charge. A Roman synod of sixty bishops had already, on the same reckoning, cut Novatian off.6

And the church kept his theology

The last turn is the one that ties the case to the capstone. Novatian was not only a rigorist schismatic; he was, before the schism, the ablest Latin theologian of his generation, and his On the Trinity is the second great Latin statement of the doctrine after Tertullian’s Against Praxeas. It opens where Tertullian’s rule of faith opens — “The Rule of truth requires that we should first of all things believe in God the Father and Lord Omnipotent” — and defends the distinct person of the Son against the same modalism Tertullian had fought, insisting that Christ is “God… in such a manner as to be the Son, not the Father.” The church kept it. Jerome describes it as “a great volume On the Trinity, a sort of epitome of the work of Tertullian, which many mistakenly ascribe to Cyprian” — the orthodox theology of a condemned man circulating, unattributed or misattributed to a saint, exactly as Tertullian’s own vocabulary circulated under the church’s use while his name lay under a cloud.7

The church made the split institutional, too. When the Council of Nicaea in 325 legislated on “those who call themselves Cathari” who returned to catholic communion, it did not treat their orders as void: “the great and holy Synod decrees that they who are ordained shall continue as they are in the clergy,” on a written profession of catholic faith and practice. The rigorist schism was condemned and its penitential doctrine rejected; the sacraments and the theology it carried were received. The church did to the Novatianists’ ministry what it did to Novatian’s book and to Tertullian’s vocabulary — kept what could stand, refused only the breach and the error — which is the very operation the capstone weighs.8

The comparison

Set Novatian beside Tertullian and the variable the whole comparative test is after stands out from a new angle. The Hippolytus and Cyprian comparanda isolate it from the honoured side: men whose fault was as grave or graver, sainted because they were reconciled and martyred. Novatian isolates it from the dishonoured side: a man whose penitential doctrine was Tertullian’s own, branded a heresiarch — and the branding, on the evidence, attaches to the schism he added, not to the rigorism the two men shared. That distinction does real work on the doctrine-or-discipline question. It shows that in the century after Tertullian the church treated rigorism-plus-schism, not rigorism alone, as the thing to cut off: the offense that made a heresy was the rival church, the jurisdiction, the counterfeit see. On that reading the comparison helps Tertullian, whose gravest disputed act stops short of Novatian’s — no rival altar, no counter-ordination, a separation that even the hostile sources leave contestable.9

But the same case cuts the other way, and honesty requires the second edge. The church did not merely condemn Novatian’s schism and leave his rigorism untouched; through Cyprian it judged the rigorist doctrine itself false — a cruelty that “takes away the healing from the atonement” — and it made “Novatianist” a standing name for a heresy of discipline. So Tertullian’s identical doctrine is not, by this comparison, shown to be harmless or orthodox; it is shown to be the very teaching the church came to reject, now carrying a heretic’s name. The comparison thus lightens one charge and sharpens another: it lifts the weight of schism off Tertullian (who may not have committed it) and lays the weight of a rejected doctrine more firmly on him (which he certainly held).10

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The historical core is secure and, unlike the Hippolytus comparandum, rests on no disputed reconstruction. That Novatian’s doctrine of the lapsed is Tertullian’s rigorism on a new occasion is the church’s own reading, marked even by Cyprian’s editors. That the formal offense was located in the schism is Cyprian’s explicit teaching (“we ought not even to be inquisitive as to what he teaches, so long as he teaches out of the pale of unity”). And that the church kept the theology while condemning the man is documented twice over — Jerome on the On the Trinity and Nicaea on the Novatianist clergy. As an isolation of the variable the comparative test is after — that the verdict on a Father tracks church order and the fortunes of a name more than the bare content of his rigour — Novatian is as strong as Cyprian and stronger than Hippolytus, and it corroborates the capstone’s man/doctrine split from the side the two saints cannot reach: the condemned schismatic whose sound theology the church nonetheless kept.

But it does not straightforwardly acquit Tertullian. The comparison’s help turns on a disanalogy that is itself contested. Novatian’s condemnation attaches to a positive, public act — a rival ordination and a counter-church at Rome — that Tertullian, on the load-bearing reading of his own timeline, never performed; so “the church condemned the schism, not the rigorism” only lightens Tertullian’s case if he founded no schism, and that is precisely what is in dispute. Grant the hostile reading — the Tertullianistae and their Carthaginian basilica — and the disanalogy narrows toward identity.11

And the rigorism it shares is the church’s rejected doctrine, not its vindicated one. The move that clears Tertullian of Novatian’s schism convicts him of Novatian’s doctrine. Once the comparison has shown that what damned Novatian formally was the breach of unity, the penitential rigor left over is not thereby made sound; the church refuted it in the same breath, and the second-repentance argument rates the “no novelty” defence of it only contested, since the settlement against Novatian is one of the reasons it does not hold. So the Novatian case cannot be enlisted, as an over-eager defender might wish, to prove that Tertullian’s rigorism was fine and only schism is punished. It proves the narrower, two-edged thing: the church distinguished the two faults, cut off the schism as heresy, and rejected the doctrine as error — and Tertullian owned the second even if he escaped the first.

Where the comparison keeps its force. What survives every qualification is the finding the whole comparative test converges on. Novatian, holding Tertullian’s doctrine, is a named heresiarch; Tertullian, holding it too, is an uncanonized-but-unanathematized example — and the difference between them is not the doctrine, which they share, but everything around it: a rival ordination on one side and (at most) a disputed splinter on the other; a condemned man’s theology kept under a saint’s name; a schism formally cut off while its orders were preserved. Honour, dishonour, and the exact shape of a verdict are decided here by church order and the fortunes of a name at least as much as by the rigour itself — which is the comparison’s whole point, and why it comes back neither a clean acquittal nor a clean conviction.

Assessment

contested. Novatian is the comparative test’s mirror-image case: not a saint honoured despite a graver fault, but a heresiarch condemned for a fault that begins in Tertullian’s own rigorism about the reconcilable sinner. As an isolation of the variable it is strong and exceptionally well-documented — the shared doctrine is the church’s own reading, the located offense (schism, not penitential rigor) is Cyprian’s explicit teaching, and the kept theology is attested by Jerome and by Nicaea’s reception of the Novatianist clergy — and it corroborates the capstone’s man/doctrine split from the condemned side. As a verdict on Tertullian it is genuinely two-edged and resolves cleanly in neither direction: it lightens the doctrinal charge, since the formal heresy tracked the rival church Tertullian arguably never built, while confirming that the church judged the shared rigorism itself false and made it a heresy’s name — so the same case that lifts the weight of schism lays the weight of a rejected doctrine more firmly on him. Strong by the light of what it reveals about how such verdicts form, indeterminate by the light of the acquittal it is sometimes made to promise. Contested.
