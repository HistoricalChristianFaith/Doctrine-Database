Was Tertullian right that a Christian may not bear the sword or hold public office?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — on the interpretation weighed here, Tertullian holds that a believer may neither serve as a soldier nor hold civil “dignity and power”: warfare is not “proper at all for Christians,” for “the Lord… in disarming Peter, unbelted every soldier,” and “one soul cannot be due to two masters — God and Caesar.” The reading is far stronger than its later reputation allows. There really is a deep ante-Nicene current against the sword: Christ’s own word that “he who uses the sword shall perish by the sword,” the entanglement of the Roman army and magistracy in oaths, sacrifices, imperial cult and capital punishment, and a chorus of second- and third-century voices — Origen, the church order that goes under Hippolytus’s name, later Lactantius — that bar the Christian from bloodshed and from idolatrous office. Tertullian states the hard pole of a genuine, and arguably majority, pre-Constantinian suspicion of the sword, and this wiki does not discount a strict reading merely because the later majority relaxed it. But the flat, absolute ban overreaches that case. The New Testament nowhere tells the soldier to leave the ranks — John the Baptist does not, Christ does not rebuke the centurion, Peter does not bid Cornelius resign — and Paul makes the magistrate’s sword God’s own minister (Rom. 13:4); Christians demonstrably served from the second century, as Tertullian himself concedes when he boasts that “we sail with you, and fight with you”; and the church that followed — the Council of Arles, then Augustine’s just-war reasoning — rejected an unconditional prohibition and honoured the soldier-saints. Strong as a witness to the primitive church’s horror of blood and of idolatrous office, overstated as a timeless, absolute ban, and genuinely disputed among historians as to how pacifist the ante-Nicene church really was. Contested.

This count is not one of those on which the ancient sources ever faulted Tertullian: no synod, no heresy-catalogue, and not Augustine’s notice among the heresies, charges him with his opposition to military service or public office. It belongs, then, not to the reckoning of whether he was a heretic — where the counts are his turn to the New Prophecy, his ban on remarriage, and his rigorism about penance — but to the prior question his rigorism raises everywhere: was the strict reading right? He sets it out in two treatises, The Chaplet (De Corona, c. 211), written to defend a soldier who had refused the military laurel, and On Idolatry (dating disputed), which weighs whether “a servant of God should take the administration of any dignity or power.” The two are bound by one thesis — that the Christian owes an undivided allegiance the sword and the magistrate’s chair both compromise — and are weighed here together.1

The claim

To begin with the real ground of the military crown, I think we must first inquire whether warfare is proper at all for Christians… Shall it be held lawful to make an occupation of the sword, when the Lord proclaims that he who uses the sword shall perish by the sword? And shall the son of peace take part in the battle when it does not become him even to sue at law?

— Tertullian, The Chaplet (De Corona) 11, ANF vol. 3. The companion statement is in On Idolatry 19: “There is no agreement between the divine and the human sacrament, the standard of Christ and the standard of the devil, the camp of light and the camp of darkness. One soul cannot be due to two masters — God and Caesar… how will a Christian man war, nay, how will he serve even in peace, without a sword, which the Lord has taken away? For albeit soldiers had come unto John… albeit, likewise, a centurion had believed; still the Lord afterward, in disarming Peter, unbelted every soldier.” The ban is two-sided. Against the soldier, Tertullian allows the one exception the Gospel forces on him — the man already enlisted when faith found him, “as in the instance of those whom John used to receive for baptism, and of those most faithful centurions” — but insists that “when a man has become a believer… there must be either an immediate abandonment of it, which has been the course with many,” and that a believer may not enter the service at all. Against the magistrate, On Idolatry 17–18 grants in principle that “a servant of God” might “take the administration of any dignity or power” and then hedges it with conditions that all but abolish it — he must be “neither sacrificing nor lending his authority to sacrifices… making proclamation or edict for no solemnity; not even taking oaths… neither sitting in judgment on any one’s life or character… binding no one, imprisoning or torturing no one — if it is credible that all this is possible” — for “all the powers and dignities of this world are not only alien to, but enemies of, God.”2

Who advances it

The reading is Tertullian’s own, pressed as of a piece with his wider rigorism: the Christian owes God an allegiance so total that the military oath (sacramentum), the standards, and the sword are rival claims upon it. But he does not stand alone, and that is the interpretation’s real strength. A strong strand of modern scholarship — C. J. Cadoux, Roland Bainton, Jean-Michel Hornus — reads the pre-Constantinian church as broadly opposed to Christians bearing arms, with Tertullian, Origen and Lactantius its most explicit witnesses; on this account he articulates something near the ante-Nicene consensus, not a private fanaticism. That older “pacifist” picture has since been contested — John Helgeland and others stress that Christians demonstrably served in numbers, that the fathers’ objection was often to army idolatry rather than to killing as such, and that the literary evidence is thin and rhetorical — so that the very question the argument turns on is one historians still divide over. What is not in dispute is that a real revulsion from bloodshed and from idolatrous office runs through the second- and third-century sources, and that Tertullian gives it its sharpest Latin voice.3

The evidence for

The Lord took away the sword. Tertullian does not read his rule off the Phrygian prophets. Its first ground is dominical: at Gethsemane Christ tells Peter, “Put up again thy sword into his place: for all they that take the sword shall perish with the sword” (Matt. 26:52), and Tertullian reads the rebuke as programmatic — “in disarming Peter, unbelted every soldier.” Around it he gathers the Gospel’s ethic of non-retaliation: the disciple is a “son of peace” whom it “does not become… even to sue at law,” who is “not the avenger even of his own wrongs,” so that to “make an occupation of the sword” is to unmake the Christian. The move is not eccentric to him: the same texts — turn the other cheek, love your enemies, beat swords into ploughshares (Isa. 2:4) — are the staple of the ante-Nicene case against Christian soldiering, and they are, on their face, in tension with an occupation whose end is killing.4

The army and the magistracy were saturated with idolatry. The second ground is the concrete one, and it is where Tertullian is strongest. The imperial service was not a religiously neutral career: the soldier swore a sacred oath (sacramentum) by the emperor’s genius, the legionary standards were themselves objects of cult, camp life was punctuated by sacrifices and the wearing of ritual crowns (the very point of De Corona), and the magistrate presided over spectacles, oaths, temple-tributes, and capital sentences. To Tertullian these are not incidental defilements but the substance of the thing: “there is no agreement between the divine and the human sacrament,” and “all the powers and dignities of this world are… enemies of God.” This is not a Montanist novelty but the mainstream ante-Nicene anxiety about idolatry applied to office, and it is why his ban on the magistracy is framed less as pacifism than as the impossibility of exercising coercive power — the sword, the prison, the judge’s sentence over “life or character” — without touching what the Christian has renounced.5

A wider ante-Nicene chorus says the same. Tertullian’s severity is not isolated. Two generations later Origen, answering Celsus’s charge that Christians shirk the public defence, concedes the fact and defends it: Christians “do not indeed fight under” the emperor, “although he require it; but we fight on his behalf, forming a special army — an army of piety — by offering our prayers to God,” and as priests they keep “their hands pure” from blood. The Roman church order that circulates under Hippolytus’s name, the Apostolic Tradition (c. 215), is blunter still in its scrutiny of trades: “A soldier of the civil authority must be taught not to kill men and to refuse to do so if he is commanded, and to refuse to take an oath”; “a military commander or civic magistrate that wears the purple must resign or be rejected”; and “if a catechumen or a believer seeks to become a soldier, they must be rejected, for they have despised God.” Lactantius, on the eve of Constantine, would draw the line as sharply: to the just man “it will not be lawful… to serve as a soldier,” for “there is no exception… that it is always unlawful to put a man to death.” Whatever the qualifications, the drift of the surviving sources is that the pre-Constantinian church looked on the sword with deep suspicion.6

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The interpretation is soundest as a witness. There is a real dominical and prophetic ethic of non-violence for the disciple; the Roman army and magistracy really were bound up with idolatry, cult-oaths, and blood, so that a scrupulous Christian in the second century had genuine and specific reasons to refuse them; and Tertullian does not stand alone but voices — at its most absolute — a suspicion of the sword shared by Origen, the Apostolic Tradition, and Lactantius. So the popular picture — that his pacifism is a sectarian oddity of his Montanist decline — is false to the record: much of it lay well inside the ante-Nicene mainstream, and its objection to idolatrous office in particular is simply the church’s ordinary horror of idolatry, not a New-Prophecy tenet. On the narrow claims — that army-idolatry is incompatible with the faith, and that the disciple is called to peace — Tertullian is on firm ground.

But the New Testament never orders the soldier out of the ranks. The flat ban runs into the same Scriptures Tertullian must labour to confine. When soldiers ask John the Baptist “what shall we do?” he tells them to stop extorting and be content with their wages — not to desert (Luke 3:14). Christ marvels at the centurion’s faith and heals his servant without a word against his calling (Matt. 8:5–13); Cornelius the centurion is baptized with his household and never told to resign his commission (Acts 10); and Paul makes the magistrate who “beareth not the sword in vain” a “minister of God… to execute wrath upon him that doeth evil” (Rom. 13:4). Tertullian is driven to grant the “already-enlisted” centurions as exceptions while barring any believer from entering the service — a distinction the texts themselves do not draw. His own strongest proof, “he who takes the sword shall perish by the sword,” was spoken to stop Peter defending Christ by force, and the church did not read it as a universal ban on the magistrate’s sword that Romans 13 affirms.7

And Christians — on Tertullian’s own testimony — already served. The absolute ban sits awkwardly with the plain fact, which Tertullian himself reports, that believers filled the legions. In the Apology he boasts to the pagans that Christians are everywhere in Roman life: “we sail with you, and fight with you [navigamus et nobiscum… militamus], and till the ground with you”; and he adduces, as a proof of Christian loyalty, the “Germanic drought… removed by the rains obtained through the prayers of the Christians who chanced to be fighting under” Marcus Aurelius — the episode later called the “Thundering Legion.” A church whose soldiers can be invoked as evidence of its good citizenship is not one that held soldiering to be simple apostasy. The third and fourth centuries would fill the calendar with soldier-saints — Marinus, Marcellus, the Theban legion, Julius the Veteran — martyred not for having enlisted but, when the crunch came, for refusing to sacrifice; their presence in the army is presupposed by their very stories.8

The church that followed rejected the unconditional ban. Once the peace of the church removed the idolatry that had made the service intolerable, the mainstream moved decisively the other way. The Council of Arles in 314 — the first great Western council, called by Constantine — decreed in its third canon that “those who throw down their arms in peacetime” be excluded from communion; whatever the vexed phrase in pace precisely means (scholars dispute it), the canon on its most natural reading enjoins Christians to keep to their military duty under a now-friendly empire, the reverse of Tertullian’s rule. And Augustine forged the reasoning that became the West’s settled mind: war waged on lawful authority, for a just cause, and without a lust for blood is no sin, for “the real evils in war are love of violence, revengeful cruelty… not the death of some who will soon die in any case”; “do not think that it is impossible for any one to please God while engaged in active military service,” he writes to the general Boniface, adducing the very centurion of the Gospel whom Tertullian had to explain away. Tertullian’s absolute prohibition did not become the church’s law; a regulated permission did.9

How much is rigorism, how much idolatry-avoidance. The two treatises do not weigh alike. De Corona is an openly New-Prophecy work: it opens by scorning the “Psychics” who have “rejected the prophecies of the Holy Spirit” and are “purposing the refusal of martyrdom,” and its pacifism is plainly of a piece with the same rigorist temper — seek martyrdom, do not flee, do not compromise — that drives the ban on flight. To that extent the flat ban shares the authority-question that dogs Tertullian’s other rigorist counts: a maximal reading pressed from the Paraclete’s sterner discipline. But the office-ban of On Idolatry does not depend on the New Prophecy at all; it is built from the ordinary ante-Nicene logic of avoiding idolatry, and would stand on that ground whatever one makes of Montanism. The honest verdict separates the strands: the horror of idolatrous office is mainstream and well-founded; the absolute refusal of the sword as such is the rigorist maximum, true to one reading of the Gospel but not the one the church settled into.10

Assessment

contested. The interpretation is strong where it is a witness and strains where it is absolute. That the disciple is summoned to peace, that “he who takes the sword shall perish by the sword,” and above all that the Roman army and magistracy were shot through with idolatry, cult-oaths, and blood — all this holds, and Tertullian voices, at its sharpest, a suspicion of the sword shared across the ante-Nicene sources by Origen, the Apostolic Tradition, and Lactantius; how far the pre-Constantinian church was pacifist is a question historians still genuinely divide over, and this wiki does not discount his strict reading merely because the later majority relaxed it. But the flat, unconditional ban overreaches that case. The New Testament nowhere orders the soldier or the magistrate to lay down his office — John, Christ, and Peter leave the centurions in place, and Paul calls the magistrate’s sword God’s minister — so Tertullian must confine those texts against their plain sense; Christians already served, as he himself concedes when he boasts that “we… fight with you”; and the church that followed, at Arles and then in Augustine’s just-war reasoning, rejected an absolute prohibition and honoured the soldier-saints. His case is stronger against idolatrous office — where his logic is the mainstream horror of idolatry, not a Montanist tenet — than as an unqualified ban on bearing arms, where it is the rigorist maximum grounded partly in the Paraclete’s sterner discipline. Strong as a witness to the primitive church’s revulsion from blood and idolatrous power, overstated as a timeless, absolute prohibition, and disputed precisely where the ante-Nicene evidence is thinnest. Contested.
