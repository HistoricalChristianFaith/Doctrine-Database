Was the New Prophecy heretical in doctrine, or only in discipline and church order?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — on the interpretation weighed here, the New Prophecy was faulted for its prophecy and its rigour, not for any error in the doctrine of God or Christ: Tertullian and the Phrygians confessed the same Trinity and the same Christ as the catholic church — a point the hostile witnesses themselves concede — and the quarrel ran on ecstatic prophecy, fasting, remarriage, and the power of the keys, which are matters of discipline and church order rather than of the creed. The descriptive half of this is close to consensus and well-grounded. But its load-bearing inference — that the heresy-charge therefore confuses a disciplinary quarrel with a doctrinal one — overreaches, because a claim to new revelation binding on the whole church touches the sufficiency and closure of the apostolic deposit (the very principle Tertullian himself had defended), and the ecstatic manner of the prophecy was condemned as false prophecy, a judgement about the criteria of true revelation and not merely about conduct. Where “discipline” ends and “doctrine” begins is exactly what the two sides dispute, and it is left disputed here.

Related arguments: the fairness of the verdict — see also Did Tertullian ever formally leave the catholic church?

This is the second face of the fairness question the whole “Was Tertullian a Heretic?” timeline circles: its companion asks whether Tertullian ever separated from the church; this one asks what he was actually faulted for once the label was applied. The two interlock — a jurisdictional falling-out and a disciplinary one are near cousins — but they are distinct claims, resting on partly different texts, and each is weighed on its own page.

The claim

…the New Prophecies are rejected, not that Montanus and Priscilla and Maximilla preach another God, nor that they disjoin Jesus Christ (from God), nor that they overturn any particular rule of faith or hope, but that they plainly teach more frequent fasting than marrying.

— Tertullian, On Fasting, Against the Psychics 1, ANF vol. 4.1 The interpretation weighed here takes Tertullian’s own account of the dispute at its word and generalizes it: the movement the catholic church condemned as the “Cataphrygian” heresy held the orthodox faith about God and Christ and differed only over the discipline the Paraclete was said to have imposed — stricter fasts, a ban on second marriage, no flight in persecution, the veiling of virgins — and over the church order that discipline implied. On this reading the “heresy” is not a heresy in the classic creedal sense at all, but a quarrel about prophecy, rigour, and authority; to file Tertullian among the heresiarchs beside Arius or Praxeas is to mistake a dispute over church order for a dispute over the rule of faith.

Who advances it

The reading is a commonplace of modern work on Montanism. Christine Trevett’s Montanism: Gender, Authority and the New Prophecy (1996) treats the movement as doctrinally orthodox in its origins, its offence lying in prophecy and rigour; William Tabbernee’s epigraphic studies press the same conclusion from the African and Phrygian evidence; and David F. Wright, asking flatly “Why Were the Montanists Condemned?,” answers that the church acted against their claim to a new and superior prophecy and the discipline it carried, not against any error in their theology proper.2 The thesis is not merely modern, however: its strongest support is that the ancient sources most hostile to the New Prophecy say the same thing. Its charter text is Tertullian’s own (above); its most striking corroboration is that even the heresy-hunters who catalogued the Phrygians granted their orthodoxy on the Trinity, and that Augustine, filing Tertullian among the heretics, expressly declined to make his doctrine the ground of the heresy.

The evidence for

The earliest charges are about the manner of prophecy and the conduct of the prophets, not the creed. The oldest surviving anti-Montanist reports, preserved by Eusebius, fault the movement first of all for how it prophesied. The Anonymous writer says Montanus “became beside himself, and being suddenly in a sort of frenzy and ecstasy, he raved, and began to babble,” prophesying “in a manner contrary to the constant custom of the Church”; the mark of the false prophet, on this account, is that he “falls into an ecstasy, in which he is without shame or fear.” When Apollonius turns from the manner to the men, his bill of particulars is disciplinary and moral, not doctrinal: “This is he who taught the dissolution of marriage; who made laws for fasting,” and, of the prophets’ worldliness, “Does a prophet dye his hair? Does a prophet stain his eyelids?… Does a prophet play with tables and dice? Does a prophet lend on usury?” Not one of the earliest charges alleges a false God or a false Christ; they allege false prophecy and a corrupt or over-strict practice.3

The hostile sources concede the movement’s orthodoxy on God and Christ. This is the argument’s firmest ground. Epiphanius, cataloguing the Phrygians in his Panarion, records that they receive the whole of the Old and New Testaments and affirm the resurrection of the dead, and that “concerning the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit they hold the same as the holy catholic church” — the concession is made by one of antiquity’s most zealous heresy-hunters, against his own polemical interest. The Latin heresy-list appended to Tertullian’s works divides the Cataphrygians into the followers of Proclus, who share only the common Montanist claim “that the Paraclete has spoken in Montanus more things than Christ brought forward into… the Gospel,” and the followers of Aeschines, who alone “affirm Christ to be Himself Son and Father” — leaving the main body orthodox on the persons. And Tertullian’s own Against Praxeas, an openly New-Prophecy treatise, is the West’s fiercest early attack on exactly the modalism the Aeschines branch fell into: the treatise that gave Latin its word Trinitas was written by a Montanist against the confusion of the persons.4

Tertullian himself confined the Paraclete’s novelty to discipline and held the rule of faith fixed. His defence of the New Prophecy is explicit that it reforms conduct, not creed. Opening On the Veiling of Virgins he lays down that “the rule of faith, indeed, is altogether one, alone immoveable and irreformable,” and only then that, “this law of faith being constant, the other succeeding points of discipline and conversation admit the novelty of correction, the grace of God… operating and advancing even to the end.” The Paraclete’s office, on his own statement, is to carry discipline to maturity while the faith stands unchanged — which is precisely why he can say in On Fasting that the quarrel touches “more frequent fasting than marrying” and not the rule of faith. The proponent of the disciplinary reading does not have to construct Tertullian’s intent; he states it.5

Augustine, cataloguing him, refuses to found the heresy on his doctrine. The one ancient writer who gives Tertullian a numbered entry among the heresies is careful about the ground. Augustine states the doctrinal oddity — that Tertullian held the soul, and even God, to be a body — and then expressly sets it aside: “yet he is not on this account said to have been made a heretic” (Nec tamen hinc haereticus dicitur factus). What made him one, in Augustine’s telling, was going over to the Cataphrygians, condemning second marriages “against apostolic teaching… as though they were fornication,” and finally propagating “his own little assemblies.” The counts are the turn to the prophecy, the rigorist ban on remarriage, and a schism — discipline and church order, exactly — and the doctrinal error Augustine did notice he explicitly declined to charge.6

Adversarial assessment

What holds. That the New Prophecy was not a trinitarian or christological heresy in its original and its African form is as near to a settled result as this material yields. The trinitarian orthodoxy is conceded by the hostile catalogues themselves; the christological deviation belongs to one late sub-branch and not to the main body, still less to Tertullian; and the anti-Montanist bill of particulars, from the Anonymous and Apollonius through Augustine, is overwhelmingly about prophecy, fasting, remarriage, and church order. To the extent the claim says “Tertullian was not condemned for error about God or Christ,” it is right, and the popular image of a great theologian who lost his orthodoxy is simply a mistake.

New binding revelation is not a merely disciplinary matter. The claim’s reach exceeds its grip when it slides from “not a creedal error” to “merely a disciplinary and jurisdictional quarrel.” The core of the New Prophecy was not a rule about fasting but the assertion that the Paraclete was still delivering revelation binding on the whole church — that, in the Latin list’s words, “the Paraclete has spoken in Montanus more things than Christ brought forward into… the Gospel.” That claim bears directly on the sufficiency and closure of the apostolic deposit, and the principle it offends is one Tertullian himself had laid down in the Prescription Against Heretics: that the rule of faith was delivered whole by the apostles and admits no addition. A movement that adds new first-person oracles of the Spirit to the once-for-all deposit is not merely keeping a stricter Lent; it is touching the doctrine of revelation. Tertullian’s own device — confining the novelty to “discipline” while the “rule of faith” stands — is precisely the point in dispute, for the authority by which discipline is newly imposed on the whole church through a living prophet is itself a claim about where revelation may still be found.7

The ecstatic manner was condemned as false prophecy — a doctrinal judgement, not a matter of taste. The earliest and most persistent charge is not against the content of the oracles but against the mode: the prophet “beside himself,” carried out of his senses, speaking in the first person as the Paraclete, “contrary to the constant custom of the Church.” To the anti-Montanist writers this was the diagnostic mark of the false prophet, set against the sober, self-possessed prophecy they took to be the true kind. That is a judgement about the criteria of genuine revelation — how the Spirit does and does not speak — and it cannot be tidily filed under “discipline and church order.” The line between doctrine and discipline that the claim needs is, at this point, one the ancient church did not draw: rejecting a self-authenticating ecstatic prophecy was, for them, a doctrinal act.8

Later Montanism did develop real christological error — which must not be read back onto Tertullian, but tells against a blanket “orthodox throughout.” The concession that the Phrygians were sound on the Trinity holds for the main body and for Tertullian’s own (Proclian) line, but not without remainder. The Aeschines branch is reported to have “affirmed Christ to be Himself Son and Father,” a Sabellian modalism; and by the later fourth century Jerome could charge the Montanists he knew with “following the doctrine of Sabellius” and forcing “the Trinity into the narrow limits of a single personality,” alongside their claim that Montanus possessed a fullness of knowledge “such as was never claimed by Paul” and their three annual fasts. Two cautions follow, cutting opposite ways. This later modalism is a genuine doctrinal deviation, so the flat claim that Montanism was never a doctrinal error overstates; but it is a development in one wing, at a distance of a century and more, and it is the sheerest anachronism to retroject it onto Tertullian — whose Against Praxeas is the founding Latin refutation of exactly that error. The honest form of the claim is narrow and dated: Tertullian’s New Prophecy was christologically orthodox; “Montanism” tout court was not uniformly so.9

Assessment

contested. The claim is soundest where it is narrowest and strains where it is broadest. That Tertullian was not condemned for any error about God or Christ — that he confessed the catholic Trinity, wrote the West’s great anti-modalist treatise as a Montanist, and was set among the heretics by an Augustine who pointedly refused to make his doctrine the ground — is well-established and close to consensus; the popular picture of a theologian who lost his orthodoxy is wrong, and to that extent the interpretation is right and valuable. But the further, load-bearing move — that the heresy-verdict therefore mistakes a disciplinary and jurisdictional quarrel for a doctrinal one — goes beyond what the evidence will carry. A claim to new revelation binding on the whole church engages the sufficiency and closure of the apostolic deposit, a doctrinal principle Tertullian himself had defended; the ecstatic manner was condemned as false prophecy, a judgement about the criteria of true revelation; and the clean partition of “discipline” from “doctrine” the claim requires is one the early church did not recognize at just these joints. Whether the New Prophecy’s offence counts as “doctrinal” thus turns on how one classifies the authority and mode of prophecy — and there strong readers genuinely divide, the historians of the earliest controversy stressing prophecy and rigour, the theologians stressing the deposit and the discernment of spirits. The narrow claim is sound; the broad one is overstated; the question they meet on is really disputed. Contested.
