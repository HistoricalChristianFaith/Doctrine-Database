Jerome on Tertullian: the notice that became the biography

Dates: c. 347–420 · Wikipedia
Summary: Almost everything the standard life of Tertullian claims as fact comes from a single short chapter written more than a century and a half after he died: chapter 53 of Jerome’s De viris illustribus (c. 392). That he was a presbyter; that he was the son of a proconsular centurion; that he stayed in the church until middle life; that the envy and abuse of the Roman clergy drove him to Montanus; that he wrote books On Ecstasy against the church, now lost; that he lived to extreme old age — each of these rests on Jerome and, in several cases, on Jerome alone. This page sets that notice out as what it is: the earliest connected biography, but a late and interested one, to be weighed rather than simply repeated. It covers Jerome’s share of the timeline of the verdict passed on Tertullian. Two of its claims — that Tertullian was ordained a presbyter, and that resentment of the Roman clergy was his motive — are contested enough to be weighed on their own; they are flagged below, not settled here.

The notice: De viris illustribus 53 (c. 392)

Jerome compiled his catalogue of Christian writers at Bethlehem about 392, modelling it on Suetonius’s lives of the pagan authors. Tertullian is number 53, placed among the writers of the age of Severus, and the whole chapter runs to a paragraph:

Tertullian the presbyter, now regarded as chief of the Latin writers after Victor and Apollonius, was from the city of Carthage in the province of Africa, and was the son of a proconsul or Centurion, a man of keen and vigorous character, he flourished chiefly in the reign of the emperor Severus and Antoninus Caracalla and wrote many volumes which we pass by because they are well known to most. I myself have seen a certain Paul an old man of Concordia, a town of Italy, who, while he himself was a very young man had been secretary to the blessed Cyprian who was already advanced in age. He said that he himself had seen how Cyprian was accustomed never to pass a day without reading Tertullian, and that he frequently said to him, “Give me the master,” meaning by this, Tertullian. He was presbyter of the church until middle life, afterwards driven by the envy and abuse of the clergy of the Roman church, he lapsed to the doctrine of Montanus, and mentions the new prophecy in many of his books. He composed, moreover, directly against the church, volumes: On modesty, On persecution, On fasts, On monogamy, six books On ecstasy, and a seventh which he wrote Against Apollonius. He is said to have lived to a decrepit old age, and to have composed many small works, which are not extant.1

Compact as it is, this is the frame in which the Latin West has read Tertullian ever since. It gives him a rank (“chief of the Latin writers”), a city, a father, a career, a motive, a booklist of the lost “Montanist” works, and an old age — and it hangs the whole of the later verdict on one verb, lapsus est, “he lapsed.”

What depends on this chapter alone

Strip out what Jerome supplies and the biography nearly empties. Tertullian’s own surviving works never name his city with certainty, never mention his father, never call him a presbyter, never describe an ordination or a quarrel with the Roman clergy, and never announce a “lapse.” The African setting can be corroborated from the works themselves; almost nothing else in the standard Life can. In particular, these four load-bearing details rest on Jerome:

  • “The presbyter.” Jerome twice calls him presbyter. Tertullian, who is free with the first person and quick to specify his standing when it serves an argument, nowhere claims holy orders; in On Exhortation to Chastity and On Monogamy he seems rather to number himself among the laity, contrasting “us laymen” with the clergy. Whether Jerome reports a fact or infers a rank from the authority of the writings is the subject of its own weighing.
  • “Son of a proconsul or Centurion.” The phrase (proconsulis centurionis filius) is itself obscure — proconsuls did not command centurions — and unattested anywhere else.
  • The Roman-clergy motive. That it was the “envy and abuse of the clergy of the Roman church” that drove Tertullian to Montanus is Jerome’s explanation, not Tertullian’s. Nothing in Tertullian’s own account of his turn to the New Prophecy — which he frames as a matter of discipline and of the Paraclete, at Carthage — mentions Rome, a grievance, or a personal slight.
  • The lost On Ecstasy. The “six books On ecstasy, and a seventh… Against Apollonius” are known only from Jerome’s lists; that Jerome had read the work is clear enough — he quotes it elsewhere in the same catalogue, reporting that Tertullian there mocked Melito the “prophet”2 — but the books themselves are gone, and the title De ecstasi is our only evidence of Tertullian’s theoretical defence of prophetic trance.

Why weigh it, rather than repeat it

Three things make the notice testimony to be assessed, not bedrock. First, its distance: Jerome wrote about 172 years after Tertullian’s death, with no continuous Carthaginian tradition behind him, and by his own admission he mined earlier catalogues (Eusebius above all) and filled the gaps. Second, its interest. Jerome was himself the fiercest Latin champion of ascetic rigor of his generation — against Helvidius on Mary, against Jovinian on marriage, against Vigilantius on relics — and Tertullian the rigorist was a useful authority to him precisely as a rigorist. He needed Tertullian’s arguments against second marriage and for fasting even as he had to keep Tertullian’s Montanism at arm’s length; the notice does both at once, praising “the master” and recording the “lapse” in the same breath. Third, the specific details modern scholarship has learned to doubt. T. D. Barnes’s study set out how thin the independent support is for the presbyterate, the centurion father, and the identification (once standard) of this Tertullian with the Roman jurist of the same name; the “envy and abuse” motive he read as Jerome’s own novelistic touch rather than reported fact.3 None of this makes the notice worthless — it remains the earliest connected account, and the Cyprian anecdote in particular carries a chain of transmission Jerome names (the aged Paul of Concordia). It makes it a source with a date, an author, and a purpose, to be read as carefully as any other on this timeline.

Two of its claims are weighed on their own terms rather than settled in a biographical notice, and they come apart on inspection. The presbyterate, with the Digest-jurist identification and the “middle life” conversion, is genuinely disputed — it turns on Tertullian’s own apparent self-classing among the laity — and is adjudicated on its own argument page (assessment: contested). The Roman-clergy motive is weaker still: an interior state attested by this one late witness and by no other, contradicted by the reasons Tertullian himself gives for the turn, and reflecting (as Christine Mohrmann showed) Jerome’s own quarrel with the Roman clergy after his expulsion from Rome in 385. Weighed on its own argument page, that motive comes back unsupported (assessment: sound — that the motive is unsupported).

Jerome the admiring reader: Letter 84 and Against Jovinianus

Jerome’s notice is not simple condemnation. Elsewhere he treats Tertullian exactly as the whole timeline shows the tradition treating him — as a heretic to be kept at a distance and a master to be quarried without pause. In Letter 84 (c. 400), defending his own use of Origen against the charge that to translate a man is to endorse him, Jerome reaches for Tertullian and Cyprian as the standing precedent: one may admire a heretic’s genius without following his errors.

The blessed Cyprian takes Tertullian for his master, as his writings prove; yet, delighted as he is with the ability of this learned and zealous writer he does not join him in following Montanus and Maximilla.4

The logic Jerome applies to Origen — read the genius, refuse the error — is the very logic by which the church went on reading Tertullian, and Jerome states it as an established habit, not a novelty. He practised it himself: in Against Jovinianus (393), building the case against second marriage, he leans on Tertullian’s youthful writing on the married state — “Tertullian, while still a young man, gave himself full play with this subject” — drawing the rigorist’s arguments into a book of his own even as, a year earlier, he had recorded the rigorist’s lapse.5 The double attitude that this whole page traces — condemned in the handbook, quoted on every other page — is already complete in Jerome, who wrote both the handbook entry and some of the quotation.

Notes

  • Jerome is the sole source for the Cyprian “da magistrum” anecdote and for the Roman-clergy motive; both reach us only through De viris illustribus 53. The daily-reading detail is corroborated indirectly by the plain, if unacknowledged, dependence of Cyprian’s treatises on Tertullian’s — treated on the summary timeline under the third-century block.
  • Jerome’s own reliability as a reporter is weighed elsewhere on this wiki, where his notices and letters are repeatedly the earliest — and sometimes the only — witness to a fact: on the Origenist controversy, on the Old Testament canon, and across a dozen belief timelines. The pattern there is the one that recurs here: invaluable, early, and to be checked against the primary texts wherever they survive.
  • The absolute date of De viris illustribus is fixed by Jerome’s own note in the closing chapter that he wrote in the fourteenth year of the emperor Theodosius — i.e. 392/393.
