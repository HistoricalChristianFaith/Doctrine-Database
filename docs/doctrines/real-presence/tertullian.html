Tertullian on the Real Presence of Christ in the Eucharist

Dates: c. 211 (the likely date of De Corona) · Wikipedia
Summary: Tertullian witnesses less to a theory of the elements than to how the early North-African church handled them: the consecrated bread and wine were guarded so carefully that to let a crumb or a drop fall to the ground was felt as a grief. That reverence is a practical corollary of real presence — you treat as Christ's body what you believe to be Christ's body.

Claims & evidence

The consecrated bread and wine are guarded from falling to the ground

In The Chaplet (De Corona), arguing that many settled Christian practices rest on unwritten custom and tradition rather than on an explicit command of Scripture, Tertullian runs through a list of such customs — the baptismal renunciations, the milk and honey given to the newly baptized, offerings for the dead, the sign of the cross — and among them sets the care taken with the Eucharist:

We take also, in congregations before daybreak, and from the hand of none but the presidents, the sacrament of the Eucharist, which the Lord both commanded to be eaten at meal-times, and enjoined to be taken by all alike. As often as the anniversary comes round, we make offerings for the dead as birthday honours… We feel pained should any wine or bread, even though our own, be cast upon the ground.

— Tertullian, The Chaplet (De Corona) 3, trans. S. Thelwall (ANF vol. 3), full text (cf. New Advent).1 The pain at a fallen fragment is the point: the care described is not the care owed to ordinary food but to something holy. The clause "even though our own" (the bread and wine were the worshippers' own offerings) underlines that what makes the loss grievous is not the cost of the gift but what it has become in the rite. Tertullian reports this as the universal practice of the church of his day, not his private scruple — evidence of how widely the elements were already treated as the body and blood themselves.

"This is my body" — the bread as the figure that makes Christ's body present

Elsewhere, against Marcion (who denied that Christ had real flesh), Tertullian turns the Words of Institution into a proof of the reality of Christ's body: a sign must be a sign of something real.

Then, having taken the bread and given it to His disciples, He made it His own body, by saying, "This is my body," that is, the figure of my body. A figure, however, there could not have been, unless there were first a veritable body.

— Tertullian, Against Marcion IV.40, trans. P. Holmes (ANF vol. 3), New Advent.2 Tertullian's word figura ("figure") has long been read in two directions. The memorialist reading takes it to mean the bread merely represents the body; the realist reading answers that for Tertullian a figura is precisely how a thing is made present and effective (as the brazen serpent "figured" the crucified Christ and yet really healed) — and that his whole argument here depends on the bread being joined to a real body, not a phantasm. The reverence of De Corona 3 weighs in favour of the second: a community that grieves over a fallen crumb is not handling a bare symbol.

Notes

  • A witness to practice, slotted on the timeline by his conduct, not his metaphysics. Tertullian never gives a worked-out account of how Christ is present, and his figura language is genuinely two-edged. His firm contribution is behavioural: the consecrated elements were, in his church, handled as holy things — reverence (De Corona 3) that the later tradition would draw out into reservation (Cyril of Alexandria) and adoration (Trent).
  • The reverence-and-reservation thread. This care for the fragments is the earliest of three witnesses on this page to the same corollary — that the elements are treated as Christ's body: Tertullian guards them from spillage (c. 211), Cyril insists they keep their power when reserved to the next day (c. 440), and Trent defines the worship of latria due to them (1551).
  • Translation note. A common older rendering of the clause reads "We feel uneasy / we are anxious lest aught of our Cup or Bread should fall upon the ground"; the substance — distress at a fallen fragment — is the same. The quotation above follows Thelwall's Ante-Nicene Fathers text.
  • His contested orthodoxy. The De Corona cited here is a work of Tertullian's Montanist years, and whether his turn to the “New Prophecy” made him a heretic — he was never tried or condemned by any synod — is weighed on the timeline “Was Tertullian a heretic?”.
