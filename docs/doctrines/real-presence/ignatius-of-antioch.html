Ignatius of Antioch on the Real Presence

Dates: c. 108 · Wikipedia
Summary: The bishop of Antioch, writing on his way to martyrdom (c. 108), gives the earliest post-apostolic witness to the Real Presence: he calls the Eucharist "the flesh of our Saviour Jesus Christ," desires "the bread of God … which is the flesh of Jesus Christ" and "the drink of God, namely His blood," and names the one bread "the medicine of immortality." For Ignatius the realist identity of the Eucharist with Christ's flesh is no abstraction — it is the very point on which he tests the Docetists, who deny that Christ had real flesh.

Claims & evidence

The Docetists' refusal of the Eucharist proves it is the flesh of Christ (Smyrnaeans 7)

They abstain from the Eucharist and from prayer, because they confess not the Eucharist to be the flesh of our Saviour Jesus Christ, which suffered for our sins, and which the Father, of His goodness, raised up again.

— Epistle to the Smyrnaeans 7 (shorter recension), source (cf. New Advent). Ignatius identifies the Docetists precisely by their abstention from the Eucharist, and gives the reason: because they will not "confess … the Eucharist to be the flesh of our Saviour." The argument cuts both ways — it is anti-Docetic (Christ had real flesh, which suffered) and eucharistic (that flesh is what the Eucharist is). The realist identity is the premise, not the conclusion.

The bread is the flesh of Christ; the drink, His blood (Romans 7)

I have no delight in corruptible food, nor in the pleasures of this life. I desire the bread of God, the heavenly bread, the bread of life, which is the flesh of Jesus Christ, the Son of God, who became afterwards of the seed of David and Abraham; and I desire the drink of God, namely His blood, which is incorruptible love and eternal life.

— Epistle to the Romans 7 (shorter recension), source (cf. New Advent). Longing for martyrdom, Ignatius disclaims earthly food and reaches instead for "the bread of God … which is the flesh of Jesus Christ" and "the drink of God, namely His blood" — the language of John 6 ("the bread of life," "my flesh … my blood") applied directly to the Eucharist.

The one bread is "the medicine of immortality" (Ephesians 20)

… breaking one and the same bread, which is the medicine of immortality, and the antidote to prevent us from dying, but [which causes] that we should live for ever in Jesus Christ.

— Epistle to the Ephesians 20 (shorter recension), source (cf. New Advent). The single loaf broken in the one assembly, under the one bishop, is "the medicine of immortality" (pharmakon athanasias) — a remedy that confers eternal life, language that presupposes the bread is no mere token.

Notes

  • Ignatius is the earliest witness on this timeline; the genuine middle recension of his seven letters is followed throughout (the longer recension's expansions are later interpolations and are not relied on here).
  • Ignatius's eucharistic realism is bound up with his insistence that there is one Eucharist under the one bishop. That ecclesial / ministerial strand ("Let that be deemed a proper Eucharist, which is [administered] either by the bishop, or by one to whom he has entrusted it," Smyrnaeans 8) bears on the ministry of the Eucharist rather than the Real Presence as such, and is treated separately.
  • How far Ignatius's "flesh and blood" language settles the literal-vs-figurative question is weighed on the John 6 argument page: his testimony is strong evidence that the realist reading is early, though by itself it does not adjudicate the later scholastic question of the mode of the presence.
