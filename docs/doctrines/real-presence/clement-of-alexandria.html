Clement of Alexandria on the Real Presence

Dates: c. 198 · Wikipedia
Summary: In The Instructor (Paedagogus, c. 198), Clement of Alexandria treats the eucharistic cup as efficacious, not merely commemorative: "the blood of the Lord is twofold," and "to drink the blood of Jesus, is to become partaker of the Lord's immortality." The mingled cup of water and the Word "is called Eucharist," and "they who by faith partake of it are sanctified both in body and soul." Clement writes within an allegorist's frame — he reads the wine, the water, the blood, and the Word as layered symbols — yet the efficacy he ascribes to the Eucharist (immortality, sanctification of the whole person) is realist in force.

Claims & evidence

To drink the blood of Jesus is to partake of His immortality (Instructor II.2)

And the blood of the Lord is twofold. For there is the blood of His flesh, by which we are redeemed from corruption; and the spiritual, that by which we are anointed. And to drink the blood of Jesus, is to become partaker of the Lord's immortality; the Spirit being the energetic principle of the Word, as blood is of flesh.

— The Instructor (Paedagogus) II.2, "On Drinking," source (cf. New Advent). Clement distinguishes a "twofold" blood of the Lord — the blood of His flesh that redeems and the spiritual blood that anoints — but both terms of the figure point to one effect: that "to drink the blood of Jesus, is to become partaker of the Lord's immortality." The drinking is salvific, not bare remembrance.

The mingled cup "is called Eucharist," and those who partake "are sanctified both in body and soul" (Instructor II.2)

And the mixture of both-of the water and of the Word-is called Eucharist, renowned and glorious grace; and they who by faith partake of it are sanctified both in body and soul. For the divine mixture, man, the Father's will has mystically compounded by the Spirit and the Word.

— The Instructor (Paedagogus) II.2, "On Drinking," source (cf. New Advent). The mingled cup — water and the Word together — "is called Eucharist," and partaking of it by faith "sanctified both in body and soul." That the Eucharist reaches the body as well as the soul anticipates the later patristic argument (Irenaeus, then the Greek fathers) that the flesh is nourished to incorruption by the eucharistic elements, and presupposes that the cup is more than a sign.

Notes

  • Clement is an allegorist: in the same chapter he reads the wine of the Supper, its mingling with water, and the "blood of the grape" as layered symbols of the Word, the Spirit, and faith. So his witness is to eucharistic efficacy — immortality and sanctification conferred through the cup — more directly than to any scholastic account of the mode of Christ's presence. His symbolism is not the memorialist's: the symbols, for Clement, genuinely convey what they signify.
  • How far this realist-efficacy language settles the literal-vs-figurative question is weighed on the John 6 argument page; Clement's testimony is evidence that, by the end of the second century, partaking of the cup was held to confer immortality and to sanctify the whole person.
