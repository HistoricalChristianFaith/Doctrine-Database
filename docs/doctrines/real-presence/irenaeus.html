Irenaeus of Lyons on the Real Presence

Dates: c. 180–189 (Against Heresies, composed at Lyons) · Wikipedia
Summary: Writing against the Gnostics, who despised the flesh and denied that the material creation could be redeemed, Irenaeus turns the Eucharist into a weapon for the goodness of the body. His argument runs the opposite way to theirs: because the bread and the cup truly become the body and blood of Christ, and because our flesh is nourished by them, the flesh is plainly capable of resurrection. The earthly bread, "when it receives the invocation of God, is no longer common bread, but the Eucharist, consisting of two realities, earthly and heavenly"; and our bodies, fed by it, "are no longer corruptible, having the hope of the resurrection." Irenaeus is the first father to be explicit about a change in the elements worked by the word or invocation over them, and the first to tie that change so tightly to the resurrection of the body.

Claims & evidence

The bread, "when it receives the invocation of God, is no longer common bread, but the Eucharist, consisting of two realities, earthly and heavenly" (Against Heresies IV.18.5)

But our opinion is in accordance with the Eucharist, and the Eucharist in turn establishes our opinion. For we offer to Him His own, announcing consistently the fellowship and union of the flesh and Spirit. For as the bread, which is produced from the earth, when it receives the invocation of God, is no longer common bread, but the Eucharist, consisting of two realities, earthly and heavenly; so also our bodies, when they receive the Eucharist, are no longer corruptible, having the hope of the resurrection to eternity.

— Against Heresies IV.18.5, source (cf. New Advent). The bread, after the invocation of God spoken over it, "is no longer common bread" — Irenaeus marks a real transition, the plain bread giving way to something it was not. He calls the result a thing "consisting of two realities, earthly and heavenly": the earthly bread remains in view, but a heavenly reality is now joined to it. The parallel he draws is the point: as the bread is no longer common but heavenly-laden, so the bodies that feed on it are no longer destined for corruption but for resurrection.

"The Eucharist of the blood and the body of Christ is made" (Against Heresies V.2.2–3)

He has acknowledged the cup (which is a part of the creation) as His own blood, from which He bedews our blood; and the bread (also a part of the creation) He has established as His own body, from which He gives increase to our bodies. When, therefore, the mingled cup and the manufactured bread receives the Word of God, and the Eucharist of the blood and the body of Christ is made, from which things the substance of our flesh is increased and supported, how can they affirm that the flesh is incapable of receiving the gift of God, which is life eternal, which [flesh] is nourished from the body and blood of the Lord, and is a member of Him?

— Against Heresies V.2.2–3, source (cf. New Advent). Here the realism is stated as flatly as anywhere in the second century: the cup is acknowledged "as His own blood," the bread "established as His own body," and when they "receive the Word of God" the "Eucharist of the blood and the body of Christ is made." The verb again is one of transformation — the Eucharist is made — and the whole point of the passage is anti-Gnostic: our flesh, which is "nourished from the body and blood of the Lord," cannot be incapable of the gift of eternal life, for it is already being fed on the Lord himself and made "a member of Him."

Notes

  • Two features make Irenaeus a landmark on this timeline. First, the change: he is the earliest father to say plainly that the bread, after the invocation/word over it, "is no longer common bread" and that "the Eucharist … is made" — language of a wrought transition, not of an unaltered token. Second, the agency: the change is tied to "the invocation of God" and the bread's "receiv[ing] the Word of God," an early witness to a consecratory understanding of the eucharistic prayer (the seed of what the West would call the words of consecration and the East the epiclesis), as already seen in Origen's "becomes by prayer."
  • The "two realities, earthly and heavenly" phrase is much cited in later debate. It asserts a real heavenly presence joined to the bread while keeping the earthly element in view; it does not by itself settle the scholastic question of how the two relate (whether the earthly substance remains, as the Lutheran reading would later press, or is changed beneath the appearances, as the transubstantiation reading would hold). Irenaeus's interest is not metaphysical precision but the goodness of the flesh: the earthly creation is fit to bear, and be transformed by, the heavenly gift. How far his realism settles the literal-vs-figurative question is weighed on the John 6 argument page.
  • The same stretch of the work (IV.17–18) carries Irenaeus's most influential statement on the Eucharist as the Church's offering. Christ "took that created thing, bread, and gave thanks, and said, 'This is My body.' And the cup likewise … He confessed to be His blood, and taught the new oblation of the new covenant; which the Church receiving from the apostles, offers to God throughout all the world" — the "pure sacrifice" offered "in every place" that Malachi foretold (Against Heresies IV.17.5).1 Irenaeus is careful that God "stood in need" of nothing: the oblation is the Church's grateful offering of creation's first-fruits, not a gift that supplies a divine lack. That eucharistic-sacrifice theme is a distinct doctrine from the presence in the elements and is treated in its own right; it is noted here only because, in Irenaeus, the offering and the presence are one continuous argument — the Church can offer the bread and cup as Christ's body and blood precisely because they have become such.
