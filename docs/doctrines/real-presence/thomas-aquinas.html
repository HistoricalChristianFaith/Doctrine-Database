Thomas Aquinas on the Real Presence of Christ in the Eucharist

Dates: c. 1274 (the Summa Theologiae, Third Part, left unfinished at his death) · Thomas Aquinas, c. 1225–1274; Dominican friar and the pre-eminent scholastic theologian of the Latin West · Wikipedia
Summary: Aquinas gives transubstantiation its full Aristotelian exposition, supplying the account of how that the dogma of the Fourth Lateran Council (1215) had named but not explained. In Summa Theologiae III, q. 75 he holds that the substance of the bread and wine does not remain after the consecration; that the whole substance of each is converted into the whole substance of Christ's body and blood — a conversion so unlike any natural change that it must be "called by a name of its own, 'transubstantiation'"; and that the sensible accidents (appearances) remain. He is the terminus of the medieval formalization the realist Fathers set in motion — and the systematic authority the Council of Trent would presuppose.

Claims & evidence

The substance of the bread does not remain (Summa Theologiae III, q. 75, a. 2)

Aquinas rejects the view that the bread's substance persists alongside Christ's body. Answering whether the substance of the bread and wine remains after the consecration, he takes it as settled that it does not, and argues that the substance is neither annihilated nor dissolved but converted:

the substance of the bread and wine does not remain in this sacrament.

— Summa Theologiae III, q. 75, a. 2, New Advent (Fathers of the English Dominican Province translation).1 This is Aquinas's decisive break from any "sacramental union" or consubstantiation reading: what was bread is no longer bread in substance once the words of consecration are spoken.

The whole substance is converted — "transubstantiation" (III, q. 75, a. 4)

The conversion, he argues, is unlike any natural change, in which one form succeeds another in an abiding matter; here the whole substance passes into another whole substance. This unique kind of change requires a name of its own:

the whole substance of the bread is changed into the whole substance of Christ's body, and the whole substance of the wine into the whole substance of Christ's blood. Hence this is not a formal, but a substantial conversion; nor is it a kind of natural movement: but, with a name of its own, it can be called "transubstantiation."

— Summa Theologiae III, q. 75, a. 4, New Advent.2 This is the Aristotelian machinery the argument page identifies as demonstrably medieval: the analysis of the change as a conversion of one metaphysical substance into another, worked with a precision unknown to the Fathers, who confessed the change without the apparatus.

The accidents (appearances) remain (III, q. 75, a. 5)

If the substance of the bread is gone, what is seen, touched, and tasted? Aquinas answers that the sensible qualities — the "accidents" — remain, so that the sacrament neither deceives the senses nor repels the communicant with the visible eating of flesh:

It is evident to sense that all the accidents of the bread and wine remain after the consecration.

— Summa Theologiae III, q. 75, a. 5, New Advent.3 Aquinas grants that these remaining accidents have no natural subject to inhere in — the change "has not properly a subject" — the point later theology would develop as accidents subsisting by divine power; it is the sharpest form of the substance/appearance distinction Trent would restate as species (appearances) remaining while the substance is wholly converted.

Notes

  • What it establishes. Aquinas supplies the how the earlier witnesses left unstated. Gregory of Nyssa and Ambrose had asserted a change of the elements; Lateran IV named it transubstantiatio; Aquinas explains it in Aristotelian terms — whole substance converted, accidents remaining. His exposition is the systematic form the Council of Trent presupposes when it defines the conversion "which the Catholic Church suitably and properly calls transubstantiation."
  • The realism it formalizes is older than the apparatus. The argument page turns on exactly this: the substance/accidents analysis and the term are Aquinas's-era achievements, but the conviction they formalize — that the elements are truly changed into Christ's body and blood — is patristic. Aquinas is the reason the "medieval-innovation" thesis is half right (about the philosophy) and half wrong (about the faith).
  • Same author, two questions, two pages. This page treats Aquinas's account of the eucharistic change (Summa III, q. 75). His account of who may consecrate — the priest alone, acting in persona Christi by a power conferred at ordination (Summa III, q. 82) — is on his ministerial-priesthood page. The two questions are the twin poles of the medieval eucharistic synthesis.
  • Provenance. The Summa Theologiae is quoted from the standard Fathers of the English Dominican Province translation as published online; its Third Part was left unfinished at Aquinas's death in 1274.
