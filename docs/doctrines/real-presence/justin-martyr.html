Justin Martyr on the Real Presence

Dates: c. 155 (the First Apology, addressed to the emperor Antoninus Pius, c. 155; the Dialogue with Trypho c. 155–160) · Wikipedia
Summary: Describing the Sunday assembly for a pagan readership in the First Apology, Justin gives the earliest surviving explicit statement that the consecrated elements are not ordinary food: "not as common bread and common drink do we receive these," but "the flesh and blood of that Jesus who was made flesh." He grounds the claim on the Incarnation and on the words of institution handed down in the "memoirs of the apostles." In the Dialogue with Trypho he calls the Eucharist of the bread and the cup the "pure sacrifice" foretold by Malachi. He is the earliest realist witness on this timeline after Ignatius.

Claims & evidence

The consecrated food is "not as common bread and common drink," but "the flesh and blood of that Jesus who was made flesh" (First Apology 66)

And this food is called among us the Eucharist, of which no one is allowed to partake but the man who believes that the things which we teach are true, and who has been washed with the washing that is for the remission of sins, and unto regeneration, and who is so living as Christ has enjoined. For not as common bread and common drink do we receive these; but in like manner as Jesus Christ our Saviour, having been made flesh by the Word of God, had both flesh and blood for our salvation, so likewise have we been taught that the food which is blessed by the prayer of His word, and from which our blood and flesh by transmutation are nourished, is the flesh and blood of that Jesus who was made flesh. For the apostles, in the memoirs composed by them, which are called Gospels, have thus delivered unto us what was enjoined upon them; that Jesus took bread, and when He had given thanks, said, "This do ye in remembrance of Me, this is My body;" and that, after the same manner, having taken the cup and given thanks, He said, "This is My blood;" and gave it to them alone.

— First Apology 66, source (cf. New Advent). Writing to defend Christians before the emperor, Justin explains the closed table and the transformation the food undergoes. The parallel he draws is decisive: as the Word "was made flesh" and so had real flesh and blood, so the food "blessed by the prayer of His word" is that flesh and blood — the reality of the Incarnation is the warrant for the reality in the Eucharist. He ties the change to the recited words of institution from the Gospels, the same consecratory logic later fathers would elaborate.

The Eucharist of the bread and cup is the "pure sacrifice" of Malachi (Dialogue with Trypho 41, 117)

[So] He then speaks of those Gentiles, namely us, who in every place offer sacrifices to Him, i.e., the bread of the Eucharist, and also the cup of the Eucharist, affirming both that we glorify His name, and that you profane [it].

— Dialogue with Trypho 41, source (cf. New Advent); repeated at Dialogue 117, where "all the sacrifices which we offer through this name … i.e., in the Eucharist of the bread and the cup" are set against the rejected sacrifices of the temple. Reading Malachi 1:10–11, Justin identifies the prophesied "pure offering … in every place" with the Christian Eucharist. The sacrificial framing bears chiefly on the ministerial priesthood and the eucharistic offering; it is adduced here as corroboration that Justin regards the Eucharist as more than a bare memorial meal.

Notes

  • The witness is doubly weighty because it is not an in-house catechesis but an apology addressed to outsiders: Justin has no motive to overstate a mystery he would rather have concealed, yet he states plainly that the food is received as flesh and blood. That makes it a strong early datum for what the mid-second-century Roman church believed itself to be doing.
  • The phrase rendered "by transmutation" (Greek kata metabolēn) has been read two ways — as the food's nourishing conversion into the communicant's own flesh (its plain grammatical sense), or as looking toward a change in the elements themselves. The ANF editor's note observes that "this passage is claimed alike by Calvinists, Lutherans, and Romanists," the language being open enough that each finds its own view in it. What is not in doubt is the governing clause: the food is "the flesh and blood of that Jesus who was made flesh." How far this settles the mode of presence is weighed on the John 6 argument page.
  • Justin locates the change in "the prayer of His word" (the word we have from Christ) — an early witness, alongside Origen, to a consecratory understanding of the eucharistic prayer and the recited words of institution, later sharpened by Ambrose into a doctrine of consecration by the words of Christ.
