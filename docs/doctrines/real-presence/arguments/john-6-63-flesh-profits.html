Does "the flesh profiteth nothing" (John 6:63) refute the realist reading?

Type: interpretive argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — as a decisive counter-text against the Real Presence the argument fails, because on the dominant patristic and grammatical reading "the flesh" names a carnal way of understanding (or the flesh considered apart from the Spirit), not the eucharistic flesh; it is sound only in the modest sense that it rules out a crude, Capernaite literalism the realists themselves reject · pressed as the memorialist linchpin by Huldrych Zwingli.

Related arguments: the New Testament words — see also Is the Real Presence taught by the New Testament, or is the language symbolic?

The question

Midway through the Bread of Life discourse, after the hearers stumble over "eat my flesh," Jesus says: "It is the spirit that quickeneth; the flesh profiteth nothing: the words that I speak unto you, they are spirit, and they are life" (John 6:63).1 The memorialist reads this as the discourse's own retraction: Jesus himself pulls back from the literal flesh-eating and reframes the whole teaching as spiritual — so "this is my body" must likewise be figurative. Is John 6:63 that key? The parent argument weighs the whole New Testament hinge; this page weighs the single verse most often pressed as its solvent.

The case for the memorialist use

An internal qualifier, in the same breath. This is not a proof-text imported from elsewhere; it is Jesus' own gloss on his own hard saying, spoken to the very disciples who "murmured at it" (6:61). The verse opposes "spirit" to "flesh" and immediately adds that his words "are spirit, and they are life" — apparently relocating the whole benefit from an eaten flesh to a believed word.2

Zwingli's linchpin. Huldrych Zwingli made 6:63 the hinge of the memorialist case, reading "is" in the Words of Institution as significat ("signifies") and the eating as a spiritual, believing remembrance. If "the flesh profiteth nothing," then a bodily eating of Christ's flesh can profit nothing either; the profit is in the quickening Spirit and the life-giving word. At Marburg (1529) he pressed the trope against Luther, and the disagreement was never resolved.3

The evidence against — what "the flesh" means

The memorialist use founders on the question of what "flesh" refers to in the sentence, and here the early readers are strikingly united: on the dominant patristic construal "the flesh profiteth nothing" does not speak of Christ's eucharistic flesh at all.

Chrysostom: the carnal manner of hearing. Chrysostom is explicit that the verse is about a way of understanding, not about Christ's body: "He speaketh not of His own flesh (God forbid!), but of those who received His words in a carnal manner. But what is 'understanding carnally'? It is looking merely to what is before our eyes, without imagining anything beyond." He drives it home with the discourse's own logic: "'He that eateth not His flesh, and drinketh not His blood, hath no life in him.' How then doth 'the flesh profit nothing,' if without it we cannot live? Seest thou that the words, 'the flesh profiteth nothing,' are spoken not of His own flesh, but of carnal hearing?"4

Augustine: the Capernaite sense. Augustine reads it the same way: "It profiteth nothing, but only in the manner in which they understood it. They indeed understood the flesh, just as when cut to pieces in a carcass, or sold in the shambles; not as when it is quickened by the Spirit." What must become spiritual is the understanding, not the flesh, which "joined to the Spirit … profiteth very much."5 Theophylact, summarizing the Greek tradition, agrees: "the flesh, that is, the carnal understanding of [Christ's words], is not at all profitable, but becomes an occasion for scandal."6

Cyril of Alexandria: flesh considered alone, apart from the Word. Cyril takes the other main line — "flesh" means flesh regarded by itself, cut off from the life-giving Word: "when the nature of the flesh is considered alone and by itself, plainly it is not life-giving … but as to Christ alone it holdeth not, by reason that Life, that is the Only-Begotten, dwelt therein." The verse, he insists, is spoken "not as repudiating His Own Flesh … but as teaching us what is the truth": the same flesh, united to the Word, "hath become wholly Life-giving."7 Tertullian, two centuries earlier, had already fixed the rule that decides the sense: "the meaning must be regulated by the subject which is spoken of" — the Spirit gives life, "the flesh profiteth nothing" toward that giving of life, but the Word "also said that it is his flesh, because the Word also was made flesh."8

The grammar of sarx. This reading is not special pleading. Throughout the Johannine and Pauline writings "flesh" (sarx) routinely names the unspiritual, natural mode of a thing rather than physical matter as such — the children of God are born "not of blood, nor of the will of the flesh" (John 1:13); "that which is born of the flesh is flesh" (John 3:6); "the flesh lusteth against the Spirit" (Galatians 5:17). On that idiom the flesh/Spirit contrast of 6:63 is a contrast of two ways of receiving — natural versus Spirit-given — not a contrast of sign versus reality, and it says nothing against the reality of the flesh given to eat.

Adversarial assessment

What holds for the memorialist. John 6:63 is a real qualifier, not a prooftexter's invention, and every serious realist reader has had to reckon with it. It genuinely bars one reading of the discourse — the crude, cannibalistic, "cut to pieces in a carcass" literalism of the murmuring disciples. To that extent the verse is a legitimate check, and the Fathers cited above grant it freely: the flesh is not to be gnawed as butcher's meat.

Where the argument overreaches. But the memorialist needs far more than that: he needs 6:63 to mean that the eucharistic eating profits nothing, that the flesh given is only a sign. That is precisely what the verse, on its dominant construal, does not say. Whether one takes the Antiochene line (flesh = carnal understanding) or the Alexandrian line (flesh = flesh apart from the indwelling Word), the sentence addresses the mode of reception, not the reality of the gift; it deflates a fleshly way of taking the words, not the flesh that is taken. Turning it into a refutation of the Real Presence is a category error — and one against which the near-unanimous early reception, in both East and West, stands as heavy evidence. Zwingli's construal ("flesh" = Christ's own flesh, whose eating profits nothing) is a possible but minority reading that the discourse's own next clauses resist: if the flesh in view were Christ's, Jesus would be unsaying "except ye eat the flesh of the Son of man … ye have no life in you" (6:53) three verses after asserting it.

Assessment

Weak. As the memorialist's decisive counter-text — the verse that dissolves the realist reading of John 6 — the argument does not hold. The dominant patristic construal, corroborated by the Johannine idiom of "flesh," is that "the flesh profiteth nothing" concerns a carnal manner of understanding, or flesh considered apart from the life-giving Word, and so never denies that the flesh given is real; it denies only a fleshly way of receiving it. The verse remains a genuine and useful qualifier — it rules out the Capernaite literalism that the realists reject too — but as a proof against the Real Presence it is weak. (This narrow finding is consistent with the parent argument's contested rating of the whole New Testament hinge: 6:63 is a live qualifier within that larger dispute, not a solvent that settles it.)
