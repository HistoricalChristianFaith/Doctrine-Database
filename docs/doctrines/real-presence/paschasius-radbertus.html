Paschasius Radbertus on the Real Presence of Christ in the Eucharist

Dates: c. 831 (De Corpore et Sanguine Domini, revised for Charles the Bald c. 844; Paschasius, abbot of Corbie, c. 785–865) · Wikipedia
Summary: Paschasius wrote the first full-length Latin treatise devoted to the Eucharist, and pressed the realist tradition to a strict identity: the body received on the altar is the very body of Christ "which was crucified and buried" — the same flesh born of the Virgin, now made present by the priest's consecration through the Spirit. His book set the terms of the ninth-century Carolingian dispute, drawing the figurative reply of his fellow Corbie monk Ratramnus. He is the medieval hinge on which the "innovation" question turns.

Claims & evidence

The sacrament is reality, not mere figure: the true body of Christ, crucified and buried

In De Corpore et Sanguine Domini (c. 831, revised and presented to Charles the Bald c. 844) — the first Latin monograph on the Eucharist — Paschasius distinguishes the outward "figure" from the inward "reality," and identifies the reality as Christ's own historical body:

What is perceived externally is a figure or mark, but what is perceived internally is entirely reality and no figure at all; and therefore nothing else is here revealed but reality and the sacrament of the body itself—the true body of Christ, which was crucified and buried, surely the sacrament of his body, which is divinely consecrated by the priest above the altar with the word of Christ through the Spirit: whence the Lord Himself exclaims, "this is my body" (Luke 22:19).

— Paschasius Radbertus, De Corpore et Sanguine Domini, as translated in Bengt Hägglund, History of Theology (St. Louis: Concordia, 1968), p. 156.1 The "figure" here is not opposed to reality but names the outward sign of a reality inwardly present — yet Paschasius insists the reality is Christ's own flesh, not a distinct sacramental body: the very identity Ratramnus would deny.

The eucharistic flesh is the flesh born of Mary — the Incarnation as the pattern of the miracle

Paschasius grounds the change on the Incarnation: as the Word once took flesh of the Virgin without seed by the power of the Holy Spirit, so the same Spirit makes Christ's body present from the substance of the bread and wine at the priest's word. He teaches that the flesh received in the sacrament is identical with the flesh "born of the Virgin Mary, that suffered on the cross and rose from the grave" — a daily miracle, invisible to sight and taste, worked by God.2 This identity-realism is the strongest Latin statement of the Real Presence before the schoolmen, and the reason later writers read Paschasius as an anticipation of transubstantiation, even though he had neither the term nor the Aristotelian analysis of substance and accidents.

Notes

  • The Carolingian dispute opens the medieval question. Paschasius's identity-realism drew the figurative reply of Ratramnus, a monk of the same abbey of Corbie writing at the same king's request. That two monks of one house could disagree so sharply is the argument page's evidence that the ninth-century West had no settled scholastic account of the Eucharist — see Is transubstantiation a medieval innovation, or the patristic realism made precise?
  • Continuity with the Fathers. His Incarnation-warrant repeats Justin Martyr's logic (as the Word truly took flesh, the consecrated food truly is that flesh), and his change-language stands in the line of Gregory of Nyssa and Ambrose. What is new is the systematic, monograph-length treatment and the sharpened identity of the sacramental and historical body.
  • Source provenance. There was no complete English translation of the treatise until Mark G. Vaillancourt's On the Body and Blood of the Lord (Corpus Christianorum in Translation 34, Brepols, 2023); the Latin is in Patrologia Latina 120 and the critical edition of Bede Paulus (CCCM 16, 1969). The verbatim passage above is given in Hägglund's History of Theology; the identity of the eucharistic flesh with the flesh of Mary, crucified and risen, is at De Corpore 4 and 7 (so the standard reference works: Catholic Encyclopedia; Encyclopedia.com).3
