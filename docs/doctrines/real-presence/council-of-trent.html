The Council of Trent on the Real Presence of Christ in the Eucharist

Dates: 11 October 1551 (Session 13 of the Council of Trent, the nineteenth ecumenical council, the third session under Pope Julius III; the Decree Concerning the Most Holy Sacrament of the Eucharist) · Wikipedia
Summary: Answering the Reformation, Trent's thirteenth session both defines the realist patristic line as transubstantiation — the substance of bread and wine changed into the body and blood of Christ — and draws out its worship corollary: because Christ is wholly and truly present, the sacrament is owed the worship of latria, the adoration due to God alone, and may be reserved, venerated, and carried in procession. This is the dogmatic terminus of the reverence-and-reservation thread that runs from Tertullian and Cyril.

Claims & evidence

The presence defined: transubstantiation

Before the worship corollary, the decree fixes what is being worshipped. Against the symbolic and spiritual readings of the Reformers, Trent defines the change of substance:

by the consecration of the bread and of the wine, a conversion is made of the whole substance of the bread into the substance of the body of Christ our Lord, and of the whole substance of the wine into the substance of his blood; which conversion is, by the holy Catholic Church, suitably and properly called Transubstantiation.

— Council of Trent, Session 13 (1551), Decree Concerning the Most Holy Eucharist, ch. 4, trans. J. Waterworth.1 This is the scholastic account named in the summary's spectrum of views; the fuller treatment of the definition as a development belongs to a later entry. What matters for this page is that the decree treats the presence as the ground of the worship that follows.

The worship corollary: the latria due to the true God is rendered to the sacrament

From the real, substantial presence the council draws the adoration corollary directly. If Christ is wholly present, then the worship owed to Christ is owed to the sacrament:

Wherefore, there is no room left for doubt, that all the faithful of Christ may, according to the custom ever received in the Catholic Church, render in veneration the worship of latria, which is due to the true God, to this most holy sacrament.

— Council of Trent, Session 13 (1551), Decree Concerning the Most Holy Eucharist, ch. 5, trans. Waterworth.2 Latria is the technical term for the adoration due to God alone, as distinct from the dulia (honour) shown to the saints — so the decree is claiming the very highest worship for the consecrated host, on the explicit ground that Christ "the true God" is there. The same session attaches an anathema to its denial:

If any one saith, that, in the holy sacrament of the Eucharist, Christ, the only-begotten Son of God, is not to be adored with the worship, even external of latria; and is, consequently, neither to be venerated with a special festive solemnity, nor to be solemnly borne about in processions… or, is not to be proposed publicly to the people to be adored, and that the adorers thereof are idolators; let him be anathema.

— Council of Trent, Session 13 (1551), Canon 6, trans. Waterworth.3 The canon names the practices the worship issues in — the feast of Corpus Christi ("a special festive solemnity"), eucharistic processions, and public exposition for adoration — and condemns the Reformed charge that to adore the host is idolatry.

Reservation defended as the ancient custom of the Church

The council also defends keeping the consecrated host beyond the liturgy — the practice whose premise Cyril of Alexandria had stated eleven centuries earlier — as a custom "most ancient" and to be retained:

The custom of reserving the holy Eucharist in the sacrarium is so ancient, that even the age of the Nicene Council recognised that usage… (the holy Synod) declares that this custom, as most salutary and necessary, is to be retained.

— Council of Trent, Session 13 (1551), Decree, ch. 6, trans. Waterworth.4 Reservation, adoration, and procession hang together on one premise: the host carried to the sick, kept in the tabernacle, or borne through the streets is still the body of Christ, because the presence does not lapse when the rite ends — exactly Cyril's "the life-giving grace is perpetual in it."

Notes

  • The terminus of the reverence-and-reservation thread. Trent is the third and latest of three witnesses on this page to the corollary that the elements are treated as Christ's body: Tertullian's grief at a fallen fragment (c. 211) and Cyril's insistence that the reserved sacrament keeps its power (c. 440) are the practice and the premise; Trent makes the worship that follows a defined dogma under anathema.
  • The point of contention with the Reformers. Canon 6 is aimed squarely at the Reformed objection that adoring the consecrated bread is creature-worship — idolatry. Trent's answer is that it is not the bread that is adored but Christ present under its appearance, which is why the whole weight rests on the prior definition of the real, substantial presence: grant the presence and the worship follows; deny it and the adoration is indeed misdirected. The dispute over whether the presence is real is weighed in the Arguments on the summary.
  • Not "added at Trent." The council presents adoration and reservation as the received custom of the church, not innovations — pointing back, as on the canon question, up the timeline. The reverence is attested already in Tertullian and the enduring presence in Cyril; what Trent adds is dogmatic definition and the anathema, in answer to a denial that had not been raised in those earlier centuries.
  • Translation provenance. The English is J. Waterworth's, The Canons and Decrees of the Sacred and Oecumenical Council of Trent (London: Dolman, 1848), the standard nineteenth-century English edition of the conciliar acts.
