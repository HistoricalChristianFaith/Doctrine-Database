Origen on the Real Presence

Dates: c. 248 · Wikipedia
Summary: In Against Celsus (Contra Celsum, c. 248), Origen answers the pagan Celsus — who held that men must offer thanks and first-fruits to the demons set over the things of this world — by insisting that Christians give thanks instead to the Creator. In that context he writes that "we also eat the bread presented to us; and this bread becomes by prayer a sacred body, which sanctifies those who sincerely partake of it." The wording is doubly notable: the bread becomes something — "a sacred body" — and it does so by prayer, a consecratory act, and the result sanctifies those who partake. Origen is the most thoroughgoing allegorist of the early fathers, and elsewhere he spiritualizes the eating of Christ's flesh; yet here the eucharistic bread is held to be made, through the Church's prayer, an efficacious holy thing.

Claims & evidence

The bread "becomes by prayer a sacred body, which sanctifies those who sincerely partake of it" (Against Celsus VIII.33)

For this reason, then, let Celsus, as one who knows not God, give thank-offerings to demons. But we give thanks to the Creator of all, and, along with thanksgiving and prayer for the blessings we have received, we also eat the bread presented to us; and this bread becomes by prayer a sacred body, which sanctifies those who sincerely partake of it.

— Against Celsus (Contra Celsum) VIII.33, source (cf. CCEL, ANF vol. 4; New Advent). The argument is anti-pagan: Celsus urged thank-offerings to the demons who govern earthly provision, and Origen counters that Christians receive their food with thanksgiving to its Maker. The eucharistic bread is his prime case — and he does not call it a bare token. It "becomes" a "sacred body" by prayer, and the partaking is efficacious: it "sanctifies those who sincerely partake of it."

Notes

  • Three features carry weight. First, the verb: the bread becomes ("γίνεται") something it was not — language of change, not of unaltered sign. Second, the agency: it becomes a sacred body by prayer, locating the effect in a consecratory act of the gathered Church, which the eucharistic prayer would later be understood to be. Third, the effect: it sanctifies the worthy communicant — efficacy, not mere remembrance.
  • The witness must be read against Origen's wider habit. He is the boldest allegorist of the ante-Nicene fathers, and in commenting on John 6 and on Leviticus he repeatedly turns the "eating" of Christ's flesh toward the feeding of the soul on the Word, warning against a crassly literal construal. That frame qualifies how far this passage settles the mode of presence. But it does not dissolve the efficacy: even within his spiritualizing reading, the consecrated bread here is held to become a holy body that conveys sanctification — the symbol genuinely effects what it signifies. How far such language settles the literal-vs-figurative question is weighed on the John 6 argument page.
  • The "becomes by prayer" phrasing is also an early witness to a consecratory understanding of the eucharistic prayer, the seed of what later tradition would call the words of consecration (and the East the epiclesis); the realist efficacy is tied to an act of the Church, not to the bread as such. The question of who may perform that act belongs to the ministerial priesthood, not to this page.
