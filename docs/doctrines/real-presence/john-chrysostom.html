John Chrysostom on the Real Presence

Dates: c. 392 · Wikipedia
Summary: The great Antiochene preacher is among the most vivid realists of the patristic age. In his Treatise on the Priesthood he stands the reader before the altar where "the Lord [is] sacrificed" and "He who sitteth on high with the Father is at that hour held in the hands of all"; in his homilies on 1 Corinthians he tells his hearers that "this which is in the cup is that which flowed from His side," and that the bread they receive simply is "the Body of Christ." Chrysostom's realism is inseparable from his sense of the priest's awesome office — the same treatise is a principal witness on the ministerial priesthood — and from his moral urgency about worthy reception.

Claims & evidence

"He who sitteth on high with the Father is at that hour held in the hands of all" (On the Priesthood 3.4)

For when thou seest the Lord sacrificed, and laid upon the altar, and the priest standing and praying over the victim, and all the worshippers empurpled with that precious blood, canst thou then think that thou art still amongst men, and standing upon the earth? … Oh! what a marvel! what love of God to man! He who sitteth on high with the Father is at that hour held in the hands of all, and gives Himself to those who are willing to embrace and grasp Him. And this all do through the eyes of faith!

— Treatise on the Priesthood (De Sacerdotio) 3.4, source (NPNF Series I, vol. 9). Comparing the eucharistic rite to Elijah's fire from heaven, Chrysostom sharpens the point: "There stands the priest, not bringing down fire from Heaven, but the Holy Spirit" (3.4) — the same epiclesis-realism as Cyril of Jerusalem, that the Spirit invoked on the gifts effects the sacrifice.

"This which is in the cup is that which flowed from His side" (Homily 24 on 1 Corinthians)

"The cup of blessing which we bless, is it not a communion of the Blood of Christ?". Very persuasively spake he, and awfully. For what he says is this: "This which is in the cup is that which flowed from His side, and of that do we partake."

Expounding Paul's "communion of the body," Chrysostom draws the identity as tightly as he can:

For what is the bread? The Body of Christ. And what do they become who partake of it? The Body of Christ: not many bodies, but one body.

— Homilies on First Corinthians, Homily 24 (on 1 Cor 10:16–17), source (NPNF Series I, vol. 12; cf. New Advent). Chrysostom presses even Paul's verb "break": "that which He suffered not on the cross, this He suffers in the oblation for thy sake, and submits to be broken, that he may fill all men."

Notes

  • The most concrete patristic idiom. Where Origen and Clement spiritualize and Cyril of Jerusalem hedges with "type," Chrysostom reaches for the boldest physical language: the worshippers "empurpled with that precious blood," the enthroned Lord "held in the hands," the draught in the cup "that which flowed from His side." He does not thereby teach the later scholastic theory of the change; his aim is homiletic awe and the call to communicate worthily. But the presence he preaches is unambiguously real, not a token of an absent Christ.
  • Realism at the service of ethics and unity. Chrysostom's realism always turns pastoral. From "we are that self-same body" he argues at once to charity — "why do we not also show forth the same love, and become also in this respect one?" — and elsewhere he thunders that to approach with a defiled conscience is to be "guilty of the body and blood of the Lord." The real presence is, for him, what makes both the danger of unworthy communion and the demand of mutual love so grave.
  • Because the Treatise on the Priesthood makes the Eucharist the ground of the priest's dignity — "what priests do here below God ratifies above" — Chrysostom is also a central witness on the ministerial priesthood; there his emphasis falls on who may offer, here on what is offered. How far his language settles the literal reading of John 6 and the Words of Institution is weighed on the John 6 argument page.
