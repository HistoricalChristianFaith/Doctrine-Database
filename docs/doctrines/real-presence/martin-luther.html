Martin Luther on the Real Presence of Christ in the Eucharist

Dates: c. 1529 (the Marburg Colloquy and the Large Catechism; Luther 1483–1546) · Wikipedia
Summary: The Wittenberg reformer broke with Rome over much, but not over the reality of the presence. Against Zwingli's memorialism, Luther insisted that the consecrated bread and wine are the true body and blood of Christ — present "in and under" the elements, on the plain force of Christ's words "This is my body." His reading is sacramental union: the body and blood are truly and bodily received, yet the bread and wine remain bread and wine (so it is not the substance-change Trent would define as transubstantiation, nor quite the "consubstantiation" popularly ascribed to him). At the Marburg Colloquy of 1529 he chalked Hoc est corpus meum on the table and refused to move off the words, breaking fellowship with the Swiss over this one article.

Claims & evidence

The consecrated bread and wine are the true body and blood of Christ

In the Large Catechism (1529), his most-used catechetical statement, Luther defines the Sacrament of the Altar in flatly realist terms — the elements, by the Word of Christ, are his body and blood:

It is the true body and blood of our Lord Jesus Christ, in and under the bread and wine which we Christians are commanded by the Word of Christ to eat and to drink.

What makes the Sacrament, for Luther, is not the priest's power over a substance but the Word joined to the element:

It is the Word (I say) which makes and distinguishes this Sacrament, so that it is not mere bread and wine, but is, and is called, the body and blood of Christ.

— The Large Catechism, Part Fifth, "Of the Sacrament of the Altar."1 Because the presence rests on the Word and not on the worthiness of the minister, Luther adds that even "a knave" who distributes the Sacrament still hands over "the true body and blood of Christ" — the presence is objective, not conjured by the recipient's faith.

"This is my body" — the words are no trope

Against the symbolic readers, who took est ("is") to mean significat ("signifies"), Luther denied that the words of institution contain any figure at all. In the Confession Concerning Christ's Supper (1528), his fullest treatise on the Supper, he presses the bare words:

…since we are confronted by God's words, "This is my body" — distinct, clear, common, definite words, which certainly are no trope, either in Scripture or in any language — we must embrace them with faith… not as hairsplitting sophistry dictates but as God says them for us, we must repeat these words after him and hold to them.

— Confession Concerning Christ's Supper (1528).2 The next year, at the colloquy Philip of Hesse convened at Marburg (1–4 October 1529) to unite the Protestant camps, Luther made the same stand in person — chalking the Latin of "This is my body" on the table and covering it with a cloth, to be uncovered whenever the Swiss pressed him:

I do not question how Christ can be God and man and how the two natures can be joined. For God is more powerful than all our ideas, and we must submit to his word. Prove that Christ's body is not there when Scripture says, "This is my body!" I will not listen to "rational proofs!" God is beyond all mathematics; the words of God are to be revered and carried out in awe. It is God who commands, "Take, eat, this is my body."

— Luther's opening statement, The Marburg Colloquy (1529).3 The parties agreed on fourteen of the fifteen Marburg Articles and deadlocked only on this one — whether Christ's body is bodily present in the bread — and Luther refused the Swiss the hand of fellowship over it.

Sacramental union, not transubstantiation: the substance is left to God

For all his realism, Luther declined the medieval account of how the presence comes about. He did not require that the bread's substance be annihilated or converted; it was enough that Christ's body and blood are truly there, however God accomplishes it. He put the point with deliberate provocation:

I have often enough asserted that I do not argue whether the wine remains wine or not. It is enough for me that Christ's blood is present; let it be with the wine as God wills. Sooner than have mere wine with the fanatics, I would agree with the pope that there is only blood.

— Confession Concerning Christ's Supper (1528).4 This is "sacramental union" (unio sacramentalis): the true body and blood present "in, with, and under" bread and wine that remain bread and wine. It differs from Rome's transubstantiation (a change of the whole substance of the bread, only the accidents remaining) precisely in leaving the substances standing — and Luther grounded the whole thing in the Word, not in a metaphysics of two substances, which is why the popular label "consubstantiation" misfits his refusal to theorize the mode.

Notes

  • The Reformation's first fault-line. Luther's stand at Marburg against Zwingli is the origin of the Lutheran/Reformed split. The two agreed against Rome on justification and against the radicals on much else, but Luther held the literal est of the words of institution to be non-negotiable, and Zwingli held Christ's ascended body to be locally in heaven and the bread its sign. Neither moved; the division outlasted them.
  • Between Zwingli and Calvin. Luther's bodily presence is the far realist pole of the Reformation spectrum, over against Zwingli's memorial and Calvin's later spiritual/pneumatic reception. Where Calvin has the believer lifted by the Spirit to feed on the body of Christ in heaven, Luther keeps the body here, delivered in the mouth with the bread.
  • John 6:63. Because the symbolic case rested heavily on "the flesh profiteth nothing" (John 6:63), the verse is weighed on its own page — does "the flesh profiteth nothing" refute the realist reading? — alongside the broader literal-or-figurative question on which Luther and the Swiss divided.
  • Reported at Marburg. Luther is widely said to have told the Swiss they were "of another spirit" (ihr habt einen andern Geist denn wir) and so refused fellowship; the sentence is reported in the colloquy tradition rather than fixed in a single verbatim transcript, and is given here as reported.
