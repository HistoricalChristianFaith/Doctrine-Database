Cyril of Alexandria on the Real Presence of Christ in the Eucharist

Dates: c. 440 (the Letter to Calosyrius; Cyril d. 444) · Wikipedia
Summary: Against a sect who held that the consecrated Eucharist lost its power if it were kept and consumed on a later day, Cyril insists that the presence does not lapse with time: Christ's body is not changed, and "the power of the consecration and his life-giving grace is perpetual in it." He is an early and explicit witness to the enduring real presence — the premise of eucharistic reservation.

Claims & evidence

The reserved Eucharist keeps its power: Christ is not changed, the life-giving grace is perpetual

In a letter to Calosyrius, bishop of Arsinoe, written against the Anthropomorphites and the false mysticism associated with them, Cyril addresses a particular error: that the Eucharist, if reserved to the following day rather than consumed at once, ceased to be efficacious. He calls the opinion senseless and answers it head-on:

Christ is not altered, nor is his holy body changed, but the power of the consecration and his life-giving grace is perpetual in it.

— Cyril of Alexandria, Letter to Calosyrius, Bishop of Arsinoe (Letter 83; Greek in Patrologia Graeca 76, 1075–76, and in Pusey’s edition), §6.1 The wording quoted here is Wace’s; the letter is now also translated in full as Letter 83 in J. I. McEnerney’s standard English series, which renders the closing clause “the power of the consecration and his life-giving grace is perpetual in his body.”2 The logic is exactly that of Irenaeus pressed forward in time: if the bread, having received the invocation, "is no longer common bread," then neither does it revert to common bread when the assembly disperses. What the consecration effects is in Christ and his unchanging body, Cyril argues, not in the passing moment of reception — so the grace "is perpetual in it." This is the conceptual basis of reservation: keeping the consecrated elements beyond the liturgy precisely because they remain what they were made.

Notes

  • A witness to the durability of the presence. Earlier witnesses on this page establish that the elements become the body and blood (Ignatius, Irenaeus, Origen); Cyril answers the next question — whether that becoming lasts. His "perpetual" rules out any view on which the presence is confined to the instant of communion. It is the same realism that grounds Tertullian's care for a fallen fragment and that Trent would carry to the adoration of the reserved sacrament.
  • The reverence-and-reservation thread. Cyril is the middle of three witnesses here to the corollary that the elements are treated as Christ's body: Tertullian guards them from spillage (c. 211), Cyril holds that they keep their power when reserved (c. 440), and Trent defines the worship of latria due to them (1551).
  • Source provenance. The Letter to Calosyrius is the same work as Cyril's tract Against the Anthropomorphites (its title runs “against those saying God is anthropomorphic”; §§1–5 refute the Anthropomorphites, §6 answers the eucharistic error, §7 the idle monks). It is numbered Letter 83 (= CPG 5383): the Greek is edited by P. E. Pusey (Sancti patris nostri Cyrilli… in D. Joannis Evangelium…, vol. iii, Oxford, 1872, pp. 603–607) and reprinted in Patrologia Graeca 76, 1075–76; and it is included in the standard English series, translated in full as Letter 83 in J. I. McEnerney, St. Cyril of Alexandria: Letters 51–110 (Fathers of the Church 77, 1987), pp. 109–111, and again in L. R. Wickham, Cyril of Alexandria: Select Letters (Oxford, 1983), pp. 214–221. The rendering quoted above is Wace's; the same passage is quoted to the same effect in modern Catholic catechetical sources (e.g. the U.S. bishops' The Real Presence of Jesus Christ in the Sacrament of the Eucharist, citing PG 76, 1076).3
