Gregory of Nyssa on the Real Presence

Dates: c. 383 (The Great Catechism, Oratio Catechetica Magna, c. 383–386; Gregory's lifespan c. 335–395) · Wikipedia
Summary: In chapter 37 of The Great Catechism Gregory gives the strongest change-of-substance statement among the Greek fathers on this timeline: the bread consecrated by the Word "is changed into the Body of God the Word," and God by the benediction "transelements" (metastoicheiōsis) the natural quality of the visible things into the immortal Body. The change is worked "at once … by means of the Word," not by digestion — an account that later Western writers saw as anticipating transubstantiation.

Claims & evidence

The consecrated bread "is changed into the Body of God the Word" — "at once … by means of the Word" (Great Catechism 37)

Rightly, then, do we believe that now also the bread which is consecrated by the Word of God is changed into the Body of God the Word. For that Body was once, by implication, bread, but has been consecrated by the inhabitation of the Word that tabernacled in the flesh. … not that it advances by the process of eating to the stage of passing into the body of the Word, but it is at once changed into the body by means of the Word, as the Word itself said, "This is My Body."

— The Great Catechism 37, source (NPNF Series II, vol. 5; cf. New Advent). Gregory argues that as the body Christ took was maintained by bread and yet "was transmuted to the dignity of Godhead" by the indwelling Word, so now the consecrated bread is changed into that same Body. He is careful to deny that the change is the ordinary conversion of food into flesh by eating: it happens at once, by the Word and the "sacred Benediction," on the model of Christ's own "This is my body."

God "transelements" the visible things into the immortal Body (Great Catechism 37)

He gives these gifts by virtue of the benediction through which He transelements the natural quality of these visible things to that immortal thing.

— The Great Catechism 37, source. The verb Gregory coins here — metastoicheioō, "to transelement," to change the underlying elements — is the sharpest term for a change of substance among the fourth-century Greek fathers, going beyond the "is made" and "becomes" of earlier witnesses to name a re-constitution of what the thing fundamentally is. The NPNF editor notes that if the received text is sound, "Gregory distinctly teaches a transmutation of the elements very like the later transubstantiation," and "distinctly teaches that the words of consecration effect the change."

Notes

  • Gregory reaches this through his "antidote" theme: humanity, poisoned by sin and death, needs the immortal Body taken in as medicine so that, "like a little leaven," it may assimilate the mortal body to its own incorruption. The realism is therefore load-bearing for his soteriology — the Body must truly be present and truly received for it to do the healing work he assigns it — and is not a passing devotional flourish.
  • His language of "transelementing" the quality of the visible things is not yet the scholastic distinction of substance and accidents that Trent would use; Gregory writes before that vocabulary existed. But the substance of the claim — that consecration changes what the bread and wine are into the Body and Blood — is the same realist conviction the West would later define. How far such change-language settles the disputed reading of the institution texts is weighed on the John 6 argument page.
  • Gregory locates the change in the Word and the benediction ("This is My Body"), aligning him with the consecration-by-the-words tradition of Ambrose rather than solely with the epiclesis emphasis of Cyril of Jerusalem — though as a Cappadocian he holds both the Word and the Spirit's invocation together.
