Ratramnus of Corbie on the Real Presence of Christ in the Eucharist

Dates: c. 843 (De Corpore et Sanguine Domini, written for Charles the Bald; Ratramnus d. c. 868) · Wikipedia
Summary: Asked by the emperor Charles the Bald whether the faithful receive Christ's body in the Eucharist "in a mystery" or "in very truth," Ratramnus — a monk of the same abbey of Corbie as Paschasius Radbertus — answered against his confrere: the consecrated elements are Christ's body and blood in figura, "in a figure," received spiritually by faith, and are not the same as the visible flesh born of Mary. Yet he is no bare symbolist — the figure genuinely bears the reality. His treatise became the great medieval counter-witness the eleventh-century reaction and, later, the Reformers would invoke.

Claims & evidence

The elements are Christ's body "in a figure," not the visible historical flesh

Charles the Bald put two questions to Ratramnus: whether the body and blood of Christ are received in the Eucharist in mysterio (in a mystery) or in veritate (in very truth), and whether it is the same body that was born of Mary and suffered. Ratramnus answers that the elements are a figure — a sign that truly holds the thing it signifies, but is not identical with the historical body:

that Bread and that Wine, figuratively, are the Body and Blood of Christ… there is a great difference betwixt the visible appearance, and the invisible substance; that is, betwixt the Sacrament of the Body, and the substance of the Body.

— Ratramnus, De Corpore et Sanguine Domini, as translated in the parallel Latin/English edition Bertram, or Ratram, concerning the Body and Blood of the Lord (Oxford, 1687), Oxford Text Archive.1 Against Paschasius's strict identity of the sacramental and the historical body, Ratramnus distinguishes the two: what is seen and tasted is the sacramentum; the body itself is received invisibly, by faith. He therefore denies that the eucharistic body is, without qualification, "the very body which was born of Mary, suffered, died, and was buried."

A figure, yet not an empty sign: the reality is truly received

Ratramnus is careful not to hollow the sacrament into a bare token. The bread and wine are figures, but figures that convey what they signify to the believing communicant:

not the True Body of Christ, but the Figure, or Sacrament of it… and yet not mere empty figures, or naked signs.

— Bertram, or Ratram, concerning the Body and Blood of the Lord (Oxford, 1687), Oxford Text Archive.2 His "figure" is the Augustinian and Platonist sacramentum — the sign through which a reality is given, not a substitute for it — which is why his treatise resists being read as a flat memorialism even as it refuses Paschasius's identity-realism.

Notes

  • The Carolingian dispute — two monks of one house. Ratramnus and Paschasius Radbertus were both monks of Corbie, both wrote a De Corpore et Sanguine Domini for Charles the Bald, and reached opposite conclusions — proof that the ninth-century West had no settled scholastic account of the Eucharist to appeal to. The disagreement is the hinge the argument page Is transubstantiation a medieval innovation, or the patristic realism made precise? narrates.
  • A treatise with a long afterlife. Ratramnus's book was condemned as Berengar's at the synod of Vercelli (1050) — then still circulating under the name of Ratramnus's contemporary John Scotus Eriugena — and was later prized by the Reformers as patristic-era evidence for a non-carnal reading of the Supper; the 1687 Oxford translation was an Anglican polemical edition to that end. He supplies the medieval counter-line that Berengar of Tours would revive in the eleventh century.
  • Figure vs. identity, not symbol vs. presence. Ratramnus's in figura stands in the same family as Cyril of Jerusalem's "type"/"antitype" and Augustine's "sign": in that idiom "figure" is not opposed to reality but is the mode of its giving. His quarrel with Paschasius is over the identity of the sacramental and historical body, not over whether Christ is truly received.
