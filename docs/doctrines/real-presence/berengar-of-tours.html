Berengar of Tours on the Real Presence of Christ in the Eucharist

Dates: c. 1070 (De Sacra Coena, his reply to Lanfranc; Berengar c. 999–1088) · Wikipedia
Summary: Berengar, master of the school of Tours, revived Ratramnus's figurative line and denied a material change of the elements: after the consecration the bread and wine remain bread and wine, and are the sign or sacrament of Christ's body and blood, truly received in spirit and by faith. Condemned at a string of synods, he was forced in 1059 to swear a grossly physical oath that the faithful crush Christ's body with their teeth, and in 1079 a milder one introducing "substantial conversion." The reaction to Berengar produced the very language that would harden into the dogma of transubstantiation — he is the dissenter whose denial forced the definition.

Claims & evidence

The bread remains bread and is the sign of Christ's body — received spiritually, by faith

Against Lanfranc of Bec, Berengar argued in De Sacra Coena ("On the Holy Supper") that the consecration does not annihilate or physically transmute the bread; it makes it a sacrament — a sign that truly conveys the reality it signifies to the believing soul. He distinguishes the outward sign received in the mouth from the inward reality (the res sacramenti) received by faith:

Eternal salvation is given us if we receive with a pure heart the body of Christ, that is, the reality of the sign [rem sacramenti], while we are receiving the body of Christ in sign [in sacramento], that is, in the holy bread of the altar, which belongs to the temporal order.

— Berengar of Tours, De Sacra Coena adversus Lanfrancum (ed. Beekenkamp, 1941, p. 158), as translated in "Berengar of Tours," New Catholic Encyclopedia.1 The bread "belongs to the temporal order" — visible, changeable — whereas Christ's body, Berengar urged, cannot be subjected to time or change; so the sacrament is Christ's body in sign, not by a physical presence of the flesh on the altar.

Not bare symbolism: the sign genuinely bears the reality

Berengar resisted the charge that he emptied the sacrament into a mere token. He would, he insisted, venerate the consecrated bread as the body of Christ — it is bread, "but not bread alone" — for through consecration the element becomes the efficacious sign of Christ's body, received in the soul. A modern historian of the controversy sums up his position: for Berengar "the Holy Eucharist, after the consecration as before, is bread and wine, not the Body of Christ, but the sign of the Body of Christ," the material elements receiving "a 'conversion,' not of their natural being, but of their spiritual significance."2

The oaths forced on him — and the language they created

Berengar's denial was condemned repeatedly (Rome and Vercelli 1050, Tours 1055, Rome 1059, Poitiers 1075, Rome 1078–79). At the Roman synod of 1059, under Nicholas II, he was made to swear an oath drafted by Cardinal Humbert of Silva Candida that the bread and wine after consecration are "the true body and blood of our Lord Jesus Christ, and are sensibly (sensualiter)… handled and broken by the hands of the priests, and crushed by the teeth of the faithful" — a formula so crudely physical that later theology quietly retired it. The milder oath of 1079, under Gregory VII, introduced the word that would become technical: the bread and wine are "substantially converted" (substantialiter converti) into Christ's flesh and blood — the first appearance of that phrase in an official ecclesiastical document.3

Notes

  • The dissenter who forced the definition. Berengar revived the line of Ratramnus (whose treatise had circulated under the name of John Scotus Eriugena and was condemned as Berengar's at Vercelli in 1050). The reaction to his denial produced the "crushed by the teeth" oath and then the "substantial conversion" formula — language no Father had used — on the road to the Fourth Lateran Council's dogmatizing of transsubstantiatio in 1215. The whole sequence is weighed on the argument page Is transubstantiation a medieval innovation, or the patristic realism made precise?
  • Where he stands on the spectrum. Berengar is not a sixteenth-century memorialist: his "sign" genuinely bears the reality, received by faith — closer to the spiritual/pneumatic pole (later associated with Calvin) than to Zwingli's bare remembrance. His quarrel is with a carnal, physical presence, not with a true reception of Christ. This is why the wiki lists him among the realist timeline's disputants, not outside it.
  • Source provenance. There is no complete published English translation of De Sacra Coena; the Latin critical text is W. H. Beekenkamp, De Sacra Coena adversus Lanfrancum (The Hague, 1941), re-edited as Rescriptum contra Lanfrannum (CCCM 84, ed. R. B. C. Huygens). The English renderings above are scholars' translations of individual passages.
