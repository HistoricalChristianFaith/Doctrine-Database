Ambrose of Milan on the Real Presence

Dates: c. 390 · Wikipedia
Summary: In his mystagogical sermons to the newly baptized — On the Mysteries (De Mysteriis) and the companion On the Sacraments (De Sacramentis) — Ambrose gives the Latin West its sharpest early statement of a conversion of the elements. Where the Greek fathers spoke of the bread "becoming" or being "made" the Body, Ambrose builds a whole argument on the premise that "by blessing nature itself is changed": before the consecration the elements are bread and wine; after the words of Christ are spoken over them, they are his Body and Blood. This conversionist language, tied firmly to the words of institution as the effective cause, is the fountainhead of the medieval Western account that Trent would define as transubstantiation.

Claims & evidence

"By blessing nature itself is changed" (On the Mysteries 9.50, 52)

Let us prove that this is not what nature made, but what the blessing consecrated, and the power of blessing is greater than that of nature, because by blessing nature itself is changed.

Ambrose runs through the Old Testament miracles in which nature was overruled at a prophet's word — the rod turned to a serpent, the Nile to blood, the sea divided, water from the rock — and argues a fortiori to the consecration:

But if the blessing of man had such power as to change nature, what are we to say of that divine consecration where the very words of the Lord and Saviour operate? For that sacrament which you receive is made what it is by the word of Christ. … But if the word of Elijah had such power as to bring down fire from heaven, shall not the word of Christ have power to change the nature of the elements?

— On the Mysteries (De Mysteriis) 9.50, 52, source (NPNF Series II, vol. 10; cf. New Advent).

"Before the blessing … another nature; after the consecration the Body is signified" (On the Mysteries 9.54)

The Lord Jesus Himself proclaims: "This is My Body." Before the blessing of the heavenly words another nature is spoken of, after the consecration the Body is signified. He Himself speaks of His Blood. Before the consecration it has another name, after it is called Blood. And you say, Amen, that is, It is true. Let the heart within confess what the mouth utters, let the soul feel what the voice speaks.

— On the Mysteries 9.54. Earlier in the same sermon Ambrose sets the eucharistic bread above the manna: "that living Bread which came down from heaven, furnishes the substance of eternal life; and whosoever shall eat of this Bread shall never die, and it is the Body of Christ" (8.47).

"Before it is consecrated, it is bread; … when the words of Christ have been spoken, it becomes the body of Christ" (On the Sacraments 4)

Before it is consecrated, it is bread; but when the words of Christ have been spoken, it becomes the body of Christ. Finally, listen to him saying: Take and eat of this all of you; for this is my body. And before the words of Christ, the cup is full of wine and water: when the words of Christ have been spoken, there the blood of Christ is made, which redeemed the people. Therefore, see how mighty is the word of Christ in converting all things.

— On the Sacraments (De Sacramentis) 4, source. On the Sacraments reads as a stenographic record of the sermons Ambrose polished into On the Mysteries; its authenticity, long doubted, is now generally accepted. The sixth book states the realist premise bluntly: as Christ "is true God … so true is his flesh … which we receive, and true is his blood" (6.1).

Notes

  • Why Ambrose matters for the later doctrine. His "before … after" formula makes the moment and cause of the change explicit — the recitation of the dominical words, "This is My Body" — and grounds its possibility in the omnipotent creative word ("Shall not the word of Christ, which was able to make out of nothing that which was not, be able to change things which already are into what they were not?", Myst. 9.52). This is the seed of the medieval Western doctrine of consecration and of the scholastic account of substantial change; Ambrose is quoted at length in the canon-law De consecratione and stands behind the Latin tradition Trent codified.
  • The qualifiers he keeps. Ambrose is not a crude physicalist. He grants that the senses report bread and wine — "I do not see the appearance of blood," to which he answers, "But it has a likeness … so that there is no horror of blood, and yet the price of redemption is accomplished" (De Sacr. 4) — and he can call the sacrament "not bodily food but spiritual … for the Body of Christ is the Body of the Divine Spirit" (Myst. 58). The change he asserts is real but not gross; the "likeness" preserves the outward appearance while the reality is the Body. This is the same balance the Greek fathers strike with "type"/"antitype," reached from the Latin side.
  • The reality-versus-appearance argument leans throughout on the virgin birth as its controlling analogy: as Christ's body was "born of the Virgin, not according to nature," so the consecrated element is "not what nature made, but what the blessing consecrated" (Myst. 9.53). Whether such language already settles the literal reading of John 6 and the Words of Institution is weighed on the John 6 argument page.
