Cyril of Jerusalem on the Real Presence

Dates: c. 350 · Wikipedia
Summary: In the Mystagogical Catecheses — the addresses delivered to the newly baptized at Jerusalem explaining the sacraments they had just received — Cyril gives the ante-Nicene realism its first sustained, catechetical statement. The fourth lecture, On the Body and Blood of Christ, tells the neophytes to "consider … the Bread and the Wine not as bare elements, for they are … the Body and Blood of Christ"; the fifth, walking them through the liturgy, describes the invocation of the Holy Spirit upon the gifts "that He may make the Bread the Body of Christ, and the Wine the Blood of Christ." Cyril also, uniquely among the earlier witnesses, pairs this realism with the vocabulary of type and antitype and "figure" — showing that in the Greek fathers such "figure" language was not a memorialist reservation but a way of naming how the visible sign holds the real gift.

Claims & evidence

"Consider the Bread and the Wine not as bare elements, for they are … the Body and Blood of Christ" (Mystagogic Catechesis 4.6)

Consider therefore the Bread and the Wine not as bare elements, for they are, according to the Lord's declaration, the Body and Blood of Christ; for even though sense suggests this to thee, yet let faith establish thee. Judge not the matter from the taste, but from faith be fully assured without misgiving, that the Body and Blood of Christ have been vouchsafed to thee.

— Catechetical Lecture 22 (Mystagogic 4), On the Body and Blood of Christ, §6, source (NPNF Series II, vol. 7; cf. New Advent). The lecture opens on 1 Corinthians 11:23 and reasons from the dominical words: "Since then He Himself declared and said of the Bread, This is My Body, who shall dare to doubt any longer?" (§1). Cyril presses the point against the evidence of the senses again at the close: the neophyte is to be "fully assured … that the seeming bread is not bread, though sensible to taste, but the Body of Christ; and that the seeming wine is not wine, though the taste will have it so, but the Blood of Christ" (§9).

The change of the elements — the Cana analogy and the epiclesis (Mystagogic 4.2; 5.7)

Cyril grounds the change in Christ's power over nature, appealing to the miracle at Cana:

He once in Cana of Galilee, turned the water into wine, akin to blood, and is it incredible that He should have turned wine into blood?

— Mystagogic 4.2. Walking the newly baptized through the eucharistic liturgy in the next lecture, he locates the change at the invocation of the Spirit over the gifts:

we beseech the merciful God to send forth His Holy Spirit upon the gifts lying before Him; that He may make the Bread the Body of Christ, and the Wine the Blood of Christ; for whatsoever the Holy Ghost has touched, is surely sanctified and changed.

— Catechetical Lecture 23 (Mystagogic 5), On the Sacred Liturgy and Communion, §7, source (cf. New Advent). This is one of the earliest explicit witnesses to a consecratory epiclesis — the presence effected by the Spirit invoked upon the elements — and the plain verb is "sanctified and changed."

Reverence for the consecrated gift (Mystagogic 5.21)

Cyril's directions for receiving communion carry the same premise as Tertullian's grief over a fallen fragment: the elements are handled as a holy thing. The communicant is to make of his left hand "a throne for the right, as for that which is to receive a King," and to guard against loss:

Wilt thou not then much more carefully keep watch, that not a crumb fall from thee of what is more precious than gold and precious stones?

— Mystagogic 5.21–22. The reverence is the practical corollary of the realism, and the same thread of careful handling and reservation runs on through Cyril of Alexandria to the Council of Trent.

Notes

  • "Figure" and "antitype" are not memorialist here. In the very lectures that call the elements "not bare elements … the Body and Blood of Christ," Cyril also says that "in the figure (typos) of Bread is given to thee His Body, and in the figure of Wine His Blood" (4.3), and bids the communicants "taste, not bread and wine, but the anti-typical (antitypa) Body and Blood of Christ" (5.20). The apparent tension is instructive: in Greek patristic usage the "type"/"antitype" vocabulary names the visible sign through which the reality is given, not a mere token standing in for an absent thing — so a father can call the consecrated bread a "figure" of the Body in one breath and insist it truly is the Body in the next. This bears directly on the literal-vs-figurative question: the Fathers' "figure" language cannot simply be enlisted for the memorialist side.
  • Authorship and date. The five Mystagogical Catecheses are transmitted under Cyril's name but some manuscripts ascribe them (in whole or part) to his successor, John of Jerusalem, and their authorship is disputed; a date in the 380s or 390s is defensible if John is the author. Either way they issue from the Jerusalem church of the mid-to-late fourth century; this timeline follows the traditional attribution and the earlier date, c. 350.
  • Cyril reads John 6 as of a piece with the eucharistic institution — "Except ye eat My flesh and drink My blood, ye have no life in you" (4.4) — while noting that the Capernaites erred because they heard it "in a spiritual sense" not at all, "supposing that He was inviting them to eat flesh." His own construal is neither crassly carnal nor merely symbolic: the gift is real, received by faith, under the "figure" of bread and wine.
