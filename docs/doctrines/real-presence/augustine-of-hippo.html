Augustine of Hippo on the Eucharist

Dates: c. 412 · Wikipedia
Summary: Augustine (354–430), bishop of Hippo, is the single most-disputed patristic witness on the Eucharist, and both sides of the later argument claim him with reason. To the newly baptized he says flatly that "the bread is the body of Christ, the cup is the blood of Christ," and in the Expositions on the Psalms he draws the sharpest realist corollary of any father before the medievals: "no one eats that flesh unless he has first worshipped … we sin in not worshipping." Yet the same Augustine makes John 6's "eat my flesh" his textbook example of a figure in On Christian Doctrine, reads the eating as incorporation into the body of Christ (the Church) rather than a carnal chewing, and tells his hearers "believe, and thou hast eaten already." He is not two minds but one: the sign truly bears the reality it signifies — sacramentum and res held together — which is exactly why later realists and figurists can each quote him against the other.

The realist strand

To the newly baptized: "the bread is the body of Christ, the cup is the blood of Christ"

Therefore, what you see is bread and a cup; your eyes also report this to you: but what your faith demands to be instructed, the bread is the body of Christ, the cup is the blood of Christ. … These, brothers, are called Sacraments, because in them one thing is seen, another is understood. What is seen has a corporeal appearance, what is understood bears spiritual fruit. … your mystery lies on the Lord's table: you receive your mystery. … Be what you see, and receive what you are.

— Sermon 272, preached on Pentecost to the newly baptized, historicalchristian.faith.1 The affirmation is unqualified — the consecrated bread is the body — and yet in the same breath Augustine turns it ecclesial: what lies on the altar is also the communicants' own "mystery," for they are the body of Christ (1 Corinthians 12:27), and are to "be what they see." The sign is real and the reality is the whole Christ, head and members. His companion Easter sermon strikes the same note: "That bread which you see on the altar, sanctified by the word of God, is the body of Christ … If you have received well, you are what you have received" (Sermon 227).2

"No one eats that flesh unless he has first worshipped … we sin in not worshipping"

For He took upon Him earth from earth; because flesh is from earth, and He received flesh from the flesh of Mary. And because He walked here in very flesh, and gave that very flesh to us to eat for our salvation; and no one eateth that flesh, unless he hath first worshipped: we have found out in what sense such a footstool of our Lord's may be worshipped, and not only that we sin not in worshipping it, but that we sin in not worshipping.

— Expositions on the Book of Psalms, on Psalm 98(99), §8 (the Vulgate Enarratio in Psalmum 98.9), on "fall down before His footstool," historicalchristian.faith.3 This is the strongest realist sentence in the pre-medieval Latin corpus, and the one most often marshalled for the adoration of the sacrament: the flesh given to eat is the very flesh born of Mary, and it must be worshipped — indeed it is sin not to. But the passage is a fulcrum in miniature, because Augustine immediately checks a carnal misreading: "But doth the flesh give life? Our Lord Himself … said, 'It is the Spirit that quickeneth, the flesh profiteth nothing.'" The disciples who left "thought of it carnally, and imagined that the Lord would cut off parts from His body." The flesh to be worshipped is real; the eating is not butchery.

The sign strand

"Eat my flesh" is a figure — because taken literally it would enjoin a crime

If the sentence is one of command, either forbidding a crime or vice, or enjoining an act of prudence or benevolence, it is not figurative. If, however, it seems to enjoin a crime or vice, or to forbid an act of prudence or benevolence, it is figurative. "Except ye eat the flesh of the Son of man," says Christ, "and drink His blood, ye have no life in you." This seems to enjoin a crime or a vice; it is therefore a figure, enjoining that we should have a share in the sufferings of our Lord, and that we should retain a sweet and profitable memory of the fact that His flesh was wounded and crucified for us.

— On Christian Doctrine (De Doctrina Christiana) III.16.24, historicalchristian.faith.4 This is the load-bearing figurative proof-text, and the reason the memorialist tradition can enlist Augustine. He states a hermeneutical rule — a command that would be a crime taken literally must be figurative — and applies it to the very verse (John 6:53) on which the realist reading rests, glossing the "eating" as sharing in Christ's Passion and holding "a sweet and profitable memory" of the crucifixion. Read alone, this is a strongly symbolic-memorial gloss; read alongside the sermons and the Psalms, it shows that for Augustine a text can be "a figure" and still convey a real presence — the sign is not opposed to the reality but the vehicle of it.

The eating is incorporation into the body of Christ — the sacrament versus the thing itself

This it is, therefore, for a man to eat that meat and to drink that drink, to dwell in Christ, and to have Christ dwelling in him. Consequently, he that dwelleth not in Christ, and in whom Christ dwelleth not, doubtless neither eateth His flesh [spiritually] nor drinketh His blood [although he may press the sacrament of the body and blood of Christ carnally and visibly with his teeth], but rather doth he eat and drink the sacrament of so great a thing to his own judgment.

— Tractates on the Gospel of John 26.18 (on John 6:56), historicalchristian.faith.5 Augustine's most characteristic move is to distinguish the outward sacramentum from the inward res (the thing signified). The unworthy communicant "presses the sacrament … with his teeth" but does not truly eat Christ's flesh, because to eat it is to "dwell in Christ" — to be a living member of his body. Hence the great cry of the same Tractate: "O sacrament of mercy, O sign of unity, O bond of love! Whoso wishes to live, let him draw nigh, believe, be incorporated, that he may be quickened" — and his summary that by this meat and drink the Lord "would have us understand the society of His body, and His members, which is the Church." The res of the sacrament, for Augustine, is the unity of the Church in Christ.

"Believe, and thou hast eaten already"

This is then to eat the meat, not that which perisheth, but that which endureth unto eternal life. To what purpose dost thou make ready teeth and stomach? Believe, and thou hast eaten already.

— Tractates on the Gospel of John 25.12 (on John 6:29), historicalchristian.faith.6 The most quotable of Augustine's spiritualizing lines, and a favourite of the Reformers: the eating that gives eternal life is faith, not mastication. It comes earlier in the same discourse than the "flesh" sayings, applied to "the work of God, that ye believe on Him whom He hath sent" (John 6:29), so it does not by itself dissolve the eucharistic eating of 6:53–56 into bare believing. But it fixes the direction of Augustine's whole reading: the profit of the sacrament is inseparable from faith and charity, never a merely physical transaction.

"The flesh profiteth nothing" — understood spiritually

"It is the Spirit that quickeneth; the flesh profiteth nothing." … It profiteth nothing, but only in the manner in which they understood it. They indeed understood the flesh, just as when cut to pieces in a carcass, or sold in the shambles; not as when it is quickened by the Spirit. … "The words that I have spoken to you are spirit and life." … Hast thou understood spiritually? "They are spirit and life." Hast thou understood carnally? So also "are they spirit and life," but are not so to thee.

— Tractates on the Gospel of John 27.5 (on John 6:63), historicalchristian.faith.7 Augustine handles John 6:63 — the qualifier both sides fight over — with unusual care, and his reading is neither the memorialist's nor the crude literalist's. "The flesh profiteth nothing" refutes only the Capernaite sense, the flesh "as cut to pieces in a carcass"; joined to the Spirit, the same flesh "profiteth very much," for "the Word was made flesh" and "it is by the flesh that the Spirit has done somewhat for our salvation." What must be spiritual is the understanding, not a swapping-out of the flesh for a symbol.

Notes

  • The fulcrum, not a fence-sitter. Augustine's even-handedness is not indecision. His governing category is the sacrament — a sacred sign in which "one thing is seen, another understood" (Sermon 272), and in which the sign genuinely conveys the reality it signifies. That single framework yields both strands at once: he can say the bread is the body and that it must be worshipped, and equally that "eat my flesh" is "a figure" and that "believe, and thou hast eaten." Later readers who force him onto one pole flatten a position that is deliberately both/and.
  • Both traditions claim him. Medieval and Catholic realists rest the adoration of the sacrament on the footstool passage (Enarr. in Ps. 98.9); Ratramnus of Corbie, Berengar, Wycliffe, and the Reformers (Zwingli, Calvin, Cranmer) rest the figurative-spiritual reading on On Christian Doctrine III.16 and the Tractates. Both cite him fairly, because both strands are really there.
  • Bearing on the arguments. Augustine's even-handedness is itself weighed on a dedicated argument page, Augustine's sign and figure: was he a symbolic witness against the Real Presence? (assessment: contested). He is also a load-bearing witness for the parent hinge, the literal-vs-figurative reading of John 6 and the Words of Institution (assessment: contested): On Christian Doctrine III.16 is a genuine, first-rank figurative proof-text, while the sermons and the Psalms are first-rank realist ones. And his handling of John 6:63 anchors the finding that "the flesh profiteth nothing" does not refute the realist reading (assessment: weak). That the same father anchors both sides is itself strong evidence that the text does not decide the question by grammar alone.
