Huldrych Zwingli on the Real Presence of Christ in the Eucharist

Dates: c. 1525 (his memorialist teaching goes public and into practice at Zürich; quoted here from the 1524 Letter to Matthew Alber and the 1530 Account of the Faith; Zwingli 1484–1531) · Wikipedia
Summary: The Zürich reformer is the fountainhead of the memorialist reading. For Zwingli the "is" of "This is my body" means signifies: the bread and wine are signs that represent Christ's body and blood and call his once-for-all sacrifice to mind, so that the Supper is a thanksgiving and a public pledge, not a vehicle of the flesh. Christ's true body, he held, ascended and sits at the right hand of the Father — and a body is in one place — so it is not eaten with the mouth but received "by the contemplation of faith." In Holy Week 1525 Zürich abolished the Mass for his plain memorial rite; at the Marburg Colloquy of 1529 he and Luther agreed on fourteen articles and split irreconcilably on this one.

Claims & evidence

"This is my body" means "this signifies my body"

Zwingli's decisive move was grammatical: the copula "is" (est) in the words of institution is figurative, standing for "signifies" (significat). He first aired the reading publicly in his 1524 letter to the Reutlingen reformer Matthäus Alber:

The meaning of Christ's words becomes perfectly plain to this effect: "This feast signifies or is the symbol by which you will recall that my body, mine, the Son of God, your Lord and Master, was given for you."

— Letter to Matthew Alber Concerning the Lord's Supper (1524).1 He defended the figurative reading by scriptural parallel: as "This is Jehovah's passover" (Exodus 12:11) names not the event but its sign, so does "This is my body." In the Account of the Faith he lays the parallel down —

the words: "This is my body," should not be understood naturally, but figuratively, just as the words: "This is Jehovah's passover"

— and observes that the paschal lamb "was not the passing over of the Lord, but it signified that such a passing over had formerly taken place" (Account of the Faith, 1530).2 The "signifies" reading was in the air — Zwingli drew on and popularized the est = significat argument of the Dutch jurist Cornelius Hoen (Honius), whose letter reached him about 1524 and which he published in 1525 — though he maintained he had held the symbolic view already.

The body is received "by the contemplation of faith," not with the mouth

Presenting his faith to the Emperor Charles V at Augsburg in 1530, Zwingli affirmed a presence — but to the mind of the believer, not on the tongue:

I believe that in the holy Eucharist, i.e., the supper of thanksgiving, the true body of Christ is present by the contemplation of faith.

What he denied, and denied vehemently, was a bodily, oral eating of the flesh — the charge he levelled at Rome and at Luther alike:

But that the body of Christ in essence and really, i.e., the natural body itself, is either present in the supper or masticated with our mouth and teeth, as the Papists or some who look back to the fleshpots of Egypt assert, we not only deny, but constantly maintain to be an error, contrary to the Word of God.

The elements, then, are not the reality but its symbols: "those things are called the body and blood of Christ which are the symbols of the true body."3

"The flesh profiteth nothing" (John 6:63)

Zwingli made the qualifier of the Bread of Life discourse the linchpin of his case: what avails is a spiritual, not a carnal, eating.

"The flesh profiteth nothing" [John 6:63], namely, eaten naturally, but eaten spiritually it profits much, for it gives life.

— Account of the Faith (1530).4 Whether the verse actually carries this weight is weighed on its own page — does "the flesh profiteth nothing" refute the realist reading? — where the near-unanimous patristic reading tells against Zwingli's use of it.

Notes

  • The memorialist fountainhead. Zwingli is the source of the symbolic/memorialist strand that runs through much of the later Reformed and Baptist tradition. His quarrel is not merely with Rome's transubstantiation but with any real presence in the elements — the first witness on this timeline to answer the doctrine's central question ("is the presence real?") in the negative.
  • Marburg (1529). Philip of Hesse convened the colloquy to unite the Protestant camps against Catholic pressure. Zwingli, Luther, Melanchthon, Oecolampadius, and Bucer produced the Marburg Articles and agreed on fourteen of the fifteen — deadlocking only on whether Christ's body is bodily present in the bread. Zwingli pressed John 6:63 and the ascension/one-place argument; Luther chalked "This is my body" on the table and would not move. The split hardened into the Lutheran/Reformed divide.
  • Not Calvin's presence. For Zwingli the sign and the thing signified stay distinct: the bread is a symbol that prompts the believing mind, and the "eating" is the congregation's remembrance and thanksgiving. Calvin would go further — the signs are efficacious instruments through which the Spirit truly feeds the believer on the body of Christ. The line separating them is sign-versus-instrument, contemplation-versus-real-feeding. (Zwingli's later statements — the 1530 Account and the 1531 Exposition — lean harder on "spiritual presence to faith," which some read as narrowing the gap, but the core difference holds.)
  • Source provenance. His fullest vernacular defence, On the Lord's Supper (1526), is available in English in G. W. Bromiley, ed., Zwingli and Bullinger (Library of Christian Classics 24, 1953), but is not freely imaged online; the verbatim quotations above are drawn from the 1524 letter and the 1530 Account of the Faith, which are.
