The Fourth Lateran Council on the Real Presence of Christ in the Eucharist

Dates: 1215 (the Twelfth Ecumenical Council, convoked by Innocent III) · Wikipedia
Summary: In the opening confession of faith, Firmiter credimus (Canon 1), the Fourth Lateran Council gave the verb transsubstantiare its first dogmatic, conciliar use: Christ's body and blood are "truly contained in the sacrament of the altar under the forms of bread and wine," the bread "being changed by divine power into the body, and the wine into the blood." In the same sentence it reserved the confecting of the sacrament to "the priest who has been duly ordained" — binding the Real Presence and the ministerial priesthood together in a single dogmatic clause. It stands between the eleventh-century Berengarian oaths and the Council of Trent as the point where the medieval West made the word official.

Claims & evidence

The bread is "changed by divine power" into Christ's body — the first conciliar use of the term

Answering the Cathar and Waldensian challenges, the council's profession of faith affirms the one Church "in which the priest and the sacrifice is Jesus Christ himself," and then states the eucharistic conversion with the new technical verb:

whose body and blood are truly contained in the sacrament of the altar under the forms of bread and wine; the bread being changed (transsubstantiatio) by divine power into the body, and the wine into the blood, so that to realize the mystery of unity we may receive of Him what He has received of us. And this sacrament no one can effect except the priest who has been duly ordained in accordance with the keys of the Church, which Jesus Christ Himself gave to the Apostles and their successors.

— Fourth Lateran Council (1215), Canon 1 (Firmiter credimus), Fordham Medieval Sourcebook.1 This is the first appearance of the verb in a dogmatic conciliar decree. The noun transubstantiatio had entered theological Latin only a little over a century earlier — first attested c. 1079 and credited to Hildebert of Lavardin — and had been used in a letter of Innocent III in 1202 before the pope brought it to the council he convoked. Lateran IV does not yet supply the Aristotelian substance/accidents analysis; that account of the mode would come with Thomas Aquinas two generations later.2

Notes

  • The word, made dogma. The council took a term the schools had been coining and using loosely and fixed it as the Church's official name for the eucharistic change. The argument page traces the sequence: the Carolingian dispute (Paschasius vs. Ratramnus) opens the question; the Berengarian oaths of 1059 and 1079 force the language of "substantial conversion"; Lateran IV (1215) dogmatizes the verb; Aquinas gives it the full Aristotelian exposition; and Trent (1551) defines it anew against the Reformers, adding that the term is the Church's own "apt and proper" coinage for an ancient conviction.
  • Real Presence and ministerial priesthood in one clause. The decree's own next words — "no one can effect [this sacrament] except the priest who has been duly ordained" — make Lateran IV a witness on the ministerial priesthood as well: the reservation of the confecting to the ordained is stated in the same breath as the conversion of the elements.
  • What Lateran IV settled and what it left open. The council defined that the bread and wine are changed into the body and blood by divine power, and named the change transsubstantiatio; it did not define how — the metaphysics of substance persisting-or-not and accidents remaining without a subject is Aquinas's contribution, weighed on the argument page. This is why the "medieval-innovation" thesis is contested: the philosophical machinery is demonstrably medieval, while the realist conviction it formalizes is patristic.
