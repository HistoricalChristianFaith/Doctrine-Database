Cyprian of Carthage on the Real Presence

Dates: c. 253 · Wikipedia
Summary: In his letter to Caecilius (Epistle 62; numbered 63 in some editions), written against the "Aquarians" who used water alone in the cup, Cyprian argues that the mingled cup of wine and water is the blood of Christ, so that "the blood of Christ is not offered if there be no wine in the cup." The whole argument presupposes a real identity of the elements with the body and blood, and of the eucharistic action with Christ's own sacrifice, which the priest re-presents. He is the first Latin witness on this timeline.

Claims & evidence

That which He called His blood was wine — so "the blood of Christ is not offered if there be no wine in the cup" (Epistle 62.9)

For, taking the cup on the eve of His passion, He blessed it, and gave it to His disciples, saying, "Drink ye all of this; for this is my blood of the New Testament, which shall be shed for many, for the remission of sins." … In which portion we find that the cup which the Lord offered was mixed, and that that was wine which He called His blood. Whence it appears that the blood of Christ is not offered if there be no wine in the cup, nor the Lord's sacrifice celebrated with a legitimate consecration unless our oblation and sacrifice respond to His passion.

— Epistle 62 (to Caecilius), On the Sacrament of the Cup of the Lord, §9, source (ANF vol. 5; cf. New Advent). The letter answers bishops who offered water only in the cup (whether from ascetic scruple or fear of the wine's smell betraying Christians under persecution). Cyprian's whole rebuttal turns on identity: because the cup is Christ's blood, and Christ's blood was shown forth in wine, a waterless cup fails to be his blood at all. Earlier in the letter he states it flatly: "the blood of Christ is assuredly not water, but wine; neither can His blood by which we are redeemed and quickened appear to be in the cup, when in the cup there is no wine whereby the blood of Christ is shown forth" (§2).

The priest offers a "true and full sacrifice" in imitation of Christ's own offering (Epistle 62.14, 17)

For if Jesus Christ, our Lord and God, is Himself the chief priest of God the Father, and has first offered Himself a sacrifice to the Father, and has commanded this to be done in commemoration of Himself, certainly that priest truly discharges the office of Christ, who imitates that which Christ did; and he then offers a true and full sacrifice in the Church to God the Father, when he proceeds to offer it according to what he sees Christ Himself to have offered.

— Epistle 62.14, source. For Cyprian the Eucharist is not merely a meal of remembrance but a sacrifice — "the Lord's passion is the sacrifice which we offer" (§17) — that the ordained priest re-presents by doing what Christ did. The bread, too, he treats realistically: "the body of the Lord cannot be flour alone or water alone, unless both should be united and joined together and compacted in the mass of one bread" (§13). The sacrificial and priestly dimension is developed further on the ministerial priesthood timeline; adduced here, it shows that Cyprian's realism about the elements is inseparable from his realism about the eucharistic action.

Notes

  • Cyprian layers a typological reading over the plain identity — Melchizedek's bread and wine (Gen 14), the wine of Prov 9, the "blood of the grape" of Gen 49 all prefigure the cup — but the types serve the literal point, not the reverse: they prove that it must be wine precisely because the wine is the blood. His symbolism (water in the cup = the people united to Christ) sits atop, and does not dissolve, the realism.
  • The letter is a rare case where a realist doctrine is argued from a disciplinary dispute rather than asserted devotionally: the practical question "wine or water?" is settled only because Cyprian and his readers already agree the cup truly is Christ's blood. That makes it strong evidence for the shared belief of the mid-third-century African church.
  • On the numbering: the letter to Caecilius is Epistle 62 in the ANF (Oxford) series followed here, and Epistle 63 in the later Migne/CSEL numbering used by much modern scholarship — the same letter under two conventions.
