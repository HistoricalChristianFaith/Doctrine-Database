Augustine of Hippo on astrology

Dates: c. 397–417 (the anti-astrology material spans the Confessions c. 397–401, On Christian Doctrine II c. 397, and City of God V c. 415–417); Augustine 354–430 · Wikipedia
Summary: Augustine, who in his twenties had consulted the astrologers (the mathematici) himself, became their most thorough patristic critic. Across three works he assembles the whole arsenal against genethlialogy: the empirical disproof from twins and from two children — a free man's son and a slave — born at the very same instant to opposite fortunes; the scriptural test case of Esau and Jacob; the logician's turning of the astrologers' own potter's-wheel dodge against them; and the theological verdict that astral fatalism destroys free will, voids reward and punishment, shifts the blame for sin onto the Creator, and rests on "a baleful fellowship between men and devils."

Astrology is a demonic pact, not a science (On Christian Doctrine II.21–23)

In the handbook on reading Scripture Augustine sorts the superstitions the Christian must renounce, and puts the horoscope-casters — the genethliaci, "now commonly called mathematici" — among them. His point is precise: even granting that the astrologer sometimes fixes the true position of the stars, the leap from the chart to a prediction of a life is fraud, and it enslaves the credulous:

Nor can we exclude from this kind of superstition those who were called genethliaci, on account of their attention to birthdays, but are now commonly called mathematici. For these, too, although they may seek with pains for the true position of the stars at the time of our birth, and may sometimes even find it out, yet in so far as they attempt thence to predict our actions, or the consequences of our actions, grievously err, and sell inexperienced men into a miserable bondage.

— On Christian Doctrine II.21, newadvent.org. He closes the section with the verdict that all such arts belong to the same class — the class of tacit contracts with demons:

All arts of this sort, therefore, are either nullities, or are part of a guilty superstition, springing out of a baleful fellowship between men and devils, and are to be utterly repudiated and avoided by the Christian as the covenants of a false and treacherous friendship.

— On Christian Doctrine II.23. The occasional true prediction is no evidence for the art: it is a deception "the false angels" are permitted to work on those who "lust after evil things."1

Esau and Jacob: one constellation, two lives (On Christian Doctrine II.22)

Augustine's favourite disproof is Scripture's own set of twins. Because Jacob followed Esau out of the womb gripping his heel, no astrologer could assign the pair anything but a single constellation — yet their lives diverged utterly:

As, for example, we are told that Esau and Jacob were born twins, and in such close succession, that Jacob, who was born last, was found to have laid hold with his hand upon the heel of his brother, who preceded him. Now, assuredly, the day and hour of the birth of these two could not be marked in any way that would not give both the same constellation. But what a difference there was between the characters, the actions, the labors, and the fortunes of these two…

— On Christian Doctrine II.22. The same case anchors the fuller treatment in City of God V.4, where Augustine notes that the two differed so sharply "that the very contrast between them produced even a mutual hostile antipathy."2

Fatalism shifts the blame for sin onto God (Confessions IV.3)

The Confessions tell the story from the inside: the young Augustine consulted the astrologers precisely because they "used no sacrifices, and invoked the aid of no spirit," yet he came to see that their determinism is morally intolerable — it makes the Creator the author of every sin:

All of which salutary advice they endeavour to destroy when they say, The cause of your sin is inevitably determined in heaven; and, This did Venus, or Saturn, or Mars; in order that man, forsooth, flesh and blood, and proud corruption, may be blameless, while the Creator and Ordainer of heaven and stars is to bear the blame.

— Confessions IV.3, newadvent.org. The same chapter records the old proconsul Vindicianus — a physician who had studied astrology "with a view to gaining his living" and abandoned it "for no other reason than that he discovered it to be utterly false" — warning Augustine "in a kind and fatherly manner" to throw the books away. Augustine's friend Nebridius scoffed at the whole art; but he clung to it still, wanting "some certain proof."3

Firminius and the slave: born at the same instant, opposite fortunes (Confessions VII.6)

The certain proof came from a friend, Firminius, who told him of two children delivered "at the same moment" — the one a rich man's son, the other a household slave — so that their charts were, "even to the minutest point," identical:

And yet Firminius, born in a high estate in his parents' house, ran his course through the prosperous paths of this world, was increased in wealth, and elevated to honours; whereas that slave — the yoke of his condition being unrelaxed — continued to serve his masters, as Firminius, who knew him, informed me.

— Confessions VII.6, newadvent.org. "Upon hearing and believing these things," he writes, "all that resistance of mine melted away." Whatever an astrologer truly foretold from a chart, he concluded, was got "not by art, but by chance" — and he generalized the point at once to Esau and Jacob.4

The potter's wheel turned against Nigidius (City of God V.3)

The astrologers had an answer to the twins problem, made famous by Nigidius Figulus: the celestial sphere spins so fast that even the tiny interval between two births spans a great arc of heaven — as two ink-strokes on a whirling potter's wheel, seemingly struck on one spot, are found far apart when the wheel stops. Augustine grants the demonstration and turns it on its author:

Thus, said he, considering the great rapidity with which the celestial sphere revolves, even though twins were born with as short an interval between their births as there was between the strokes which I gave this wheel, that brief interval of time is equivalent to a very great distance in the celestial sphere… This argument is more fragile than the vessels which are fashioned by the rotation of that wheel.

— City of God V.3, newadvent.org. The dodge is self-defeating: if the decisive interval is too small for any observer to catch, then the astrologer, who cannot mark it either, has forfeited his own art — the wheel-argument only serves "to whirl round men who have hearts of clay" (V.4).5

Boy-and-girl twins: one conception, one horoscope, opposite lives (City of God V.6)

The sharpest form of the disproof is twins of different sexes, conceived "at the same moment… under the same position of the stars," who differ in the most bodily thing of all and in their whole course of life:

I know two of different sexes who are twins… he is married; she is a sacred virgin: he has begotten a numerous offspring; she has never even married.

— City of God V.6. From which "it does not follow that the wills of men are to be made subject to the position of the stars." Man alone in the world, Augustine insists (V.7), God "has bestowed free wills"; and the astrologers' occasional hits are to be "attributed to the occult inspiration of spirits not of the best kind," not to any real art.6

What Augustine does not deny: a licit sense of "fate" (City of God V.8–10)

Augustine is careful to save what is true in the vocabulary of "fate." If by fatum one means not the stars but "the connection of causes which depends on the will of God" — the whole ordered chain that God foreknows and governs — then the word can be used, so long as one grants "that the wills of all are not from Him" as compelled effects. His quarrel is not with providence or foreknowledge (which he defends at length), but with the specific claim that the position of the stars causes what we do. Divine foreknowledge and human free will stand together; astral necessity is the thing to be denied.7

Notes

  • Augustine gathers arguments already current in the Greek East — the twins disproof is as old as Carneades and Cicero, and Basil of Caesarea had pressed the free-will and divine-justice objections a generation earlier — but he welds them into the fullest single dossier in antiquity. See the cross-cutting twins argument for how the disproof holds up.
  • His autobiographical route — devotee, then doubter, then critic — gives the polemic its force: he is refuting an art he had practised, and the decisive blow (Firminius's story) is empirical, not merely theological.
  • On the Magi and the star of Bethlehem — the standing embarrassment for any Christian critic of astrology — Augustine holds, with the tradition, that the star was a sign, not a nativity; see the star-of-Bethlehem argument.
