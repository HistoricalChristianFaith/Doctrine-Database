John Chrysostom on astrology

Dates: c. 390 (the sixth of the Homilies on the Gospel of Matthew, preached at Antioch, on Matthew 2:1–2, the coming of the Magi); Chrysostom c. 349–407 · Wikipedia
Summary: Chrysostom's sixth homily on Matthew is the fullest patristic handling of the puzzle the Magi set for the anti-astrology consensus. He meets it on three fronts. First, the self-refutation: if Christ were born under astrology's law, how did He come to "put down astrology, and take away fate"? Second, and at greatest length, that the Bethlehem star was "not of the common sort, or rather not a star at all," but "some invisible power" — proved "from its very course," for it moved north to south (not east to west), shone in mid-day while the sun blazed, and started, stopped, hid, and reappeared with the Magi, coming at last to stand over the very head of the Child: conduct "not… of a star, but of some power highly endued with reason." Third, that the Magi's procedure was not astrology at all — genethlialogy reasons from a birth-hour to a fate, but the Magi reasoned from a star to seek a newborn king, running the logic the other way.

Claims & evidence

The self-refutation: Christ came to put down astrology and take away fate

Chrysostom's opening move is the argument from purpose. Matthew's own narrative has the Magi led by a heavenly sign; the astrologer seizes on this as a warrant. But the effect of Christ's coming was the ruin of the art, not its endorsement — so the sign cannot have been an act of astrology submitting the Lord to the stars.

How then, if He had His birth according to that law, did He put down astrology, and take away fate, and stop the mouths of demons, and cast out error, and overthrow all such sorcery?

— Homilies on the Gospel of Matthew VI.1, newadvent.org. The reasoning is the standing patristic reply to the Magi's star: the star that led astrologers to Christ is the star that broke astrology. It places Chrysostom alongside Ignatius, Tertullian, and Gregory the Great in the reading weighed on the star-of-Bethlehem argument.1

Not of the common sort, or rather not a star at all

The heart of the homily is a demonstration that the object the astrologer wants to claim was never a star. Chrysostom argues it "from its very course" — the star's behaviour breaks every rule real stars keep, and so betrays a rational power wearing a star's appearance.

Thus, that this star was not of the common sort, or rather not a star at all, as it seems at least to me, but some invisible power transformed into this appearance, is in the first place evident from its very course.

— Homilies on the Gospel of Matthew VI.2, newadvent.org. What follows is the catalogue of anomalies that proves the point.2

It moved north to south, not east to west

For there is not, there is not any star that moves by this way, but whether it be the sun you mention, or the moon, or all the other stars, we see them going from east to west; but this was wafted from north to south; for so is Palestine situated with respect to Persia.

— Homilies on the Gospel of Matthew VI.2, newadvent.org.3

It shone in mid-day, while the sun was up

For it appears not in the night, but in mid-day, while the sun is shining; and this is not within the power of a star… But this by the excess of its own splendour overcame even the beams of the sun.

— Homilies on the Gospel of Matthew VI.2, newadvent.org.4

It hid and reappeared, and started and stopped with the travellers

The star does not merely wander oddly; it keeps pace with a human errand, showing itself and vanishing, halting and resuming, exactly as the Magi's journey required — and finally leaves the height of heaven altogether to hang over one roof.

…when they were to move, it moved; when to stand, it stood, dispensing all as need required.

…all which is not like the motion of a star, but of some power highly endued with reason.

— Homilies on the Gospel of Matthew VI.2, newadvent.org. And to point out the Child it could not act as a star acts, but had to descend: "unless it left that height and came down, and stood over the very head of the young child" — which is what the evangelist means: "Lo, the star went before them, till it came and stood over where the young Child was."5

The Magi's reasoning ran backwards, so it was not astrology at all

Chrysostom's last stroke is to deny that the Magi practised astrology in the first place. Genethlialogy predicts a fate from the hour of a birth already known; the Magi did the reverse — they saw a sign and inferred that a king had been born, going out to find and worship the newborn. That is not the astrologer's business.

For surely this is not the business of astrology, to know from the stars who are born, but from the hour when men are born to predict what shall befall them.

— Homilies on the Gospel of Matthew VI.3, newadvent.org. The point is subtle and often missed: even granting the Magi read the sky, they did not cast a nativity — the specific art the Fathers condemn — so the Gospel gives astrology nothing it can use.6

Notes

  • Chrysostom's homily is the most detailed of the patristic answers to the Magi's star, and the one that leans hardest on the "no star at all" reading: where Ignatius simply declares the star broke every magic and Gregory the Great turns on Matthew's word order, Chrysostom builds an empirical case from the star's impossible motions. The three readings are complementary, and all three feed the star-of-Bethlehem argument.
  • The "ran the logic backwards" point is his own distinctive contribution: it concedes that a star heralded the birth while denying that this is genethlialogy, which reasons only from a birth-hour to a destiny. It anticipates the modern observation that a star-guided visit and a birth-horoscope are different operations.
  • Quotations follow the NPNF (first series, vol. 10) translation by George Prevost, revised by M. B. Riddle; the homily is numbered VI in that edition (on Matthew 2:1–2).
