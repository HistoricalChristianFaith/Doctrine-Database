Did the fallen angels teach astrology?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — that astrology was not discovered by men but taught to them by the rebel angels of Genesis 6 has an explicit Second-Temple pedigree (1 Enoch 8) and a conscious patristic uptake, so as a reading of the tradition it is sound; but as an account of astrology's actual origin it is a theological aetiology laid over the documented Babylonian human institution, and it competes with the same Fathers' other explanations of Fate · advanced by Tatian and Tertullian (and, on the Nephilim side, Clement and Commodian), rooted in 1 Enoch 8.

Related doctrines: the fallen angels of Genesis 6 — this is a genuine cross-doctrine claim; see also, on the Nephilim, the Tertullian and Book of Enoch pages, where the same Watcher-instruction tradition is treated under the sons of God and the Nephilim.

The claim

And Azazel taught men to make swords, and knives, and shields, and breastplates… Semjaza taught enchantments, and root-cuttings, Armaros the resolving of enchantments, Baraqijal (taught) astrology, Kokabel the constellations, Ezeqeel the knowledge of the clouds, Araqiel the signs of the earth, Shamsiel the signs of the sun, and Sariel the course of the moon.

— 1 Enoch 8:1, 3 (R. H. Charles trans.), ccel.org. The assertion: astral divination is not a human discovery but a revealed art — one of the "forbidden things" the two hundred Watchers who descended on Mount Hermon (1 Enoch 6–7) taught the women they took as wives. In the Enochic list the star-lore is parcelled out by name: Baraqijal teaches astrology, Kokabel ("star of God") the constellations, Sariel the course of the moon — the astrologer's raw materials handed down from the rebel angels rather than read off the sky by human ingenuity.1

Who advances it

The reading is a strand of the same Watcher tradition that carries the Nephilim doctrine, and it surfaces in the anti-astrology Fathers as an aetiology of the art they are condemning:

  • The demons "introduced Fate" (Tatian, Address to the Greeks 8–9, c. 165): the fallen powers, "having shown [men] a plan of the position of the stars, like dice-players… introduced Fate, a flagrant injustice," and "the delineation of the zodiacal circle is the work of gods" — the machinery of astrology traced to rebel spirits, not human observation.
  • "The deserters from God… were the discoverers of this curious art" (Tertullian, On Idolatry 9, c. 203): astrology is a species of condemned magic whose origin lies with "those angels, the deserters from God, the lovers of women" — a direct echo of the Watcher legend, which Tertullian elsewhere spells out (On the Apparel of Women I.2): the angels "who rushed from heaven on the daughters of men" had "traced out every curious art, even to the interpretation of the stars."
  • The angels "told to the women the secrets" (Clement of Alexandria, c. 200) and the giants "taught the arts" (Commodian, c. 250): the Nephilim witnesses keep the same origin-story of the forbidden sciences, of which astral divination is one.

So the patristic claim is not free invention: it is a conscious appropriation of 1 Enoch 8, the Second-Temple text that already lists astrology among the Watchers' teachings.2

The evidence for

As a reading of the tradition the claim is strong. The textual pedigree is explicit and old: 1 Enoch 8 (3rd–2nd c. BC) names astrology, the constellations, and the moon's course among the arts the Watchers reveal — the wiki does not have to reconstruct the connection, the source states it. The patristic uptake is conscious, not accidental: Tertullian's "deserters from God, the lovers of women" is unmistakably the Genesis 6 / Enochic angels, and Tatian independently roots the zodiac and Fate in the rebel powers. The reading also does real explanatory work within the anti-astrology case: if the art was taught by rebels to enslave, then its determinism is not a neutral science mis-drawn but a bondage to be renounced — which is exactly how Tatian frames the Christian's liberation ("we are superior to Fate… and we reject its lawgivers") and how Tertullian ties the astrologer's conversion to baptism ("You know nothing, astrologer, if you know not that you should be a Christian"). And on this wiki's terms the supernatural is not excluded a priori: that a rebel intelligence could have disclosed such lore is not, by itself, a defeater.3

Adversarial assessment

What holds. Two things stand firmly. First, the descriptive claim — that the Second-Temple and early patristic tradition attributed astrology to the fallen angels — is simply true, and well-attested (1 Enoch 8; Tatian; Tertullian; Clement; Commodian). Second, the theological point the aetiology serves — that astral determinism is a spiritual servitude, not a value-neutral inquiry — is coherent with the wider polemic and needs no naturalistic apology.

What is overstated or underdetermined. The step from "the tradition said so" to "this is how astrology actually arose" outruns the evidence. The whole weight rests on a single legendary stream: 1 Enoch 8 is an apocalyptic aetiology, not a historical record, and every patristic voice here is drawing on it rather than on any independent line of evidence. Against that stands the documented history of astrology as a human Babylonian institution — celestial omen-lists (Enūma Anu Enlil) and, later, the horoscopic astrology the Fathers actually attack — developed by generations of Chaldean scribes, which the Fathers themselves elsewhere concede when they call it a "Chaldean" science and expose its arithmetic (so Hippolytus, laying bare the horoscope precisely to discredit it). The Enochic aetiology and the Babylonian record are not flatly contradictory — the legend can be read as naming the ultimate source behind a knowledge that then spread by human hands — but that reconciliation is a harmonization the texts do not perform, and it leaves the historical claim resting on the myth alone. The aetiology also competes with the Fathers' own alternative explanations, which do not need it: Tatian's demons "introduce" Fate as a deception rather than transmit a syllabus; Origen grounds the whole error in men mistaking the stars' God-given signs for causes (Genesis 1:14), a purely epistemic account with no Watcher-pedagogy at all; and Basil, Gregory of Nyssa, and Augustine refute astrology on logical, empirical, and moral grounds without invoking the fallen angels once. The "taught by rebel angels" origin is thus one option inside the anti-astrology consensus, not its backbone.4

Assessment

Contested — strong by one measure, weak by another. As a description of the tradition, and as theology, the claim is sound: 1 Enoch 8 explicitly lists astrology among the Watchers' teachings, and Tatian and Tertullian consciously invoke that pedigree to cast astral fate as a bondage the Christian renounces. But as an account of astrology's actual origin it is a theological aetiology resting on a single legendary stream, laid over the documented Babylonian human institution and standing apart from the logical, empirical, and moral arguments on which the same Fathers principally rest their case. Trust the tradition-history (the Fathers really did root the art in the fallen angels, echoing 1 Enoch); treat the historical claim that rebel angels are the origin of astrology as a patristic aetiology — not excluded by this wiki's openness to the supernatural, but not established by it either (possibility is not proof).
