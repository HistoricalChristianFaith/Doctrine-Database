Clement of Alexandria on astronomy and astrology

Dates: c. 198–203 (the Stromata / Stromateis); Clement fl. c. 150–215 · Wikipedia
Summary: Alexandria's most accommodating voice draws the distinction the later tradition would keep sharp. For Clement, the study of the heavens is not a rival to faith but a rung on the ladder to God: the true "Gnostic" takes from every branch of learning — music, arithmetic, geometry, astronomy — whatever leads to truth, and by astronomy in particular the mind is "elevated along with heaven" and rises, as Abraham did, "to the knowledge of Him who created" the stars. What Clement praises is the observation of the heavenly courses, not the casting of nativities: astrology proper he lists among the human arts the Egyptians and Chaldeans "introduced," and the Chaldean claim to read destiny from the seven planets he reports at arm's length as a Gentile doctrine, not a Christian one. He is thus the headwater of the careful line — kept by Origen, Basil, Augustine, and finally Isidore's textbook heading — between legitimate astronomy and the superstition of the horoscope.

Astronomy "lifts the mind to God" (Stromata VI.11)

In the sixth book of the Stromata, describing how the mature Christian ("the Gnostic") avails himself of all human learning as a preparation for knowledge, Clement runs through the sciences of the quadrivium and assigns each its use. Astronomy's use is the highest: it draws the mind up from the earth toward the divine order of the heavens, and from that contemplation to the heavens' Maker — the very ascent he credits to Abraham the star-gazer:

And by astronomy, again, raised from the earth in his mind, he is elevated along with heaven, and will revolve with its revolution; studying ever divine things, and their harmony with each other; from which Abraham starting, ascended to the knowledge of Him who created them.

— Stromata VI.11, newadvent.org (the chapter is numbered X in the ANF/Ante-Nicene edition, "The Gnostic Avails Himself of the Help of All Human Knowledge"). The frame is explicit: "to him knowledge is the principal thing… he applies to the subjects that are a training for knowledge, taking from each branch of study its contribution to the truth." Astronomy is one of those contributions — a good that lifts the mind toward its Author, not a snare to be shunned.1

Astrology among the arts men "introduced" (Stromata I.16)

Clement does not trace astrology to fallen angels, as Tatian and Tertullian would. In his catalogue of the useful arts the "barbarians" invented and passed to the Greeks, he lists astrology plainly among human discoveries — the work of the Egyptians and the Chaldeans, set beside geometry, augury, and the alphabet:

And barbarians were inventors not only of philosophy, but almost of every art. The Egyptians were the first to introduce astrology among men. Similarly also the Chaldeans.

— Stromata I.16, newadvent.org. Clement's interest here is the antiquity and inventiveness of the "barbarian" nations, not a verdict on the art; but the framing matters for the timeline. Where the demonological strand makes astrology a thing taught to men by the rebel angels, Clement treats it as a human institution the star-watching peoples of the East devised — a difference weighed on the fallen-angels argument page.2

The Chaldeans' astral "sympathy" — reported, not endorsed (Stromata VI.16)

When Clement does describe the astrologers' distinctive doctrine — that the courses of the seven planets govern human life — he sets it out as a Gentile teaching, in the mouths of "the Chaldeans," not as his own:

The mathematicians also say that the planets, which perform their course around the earth, are seven; by which the Chaldeans think that all which concerns mortal life is effected through sympathy, in consequence of which they also undertake to tell things respecting the future.

— Stromata VI.16, newadvent.org. The they think and they undertake keep the claim at arm's length: Clement is recording what the nativity-casters hold, not affirming it. His own use of the number seven in the same passage is symbolic and scriptural, not predictive — the observation of the heavens is his; the reading of destiny from them is theirs.3

Notes

  • Astronomy vs. astrology. Clement is the earliest clear statement of the distinction that becomes the tradition's settled position: the study of the heavenly courses is praised, while the casting of nativities is refused. That line runs through Origen (the stars "signify but do not cause"), Basil and Augustine, and is fixed in a textbook heading by Isidore of Seville ("the difference between astronomy and astrology"). The modern "church versus science" charge — that the Fathers' war on astrology was a war on astronomy too — is weighed, and found wanting, on the astronomy argument page, where Clement is a lead witness.
  • The accommodating temper. Clement's readiness to "spoil the Egyptians" — to take what is true in Greek and barbarian learning as a stepping-stone to Christ — makes him gentler toward star-science than the polemicists who follow. He nowhere mounts Basil's sustained attack on genethlialogy; his contribution is to fence off what is legitimate, so that the later refutations fall on the horoscope alone and not on the heavens.
  • Abraham the astronomer. The Abraham-ascending-from-the-stars motif is a favourite of Clement's (he repeats it for arithmetic in the next chapter) and a commonplace of Hellenistic-Jewish tradition. It cuts against astral fatalism from the other side: the patriarch looked up at the same heavens the Chaldeans read for destiny, and passed through them to the God who made them — contemplation, not divination.
