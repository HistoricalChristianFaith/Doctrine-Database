Hippolytus of Rome on astrology

Dates: c. 225 (the Refutation of All Heresies, Book IV); Hippolytus c. 170–235 · Wikipedia
Summary: Hippolytus opens his Refutation of All Heresies not with a heresy but with astrology — he devotes the beginning of Book IV to a patient, technical exposition of the Chaldeans' method, the fixing of the horoscope and the arithmetic of the zodiac, precisely in order to discredit it. His aim is double: to show the "futile art" collapses on its own terms, and to expose the heretics who had simply borrowed the astrologers' machinery for their own systems. His attack is unusually concrete: he describes the assistant who strikes a metal rim to signal the instant of birth to the star-watcher on the roof, then shows that the birth is too indefinite, and the signal too slow, for the horoscope ever to be fixed — and that once the horoscope falls, "the entire system of the Chaldeans vanishes along with" it.

The horoscope is the foundation of the whole art (Refutation IV.3)

Astrology, Hippolytus explains, is built on a single load-bearing operation — catching the point of the zodiac rising on the eastern horizon at the moment of birth. Everything else is derived from it:

The originating principle, and, as it were, foundation, of the entire art, is fixing the horoscope.

— Refutation IV.3, newadvent.org. He lays out the machinery in order to turn it against itself: the whole scheme depends on this one measurement, so if the measurement cannot be made, nothing built on it stands.1

The metallic rim: the instant of birth cannot be caught (Refutation IV.4)

The most vivid passage describes the actual apparatus of the birth-chart. An attendant sits by the woman in labour and, at the moment of delivery, strikes a metal rim — a gong — to signal the astrologer stationed on a height, who then marks the zodiacal sign rising at that instant:

For when they allege that the person sitting beside the woman in travail at the time of parturition gives, by striking a metallic rim, a sign to the Chaldean, who from an elevated place is contemplating the stars, and he, looking towards heaven, marks down the rising zodiacal sign; in the first place, we shall prove to them, that when parturition happens indefinitely, as we have shown a little before, neither is it easy to signify this (birth) by striking the metallic rim.

— Refutation IV.4. The objection is twofold. The birth itself "happens indefinitely" — there is no knife-edge instant to signal. And even granting one, the sound takes time to travel: as with an axe-blow seen before it is heard, "a sufficiently long time after the descent of the axe, the sound of the stroke is heard," so the signal reaches the star-watcher only after an interval, and the sign he marks is no longer the sign that was rising. The horoscope is therefore never "infallible." This is the same practical objection Basil would press a century and a half later — the decisive interval is real but unobservable — here worked out through the physics of the signal itself.2

Remove the horoscope, and the system collapses (Refutation IV.3)

Hippolytus draws the structural conclusion. Because every other point of the chart — the meridian above, the setting-point at the western horizon, the anti-meridian below — is reckoned from the ascendant, the failure of the ascendant is the failure of everything:

Whence, if the horoscope be removed, it necessarily follows that neither any celestial object is recognisable in the meridian, or at the horizon, or in the point of the heavens opposite the meridian; but if these be not comprehended, the entire system of the Chaldeans vanishes along with (them).

— Refutation IV.3. Having shown that the horoscope cannot in fact be fixed, he has thereby (on his own argument) dissolved the art root and branch.3

Why expose the technique: the art deceives, and the heretics borrowed it (Refutation IV.2)

Hippolytus is explicit that this technical tour has a polemical purpose. Astrology is a "futile art" that trades on false hope:

…we shall not hesitate to furnish a brief refutation respecting these, establishing that the futile art is calculated both to deceive and blind the soul indulging in vain expectations…

— Refutation IV.2. But the deeper motive is the book's governing thesis: that the heresies are not fresh revelations but recycled pagan learning. By first laying the astrologers' apparatus bare, Hippolytus can later show that the heresiarchs "derived their system" from it — that the elaborate cosmologies of the Gnostics are the Chaldeans' machinery in disguise. The exposé of astrology thus does double duty: it refutes the star-casters and strips the heretics of their supposed originality.4

Notes

  • Hippolytus's line of attack is distinctive among the Fathers for being internal and mechanical: rather than pressing the moral consequences of fatalism (that it voids reward and punishment), as Basil and Augustine chiefly do, he dismantles the observational procedure of casting a horoscope and argues the art fails at its first step. The moral and free-will objections he largely leaves to others.
  • His purpose — showing that the heretics borrowed the astrologers' apparatus — means his interest is never in star-lore for its own sake; he is not condemning the study of the heavens but the divinatory use of it (see the summary timeline and the astronomy-versus-astrology argument).
  • The unfindable-instant objection feeds the cross-cutting twins argument: whatever interval is supposed to distinguish two horoscopes is too small for the astrologer himself to observe or signal.
