Methodius of Olympus on astrology

Dates: c. 290 (the Symposium, or Banquet of the Ten Virgins, Discourse VIII, spoken by Thecla); Methodius c. 260–311, bishop and martyr · Wikipedia
Summary: In the eighth discourse of his Symposium, Methodius puts an extended refutation of genethlialogy into the mouth of Thecla, its principal speaker. His distinctive line is historical: the zodiac and its figures are recent human contrivances, catalogued out of "men and beasts," so that men once lived, and their forefathers were born, before any of the signs supposed to govern nativity existed — "our ancestors lived without destiny." He then presses the standard moral reductio — if fate compelled the murderer's hand, the law that punishes murder would be at war with destiny, so either the law or fate must go — and concludes that "to do good or evil is in our own power, and not decided by the stars."

Claims & evidence

The constellations are a recent human invention, so no one was "born under a sign"

Methodius' opening move is chronological. The figures the astrologers cast nativities by — the Lion, the Crab, the Twins, and the rest — were, he insists, only "lately" gathered into constellations out of the shapes of men and beasts. So if fate is bound to the moment of birth, why did it not operate "from the very time when the race of man began to be," long before those signs were mapped?

why was not his generation and birth from the very time when the race of man began to be? And if it was, what is the need of those which had lately been placed among the stars, of the Lion, the Crab, the Twins, the Virgin, the Bull, the Balance, the Scorpion, the Ram, the Archer, the Fishes, the Goat, the Watercarrier, Perseus, Cassiopeia, Cepheus, Pegasus, Hydra, the Raven, the Cup, the Lyre, the Dragon, and others, from which you introduce, by your instructions, many to the knowledge of mathematics…

— Symposium VIII, newadvent.org. If the sky's nativity-signs are datable inventions, then generations of human beings were born and lived their lives before any of them existed, and cannot have had a "natal destiny" fixed by them.1

"Our ancestors lived without destiny"

The conclusion of the historical argument is stated flatly: the signs were placed among the stars "from men and beasts," and before that there was no fate to be born under.

And thus, according to them, there was no natal destiny before Europe or Phryxos, and the Dioscuroi, and the other signs of the Zodiac, which were placed among the constellations, from men and beasts. But our ancestors lived without destiny.

— Symposium VIII, newadvent.org. The astrologers' own mythology defeats them: the very stories by which the signs were named (Europa and the Bull, Phrixus and the Ram, the Dioscuri as the Twins) date the signs to particular events in human legend, and everything earlier — the whole first stretch of mankind — had no horoscope at all.2

If fate compels, law is meaningless

Methodius then presses the moral reductio that recurs across this timeline (Basil, Gregory of Nyssa, Augustine): if destiny, not the will, drives a man to murder, then the law that forbids and punishes murder is set directly against fate — and the two cannot both stand.

Either, then, there is destiny and there was no need of laws; or there are laws and they are not in accordance with destiny.

— Symposium VIII, newadvent.org. Since human societies plainly do make laws and hold men accountable under them, the horn to be abandoned is astral fate: conduct is not decreed by the stars.3

Good and evil are in our own power

The positive conclusion is the shared patristic one — that virtue and vice are the soul's own doing — grounded by Methodius in the two contrary "motions" a person feels within.

to do good or evil is in our own power, and not decided by the stars. For there are two motions in us, the lust of the flesh and that of the soul…

— Symposium VIII, newadvent.org. The inward struggle between flesh and soul is itself evidence of freedom: a being simply steered by the heavens would feel no such contest, and there would be nothing to reward or punish.4

Notes

  • The "recent invention" argument is Methodius' signature contribution, and it anticipates the historical/aetiological line later fathers develop — Isidore's care to date and distinguish the arts, and the general patristic insistence that the Chaldean art is a human institution, not a fixture of nature. It runs alongside, not through, the demonological account of astrology's origin weighed on the fallen-angels argument: for Methodius the signs are a late human catalogue, named from myth.
  • The moral reductio (fate versus law) and the "in our own power" conclusion are the common property of the anti-astrology tradition on this timeline — compare Basil (reward and punishment made pointless), Gregory of Nyssa (the diversity of national law), and Augustine on the blame astral fate shifts onto the Creator.
  • The discourse is spoken by Thecla, the crowned victor of Methodius' dialogue; the anti-astrology material sits within her larger praise of chastity and free choice. Chapter divisions vary slightly between printings of the ANF text; the passages quoted here fall in the astrology section of Discourse VIII.
