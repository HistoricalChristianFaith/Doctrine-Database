Origen on astrology

Dates: c. 231 (the discussion survives as Philocalia 23, excerpted from Book III of the Commentary on Genesis, on Genesis 1:14; anthologized c. 360s by Basil and Gregory of Nazianzus); Origen c. 184–253 · Wikipedia
Summary: Origen frames the most sophisticated patristic position, and he builds it on the very proof-text the astrologers claimed — God's word that the lights of heaven should "be for signs" (Genesis 1:14). His move is a single distinction, pressed to its consequences: the stars signify events but do not cause them. They are a book whose characters the angelic "Powers" (and, exceptionally, inspired men) can read, while the ordinary astrologer, mistaking a sign for its author, was "deceived" into supposing the stars "were the original causes of the events which the Word says the signs indicate." With that one line Origen keeps divine foreknowledge intact and leaves human free will untouched: that God (or an angel) can read the future off the sky no more compels a man than a written prophecy does. It is the hinge the later anti-astrology tradition leans on.

The stakes: astral necessity destroys free will (Philocalia 23.1–2)

Origen opens by naming what is really at issue. It is not the innocent among the nations who "err in the matter of fate" that troubles him most, but Christians "distracted at the thought that human affairs may be governed by necessity, and cannot possibly be otherwise than is ruled by the stars." If that were true, he argues, the entire moral and evangelical order collapses:

And a consequence of this opinion is the complete destruction of Free Will; and a further result is that praise and blame are unmeaning, and the distinction between acceptable conduct and conduct deserving of blame is lost. And if this be so, there is an end of that Divine judgment which we preach; an end also of God's threats of punishment awaiting sinners; an end, too, of a blessed future for those who have devoted themselves to the higher life.

— Philocalia 23.1. Worse, astral fatalism would make God the author of every sin: the men who "through the revolution of the whole heavens… abandon themselves to the most savage and inhuman practices, murders, and piratical outrages" would be guiltless, and the blame would fall on the Creator who "so ordered the universe." "The champions of these noble principles," Origen concludes, "may free their fellow-men from all guilt, but they make God the cause of all that is bad and blameworthy."1 This is the same free-will and divine-justice objection Basil and Augustine would press — but Origen alone must reconcile it with his equally firm claim that the heavens really do foreshow events.

Signs, not causes: the Powers read the book (Philocalia 23.6)

The reconciliation is the heart of the piece. Origen grants that the stars are genuinely "for signs" — their motions are so ordered "that observers may take and know the signs from the grouping of all those stars." But he immediately withdraws the reading from human hands and, more importantly, denies that a sign is a cause:

And when I say "observers," I do not mean men (for ability to really learn from the movements of the stars what will befall every individual soul… far transcends human capacity), but I refer to the Powers, which must for many reasons have this knowledge… But men being deceived by certain observations, or even by the teaching of Angels who have lost their proper rank… supposed that they from whom they thought they received the signs were the original causes of the events which the Word says the signs indicate.

— Philocalia 23.6. Two errors are diagnosed at once: the astrologer overreaches his capacity (the true readers are the angelic "Powers," and the fallen angels who taught men the art did so "to the injury of our race"), and, underneath that, he commits a category mistake — treating the indicator as the agent. Origen makes the logic explicit later: even where a horoscope's predictions come true, the practitioner "will say that they are right, not because the stars cause the events, but only because they indicate them."2

The heavens as one of God's books (Philocalia 23.16, 20)

The positive image Origen offers to replace astral causation is that of a text. A prophecy foretells without compelling; so does the sky. The future can be written in the stars exactly as it is written in the Prophets, and reading it there no more forces the outcome than reading it in a scroll:

…so neither do the signs, which God appointed to be indicative, interfere with our Free Will; but, like a volume of prophecy, the heavens as a whole, being as it were one of God's books, may contain the future.

— Philocalia 23.16. Answering finally why God should have made such signs at all, Origen says they are set for the blessed and the angelic "Powers who administer human affairs" to read — and gives the passage its most memorable phrase, the heavens as a book:

God created in the heavens beings who have been taught and shall be taught… to read the signs which God gives as if they were written and stamped on the face of the sky… for all of them have been from everlasting to everlasting engraved in the book of the heavens, a book worthy of God.

— Philocalia 23.20. The Magi, on this account, were not vindicated astrologers but rare men permitted to read a page of that book — a foreknowledge granted, not a fate imposed.3

Notes

  • Origen shares the pro-astrology proof-text — "let them be for signs" (Genesis 1:14) — and does not deny it. His answer is not to read the verse away but to read it strictly: signs, precisely, and signs are not causes. This is why his position is the most sophisticated in the patristic dossier: it concedes the strongest point of the astrologers' case (the heavens really do foreshow) and defeats the inference from it (therefore they compel) rather than denying the premise. See the summary timeline.
  • The distinction preserves what the flat rejections could not: divine foreknowledge and real prediction from the heavens, without astral necessity. It is the same reconciliation of foreknowledge with freedom that Origen presses against determinist readings of Pharaoh's hardening elsewhere in Philocalia 23 and 27 — God's knowing an act does not cause it.
  • Origen locates the human art's origin partly in "the teaching of Angels who have lost their proper rank," echoing the older tradition (Tatian, Tertullian) that the fallen angels taught the forbidden sky-craft — see the sons of God and the Nephilim. But for Origen the deeper fault is not the art's pedigree but its logic: mistaking a sign for a cause.
