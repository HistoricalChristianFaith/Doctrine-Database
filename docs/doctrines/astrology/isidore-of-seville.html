Isidore of Seville on astrology

Dates: c. 620 (the Etymologies, Book III, chapters 24–27, on astronomy and astrology; the work was compiled c. 615–630 and left unfinished at Isidore's death); Isidore c. 560–636, archbishop of Seville · Wikipedia
Summary: Isidore is the encyclopedist who fixes the distinction the earlier Fathers had drawn, and hands it to the Latin Middle Ages in a textbook heading. In the Etymologies he defines astronomy as "the law of the stars," a legitimate science of the heavens' courses; then, under the chapter title "The difference between astronomy and astrology," he cuts astrology in two — a "natural" part (the seasons, the reckoning of sun and moon) that is kept, and a "superstitious" part, "that which the mathematici follow, who prophesy by the stars… and endeavor to predict the nativities and characters of men," which is condemned. Only the nativity-casting is refused; the star-science survives.

Claims & evidence

Astronomy is "the law of the stars" — a legitimate science of the heavens

Isidore opens with astronomy proper, and treats it as an honourable discipline: the rational study of the heavenly bodies' motions and figures. There is nothing here to condemn — the observation of the courses of the sky is knowledge, not superstition.

Astronomy is the law of the stars, and it traces with inquiring reason the courses of the heavenly bodies, and their figures, and the regular movements of the stars with reference to one another and to the earth.

— Etymologies III.24 ("On the name of astronomy"), Brehaut 1912 translation. That astronomy is a good keeps Isidore in the line running from Clement and Origen through Basil and Augustine: the Church rejects astral fatalism, not the study of the heavens. It is the standing evidence against the modern charge weighed on the astronomy argument.1

The difference between astronomy and astrology: astrology "in part natural, in part superstitious"

The pivotal chapter is titled, precisely, "The difference between astronomy and astrology" — a heading that carries the patristic distinction into the medieval curriculum. Astronomy embraces the motions of the heavens; astrology Isidore splits in two, and the split is the whole point.

There is some difference between astronomy and astrology. For astronomy embraces the revolution of the heavens, the rise, setting, and motion of the heavenly bodies, and the origin of their names. Astrology, on the other hand, is in part natural, in part superstitious.

— Etymologies III.27, Brehaut 1912. By dividing the word itself — rather than condemning "astrology" wholesale — Isidore isolates exactly what the Fathers had always targeted (genethlialogy) from what they had always allowed (the reckoning of times and seasons).2

Natural astrology — the seasons and the courses of sun, moon, and stars — is kept

The "natural" part is nothing more than the practical astronomy of the calendar: watching the luminaries to know the seasons. Isidore leaves it standing.

It is natural astrology when it describes the courses of the sun and the moon and the stars, and the regular succession of the seasons.

— Etymologies III.27, Brehaut 1912. This is the ordinary, God-given use of the lights that Genesis 1:14 assigns them — "for signs, and for seasons" — read as reckoning, not as fate.3

Superstitious astrology — the mathematici who cast nativities — is condemned

Only the second part is rejected, and Isidore names it exactly: the horoscope-casters who parcel the zodiac out over the body and read a man's birth and character from the stars. This — not the study of the heavens — is the superstition.

Superstitious astrology is that which the mathematici follow, who prophesy by the stars, and who distribute the twelve signs of the heavens among the individual parts of the soul or body, and endeavor to predict the nativities and characters of men from the course of the stars.

— Etymologies III.27, Brehaut 1912. The mathematici are the same nativity-casters Augustine had renounced and Basil ridiculed; the "twelve signs distributed among the parts of the body" (melothesia) is the very machinery Hippolytus laid bare in order to discredit. Isidore keeps the line drawn where the tradition had always drawn it — sparing the star-science, condemning the genethlialogy.4

Who discovered it: Egyptians, Chaldeans — and Abraham

Isidore's chapter on the origins repeats the ancient tradition of the art's inventors, and preserves the Abraham motif that Clement had used to save astronomy: the patriarch as an early astronomer who read the Creator behind the stars.

The Egyptians were the first to discover astronomy. And the Chaldeans first taught astrology and the observance of nativity. Moreover, Josephus asserts that Abraham taught astrology to the Egyptians.

— Etymologies III.25 ("On its discoverers"), Brehaut 1912. That the Chaldeans "first taught… the observance of nativity" locates the birth-horoscope in a documented human institution — a lineage the tradition otherwise sometimes traced to the fallen angels (see the fallen-angels argument). The Abraham note comes from Josephus (Antiquities I.8) and echoes Clement's use of the patriarch as the man who "ascended… to the knowledge of Him who created" the stars.5

Notes

  • Isidore's contribution is not a fresh argument but a definition — and, because the Etymologies was the most-copied reference book of the early Middle Ages, the one that carried the patristic astronomy/astrology distinction into the schools. The chapter heading "The difference between astronomy and astrology" is itself the anti-astrology tradition crystallized into a curriculum entry.
  • His split of the word into a "natural" and a "superstitious" part is the neatest statement on the timeline of the line the Fathers drew from Clement onward: condemn the casting of nativities, keep the study of the heavens. It is the terminal witness for the astronomy argument.
  • Chapter and section numbering varies by edition; Brehaut (1912) numbers the astronomy material III.24–27, and the standard modern translation (Barney, Lewis, Beach, and Berghof, Cambridge, 2006) numbers it within Book III as well. The passages here are quoted from Brehaut, An Encyclopedist of the Dark Ages.
