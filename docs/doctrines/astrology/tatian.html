Tatian on astrology

Dates: c. 165–175 (the Address to the Greeks / Oratio ad Graecos); Tatian fl. c. 120–180 · Wikipedia
Summary: Tatian, the Assyrian pupil of Justin Martyr, gives the earliest sustained Christian account of where Fate came from. In chapters 8–9 of his Address to the Greeks he does not merely deny astral determinism — he traces it to the fallen angels turned demons, who "introduced Fate" by teaching men to read the positions of the stars, and who set the animal-figures of the zodiac in the sky to make their own tyranny look like a natural order. Against that bondage he sets the Christian's freedom: "we are superior to Fate… and… we reject its lawgivers." The passage is a headwater of the tradition — both of the demonological aetiology later pressed by Tertullian, and of the careful refusal of astral necessity that runs through Basil, Gregory of Nyssa, and Augustine.

The demons "introduced Fate" (Address 8)

Tatian's demonology (chapters 7–8) makes the rebel spiritual powers the authors of astral determinism. Having fallen, the demons taught men to read destiny from the sky — a game of chance dressed up as a science:

For, having shown them a plan of the position of the stars, like dice-players, they introduced Fate, a flagrant injustice. For the judge and the judged are made so by Fate; the murderers and the murdered, the wealthy and the needy, are the offspring of the same Fate; and every nativity is regarded as a theatrical entertainment…

— Address to the Greeks 8, newadvent.org. The moral scandal is exactly the one Basil and Augustine would later press: if Fate makes "the judge and the judged" alike, then guilt and desert dissolve, and the astrologer's "nativity" is a puppet-show. Tatian names the source plainly: "Such are the demons; these are they who laid down the doctrine of Fate."1

The zodiac is the demons' handiwork (Address 9)

In chapter 9 Tatian turns to the zodiac itself. The constellations — the beasts and figures traced on the sky — are not a map of nature's order but the demons' own fabrication, set up so that they might be worshipped as gods and their rule over human life seem written into the heavens:

The delineation of the zodiacal circle is the work of gods.

— Address to the Greeks 9. (Tatian speaks of "gods" here as the pagans do — the very beings he has just unmasked as fallen demons.) The point anticipates Basil's mockery of reading a man's character off the animal a sign is merely named after: the animal-figures in the sky are an artifact, not a cause.2

"We are superior to Fate" (Address 9)

The counter-claim is the Christian's release from that bondage. Where the pagan follows "wandering" stars and the demons who lawgive through them, the Christian knows the one unwandering Lord and is therefore free of Fate and its ordinances:

But we are superior to Fate, and instead of wandering demons, we have learned to know one Lord who wanders not; and, as we do not follow the guidance of Fate, we reject its lawgivers.

— Address to the Greeks 9. The word "wandering" (the planets are the "wanderers," planetai) turns the astrologers' own vocabulary against them: the heavenly bodies they consult are themselves erratic, while the God the Christian follows is not. To "reject its lawgivers" is to renounce both the demons and the astral Fate they legislate — a freedom that is, for Tatian, the very content of conversion.3

Notes

  • Fallen angels as the teachers of the art. Tatian's derivation of Fate from the demons is the earliest Christian statement of the aetiology that Tertullian would sharpen ("those angels, the deserters from God… were… the discoverers of this curious art") and that ultimately rests on 1 Enoch 8, where the Watchers teach astrology to men. That thesis is weighed on its own merits on the fallen-angels argument page, and the shared Watcher-instruction tradition ties this doctrine to the sons of God and the Nephilim.
  • The free-will core. Beneath the demonology lies the argument the whole tradition shares: astral Fate voids moral responsibility, since it makes "the judge and the judged" alike its offspring. That is the same objection Basil ("neither reward for justice, nor punishment for sin"), Gregory of Nyssa, and Augustine develop at length.
  • Astronomy vs. astrology. Tatian's language is unusually sweeping — he calls the very "delineation of the zodiacal circle" the demons' work — and is cited as the strongest patristic evidence that the war on astrology spilled over onto star-science as such. Even so, his target is astral Fate and the worship of the heavenly bodies, not the observation of their courses; the point is weighed on the astronomy argument page.
