Gregory of Nyssa on astrology

Dates: c. 380 (the dialogue Against Fate, Contra Fatum, composed after a debate at Constantinople); Gregory c. 335–395 · Wikipedia
Summary: In a short dialogue recording a debate he held at Constantinople with a pagan philosopher who defended astral fate, Gregory refutes genethlialogy on the familiar free-will ground that his brother Basil had laid down — but he adds a distinctive empirical argument all his own: whole cities of people, born at every different hour and under every different sign, are wiped out together "in a single instant" by an earthquake or a flood, a common fate that no scheme of individual horoscopes can account for. He mocks the animal-qualities read off the constellations as incoherent, and appeals (like Bardaisan) to the diversity of national customs, concluding that "destiny and fate seems determined by each person's free will."

Claims & evidence

The many who die in one instant refute the individual horoscope

The astrologer's claim, as Gregory frames it, is that "fate determines [a person's] life at the hour of birth by the formation of stars, [and] this order remains constant throughout life" — each nativity fixed by a unique configuration of the heavens. Against this Gregory turns a public catastrophe: when a city of Bithynia was destroyed by earthquake and fire, people of every age, rank, and condition — who by hypothesis were born at different hours and so cast under different stars — all perished at the same moment:

Many perished in a single instant: children, infants, those in middle and old age, the free and slaves, victors and vanquished, wealthy and poor, the strong and weak.

— Against Fate, archived English translation. If each of these lives had really been governed by its own separate birth-chart, no single event should have gathered them all into one death at one hour; the shared fate has no shared horoscope behind it. This mass-simultaneous-death argument is Gregory's own contribution to the tradition, not found in Basil's Hexaemeron.1

The animal-qualities of the signs are incoherent

Like Basil before him, Gregory finds it absurd to read a man's character off the beast a constellation is merely named after — the servility of a bull under the yoke, the profit of a ram's fleece:

You say that Taurus is servile because it [a bull] is under the yoke and that Aries [a ram] represents profit gained from wool.

— Against Fate, archived English translation. The reasoning collapses on inspection: the bull is yoked by force, not by nature, and the ram does not "willingly" yield its wool — so the qualities the astrologers project onto the signs do not even hold of the animals whose names the signs bear.2

The diversity of customs points to free will, not the stars

Gregory presses a further line that Bardaisan of Edessa had used before him: whole nations, spread under all the same stars as everyone else, keep customs peculiar to themselves — so it is law and choice, not the heavens, that govern conduct. Since a given outrage is practised by one nation alone though its people are born under every sign, the cause cannot lie in the stars:

Since the Persians alone are guilty of such outrageous behavior, it clearly follows that destiny and fate seems determined by each person's free will.

— Against Fate, archived English translation. The conclusion is the standard patristic one — virtue and vice are "in our own power" — but Gregory reaches it by the argument from the diversity of national law rather than by Basil's appeal to reward and punishment.3

Notes

  • The mass-simultaneous-death argument is a mirror image of the twins argument. Twins press the point from below (one horoscope, two opposite fates); Gregory presses it from above (many horoscopes, one identical fate). Both dissolve the supposed one-to-one link between the birth-chart and the life.
  • Gregory is the younger brother of Basil of Caesarea, whose sixth Hexaemeron homily is the Greek source for the free-will, animal-signs, and divine-justice arguments; Gregory develops them in dialogue form and adds the collective-catastrophe case. The diversity-of-customs argument he shares with Bardaisan, the earliest Syriac witness on this timeline.
  • The treatise is cast as the record of a live debate: at Constantinople Gregory disputed with a pagan philosopher who defended astral determinism, and wrote up the exchange afterward at a correspondent's request. The Greek is edited in J. A. McDonough, Gregorii Nysseni Opera III.2 (Brill, 1987); the English quoted here is an anonymous online translation.
