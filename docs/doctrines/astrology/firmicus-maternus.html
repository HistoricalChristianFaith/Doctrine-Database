Firmicus Maternus on astrology

Dates: fl. c. 334–350; the astrological Mathesis c. 334–337, the Christian De errore profanarum religionum c. 346–348 · Firmicus a Sicilian of the senatorial class (vir clarissimus) · Wikipedia
Summary: Firmicus Maternus is the tradition's most striking single counter-case — one man who wrote both the fullest surviving Latin handbook of astrology and, a decade or so later, a Christian tract urging the emperors to stamp paganism out. His Matheseos libri VIII (the "Mathesis," c. 334–337) is an eight-book defence and manual of genethlialogy that expressly answers the moral objections the Fathers pressed — and answers them not by denying Providence but by folding astral fate under a supreme God, exempting the emperor from the stars, and demanding that the astrologer himself be a man of blameless piety. Whether he was already a Christian when he wrote it is disputed; what is certain is that by De errore (c. 346–348) he writes as one "formed by the instruction of the sacred readings." He therefore embodies the whole doctrine's ambiguity at once: the same educated late-Roman Christian world that produced Basil's Hexaemeron also produced, and read, the Mathesis.

The Mathesis: a defence, not a rejection (c. 334–337)

Where the Fathers class astrology as demonic fraud, Firmicus presents it as a sacred science — divina scientia — whose practitioner is a kind of priest. The whole work is framed as an answer to the stock objections (that fate destroys virtue, that the art is impious), and its solution is to subordinate the stars to a single supreme divine power rather than to abolish them. The art is real and its decrees binding; the astrologer's duty is to read them reverently.1

The emperor exempt from the stars (Mathesis II.30)

The most revealing passage is Firmicus's carve-out for the reigning emperor. The astrologer, he warns, must never cast the emperor's nativity — and not out of mere prudence, but because the emperor genuinely stands outside the fatal order, his destiny reserved to the highest God:

In fact no astrologer is able to find out anything true about the destiny of the Emperor. For the Emperor alone is not subject to the course of the stars and in his fate alone the stars have no power of decreeing. Since he is master of the whole universe, his destiny is governed by the judgment of the Highest God…

— Mathesis II.30, trans. Jean Rhys Bram. (Latin: "Solus enim imperator stellarum non subiacet cursibus et solus est, in cuius fato stellae decernendi non habeant potestatem.") The theology is telling: rather than deny that the stars decree, Firmicus lets them decree over everyone but the emperor, whom the "Highest God" governs directly. It is an accommodation, not a refutation — astral fate is kept, and only bent around the sacred person of the prince.2

The astrologer must be pure (Mathesis II.30)

Firmicus also meets the moral charge head-on, and in a way that reads almost like a rule of life. The genuine astrologer, addressed through the work's dedicatee Lollianus Mavortius, is to be a man of blameless conduct — sober, honest, no taker of oaths for money, no frequenter of "nocturnal sacrifices," open and available to all:

Esto pudicus integer sobrius, parvo victu, parvis opibus contentus, ne istius divinae scientiae gloriam ignobilis pecuniae cupiditas infamet.

— Mathesis II.30 ("Be modest, upright, sober, content with little food and small means, lest the love of base money defame the glory of this divine science"). He goes on: keep your friends' loyalty; "keep your honesty uncorrupted in all your activities; never stain your self-respect by becoming a false witness"; and "be pure and chaste" before approaching the work. The point of the passage is apologetic — to answer exactly the moral revulsion the Fathers express by insisting that this art, rightly practised, ennobles rather than corrupts its servant.3

The convert: De errore profanarum religionum (c. 346–348)

A decade or so later the same author writes as a Christian, and a militant one. De errore profanarum religionum ("On the error of the profane religions") is addressed to the emperors Constantius and Constans and urges them to abolish the pagan cults by force. Its voice is unmistakably churchly — it quotes the Shema, preaches the blood of the Lamb, and sets the sacraments against the mysteries:

Seek the bread of Christ, the cup of Christ, that the substance of man, scorning the frailty of earth, may be sated with immortal nourishment.

— De errore, trans. Clarence Forbes. Firmicus describes himself as now "formed by the instruction of the sacred readings" (sacrarum lectionum institutione formatus), the clearest internal marker of a conversion that fell between the two books. Notably, the convert does not recant the Mathesis or single out astrology for attack; his fire is turned on the mystery-cults and the worship of the elements, not on the horoscope.4

Notes

  • Was he already a Christian when he wrote the Mathesis? The question is genuinely open. The mainstream view (Mommsen, Wendland, Norden, and Forbes in the standard ACW edition) is that the Mathesis is a pagan work and that Firmicus converted in the years between it and De errore. A minority view (Franz Skutsch, 1910) held that the doxologies in Books V and VII already sound Christian, so that he may have been a Christian — or at least steeped in Christianity — when he wrote it. Either way the figure is remarkable: a man of the Christian empire could compose the most complete Latin defence of astrology and then, as a baptized believer, campaign against paganism — without ever treating the two as flatly incompatible.
  • Firmicus is the concrete instance behind Clarence Forbes's observation, in the introduction to his translation, that "in spite of St. Augustine's polemic, there were a few cases in ancient and modern times where believing Christians were devotees of astrology, though of course they denied the divinity of the celestial bodies." His accommodation is exactly of that kind: the stars decree, but under a supreme God, and the emperor stands free of them. See the argument on the accommodationist strand and the summary timeline.
  • The exemption of the emperor from fate was not idiosyncratic piety alone but also prudence: casting an emperor's nativity (or asking when he would die) was a capital offence under Roman law, and Firmicus's theological carve-out doubles as a safe-conduct. It shows how the art was domesticated to the imperial order rather than driven out of it.
