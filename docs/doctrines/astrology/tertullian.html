Tertullian on astrology

Dates: c. 203–206 (On Idolatry / De idololatria 9); Tertullian fl. c. 155–220 · Wikipedia
Summary: Answering a professing astrologer who had defended his craft as a livelihood, Tertullian devotes the ninth chapter of On Idolatry to the art. He makes three distinct moves that echo down the tradition: astrology is a species of the condemned genus magic; it was not discovered but taught, by the deserter angels who also fell for the daughters of men (the Watcher tradition of Genesis 6 / 1 Enoch); and it was permitted only until the Gospel — a salvation-history scheme in which the Magi's star is the last legitimate horoscope, and the Magi themselves, warned to go home "by another way," figure the astrologer who forsakes his craft on finding Christ. His closing epigram fixes the point: "You know nothing, astrologer, if you know not that you should be a Christian."

Astrology is a species of condemned magic

Tertullian catalogues the trades a Christian may not practise because they serve idolatry, and comes to astrology as one of the professions "liable to the charge of idolatry." The astrologer, he says, honours idols "whose names he has inscribed on the heaven, to whom he has attributed all God's power" — and, more fundamentally, the whole art is a branch of the magic that Scripture condemns:

But yet, when magic is punished, of which astrology is a species, of course the species is condemned in the genus. After the Gospel, you will nowhere find either sophists, Chaldeans, enchanters, diviners, or magicians, except as clearly punished.

— On Idolatry 9, newadvent.org. The "mutual alliance of magic and astrology" is, for Tertullian, no accident: the two stand or fall together, and both are proscribed "after the Gospel."1

Taught by the deserter angels (the Watcher tradition)

Tertullian gives astrology a demonological pedigree. Its origin is not human ingenuity but the instruction of the rebel angels — the "sons of God" who left their station and took wives of the daughters of men — who taught the art to mankind and were condemned for it:

One proposition I lay down: that those angels, the deserters from God, the lovers of women, were likewise the discoverers of this curious art, on that account also condemned by God.

— On Idolatry 9. He presses the parallel between the astrologers and their angelic teachers as a divine sentence written into history: "The astrologers are expelled just like their angels. The city and Italy are interdicted to the astrologers, just as heaven to their angels" — a reference to the Roman expulsions of the mathematici, read as an earthly echo of the angels' fall.2

Permitted only "until the Gospel"

Tertullian concedes that the astrologers' art was once real and even licensed — the Magi were, after all, "the first to announce Christ's birth." But he folds that concession into a salvation-history scheme: the Chaldean science was allowed only up to the coming of Christ, and abolished by it. After the Nativity, no horoscope is legitimate:

But, however, that science has been allowed until the Gospel, in order that after Christ's birth no one should thenceforward interpret any one's nativity by the heaven.

— On Idolatry 9. The gifts the Magi brought — frankincense, myrrh, and gold — he reads as the valedictory of the old order, offered "to be, as it were, the close of worldly sacrifice and glory, which Christ was about to do away." The distinctive "until the Gospel" periodization is what makes the Magi's star, for Tertullian, the last astrology rather than a vindication of the art — the theme weighed on the star-of-Bethlehem argument.3

The Magi warned to "walk otherwise"

The detail that the Magi, "being warned in a dream," returned to their own country "another way" (Matthew 2:12) Tertullian turns into an allegory of conversion. The change of route figures the astrologer's abandonment of his craft: to leave the old road is to leave the old profession:

It means this: that they should not walk in their ancient path… Just so we ought to understand by it the right Way and Discipline. And so the precept was rather, that thenceforward they should walk otherwise.

— On Idolatry 9. The chapter closes on the epigram that states the whole point — that the only true knowledge the astrologer can reach is that he must abandon the stars for Christ:

You know nothing, astrologer, if you know not that you should be a Christian. If you did know it, you ought to have known this also, that you should have nothing more to do with that profession of yours.

— On Idolatry 9.4

Notes

  • The deserter-angels aetiology. Tertullian's claim that the fallen angels "were likewise the discoverers of this curious art" is the sharpest patristic form of the demonological origin already gestured at by Tatian ("the demons… introduced Fate"), and rooted in 1 Enoch 8, where the Watchers teach astrology to men. He repeats it in On the Apparel of Women I.2, where the same fallen angels disclose "the properties of herbs," metallurgy, and "the interpretation of the stars." That thesis is weighed on its own merits on the fallen-angels argument page, and the shared Watcher-instruction tradition ties this doctrine to the sons of God and the Nephilim.
  • "Until the Gospel" as an answer to the Magi. Tertullian is one of the four Fathers (with Ignatius, Chrysostom, and Gregory the Great) whose reading turns the embarrassment of the star-following Magi into a proof that Christ ended astrology. His periodizing move — the art licensed up to the Nativity, void after it — is distinctive; it reads a verdict into Matthew 2 the narrative itself withholds, which is why the same episode could be, and was, cited to the opposite end. See the star-of-Bethlehem argument.
  • Magic and astrology together. Tertullian treats astrology strictly as a species of magic, condemned in its genus — not as a mistaken science to be corrected but as a forbidden art to be renounced. Unlike Clement and Origen, he draws no careful line preserving a legitimate astronomy; his interest is wholly in the idolatry and the demonic pedigree, not in saving what is true in the study of the heavens.
  • His contested orthodoxy. Tertullian spent his later years in the Montanist “New Prophecy,” and whether that made him a heretic — he was never tried or condemned by any synod — is weighed on the timeline “Was Tertullian a heretic?”, where he stands as a witness on some twenty of this wiki's belief-timelines, this among them.
