Basil of Caesarea on astrology

Dates: c. 378 (the Hexaemeron, Homily VI, preached in Lent); Basil 330–379 · Wikipedia
Summary: In his sixth homily on the six days of creation — the sermon on the fourth day, when the lights were set in the firmament — Basil turns aside for the most sustained attack on genethlialogy in the Greek Fathers. He presses three lines at once: the practical (the birth-instant can never actually be fixed, so the whole casting is guesswork), the ridiculous (deriving a man's character from the beast whose name a constellation happens to bear), and the moral (if the stars settle our virtues and vices, then law, judgment, reward, and punishment are void — and God, who made the "evil" stars, becomes the author of evil). His treatment set the pattern later borrowed by Ambrose, Gregory of Nyssa, and the Latin West.

The hour of birth can never be fixed (Hexaemeron VI.5)

Astrology depends on catching the precise moment of birth as a point of the zodiac crosses the horizon. Basil observes that the astrologers themselves, to save the art, had to slice time ever finer — and in doing so destroyed it, since no observer can seize a moment so small:

The inventors of astrology seeing that in the extent of time many signs escaped them, divided it and enclosed each part in narrow limits, as if in the least and shortest interval, in a moment, in the twinkling of an eye, to speak with the Apostle, the greatest difference should be found between one birth and another.

— Hexaemeron VI.5, newadvent.org. The conclusion is blunt:

Thus, if it is impossible to find exactly the hour of birth, and if the least change can upset all, then both those who give themselves up to this imaginary science and those who listen to them open-mouthed, as if they could learn from them the future, are supremely ridiculous.

This is the same practical objection Augustine would later dramatize with Nigidius's potter's wheel: the decisive interval is real but unobservable, so the astrologer cannot read it.1

Character from the name of a beast (Hexaemeron VI.6)

Basil's most withering passage mocks the mechanism by which the constellations are supposed to stamp a temperament — by the qualities of the animal each sign is merely named after:

Such an one will have curly hair and bright eyes, because he is born under the Ram; such is the appearance of a ram… Another is born under the Bull: he will be enured to hardship and of a slavish character, because the bull bows under the yoke. Another is born under the Scorpion; like to this venomous reptile he will be a striker. He who is born under the Balance will be just, thanks to the justness of our balances. Is not this the height of folly?

— Hexaemeron VI.6. The point is that the "Ram" and "Bull" are human labels arbitrarily hung on patterns of stars; to read a man's nature off the herd-behaviour of the animal named is to mistake a name for a cause.2

Fatalism voids law, judgment, and God's justice (Hexaemeron VI.7)

The heart of the homily is moral. If character and conduct are "the fatal consequence of our birth," then the whole moral order — legislation, courts, praise, blame — is pointless, and the Christian's hope of reward is gone:

If the origin of our virtues and of our vices is not in ourselves, but is the fatal consequence of our birth, it is useless for legislators to prescribe for us what we ought to do, and what we ought to avoid; it is useless for judges to honour virtue and to punish vice.

As for us Christians, we shall see our great hopes vanish, since from the moment that man does not act with freedom, there is neither reward for justice, nor punishment for sin.

— Hexaemeron VI.7. And there is a still deeper objection: the astrologers speak of "malignant" and "benevolent" stars, but the stars were all made by God — so their scheme makes the Creator responsible for evil: "If evil is inherent in their nature, the Creator is the author of evil." Basil will not have it.3

Notes

  • Basil does not condemn the study of the heavens as such: the Hexaemeron is itself a work of natural science that admires the order of the luminaries. What he attacks is genethlialogy — the casting of nativities and the reading of destiny from the stars — a distinction the later tradition keeps sharp (see the summary timeline, and Isidore's formal split of astronomia from superstitious astrologia).
  • The free-will and divine-justice arguments here are the ones Augustine presses in the Confessions and City of God, and that Gregory of Nyssa develops in his Against Fate. Basil is their common Greek source.
  • The practical objection — that the birth-instant is unfindable — feeds the cross-cutting twins argument: whatever interval is supposed to distinguish two horoscopes is too small for the astrologer himself to observe.
