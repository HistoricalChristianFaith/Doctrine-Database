Gregory the Great on astrology

Dates: c. 591 (the tenth of the Homilies on the Gospels, preached at Rome for the Epiphany on Matthew 2:1–12); Gregory c. 540–604, pope from 590 · Wikipedia
Summary: Preaching on the Magi, Gregory names the error he is answering: the Priscillianist heretics who held "that every man is born under the influence of a star," and who cited the Bethlehem star — "His star," they said, the star ruling His fate — in support. Gregory turns the Gospel's own word order against them. The star "went before them until it came and stood over where the Child was"; so it was "not the Child who followed the star, but the star that followed the Child." Man, moreover, was made before and above the luminaries: the stars were made "for man," not man for the stars. And Jacob, born gripping Esau's heel yet utterly unlike him in life, shows that a shared birth-instant fixes no shared fate.

Claims & evidence

The error named: the Priscillianists' astral determinism, propped on the Magi's star

Gregory is unusual on this timeline in naming the living heresy he answers. The Priscillianists — a Spanish movement already condemned by councils and by Leo the Great — taught astral fatalism, and enlisted the very Gospel of the Epiphany to defend it: if the Lord Himself had a natal star, they reasoned, then every birth is starred.

It is well to know that one of the errors of the Priscillianist heretics consists in believing that every man is born under the influence of a star. In order to confirm this notion, they bring forward the instance of the star of Bethlehem that appeared when the Lord was born, and which they call His star, that is, the star ruling His fate and destiny.

— Homilies on the Gospels 10, thedivinelamp (older English translation). The objection is the standing puzzle of this doctrine: the one narrative in which a star heralds a birth is the one the Fathers must not let the astrologers keep. Gregory answers it not by denying the star but by reading it more closely.1

Not the Child who followed the star, but the star that followed the Child

The refutation turns on Matthew's sequence. The star does not lead the Child through the sky; it goes ahead of the Magi and halts over the Child — behaving as a servant that attends its Lord, not a power that governs Him.

It went before them until it came and stood over where the Child was. Whence we see that it was not the Child who followed the star, but the star that followed the Child, as if to show that the Child ruled over the star, instead of the star ruling over Him.

— Homilies on the Gospels 10, thedivinelamp. This is the Fathers' recurring answer to the Magi's star — that it subordinated the heavens to Christ rather than submitting Him to them — and it places Gregory alongside Ignatius, Tertullian, and Chrysostom in the reading weighed on the star-of-Bethlehem argument.2

The stars were made for man, not man for the stars

Gregory then reverses the order of dignity the astrologers assume. To let the stars rule a man's destiny is to set beneath him the very things created to serve him — an inversion of the creation order, in which the luminaries are made for the sake of the creature who bears God's image.

Neither was man made for the stars, but the stars for man; and if we say that they rule over his destiny, we set them above him, for whose service they were created.

— Homilies on the Gospels 10, thedivinelamp. The same argument-from-the-order-of-creation is pressed more elaborately by Ambrose, who notes that the luminaries were made only on the fourth day, after the earth had greened — "the beetle is older than the sun." For both, the life of the world cannot hang on lights kindled to serve it.3

Jacob and Esau: one birth, opposite lives

Gregory closes with the empirical counter-example the tradition returns to again and again — twins, and pre-eminently the twins Jacob and Esau, born at a single moment yet leading wholly different lives. If one horoscope fixed one fate, the two who share it most exactly should share their destinies; they do not.

When Jacob came out of his mother's womb, and his hand took hold of his brother Esau's heel, the first could not have been perfectly born without the second immediately following. Yet such was not in after-life the position of these two brothers, whom their mother brought forth at one birth.

— Homilies on the Gospels 10, thedivinelamp. Esau and Jacob are the Scriptural twins of the anti-astrology dossier — used the same way by Augustine (City of God V) and folded into the wider twins argument.4

Notes

  • Gregory's homily is a late-sixth-century restatement of the settled patristic case rather than a fresh contribution: the star-follows-the-Child reading (cf. Chrysostom), the order-of-creation argument (cf. Ambrose), and the Jacob-and-Esau counter-example (cf. Augustine) are all inherited. Its value is as evidence that the consensus was still being preached — and still had a live opponent, the Priscillianists, whose astral determinism the councils were condemning into Gregory's own era.
  • The Priscillianists' appeal to the Magi's star is the clearest ancient instance of the same narrative being read to the opposite conclusion from the Fathers' — which is why the star-of-Bethlehem argument is rated contested as exegesis even while it is mainstream as theology.
  • The passages are quoted from the older English rendering of Homily 10 (on Matthew 2:1–12, for the Epiphany); the standard modern translation is David Hurst, Forty Gospel Homilies (Cistercian, 1990), where this is Homily 10.
