Bardaisan of Edessa on astrology

Dates: c. 200 (The Book of the Laws of Countries, recorded by his pupil Philip); Bardaisan 154–222 · Wikipedia
Summary: The earliest Syriac voice in the astrology debate mounts an attack found nowhere else in the second century: an argument from the diversity of human law. In The Book of the Laws of Countries — a dialogue set down by his pupil Philip — Bardaisan grants the stars a real hand in the accidents of fortune (sickness and health, riches and poverty), but denies that they compel the human will. His proof is empirical and social: whole nations living under the very same heavens keep utterly incompatible customs, and the "new race" of Christians keeps one law in every country regardless of the stars — so the decree of the stars cannot be what governs how men act. He sorts the world into three domains: what is ruled by Nature, what is ruled by Fate, and what is left to human freedom.

Nations under one sky, many laws

Bardaisan's central move is to set the astrologers' claim against the map. If a man's conduct were fixed by the configuration of the stars at his birth, then peoples born "at all hours and on all days" ought to scatter across the whole range of behaviour — yet entire nations, born under every possible nativity, keep a single distinctive law. The Seres (the silk-people of the far East) are his first case:

The Seres have laws forbidding to kill, or to commit impurity, or to worship idols; and in the whole of Serica there are no idols, and no harlots, nor any one that kills a man, nor any that is killed: although they, like other men, are born at all hours and on all days.

The astrologer's fatal planets, then, get no purchase on a whole people at once:

Thus the fierce Mars, whenever he is "posited" in the zenith, does not overpower the freedom of the Seres, and compel a man to shed the blood of his fellow with an iron weapon; nor does Venus, when posited with Mars, compel any man whatever among the Seres to consort with his neighbour's wife, or with any other woman.

The Brahmans of India furnish a parallel case — "many thousands and tens of thousands" bound by one law "forbidding to kill at all, or to pay reverence to idols, or to commit impurity, or to eat flesh, or to drink wine." Custom, not the horoscope, is what actually governs a nation's life:

The truth is, that in all countries, every day, and at all hours, men are born under Nativities diverse from one another, and the laws of men prevail over the decree of the stars, and they are governed by their customs.

— The Book of the Laws of Countries. The argument is not that no two men differ, but that a shared law overrides the supposed diversity of their charts — which a determining astral Fate could not allow.1

The new race of Christians — one law under every star

Bardaisan then presses the point with the most striking case available to him: the Christians, a people scattered across every clime and therefore under every conceivable configuration of the heavens, yet keeping one law wherever they are:

And what shall we say of the new race of us Christians, whom Christ at His advent planted in every country and in every region? For, lo! Wherever we are, we are all called after the one name of Christ — Christians. On one day, the first of the week, we assemble ourselves together…

Nor does the Fate of the celestial Governors compel them to make use of things which they regard as impure.

— The Book of the Laws of Countries. That a single moral discipline holds across peoples of every latitude and nativity is, for Bardaisan, a live disproof of astral compulsion: the "celestial Governors" (the seven planets) do not override the convert's new allegiance.2

Nature, Fate, and Freedom — what the stars can and cannot do

Bardaisan is not, however, a flat denier of astral power — and this is what distinguishes him from the later Fathers. He divides the world into three jurisdictions. Some things are governed by Nature (common to all men), some by Fate (the stars, which do reach the accidents of outward fortune), and some by Freedom (the will, which the stars cannot force):

On the other hand, sickness and health, and riches and poverty, things which are not within the scope of their freedom, befall them wherever they are. For although the freedom of man is not influenced by the compulsion of the Seven, or, if at any time it is influenced, it is able to withstand the influences exerted upon it…

And thus we men are found to be governed by Nature all alike, and by Fate variously, and by our freedom each as he chooses.

— The Book of the Laws of Countries. So Bardaisan still grants the stars a hand in wealth, poverty, and bodily fortune — his firm denial is only that they compel the moral will. This is a narrower thesis than Basil's or Augustine's wholesale rejection, and it is why later heresiologists (and modern historians) treat him as standing at the edge of orthodoxy on this question rather than squarely within it.3

Notes

  • The diversity-of-customs argument (the same law held by whole nations born under every star) is Bardaisan's distinctive contribution; it is a social-empirical cousin of the logical twins argument — where twins share one chart yet meet opposite fates, here a whole nation of diverse charts shares one law. Both press the gap between the horoscope and the observed life.
  • Bardaisan reaches the same free-will conclusion as the later Greek Fathers (Tatian, Basil, Gregory of Nyssa) but by a different road — and with a qualification they drop: he concedes the stars a genuine sway over fortune while denying it over the will. His later reputation was clouded (Ephrem and others charged his school with astrological and dualist errors), so the Book of the Laws is best read as the anti-fatalist core of his teaching, whatever his followers later made of the rest.
  • The text survives in Syriac and is preserved as a dialogue recorded by his pupil Philip; a Greek version of its argument was excerpted by Eusebius (Praeparatio Evangelica VI.10) and by the Recognitions of pseudo-Clement, an early sign of how widely the diversity-of-customs argument circulated.
