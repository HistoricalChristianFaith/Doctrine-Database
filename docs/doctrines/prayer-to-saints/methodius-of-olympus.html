Methodius of Olympus on prayer to the saints

Dates: Methodius of Olympus c. 250–311 (his genuine works c. 290) · Wikipedia
Summary: Methodius is regularly listed — including in the popular online exchange this timeline answers — as the earliest father in whom the explicit invocation of the saints appears, which would make him a chronologically pivotal pre-Nicene witness. The text that earns him the place is the Oration on Simeon and Anna, whose closing section addresses the Virgin in the second person ("Hail to thee for ever, thou virgin mother of God …") and asks both Mary and Simeon to remember and advocate for the living. But that oration is not Methodius's: it is a later, pseudonymous composition — on the usual judgement fifth- or sixth-century — whose Theotokos-language the nineteenth-century editors already flagged as "hardly appropriate to the period preceding Cyril of Alexandria." Methodius's one complete genuine Greek work, the Symposium, contains no such invocation. So the "earliest witness" disappears on inspection: far from narrowing the development window, the misattributed text, once correctly dated, sits squarely inside the post-Ephesus flowering and tells for the development reading rather than against it.

The passage that names him: the invocation in the Oration on Simeon and Anna

The Oration on Simeon and Anna — a homily on the Presentation (Luke 2) transmitted under Methodius's name — closes, in its fourteenth section, with a direct address to the Virgin and then to Simeon. It is genuine second-person invocation, not merely a statement that the saints intercede:

Hail to thee for ever, thou virgin mother of God, our unceasing joy, for unto thee do I again return. … Wherefore, we pray thee, the most excellent among women, who boastest in the confidence of thy maternal honours, that thou wouldest unceasingly keep us in remembrance. O holy mother of God, remember us, I say, who make our boast in thee …

The same breath turns to Simeon and asks his patronage:

And do thou also, O honoured and venerable Simeon, thou earliest host of our holy religion, and teacher of the resurrection of the faithful, be our patron and advocate with that Saviour God, whom thou wast deemed worthy to receive into thine arms.

Taken at face value and dated to Methodius's lifetime (he died c. 311), this would be the clearest pre-Nicene example of invocation on the timeline — second-person petition to a named saint and to the Mother of God, decades before the Cappadocian homilies. Everything therefore turns on whether the oration is really his.1

Why it is not Methodius: a later work under an old name

It is not. The oration is one of several pieces — most famously the seventh-century Apocalypse of Pseudo-Methodius — that travel under Methodius's name but are the work of later hands. Three converging marks place this homily well after his death:

  • The Marian title is anachronistic. The address turns on calling Mary Theotokos ("God-bearer") as a settled, celebrated title — the very word that became the touchstone of orthodoxy only through the Nestorian controversy and the Council of Ephesus (431). The editors of the Ante-Nicene Fathers note exactly this at the words "thou virgin mother of God": "[He again apostrophizes the Blessed Theotocos, but in language hardly appropriate to the period preceding Cyril of Alexandria.]" A developed Theotokos-piety of this kind is a fifth-century phenomenon, not a third.
  • The text shows signs of working-over. At "thou art its middle and end" the same editors add: "[Not so, for he ends with a noble strain of worship to the Son of God. This expression suggests interpolation.]" — the homily's high-flown apostrophes to Mary sit awkwardly against its own Christ-centred doxology.
  • The scholarly verdict is settled against authenticity. The standard reference judgement is blunt: the oration "is sometimes quoted as an example of early Christian veneration of Mary as the Ever-virgin Mother of God [but] the work is not genuine, [and] belongs to the 5–6th century AD."

The honest upshot is that the homily is real evidence for invocation of Mary and the saints — but evidence for the fifth or sixth century, where it belongs, not for c. 290. Attributing it to Methodius does not push invocation back into the pre-Nicene period; it simply mislabels a later witness with an older name.2

The genuine Methodius does not invoke

Set the spurious homily aside and the authentic Methodius adds nothing on the invocation side of the ledger. His one work surviving complete in Greek is the Symposium (the Banquet of the Ten Virgins), a Platonic dialogue in which ten virgins in the garden of Arete each praise chastity, closing with Thekla's hymn to Christ the Bridegroom of the Church. Its concern is virginity and the soul's restoration; it contains no second-person petition to a saint or to Mary. The surviving fragments and the other genuine remains (e.g. On the Resurrection, On Free Will) are likewise silent on invocation. Whatever Methodius himself held about the cult of the saints, the texts do not show him praying to them — which is why his proper place on this timeline is as a correction of a misattribution, not as its earliest pre-Nicene witness.3

Bearing on the dating question

This matters for the development-vs-apostolicity question. The continuity reading needs genuine early witnesses to invocation; a third-century Methodius addressing the Theotokos would be a strong one. But the witness evaporates: the address is pseudo-Methodian and post-Ephesus, so it cannot be set beside the genuinely early data-points — Hippolytus's address to the three youths (c. 204), the San Sebastiano graffiti (from c. 250), the disputed Sub Tuum Praesidium — as a fourth pre-Nicene pillar. If anything it cuts the other way: the developed Theotokos-invocation of the oration is precisely the fifth-century flowering the development reading predicts, and the ANF editors' own dating of its language ("preceding Cyril of Alexandria") is a small confirmation that this register of Marian prayer is late. The lesson is the one the whole timeline insists on — date the text, not the name on it — and a popular list that opens "asking the saints for their intercession is in Methodius …" opens, in fact, with a misdated homily.4
