Augustine of Hippo — the careful brake: martyrs honoured, latria reserved to God

Dates: c. 426 (the witnesses span Reply to Faustus c. 400, Confessions c. 397–401, On Care to Be Had for the Dead c. 421–424, and The City of God c. 413–426); Augustine 354–430 · Wikipedia · back to timeline
Summary: Augustine is the timeline's most important qualifying witness — the figure who both practises the cult of the martyrs and polices its boundaries, a century after Origen's similar restrictiveness. He affirms unreservedly that the martyrs in glory intercede for the living ("the martyrs do these wonders, or rather God does them while they pray and assist") and that the dead are aided by the prayers and the Eucharistic offering of the church. But he draws a hard line around the cult: "we do not build temples, and ordain priests, rites, and sacrifices for these same martyrs; for they are not our gods, but their God is our God." Sacrifice and the worship the Greeks call latria belong to God alone; at the altar over a martyr's tomb the offering is made "to the God of martyrs," and the martyr "is named in their own place and rank … but [is] not invoked by the sacrificing priest." And in On Care to Be Had for the Dead he is strikingly cautious about the mechanism the whole practice presumes: that the departed should "by their proper nature" know and attend to the affairs of the living is, he says, "impossible" — the martyrs aid us only by a divine power, and exactly how surpasses, he admits, the strength of his understanding. So Augustine attests the intercession-belief at full strength while refusing to let it harden into the worship of the saints, and hesitating over the very thing strict invocation assumes.

"They are not our gods, but their God is our God" — The City of God VIII.27

Closing his long argument against the Platonists' veneration of demons and deified men, Augustine turns to the obvious objection — do not Christians do the same with their martyrs? — and answers by sharply distinguishing honour from worship:

But, nevertheless, we do not build temples, and ordain priests, rites, and sacrifices for these same martyrs; for they are not our gods, but their God is our God. Certainly we honor their reliquaries, as the memorials of holy men of God who strove for the truth even to the death of their bodies… But who ever heard a priest of the faithful, standing at an altar built for the honor and worship of God over the holy body of some martyr, say in the prayers, I offer to thee a sacrifice, O Peter, or O Paul, or O Cyprian? for it is to God that sacrifices are offered at their tombs… Therefore, whatever honors the religions may pay in the places of the martyrs, they are but honors rendered to their memory, not sacred rites or sacrifices offered to dead men as to gods.

The point is precise, and precisely the one this timeline reads everything through. Augustine grants the martyrs real and warm honour — their relics, their tombs, their memory, altars raised over their bodies — and he expects that honour to "stir ourselves up to imitate them … calling to our help that same God on whom they called." What he denies is that any of this is worship: no temples, no priesthood, no sacrifice directed to the saint. The liturgical test he proposes — no celebrant ever says "I offer to thee a sacrifice, O Peter" — is exactly the boundary the later medieval cult would press against, and Augustine plants it firmly on the side of God alone.1

"They pray and assist" — yet "not invoked by the sacrificing priest" (City of God XXII.10; Reply to Faustus XX.21)

The same both/and is sharpest where Augustine defends the martyrs' miracles against the pagan gods'. He affirms, without hedging, that the martyrs do intercede — the intercession-belief at full voice — while again barring their invocation in the sacrifice:

…but the martyrs do these wonders, or rather God does them while they pray and assist… to our martyrs we build, not temples as if they were gods, but monuments as to dead men whose spirits live with God. Neither do we erect altars at these monuments that we may sacrifice to the martyrs, but to the one God of the martyrs and of ourselves; and in this sacrifice they are named in their own place and rank as men of God who conquered the world by confessing Him, but they are not invoked by the sacrificing priest. For it is to God, not to them, he sacrifices, though he sacrifices at their monument.

"They pray and assist" is intercession-belief stated as plainly as any witness on this timeline states it; "named … but not invoked by the sacrificing priest" is the brake. Augustine had drawn the same line a quarter-century earlier in Reply to Faustus the Manichaean, answering the Manichee charge that Christians had merely swapped idols for martyrs. There he names the technical distinction that would govern all later Western theology of the saints — latria, the worship owed to God alone:

It is true that Christians pay religious honor to the memory of the martyrs, both to excite us to imitate them and to obtain a share in their merits, and the assistance of their prayers. But we build altars not to any martyr, but to the God of martyrs… What is properly divine worship, which the Greeks call latria, and for which there is no word in Latin, both in doctrine and in practice, we give only to God. To this worship belongs the offering of sacrifices… Accordingly we never offer, or require any one to offer, sacrifice to a martyr, or to a holy soul, or to any angel.

Note that Augustine here positively wants "the assistance of their prayers" — he is no critic of recourse to the saints' intercession. His concern throughout is the kind of honour: dulia, the affectionate veneration owed to holy men, may be paid to the martyrs; latria, sacrificial worship, may not. The distinction is not yet given those two Greek labels as a matched pair here, but the substance — worship to God alone, honour-and-imitation to the saints — is fixed.2

"Impossible … by their proper nature" — the caution of On Care to Be Had for the Dead

Augustine's most distinctive contribution to this timeline is not his defence of honouring the martyrs — many fourth-century witnesses do that — but his unease about the mechanism that strict invocation takes for granted: that the holy dead can hear and attend to the petitions of the living. Asked by Paulinus of Nola whether burial near a saint's tomb benefits the dead, Augustine wrote De cura pro mortuis gerenda (c. 421–424) and there pressed the hard question. Citing Isaiah's "Abraham hath not known us, and Israel is not cognizant of us," he reasons that even the great patriarchs were "ignorant what was doing towards the People" descended from them — so "how are the dead mixed up with affairs and doings of the living, either for cognizance or help?" His answer about the martyrs is carefully threaded:

We are not to think then, that to be interested in the affairs of the living is in the power of any departed who please, only because to some men's healing or help the Martyrs be present: but rather we are to understand that it must needs be by a Divine power that the Martyrs are interested in affairs of the living, from the very fact that for the departed to be by their proper nature interested in affairs of the living is impossible.

And as to how the martyrs help those who pray at their shrines, he confesses the limit of his knowledge — weighing whether the martyrs are themselves present, or whether God hears their prayers and acts through angels (just "like as we pray for the dead, to whom however we are not present, nor know where they be"):

Howbeit it is a question which surpasses the strength of my understanding, after what manner the Martyrs aid them who by them, it is certain, are helped… A matter is this, too high that I should have power to attain unto it, too abstruse that I should be able to search it out.

This is the strictest qualification any pro-cult witness on the timeline enters. Augustine does not deny that the martyrs aid us — "it is certain" they do — but he denies that the dead, of their own nature, perceive the living at all; whatever knowledge or help they extend is God's gift, mediated, and its workings hidden. That doubt cuts directly against the naïve picture invocation assumes (that one may simply speak to a saint and be heard), and it is why this timeline files Augustine as the careful brake rather than a proponent.3

The mirror practice: prayer for the dead (Confessions IX)

Prayer to the holy dead is the mirror of prayer for the dead, and Augustine is one of the most influential early witnesses to the latter. In Confessions IX his mother Monica, dying at Ostia c. 387, makes her one request:

Lay this body anywhere, let not the care for it trouble you at all. This only I ask, that you will remember me at the Lord's altar, wherever you be.

Augustine then offers exactly such a prayer for her soul, asking God to forgive her sins — the Eucharistic intercession for the dead that he elsewhere defends as profiting the departed:

Forgive her, O Lord, forgive her, I beseech You; enter not into judgment with her. Let Your mercy be exalted above Your justice.

This belongs on the prayer-to-the-saints timeline as its counterpart: the living interceding for the dead at the altar, just as the developed cult has the dead interceding for the living. Augustine holds the first warmly and the second with the qualifications above — a useful index of how the two halves of the communion of the saints stood, and did not yet stand, around the year 400.4

Where Augustine sits on the timeline

Augustine comes last among the great patristic witnesses gathered here, just after Jerome and the Vigilantius controversy (c. 406), and the contrast with Jerome marks the breadth of opinion still live around 400. Jerome closes his eulogy of Paula by turning and addressing her directly — "aid with your prayers the old age of your votary" — the strict, second-person invocation. Augustine never takes that step: he defends the martyrs' intercession and prayer for the dead, but he reserves worship to God, bars the saints' invocation "by the sacrificing priest," and openly doubts that the dead naturally perceive us at all. He thus plays for the Latin West the role Origen played in the Greek East a century before — the careful theologian who affirms the saints' prayers while drawing firm limits around how, and to whom, the living may pray. For the development question this makes Augustine double-edged: that so central a figure is a brake, not a proponent, tells against invocation's being already the settled universal practice — yet the very honours he describes (relic-cult, altars over tombs, miracles at shrines, the martyrs' assisting prayers) show how developed the surrounding cult already was.5

Notes

  • This witness bears most directly on the development-vs-apostolicity question, where Augustine is "the brake": his caution and his hard line on latria are part of the case that the developed, public practice of invoking the saints crystallises only in the later fourth century. He also sharpens the invocation / intercession-belief distinction — he holds the intercession-belief at full strength while refusing invocation in worship, keeping the two senses apart in a single breath.
  • Augustine appears elsewhere on this wiki as a load-bearing witness to the intermediate state and to purgatory and prayer for the dead — the doctrines that frame the "living and the dead" family this one belongs to. The Confessions IX prayer for Monica is the same datum read there.
