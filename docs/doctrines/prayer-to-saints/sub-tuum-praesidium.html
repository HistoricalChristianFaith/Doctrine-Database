The Sub Tuum Praesidium (Papyrus Rylands Greek 470)

Dates: the papyrus c. 250 – c. 700 (contested; see the dating debate below) · Wikipedia
Summary: An anonymous Greek prayer on an Egyptian papyrus fragment in the John Rylands Library, Manchester — the earliest known petitionary prayer addressed directly to Mary, and the earliest surviving text to call her Theotokos ("God-bearer"). It is a second-person invocation ("Beneath thy compassion we take refuge, O Theotokos … rescue us"), so on its older proposed dates it is one of the strongest single data-points for the antiquity of invocation (as distinct from mere intercession-belief). Its date is genuinely disputed — palaeographers have placed the hand anywhere from the third century to the eighth — and that contest governs how much weight the witness can bear.

The prayer

The fragment preserves, in a fragmentary state with letters supplied by its editor where the papyrus is damaged, the prayer known from its later Latin incipit as the Sub Tuum Praesidium. The reconstructed Greek reads:

Ὑπὸ τὴν σὴν εὐσπλαγχνίαν
καταφεύγομεν, Θεοτόκε·
τὰς ἡμῶν ἱκεσίας μὴ παρίδῃς ἐν περιστάσει,
ἀλλ᾽ ἐκ κινδύνων ῥῦσαι ἡμᾶς,
μόνη ἁγνή, μόνη εὐλογημένη.

In English:

Beneath thy compassion we take refuge, O Theotokos [God-bearer]: do not despise our petitions in time of trouble, but rescue us from dangers, thou alone pure, thou alone blessed.

— text and translation as given for Papyrus Rylands Greek 470; the later Byzantine liturgical recension expands and smooths the wording (e.g. λύτρωσαι for the papyrus's ῥῦσαι, and a closing "ever-virgin"), but the core invocation is already here.1 What makes the fragment matter for this timeline is its grammatical form: Θεοτόκε and μόνη ἁγνή are vocatives, and ῥῦσαι ἡμᾶς ("rescue us") is a direct imperative. This is not a statement that Mary intercedes; it is the living addressing her and asking her to act — invocation in the strict sense.

The manuscript

The fragment is Papyrus Rylands Greek 470 (P.Ryl. III 470), a small leaf of papyrus from Egypt, purchased by the John Rylands Library in Manchester in 1917 and first published by the papyrologist C. H. Roberts in 1938 in the third volume of the library's Catalogue of the Greek and Latin Papyri. Roberts identified the text as an early copy of the Sub Tuum Praesidium and translated the surviving lines. Some scholars judge the physical object an amulet — a prayer carried for personal protection — rather than a leaf from a liturgical book, which bears on both its function and its script.2

The dating debate

The papyrus carries no date; everything turns on palaeography — the reading of the handwriting against dated comparanda — and the experts disagree across nearly five centuries. The contest is the heart of the witness, because an early date makes this both the earliest Marian invocation and the earliest use of Theotokos, while a late date blunts both claims.

  • Edgar Lobel (early): "not later than the third century." Roberts's colleague Lobel, the Oxyrhynchus papyrologist, judged on the hand alone that the fragment could hardly be later than the third century — most probably written c. 250–280. This is the date that, if right, makes the fragment sensational.
  • C. H. Roberts (the editor): the later fourth century. Roberts accepted Lobel's palaeographical instinct but balked on theological grounds, calling it "almost incredible that a prayer addressed so directly to the Virgin in these terms could be written in the third century," and so opted for "not before the second half of the 4th century" — a compromise reached partly by doctrinal expectation, not by the script.
  • Otto Stegmüller (1952): the script cannot decide. Stegmüller noted that the formal "chancery" hand stayed essentially the same for centuries, so palaeography could fix only the earliest possible point (the beginning of the fourth century) and "is unable to decide whether the script was written in the 4th, 5th or 6th century."
  • Hans Förster (1995, 2005): the sixth–seventh century, perhaps later. Förster reopened the question with a detailed analysis of the letter-forms (the narrow tall letters, the alpha with a broken crossbar, the distinctive mu, the dot-like thickening at stroke-ends) and the brown ink, comparing them to securely-dated later papyri and to Coptic scribal habits of the eighth–ninth century. He concluded that a third- or fourth-century date is "very improbable" and placed the fragment between the sixth and seventh centuries. Theodore de Bruyn and Arne Effenberger endorsed the reassessment; the Trismegistos database now records the fragment as c. AD 700–900.3

The state of play, fairly stated: the confident third-century date rests on Lobel's brief palaeographical remark, which later specialists (Stegmüller, Förster, de Bruyn) judge cannot bear the weight once put on it, so the scholarly centre of gravity has drifted substantially later. But the question is not closed — C. Römer called Förster's redating unconvincing, and the comparanda Förster invokes establish that such a hand can be late, not that this one must be. The honest reading is that the papyrus is undated and the range runs from the third century to roughly the eighth, with recent palaeography favouring the later end.4

One distinction keeps the debate honest: dating the papyrus is not the same as dating the prayer. Even on Förster's late date, the Manchester fragment would be a later copy of a composition that could be older; conversely, an early papyrus would not prove the wording was already fixed. Independent of the papyrus, the prayer is attested in the Georgian Iadgari (the chant-book of the Jerusalem liturgy), placing it in liturgical use by the fifth century — so the Sub Tuum is securely a late-antique text whatever the Rylands fragment's date, and only its claim to third-century antiquity hangs on the contested palaeography.5

Why Theotokos is the crux

The fragment's second claim to fame is the word Θεοτόκε / Θεοτόκος ("God-bearer," "Mother of God"). That title became the flash-point of the Nestorian controversy and was dogmatically endorsed at the Council of Ephesus (431). If the papyrus is third-century, it is by far the earliest known use of the term, evidence that Marian devotion using Theotokos long predates the conciliar debate; if it is sixth-century or later, it tells us nothing the post-Ephesus record does not already show. So the same palaeographical question decides two firsts at once — the earliest Marian invocation and the earliest Marian title — which is why the dating of one small fragment has drawn such disproportionate attention.6

Notes

  • This witness bears directly on two arguments in this doctrine's Arguments section. It is the clearest early instance of the invocation / intercession-belief distinction on the invocation side — unambiguous second-person petition, not a statement that the saints pray for us — and it is a flagship (and contested) data-point in the development-vs-apostolicity question, where its early date would weigh for continuity and its late date for development.
  • Because the fragment is anonymous and undated, it cannot be slotted on the timeline at a fixed year the way a named father can; its position depends on which palaeographical verdict one accepts. The page therefore records the full contested range rather than asserting a single date.
