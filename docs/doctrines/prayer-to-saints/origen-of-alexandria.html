Origen of Alexandria on prayer to the saints

Dates: c. 233–234 (On Prayer / Peri Euchēs) and c. 248 (Against Celsus / Contra Celsum); Origen of Alexandria c. 184–253 · Wikipedia · back to timeline
Summary: Origen is the most carefully reasoned early witness on this question, and a genuinely two-sided one. He holds, with the whole early church, that the saints already at rest and the angels pray with and for the living — he even allows that one may make a request ("Paul or Peter … benefit us") to a saint. Yet he draws an explicit line the later cult would blur: prayer "in its full meaning" (proseuchē, the worship-act of petition) is to be offered "to God alone, the Father of All," through Christ the High Priest — "not to any begotten being, not even to Christ himself" addressed apart from the Father, and certainly not to angels. He is therefore the hinge witness the development reading leans on: the intercession-belief is firmly in place by c. 233, but second-person prayer to the saints is precisely the thing his careful taxonomy holds back.

The four kinds of prayer, and to whom each is owed

In On Prayer, written at Caesarea c. 233–234, Origen takes up Paul's fourfold list in 1 Timothy 2:1 — "requests (deēseis), prayers (proseuchai), intercessions (enteuxeis), thanksgivings (eucharistiai)" — and asks, of each kind in turn, to whom it may rightly be addressed. His answer is graded. Request, intercession, and thanksgiving may be offered even to fellow human beings; but prayer in the strict sense may be offered to God alone:

Now request and intercession and thanksgiving, it is not out of place to offer even to men—the two latter, intercession and thanksgiving, not only to saintly men but also to others. But request to saints alone, should some Paul or Peter appear, to benefit us by making us worthy to obtain the authority which has been given to them to forgive sins …

— so a request (the lowest of the four registers) may be directed to a saint such as Peter or Paul. But the moment Origen reaches prayer proper, the door closes:

But if we accept prayer in its full meaning, we may not ever pray to any begotten being, not even to Christ himself, but only to the God and Father of All to whom our Savior both prayed himself … and teaches us to pray.

The reasoning is christological and exegetical: when the disciples asked to be taught to pray, Jesus "does not teach men to pray to Himself but to the Father," saying "Our Father in heaven." Origen runs through the logical options — pray to the Son and not the Father, to both, or to the Father alone — and concludes that only the last is right:

It remains, accordingly, to pray to God alone, the Father of All, not however apart from the High Priest who has been appointed by the Father with swearing of an oath … so we are not to offer any prayer to the Father apart from Him.

Prayer in the full sense, then, runs to the Father through the Son; it does not branch off to the saints. That is the restriction the later debate turns on.1

The other side: the saints and angels pray with us

The same treatise is just as emphatic that the blessed dead and the angels are active on the living's behalf — the intercession-belief in full force. Earlier in On Prayer Origen argues that "these pray along with those who genuinely pray — not only the high priest but also the angels … and also the souls of the saints already at rest," adducing Raphael, who "present[s] the prayers of saints" before God (Tobit), and the appearance of "God's prophet Jeremiah," who "prays much for the people and the sacred city" (2 Maccabees 15). Because love of neighbour is "possessed in a far higher degree by saints already at rest than by those who are in human weakness," they continue to care for the church below. And of the angels he writes:

… we may surmise that the presence of the angels who exercise oversight and ministry for God is sometimes brought into conjunction with a particular suppliant in order that they may join in breathing his petitions. Nay more, beholding ever the face of the Father in heaven … the angel of each man, even of "little ones" within the church, both prays with us, and acts with us where possible, for the objects of our prayer.

This is exactly the distinction the whole timeline must be read through: Origen affirms, without reserve, that the saints and angels intercede — they "pray with" and "for" us — while reserving prayer to in the full sense for God. He holds both halves in one breath, which is why he is quoted on both sides and belongs to neither cleanly.2

Against Celsus: honour the angels, but do not invoke them

About fifteen years later, in Against Celsus (c. 248), the restriction sharpens into an explicit refusal to invoke angels — written against the pagan Celsus, who taxed the Christians both with worshipping a man and with inconsistency for not also worshipping God's other ministers. Origen grants that the angels "ascend, bearing the supplications of men" and "come down … conveying to each one … something enjoined by God," yet denies that this makes them recipients of prayer:

… but not so that we are commanded to honour and worship in place of God those who minister to us, and bear to us His blessings. For every prayer, and supplication, and intercession, and thanksgiving, is to be sent up to the Supreme God through the High Priest, who is above all the angels, the living Word and God.

In the next chapter he states the principle directly — knowledge of the angels' true nature "will not permit us to pray with confidence to any other than to the Supreme God" — while still affirming the angels' help:

For to invoke angels without having obtained a knowledge of their nature greater than is possessed by men, would be contrary to reason. … And it is enough to secure that the holy angels of God be propitious to us, and that they do all things on our behalf, that our disposition of mind towards God should imitate … the example of these holy angels …

The pattern recurs across Book 8: Christians "worship with all our power the one God, and His only Son … by prayers and supplications," declining to "worship and serve" even genuine ministers of God (VIII.13); the guardian angel "offers up his prayers through the one High Priest to the God of all, and also joins his own prayers with those of the man who is committed to his keeping" (VIII.36); Christians are simply those "who pray to God alone through Jesus" (VIII.37). The angels assist, accompany, and carry up our prayers — but they are not prayed to. The line Origen draws in On Prayer is, in Against Celsus, the public confession of the church he speaks for.3

Why Origen is a load-bearing qualifying witness

Origen is the figure the development reading leans on most heavily, and the reason is the precision above. By the mid-third century the church's most learned theologian has worked out, in writing, a careful theology of prayer in which: the saints and angels genuinely intercede; a saint may even be the object of a request; but prayer in the full, worship-bearing sense is reserved to the Father through Christ, and addressing angels (and, the structure implies, the saints) in that register is "contrary to reason." This is what the development side means by "the ambivalence of Origen" — though "ambivalence" undersells it: it is not vagueness but a worked-out both/and, and one that permits more (request to saints) than the slogan suggests while reserving more (prayer proper) than a continuity reading would want. Two cautions keep the witness honest:

It does not prove invocation was absent. Origen is prescribing the right ordering of prayer, not reporting the practice of every congregation; an argument that the apologists' silence is non-treatment rather than absence can point out that a theologian drawing a line is evidence the line was sometimes crossed. His near-contemporary data-points — the catacomb petitions to Peter and Paul begin c. 250, the same decade as Against Celsus — show second-person petition to the apostle-martyrs already scratched into Roman plaster while Origen was writing.

But it does weigh for development. That the most careful early mind on the subject reserves prayer in the full sense to God, and explicitly bars invoking even the angels, tells against the claim that the developed, liturgical invocation of the Cappadocian homilies was already the settled practice of the church c. 233. Origen marks the state of the question at the hinge: intercession-belief firm and universal; request to a saint conceivable; prayer to the saints, in the worship sense, not yet a thing he will sanction. The fourth-century flowering is a real development out of that position, not a continuation of it.4

Notes

  • This witness bears on both of this doctrine's grouped arguments. It is the sharpest ancient statement of the invocation / intercession-belief distinction — Origen affirms the saints "pray with" us yet reserves prayer "in its full meaning" to God — and it is the central figure on the development side of the development-vs-apostolicity question, where his careful reservation of prayer to God is weighed against the near-contemporary catacomb evidence that the line was being crossed in practice.
  • The nearest companions on the timeline are the disputed Sub Tuum Praesidium (the earliest petitionary prayer to Mary, third- or fourth-century) and the catacomb petitions to Peter and Paul (from c. 250) — both of them second-person invocation, written within a generation of Origen's restriction, which is exactly why the dating question is genuinely contested.
