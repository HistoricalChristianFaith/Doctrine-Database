Gregory the Dialogist on seeking the martyrs and praying to St. Peter

Dates: c. 604 — the Dialogues (593–594), the Forty Gospel Homilies (590–592), and the Register of letters (590–604); Pope Gregory I c. 540–604 · Wikipedia
Summary: Gregory the Great — "the Dialogist" — is the Latin West's most influential witness on the far side of the fourth-century flowering: with him the cult of the saints and recourse to their patronage are wholly settled, and he crosses the line the whole timeline watches for. In the Dialogues he states his own theology of the point directly — the martyrs "be there present to hear our prayers" of those who "seek them with a pure mind," even where their bodies do not lie — and he recounts, approvingly, a paralytic maid who "prayed to St. Peter" for healing and was answered by the apostle in a vision, calling Peter "our pastor and patron." As pope he closes an official letter to a queen with a benediction that God prosper her "through the intercession of the blessed Peter, Prince of the apostles, to whom you commend them." So Gregory attests invocation in the strict sense — the living seeking and addressing the saints, whom he holds to hear those petitions — not merely the belief that the saints intercede; and he does so from the papal chair, without a trace of the caution his North-African predecessor Augustine had urged two centuries before.

The martyrs "be there present to hear our prayers" — Dialogues II

In the second book of the Dialogues — the Life of St. Benedict — Gregory's interlocutor Peter raises the puzzle that martyrs seem to work greater miracles at shrines that hold only their relics than where their bodies actually rest. Gregory's answer states, as his own settled theology, that the saints hear the prayers directed to them wherever those prayers are made:

Where the holy martyrs lie in their bodies, there is no doubt, Peter, but that they are able to work many miracles, yea and also do work infinite, to such as seek them with a pure mind. But for as much as simple people might have some doubt whether they be present, and do in those places hear their prayers where their bodies be not, necessary it is that they should in those places shew greater miracles, where weak souls may most doubt of their presence. But he whose mind is fixed in God, hath so much the greater merit of his faith in that he both knoweth that they rest not there in body, and yet be there present to hear our prayers.

This is the load-bearing sentence. Gregory does not merely say the martyrs pray for the church in a general way; he teaches that the faithful seek the martyrs, direct prayers to them, and that the martyrs — though absent in body — "be there present to hear our prayers." That is invocation-belief in its full form: the saints are addressed, and they hear. It is the premise the practice of praying to the saints rests on, stated flatly by a pope as the mark of the strong-minded believer rather than a concession to the weak.1

A paralytic "prayed to St. Peter" and was answered — Dialogues III

The third book supplies the strict, second-person act in narrative form. Telling of Abundius, a keeper of the church of St. Peter, Gregory recounts a young woman crippled by palsy who had long prayed to the apostle himself for healing, and whom St. Peter answered by appearing to her in the night:

For a certain young maid, that frequented his church, was so pitifully sick of the palsy, that she crept upon her hands, and, for very weakness, drew her body upon the ground. Long time had she prayed to St. Peter for help of this her infirmity: who upon a night in a vision, stood by her and spake thus: "Go unto Abundius, and desire his help, and he shall restore thee to thine health." … Then quoth she: "Our pastor and patron, blessed St. Peter the Apostle, hath sent me, that you should help me of this my disease."

Three things fix the witness precisely. First, the act is invocation in the strict sense: the maid does not merely believe Peter prays for the church — over a long time she prays to St. Peter directly for her own healing, and calls him "our pastor and patron." Second, Gregory relates it approvingly, as evidence that "the blessed Apostle St. Peter did by miracle declare" his favour toward the faithful keeper Abundius — a proof of the apostle's heavenly power, not a practice he reports in order to correct it. Third, like Chrysostom's emperor "begging the saints to be his advocates," the strict petition here is described in a witness's story rather than uttered by Gregory in the first person; but Gregory's own doctrinal statement in Book II — that the martyrs hear the prayers of those who seek them — supplies exactly the theology the maid's prayer assumes, so the narrative and the doctrine reinforce each other.2

"Through the intercession of the blessed Peter" — the papal Register

What the Dialogues preach, Gregory's official correspondence assumes. Closing a letter to Brunichild, Queen of the Franks, he grounds the queen's hoped-for welfare not in a bare wish but in the apostle's mediation:

May Almighty God ever keep your Excellency in His fear, and so fulfil your desires and those of our sons the most excellent kings your grandsons, through the intercession of the blessed Peter, Prince of the apostles, to whom you commend them, as to grant you to have stable joy in their continual welfare, as you desire.

Two features are worth naming. This is intercession-belief in the precise sense the timeline distinguishes: the petition is addressed to God, "through the intercession of the blessed Peter," with no saint spoken to in the second person — the "with/before God" side of the to-vs-with line, exactly like the Council of Constantinople's 381 refrain. But it is voiced by the Roman pontiff in a diplomatic letter of state to a reigning queen — the practice is so settled that it furnishes the ordinary closing benediction of papal correspondence, and Gregory adds that the queen "commends" her family to Peter, the language of taking a patron. Gregory also routinely sent correspondents "keys of the blessed Peter" containing filings from the apostle's chains, as tokens of that same protecting patronage.3

Where Gregory sits on the timeline

Gregory dies in 604, nearly two centuries after Augustine (d. 430) and the fourth-century flowering in the Cappadocians, Ambrose, and Chrysostom. He is therefore not evidence for the origin of invocation but for its full establishment: by his day it is the settled devotion of the Latin West, taught by a pope as sound faith and assumed in the machinery of papal government. Set beside Augustine — who honoured the martyrs yet barred sacrifice and invocation in worship to them and doubted the dead naturally perceive the living — Gregory shows how completely the Augustinian brake had been released within the Latin church that revered them both. His witness bears chiefly on the development-vs-apostolicity ledger, as the terminus of the "flowering" the cult-historian charts, and on the invocation / intercession-belief distinction, where he furnishes both senses at once: the Dialogues attest the saints being sought and heard (invocation), the papal letter the saints' mediation before God (intercession-belief).4

Notes

  • Gregory already stands on two neighbouring timelines in this same doctrine-cluster (the living and the dead): the intermediate state (Abraham's bosom as the "secret rest of the fathers"; the soul's survival; immediate particular judgment) and purgatory (the cleansing fire before judgment for lighter faults). His picture of the holy dead as conscious, active, and near — hearing prayers, working miracles at their shrines — is the same intermediate-state theology turned toward the living: if the saints are awake and present, they can be sought.
  • The object of the maid's petition and of the papal benediction alike is St. Peter — the same apostle invoked in the third-century San Sebastiano graffiti ("Paule et Petre petite pro nobis") and by Chrysostom's emperor. In Gregory the Roman cult of Peter has become both popular devotion (the paralytic at his church) and papal policy (the chain-relic "keys," the letters commending petitioners to him).
