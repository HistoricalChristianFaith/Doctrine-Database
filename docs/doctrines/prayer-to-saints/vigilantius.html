Vigilantius of Calagurris — the dissenting voice (the earliest articulate critic of the cult of the saints)

Dates: c. 406 (the lost tract attacking relics, vigils, and prayer to the dead, answered by Jerome the same year); Vigilantius b. c. 370 at Calagurris in Aquitaine, a presbyter in Gaul · Wikipedia · back to timeline
Summary: Vigilantius is the timeline's first articulate opponent of the practice — a Gallic presbyter who, around 406, wrote against the reverence paid to relics, the night-vigils at the martyrs' shrines, the lighting of tapers, and, at the root, the belief that the dead saints can hear or help the living. His significance is double: he proves that by the early fifth century the cult of the saints was developed enough to provoke a reasoned protest from within the Church, and his objection — that "the souls of the martyrs" cannot "hover round" their tombs to hear petitioners — is precisely an attack on the premise invocation rests on. Everything we know of Vigilantius's own words survives only in the hostile quotation of his opponent, Jerome, whose Contra Vigilantium is part refutation and part invective; his own tract is lost, so his position must be reconstructed through a deeply unfriendly witness and held at that remove.

What Vigilantius argued — as Jerome quotes him

Vigilantius had stayed with Jerome at Bethlehem in the 390s before returning to Gaul, where (with the support of his bishop, Exsuperius of Toulouse) his views spread. The reverend presbyters Riparius and Desiderius sent his tract to Jerome, who quotes its sharpest lines in order to demolish them. On relics and the honour paid to the martyrs' remains:

What need is there for you not only to pay such honour, not to say adoration, to the thing, whatever it may be, which you carry about in a little vessel and worship? … Why do you kiss and adore a bit of powder wrapped up in a cloth?

And, going to the heart of the matter for this timeline, Vigilantius denied that the saints can be present to hear those who pray to them, and denied post-mortem intercession outright:

Is it the case that the souls of the martyrs love their ashes, and hover round them, and are always present, lest haply if any one come to pray and they were absent, they could not hear?

… so long as we are alive we can pray for one another; but once we die, the prayer of no person for another can be heard…

Read in this timeline's terms, Vigilantius rejects both the cult of relics and the two beliefs that underwrite invocation: that the saints in glory pray for the living (intercession), and that they can perceive and hear those who address them (the presupposition of invocation). His mocking question — whether the souls of the martyrs "hover round" their tombs so as to "hear" petitioners — is, stripped of Jerome's scorn, exactly the objection a critic of the practice would press: the living cannot know the dead hear them. That the objection had to be answered, and answered at length, is itself evidence of how settled the practice had become by 406.1

The problem of the source — a critic preserved only by his enemy

No work of Vigilantius survives. He is a real historical figure — Jerome had met him, and earlier mentions him in his correspondence (Letter 61, a rebuke) — but his theology is recoverable only from the very treatise written to crush it, and Contra Vigilantium is, by common scholarly judgment, the most abusive thing Jerome ever wrote: he puns the man's name into Dormitantius ("Sleepy"), calls him a drunken tavern-keeper "animated by an unclean spirit," and assigns him to the heresy of "Eunomius" and the long-dead "heresy of Cain." Such a reporter is liable to sharpen, caricature, or torque his opponent's claims; the bare quotations Jerome encloses in his opponent's voice are the most reliable residue, but even these reach us only through him. The honest summary, then, is that Vigilantius's position is attested secondhand, by a hostile witness: useful as proof that an articulate internal critique of the cult of the saints existed c. 406, but to be cited with the caveat that we hear the critic only through the man who set out to silence him.2

Where Vigilantius sits on the timeline

Vigilantius is the counter-witness among the late-fourth- and early-fifth-century figures — the Cappadocians (Basil, the two Gregories), Ambrose, Chrysostom, and Jerome — in whom the cult of the saints is otherwise simply assumed and praised. He does not show the practice was rare; he shows it was contested. And he is not alone in unease: Jerome's near-contemporary Augustine is, in his own measured way, a brake on the same practice, honouring the martyrs while barring sacrifice and invocation in worship to them and doubting that the dead naturally perceive the living — the very point Vigilantius pressed more bluntly. Vigilantius marks the outer edge of dissent that was still possible inside the Church around 400, before the cult's near-total prevalence in the centuries that followed.3

Notes

  • This witness bears on the development-vs-apostolicity question: Vigilantius's protest is a data-point on both sides of the ledger. That a reasoned critique was possible and needed answering tells against the practice being uncontested and ancient; that it provoked the consensus of "the bishops of the whole world" (Jerome's claim) against a lone dissenter tells for the practice being, by 406, already catholic.
  • For the act Vigilantius attacks — addressing a saint in the expectation of being heard — see the invocation / intercession-belief distinction: his sharpest objection ("can they hear?") targets invocation's presupposition, while his denial that "the prayer of no person for another can be heard" after death targets intercession-belief itself.
