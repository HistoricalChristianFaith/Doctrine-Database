Gregory of Nazianzus addressing the holy dead

Dates: c. 379–381 (the relevant orations: Oration 18 on his father, c. 374; Oration 24 on Cyprian, c. 379; Oration 43 on Basil, c. 381); Gregory of Nazianzus c. 329–390 · Wikipedia · back to timeline
Summary: Gregory's funeral orations close, again and again, by turning to address the departed in the second person and asking them to act for the living — to "gaze upon us from above," to stay an affliction by their prayers, to guide the flock, to shepherd the congregation. Two of these (to his own father, to his friend Basil) can be read down as funeral-oration apostrophe to an intimate. But the third — Oration 24, to the martyr Cyprian, a man Gregory never knew, on the saint's feast day, for the Church's ongoing protection — resists that deflation: it is petition addressed to a saint qua saint, and it is among the clearest fourth-century instances of invocation in the strict sense.

Oration 43: the closing address to Basil (c. 381)

Gregory's panegyric on Basil — delivered at Nazianzus around 381, probably on the anniversary of Basil's death (1 January 379) — states the premise plainly before it turns to address. Basil, Gregory says, is now

in heaven, where, if I mistake not, he is offering sacrifices for us, and praying for the people, for though he has left us, he [has] not entirely left us.

And then the famous final apostrophe carries that premise into direct petition:

Yet mayest thou gaze upon us from above, thou divine and sacred person; either stay by thy entreaties our thorn in the flesh, given to us by God for o[u]r discipline, or prevail upon us to bear it boldly, and guide all our life towards that which is most for our profit. And if we be translated, do thou receive us there also in thine own tabernacle.

This is not merely grief addressing the dead: Gregory issues concrete requests — stay the thorn in the flesh by thy entreaties (Basil's intercession is asked to relieve an affliction), guide all our life, and receive us in thine own tabernacle. The "praying for the people… offering sacrifices for us" of the preceding section supplies the intercessory ground on which the petition rests.1

Oration 18: the closing address to his father (c. 374)

The earliest of the three, the funeral oration on Gregory the Elder, sets out the same theology — the departed's intercession is now more powerful than his earthly ministry was —

his intercession is of more avail now than was his instruction in former days, since he is closer to God, now that he has shaken off his bodily fetters.

and closes by asking the dead father to act for the living:

Make known to us where thou art in glory, and the light which encircles thee, and receive into the same abode thy partner soon to follow thee, and the children whom thou hadst laid to rest before thee, and me also… and mayest thou guide and free from peril, as I earnestly entreat, the whole flock and all the clergy, whose father thou art said to be.

"As I earnestly entreat" makes the petitionary force explicit: Gregory is not only mourning but asking the departed to guide and free from peril the whole flock. The setting — a son's funeral oration for his father — means an objector can read it as the grief-apostrophe of an intimate; but the requests are real, and the theology of §6 ("his intercession is of more avail now") is the doctrine that licenses them.2

Oration 24: the address to the martyr Cyprian (c. 379)

The decisive case is the panegyric on Cyprian, preached in Constantinople around early October 379. Here the deflationary reading available for Orations 18 and 43 — "this is just a funeral oration's apostrophe to a friend or father" — runs out: Cyprian is a martyr Gregory never knew (he opens by half-joking that he had nearly forgotten him), the occasion is the saint's feast, and the petition is for the Church's ongoing protection. The same oration reports the martyr's relics performing exorcisms and healings — the developed late-fourth-century cult fully in view. In the closing invocation Gregory turns to Cyprian in the second person and prays the dead martyr to watch over and shepherd the living congregation — in the Greek, optatives of request (epopteuois… poimainois, ἤ sympoimainois): "supervising… and shepherding, or rather co-shepherding, the holy sheep of this congregation," and driving off the "predatory wolves" of heresy.3

A near-identical close ends Oration 21 on Athanasius (§37): Gregory asks the dead Athanasius to "cast upon us from above a propitious glance" and to "conduct this people" in its worship of the Trinity. Across 374–381, then, the pattern is consistent: Gregory's addresses to the holy dead carry petitions — receive me, guide the flock, stay the thorn, shepherd the congregation — not bare grief. Oration 24 is the one that cannot be reduced to mourning an intimate, and it is the strongest of Gregory's witnesses to invocation in the strict sense.4

Reading: genuine invocation, with the funeral-oration caveat noted

Read through the lens the whole timeline must be read through, Gregory crosses the line his older contemporary Basil approached but did not quite step over. Basil apostrophizes the Forty Martyrs in praise and urges the living to bring petitions to them; Gregory turns and addresses the departed with imperatives of request. The honest qualification is that the genre is the funeral oration, in which rhetorical address to the deceased is conventional — so Orations 18 and 43, spoken over his own father and his closest friend, can be partly discounted as the apostrophe of grief. Oration 24 removes that escape: the addressee is a stranger-martyr, the occasion liturgical, the request for the Church. For the development-vs-apostolicity question this is the strong end of the fourth-century evidence — with Gregory of Nyssa's encomium on Theodore, the first unambiguous direct petitions to the saints — the point at which the cult-historian's "the practice crystallizes in the later fourth century" is most firmly anchored.

Notes

  • This witness bears on both grouped arguments. It is a clear case on the far side of the invocation / intercession-belief distinction — not merely believing the saints intercede but addressing them with petitions — and it is the strong-end data-point in the development-vs-apostolicity question.
  • The nearest companions on the timeline are Basil of Caesarea (intercession-belief and apostrophic praise of the Forty Martyrs, just short of petition) and Gregory of Nyssa (the encomium on Theodore, the single clearest fourth-century invocation), Gregory's brother by friendship and the third of the Cappadocians.
  • Oration 24 famously conflates two Cyprians — Cyprian of Carthage (martyred 258) and the legendary Cyprian "the magician" of Antioch — blending their hagiographies under one name; this affects the identity of the saint addressed, not the fact that Gregory addresses him.
