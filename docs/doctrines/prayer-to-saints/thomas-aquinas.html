Thomas Aquinas on whether we ought to pray to the saints

Dates: c. 1272 — the Secunda Secundae of the Summa Theologiae (the treatise on prayer, II-II q.83), composed c. 1271–1272; Thomas Aquinas c. 1225–1274 · Wikipedia
Summary: The high-scholastic settlement of the practice. Aquinas asks flatly "whether we ought to pray to God alone?" and answers with the distinction that has organised Catholic teaching ever since: prayer can be offered to a person in two ways — to be fulfilled by him, or to be obtained through him — and in the first way we pray to God alone, but in the second way we do rightly pray to the saints, "not that God may through them know our petitions, but that our prayers may be effective through their prayers and merits." So the saints are addressed as advocates, not as the source of the goods sought; invoking them takes nothing from the worship due God alone, and rests on the prior truth (q.83 a.11) that the blessed in heaven, the closer they are to God and the greater their charity, the more they pray for the living.

Claims & evidence

Two ways of praying: to God to fulfil, to the saints that our prayers be effective through theirs

Prayer is offered to a person in two ways: first, as to be fulfilled by him, secondly, as to be obtained through him. In the first way we offer prayer to God alone, since all our prayers ought to be directed to the acquisition of grace and glory, which God alone gives … But in the second way we pray to the saints, whether angels or men, not that God may through them know our petitions, but that our prayers may be effective through their prayers and merits. Hence it is written (Apoc. 8:4) that "the smoke of the incense," namely "the prayers of the saints ascended up before God." This is also clear from the very style employed by the Church in praying: since we beseech the Blessed Trinity "to have mercy on us," while we ask any of the saints "to pray for us."

— Summa Theologiae II-II q.83 a.4 ("Whether we ought to pray to God alone?"), respondeo, trans. Fathers of the English Dominican Province, source. The sed contra of the same article grounds the answer in Scripture — "Call … if there be any that will answer thee, and turn to some of the saints" (Job 5:1) — and the article is Aquinas's most-cited statement on invocation. The distinction it draws is exactly the one this timeline watches for: praying to the saints is licit precisely because it is not the same act as the adoration owed to God, who alone is asked to fulfil the petition.1

Worship goes to God alone; the saints are invoked as our advocates

To Him alone do we offer religious worship when praying, from Whom we seek to obtain what we pray for, because by so doing we confess that He is the Author of our goods: but not to those whom we call upon as our advocates in God's presence.

— Summa Theologiae II-II q.83 a.4, reply to objection 1, source. This is Aquinas's answer to the charge that invocation is an act of religious worship misdirected to a creature: the latria of prayer belongs to God as "the Author of our goods"; the saints are not worshipped but "call[ed] upon … as our advocates." It is the scholastic form of the Nicene proskynesis/latreia distinction, and the reading the Council of Trent would make dogma three centuries later.2

The saints in heaven pray for us — the closer to God, the more efficacious

As Jerome says (Cont. Vigilant. 6), the error of Vigilantius consisted in saying that "while we live, we can pray one for another; but that after we are dead, none of our prayers for others can be heard" … But this is absolutely false, because, since prayers offered for others proceed from charity … the greater the charity of the saints in heaven, the more they pray for wayfarers, since the latter can be helped by prayers: and the more closely they are united to God, the more are their prayers efficacious.

— Summa Theologiae II-II q.83 a.11 ("Whether the saints in heaven pray for us?"), respondeo, source. This is the intercession-belief on which the invocation of a.4 depends, defended by Aquinas exactly where Jerome had defended it — against Vigilantius — so that the scholastic settlement is explicitly continuous with the patristic controversy this timeline records at c. 406. Aquinas addresses the natural objection (how do the dead know our petitions?) by following Gregory the Great: whatever it is fitting the blessed should know "is made known to them in the Word," so "the petitions which we raise to them are known to them through Divine manifestation" (a.4 ad 2).3

Where Aquinas sits on the timeline

Aquinas writes c. 1272, deep in the medieval consolidation of the practice and centuries past its fourth-century rise. He is not a witness to invocation's origin but to its mature theological form: the practice is now so settled that the question is not whether to pray to the saints but how to state it without derogating from Christ — and Aquinas supplies the formula (two ways of praying; worship to God, advocacy to the saints; the saints' impetration "through their previous merits and through God's acceptance"). He fills the long gap between Gregory the Great (604) and the Reformation dispute, and his distinction is the direct antecedent of the Tridentine decree that invoking the saints "is good and useful" and does not oppose "the honour of the one mediator." His witness bears on the invocation / intercession-belief distinction — which he draws sharply, licensing the second and grounding it on the first — more than on the dating question, on which, as a thirteenth-century systematiser, he is downstream evidence only.

Notes

  • Aquinas's fullest answer to the one-mediator objection (1 Tim. 2:5) is not in q.83 but in Summa Theologiae III q.26, where he treats Christ as the one mediator and the saints' intercession as a participated, secondary mediation that does not detract from it — the same move the Council of Trent and, on the other side, Calvin would contest.
  • The explicit appeal to Jerome's Against Vigilantius ties the scholastic settlement back to the timeline's first recorded controversy (Jerome vs. Vigilantius, c. 406): Aquinas ratifies Jerome's side and names Vigilantius's denial the "error."
