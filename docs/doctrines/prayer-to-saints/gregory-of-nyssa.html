Gregory of Nyssa invoking St. Theodore the Recruit

Dates: c. 380–381 (the Encomium on St. Theodore the Recruit; some scholars date it to 386); Gregory of Nyssa c. 335–395 · Wikipedia · back to timeline
Summary: Gregory of Nyssa's encomium on the soldier-martyr Theodore contains what is widely cited as the single clearest fourth-century invocation of a saint — an unqualified second-person petition asking the dead Theodore to come, to intercede for the imperilled country, to "fight for us" against the barbarians threatening the frontier, and even to gather his fellow-martyrs and the apostles into the prayer. Where Basil apostrophizes the Forty Martyrs in praise and Gregory of Nazianzus addresses friends and one stranger-martyr, Nyssa's Theodore is the text in which the strict invocation is least deniable. By contrast his Life of Macrina — full of reverence for the holy dead — addresses every prayer to God and asks the departed for nothing, sharpening the line the encomium crosses.

The invocation of Theodore

The encomium was preached at Theodore's martyrium at Euchaita in Pontus, on the martyr's feast, with the Gothic ("Scythian") raids on the frontier a present anxiety. After narrating the martyrdom, Gregory turns and addresses the dead Theodore directly:

We beseech you, whether you dwell in the air above or in some celestial circle or angelic chorus, that you assist the Lord or worship him as a faithful servant with the powers and virtues. Come from that place to those who beseech you, invisible friend!

The petition then names the country's danger and asks the martyr to act:

We lack many benefactors. Intercede on behalf of the people that they may share one kingdom because the martyr's country is one of affliction… We fear afflictions and expect danger because we are close to the ungodly Sythians who grieve us with war. As a soldier, fight for us; as a martyr, grant courage to your fellow servants. Since you have prevailed over this life yet are familiar with humanity's sufferings and needs, grant peace that the festivals may continue, that the furious, insolent, mad barbarians might not triumph over the temples or altars and that they might not tread the holy place.

And, should one martyr's prayer not suffice, Gregory asks Theodore to recruit the others:

Should we experience stress and dishonor, let your people beseech the chorus of your fellow martyrs; the prayers of many just people will exonerate sin. Remember Peter, awaken Paul along with John the theologian and beloved disciple, who are solicitous on behalf of the churches which they have founded…

("Sythians" reproduces the translator's spelling.) The imperatives are directed at Theodore — come, intercede, fight for us, grant peace, remember Peter, awaken Paul. This is not the assertion that the martyr prays, nor praise of what he is; it is a prayer to the martyr, asking him to act and to enlist his fellow saints in the asking.1

Why this is the clearest case: invocation, not apostrophe or intercession-belief

Read through the lens the whole timeline must be read through, the Theodore encomium is the text where the three categories the timeline keeps distinct come apart most cleanly, and Nyssa lands squarely on the strictest one:

  • It is not merely intercession-belief (the third-person conviction that the saints pray for us), which Cyril of Jerusalem lodged in the liturgy and which Basil assumes. Gregory does more than report that Theodore intercedes; he asks him to.
  • It is not merely encomiastic apostrophe — vocative praise of the kind Basil pours on the Forty Martyrs. The verbs are imperatives of request, not exclamations of honour, and they ask the martyr to do specific things for the living (drive off the barbarians, grant peace, recruit the apostles).
  • It is strict invocation: a second-person petition addressed to a departed saint for his help and his intercession. The Oxford Cult of Saints in Late Antiquity project reads it exactly so — the martyr asked to "invite other saints to his supplication."

For the development-vs-apostolicity question this is the keystone of the development reading's strong case: by c. 380 the public, fully articulated practice of invoking a saint — by name, in the second person, in the liturgy, for the protection of the empire — is in evidence. It does not by itself reach back to the apostolic age (the cult of Theodore is itself a fourth-century growth), but it fixes the upper terminus: invocation in the strict sense is unmistakably present, and public, in the Cappadocian generation.2

The contrast: the Life of Macrina — veneration without petition

It sharpens the encomium to set it beside Gregory's Life of St. Macrina, written for his own sister shortly after her death (19 July 379). The Life is saturated with reverence for the holy dead — Gregory even sees the radiance of "martyrs' relics" in Macrina's body — yet every prayer in it is addressed to God, and no one asks Macrina for anything. The mother's deathbed blessing ("To Thee, O Lord, I give the fruit of my womb"), Macrina's own dying prayer ("Thou, O Lord, hast freed us from the fear of death"), and the sisters' lament are addressed Godward or, in the lament, are apostrophe of grief and praise ("The light of our eyes has gone out") — never petition to the departed. The Life thus shows that Gregory could write at length about a holy dead person without invoking her: the second-person petition in the Theodore encomium is therefore a deliberate, datable act, not an unconscious habit of speech — and the same author supplies, in two works of the same years, a clean example of each side of the distinction.3

A note on dating

The year of the encomium is genuinely disputed. Jean Daniélou placed it in 381 (anchoring on the close of Theodosius's war with the Goths "the previous year"); Quasten favoured December 386; the Oxford Cult of Saints project says cautiously "of the 380s," and some recent scholars argue for 17 February 380. Nothing in the assessment depends on the exact year: on any of these datings the encomium falls within the late-fourth-century Cappadocian flowering and provides a firmly fourth-century instance of strict invocation. The page dates it c. 380–381 while noting that 386 is defensible.4

Notes

  • This witness bears on both grouped arguments. It is the clearest case on the far side of the invocation / intercession-belief distinction — strict second-person petition to a saint — and it is the keystone data-point in the development-vs-apostolicity question, marking the late-fourth-century flowering of public invocation.
  • The nearest companions on the timeline are Basil of Caesarea and Gregory of Nazianzus, the other two Cappadocians — Basil approaching the line in praise of the Forty Martyrs, Nazianzus crossing it in the address to Cyprian, and Nyssa here crossing it least ambiguously of all.
  • Gregory's homily In Praise of the Forty Martyrs, by contrast, stays (like Basil's) in the register of intercession-belief and exhortation to the living ("Let your petitions be with the martyrs") rather than direct petition — another instance, within Nyssa's own corpus, of the line the Theodore encomium crosses.
