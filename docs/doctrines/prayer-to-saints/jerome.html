Jerome — the explicit defense of the saints' intercession, and his own prayer to Paula

Dates: c. 406 (Against Vigilantius / Contra Vigilantium, written in a single night at Bethlehem; with the Epitaph on Saint Paula, Letter 108, c. 404); Jerome c. 347–420 · Wikipedia
Summary: Jerome is the first witness on this timeline to defend recourse to the saints against a named opponent who attacked it. When the Gallic presbyter Vigilantius denied that the martyrs can hear or help the living — "once we die, the prayer of no person for another can be heard" — Jerome answered in Contra Vigilantium (c. 406) that "if Apostles and martyrs while still in the body can pray for others … how much more must they do so when once they have won their crowns." That the practice was both established enough to defend and contested enough to need defending is exactly what the treatise shows. The bulk of Jerome's argument there is a defense of the saints' intercession (that the holy dead pray for us) and of the cult of relics; the strict, second-person invocation appears two years earlier in his own voice, where, closing his eulogy of his departed friend Paula, he turns and addresses her directly: "And now, Paula, farewell, and aid with your prayers the old age of your votary." So Jerome supplies both halves of the distinction this timeline reads everything through — an explicit, polemical defense of intercession-belief, and a plain instance of invocation — in the first decade of the fifth century.

"How much more … when once they have won their crowns" — Contra Vigilantium

Around 406 the presbyter Vigilantius of Calagurris, in Aquitaine, circulated a tract attacking the reverence paid to relics, the night-vigils at the martyrs' shrines, the burning of tapers, and — at the root — the very idea that the dead saints could pray for the living. The reverend presbyters Riparius and Desiderius sent the tract to Jerome at Bethlehem, who dashed off his reply, by his own account, in a single night. Jerome quotes his opponent's denial squarely:

You say, in your pamphlet, that so long as we are alive we can pray for one another; but once we die, the prayer of no person for another can be heard…

Against this Jerome mounts the argument from the lesser to the greater that becomes the standard patristic defense of the saints' intercession:

If Apostles and martyrs while still in the body can pray for others, when they ought still to be anxious for themselves, how much more must they do so when once they have won their crowns, overcome, and triumphed? A single man, Moses, oft wins pardon from God for six hundred thousand armed men; and Stephen, the follower of his Lord and the first Christian martyr, entreats pardon for his persecutors; and when once they have entered on their life with Christ, shall they have less power than before?

The precise force of the passage is worth fixing. What Jerome defends here is, in this timeline's vocabulary, intercession-belief — the claim that the saints in glory pray for those still on earth, and do so the more powerfully for being crowned. He grounds it in the saints being "not… dead, but… asleep" and in the Lamb's omnipresence ("If the Lamb is present everywhere, the same must be believed respecting those who are with the Lamb"), so that they are not, as Vigilantius held, confined to their tombs. The treatise's wider burden is the cult of relics: Jerome ridicules Vigilantius for calling the martyrs' bones "a bit of powder wrapped up in a cloth," and points to emperors translating relics and bishops building altars over the tombs of Peter and Paul as proof that the whole Church honours them. What the treatise does not contain is Jerome himself addressing a saint in the second person; its argument is that the saints can and do pray for us, the premise invocation rests on rather than the act of invocation itself. Its value is twofold and historical: it shows that by c. 406 the doctrine of the saints' intercession was developed enough to be defended as orthodoxy and contested enough to draw a named critic — and it preserves, in Jerome's hostile quotation, the earliest articulate objection to the practice this timeline follows.1

"Aid with your prayers the old age of your votary" — Jerome's address to the departed Paula

The strict step — second-person petition addressed to a saint — appears in Jerome's own voice about two years before the Vigilantius treatise. Paula, the noble Roman widow who had followed Jerome to the Holy Land and founded the monasteries at Bethlehem, died early in 404; Jerome's long memorial letter to her daughter Eustochium (Letter 108, the Epitaphium Sanctae Paulae) ends by turning from narration to direct address of the dead woman:

And now, Paula, farewell, and aid with your prayers the old age of your votary. Your faith and your works unite you to Christ; thus standing in His presence you will the more readily gain what you ask.

This is invocation in the strict sense: Jerome speaks not about Paula but to her ("aid… your votary"), asking her to help him by her prayers, on the express ground that she now stands in Christ's presence and "will the more readily gain what you ask." It is mild and personal — a bereaved friend's parting words to a holy woman he is confident is with the Lord — rather than a liturgical formula or a cult before a shrine. But in the analysis this timeline uses it crosses the line that Jerome's Contra Vigilantium argument only approaches: there he defends that the saints pray for us; here he asks one of them to. Taken together, Jerome attests both the contested belief and the practice it underwrites, in his own hand, in the first decade of the fifth century.2

Where Jerome sits on the timeline

Jerome belongs with the late-fourth- and early-fifth-century witnesses in whom the developed cult of the saints is simply assumed — a little after the Cappadocians (Basil, c. 373; Gregory of Nazianzus and Gregory of Nyssa, c. 379–381), Ambrose (c. 377), and John Chrysostom (c. 390) — but distinguished from all of them by the polemical setting. The Cappadocians and Chrysostom practise and praise recourse to the saints; Jerome is the first here to argue for it against an opponent who rejects it root and branch. That makes the Jerome–Vigilantius exchange the place on this timeline where the practice is shown not merely assumed but disputed: the same decade yields both the most full-throated patristic defense and the most articulate patristic critique (Vigilantius, preserved only through Jerome's hostile report). Jerome sits chronologically just before Augustine, who — though he honours the martyrs — is the careful brake, barring sacrifice and invocation in worship to the saints and doubting that the dead naturally perceive us; the contrast between Jerome's confident "aid with your prayers" and Augustine's caution marks the breadth of opinion still possible around the year 400.3

Notes

  • This witness bears on both of this doctrine's grouped arguments. For the invocation / intercession-belief distinction Jerome is an unusually clean test case: Contra Vigilantium defends the belief (the saints pray for us) while Letter 108 performs the act (asking a saint to pray) — the two senses, in one author, two years apart. For the development-vs-apostolicity question the treatise is a double-edged data-point: that the practice needed defending in 406 shows it was contested, but Jerome casts Vigilantius as a lone "monster" against the consensus of "the bishops of the whole world," presenting the practice as already catholic.
  • The opponent Jerome answers, Vigilantius, has his own block on this timeline as the earliest articulate critic of the practice — though his words survive only as Jerome quotes them to refute them.
