Cyril of Jerusalem on the saints in the liturgy

Dates: c. 350 (the Mystagogical Catecheses, traditionally placed near Cyril's accession; see the authorship note below, which may push the text to c. 390); Cyril of Jerusalem c. 313–386 · Wikipedia · back to timeline
Summary: Cyril's value to this question is that his evidence is liturgical, not merely homiletic: describing the Eucharistic prayer step by step for the newly baptized, he reports that the church commemorates the patriarchs, prophets, apostles and martyrs "that at their prayers and intercessions God would receive our petition." This is the intercession-belief woven into the central act of corporate worship in mid-fourth-century Jerusalem — firm evidence that the saints' ongoing intercession was a settled, official assumption of the rite. But it is precisely not a second-person address to the saints: the prayer is offered to God, and the saints' intercessions are named as the ground on which God is asked to act. Cyril is therefore a clean test-case for the distinction the whole timeline turns on — intercession-belief, here institutionalized in the liturgy, without (in this passage) explicit invocation.

The commemoration of the saints in the Eucharistic prayer

The last of Cyril's catechetical lectures — the fifth of the Mystagogical Catecheses, delivered to the newly baptized in the week after Easter — walks through the liturgy of the faithful in order: the washing of hands, the kiss of peace, the Sursum corda ("Lift up your hearts"), the Sanctus, the invocation of the Spirit upon the gifts, and then the great intercessions. Having described the church praying "for the common peace of the Churches, for the welfare of the world … for all who stand in need of succour," Cyril turns to the commemoration of the holy dead:

Then we commemorate also those who have fallen asleep before us, first Patriarchs, Prophets, Apostles, Martyrs, that at their prayers and intercessions God would receive our petition. Then on behalf also of the Holy Fathers and Bishops who have fallen asleep before us, and in a word of all who in past years have fallen asleep among us, believing that it will be a very great benefit to the souls, for whom the supplication is put up, while that holy and most awful sacrifice is set forth.

Two distinct moves sit side by side here, and they should not be run together. The first is the commemoration of the glorified saints — patriarchs, prophets, apostles, martyrs — so that "at their prayers and intercessions God would receive our petition": the church leans on the intercession the saints are believed to be making. The second is the church's prayer for the lesser dead (bishops, fathers, all the departed), the practice of prayer for the dead that is the mirror image of this one. The witness this page records is the first: the saints' intercession is not an inference Cyril draws in passing but a fixed station of the canon of the Mass as he knew it.1

Intercession-belief, liturgically institutionalized — but not (here) invocation

Read through the lens the whole timeline must be read through, Cyril sits on a precise point. The passage is decisive evidence for one half of the distinction and silent on the other. It establishes that by the mid-fourth century the belief that the saints in glory intercede for the living was not merely a private conviction but a corporate, official assumption of the Eucharistic liturgy — named aloud at the altar, at the most solemn moment of the rite. That is real and weighty: it shows how thoroughly the intercession-belief had been absorbed into public worship.

What it does not show is invocation in the strict sense — the second-person address "O holy martyr, pray for me." Grammatically the prayer here is directed to God: God is asked to "receive our petition," and the saints' "prayers and intercessions" are the ground on which that request is pressed. The saints are spoken of, not spoken to. This is exactly the structure Origen would have allowed and the Sub Tuum Praesidium would have exceeded: the saints are believed to pray, and the church relies on it, but the congregation does not (in this text) turn and address them.2

For the development-vs-apostolicity question the witness cuts both ways, and should be presented without overclaiming in either direction. For the continuity reading, it shows the intercession-belief so deeply settled by c. 350 that it had a fixed liturgical slot — not the artefact of a sudden late-century innovation. For the development reading, it shows that what is institutionalized in the mid-fourth century is the intercession-belief, not direct invocation: the unambiguous second-person addresses come with the Cappadocian homilies of the same generation and after, and Cyril's careful liturgical formula ("at their prayers and intercessions") is one step back from them. He documents the soil out of which invocation grew, not invocation itself.

A note on dating and authorship

The date assigned here carries a real scholarly caveat. The eighteen Catechetical Lectures proper are securely Cyril's and are usually dated to around 350. The five Mystagogical Catecheses (Lectures 19–23), from which this passage comes, are textually less secure: some manuscripts ascribe them to Cyril, others to his successor John of Jerusalem (bishop 387–417), and a number of scholars judge them a later redaction — possibly John's — from the 380s or 390s. The question matters for this timeline only at the margin. If the text is Cyril's own, it is a mid-century witness antedating most of the Cappadocian material; if it is John's, it falls squarely within the same late-fourth-century flowering. Either way it testifies to the saints' intercession holding a fixed place in the Jerusalem liturgy of the second half of the fourth century. The page dates it c. 350 (the traditional ascription) while flagging that a later date is defensible; nothing in the assessment above depends on resolving it.3

Notes

  • This witness bears on both of this doctrine's grouped arguments. It is a textbook case of the invocation / intercession-belief distinction — intercession-belief, here lodged in the canon of the Mass, expressed about the saints rather than to them — and it is a data-point on the development side of the development-vs-apostolicity question, showing the intercession-belief institutionalized by mid-century while explicit invocation belongs to the Cappadocian generation.
  • The nearest companions on the timeline are Origen (who likewise affirms the saints' intercession while reserving prayer "in the full sense" to God) on the earlier side, and the fourth-century flowering of explicit invocation in the Cappadocians on the later side. The mirror practice — the prayer for the dead Cyril describes in the same breath — belongs to purgatory and prayer for the dead.
