The Second Council of Nicaea on venerating and invoking the saints

Dates: c. 787 — the Seventh Ecumenical Council, seven sessions at Nicaea (September–October 787), summoned by the Empress Irene and Patriarch Tarasius to reverse the iconoclasm of the Council of Hieria (754); the doctrinal Definition (horos) was decreed at the seventh session · Wikipedia
Summary: The council whose central business was the veneration of images, but which in the same act fixed the enduring technical vocabulary the whole later defence of invoking the saints rests on — distinguishing the honorific reverence (τιμητικὴ προσκύνησις) paid to images, relics, the Cross and the saints from the adoration (λατρεία) that belongs to God alone — and formally received into its record the confessions of returning bishops who "ask for the intercessions" of the Mother of God and of all the saints. It is not a witness to the origin of invocation (that lies four centuries back) but to its settled, conciliar status in the East by the late eighth century; and its acts preserve the striking datum that even the iconoclasts it condemned had taken the invocation of the saints for granted as orthodox.

Claims & evidence

Veneration (proskynesis) of the saints and their images, not the adoration (latreia) due to God alone

… the venerable and holy images, as well in painting and mosaic as of other fit materials, should be set forth in the holy churches of God … and to these should be given due salutation and honourable reverence (ἀςπασμὸν καὶ τιμητικὴν προσκύνησιν), not indeed that true worship of faith (λατρείαν) which pertains alone to the divine nature … For the honour which is paid to the image passes on to that which the image represents, and he who reveres the image reveres in it the subject represented.

— The Definition of the Second Council of Nice (the horos, seventh session), trans. H. R. Percival, The Seven Ecumenical Councils (NPNF2 vol. 14), source. The distinction the council draws here — proskynesis (relative, honorific reverence) for images, relics, the Cross, and the saints, over against latreia (adoration) reserved to God — became the standard grammar in which every later Catholic and Orthodox defence of invoking the saints is stated. The horos itself speaks directly of images and, in the same breath, of the veneration of relics; the explicit petitioning of the saints appears in the confessions the council received (below).1

"I ask for the intercessions of our spotless Lady the Holy Mother of God … and of all the Saints"

Likewise also the venerable images of the incarnation of our Lord Jesus Christ … and of our spotless Lady, the holy Mother of God; and of the angels like unto God; and of the holy Apostles, Prophets, Martyrs, and of all the Saints — the sacred images of all these, I salute and venerate … I ask for the intercessions (πρεσβείας) of our spotless Lady the Holy Mother of God, and those of the holy and heavenly powers, and those of all the Saints.

— the written confession of Basil, bishop of Ancyra, read on his return from iconoclasm and received by the council in its first session (24 September 787), Percival, NPNF2 vol. 14, source. This is the council's own affirmation of strict invocation in the timeline's sense — not merely a belief that the saints intercede, but the living directly asking the Mother of God and the saints for their intercessions — formally received as the orthodoxy a repentant bishop must profess. Basil adds that he receives the saints' relics "with all honour" and "venerate[s] these with honour" (τιμητικῶς προσκυνῶ), "hoping to have a share in their holiness."2

Even the iconoclasts affirmed invoking the saints — the 754 Epitome preserved in the acts

Read into the acts of 787 to be refuted point by point was the Epitome of the earlier Iconoclast Council of Hieria (754). Its quarrel with the orthodox was over images alone; on the saints it stood exactly where 787 would, and its own anathemas defend their invocation:

(15) If anyone shall not confess the holy ever-virgin Mary, truly and properly the Mother of God, to be higher than every creature … and does not with sincere faith seek her intercessions as of one having confidence in her access to our God, … let him be anathema. … (17) If anyone denies the profit of the invocation of Saints … let him be anathema.

— The Epitome of the Definition of the Iconoclastic Conciliabulum held in Constantinople, A.D. 754, Percival, NPNF2 vol. 14, source. These numbered anathemas belong to the iconoclast synod of 754, not to the horos of 787 (the same list, at (16), condemns representing the saints "in lifeless pictures with material colours" — the iconoclast position 787 overturned); they are preserved because the acts of 787 quote the 754 document in full. Their bearing on this timeline is that both the pro-image council of 787 and the anti-image council of 754 took the invocation of the saints and the Theotokos as settled orthodoxy — the practice is common ground of eighth-century Eastern Christianity, disputed by neither party.3

Where Nicaea II sits on the timeline

The council meets in 787, nearly two centuries after Gregory the Great (d. 604) and four after the fourth-century flowering in the Cappadocians, Ambrose, and Chrysostom. It is therefore not evidence for the rise of invocation but for its conciliar consolidation in the East: by 787 asking the saints and the Theotokos for their intercessions is the confessed orthodoxy a bishop must hold, and the proskynesis/latreia distinction supplies the theological guard-rail — honour to the saint, worship to God alone — that later councils in the West (Trent, 1563) and the Reformers who rejected the practice (Calvin) would both argue over. It bears chiefly on the development-vs-apostolicity ledger, as a late, formal terminus of the "flowering," and on the invocation / intercession-belief distinction, where Basil of Ancyra's received confession furnishes the strict, second-person petition itself.

Notes

  • A common citation trap: the arresting anathema "If anyone denies the profit of the invocation of Saints, let him be anathema" is frequently quoted as a canon of Nicaea II (787). It is in fact item (17) of the iconoclast council of 754, printed in the acts of 787 as the text to be answered. The point it makes for this timeline holds either way — the invocation of the saints was uncontested between the two eighth-century parties — but the attribution should be to 754, not to the 787 horos.
  • The proskynesis vs. latreia distinction the council fixes is the same one pressed centuries earlier, in substance, by Augustine (the martyrs "are named … but they are not invoked by the sacrificing priest," worship reserved to God) and, in the Reformation dispute, the very line the Council of Trent would invoke to answer the charge of idolatry and Calvin would judge insufficient.
