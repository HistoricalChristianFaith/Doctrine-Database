Irenaeus of Lyons on prayer to the saints

Dates: c. 202 (Against Heresies) — Irenaeus of Lyons c. 130–202 · Wikipedia
Summary: Irenaeus is the earliest figure this timeline reaches, and he sits on the invocation-absent side — but as a witness of silence, not of protest. In a large corpus much concerned with the martyrs, the dead, and the shape of prayer, he never addresses a departed saint, and he offers no clear statement even of the later intercession-belief. His one positive account of how the church prays runs the other way: she "directs her prayers to the Lord, who made all things … calling upon the name of our Lord Jesus Christ," and works her miracles "in His name" and not "by means of angelic invocations." Whether that last clause bears on the invocation question is itself disputed (Grabe read it so; Massuet restricted it to the conjuring of evil spirits), so Irenaeus is best weighed for what he plainly does and does not do — Christ-directed prayer affirmed, invocation of any saint absent.

Claims & evidence

The church prays to the Lord and works miracles in Christ's name — not "by means of angelic invocations"

In Against Heresies II.32 Irenaeus is answering the Gnostics' claim to work wonders like Christ's. His reply distinguishes the true church's miracles — exorcisms, healings, even raisings — from the magicians' phantasms by their source and their manner. The church's power is exercised freely, for benefit not deception, and above all it is invoked in one Name only:

Wherefore, also, those who are in truth His disciples, receiving grace from Him, do in His name perform [miracles], so as to promote the welfare of other men, according to the gift which each one has received from Him. For some do certainly and truly drive out devils, so that those who have thus been cleansed from evil spirits frequently both believe [in Christ], and join themselves to the Church. … Others still, heal the sick by laying their hands upon them, and they are made whole. Yea, moreover, as I have said, the dead even have been raised up, and remained among us for many years. …

Nor does she perform anything by means of angelic invocations, or by incantations, or by any other wicked curious art; but, directing her prayers to the Lord, who made all things, in a pure, sincere, and straightforward spirit, and calling upon the name of our Lord Jesus Christ, she has been accustomed to work miracles for the advantage of mankind, and not to lead them into error.

— Against Heresies II.32.4–5, trans. Ante-Nicene Fathers vol. 1, newadvent.org; also historicalchristian.faith. The positive half of this is unambiguous and pertinent: when Irenaeus describes how his church prays, the prayer is "directed to the Lord" and offered by "calling upon the name of our Lord Jesus Christ." No intermediary is addressed; the object of prayer and the name invoked are God and Christ. The negative half — "nor … by means of angelic invocations" — is the tantalizing clause, but its scope is contested (see the next section), so it cannot be pressed into a rejection of praying to the saints without overreading it.1

What "angelic invocations" means here — a dispute already in the footnotes (Grabe vs. Massuet)

The clause "nor does she perform anything by means of angelic invocations" invites a modern reader to hear a verdict on invoking heavenly beings. But its context is magic: Irenaeus is contrasting the church with Simon Magus, Menander, and Carpocrates, whose "curious arts," "incantations," and conjured phantasms he has just described. The two standard editors of the Latin text split on how far to press it, and the Ante-Nicene Fathers preserves the disagreement in a note:

Grabe contends that these words imply that no invocations of angels, good or bad, were practised in the primitive Church. Massuet, on the other hand, maintains that the words of Irenaeus are plainly to be restricted to evil spirits, and have no bearing on the general question of angelic invocation.

— editorial note to Against Heresies II.32.5, Ante-Nicene Fathers vol. 1, newadvent.org. The honest reading lies between: in its immediate setting "angelic invocations" most naturally means the magical summoning of spirits (Massuet's point has real force — this is not a treatise clause about the cult of the martyrs), so the passage will not by itself prove Irenaeus opposed praying to saints. Yet Grabe's inference is not baseless either: Irenaeus reaches for "directing her prayers to the Lord … calling upon the name of our Lord Jesus Christ" as the church's self-evident contrast to invoking any other power — the reflex of a writer for whom prayer and the invoked Name are simply Christ's. The clause is therefore a data-point on the direction and object of prayer in his church, not a polemic against invocation of the holy dead; it should be cited for what it shows and not for what it merely suggests.2

His intermediate-state doctrine gives no devotional foothold: the dead wait, they do not yet reign

Irenaeus's picture of the departed cuts mildly against the later devotional premise. Against those who taught that the soul flies "above the heavens" at death, he insists the souls of the disciples "go away into the invisible place allotted to them by God, and there remain until the resurrection, awaiting that event" — following the Master, who did not ascend at death but "tarried" three days among the dead:

the souls of His disciples also, upon whose account the Lord underwent these things, shall go away into the invisible place allotted to them by God, and there remain until the resurrection, awaiting that event; then receiving their bodies, and rising in their entirety, that is bodily, just as the Lord arose, they shall come thus into the presence of God.

— Against Heresies V.31.2, historicalchristian.faith (fuller treatment on his intermediate-state page). Where the developed cult of the saints presumes the martyrs already in glory, near God's throne, hearing and forwarding the petitions of the living, Irenaeus's "delayed-glory" account has even the righteous waiting in an appointed interim place until the bodily resurrection. That does not exclude their praying for the church — Cyprian a half-century later holds both the waiting-in-paradise and the still-praying — but Irenaeus never draws the second inference, and his stress on the not-yet of the disciples' glory offers no natural platform for turning to address them. His silence about invocation is thus of a piece with the shape of his eschatology.3

Notes

  • This is a witness of silence with a positive datum, and it should be weighed as exactly that — not more. Irenaeus never invokes a saint and never plainly states the intercession-belief; what he does supply is a positive account of the church's prayer as Christ-directed (II.32.5) and an eschatology that keeps the righteous waiting rather than reigning (V.31). The commenter's own formulation is the fair one: there is no explicit evidence that Irenaeus prayed to the saints, and nothing that says he did not — with the added texture that his one relevant passage runs the God-and-Christ direction.
  • The caution that keeps the page honest: angels are not departed human saints, and "angelic invocations" in II.32.5 most likely names the magical conjuring of spirits, not the later cult of the martyrs. Do not enlist Irenaeus as a father who "condemned" praying to saints; he addresses a different target. He belongs on the invocation-absent side of the ledger by what is present and what is missing in him, not by any protest he lodged.
  • He anchors the pre-Nicene, earliest end of the invocation / intercession-belief distinction, and feeds the development side of the development-vs-apostolicity question — the argument from the apologists' silence, of which Irenaeus (c. 202) is the first substantial instance. He stands beside the two other pre-Nicene witnesses on that side: Origen's reservation of prayer "in the full sense" to God (c. 233) and Cyprian's intercession-belief held without invocation (c. 252).
  • Irenaeus's Christ-and-God-directed account of prayer here is the same instinct visible in his Eucharistic theology (the bread receives "the invocation of God" and becomes the body of Christ) — the invoked Name throughout is the Lord's.
