The Council of Trent on the invocation of the saints

Dates: c. 1563 — Session 25, the twenty-fifth and final session of the Council of Trent (3–4 December 1563), the "Decree on the Invocation, Veneration, and Relics of Saints, and on Sacred Images" · Wikipedia
Summary: The Catholic Church's dogmatic answer to the Reformation on this exact point. In its last session Trent decrees that the saints reigning with Christ pray to God for men, that "it is good and useful suppliantly to invoke them," and condemns as impious those who deny that the saints are to be invoked or who call such invocation idolatry or an affront to the one mediator. It thus makes conciliar and binding the settlement Aquinas had systematised and answers Calvin and the Reformers head-on — carefully framing invocation as obtaining benefits from God through Christ, so as to guard the "honour of the one mediator" the Reformers had said it destroyed.

Claims & evidence

The saints pray for us, and it is good and useful to invoke them

the saints, who reign together with Christ, offer up their own prayers to God for men; that it is good and useful suppliantly to invoke them, and to have recourse to their prayers, aid, (and) help for obtaining benefits from God, through His Son, Jesus Christ our Lord, who is our alone Redeemer and Saviour.

— Decree on the Invocation, Veneration, and Relics of Saints, and on Sacred Images, Session 25 (1563), trans. J. Waterworth, The Canons and Decrees of the Council of Trent (1848), source. The decree states both senses this timeline distinguishes, and orders them: the saints intercede ("offer up their own prayers to God for men"), and on that basis the living may invoke them ("suppliantly to invoke them, and to have recourse to their prayers"). The whole is bracketed "through His Son, Jesus Christ our Lord, who is our alone Redeemer and Saviour" — the guard-clause that invocation gains benefits from God through Christ, not around him.1

Condemnation of those who deny invocation or call it idolatry

they think impiously, who deny that the saints, who enjoy eternal happiness in heaven, are to be invocated; or who assert either that they do not pray for men; or, that the invocation of them to pray for each of us even in particular, is idolatry; or, that it is repugnant to the word of God; and is opposed to the honour of the one mediator of God and men, Christ Jesus; or, that it is foolish to supplicate, vocally, or mentally, those who reign in heaven.

— ibid., source. This clause answers the Reformers almost verbatim: Calvin had charged that invocation "robs God of his glory," "destroys the intercession of Christ," and "is repugnant to the word of God," and the Augsburg Confession that Scripture "teaches not the invocation of saints." Trent takes up each phrase — idolatry, repugnant to the word of God, opposed to the one mediator, foolish — and pronounces those who so hold to "think impiously." The doctrine that had been contested since Vigilantius is now, for the Catholic Church, defined dogma.2

Where Trent sits on the timeline

Trent legislates in 1563, four years after Calvin's final Institutes (1559) and thirty-three after the Augsburg Confession (1530): it is the Counter-Reformation's formal reply, not an independent early witness. Its value on this timeline is that it fixes, at the highest conciliar level and in explicit dialogue with the Reformers, the two-sided shape of the doctrine the whole page has traced — the saints intercede (uncontested since the earliest fathers) and may therefore be invoked (the narrower, later-attested act) — and it does so while conceding the Reformers' central concern, that Christ is "our alone Redeemer and Saviour," by routing all benefit through him. With Trent and Calvin the timeline reaches the settled, permanent division: the practice defined and defended by Rome, denied by the Reformation churches. Its bearing is on the invocation / intercession-belief distinction (which it embeds in the very grammar of the decree) rather than on the dating question, on which, as a sixteenth-century council, it is a terminus and not a source.

Notes

  • Trent's guard-clause — benefits obtained from God "through His Son, Jesus Christ our Lord, who is our alone Redeemer and Saviour" — is the conciliar form of Aquinas's distinction (worship to God as "the Author of our goods," the saints invoked "as our advocates") and of the Nicene proskynesis/latreia line. The Reformers judged the guard-clause insufficient; Trent judged it decisive. That disagreement, not the fact of the saints' intercession, is the nub of the division.
  • The same decree treats the veneration of relics and of sacred images alongside invocation — the three questions the Reformation had joined together — and, like Nicaea II, insists the honour paid to images "is referred to the prototypes which they represent," reserving worship to God.
