Do the San Sebastiano catacomb graffiti attest third-century invocation of the saints?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: sound — as evidence that invocation in the strict sense (the living directly addressing a saint and asking his prayers) was practised by ordinary Roman Christians from the second half of the third century. The hundreds of graffiti scratched into the plaster of the triclia at the apostles' memorial on the Appian Way include unambiguous second-person petitions to Peter and Paul — "Paule et Petre petite pro Victore" ("Paul and Peter, intercede for Victor"), "in mente habete nos" ("keep us in mind"), even "in your prayers" — and the standard epigraphic scholarship (ICUR, Carletti, Felle) reads them as genuine requests for intercession, datable c. 250/260 – c. 320 on the archaeological sequence. What the graffiti do not establish is carried on the sibling page: this is the cult of the two apostles at their relic-shrine, bound up with the inherited funerary banquet (refrigerium), not yet the generalised invocation of any saint, and it begins c. 250, not in the apostolic age — so it refutes "invented in the 380s" without settling the larger development question

The claim

The continuity side of the development debate rests much of its weight on epigraphic evidence: where the literary apologists of the first three centuries are silent, the wall-scratchings of the ordinary faithful are not. The strongest case is the corpus of graffiti from the cemetery ad Catacumbas on the Via Appia — the Memoria Apostolorum, the site now under the Basilica of San Sebastiano. Beneath a porticoed banqueting hall (the triclia), excavation recovered the plaster of its walls covered with roughly five to six hundred scratched petitions, the great majority addressed to Peter and Paul. The claim weighed here is narrow and concrete: these are second-person prayers to the saints — invocation in the strict sense — and they are securely third-century, decades before the Cappadocian homilies usually treated as the first unambiguous invocations. If sound, the silence of the apologists cannot be read as the absence of the practice, because the practice is written on the wall.

The graffiti

The corpus is large and well published. Antonio Ferrua's edition in volume V of the Inscriptiones Christianae Urbis Romae presents the graffiti from the plaster of the Memoria Apostolorum (Ferrua counted some 640; Antonio Felle's 2012 re-edition fixes the published total at 538), the basic scholarly record, with the fullest recent commentary by Carlo Carletti.1 Three formulae recur, and all three are addressed to the apostles:

  • The "petite pro" petition — a direct request that the apostles intercede for a named person: "Paule et Petre petite pro Victore" ("Paul and Peter, intercede for Victor"); "Paule et Petre petite pro nobis omnibus" ("Paul and Peter, intercede for us all"); and, doubling the verb, "Paule et Petre … petite et rogate" for one Eros ("Paul and Peter, intercede and pray for Eros").2
  • The "in mente habete" petition — "remember us / keep us in mind": "Petre et Paule in mente habete nos" ("Peter and Paul, keep us in mind"), and the still more explicit "in mente habete … in orationibus vestris" ("keep [us] in mind in your prayers") — a request, in so many words, to be remembered in the apostles' prayers.3
  • The refrigerium note — a record of the commemorative meal held at the spot: "Petro et Paulo Tomius Coelius refrigerium fecit" ("To Peter and Paul, Tomius Coelius held a banquet"); "XIIII kal(endas) apriles refrigeravi Parthenius" (a dated refrigerium); and the two senses fused, "Petre et Paule in mente abete Ursinum in refrigerium" ("Peter and Paul, keep Ursinus in mind, in [the eternal] refreshment") — where refrigerium has shifted from the literal funeral banquet to the otherworldly rest petitioned for the dead.4

The date. The graffiti are dated not by their own wording but by the archaeological sequence. The triclia was built over a filled-in cemetery in the middle of the third century and was demolished when Constantine raised the Basilica Apostolorum over the site (c. 320s); the petitions scratched on its plaster therefore span roughly seventy years, c. 250/260 to c. 310/320 — Carletti's range is "from the years 250–260 down to about the first decade or two of the fourth century."5 That the apostles' bodies were briefly translated here in 258 (during the Valerian persecution) is an old tradition often attached to the site, but it is disputed and the dating of the graffiti does not depend on it: the Depositio Martyrum independently calendars a 29 June cult of Peter in catacumbas on the Appian Way, fixing organised veneration here in the third century whatever the relics did.6

How the evidence reads — and the counter-readings

The mainstream reading: genuine invocation. The standard interpreters take the petitions at face value. Carletti finds in them "the spontaneous and almost confidential habit of an interpersonal relationship with the two apostles," visitors asking Peter and Paul — treated as heavenly advocates — to intercede for the living and the dead; the Oxford Cult of Saints in Late Antiquity project catalogues them as among the earliest evidence for the cult of the apostle-martyrs, "at which the apostolic martyrs were evidently invoked."7 On the invocation / intercession-belief distinction these fall cleanly on the invocation side: they are not third-person statements that the saints pray for us, but second-person addresses asking the saints to do so. As a piece of evidence that invocation was practised, they are about as clean as the epigraphic record gets.

Three counter-readings test how much they prove:

  1. "It is only the funeral banquet, not prayer." The graffiti are entangled with the refrigerium, the Greco-Roman commemorative meal for the dead — Graydon Snyder and, more provocatively, Ramsay MacMullen read the whole complex as a popular Christianity continuous with the pagan cult of the dead, the apostles folded into an inherited rite (MacMullen goes as far as "ancestor worship").8 This locates the practice in its real cultural matrix, and it rightly warns against reading a later, refined theology of invocation back into a third-century picnic. But it does not dissolve the address: even the meal-graffiti petition the apostles — "keep Ursinus in mind," "intercede for us all," "in your prayers." Whatever the banquet's pagan ancestry, the words on the wall ask the apostles to pray. The continuity-with-paganism reading sharpens the context; it does not turn a petition into something other than a petition.
  2. "Address need not be invocation." One might urge that "remember us" is the etiquette of a memorial meal rather than a true prayer. The "in orationibus vestris" formula closes this gap: to ask to be kept "in your prayers" is, explicitly, to ask the apostles to pray — an intercession-request, not a graveside courtesy. And the "petite pro" verb is the ordinary Latin of asking someone to intercede. The address is petitionary by its own grammar.
  3. "The apostles at their shrine are not 'the saints' in general." This is the real limit, and it is conceded. The petitions are to the two apostle-martyrs, at the place of their relics, within the rising martyr-cult — not yet the generalised invocation of any holy person that the fourth century develops. They establish that the faithful invoked these saints by c. 260; they do not by themselves establish a broader or apostolic-age practice. That inference is exactly what the development question leaves contested — but it is a limit on the scope of the conclusion, not a defeat of it.

Adversarial assessment

The narrow claim survives every counter-reading. These are written, datable, second-person petitions asking Peter and Paul to intercede, produced by ordinary Christians at Rome from the second half of the third century; that is the unanimous reading of the editors who have actually worked the corpus (Ferrua, Carletti, Felle, the CSLA catalogue), and the revisionist accounts (Snyder, MacMullen) dispute the theological tidiness of the practice, not the fact of the address. So invocation — in the strict sense the whole timeline turns on, the living addressing a saint and asking his prayers — is attested epigraphically a century before the Cappadocian homilies, and the apologists' silence cannot be read as the practice's absence.

The conclusion must be held to its scope, and overreach in either direction flagged. It is more than the intercession-belief alone (these are genuine invocations, not just statements that the saints pray), and the proponent who cites them is right to. But it is less than "multiple developed prayers to the saints in the third century" sometimes implies: it is the cult of two apostles at one relic-shrine, inseparable from the funerary refrigerium, beginning c. 250 — not the generalised invocation of any saint and not apostolic. The graffiti do what a flagship piece of evidence should: they make "invented in the 380s" indefensible. They do not, by themselves, carry the further claim that invocation is apostolic — which is why the larger development-vs-apostolicity question remains genuinely contested even though this particular argument is sound.

Assessment

Sound — for what it actually claims. The San Sebastiano triclia graffiti are genuine, datable, second-person invocations of Peter and Paul, asking the apostles' intercession for named living and dead petitioners, scratched by ordinary pilgrims c. 250/260 – c. 320. This is the mainstream epigraphic reading (ICUR / Ferrua, Carletti, Felle, the Oxford Cult of Saints project), and it is not undone by the refrigerium-and-cult-of-the-dead context the revisionists rightly stress, since even the banquet-graffiti address and petition the apostles. As evidence that invocation in the strict sense — the living addressing a saint, not merely believing the saints pray — was a third-century reality, the graffiti are decisive, and they refute any claim that the practice was invented in the late fourth century. Their limits are equally real and belong to the sibling argument: this is the cult of the two apostle-martyrs at their Roman shrine, entangled with an inherited funerary rite, and it begins c. 250, not in the apostolic age — so the graffiti settle that invocation is older than the Cappadocians without settling that it is apostolic or universal, which is why the broader development question stays contested while this one stands.
