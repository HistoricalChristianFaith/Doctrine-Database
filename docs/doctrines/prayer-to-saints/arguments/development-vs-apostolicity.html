Is invocation of the saints a fourth-century development, or an older practice the apologists didn't record?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — genuinely disputed, strong by one discipline's lights and weak by another's. By the history of the cult of saints the development reading is well-grounded: the public, liturgical practice of invoking the saints crystallises in the later fourth century (Peter Brown: the cult "took rise" then), Origen reserves prayer in the full sense to God, and Augustine is a brake, not a proponent. But the continuity reply has real evidence — second-person petitions to the apostles in the mid-third-century catacombs, the disputed Sub Tuum Praesidium, Hippolytus's address to the three youths c. 204 — and a forceful argument from silence: a practice does not go from "pagan" to near-universal, uncontested, in a century without older roots. Both sides overstate their flagship evidence; no clear winner · back to timeline · advanced by Gavin Ortlund (development) against a "Protestant inquiring into the Ancient Church" (continuity)

Related arguments: invocation of the saints — see also praying to the saints vs. praying with them, the definitional distinction this dating question depends on.

An argument page weighs a single sub-claim adduced in support of a main interpretation — a proof-text, a parallel, a historical thesis — and renders an adversarial assessment of the interpretation it serves. Unlike a person-detail page it sits across the timeline rather than at one date. Every factual claim is sourced; the proponents' own words are quoted verbatim.

The claim

The question is a head-to-head between two readings of the same evidence, both stated in one YouTube exchange. Gavin Ortlund argues the practice is a genuine post-apostolic development:

I'd say the ambivalence/unfamiliarity of Origen, and the strictures/warnings of later figures like Augustine on certain forms of the practice, and the generally mild nature of a lot of earliest examples of the practice, fits with my proposal of development. It genuinely looks like its forming, coming to be, in the mid to late patristic era.

— Gavin Ortlund, replying in the thread under his own video, Truth Unites (YouTube).1 Against him, a "Protestant inquiring into the Ancient Church" reads the same silence the other way:

It is hard for me to imagine that within 100 years, the practice is viewed as pagan to basically universal across the entire known church and is in almost all the bulwarks of Orthodoxy. … It would seem that the Apologetics writers of the 1st, 2nd, [and] 3rd centuries just didn't touch on it as much. … many believed that the saints were praying for us, there is just no explicit evidence that people like Cyprian and Iranaeus [Irenaeus] prayed to the saints. However, there is nothing that says they didn't.

— the inquirer, same thread.2 The disagreement is not over the facts but over what the silence of the first three centuries means: the forming of a new practice (Ortlund), or the non-treatment of one already present (the inquirer). Both arguments presuppose the invocation / intercession-belief distinction — they are disputing the date of invocation, not of the belief that the saints pray for us, which both grant is early.

The case for development

The developed, public, liturgical practice of invoking the saints is demonstrably a fourth-century flowering. The historian of the cult, Peter Brown, frames it exactly so: "Christianity … was born without such a cult; it took rise and that rise needs chronicling" — and he dates the rise, with its shrines, patronage, and praesentia, to the fourth century, while explicitly refusing to dismiss it as vulgar superstition (it engaged Ambrose, Augustine, the Gregorys, Paulinus, Prudentius).3 The first unambiguous direct invocations of a saint in a public homily belong to the later fourth-century Cappadocians: Gregory of Nyssa, in his Encomium on Theodore (c. 380s), turns from the congregation to the martyr — "leave what you are doing up there for a little while … Intercede on behalf of your homeland with our common King" — counted as the first recorded invocation of a soldier-saint as wartime defender; Gregory of Nazianzus addresses Cyprian in Oration 24 (379/380); Basil hails the Forty Martyrs as "common friends of the human race, strong ambassadors."4 Behind that flowering, the two greatest theologians on either side of it are cautious at precisely the point the practice would later occupy. Origen (d. c. 253) reserves prayer "in its full meaning" for "the God and Father of All, not even to Christ himself," routing every petition "through the High Priest" — affirming that the saints pray for us, but withholding worship-prayer from any creature.5 Augustine (d. 430), at the very turn of the century when invocation is supposed to be ancient and universal, bars invocation-in-worship to the martyrs ("they are not invoked by the sacrificing priest") and doubts the dead even perceive earthly affairs ("if so great Patriarchs were ignorant what was doing towards the People …").6 Tellingly, the Catholic theorist of doctrinal development, John Henry Newman, concedes the practice is not explicit in the earliest centuries and defends it as a legitimate development rather than an apostolic datum — so even the sympathetic frame grants the lateness it then justifies.7

The case for continuity

The continuity reply does not deny the fourth-century flowering; it denies that the flowering is the beginning. First, the argument from silence has real force in this genre. The apologists of the first three centuries wrote to pagans, defending Christianity against the charge of impiety and ridiculing the worship of dead men and heroes; their occasions gave them little reason to describe the church's internal devotional life, so the absence of a discussion of invocation is weak evidence of the absence of the practice — "there is nothing that says they didn't." Second, the silence is not in fact total: genuine second-person petitions to the saints predate the Cappadocian homilies. The graffiti at the apostles' memorial on the Appian Way (the Ad Catacumbas / San Sebastiano site), mid-third to early-fourth century, address Peter and Paul directly — "Paule et Petre petite pro nobis omnibus" ("Paul and Peter, intercede for us all"), with dozens of like petitions scratched by ordinary pilgrims.8 The Sub Tuum Praesidium, the earliest petitionary prayer to Mary, is on its papyrus's oldest proposed date (Lobel: "not later than the third century") a fully-formed second-person invocation — "Beneath thy compassion we take refuge … do not despise our petitions."9 And Hippolytus of Rome, c. 204, breaks off his commentary to address the three youths of Daniel: "remember me, I entreat you, that I also may obtain the same lot of martyrdom with you."10 Third, the very speed and lack of controversy of the fourth-century spread tells against pure novelty: a practice that went from absent to near-universal across the Cappadocians, Ambrose, Ephrem, Cyril of Jerusalem, and Chrysostom — and was assumed in a synodical letter of the Council of Constantinople (381), which asks that God act "by the prayers of the Saints" — without provoking the controversy that real innovations attract (compare how Aerius's denial of prayer for the dead, c. 355, was promptly catalogued among the heresies) is better explained as the surfacing of something older than as an invention.11

Adversarial assessment

What is strong on each side. The development reading owns the visible record: the developed practice — invocation in public homily and liturgy, the shrine-cult, the saint as patron — is incontestably a fourth-century phenomenon, and this is mainstream scholarship, not a Protestant special plea (Brown). Origen's reservation and Augustine's brake are real and awkward for the continuity side: at the two hinges of the period, East and West, the greatest theologians decline to endorse worship-prayer to creatures, and Augustine even doubts the dead perceive us. And the inquirer himself concedes the decisive datum — "no explicit evidence that … Cyprian and Irenaeus prayed to the saints." Yet the continuity reply lands its own blows. The argument from silence is unusually strong here, because the silent witnesses belong to a genre (anti-pagan apologetic) with no occasion to discuss internal devotion; and the silence is punctured by real pre-Cappadocian invocation (the catacomb petitions, at minimum), so "invented in the 380s" is simply false. The uncontested rapidity of the spread is a genuine datum the development reading must explain.

Where each overstates its flagship evidence.

  1. "Viewed as pagan" overstates the silence (continuity side). The apologists ridiculed the pagan cult of dead heroes; they did not address, and so did not condemn as pagan, the Christian invocation of the saints. So the dramatic "from pagan to universal in 100 years" frame imports a hostility the sources do not record — the honest description is "from unattested to universal," which is weaker for continuity than "from condemned to universal" would be.
  2. The third-century pillars are softer than asserted (continuity side). The catacomb petitions are to the apostles at their shrine — the martyr-cult at a relic-site, not yet the general invocation of any saint — and the headline Sub Tuum date is genuinely disputed: its editor C. H. Roberts thought it "almost incredible" before the later fourth century, and Hans Förster argues for the sixth or seventh.12 Hippolytus's apostrophe is a one-off rhetorical flourish to Old Testament figures within a martyrdom-exhortation, not evidence of a settled cult. The continuity case has real roots, but thinner and later than "multiple prayers in the third century" implies.
  3. "Origen's ambivalence" is weaker support than it looks (development side). Origen does not merely hesitate over invocation; in the same passage he permits "request to saints … should some Paul or Peter appear," reserving only worship-prayer to God. So Origen attests an early acceptance of addressing the saints in the lower key, which cuts toward continuity as much as his reservation cuts toward development.13 And Brown's "the cult took rise in the fourth century" is a claim about the public cult of saints as a social institution, not a precise finding that no one invoked a saint before 350 — so it should not be pressed as proof of a hard fourth-century origin for invocation as such.

The two readings are, in the end, strong in different registers. As a claim about the developed, public, attested practice, development wins: that is a fourth-century flowering, full stop. As a claim about the origin of invocation as an act, neither wins: the third-century evidence is real but thin and partly disputed, the apostolic-era evidence is silent in a genre that would be silent either way, and the rapid uncontested spread is a double-edged datum. The dispute is partly about burden of proof — whether silence in apologetic literature counts for absence — which no evidence on either side can discharge.

Assessment

Contested. The question has no clear winner because the two disciplines that bear on it pull opposite ways. By the history of the cult of saints, the developed and public practice of invoking the saints is a later-fourth-century flowering — mainstream scholarship (Brown), with the Cappadocians supplying the first unambiguous invocations, Origen reserving worship-prayer to God, Augustine a brake, and even the Catholic development-theorist (Newman) conceding the lateness. By the reading of the earliest evidence, invocation has genuine third-century roots — the catacomb petitions to Peter and Paul, Hippolytus's address to the three youths, and (on its older dating) the Sub Tuum — and the silence of the first three centuries falls in a genre (anti-pagan apologetic) that would be silent whether or not the practice existed, so it cannot be read as absence. Each side overstates its flagship evidence: the "pagan to universal in 100 years" frame imports a hostility the apologists never voiced, the third-century pillars are softer and more disputed than claimed, and "Origen's ambivalence" understates that Origen actually permits request to the saints. The honest verdict is a real fourth-century flowering of a practice with genuine but thin earlier roots, the apostolic-era record neither attesting nor excluding it — strong development by the cult-historian's lights, a real continuity-and-silence case by the early-evidence reader's, and a genuine standoff between them.
