Clement of Alexandria on remarriage

Dates: c. 200 (Stromateis III) · Wikipedia
Summary: Clement holds the balanced catholic middle against two extremes at once. In Stromateis III he answers the Gnostic encratites, who forbade all marriage as evil, and the libertines, who thought conduct indifferent — keeping second marriage lawful as the apostle’s concession while still preferring continence and the single marriage.

Claims & evidence

The church stands between two errors — the libertines and the encratites

…teach that one ought to live on the principle that it is a matter of indifference whether one does right or wrong…

— Clement, Stromateis III.5.40, trans. Oulton & Chadwick, Alexandrian Christianity (LCC 2). Clement sorts the heresies into opposite errors — the libertines who make right and wrong indifferent, and those who “impiously affect continence” — and sets the church’s teaching on marriage between them. (Book III is the one book the old Ante-Nicene Fathers left untranslated in Latin; the standard English is the Oulton–Chadwick version cited here.)

Second marriage is the apostle’s concession — but the single marriage is preferred

…if from sympathy the apostle allows a man a second marriage because he cannot control himself and burns with passion…

— Clement, Stromateis III.12.82, trans. Oulton & Chadwick, Alexandrian Christianity (LCC 2). Clement reads remarriage exactly as Paul does — a concession to those who “burn,” not a good to be sought — and cites the apostle’s own ranking:

The wife is bound to her husband so long as he is alive, but if he dies, she is free to marry, only in the Lord. But she is happier… if she remains as she [is].

— Clement, Stromateis III.12.82 (quoting 1 Cor. 7:39–40), trans. Oulton & Chadwick.

Notes

  • Clement is the eastern counterpart of the Latin mean later held by Ambrose and Augustine: remarriage tolerated as concession, continence preferred, and the encratite prohibition of marriage rejected as heresy — the same encratite pole Ambrose glances at when he notes the heretics “who forbid” second marriages.
  • Against Clement’s deliberate mean, his younger Carthaginian contemporary Tertullian was moving the other way — toward the very prohibition Clement identified as an encratite error.
