The Council of Neocaesarea on remarriage

Dates: c. 315 · Wikipedia
Summary: Neocaesarea shows the deprecation of remarriage hardening into canon law — but in a form that proves the point: the second marriage is penanced, not forbidden. A presbyter may not even attend a digamist’s wedding-feast, yet the digamist is a penitent, not a fornicator, and his marriage stands.

Claims & evidence

A presbyter may not feast at a second wedding — the digamist is “worthy of penance”

A presbyter shall not be a guest at the nuptials of persons contracting a second marriage; for, since the digamist is worthy of penance, what kind of a presbyter shall he be, who, by being present at the feast, sanctioned the marriage?

— Council of Neocaesarea, canon 7, NPNF2 vol. 14. Canon 3 likewise sets a term of penance for those “who fall into many marriages,” the penance shortened by their “manifest” repentance. The judgment underneath is a defect to be cleansed, not a crime to be punished.

The church penanced digamists — but never called their marriage illicit

The standard commentary on the canon draws the distinction the whole timeline turns on: the church “never disapproved of, nor reputed second… marriages illicit,” yet “enjoined a penance upon digamists.”1 That is precisely the mean the church held against both poles: remarriage is lawful (against Tertullian) but deprecated (against those who would call it simply a good). The universal bar on ordaining the twice-married rests on 1 Tim. 3:2; the temporary penance on the laity was an eastern discipline.

Notes

  • Neocaesarea institutionalizes what Paul began (the twice-married barred from office) and Hermas voiced (no sin, but lesser honour): the deprecation is now canon law, the prohibition still refused.
  • The eastern penitential discipline is spelled out in numbers by Basil of Caesarea a generation later — a year’s penance for a digamist, longer for a trigamist — and softened at Laodicea.
