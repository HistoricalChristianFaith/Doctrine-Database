Origen of Alexandria on remarriage

Dates: c. 245 (Commentary on Matthew) · Wikipedia
Summary: Origen holds the same permitted-but-inferior mean as Clement, and reasons his way to its sharpest tension. Remarriage after a spouse’s death is “no reproach” and lawful; yet the twice-married are barred from church office — a bar Origen frankly finds hard to justify against a chaste digamist, and files, with Paul’s other indulgences, among the “laws given by concession to human weakness.”

Claims & evidence

Remarriage after widowhood is no reproach

Commenting on the law of marriage in Romans 7 — the wife bound only while her husband lives — Origen grants that “there is no reproach for a woman whose first husband has died if she enters a second marriage,” reading the passage’s deeper sense of the soul freed to be joined to Christ.1 On the lawfulness of the widow’s remarriage Origen stands with Paul, not with Tertullian.

Yet the twice-married are shut out of church office

…for Paul wishes no one of those of the church, who has attained to any eminence beyond the many, as is attained in the administration of the sacraments, to make trial of a second marriage.

— Origen, Commentary on Matthew XIV.22, ANF vol. 9. Origen grounds the disqualification in the “husband of one wife” of the Pastorals (1 Tim. 3:2) — the same texts Paul used — and reads the apostolic writings as “prohibit[ing] those who have been twice married from ruling over the church.”

The bar as a “concession to human weakness” — and Origen’s own perplexity

Now, when we saw that some who have been married twice may be much better than those who have been married once, we were perplexed why Paul does not at all permit those who have been twice married to be appointed to ecclesiastical dignities…

— Origen, Commentary on Matthew XIV.22, ANF vol. 9. He heads the next section “Some Laws Given by Concession to Human Weakness” (XIV.23), classing the whole apparatus of permitted-but-deprecated marriage among Paul’s indulgences. The remarriage is allowed; it simply belongs to the weaker, not the perfect, Christian — permission and deprecation held together, exactly the mean the church kept.

Notes

  • The office-bar is the constant of the whole tradition — Paul, Origen, Neocaesarea, and the Council in Trullo all keep it — while the laity are never forbidden to remarry. Origen is valuable precisely because he questions the bar rather than merely enforcing it, and still keeps the marriage itself lawful.
  • Origen is sometimes reported (via his Homily on Jeremiah 20.4) as an early witness to the technical sense of digamos, the remarried widow or widower; that report is secondhand and no primary text is set here.
