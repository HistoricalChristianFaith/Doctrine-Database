Augustine of Hippo on remarriage

Dates: c. 414 (On the Good of Widowhood) · Wikipedia
Summary: Augustine states the settled Latin verdict. In On the Good of Widowhood he refuses to condemn any lawful marriage — second, third, or more — and names Tertullian as the man who overstepped by attacking second marriages “as though unlawful.” The single life is not better because marriage is bad; it is a greater good honoured by the submission of a real good.

Claims & evidence

Second marriages are lawful and not condemned — Tertullian named as the man who overstepped

…second marriages are not condemned, but are set in lower honor… For hence, specially, the heresies of the Cataphryges and of the Novatians swelled, which Tertullian also, inflated with cheeks full of sound not of wisdom, whilst with railing tooth he attacks second marriages, as though unlawful, which the Apostle with sober mind allows to be altogether lawful.

— Augustine, On the Good of Widowhood 6, NPNF1 vol. 3. Augustine draws the exact line Tertullian crossed: the apostle allows the second marriage as “altogether lawful,” and to attack it “as though unlawful” is the error of the Cataphrygians (the Montanists) — the very charge he lays against Tertullian in his catalogue of heresies.

He dares condemn no marriage — not even a third or fourth

Men are wont to move a question concerning a third or fourth marriage, and even more numerous marriages than this. On which to make answer strictly, I dare neither to condemn any marriage, nor to take from these the shame of their great number… For who am I, that I should think that that must be defined which I see that the Apostle hath not defined?

— Augustine, On the Good of Widowhood 15, NPNF1 vol. 3. Where Tertullian bound the whole church beyond Paul, Augustine deliberately declines to define what the apostle left undefined — the settled catholic restraint.

Good and better: “despise, not condemn”

…marriage chastity is a good, but widowed continence is a better good. Therefore this better good is honored by the submission of that other, not that other condemned by the praise of this that is better.

— Augustine, On the Good of Widowhood 6, NPNF1 vol. 3. To the widow herself Augustine puts it as a counsel of the will: “let this be your mind, not that you condemn them, but that you despise them” (On the Good of Widowhood 14) — a personal preference, never a verdict of sin against those who remarry.

Notes

  • Augustine’s image of the graded goods is scriptural: Ruth, twice-married, is “blessed,” and Anna, the long widow, “more blessed” (On the Good of Widowhood 5, 10) — good outranked by better, neither of them evil.
  • This is the same Augustine who, cataloguing Tertullian among the heresies in De haeresibus 86, set aside the doctrinal charge and rested the heresy on the turn to the Cataphrygians, the ban on second marriages “against apostolic teaching, as though they were fornication,” and the final schism — developed on the remarriage-as-fornication argument.
  • He completes the Latin consensus with Ambrose and Jerome: remarriage lawful and no sin, widowhood the better good.
