Paul the Apostle on remarriage

Dates: c. 55 (1 Corinthians) · Wikipedia
Summary: Paul is the baseline the whole later tradition reads. He plainly permits the widowed to remarry — a widow is “at liberty to be married to whom she will” and is “no adulteress” if she does — yet just as plainly rates the single life the “happier,” and bars the twice-married from church office and the widows’ roll. Permission with a preference: the tension every later witness inherits.

Claims & evidence

Remarriage of the widowed is lawful — she is “no adulteress”

The wife is bound by the law as long as her husband liveth; but if her husband be dead, she is at liberty to be married to whom she will; only in the Lord.

— 1 Corinthians 7:39, 1 Cor. 7:39. Paul says the same in Romans, and there rebuts in advance the very charge Tertullian would later press — that a second union is adultery:

For the woman which hath an husband is bound by the law to her husband so long as he liveth… but if the husband be dead, she is free from that law; so that she is no adulteress, though she be married to another man.

— Romans 7:2–3, Rom. 7:2–3. And of the younger widows he goes further than permission to positive counsel: “I will therefore that the younger women marry, bear children, guide the house” (1 Tim. 5:14).

Yet the single state is the “happier” one, and remarriage a concession

I say therefore to the unmarried and widows, It is good for them if they abide even as I. But if they cannot contain, let them marry: for it is better to marry than to burn.

— 1 Corinthians 7:8–9, 1 Cor. 7:8–9. Of the widow free to remarry he adds, “she is happier if she so abide, after my judgment” (1 Cor. 7:40). Marriage is a remedy against burning and a lawful good; abiding single is the better counsel. The whole later deprecation of remarriage takes its warrant from this ranking.

The twice-married are barred from office and the widows’ roll

A bishop then must be blameless, the husband of one wife…

— 1 Timothy 3:2, 1 Tim. 3:2; and the order of enrolled widows is confined to her who has been “the wife of one man” (1 Tim. 5:9). The Pastorals attach a real disability to the second marriage — exclusion from office and from the widows’ roll — without ever calling the marriage itself sinful. This is the seed of the church’s enduring rule: digamists are barred from the clergy, and their marriage is deprecated, but it is not forbidden or called fornication.

Notes

  • Paul’s two-sidedness is the hinge of the whole timeline. Every later witness leans on one side or the other: Hermas and Augustine keep both (“no sin… yet greater honour”); Athenagoras and Tertullian press the deprecation until it hardens into a ban that Paul’s own “no adulteress” forbids.
  • Tertullian, forced to reckon with 1 Cor. 7:39, argued in On Monogamy 11 that the Greek had been corrupted so as to restrict the permission to women already widowed at their conversion — weighed on the remarriage-as-fornication argument.
