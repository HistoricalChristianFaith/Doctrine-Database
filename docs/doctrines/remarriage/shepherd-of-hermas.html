The Shepherd of Hermas on remarriage

Dates: c. 140 · Wikipedia
Summary: The Shepherd gives the earliest post-apostolic answer, and it reproduces Paul’s own two-sidedness in a single sentence: the widowed who remarry “do not sin,” but those who stay single “gain greater honour and glory with the Lord.” Permission with deprecation — the mainstream from which Tertullian’s later ban departs.

Claims & evidence

Remarriage is no sin, but staying single earns greater honour

If a wife or husband die, and the widower or widow marry, does he or she commit sin? There is no sin in marrying again, said he; but if they remain unmarried, they gain greater honour and glory with the Lord; but if they marry, they do not sin.

— Hermas, The Shepherd, Commandment (Mandate) 4.4, ANF vol. 2. The catechetical framing — a plain question put to the angelic Shepherd, a plain answer — makes the second-century church’s working rule about as explicit as it ever gets: remarriage is licit, celibacy the counsel of perfection. Both halves are held at once, with no hint that the second marriage is shameful, let alone fornication.

Notes

  • Hermas is the baseline against which the timeline’s sharpenings measure. His “do not sin… yet greater honour” is the same balance Augustine restates nearly three centuries later (“not to condemn any marriages… a better good”) — the enduring catholic mean.
  • Against it, Athenagoras (c. 177) already presses the deprecation toward “specious adultery,” and Tertullian (c. 210) turns the counsel into a prohibition — the two poles the mainstream never adopted.
