Basil of Caesarea on remarriage

Dates: c. 375 (the Canonical Epistles) · Wikipedia
Summary: Basil fixes the eastern penitential discipline in numbers. A second marriage draws a year’s penance, a third three or four years; and while he pointedly refuses to dignify the third union with the name “marriage” — calling it “polygamy… rather… limited fornication” — he still receives the penitents back into the church. The severity is real, the toleration equally real.

Claims & evidence

A year’s penance for the second marriage, longer for the third

…one year for digamy (some authorities say two years); for trigamy men are separated for three and often for four years; but this is no longer described as marriage at all, but as polygamy; nay rather as limited fornication.

— Basil, Letter 188 (First Canonical Epistle), canon 4, NPNF2 vol. 8. The sharp word is reserved for the third and further marriages, not the second: the digamist is penanced a year and restored; only serial remarriage draws the name “fornication,” and even then the offender is received “on showing some fruits of repentance.”

Notes

  • Basil’s discipline is the numerical form of the mean the whole church kept: remarriage is a defect to be cleansed by penance, never a bar to communion. It is the same judgment Neocaesarea canonized a generation earlier and the Council in Trullo would confirm three centuries later.
  • His refusal of the name “marriage” to the third union — not the second — marks how far the mainstream stood from Tertullian, who denied the name to the second. Basil’s own canon is cited by name in the Council in Trullo’s canons.
