The Council in Trullo on remarriage

Dates: 692 (the Quinisext Council) · Wikipedia
Summary: Trullo crystallizes the East’s settled discipline and names its guiding principle: a blend of Roman “exact perfection” and Constantinopolitan “kindness and consideration,” so “that neither gentleness fall into licence, nor severity into harshness.” The twice-married are barred from the clergy and their laxity penanced — but the underlying marriage is regulated, not abolished.

Claims & evidence

Mercy and rigour blended — “neither gentleness fall into licence, nor severity into harshness”

…whereas they of the most holy Roman Church purpose to keep the rule of exact perfection, but those who are under the throne of this heaven-protected and royal city keep that of kindness and consideration, so blending both together… that neither gentleness fall into licence, nor severity into harshness…

— Council in Trullo, canon 3, NPNF2 vol. 14. The canon renews the old rule that a man “joined in two marriages after his baptism” cannot be a bishop, presbyter, or deacon, and disciplines clergy who had lapsed into a second marriage — the enduring bar first laid down by Paul. The programmatic middle term — mercy without licence, rigour without harshness — is the East’s own statement of the mean the whole timeline has traced.

The eventual crystallization: a third marriage tolerated under penance, a fourth forbidden

The discipline Trullo confirmed hardened, over the next two centuries, into a fixed Eastern rule — a first marriage the norm, a second a penanced concession, a third tolerated only under penance and age-or-childlessness limits, and a fourth absolutely forbidden. The rule was tested and settled by the tetragamy affair of the emperor Leo VI, who took an uncanonical fourth wife (c. 906) to secure an heir; after the deposition of the patriarch who resisted him, the dispute closed with the Tomos of Union (920), which condemned fourth marriages outright and regulated third ones.1 The often-quoted maxim summing up the graded verdict — that a first marriage is law, a second indulgence, a third transgression, and a fourth beyond the pale — is the East’s crisp final form of the deprecation this timeline began with in Paul.

Notes

  • Trullo confirms, three and a half centuries on, exactly what Neocaesarea and Basil had set down: remarriage deprecated and penanced, the twice-married barred from office, the marriage itself never called simple sin. The East never followed Tertullian’s prohibition of the second marriage; it drew its absolute line only at the fourth.
  • The council’s canon 87, invoking Basil and Jeremiah, treats divorce-and-remarriage (leaving a living spouse) as adultery — a distinct question from the remarriage of the widowed traced on this timeline.
