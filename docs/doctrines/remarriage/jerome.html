Jerome on remarriage

Dates: c. 393 (Against Jovinianus) · Wikipedia
Summary: Jerome is the test case for the line between deprecating remarriage and forbidding it. The age’s fiercest Latin champion of virginity, he quarried Tertullian’s monogamist works and pressed the denigration of marriage so hard in Against Jovinianus that friends withdrew the book from circulation. Yet at the decisive point Jerome refuses to cross where Tertullian crossed: “I do not condemn second, nor third, nor… eighth marriages.”

Claims & evidence

He leaned on Tertullian — and marked the debt

At all events Tertullian, while still a young man, gave himself full play with this subject. And my teacher, Gregory of Nazianzus, discussed virginity and marriage in some Greek verses.

— Jerome, Against Jovinianus I.13, NPNF2 vol. 6. Jerome names Tertullian as a source for his own case against remarriage — the direct line by which Tertullian’s severity reached the Latin West.

But he will not condemn the second marriage — the line he would not cross

‘All things are lawful, but not all things are expedient.’ I do not condemn second, nor third, nor, pardon the expression, eighth marriages… Things that are equally lawful must be weighed in an even balance.

— Jerome, Against Jovinianus I.15, NPNF2 vol. 6. However hard he disparages remarriage, Jerome grants that Paul allowed it and did not cap the number, and so refuses to call it sin. His deprecation is a rhetorical strategy, not a prohibition — and precisely there he parts from Tertullian.

The storm the book raised — and Jerome’s defence

Read, I pray you, and diligently consider the words of the apostle, and you will then see that — with a view to avoid misrepresentation — I have been much more gentle towards married persons than he was disposed to be.

— Jerome, Letter 49 (to Pammachius), 3, NPNF2 vol. 6. When Against Jovinianus was read as condemning marriage outright, Pammachius withdrew copies from circulation; Jerome answered that “words once uttered cannot be recalled” (Letter 49.2) and insisted he had been gentler to the married than Paul was. In Letter 54, urging the widow Furia to remain unmarried, he still calls the point of law “the apostle’s concession of second marriages” (Letter 54.18) — the concession granted even as the counsel drives the other way.

Notes

  • Jerome is the hinge of the assessment on the remarriage-as-fornication argument: when even the man who followed Tertullian furthest draws back at calling remarriage sin, Tertullian’s ban is shown to be a step past the church’s severity, not its expression.
  • He stands between Ambrose (“I do not forbid second marriages”) and Augustine (“not to condemn any marriages”) — the three Latin doctors converging on the same verdict: lawful, but the single life is better.
