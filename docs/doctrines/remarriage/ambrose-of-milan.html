Ambrose of Milan on remarriage

Dates: c. 377 (Concerning Widows) · Wikipedia
Summary: Ambrose is the careful Latin mean. In Concerning Widows he presses the honour of perpetual widowhood as hard as anyone and dissuades widows from remarrying — but he draws the line Tertullian crossed, stating outright that he does not forbid second marriages, only does not advise them, and that the apostle himself does not condemn them.

Claims & evidence

“I do not forbid second marriages, only I do not advise them”

This, however, I say as a counsel, we do not order it as a precept, stirring up the wills of widows rather than binding them; for I do not forbid second marriages, only I do not advise them… I do not forbid second, but do not approve of often repeated marriages, for not everything is expedient which is lawful.

— Ambrose, Concerning Widows 11.68, NPNF2 vol. 10. The counsel is a counsel, not a command: Ambrose stirs the will toward widowhood without binding it, and keeps “lawful” and “expedient” firmly apart — exactly the distinction Tertullian collapsed.

The apostle does not condemn the second marriage

Nor in truth is any one excluded from the devotion of widowhood, if after entering upon a second marriage, which the precepts of the Apostle certainly do not condemn as though the fruit of chastity were lost, if she be again loosed from her husband.

— Ambrose, Concerning Widows 2.10, NPNF2 vol. 10. Ambrose grounds the permission where Paul does — even a twice-married woman, widowed again, may still take up the devotion of widowhood; her chastity is “tardy” but real.

Marriage honourable, chastity more honourable — good and better

It is then lawful to marry, but it is more seemly to abstain, for there are bonds in marriage… Marriage, then, is honourable, but chastity is more honourable, for ‘he that giveth his virgin in marriage doeth well, but he that giveth her not in marriage doeth better.’

— Ambrose, Concerning Widows 11.69, 12.72, NPNF2 vol. 10. The whole treatise sits on the Pauline ranking — a real good outranked by a greater — not on any charge against the lawful union.

Notes

  • Ambrose is the direct Latin bridge to Augustine, whose On the Good of Widowhood settles the same “good, but better” verdict and names Tertullian as the one who overstepped it.
  • In Concerning Widows Ambrose also glances at the heretics “who forbid” second marriages — the encratite pole that Clement of Alexandria had answered two centuries earlier: chastity is recommended, not commanded.
