Tertullian on remarriage

Dates: c. 210 (the monogamist treatises) · Wikipedia
Summary: Tertullian is the hard pole of the Latin tradition. Where Paul permits the widowed to remarry, Tertullian — in his later, openly Montanist works — forbids it outright and brands the second marriage “a species of fornication.” His position sharpened in stages: from the early To His Wife, which urges widowhood while conceding remarriage is lawful, to On Monogamy, which makes it a sin the Paraclete has forbidden.

Claims & evidence

Early (To His Wife, c. 203): remarriage is permitted, but the single marriage is better

…the Apostle… permits marrying indeed, but prefers abstinence; the former on account of the snares of temptations, the latter on account of the straits of the times.

— Tertullian, To His Wife I.3, ANF vol. 4. Written to his own wife against the day of his death, the treatise urges her to remain a widow but concedes that second marriage is a lawful thing, only depreciated — and confines the disability of the twice-married to church office and the widows’ order, which is still the catholic line.

Later (On Exhortation to Chastity 9, c. 208–212): a second marriage is “a species of fornication”

If we look deeply into his meanings, and interpret them, second marriage will have to be termed no other than a species of fornication.

— Tertullian, On Exhortation to Chastity 9, ANF vol. 4. Addressed to a recently widowed friend to dissuade him from remarrying, the chapter reasons from the concupiscence marriage shares with fornication. The counsel of the earlier treatise has now hardened into a prohibition, and the remarriage of a widow or widower is classed as stuprum.

The ground of the ban: the Paraclete has abrogated what Paul allowed

…why may not the Paraclete, too, have abrogated an indulgence which Paul granted — because second marriage withal ‘was not from the beginning’…?

— Tertullian, On Monogamy 14, ANF vol. 4. When he has to reckon with Paul’s plain permission, Tertullian does not out-argue it but overrides it by a higher warrant: the New Prophecy as restitutor of a stricter original rule, licensed to cancel an apostolic concession. Where that will not serve, he claims at On Monogamy 11 that the Greek of 1 Cor. 7:39 was corrupted “through the either crafty or simple alteration of two syllables” so as to restrict Paul’s permission to women already widowed at their conversion.

Notes

  • Whether Tertullian’s ban is best read as a severe reading of a real early deprecation of remarriage or as a false doctrine against apostolic teaching is weighed on the remarriage-as-fornication argument — Augustine’s central named count when he catalogued Tertullian among the heresies. Assessment there: contested.
  • Tertullian’s wider place in the church — whether his rigorism amounts to heresy or intramural severity — is the subject of the “Was Tertullian a Heretic?” timeline.
  • He stands at the extreme end of a real spectrum: Athenagoras had already called remarriage a “specious adultery” a generation before the New Prophecy, and the church went on barring digamists from the clergy and penancing them — but it never followed Tertullian in forbidding the laity to remarry or calling their marriages fornication. Even Jerome, who quarried these very treatises, drew back at that line.
