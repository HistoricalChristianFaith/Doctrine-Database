Was Tertullian a Heretic?

Tertullian of Carthage (c. 155–c. 220) was the first great Christian theologian to write in Latin, and the Latin Church has never quite known what to do with him. He gave the West the words in which it would state its central mystery — trinitas, persona, substantia — along with much else of its theological Latin (sacramentum, satisfactio); he wrote the fiercest surviving defence of the rule of faith against the heretics, the Prescription Against Heretics, whose argument the Church went on using against its enemies for centuries. And he is quoted with approval, on point after point, by the same tradition that never gave him the title “saint,” listed his books among those “not received,” and paired him with Origen as the standing warning about brilliant men who fell. For at some point in middle life Tertullian went over to the New Prophecy — the ecstatic movement out of Phrygia its enemies called Montanism — and, on one later report, ended in a rigorist splinter of his own, the Tertullianistae.

The subject of this page is not Tertullian's theology as such but the verdict passed on him: how, and how far, the Church came to reckon its first Latin theologian a heretic. Here the contrast with Origen — his natural companion, the two of them paired by Vincent of Lérins as the type of the learned teacher undone by his own gifts — is the heart of the story. Origen was anathematized by name, three centuries after his death, by a string of emperors and popes and (on the usual reading) an ecumenical council; Tertullian never was. No synod tried him, no council condemned him, no anathema carries his name. His “condemnation” is reputational, not conciliar — assembled after the fact out of a heresy-catalogue entry, a book-list, a biographical notice, and the drift of later citation — and that absence of a trial is itself the thing to watch: this is a timeline of how a verdict forms when no court ever sits.

Its “witnesses” are therefore the datable judgements and reports, earliest first: Tertullian's own later works, in which he embraces the Paraclete and turns on the “soulish” Christians who will not; the silence of the generation after him, above all Cyprian of Carthage, who is said to have read him every day and yet never named him; Eusebius, who knew him as a master of Roman law; the anonymous heresy-list that goes under his own name; Jerome, who supplies almost every biographical “fact” and most of the damage; Augustine, who first gives him a numbered entry among the heresies and the only notice of the Tertullianistae; Vincent of Lérins; the book-list called the Decretum Gelasianum; the medieval and Reformation reception; and the modern reassessment. Two questions run through it all: what exactly was he faulted for, and was it fair? As with Origen, the deepest paradox is that the tradition never stopped reading him — Tertullian stands as a witness on some twenty of this wiki's belief-timelines, from the origin of the soul and infant baptism to the Eucharist and the perpetual virginity of Mary — a man condemned in the handbook and quoted on every other page.

Timeline

Tertullian builds the Latin rule of faith — and coins the word “Trinity” (c. 200–210)

Before he was anyone's example of a fall, Tertullian was the Church's sharpest weapon against the heretics. The Prescription Against Heretics, written about 203, borrows a term from Roman law — the praescriptio, a plea that stops a lawsuit before its merits are ever heard — and turns it against the Gnostics: the Scriptures belong to the Church that has held the rule of faith from the apostles, and heretics, being no part of that succession, have no standing to argue from them at all. He states that rule in a single long sentence — there is “one only God, and… He is none other than the Creator of the world, who produced all things out of nothing through His own Word, first of all sent forth” — and sets philosophy against it in the line every later century quoted: “What indeed has Athens to do with Jerusalem? What concord is there between the Academy and the Church?”1

One passage of that book reads, in retrospect, like a prophecy written against its own author. Answering the objection that trusted Christians sometimes defect, Tertullian asks how it is “that this woman or that man, who were the most faithful, the most prudent, and the most approved in the church, have gone over to the other side” — and answers that a defector was never truly approved, since “no one is a Christian but he who perseveres even to the end.” “But what if a bishop, if a deacon, if a widow, if a virgin, if a doctor, if even a martyr, have fallen from the rule of faith,” he presses, “will heresies on that account appear to possess the truth? Do we prove the faith by the persons, or the persons by the faith?”2 It is the exact principle — that a teacher's later lapse leaves his earlier truth untouched — on which the Church would later go on reading Tertullian himself. He wrote the rule for his own case before he was the case.

The clearest single measure of how much the tradition owes him is that its very word for God is partly his. In Against Praxeas, written about 210 against a Roman teacher who had collapsed Father, Son and Spirit into one person, Tertullian set down the formula the Latin West has used ever since. The one God is guarded by “the mystery of the dispensation… which distributes the Unity into a Trinity, placing in their order the three Persons — the Father, the Son, and the Holy Ghost: three, however, not in condition, but in degree; not in substance, but in form; not in power, but in aspect; yet of one substance, and of one condition, and of one power.”3 Trinitas, persona, and substantia in their theological senses are, in Latin, largely his coinages. And here the paradox tightens to a single book, for the treatise that gave the West its doctrine of the Trinity is itself an openly Montanist one. Its author writes as a man “better instructed by the Paraclete, who leads men… into all truth”; he reports that he and his “subsequently withdrew from the carnally-minded… on our acknowledgment and maintenance of the Paraclete”; and he blames Praxeas for having, at Rome, “put to flight the Paraclete” even as he “crucified the Father.”4 The vocabulary the Church kept and the allegiance it came to condemn issue from the same pen, in the same years — which is the difficulty the rest of this timeline works out.

Tertullian’s own testimony: the turn to the New Prophecy (c. 205–212)

The primary evidence that Tertullian “lapsed” is not a synod or a rival’s report but his own late works, in which the Paraclete promised in John’s Gospel becomes a living, still-speaking authority. Its office, he now argues, is to carry Christian discipline past what the apostles left: in On the Veiling of Virgins he lays down that “the reason why the Lord sent the Paraclete was, that, since human mediocrity was unable to take in all things at once, discipline should, little by little, be directed, and ordained, and carried on to perfection, by that Vicar of the Lord, the Holy Spirit,” righteousness having been “first in a rudimentary state” and only “now, through the Paraclete… settling into maturity.”5 He quotes the Phrygian prophets as though they were Scripture: in On Exhortation to Chastity “through the holy prophetess Prisca the Gospel is thus preached: that ‘the holy minister knows how to minister sanctity.’”6 The absolute dates are contested and the “Catholic” and “Montanist” works cannot be cleanly sorted; the sequence followed here is the relative chronology of T. D. Barnes, which places the openly New-Prophecy treatises late, roughly 207–212.7

From this authority he presses a hard disciplinary programme — no second marriage, longer and stricter fasts, no flight in persecution, the veiling of unmarried girls — and divides Christendom into two camps by their reception of the Spirit. In On Monogamy the line is drawn from his own side: “Among us… whom the recognition of spiritual gifts entitles to be deservedly called Spiritual, continence is as religious as licence is modest… We admit one marriage, just as we do one God. But to the Psychics, since they receive not the Spirit, the things which are the Spirit’s are not pleasing.”8 The psychici — the “soulish,” merely natural Christians — are the ordinary Catholics who will not follow the Paraclete into the stricter rule; the spiritales are those who will. It is a party name, and Tertullian wields it against the great body of the Church.

At its sharpest the polemic turns on the hierarchy itself. Opening On Modesty, he answers a bishop who had relaxed the penance for adultery: “I hear that there has even been an edict set forth, and a peremptory one too. The Pontifex Maximus — that is, the bishop of bishops — issues an edict: ‘I remit, to such as have discharged (the requirements of) repentance, the sins both of adultery and of fornication.’” He denies that the power of the keys is the bishops’ to use so: the Church “will forgive sins: but (it will be) the Church of the Spirit, by means of a spiritual man; not the Church which consists of a number of bishops.”9 This is the passage later readers found hardest to forgive, and the nearest he ever comes to setting a “church of the Spirit” over against the institutional Church.

Yet on the decisive question — did he ever place himself outside the Church? — his own texts pull the other way. By his account the quarrel is over discipline, not the creed: in On Fasting he insists the New Prophecies are rejected “not that Montanus and Priscilla and Maximilla preach another God, nor that they disjoin Jesus Christ (from God), nor that they overturn any particular rule of faith or hope, but that they plainly teach more frequent fasting than marrying.”10 And in On the Soul the setting is an ordinary congregation: “We have now amongst us a sister whose lot it has been to be favoured with sundry gifts of revelation, which she experiences in the Spirit by ecstatic vision amidst the sacred rites of the Lord’s day in the church… After the people are dismissed at the conclusion of the sacred services, she is in the regular habit of reporting to us whatever things she may have seen in vision.”11 The prophesying happens inside the normal Sunday assembly, not a breakaway conventicle. Even the withdrawal he elsewhere claims — that he and his “subsequently withdrew from the carnally-minded” over the Paraclete (quoted above) — reads, on this evidence, as a rigorist faction within the Carthaginian church rather than a departure from it: an ecclesiola in ecclesia, on the reading urged by William Tabbernee and Douglas Powell. The notice of a separate sect, the Tertullianistae, surfaces only much later, in Augustine — further down this timeline — and never from Tertullian’s own hand.12

The generation after: Cyprian reads him daily, the heresy-hunters pass him over (c. 220–260)

The most revealing witness to Tertullian’s standing is the one hardest to read: the generation that followed him left, so far as survives, almost nothing about him at all — and the silence is of a peculiar kind. No contemporary or near-contemporary calls him a heretic. No synod tried him; no rival’s treatise answers him by name; no third-century heresy-catalogue lists a “Tertullianist” error. What the century does show is that he went on being read — hard, and by the most orthodox hands in his own city — while his name went unspoken.

The chief case is Cyprian, bishop of Carthage from 248 and the African church’s martyr-hero, who leaned on Tertullian throughout and never once names him. The whole framework of Cyprian’s treatise on the Lord’s Prayer follows Tertullian’s On Prayer, clause by clause; his book on patience reworks Tertullian’s On Patience, and his tract on the dress of virgins draws on the On the Apparel of Women — each recast into smoother, more “literary” Latin, and in each the source goes unacknowledged.13 That Cyprian read him at all we know from a single, much later source: Jerome, writing about 393, reports that he had met in Italy an old man named Paul of Concordia who as a youth had been secretary to the aged Cyprian, and who said that Cyprian “was accustomed never to pass a day without reading Tertullian, and that he frequently said to him, ‘Give me the master’ [da magistrum], meaning by this, Tertullian.”14 Jerome is the only source for the anecdote, at second hand and a century and a half after the fact; it should be weighed as such. But the silent dependence in Cyprian’s own text needs no anecdote to establish it, and it is genuinely two-edged. If Tertullian were by then a notorious heretic in Carthage, it is striking that the bishop read him daily and built his own treatises on him; if he were in good standing, it is equally striking that so devoted a reader never names him. The natural reading — that the master’s turn to the New Prophecy had put his name under enough of a cloud to make open citation awkward even for an admirer — is plausible and often urged, but it is an inference we supply, not something Cyprian says. The bare datum is silent, constant use.15

The same blank appears where a verdict would be loudest: in the heresy-hunters who catalogued Montanism in Tertullian’s own century. Hippolytus of Rome, writing his Refutation of All Heresies about 222–235, twice handles the sect — “The Phrygians… derive the principles of their heresy from a certain Montanus, and Priscilla, and Maximilla, and regard these wretched women as prophetesses, and Montanus as a prophet” — and names its founders and its errors without a word of Tertullian, the one Latin of real stature the movement ever won.16 So too the earliest Latin heresy-list to cover the group, the anonymous Against All Heresies that has come down appended to Tertullian’s own works (and, ironically, long circulated under his name; scholars trace it to Hippolytus’s lost Syntagma). It divides the “Cataphrygians” into the followers of Proclus and of Aeschines and sets out their common blasphemy — “that the Paraclete has spoken in Montanus more things than Christ brought forward into the Gospel” — but knows no “Tertullianist” branch and never mentions Tertullian.17 The hostile catalogues of Montanism from his own century, in short, contain no Tertullian the heresiarch; that figure is not yet on the map.

What the third century leaves, then, is not a judgement but its absence — and, alongside it, the plain fact of continued use. Beyond Cyprian, the Roman schismatic Novatian built his own On the Trinity — on Jerome’s description, “a sort of epitome of the work of Tertullian” — so closely that it was later mistaken for Cyprian’s.18 The century that came after Tertullian mined him and did not name him, and the men who hunted the Phrygians did not count him among them. An argument from silence proves little on its own, and it is set down here as silence, not smuggled in as acquittal; but it is the ground on which every later verdict was built, and every one of those verdicts is later.
