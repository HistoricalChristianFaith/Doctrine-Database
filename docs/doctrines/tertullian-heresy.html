Was Tertullian a Heretic?

Tertullian of Carthage (c. 155–c. 220) was the first great Christian theologian to write in Latin, and the Latin Church has never quite known what to do with him. He gave the West the words in which it would state its central mystery — trinitas, persona, substantia — along with much else of its theological Latin (sacramentum, satisfactio); he wrote the fiercest surviving defence of the rule of faith against the heretics, the Prescription Against Heretics, whose argument the Church went on using against its enemies for centuries. And he is quoted with approval, on point after point, by the same tradition that never gave him the title “saint,” listed his books among those “not received,” and paired him with Origen as the standing warning about brilliant men who fell. For at some point in middle life Tertullian went over to the New Prophecy — the ecstatic movement out of Phrygia its enemies called Montanism — and, on one later report, ended in a rigorist splinter of his own, the Tertullianistae.

The subject of this page is not Tertullian's theology as such but the verdict passed on him: how, and how far, the Church came to reckon its first Latin theologian a heretic. Here the contrast with Origen — his natural companion, the two of them paired by Vincent of Lérins as the type of the learned teacher undone by his own gifts — is the heart of the story. Origen was anathematized by name, three centuries after his death, by a string of emperors and popes and (on the usual reading) an ecumenical council; Tertullian never was. No synod tried him, no council condemned him, no anathema carries his name. His “condemnation” is reputational, not conciliar — assembled after the fact out of a heresy-catalogue entry, a book-list, a biographical notice, and the drift of later citation — and that absence of a trial is itself the thing to watch: this is a timeline of how a verdict forms when no court ever sits.

Its “witnesses” are therefore the datable judgements and reports, earliest first: Tertullian's own later works, in which he embraces the Paraclete and turns on the “soulish” Christians who will not; the silence of the generation after him, above all Cyprian of Carthage, who is said to have read him every day and yet never named him; Eusebius, who knew him as a master of Roman law; the anonymous heresy-list that goes under his own name; Jerome, who supplies almost every biographical “fact” and most of the damage; Augustine, who first gives him a numbered entry among the heresies and the only notice of the Tertullianistae; Vincent of Lérins; the book-list called the Decretum Gelasianum; the medieval and Reformation reception; and the modern reassessment. Two questions run through it all: what exactly was he faulted for, and was it fair? As with Origen, the deepest paradox is that the tradition never stopped reading him — Tertullian stands as a witness on some twenty of this wiki's belief-timelines, from the origin of the soul and infant baptism to the Eucharist and the perpetual virginity of Mary — a man condemned in the handbook and quoted on every other page.

Timeline

Tertullian builds the Latin rule of faith — and coins the word “Trinity” (c. 200–210)

Before he was anyone's example of a fall, Tertullian was the Church's sharpest weapon against the heretics. The Prescription Against Heretics, written about 203, borrows a term from Roman law — the praescriptio, a plea that stops a lawsuit before its merits are ever heard — and turns it against the Gnostics: the Scriptures belong to the Church that has held the rule of faith from the apostles, and heretics, being no part of that succession, have no standing to argue from them at all. He states that rule in a single long sentence — there is “one only God, and… He is none other than the Creator of the world, who produced all things out of nothing through His own Word, first of all sent forth” — and sets philosophy against it in the line every later century quoted: “What indeed has Athens to do with Jerusalem? What concord is there between the Academy and the Church?”1

One passage of that book reads, in retrospect, like a prophecy written against its own author. Answering the objection that trusted Christians sometimes defect, Tertullian asks how it is “that this woman or that man, who were the most faithful, the most prudent, and the most approved in the church, have gone over to the other side” — and answers that a defector was never truly approved, since “no one is a Christian but he who perseveres even to the end.” “But what if a bishop, if a deacon, if a widow, if a virgin, if a doctor, if even a martyr, have fallen from the rule of faith,” he presses, “will heresies on that account appear to possess the truth? Do we prove the faith by the persons, or the persons by the faith?”2 It is the exact principle — that a teacher's later lapse leaves his earlier truth untouched — on which the Church would later go on reading Tertullian himself. He wrote the rule for his own case before he was the case.

The clearest single measure of how much the tradition owes him is that its very word for God is partly his. In Against Praxeas, written about 210 against a Roman teacher who had collapsed Father, Son and Spirit into one person, Tertullian set down the formula the Latin West has used ever since. The one God is guarded by “the mystery of the dispensation… which distributes the Unity into a Trinity, placing in their order the three Persons — the Father, the Son, and the Holy Ghost: three, however, not in condition, but in degree; not in substance, but in form; not in power, but in aspect; yet of one substance, and of one condition, and of one power.”3 Trinitas, persona, and substantia in their theological senses are, in Latin, largely his coinages. And here the paradox tightens to a single book, for the treatise that gave the West its doctrine of the Trinity is itself an openly Montanist one. Its author writes as a man “better instructed by the Paraclete, who leads men… into all truth”; he reports that he and his “subsequently withdrew from the carnally-minded… on our acknowledgment and maintenance of the Paraclete”; and he blames Praxeas for having, at Rome, “put to flight the Paraclete” even as he “crucified the Father.”4 The vocabulary the Church kept and the allegiance it came to condemn issue from the same pen, in the same years — which is the difficulty the rest of this timeline works out.

Tertullian’s own testimony: the turn to the New Prophecy (c. 205–212)

The primary evidence that Tertullian “lapsed” is not a synod or a rival’s report but his own late works, in which the Paraclete promised in John’s Gospel becomes a living, still-speaking authority. Its office, he now argues, is to carry Christian discipline past what the apostles left: in On the Veiling of Virgins he lays down that “the reason why the Lord sent the Paraclete was, that, since human mediocrity was unable to take in all things at once, discipline should, little by little, be directed, and ordained, and carried on to perfection, by that Vicar of the Lord, the Holy Spirit,” righteousness having been “first in a rudimentary state” and only “now, through the Paraclete… settling into maturity.”5 He quotes the Phrygian prophets as though they were Scripture: in On Exhortation to Chastity “through the holy prophetess Prisca the Gospel is thus preached: that ‘the holy minister knows how to minister sanctity.’”6 The absolute dates are contested and the “Catholic” and “Montanist” works cannot be cleanly sorted; the sequence followed here is the relative chronology of T. D. Barnes, which places the openly New-Prophecy treatises late, roughly 207–212.7

From this authority he presses a hard disciplinary programme — no second marriage, longer and stricter fasts, no flight in persecution, the veiling of unmarried girls — and divides Christendom into two camps by their reception of the Spirit. In On Monogamy the line is drawn from his own side: “Among us… whom the recognition of spiritual gifts entitles to be deservedly called Spiritual, continence is as religious as licence is modest… We admit one marriage, just as we do one God. But to the Psychics, since they receive not the Spirit, the things which are the Spirit’s are not pleasing.”8 The psychici — the “soulish,” merely natural Christians — are the ordinary Catholics who will not follow the Paraclete into the stricter rule; the spiritales are those who will. It is a party name, and Tertullian wields it against the great body of the Church.

At its sharpest the polemic turns on the hierarchy itself. Opening On Modesty, he answers a bishop who had relaxed the penance for adultery: “I hear that there has even been an edict set forth, and a peremptory one too. The Pontifex Maximus — that is, the bishop of bishops — issues an edict: ‘I remit, to such as have discharged (the requirements of) repentance, the sins both of adultery and of fornication.’” He denies that the power of the keys is the bishops’ to use so: the Church “will forgive sins: but (it will be) the Church of the Spirit, by means of a spiritual man; not the Church which consists of a number of bishops.”9 This is the passage later readers found hardest to forgive, and the nearest he ever comes to setting a “church of the Spirit” over against the institutional Church.

Yet on the decisive question — did he ever place himself outside the Church? — his own texts pull the other way. By his account the quarrel is over discipline, not the creed: in On Fasting he insists the New Prophecies are rejected “not that Montanus and Priscilla and Maximilla preach another God, nor that they disjoin Jesus Christ (from God), nor that they overturn any particular rule of faith or hope, but that they plainly teach more frequent fasting than marrying.”10 And in On the Soul the setting is an ordinary congregation: “We have now amongst us a sister whose lot it has been to be favoured with sundry gifts of revelation, which she experiences in the Spirit by ecstatic vision amidst the sacred rites of the Lord’s day in the church… After the people are dismissed at the conclusion of the sacred services, she is in the regular habit of reporting to us whatever things she may have seen in vision.”11 The prophesying happens inside the normal Sunday assembly, not a breakaway conventicle. Even the withdrawal he elsewhere claims — that he and his “subsequently withdrew from the carnally-minded” over the Paraclete (quoted above) — reads, on this evidence, as a rigorist faction within the Carthaginian church rather than a departure from it: an ecclesiola in ecclesia, on the reading urged by William Tabbernee and Douglas Powell. The notice of a separate sect, the Tertullianistae, surfaces only much later, in Augustine — further down this timeline — and never from Tertullian’s own hand.12

The generation after: Cyprian reads him daily, the heresy-hunters pass him over (c. 220–260)

The most revealing witness to Tertullian’s standing is the one hardest to read: the generation that followed him left, so far as survives, almost nothing about him at all — and the silence is of a peculiar kind. No contemporary or near-contemporary calls him a heretic. No synod tried him; no rival’s treatise answers him by name; no third-century heresy-catalogue lists a “Tertullianist” error. What the century does show is that he went on being read — hard, and by the most orthodox hands in his own city — while his name went unspoken.

The chief case is Cyprian, bishop of Carthage from 248 and the African church’s martyr-hero, who leaned on Tertullian throughout and never once names him. The whole framework of Cyprian’s treatise on the Lord’s Prayer follows Tertullian’s On Prayer, clause by clause; his book on patience reworks Tertullian’s On Patience, and his tract on the dress of virgins draws on the On the Apparel of Women — each recast into smoother, more “literary” Latin, and in each the source goes unacknowledged.13 That Cyprian read him at all we know from a single, much later source: Jerome, writing about 393, reports that he had met in Italy an old man named Paul of Concordia who as a youth had been secretary to the aged Cyprian, and who said that Cyprian “was accustomed never to pass a day without reading Tertullian, and that he frequently said to him, ‘Give me the master’ [da magistrum], meaning by this, Tertullian.”14 Jerome is the only source for the anecdote, at second hand and a century and a half after the fact; it should be weighed as such. But the silent dependence in Cyprian’s own text needs no anecdote to establish it, and it is genuinely two-edged. If Tertullian were by then a notorious heretic in Carthage, it is striking that the bishop read him daily and built his own treatises on him; if he were in good standing, it is equally striking that so devoted a reader never names him. The natural reading — that the master’s turn to the New Prophecy had put his name under enough of a cloud to make open citation awkward even for an admirer — is plausible and often urged, but it is an inference we supply, not something Cyprian says. The bare datum is silent, constant use.15

The same blank appears where a verdict would be loudest: in the heresy-hunters who catalogued Montanism in Tertullian’s own century. Hippolytus of Rome, writing his Refutation of All Heresies about 222–235, twice handles the sect — “The Phrygians… derive the principles of their heresy from a certain Montanus, and Priscilla, and Maximilla, and regard these wretched women as prophetesses, and Montanus as a prophet” — and names its founders and its errors without a word of Tertullian, the one Latin of real stature the movement ever won.16 So too the earliest Latin heresy-list to cover the group, the anonymous Against All Heresies that has come down appended to Tertullian’s own works (and, ironically, long circulated under his name; scholars trace it to Hippolytus’s lost Syntagma). It divides the “Cataphrygians” into the followers of Proclus and of Aeschines and sets out their common blasphemy — “that the Paraclete has spoken in Montanus more things than Christ brought forward into the Gospel” — but knows no “Tertullianist” branch and never mentions Tertullian.17 The hostile catalogues of Montanism from his own century, in short, contain no Tertullian the heresiarch; that figure is not yet on the map.

What the third century leaves, then, is not a judgement but its absence — and, alongside it, the plain fact of continued use. Beyond Cyprian, the Roman schismatic Novatian built his own On the Trinity — on Jerome’s description, “a sort of epitome of the work of Tertullian” — so closely that it was later mistaken for Cyprian’s.18 The century that came after Tertullian mined him and did not name him, and the men who hunted the Phrygians did not count him among them. An argument from silence proves little on its own, and it is set down here as silence, not smuggled in as acquittal; but it is the ground on which every later verdict was built, and every one of those verdicts is later.

Jerome writes the biography — and hangs the verdict on one verb (c. 392)

The connected life of Tertullian begins, almost entirely, with a single paragraph written more than a century and a half after he died. About 392, cataloguing the Christian writers on the model of Suetonius, Jerome gave Tertullian chapter 53 of his De viris illustribus — and nearly every “fact” the standard biography repeats descends from it: that he was “the presbyter,” chief of the Latin writers after Victor and Apollonius; that he was a Carthaginian, “the son of a proconsul or Centurion”; that he was “presbyter of the church until middle life”; that, “driven by the envy and abuse of the clergy of the Roman church, he lapsed to the doctrine of Montanus”; that he wrote “directly against the church” the treatises On Modesty, On Fasts, On Monogamy and “six books On ecstasy” (now lost); and that “he is said to have lived to a decrepit old age.”19 This is the frame in which the West has read him ever since, and it hangs the whole later verdict on one verb, lapsus est, “he lapsed.”

It should be entered here as testimony to be weighed, not as the settled frame of the case. Jerome wrote about 172 years after Tertullian’s death, with no continuous Carthaginian tradition behind him; he had his own uses for a rigorist ascetic authority — he was himself the age’s fiercest Latin champion of fasting and against second marriage, and he quarried Tertullian’s rigorist works even as he recorded the “lapse” (in Against Jovinianus he leans on Tertullian on marriage; in Letter 84 he makes Cyprian’s admiration of “the master” the very model of reading a heretic’s genius without his error).20 And several of his load-bearing details are doubted by modern scholarship: Tertullian’s own works never call him a presbyter — he seems rather to number himself among the laity — never mention his father, and never say a word about Rome or a grievance against its clergy, so that the presbyterate and the “envy and abuse” motive alike read, to critics from T. D. Barnes onward, as Jerome’s inference or embellishment rather than reported fact.21

Two of the chapter’s claims are contested enough to be weighed on their own rather than settled in a biographical notice: was Tertullian in fact ordained a presbyter, and was resentment of the Roman clergy his motive for the turn to the New Prophecy. Both are genuinely disputed — the first against Tertullian’s own apparent self-classing among the laity, the second against the silence of his writings about Rome — and are left open here. What the notice establishes beyond dispute is smaller and more revealing: that as late as the 390s there was still no verdict to report, only a life to compose, and that the man who composed it did so while borrowing from his subject on the facing page. The full notice, and the case for reading it critically, are set out on Jerome’s page.22

Augustine catalogues him — and rests the heresy on rigour, not doctrine (c. 428)

The verdict finally becomes a catalogue entry with Augustine. Near the end of his life, compiling a handbook of the heresies for the Carthaginian deacon Quodvultdeus, he gave Tertullian something no earlier writer had: his own numbered notice, chapter 86 of the De haeresibus. It is the moment Tertullian becomes a listed heresiarch in the Latin West — and, at the same stroke, the single piece of ancient evidence that there was ever a distinct Tertullianist sect. Augustine keeps it separate from the Montanists, whom he catalogues apart as the Cataphrygians (chapter 26). And he reports its end as an eyewitness: the Tertullianistae had dwindled “little by little down to our own time” to a last remnant in Carthage, and when he was there some years before, “the very few who remained went over into the catholic church and handed their basilica… over to the catholic church.”23

The notice is more careful than its later use of it would suggest. Augustine states the doctrinal charge — that Tertullian held the soul, and even God, to be a body — and then expressly refuses to make it the ground of the heresy: “yet he is not on this account said to have been made a heretic.” What condemned him, in Augustine’s telling, was discipline. “It is not for this that Tertullian was made a heretic,” he writes, “but because, going over to the Cataphrygians — whom he had earlier destroyed — he began also to condemn second marriages, against apostolic teaching, as though they were fornication; and afterwards, divided even from them, he propagated his own little assemblies.” The turn to the New Prophecy, the rigorist ban on remarriage, and a final schism from the Montanists themselves are the three counts — and the last clause, sua conventicula propagavit, is the whole textual basis for a Tertullian-founded sect.24

Two things pull against each other here, and both matter for the verdict. This is the earliest and effectively the only ancient witness to a separate Tertullianist body; every later report (the anonymous Praedestinatus, c. 435) merely copies it. Yet Augustine wrote some two hundred years after Tertullian, and his confident “from Tertullian” is an inference from the books (sicut scripta eius indicant), not a documented succession — and Tertullian’s own works announce no sect, no name, no second schism. Whether the group was really one Tertullian founded, or a later Carthaginian rigorist circle that took his name, or simply the local New Prophecy congregation relabelled, is genuinely disputed, and is left open here. The same Augustine who files him under the heresies handles him gently everywhere else: he presses Tertullian’s corporealism hard on the origin of the soul yet cannot quite give up the traducianism it serves, and against the Manichees he dates their fraud by naming Tertullian, beside Cyprian, as a fixed marker of the church’s earlier age. The full notice, with the Latin and the scholarly dispute, is on Augustine’s page.25

The West settles into a posture: Vincent’s lament and the book that will not receive him (434 – c. 519)

By the fifth century the Latin verdict is settled enough to be used as a teaching example — and its classic statement, in Vincent of Lérins, is a lament, not an anathema. In the Commonitorium of 434 Vincent devotes a chapter to Tertullian as the standing Latin warning, the exact counterpart of the chapter he had just given Origen: as one is to the Greeks, so is the other to the Latins. “For as Origen holds by far the first place among the Greeks, so does Tertullian among the Latins,” he begins, and piles up the praise before the fall — Tertullian was the most learned of his people, “every word almost was a sentence; every sentence a victory,” and the Marcionites, Apelleians, Praxeans, Hermogenians, Jews, heathens and Gnostics all knew the force of his “many and ponderous volumes.” Then the turn: “Yet this man also… this Tertullian, I say, too little tenacious of Catholic doctrine, that is, of the universal and ancient faith, more eloquent by far than faithful, changed his belief,” so that “by his subsequent error he detracted from the authority of his approved writings.” The pairing with Origen is not incidental: Tertullian is the Latin instance of Vincent’s one thesis — that the very brilliance of a teacher is the peril, and that novelty, not learning, is the mark of heresy.26 It is worth marking what Vincent does not do. He passes no sentence, cites no synod, pronounces no anathema; he grieves. Origen, his companion in the argument, had by 434 been anathematized by name by a string of emperors, popes and councils — Tertullian never was, and Vincent’s chapter, for all its severity, is a warning held up to students, not a verdict handed down by a court.

Where a Roman ruling might be looked for, there is only a book-list — and not the one it claims to be. The so-called Decretum Gelasianum, a register of works “to be received and not received” that travelled under the name of Pope Gelasius I, was for centuries read as a papal decree; it is neither papal nor Gelasian. Von Dobschütz’s edition established, and F. C. Burkitt summarized, that it is an anonymous private compilation of the first half of the sixth century, most probably Italian — the section ascribed to Damasus quotes Augustine, so the ascription “is of no historical value,” and had it been an official decree of Gelasius “it would have been known and used by Dionysius Exiguus and Cassiodorus.”27 In its fifth part, a “list of apocryphal books” — “the remaining writings which have been compiled or been recognised by heretics or schismatics the Catholic and Apostolic Roman Church does not in any way receive” — the entry falls with two words: opuscula Tertulliani apocrypha, “the works of Tertullian… apocryphal.” He keeps distinguished company on the rejected shelf: the same list refuses the works of Arnobius, Lactantius, Cassian, Clement of Alexandria, Africanus, Commodian, Victorinus of Pettau, and even — the paradox at its sharpest — Thascius Cyprianus, the martyr-bishop who had read Tertullian every day.28

Between them, Vincent’s lament and the anonymous book-list fix the West’s posture toward its first Latin theologian for a thousand years: read him, quote him, learn from him — and never canonize him. And a finding of this timeline is worth stating flatly, because it is the whole difference from Origen’s case: no council ever tried Tertullian, and no genuine papal act ever names him. What looks, at a distance, like an official Roman censure — the Decretum’s placing of his books among the “apocrypha” — turns out on inspection not to be papal at all, but a private scholar’s handbook that later ages mistook for a decree. The condemnation of Tertullian is, to the end, a thing assembled from catalogues and laments rather than pronounced from a chair: the West quietly declined to receive his books and left him uncanonized, but it never summoned him, tried him, or anathematized his name.29

The medieval near-silence and the 1521 recovery (c. 850–1539)

For the thousand years after the West declined to receive his books, it very nearly lost them. Tertullian was among the least-copied of the major Latin fathers, and his survival through the Middle Ages, in the judgement of a modern editor, “must be regarded as almost a miracle” — the copies having all but vanished from the libraries of Italy once his works had been set among the rejected. What copying there was tended to be furtive: later ages, one historian of the period notes, “took advantage of his unsound reputation to copy from his writings abundantly — without giving his name.”30 The whole surviving text now rests on a mere handful of witnesses. The oldest and most important is the ninth-century Codex Agobardinus (Paris, lat. 1622), given to the cathedral of Lyons by its archbishop Agobard; it is “the only witness to much of what it contains” — the sole surviving copy of the two books To the Nations and of On the Veiling of Virgins, among others — and its rear third is simply gone, breaking off in the middle of On the Flesh of Christ, so that the further works its table of contents once listed are lost with it. Beside it the text stands on only two more independent pillars: the twelfth-century Codex Trecensis at Troyes, and the lost Cluny–Hirsau corpus that became the source of the first printed edition.31 The most telling loss of all is the one work that would have shown his New Prophecy whole: the six books On Ecstasy, his defence of the Montanist manner of prophesying, with a seventh added against Apollonius — the very treatise Jerome named — perished entirely, and not a line of it survives.32

He returned to print, and to controversy, at a single stroke. On the first of July 1521, at Basel, the humanist Beatus Rhenanus brought out through the press of Johann Froben — on Erasmus’s prompting — the editio princeps of Tertullian’s works, its title-page announcing them “drawn out of the shadows and rescued from neglect” (è tenebris eruta); Rhenanus revised and enlarged the edition twice more, in 1528 and 1539.33 The timing dropped Tertullian straight into the confessional quarrel, and both sides reached for him. Catholic controversialists turned his Prescription Against Heretics against the Reformers exactly as he had turned it against the Gnostics — that those who broke with the Church’s tradition had forfeited any right to argue from her Scriptures at all; Protestants such as Martin Chemnitz answered that his “rule of faith” had drawn its whole content from Scripture and so told for them, and pressed his rigorist assault on Rome’s penitential power against the later papacy’s. Rhenanus’s own Reforming annotations were in turn condemned, going onto the Index of Paul IV in 1559. Tertullian’s arguments, as one modern account of his reception puts it, “appeared, refashioned and restated in varying ways, during the Reformation, in the writings of Catholics resisting Protestant rulers and of Protestants resisting Catholics.”34

The modern reassessment: the heretic who may never have left (1971–2007)

Modern scholarship has quietly taken apart the frame Jerome built. Beginning with T. D. Barnes’s Tertullian: A Historical and Literary Study (1971), and above all with Douglas Powell’s 1975 article “Tertullianists and Cataphrygians,” a line of specialists — Powell, then David Rankin (1995), Geoffrey Dunn (2004), and David Wilhite (2007) — has argued that Tertullian probably never left the catholic Church at all. There is, in his own writings, no notice of a synod, an excommunication, or a departure; he speaks throughout as a man inside the Church, and the North African New Prophecy looks less like a breakaway sect than a rigorist current within the Carthaginian congregation — the ecclesiola in ecclesia of Powell’s reading. On this view the “Montanist schism” and the separate “Tertullianist sect” alike are largely the constructions of later heresiology, working back from Jerome and Augustine; on the strongest form of the case he probably never really left the Church at all.35

And the paradox the tradition never resolved only sharpened with time, for the words held even where the man was doubted. The West went on confessing its central mystery in Tertullian’s Latin: trinitas he appears to have coined outright, the first to apply it, in Latin, to God, while persona, substantia, and satisfactio are the mask of the theatre, the substance of the philosophers, and the discharged debt of the law-courts, each drafted by him into the service of theology and never again discharged from it. He is, as the older reference works grant even while cataloguing his fall, “the first of Latin theological writers,” who “must have invented a theological idiom and have coined new expressions.”36

One thing the reassessment has not changed, and cannot: he is still not a saint. Neither the Roman nor the Orthodox Church has ever canonized Tertullian or given him a feast day; he is the standing example, with his companion Origen, of the Father who is honoured and read but not venerated — “never recognized as a saint by the Eastern or Western Catholic churches,” as a modern reference work states it, his lapse to the New Prophecy keeping even so great a writer off the calendar. That is the final shape of the verdict this timeline has followed: no council ever tried him and no genuine papal act ever named him, yet the Church quietly withheld the one honour it gives its teachers, and withholds it still. He remains what he has been since Vincent and the book-list — condemned in the handbook, uncanonized in the calendar, and quoted on every other page.37

Arguments

Related arguments — the fairness of the verdict

Five questions decide whether the title “heretic” was fairly earned, and each is weighed on its own page. The first asks whether Tertullian ever formally separated from the church of Carthage; the second asks what he was actually faulted for once the label was applied — a doctrinal error, or a matter of prophecy and discipline; the last three take up the specific rigorist counts later readers found hardest to forgive — his denial that the church could ever forgive adultery, apostasy, and the other capital sins, his ban on remarriage as a species of fornication, and his rule that a Christian may neither flee persecution nor buy immunity from it — and ask whether each was a defensible reading of a still-unsettled discipline or a rigorist novelty. All five interlock but rest on partly different texts, and all five come back, on the present reading, contested.

Did Tertullian ever formally leave the catholic church? (assessment: contested)

Whether Tertullian ever formally separated from the church of Carthage is the load-bearing question of this whole timeline, because nearly every later verdict presupposes an answer to it — and it is genuinely disputed. The revisionist reading that has organized the field since Barnes (1971) and Powell (1975) holds that he never left: his own late works record no synod, no excommunication, and no departure; On the Soul 9 places the New Prophecy’s visionary inside the ordinary Sunday assembly; On Fasting 1 insists the quarrel touched discipline, not the creed; he argues as an insider, marking himself off from the “heretics” while scolding the “Psychics”; and the next generation quarried him without apology. On this view the African New Prophecy was a rigorist party within the congregation — an ecclesiola in ecclesia — and the “schism” is a construction of later reporters. But the reading does not carry cleanly: On Modesty 21 opposes “the Church of the Spirit” to “the Church which consists of a number of bishops,” the Psychici polemic is separatist in tone, and Augustine reports an actual Tertullianist body with its own basilica in Carthage that survived into his own day — a hard datum the silence-based case cannot dissolve. Strong scholars stand on both sides, and the evidence compels no verdict. Contested.38

Was the New Prophecy heretical in doctrine, or only in discipline and church order? (assessment: contested)

What, exactly, was Tertullian faulted for once he was reckoned a heretic — an error about God or Christ, or his prophecy and his rigour? The disciplinary reading is strong where it is narrow. Tertullian himself says the New Prophecy is rejected “not that Montanus and Priscilla and Maximilla preach another God, nor that they disjoin Jesus Christ (from God)… but that they plainly teach more frequent fasting than marrying,” and he confines the Paraclete’s novelty to discipline while the “rule of faith… is altogether one, alone immoveable and irreformable”; the earliest hostile reports (Eusebius’ Anonymous and Apollonius) charge ecstatic false prophecy, broken marriages, fasting-laws, hair-dye, dice and usury, not a false creed; Epiphanius grants that the Phrygians hold the Trinity as the catholic church does; and Augustine, cataloguing Tertullian among the heresies, expressly declines to found the heresy on his doctrine, resting it on the turn to the Cataphrygians, the ban on second marriage, and schism. But the broad form overreaches. A claim to new revelation binding on the whole church — that “the Paraclete has spoken in Montanus more things than Christ brought forward into the Gospel” — touches the closure of the apostolic deposit Tertullian himself had defended; the ecstatic manner was condemned as false prophecy, a judgement about the criteria of true revelation; and a later Montanist wing did fall into real modalism (“Christ Himself Son and Father”), which must not be read back onto Tertullian’s anti-modalist line but does bar a blanket “orthodox throughout.” The narrow claim is sound, the broad one overstated, and where discipline ends and doctrine begins is exactly what divides the field. Contested.39

Was his denial of church forgiveness for adultery and apostasy a defensible reading of early discipline, or a Montanist novelty? (assessment: contested)

The single count later readers found hardest to forgive is the passage in On Modesty where Tertullian denies the bishops any power to reconcile the gravest sinners — answering the edict (Callistus’s, on the traditional view) that extended peace to repentant adulterers by consigning adultery, fornication, idolatry, murder, and apostasy to a class of “irremissible” sins the church cannot forgive at all. The interpretation weighed there does not defend that verdict as the church’s eventual practice but as a defensible reading of the discipline of his own age: pre-Nicene penance was severe, the post-baptismal “second repentance” was already a once-only remedy, and a real rigorist current — the Shepherd of Hermas’s anxiety, then the Novatianist schism — feared the capital sins beyond the church’s reach, so that the laxer practice prevailed by development and judging him by it is anachronistic. That frame holds; whether the primitive rule reserved the capital sins (Harnack) or always reconciled them (d’Alès, Poschmann, Rahner) is a century-old scholarly dispute left open here. But the load-bearing claim of “no novelty” overreaches: Tertullian’s own earlier On Repentance had offered the grave sinner that reconciling penance; the Shepherd, which granted it to adulterers, he had to reject to hold his line; he re-grounds the denial on the Paraclete and the Psychici/Spiritales split, conceding he had changed his mind; and the church, settling the same question against Novatian, ratified the opposite reading. Well-grounded as history, overstated as exculpation, and disputed at its root. Contested.40

Was his ban on second marriage a severe reading of Paul, or a false doctrine? (assessment: contested)

The count on which Augustine actually hung the heresy — that Tertullian “began also to condemn second marriages, against apostolic teaching, as though they were fornication” — is the ban on remarriage after a spouse’s death. The interpretation weighed there reads it not as a private aberration but as the extreme pole of a real early deprecation of remarriage: Paul rates the widow “happier” unmarried and bars the twice-married from office (1 Cor. 7:40; 1 Tim. 3:2, 5:9), Athenagoras had called remarriage a “specious adultery” a generation before Montanus, the church barred digamists from the clergy everywhere and penanced them in the East for centuries, and Tertullian’s own critics filed the charge under discipline, not the creed — so his fault reads, on this view, as an over-strict discipline rather than a false doctrine. That frame holds for the church’s deprecation of remarriage. But the outright ban overreaches it: Tertullian forbids what the apostle expressly permits (Rom. 7:2–3; 1 Cor. 7:39; 1 Tim. 5:14), re-labels as “fornication” a union Paul calls “no adulteress,” and rests the prohibition on the Paraclete’s abrogation of an apostolic concession rather than on exegesis — even emending the text to secure it — which turns the count, at root, into a question of authority. He conceded he had changed his own earlier view; the church settled it the other way; and Jerome, who mined On Monogamy and pressed the same denigration of marriage, was charged with condemning marriage, saw the book suppressed, and drew back at exactly this line (“I do not condemn second, nor third… marriages”). Strong as ascetic discipline, weak as exegesis, disputed where discipline ends and doctrine begins. Contested.41

Was Tertullian right that flight in persecution is forbidden? (assessment: contested)

The treatise On Flight in Persecution carries the rigorist programme to its last count: since persecution is God’s own instrument for sifting His people, a Christian may neither flee it nor ransom himself, for “the refusal of martyrdom is denial.” Part of the position holds. Scripture does summon the disciple to confess and not to fear (Matt. 10:28, 10:32–33), and the sharper thesis — that to buy immunity is a form of denial — was ratified a generation later, when the church counted the libellatici, who purchased certificates of sacrifice they never performed, among the lapsed. But the flat ban on flight overreaches. Christ himself commanded the persecuted to “flee ye into another” city (Matt. 10:23), which Tertullian must confine to the apostles against the plain reading of Jerome, Chrysostom, and Augustine; the church’s greatest confessors — Polycarp, who withdrew to a farm while his Martyrdom refused to praise the volunteer Quintus, Cyprian, who withdrew from Carthage and defended it from this very verse, and Athanasius, who wrote a whole Defence of his Flight — fled and were honoured for it, not condemned; and Tertullian, who in his earlier catholic To His Wife had granted flight as a lawful permission, grounds the reversal not on exegesis but on the Paraclete’s oracle (“seek not to die… in soft fevers, but to die the martyr’s death”), so that here too the count turns, at root, into a question of authority. Strong as a refusal to buy immunity, overstated as a prohibition of flight against Christ’s own word and the practice of the saints. Contested.42

Was Tertullian’s corporeal soul crude materialism, or a defensible anti-Platonism? (assessment: contested)

The one doctrinal charge in the case stands apart from the disciplinary counts above: Tertullian held the soul to be a body (corpus animae) and carried the principle up to God — “who will deny that God is a body, although ‘God is a Spirit’?” It is the charge Augustine names and then expressly sets aside (“yet he is not on this account said to have been made a heretic”), so the question weighed here is the one that page defers: granting the corporealism was not the ground of the heresy, was it itself an error? The interpretation reads his corpus as the Stoic “body” — whatever really exists and can act — doing the work of an as-yet-uncoined substantia, against the Platonic and Gnostic construction of soul and spirit as immaterial nothings; on that core — the soul is a created thing, not a fragment of the divine — he is orthodox, and his Platonizing opponents were the ones later condemned. That much holds, and the flat charge of crude materialism misses him. But the reduction to a mere vocabulary problem overreaches: he gives the soul figure, the three dimensions, and colour — the last on a Montanist visionary’s report — and reasons from a thoroughgoing Stoic physics (only a body can suffer; the soul suffers in Hades; therefore it is a body), not a bare term; the corporeal God is a further step later orthodoxy firmly refused; and Augustine, who leaned to Tertullian’s own traducianism and defended him elsewhere, still judged the corporealism an error and argued the soul incorporeal. Defensible by his realist intent and his sources’ usage, deficient by the incorporealist metaphysics the church settled into, with strong readers on both sides. The doctrine’s full setting is on his page on the origin of the soul. Contested.43
