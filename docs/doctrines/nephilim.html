The Sons of God and the Nephilim (Genesis 6)

Genesis 6:1–4 reports that the "sons of God" saw that the "daughters of men" were fair, took wives, and that the Nephilim — rendered giants (gigantes) by the Septuagint — "were on the earth in those days, and also afterward." Two questions divide its interpreters:

  1. Who are the "sons of God"? Fallen angels (the "Watchers"), or the righteous line of Seth?
  2. What are the Nephilim? Hybrid offspring of an angelic–human union, or simply mighty, violent men of renown?

The earliest attested reading — dominant in Second Temple Judaism and in the earliest Church — is the angelic / supernatural one: the sons of God are rebel angels, and the Nephilim are their giant offspring, whose disembodied spirits were later identified with the demons.1 The Septuagint (c. 250 BC) already reflects this, rendering the Nephilim gígantes ("giants") and, in many copies, the sons of God as "angels of God."2 A second reading identifies the sons of God with the godly line of Seth and the daughters of men with the line of Cain, making the Nephilim merely violent men; it is first stated explicitly by Julius Africanus3 and becomes the standard reading after Augustine, who rejected the angelic interpretation and the apocryphal Enochic "fables."4 The modern source that prompted this page — Fr. Stephen De Young — argues for recovering the ancient supernatural reading and connects the Nephilim to the later "giant" tribes (Rephaim, Anakim) and Israel's conquest.5

A note on the shape of this debate. De Young (in the Lord of Spirits podcast) argues the angelic reading was the "universal view of the first three centuries, almost four centuries, of the Church," and that the Sethite reading originated in Rabbinic Judaism (which had by then rejected the Enochic literature as "too Christian") and entered Christianity only with Julius Africanus → Ephrem → Augustine. He holds the later Fathers rejected a misunderstanding — literal angel–human interbreeding — rather than the actual ancient claim, which located the Nephilim's begetting in a pagan sacral-kingship fertility ritual.6 This framing is one party's; the timeline below records each figure's own words. The New Testament witness he cites (Jude, 2 Peter) is now its own timeline entry below; George Syncellus's 9th-c. angelic reading is now a timeline entry too (sourced to his own Chronography). Each of the three framing claims is now weighed adversarially in the Arguments & cruxes section below: the Rabbinic origin of the Sethite reading (the "Africanus → Ephrem → Augustine" pipeline) on its own page (assessment: plausible); the premise beneath "the universal view… as Scripture," the canonicity of 1 Enoch (assessment: weak); and the sacral-kingship ritual mechanism itself (assessment: sound).

Timeline

Book of Enoch / 1 Enoch (c. 300 BC)

1 Enoch gives the fullest ancient form of the angelic reading: two hundred angels ("Watchers"), led by Semjaza, descended on Mount Hermon, took human wives, and "bare great giants," while teaching humanity sorcery, metallurgy, and astrology.1 Critically, it teaches that when the giants die, "the spirits of the giants" become "evil spirits upon the earth" — the origin of demons.7

The Septuagint (LXX) (c. 250 BC)

The Septuagint, the Greek Torah of Alexandria and the version the New Testament quotes, renders the Nephilim as gígantes — "giants" — and, in many copies (so Augustine testifies), the "sons of God" as "angels of God."2 As the earliest translational interpretation, it reads Genesis 6 as angelic beings begetting giants.

Book of Jubilees (c. 100 BC)

Jubilees retells the same event: "the angels of God … took themselves wives … and they bare unto them sons and they were giants." God bound the angels in the depths of the earth and had their giant sons slay one another by the sword.8 After the Flood, when the dead giants' spirits afflict mankind, the chief spirit Mastema wins God's leave for "the tenth part" to remain and torment the wicked — the 90% / 10% schema behind the Gospel demons' cry, "Have you come to torment us before the time?"9

The Wisdom of Solomon (c. 50 BC)

Wisdom names "the proud giants" as those who perished in Noah's flood,10 and traces idolatry to the deified dead — a dead man "honoured… as a god" with "ceremonies and sacrifices" — a cult that ran to child sacrifice and "secret ceremonies."11 It is a Second Temple witness both to the giants of Genesis 6 and to the human-sacrifice cult that later interpreters (and Fr. Stephen De Young) attach to them.

Philo of Alexandria (c. 50)

Philo identifies the angels of Genesis 6 with the "souls" or "demons" that fill the air, and states plainly that "these giants were sprung from a combined procreation of two natures, namely, from angels and mortal women."12 Yet he also reads the passage allegorically — men "born of earth," "of heaven," and "of God" — treating giant partly as a moral type of the earth-bound soul (Nimrod).13

The New Testament: Jude & 2 Peter (c. AD 60–90)

The New Testament itself attests the angelic reading: Jude speaks of "the angels which kept not their first estate" reserved "in everlasting chains under darkness," and quotes Enoch, "the seventh from Adam," as a prophet;14 2 Peter sets "the angels that sinned," cast into Tartarus, immediately beside Noah and the flood.15 The earliest Fathers (Justin, Clement, Tertullian, Origen) read these texts as the Genesis 6 event. (1 Peter 3:19's "spirits in prison," which De Young also enlists, is read by every patristic commentator in our database as human — Christ's descent to the dead, or his preaching through Noah — yet much modern scholarship reads it as the imprisoned Watchers, as De Young does; it is contested, and adjudicated on its own argument page.)

Josephus (c. 100)

Josephus records that "many angels of God accompanied with women, and begat sons that proved unjust," comparing them to the giants of Greek legend.16

Justin Martyr (c. 165)

Justin holds that the angels appointed over humanity "were captivated by love of women, and begat children who are those that are called demons," who then enslaved the human race and were mistaken by the poets for gods.17

Athenagoras of Athens (c. 177)

Athenagoras, pleading the Christians' cause before Marcus Aurelius, reads Genesis 6 as angelic history: angels set over matter "fell into impure love of virgins," and "of these lovers of virgins … were begotten those who are called giants," whose souls are "the demons who wander about the world."18

Irenaeus (c. 202)

Irenaeus writes that "angels were united with the daughters of the race of mankind; and they bore to them sons who for their exceeding greatness were called giants," and that the angels brought "teachings of wickedness" — leading to the flood.19 He also offers "Titan" as his leading candidate for the name of 666, tying the Antichrist to the Titan/giant tradition.20

Clement of Alexandria (c. 215)

Clement refers to "the angels who … having sunk into pleasures, told to the women the secrets which had come to their knowledge" — the angelic reading, applied to the origin of forbidden arts.21

Tertullian (c. 220)

Tertullian speaks of "those angels … who rushed from heaven on the daughters of men," who taught women metallurgy, the properties of herbs, enchantments, astrology, and cosmetics — and whom Christians "are destined to judge."22 He further treats the Book of Enoch itself as inspired Scripture, defending its survival of the Flood through Noah — the earliest patristic answer to that objection, and (not Jerome, as is sometimes said) the real source of the argument.22

Julius Africanus (c. 240)

Julius Africanus is the earliest writer to articulate the Sethite reading: the "sons of God" are "the descendants of Seth … on account of the righteous men and patriarchs," and the daughters are the line of Cain — though he records the angelic view as the alternative.3

Commodian (c. 250)

Commodian, in catechetical Latin verse, keeps the whole Enochic sequence: angels "sent down" despised God's laws, were turned by "the beauty of women," and "from their seed giants are said to have been born"; the giants taught the arts, and their dead spirits "now subvert many bodies" and are worshipped "as gods."23

Origen of Alexandria (c. 253)

Origen groups the "sons of God" of Genesis 6 with the fallen beings who "left their own dwelling" and "did not preserve their own principality," reading the episode through the lens of angelic fall.24

Lactantius (c. 310)

Lactantius, the first systematic Latin theologian, makes the fall the "fountain of errors" of paganism: God's guardian angels were "polluted … by intercourse with women" and "fell to the earth," and their offspring — "neither angels nor men, but bearing a kind of mixed nature" — became the earthly demons, "the authors of all the evils which are done."25

Eusebius of Caesarea (c. 339)

Eusebius takes the giants born of "the angels of God" and the daughters of men to be daemons whose "spirits have been deified by the subsequent generations of men" — the pagan gods of Greek legend.26

Ephrem the Syrian (c. 373)

Ephrem holds the Sethite reading firmly: "[Moses] called the sons of Seth 'sons of God,' … The beautiful daughters of men … were the daughters of Cain."27

Basil of Caesarea (c. 379)

Basil does not adjudicate the origin question but uses the giants morally, as types of prideful strength: "Such was the arrogance of the giants because of their strength. Such also was the God-defying pride of the witless Goliath."28

Nemesius of Emesa (c. 390)

Nemesius, commenting on the fall behind Genesis 6, holds that "of the incorporeal beings, only angels fell away, and not all of them, but some only, that inclined to things below" — an angelic framing.29

Ambrose of Milan (c. 397)

Ambrose explicitly rejects the "children of the earth" reading of the poets and "asserts that they were generated from angels and women," the name giant expressing their bodily magnitude — though he draws a moral lesson about flesh over soul.30

John Chrysostom (c. 407)

Chrysostom vigorously attacks the angelic reading as "blasphemy" and "the height of absurdity" — angels are nowhere called "sons of God," and being incorporeal they cannot marry — and identifies the sons of God as the descendants of Seth and Enosh.31

Jerome (c. 420)

Jerome reports that Aquila rendered the phrase "sons of gods," understanding "the Holy Ones or Angels," and judges that "it is fitting for the name of falling ones to apply to angels" — leaning to the angelic/Hebrew reading.32 33

Augustine of Hippo (c. 430)

Augustine gives the decisive de-mythologized reading: the "sons of God" were "the sons of Seth" who "sunk into this community when they forsook righteousness," the giants were real but ordinary men, and the Enochic writings "about the giants, saying that their fathers were not men" are apocryphal "fables" without canonical authority.4 He leaves open only whether some aerial spirits can assault women, while denying that God's holy angels fell this way.34

John Cassian (c. 435)

Cassian follows the Sethite reading: the "seed of Seth … was by reason of its sanctity termed 'angels of God,' or … 'sons of God,'" the daughters were of Cain's line, and their offspring "were termed giants by reason of the size of their bodies and their cruelty."35

Cyril of Alexandria (c. 444)

Cyril reads the "sons of the gods" (Aquila) or "sons of the rulers" (Symmachus) as "the descendants of Seth and of Enosh … because of the piety and godliness which was in them."36

Philoxenus of Mabbug (c. 523)

Philoxenus assumes the Sethite reading, treating the episode as the conquest of "the children of Seth" by "the lust of the belly," for which "they were rejected from the household of God."37

Bede (c. 735)

Bede transmits the Sethite reading to the medieval West: "sons of God" are the lineage of Seth, "men" the offspring of Cain; the giants were "men with immense bodies and excessive power," and he notes the Hebrew "fallen ones."38 39

George Syncellus (c. 810)

George Syncellus, a monk of Constantinople, transcribes long Greek extracts of 1 Enoch's "Book of the Watchers" into the antediluvian section of his universal chronicle, treating them as history: the angels (Egregori) descended in the days of Jared on Mount Hermon, took the daughters of men, and "bare … great Giants. Now the Giants begat the Naphilim."40 He is the latest pre-modern witness here to the angelic reading, and the conduit through whom both the Greek Enoch and Julius Africanus's Sethite fragment survive. (The "apostolic apocryphon" label sometimes attached to him is in fact De Young's gloss, which De Young himself credits to St. Nikephoros I — not Syncellus; its primary basis is Nicephorus's Stichometry, which lists Enoch among the apocrypha of the Old Testament.)

Bonaventure (c. 1274)

Bonaventure uses the passage typologically rather than to settle its identity: the sons of God taking the daughters of men, alongside Lamech's bigamy, prefigures the lawlessness that brings judgment and points to the Antichrist.41

Thomas Aquinas (c. 1274)

Aquinas holds the Sethite reading he inherits from Augustine — the "sons of God" are the line of Seth, and "they were not all giants" — yet he uniquely preserves the incubus tradition: demons do procure intercourse with women, but any offspring "is not the child of a demon, but of a man," begotten from human seed the demon transfers (a "two-source" logic oddly parallel to De Young's).42 On 1 Peter 3:19 he reads the "spirits in prison" as human, preferring Augustine's "preached through Noah" reading.43

Fr. Stephen De Young (with Fr. Andrew Stephen Damick) (2018–2026)

De Young argues for recovering the ancient supernatural reading: the Nephilim are the offspring of demonic spirits and human women, both giant and tyrannical, continuous with the Babylonian apkallu traditions preserved in 1 Enoch and the Book of the Giants. He extends the theme to the later "giant" tribes — the Rephaim (Og of Bashan), the Anakim, the Emim — and reads Israel's conquest as a war against demonic powers, fulfilled when David's men slew the last giants of Gath.5 In the Lord of Spirits episode "Land of Giants" (with Fr. Andrew Stephen Damick) he adds the mechanism — a pagan sacral-kingship fertility ritual producing a child of "two fathers," so the objection that demons cannot interbreed misses what the tradition claimed — and a "demonization" theology in which the giants are an inverse of theosis and their dead spirits become the "unclean spirits" of the Gospels.6 In a later long-form interview on the Tucker Carlson Show (2026) he ties the antediluvian pattern to the Sumerian King List (its seventh king, inventor of the solar calendar, mirroring Enoch — "the seventh from Adam," who lives 365 years), develops a "ladder" by which a man becomes a fully "demonized" giant, and — while reading the giants as ritual kings and demonized men rather than literal fossils — argues the same cult persists covertly today.44

Doug Wilson (c. 2021)

Doug Wilson, a Reformed/CREC pastor, is a second modern proponent of the angelic reading: the "sons of God" (bene elohim) are celestial beings — never the line of Seth — and the Nephilim their giant offspring (Gen 6:1–8). Against De Young's ritual mechanism he holds a literal-embodiment one — the angels "relinquished their celestial bodies" (Jude 6's habitation; cf. 2 Cor 5:2) to take human wives — and reads 1 Peter 3:18–20 as Christ heralding victory (not offering the gospel) to those angels imprisoned in Tartarus, framing the Flood as judgment on a "genetic-engineering" / "being-as-God" rebellion of which modern bioethics is the continuation. (He overstates that the Sethite reading "doesn't really come into its own until the modern era" — the timeline above dates it to Julius Africanus, c. 240, and Augustine, c. 430; noted on his page.)45

Arguments & cruxes

Sub-claims marshaled for (or against) a reading above, which cut across the timeline rather than sitting at one date — each weighed adversarially on its own page, with an assessment of the interpretation it serves. See project.md §4 for the page type.

The sacred-marriage (sacral-kingship) complex

De Young's answer to the "spirits can't interbreed" objection: a sacral-kingship bed-rite in which a divinized king unites with a spirit-as-woman. The mechanism page states the claim; the other three weigh the adduced parallels — Babylonian (Og's bed), Khmer, and Japanese.

The sacred-marriage rite as the mechanism of the Nephilim (assessment: sound)

The sacred-marriage rite as the mechanism of the Nephilim — De Young's answer to "spirits can't interbreed": the Nephilim were begotten not by angels copulating with women but through a pagan sacral-kingship fertility ritual, in which a divinized king ritually embodies a god and unites with a woman on a cultic bed, the child reckoned to have "two fathers" and be "two-thirds divine" (as Gilgamesh). The form of the mechanism is real: a documented human-king bed-rite exists — the Sumerian hieros gamos (the king as Dumuzi uniting with Inanna's priestess on a prepared bed; Iddin-Dagan A, Šulgi X) — so it is more than apologetic invention, and corrects the impression (from the Og's-bed page, which weighed only the divine Marduk–Zarpanitu rite) that no human-king rite is attested. But three steps overreach: the rite's documented purpose is legitimation and the land's fertility, not "to produce the next king"; whether it involved literal sex is genuinely contested (Lapinkivi); and the "two-thirds divine offspring" is borrowed from a separate divine-birth motif (Gilgamesh born of the goddess Ninsun), not from the sacred marriage itself.46

Og's bed ≅ the bed of Marduk (assessment: plausible)

Og's bed and the bed of Marduk — De Young reads Og's iron bed (Deut 3:11, 9×4 cubits) as a deliberate echo of Marduk's cultic bed at Babylon, tagging Og as an heir of Babylonian sacral kingship. The core parallel is well-grounded — a peer-reviewed argument (Lindquist, CBQ 2011) resting on a real cuneiform text (the Esagil tablet, ed. A. R. George). But three features of De Young's framing overstate it: no bed was excavated; the dimensioned bed is in the Esagila temple, not the Etemenanki ziggurat summit (that couch is Herodotus's, undimensioned); and the rite is a divine marriage of two gods, not one "to produce the next king."47

The Khmer nāga-tower rite (Zhou Daguan) as a sacral-kingship parallel (assessment: sound)

The Khmer nāga-tower rite — another of De Young's "sacral king sleeps with a spirit" parallels, this one a firsthand thirteenth-century report. The Chinese envoy Zhou Daguan (at Angkor 1296–97) records that in a gold tower at the heart of the royal palace the Khmer king coupled each night with a nine-headed nāga serpent-spirit — "the lord of the earth for the entire country" — that appeared as a woman, his own life and the realm's welfare resting on the union; and the dynasty separately claimed descent from a king's marriage to a nāga princess (Kaundinya and Soma). As a documented witness that "a divinized king ritually unites with a spirit-as-woman" is a real institution, the parallel is solid — among his strongest, since the partner is explicitly a spirit and the union explicitly nightly and sexual. The one overlay to flag is his recurring "to produce the next king": the rite's attested function is the king's legitimacy and the land's welfare, not heir-production (though the heir/lineage motif does surface in the foundation myth); and Zhou reports it as what "the local people all say" of a secret tower.48

The Japanese accession rite (Daijōsai) as a surviving sacral-kingship bed-rite (assessment: contested)

The Daijōsai as a surviving sacral-kingship bed-rite — De Young's claim that his sacral-kingship sex-rite survived "into living memory" in the Japanese emperor's accession ceremony, where he "spends the night with the sun goddess," its sexual element "removed after WWII," so "Hirohito would have been the last one." The core is real: the rite is the Daijōsai, in which the emperor is secluded overnight with a bed-like god-seat (the shinza) to commune with the sun goddess Amaterasu, and a credentialed scholar (R. S. Ellwood, The Feast of Kingship, 1973) reads it as a hieros gamos producing "the next heir to the throne" — a striking match to his ANE mechanism, and the rite has genuinely survived into living memory. But the specifics fail: the sacred-marriage reading is a contested minority view (Blacker calls it "unconvincing"; the dominant readings are communion, hospitality, and rebirth); the "sexual element removed after WWII" turns a discredited pre-war interpretation into a ritual act that was excised; and "Hirohito the last one" is flatly wrong — Akihito performed the Daijōsai in 1990 and Naruhito in 2019.49

1 Peter 3:19 — the "spirits in prison" (assessment: contested)

1 Peter 3:19 — whether the "spirits in prison" Christ preached to are the imprisoned Watchers (so De Young, with much modern criticism — Dalton, Bauckham, Davids, Schreiner) or the human dead (so every patristic commentator in our database — the Descensus, or Christ preaching through Noah). Genuinely contested: a weak leg by patristic-reception lights, a strong one by modern-critical lights. Unlike Jude 6/14 and 2 Peter 2:4, which carry firm support in both eras. (A separate crux weighs the content of that proclamation — herald of victory versus offer of salvation — on the intermediate state's content-of-the-proclamation page.)50

The Sumerian King List ≅ Enoch, the seventh antediluvian (assessment: sound)

The Sumerian King List ≅ Enoch — De Young reads Enoch, "the seventh from Adam" (Jude 14) who lives 365 years, as a counterpart of the seventh antediluvian king of the Sumerian King List (Enmeduranki of Sippar, the sun-god's city), and floats a base-60 "encoding" of the Genesis 5 ages. The core parallel is well-grounded mainstream scholarship (VanderKam, Kvanvig, Collins; even the seventh sage Utuabzu who "ascended to heaven"). But his clause "the seventh king creates the solar calendar" overstates it — Enmeduranki is remembered as the founder of divination, not a calendar (that role is Enoch's, retrojected) — and the sexagesimal reading of the ages is real but unsettled scholarship, which he rightly says no one has "cracked."51

The Rephaim as the spirits of dead kings, KTU 1.161 (assessment: sound)

The Rephaim as the spirits of dead kings — De Young grounds the biblical Rephaim (and Og, "the last of the Rephaim") in a Ugaritic royal funerary liturgy from Ras Shamra, "this is what Isaiah 14 and Psalm 88 mean by the Rephaim." The identification — Rephaim = the deified spirits of dead kings, attested in KTU 1.161 — is near-consensus scholarship (Levine–de Tarragon, Lewis, Pardee) and genuinely fits Isaiah 14's dead kings rising to greet a new arrival. But his framing inverts the rite: he calls it "ritual magic to ward off the Rephaim" so the dead king could "pass by them safely," whereas the text summons and welcomes the royal dead and seeks their blessing on the living dynasty — the dead king is received into their company, not steered past it; and the leap to the giant Rephaim is a separable further step.52

The canonicity of 1 Enoch — was it received as Scripture? (assessment: weak)

The canonicity of 1 Enoch — whether the early Church held 1 Enoch as authoritative Scripture, the reception prop beneath De Young's claim that the angelic reading was "the universal view of the first three centuries." The early esteem is real and named: Jude quotes Enoch as prophecy, Tertullian defends it as inspired Scripture, and the fourth-century decline in its standing is genuine history. But "authoritative Scripture" overstates it: the reception was contested in its own day — Origen, who cites Enoch, still says it does "not at all circulate in the Churches as divine" — and the canon-defining Fathers (Athanasius, Jerome, Augustine) excluded it, with full canonical reception surviving only in the Ethiopian tradition.53

Does Augustine's case against the angelic reading hold? (assessment: sound)

Augustine's case — whether the turning-point witness's argument against the angelic reading (Augustine, City of God XV.23) actually works. The sharpest modern attack on it — De Young's charge that it argues in a circle (he dismisses the angelic origin of the giants because it leans on the Enochic books, and dismisses those books partly because they teach it) — overstates a real but narrow soft spot: Augustine's decisive ground is canon-history (the books' obscure provenance and non-reception by the priestly custodians of the pre-Christian Hebrew canon), and his Sethite reading is drawn from the canonical Genesis text itself (the LXX "sons of God" read of Seth's line, with Ps 82), not from Enoch's exclusion — so the case is not viciously circular. But it is not decisive either: it leans on an unargued angelological premise (holy angels cannot so fall) that the opposing camp denies, and Augustine himself could not settle 1 Peter 3:19, the verse that reading leans on. The core holds; it does not close the question.54

Are the unclean spirits the spirits of the dead Nephilim? (assessment: contested)

The unclean spirits as the dead Nephilim — whether the Gospels' "unclean spirits" / demons are the surviving spirits of the dead giants. Judged on its strongest case (not any one proponent's wording), the reading is well-grounded: it is explicit in 1 Enoch 15 / Jubilees 10 and stated by a broad ante-Nicene chorus — Justin, Athenagoras ("the souls of the giants… are the demons who wander about the world"), Commodian, Lactantius — arguably the default demonology of the 2nd–3rd c. What keeps it contested: the NT never asserts the etiology; the rival identification of demons with the apostate angels ("the devil and his angels," Matt 25:41) competes and becomes the mature tradition's view; and the reading presupposes the angelic interpretation, so it is unavailable to the whole Sethite camp. A strong interpretive option, not a settled doctrine. (The modern reviver, De Young, props it on a Chrysostom homily — wrong-camp and about the living, not the dead — but the interpretation does not depend on that prop; flagged on his page.)55

Is Chrysostom's Sethite reading genuinely his, or a later editor's hand? (assessment: weak)

Chrysostom's Genesis homilies — authorship and date — whether Chrysostom can be firmly counted a Sethite witness, or whether (so De Young) the Sethite material is late and possibly "another hand." The doubt does not hold. Its two props both fail: the "412–413" date is found in no study of the homilies and falls after Chrysostom's death (407) — the consensus places the Sethite Homily 22 at Antioch, c. 388, within his lifetime — and the appeal to St. Photius misattributes to him an authorship doubt he never raised (his Bibliotheca gives a lower count, 61, which the editors explain as folios lost from his copy). The genuine kernel — two overlapping Genesis series whose relation is a real scholarly puzzle, and a possible post-401 recension of the written text — leaves both series authentically Chrysostom's, so he stands as a firm Sethite voice.56

Did the Sethite reading originate as a Rabbinic reaction against the Enochic reading? (assessment: plausible)

The Rabbinic origin of the Sethite reading — De Young's historiographical thesis that the angelic reading is original, the rival Sethite reading a later Rabbinic-Jewish invention (a repudiation of the now Christian-coded Enochic books as "too Christian"), and that it entered Christianity via Julius Africanus → Ephrem → Augustine. The spine is well-grounded: the angelic reading is the oldest (Alexander), the earliest rejection of it is Jewish and second-century (R. Simeon b. Yohai's curse, Genesis Rabbah 26:5), the Enochic tradition genuinely diverged between the communities (Reed), and the "too-Christian" motive has an ancient witness (Tertullian: the Jews rejected Enoch "just like all the other portions… which tell of Christ"). But the compression overstates and mislabels: the rabbinic reading was "sons of judges/nobles," not "sons of Seth"; the explicitly Sethite reading is first attested in a Christian writer, Julius Africanus; the "Africanus → Ephrem → Augustine" pipeline is undocumented (three independent Christian developments — Augustine builds it from the canonical text); and the mono-causal "reaction against Christianity" flattens a multi-causal (post-70 boundary-drawing; the "two powers"/Metatron anxiety — Segal), never-total process (the angelic myth persisted and revived in Judaism).57

Does the Wisdom of Solomon attest the giant-cult's human sacrifice and cannibalism? (assessment: sound)

The Wisdom of Solomon on the giant-cult's human sacrifice and cannibalism — De Young rests his picture of the giant-clans' "ongoing ritual life" (idolatry that shades into child-sacrifice and cannibalism) on "the book of Wisdom, the Wisdom of Solomon." The proof-text holds, and unusually directly: Wisdom 12:3–6, of the Canaanites by name, stacks "wicked sacrifices," "merciless murderers of children" (child-sacrifice), and "devourers of man's flesh, and the feasts of blood" (cannibalism) into a single sentence, with the idolatry→child-sacrifice spine restated at 14:23 and idolatry called "the beginning, the cause, and the end, of all evil" (14:27). The only qualification is a citation precision, not a defeater: the cannibalism clause is specifically Wisdom 12:5, not the 14:23 idolatry-list, so the man-eating link rests on chapter 12. A Second Temple polemical witness — exactly the kind his argument invokes — and distinct from the 1 Enoch 7 strand he cites elsewhere.58

The angels' "habitation" (oikētērion) as a relinquished celestial body (assessment: weak)

The angels' "habitation" as a relinquished celestial body — Doug Wilson's competing answer to "how spirits beget" (against De Young's ritual one): the angels who "left their own habitation" (Jude 6, oikētērion) relinquished their celestial bodies to marry human women, the warrant being that the word's only other NT use, 2 Corinthians 5:2, denotes the embodied resurrection body. The premise holds at one end — in 2 Cor 5:2 oikētērion really does denote a body (so Chrysostom, Theophylact, Aquinas). But the transfer to Jude fails: oikētērion means "dwelling / abode," and the body-sense in 2 Cor is supplied by Paul's clothing-and-tabernacle metaphor, not the word; carrying it into Jude — a different author whose own context is steeped in 1 Enoch (the Watchers "left the high, holy, and eternal heaven"), with the parallel ἀρχήν ("first estate") and the verb "left/abandoned" — is illegitimate totality transfer. The whole patristic database reads Jude's "habitation" as the angels' heavenly station, never a shed body (Bede: "the seats of the heavenly kingdom"; Theophylact: "the heavenly manner of life").59

Does Matthew 22:30 ("they neither marry") foreclose the angelic reading? (assessment: sound)

Matthew 22:30 and the angelic reading — Doug Wilson's rebuttal of the oldest patristic objection (Chrysostom, Homily 22 on Genesis: angels are incorporeal, and since "they neither marry," it is "not for that kind of incorporeal creature to ever feel the onset of desire"). Wilson answers that Jesus reports what the angels in heaven do, not what cannot be done. The narrow point holds: the saying is scoped to the angels "in heaven" (Mark: "which are in heaven"), Scripture itself grounds the non-marriage in deathlessness ("neither can they die any more: for they are equal unto the angels," Luke 20:36) rather than incapacity, and the patristic exposition of the verse says the same (Chrysostom on Matthew: "because they are as angels, therefore they do not marry"; Pseudo-Chrysostom: "where the law of death is taken away, the cause of birth is taken away likewise") — so angels who "left their own habitation" (Jude 6) fall outside it. But the win is defensive and partial: it removes only the Matthew 22:30 prop, leaving Chrysostom's deeper incorporeality premise to be met (if at all) by a separate embodiment mechanism (Wilson's oikētērion, weak; De Young's sacral-marriage rite, sound).60

Does Beowulf witness the angelic reading of Genesis 6? (assessment: weak)

Beowulf as a witness to the angelic reading — Doug Wilson lists "the Beowulf tradition" alongside Josephus and 1 Enoch as the "entire ancient world" reading Genesis 6 as a "perverse celestial/earthly connection." But the Old English poem genealogizes Grendel and the gigantas from Cain (Gen 4, "in Caines cynne"), a human ancestor — and depicts the Flood drowning that "race of giants … estranged from God." It is a real reception witness to the antediluvian-giant-and-Flood tradition (and the poet had absorbed Enochic giant-lore — Kaske), but it routes the giants through Cain's human line, the Augustinian/Sethite-adjacent harmonization (Mellinkoff, Orchard) that displaced the angelic reading — so on the disputed point it witnesses for the rival camp, not Wilson's.61
