Jerome on Simon Peter

Dates: c. 393 · Wikipedia
Summary: The compact Latin summary that fixed Peter's biography for the West. Jerome condenses the Greek tradition into a single paragraph — origin, Antioch, Rome, the twenty-five-year chair, the head-downward crucifixion, and the Vatican grave — and, along the way, sorts the genuine Petrine writings from the apocryphal.

Claims & evidence

The whole life in one paragraph

Simon Peter the son of John, from the village of Bethsaida in the province of Galilee, brother of Andrew the apostle, and himself chief of the apostles, after having been bishop of the church of Antioch and having preached to the Dispersion—the believers in circumcision, in Pontus, Galatia, Cappadocia, Asia and Bithynia—pushed on to Rome in the second year of Claudius to over-throw Simon Magus, and held the sacerdotal chair there for twenty-five years until the last, that is the fourteenth, year of Nero. At his hands he received the crown of martyrdom being nailed to the cross with his head towards the ground and his feet raised on high, asserting that he was unworthy to be crucified in the same manner as his Lord.

— On Illustrious Men 1, source. Jerome follows Eusebius throughout: the mission field is Origen's, the “second year of Claudius” and the twenty-five-year episcopate are Eusebius's chronology, and the inverted crucifixion with its humility-motive comes from Origen and the Acts.

The Petrine writings, genuine and spurious — and the Vatican grave

He wrote two epistles which are called Catholic, the second of which, on account of its difference from the first in style, is considered by many not to be by him. … the books, of which one is entitled his Acts, another his Gospel, a third his Preaching, a fourth his Revelation, a fifth his “Judgment” are rejected as apocryphal. Buried at Rome in the Vatican near the triumphal way he is venerated by the whole world.

— On Illustrious Men 1, source. Jerome already brackets 2 Peter's authorship as doubted “by many,” and dismisses the Acts, Gospel, Preaching, Apocalypse, and Judgment of Peter as apocrypha — among them the very work that supplied the head-downward scene he had just reported.

Notes

  • Influence. Because Jerome's little book became the standard Latin reference, his one paragraph — not the scattered Greek fragments behind it — is what carried Peter's biography into the medieval West and the apostle-lists.
  • Jerome depends on Eusebius and Origen; his authority does not add an independent witness to the head-downward posture, which still traces back to the same second- and third-century roots weighed at Was Peter crucified head-downward?
  • Jerome appears across this wiki as a major witness in his own right — on the Antioch clash (Jerome and Augustine) and the Origenist controversy (Jerome and Rufinus).
