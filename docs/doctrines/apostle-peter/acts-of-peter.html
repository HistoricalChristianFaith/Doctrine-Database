The Acts of Peter on Simon Peter

Dates: c. 190 (the martyrdom material; the fuller Greek Acts of Peter and Paul is a later recension) · Wikipedia
Summary: A second-century apocryphal romance, of little worth as biography but of real value as evidence for how Peter was venerated. It is the source of the two most famous scenes of his death — the Quo vadis and the head-downward crucifixion — and the fountainhead of the inverted-cross tradition.

Claims & evidence

“Lord, whither art Thou going?” (the Quo vadis)

A few days ago, being exhorted by the brethren, I was going away; and my Lord Jesus Christ met me, and having adored Him, I said, Lord, whither art Thou going? And He said to me, I am going to Rome to be crucified. And I said to Him, Lord, wast Thou not crucified once for all? And the Lord answering, said, I saw thee fleeing from death, and I wish to be crucified instead of thee.

— Acts of Peter and Paul 42, source. Fleeing Rome to escape martyrdom, Peter meets Christ on the road; shamed by the vision, he turns back to die. The scene is the origin of the Domine, quo vadis? legend and the Roman church built on the Appian Way to mark it.

Crucified head-downward, “not worthy… like my Lord”

Since my Lord Jesus Christ, who came down from the heaven upon the earth, was raised upon the cross upright, and He has deigned to call to heaven me, who am of the earth, my cross ought to be fixed head down most, so as to direct my feet towards heaven; for I am not worthy to be crucified like my Lord. Then, having reversed the cross, they nailed his feet up.

— Acts of Peter and Paul 41, source. This later Greek recension gives the familiar humility phrasing — that Peter asked to be inverted so as not to imitate his Lord's death. The earliest form of the martyrdom, in the second-century Acts of Peter (the “Vercelli Acts,” preserved in M. R. James's translation), reads the reversed cross rather as a cosmic symbol — Adam fell “head downwards” and inverted the world, and Peter's posture sets creation upright again. The plain unworthiness-motive is thus a later softening; on how much of any of this is history, see Was Peter crucified head-downward?

Notes

  • Reliability. The apocryphal Acts are romance-hagiography, often carrying encratite or gnostic tendencies, and are of low biographical value; they are treated here as early witnesses to the apostles' veneration, never laundered into fact. See the shared caution on the apostles hub.
  • The Quo vadis and the inverted crucifixion have no independent early attestation outside this tradition; on how much weight the head-downward manner can bear, see Was Peter crucified head-downward?
