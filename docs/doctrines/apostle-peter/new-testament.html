The New Testament on Simon Peter

Dates: c. 30–90 (the span of the New Testament writings) · Wikipedia
Summary: The earliest and most reliable layer. It gives Peter's origin, family, name, and leadership in unusual detail, but of his death records only Jesus' prophecy of it — not the place or manner, which the later tradition supplies.

Claims & evidence

Origin: a fisherman of Bethsaida, brother of Andrew

Now Philip was of Bethsaida, the city of Andrew and Peter.

— Gospel of John, John 1:44. Simon and his brother Andrew are fishermen called from their nets by the Sea of Galilee, partners of James and John; the family works out of Capernaum, where Jesus stays in Peter's house.

The name: Simon renamed Cephas / Peter

And he brought him to Jesus. And when Jesus beheld him, he said, Thou art Simon the son of Jona: thou shalt be called Cephas, which is by interpretation, A stone.

— Gospel of John, John 1:42. “Cephas” is Aramaic kepha, “rock”; “Peter” renders it into Greek (Petros). On the confession at Caesarea Philippi Jesus builds the name into a saying:

And I say also unto thee, That thou art Peter, and upon this rock I will build my church; and the gates of hell shall not prevail against it.

— Gospel of Matthew, Matthew 16:18. (What the “rock” is, and what it implies for later claims of Roman primacy, is a separate question, treated under those doctrines, not here.)

Family: a married apostle

But Simon's wife's mother lay sick of a fever, and anon they tell him of her.

— Gospel of Mark, Mark 1:30. That Peter had a wife who accompanied him in his ministry is confirmed by Paul:

Have we not power to lead about a sister, a wife, as well as other apostles, and as the brethren of the Lord, and Cephas?

— 1 Corinthians, 1 Corinthians 9:5.

Leadership — and its limits: the clash at Antioch

Peter heads every Gospel list of the Twelve, confesses Jesus as the Christ, preaches the first Christian sermon at Pentecost, and leads the Jerusalem church through Acts 1–12. Yet his leadership is not portrayed as flawless: when he withdrew from eating with Gentile believers at Antioch, Paul opposed him publicly.

But when Peter was come to Antioch, I withstood him to the face, because he was to be blamed.

— Galatians, Galatians 2:11. (How to read this rebuke — a real fault, or a staged pretense — is itself an old dispute, weighed under Jerome and Augustine.)

His death: foretold, not narrated

Verily, verily, I say unto thee, When thou wast young, thou girdedst thyself, and walkedst whither thou wouldest: but when thou shalt be old, thou shalt stretch forth thy hands, and another shall gird thee, and carry thee whither thou wouldest not.

— Gospel of John, John 21:18–19, which the Evangelist glosses: “This spake he, signifying by what death he should glorify God.” The “stretching forth of the hands” was read by many Fathers as pointing to crucifixion. The New Testament names neither the place nor the manner of Peter's death.

“Babylon”: a hint of Rome

The church that is at Babylon, elected together with you, saluteth you; and so doth Marcus my son.

— 1 Peter, 1 Peter 5:13. Already in the second century “Babylon” was understood as a coded name for Rome (as it plainly is in Revelation), and Peter's association here with “Marcus” dovetails with the tradition that Mark set down Peter's preaching at Rome. This is the nearest the New Testament comes to placing Peter in the capital.
