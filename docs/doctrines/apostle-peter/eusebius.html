Eusebius of Caesarea on Simon Peter

Dates: c. 325 · Wikipedia
Summary: The master synthesis. Eusebius weaves the earlier witnesses into the first connected account of Peter's Roman ministry and death, and — crucially — preserves the fragments of Gaius, Dionysius of Corinth, and Origen on which the whole tradition rests.

Claims & evidence

Peter to Rome under Claudius, against Simon Magus

But this did not last long. For immediately, during the reign of Claudius, the all-good and gracious Providence, which watches over all things, led Peter, that strongest and greatest of the apostles, and the one who on account of his virtue was the speaker for all the others, to Rome against this great corrupter of life.

— Ecclesiastical History 2.14. In the next chapter, Peter's Roman hearers beg Mark, “a follower of Peter,” to leave them a written record of his preaching — the origin Eusebius assigns to the Gospel of Mark (2.15).

Peter crucified and Paul beheaded under Nero — with the confirming witnesses

It is, therefore, recorded that Paul was beheaded in Rome itself, and that Peter likewise was crucified under Nero. This account of Peter and Paul is substantiated by the fact that their names are preserved in the cemeteries of that place even to the present day.

— Ecclesiastical History 2.25. Eusebius then quotes two earlier witnesses. Gaius of Rome (c. 200):

But I can show the trophies of the apostles. For if you will go to the Vatican or to the Ostian way, you will find the trophies of those who laid the foundations of this church.

and Dionysius, bishop of Corinth (c. 170):

For both of them planted and likewise taught us in our Corinth. And they taught together in like manner in Italy, and suffered martyrdom at the same time.

— both in Ecclesiastical History 2.25. Eusebius adds Origen's report that Peter was “crucified head-downwards” at his own request (3.1).

Notes

  • A compilation, only as strong as its sources. Eusebius's value is that he preserves the older witnesses — Gaius's “trophies” and Dionysius's testimony are known to us only through him. Each tradition he relays must be judged on its own date, not taken as a block; see the apostles hub.
  • Gaius's “trophy” on the Vatican is the literary anchor of the twentieth-century excavations beneath St Peter's, discussed at Did Peter lead and die at Rome?
