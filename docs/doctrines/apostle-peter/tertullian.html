Tertullian on Simon Peter

Dates: c. 200 · Wikipedia
Summary: The earliest surviving writer to state plainly that Peter died by crucifixion at Rome. He specifies the mode (a cross) but not the posture — nothing here of the head-downward detail.

Claims & evidence

“Where Peter endures a passion like his Lord's”

Since, moreover, you are close upon Italy, you have Rome, from which there comes even into our own hands the very authority (of apostles themselves). How happy is its church, on which apostles poured forth all their doctrine along with their blood! where Peter endures a passion like his Lord's! where Paul wins his crown in a death like John's where the Apostle John was first plunged, unhurt, into boiling oil, and thence remitted to his island-exile!

— Prescription against Heretics 36, source. “A passion like his Lord's” means death on a cross, as Jesus died; Tertullian sets it beside Paul's beheading (“a death like John's,” i.e. the Baptist's) and John's escape from the boiling oil. Elsewhere he adds the agent: “that Peter is girt by another, when he is bound to the cross” (Scorpiace 15), reading John 21:18 of the crucifixion.

Notes

  • What it establishes. By c. 200, in Latin Africa, the manner of Peter's death was fixed as crucifixion at Rome — independent of the Greek witnesses and earlier than Origen. This is a load-bearing link in the case for Peter at Rome.
  • What it does not say. Tertullian, who elsewhere delights in vivid detail, says nothing of an inverted cross. His silence is one reason the head-downward posture — first recorded a generation later by Origen — is judged a legendary accretion; see Was Peter crucified head-downward?
