Clement of Rome on Simon Peter

Dates: c. 96 · Wikipedia
Summary: The earliest witness after the New Testament to Peter's martyrdom. Writing from Rome to Corinth within a generation of the event, Clement treats the deaths of Peter and Paul as recent and celebrated — but names neither the manner nor the place.

Claims & evidence

Peter and Paul, “the greatest and most righteous pillars,” put to death

But not to dwell upon ancient examples, let us come to the most recent spiritual heroes. Let us take the noble examples furnished in our own generation. Through envy and jealousy, the greatest and most righteous pillars [of the Church] have been persecuted and put to death. Let us set before our eyes the illustrious apostles. Peter, through unrighteous envy, endured not one or two, but numerous labours and when he had at length suffered martyrdom, departed to the place of glory due to him.

— First Epistle to the Corinthians 5, source. Clement pairs Peter with Paul, who “after preaching both in the east and west … came to the extreme limit of the west, and suffered martyrdom under the prefects.”

Notes

  • What it establishes. That Peter was martyred was already common knowledge in Rome by the 90s, told as a matter of “our own generation.” Written from Rome, and coupling Peter with Paul (whose Roman death is not disputed), the passage is a cornerstone of the argument that Peter too died at Rome — see Did Peter lead and die at Rome?
  • What it does not say. Clement gives no method (no cross, let alone an inverted one) and no place by name; the martyrdom's Roman setting is inferred from his standpoint and context, not stated. He is silent on the head-downward detail that later sources add.
  • Clement appears elsewhere on this wiki as an early witness in his own right; his letter is dated c. 96, the traditional close of the apostolic age.
