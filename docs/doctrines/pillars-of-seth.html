The Pillars of Seth

According to a tradition first written down by the Jewish historian Josephus (c. AD 93–94), the righteous descendants of Seth were the inventors of astronomy — "that peculiar sort of wisdom which is concerned with the heavenly bodies, and their order" — and, warned by Adam's prophecy that the world would be destroyed once by fire and once by water, they inscribed their discoveries on two pillars, one of brick and one of stone, so that whichever catastrophe came, the knowledge would survive. The stone pillar, Josephus adds, still stood "in the land of Siriad" in his own day. This page follows that legend of antediluvian wisdom preserved through the Flood — where it came from, and what later readers made of it.

The interest of the story is that it is a pious counter-aetiology of astronomy. The very celestial science that one ancient stream — the Watcher tradition of 1 Enoch 8, taken up by Tatian and Tertullian — blamed on the rebel angels who "taught" it to enslave mankind, this tradition credits instead to the holy line of Seth, discovering it by their own virtue.1 The same knowledge, two origins — impious revelation or righteous discovery — and this legend supplies the righteous half of the pair (weighed on the astrology timeline's fallen-angels argument). It also sits at the crossroads of two further doctrines: it is a knowledge-survives-the-deluge motif, tied to Noah's Flood; and it belongs to the wider lore of the antediluvian sages — with Enoch the seventh from Adam — that runs through the sons of God and the Nephilim.2

Timeline

Listed earliest first. Josephus is the earliest datable witness to the legend in the form the later tradition received; the story's crossroads with the origin of astronomy, the Flood, and the antediluvian sages are noted above.

Josephus (c. 100)

Josephus gives the legend its classic form in the Antiquities of the Jews (I.2.3). Seth's posterity, virtuous men, "were the inventors of that peculiar sort of wisdom which is concerned with the heavenly bodies, and their order"; and foreseeing on "Adam's prediction that the world was to be destroyed at one time by the force of fire, and at another time by the violence and quantity of water, they made two pillars, the one of brick, the other of stone," inscribing their discoveries on both so that one would outlast whichever destruction came — the stone pillar, he reports, still "remains in the land of Siriad to this day."3

Josephus himself preserves, elsewhere in the same book, the rival account of celestial knowledge — the "many angels of God" who consorted with women and begat unjust giants (I.3.1) — so the two origins of astronomy, the Sethite and the angelic, sit side by side in his own text (his angelic passage is treated on the Nephilim timeline).4
