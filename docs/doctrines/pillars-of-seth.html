The Pillars of Seth

According to a tradition first written down by the Jewish historian Josephus (c. AD 93–94), the righteous descendants of Seth were the inventors of astronomy — "that peculiar sort of wisdom which is concerned with the heavenly bodies, and their order" — and, warned by Adam's prophecy that the world would be destroyed once by fire and once by water, they inscribed their discoveries on two pillars, one of brick and one of stone, so that whichever catastrophe came, the knowledge would survive. The stone pillar, Josephus adds, still stood "in the land of Siriad" in his own day. This page follows that legend of antediluvian wisdom preserved through the Flood — where it came from, and what later readers made of it.

The interest of the story is that it is a pious counter-aetiology of astronomy. The very celestial science that one ancient stream — the Watcher tradition of 1 Enoch 8, taken up by Tatian and Tertullian — blamed on the rebel angels who "taught" it to enslave mankind, this tradition credits instead to the holy line of Seth, discovering it by their own virtue.1 The same knowledge, two origins — impious revelation or righteous discovery — and this legend supplies the righteous half of the pair (weighed on the astrology timeline's fallen-angels argument). It also sits at the crossroads of two further doctrines: it is a knowledge-survives-the-deluge motif, tied to Noah's Flood; and it belongs to the wider lore of the antediluvian sages — with Enoch the seventh from Adam — that runs through the sons of God and the Nephilim.2 That dignifying of Seth's posterity as the righteous race is itself older than the legend: a generation before Josephus, Philo of Alexandria (early 1st c.) already made Seth the "author" of the race of "those who love virtue and piety" — the virtuous line "enlarged" down through Noah, Abraham, and Moses. But Philo's Seth is pure moral allegory: no two pillars, no astronomy, no antediluvian discoveries carried through the Flood. The knowledge-preserving legend this page follows is Josephus's addition, not Philo's — so Philo attests the theme the legend presupposes, not the legend itself.3

Timeline

Listed earliest first. Josephus is the earliest datable witness to the legend in the form the later tradition received. From him it passed not through the doctrinal Fathers — who are silent on it — but through the chronicles and Bible-histories: in the Latin West, Isidore of Seville's Chronica (c. 620), Peter Comestor's Historia Scholastica (c. 1170), and Ranulf Higden's Polychronicon (c. 1350). Along that Latin chain the legend drifted — Josephus's righteous line of Seth became, with Comestor, the Cainite musician Jubal — so that what the medieval West often received under the story's name were the pillars of Cain's line. The story's crossroads with the origin of astronomy, the Flood, and the antediluvian sages are noted above.

Josephus (c. 100)

Josephus gives the legend its classic form in the Antiquities of the Jews (I.2.3). Seth's posterity, virtuous men, "were the inventors of that peculiar sort of wisdom which is concerned with the heavenly bodies, and their order"; and foreseeing on "Adam's prediction that the world was to be destroyed at one time by the force of fire, and at another time by the violence and quantity of water, they made two pillars, the one of brick, the other of stone," inscribing their discoveries on both so that one would outlast whichever destruction came — the stone pillar, he reports, still "remains in the land of Siriad to this day."4

Josephus himself preserves, elsewhere in the same book, the rival account of celestial knowledge — the "many angels of God" who consorted with women and begat unjust giants (I.3.1) — so the two origins of astronomy, the Sethite and the angelic, sit side by side in his own text (his angelic passage is treated on the Nephilim timeline).5

Isidore of Seville (c. 620)

Isidore carries the legend into the Latin West. His universal chronicle, the Chronica, reports "as Josephus reports, some men, who knew that they were about to die by either fire or water, inscribed their discoveries on two columns made of brick and stone," the stone columns said to "remain in Syria to this very day." Isidore tells it generically, of "some men," and lodges it one entry below his notice of the Cainite Jubal, "from the line of Cain," inventor of music before the flood — the two ingredients a later hand would fuse. (The legend is not in his better-known Etymologies, which carries only the Cainite-music note.)6

Peter Comestor (c. 1170)

Peter Comestor's Historia Scholastica — the "school Bible" of the medieval West, surviving in some 800 manuscripts and the great conduit for Josephus's material — retells the pillars and cites Josephus by name, yet makes the decisive change: he ascribes them not to Seth's line but to the Cainite musician Jubal. "He wrote it on two columns, the whole of it on each, as Josephus says — one of marble, the other of brick — of which the one would not be washed away by the flood, the other not dissolved by fire." With Comestor the pillars of Seth become, in the West, the pillars of Cain's line.7

Ranulf Higden (c. 1350)

Ranulf Higden's Polychronicon — Englished by John Trevisa in 1387 — carries both forms. Its main text keeps the plain Josephan version, "men" who foresaw fire and flood and "closede hem in tweie greet pileres i-made of marbyl and of brend tyle"; but in the chapter on Jubal several manuscripts splice in Comestor's Cainite retelling verbatim. The one chronicle thus documents the legend's two streams — the older generic/Sethite and the medieval Latin Cainite — and it is the Polychronicon that the later Masonic "Old Charges" (the Cooke MS) would quarry.8

Arguments

Related arguments — the origins of the legend

Four source-critical questions about where the pillars legend came from and how far to trust it: whether its fire-and-water framing was already an ancient tradition, whether Josephus's specific report (Antiquities I.2.3) is reliable history, whether the Sethite legend is, at bottom, a recasting of the older Enoch tradition, and whether it is the pious inversion of an older impious mirror — the Watchers' illicit astronomy, carved on stone and surviving the Flood, whose recovery is a sin.

Is Josephus's pillars-of-Seth report historical? (assessment: contested)

Is Josephus's pillars-of-Seth report historical? weighs the two objections William Whiston attached to his own translation: that Josephus confused Seth son of Adam with the pharaoh Sesostris, the actual erector of a pillar "in the land of Siriad," and that no stone pillar could have survived a world-covering flood. The verdict is contested, on a split the story itself invites. As a tradition — antediluvian discoveries kept through catastrophe on paired durable pillars — the legend is genuinely ancient and coherent, with independent Babylonian (Berossus) and Egyptian (Manetho / the Hermetic Book of Sothis) analogues; but Josephus's specific report, that the very Sethite stone pillar still stood in his day, is weak — it certifies an unseen monument that matches the Egyptian Hermes-stelae far better than the Sethite frame, and its survival-logic strains against the universal Flood the tradition presupposes. Whiston's headline objection nonetheless misfires: the Seth-for-Sesostris conflation rests on reading Josephus's Seiris as "Syria" (it denotes Egypt, land of the Sirius-worshippers) and on Herodotus's separately-misidentified Sesostris stelae, so the alleged confusion is Whiston's inference, not Josephus's blunder.9

Is the pillars-of-Seth legend a displaced Enoch tradition? (assessment: plausible)

Is the pillars-of-Seth legend a displaced Enoch tradition? asks whether Josephus's story is, at bottom, an Enoch tradition wearing Seth's name. The roles Josephus gives the Sethites — discovering the science of the heavenly bodies, writing it down, and inscribing it on two durable media so it survives the coming destructions by fire and water — are the very roles Jewish lore reserves for Enoch, "the seventh from Adam": Jubilees 4:17 makes him the first man to learn writing and record "the signs of heaven," the Astronomical Book of 1 Enoch (72–82) has Uriel reveal him the calendar, and the closest formal parallel — the sage inscribing his knowledge on marble and brick against fire and flood — is told of Enoch himself in the Byzantine Palaea Historica. The verdict is plausible. The narrow point is sound and mainstream: the pillars story's roles are Enochic, and the pious counter-aetiology this page follows is, in substance, Enoch's. But that the Sethite legend is specifically a displacement of a prior Enochic pillars-story does not clearly beat the reading that an independently ancient virtuous-Seth theme (Philo) and a non-Enochic Egyptian/Babylonian stele-tradition (Manetho, Berossus) were equally in play — parallel branches of one antediluvian-sage complex, cross-pollinating. And the named "pillars of Enoch" is a late Masonic back-formation (Anderson, 1723), not ancient attestation.10

Is the fire-and-water double-destruction an ancient tradition? (assessment: sound)

Is the fire-and-water double-destruction an ancient tradition? tests William Whiston's note that the expectation of a world destroyed once by fire and once by water — the premise the whole legend hangs on — is not Josephus's invention but "an ancient tradition, which all antiquity witnesses." The verdict is sound. The twofold-catastrophe pattern is attested independently across three streams that do not derive from Josephus: Plato presents it as old Egyptian priestly lore (Timaeus 22c: "the greatest [destructions] are by fire and water"); Berossus and the Stoics fix it as the great-year cycle of ekpyrosis and kataklysmos; and the first-century Jewish Life of Adam and Eve supplies the closest parallel of all — Seth inscribing the family testament on paired tablets of stone and clay against a judgment "first by water and second by fire," each medium chosen to outlast the catastrophe the other cannot. So Josephus inherited the legend's framing rather than fabricating it. Two limits hold the finding to its scope: it certifies the framing, not Josephus's separate report that a real Sethite pillar still stood (weighed on the historicity page); and Whiston's further move — that Adam himself foretold the two destructions — is the legend's narrative dress, not part of what "all antiquity witnesses."11

Is the pillars-of-Seth legend the pious inversion of the Watchers' antediluvian astrology? (assessment: plausible)

Is the pillars-of-Seth legend the pious inversion of the Watchers' antediluvian astrology? asks whether the pious device — celestial knowledge carved on stone, surviving the Flood, recovered afterward — is an inversion of an older impious mirror. It is: Jubilees 8 tells the same stone-and-Flood story of the Watchers' illicit astronomy, whose post-flood recovery by the second Cainan is a sin ("he transcribed it and sinned owing to it; for it contained the teaching of the Watchers… the omens of the sun and moon and stars"), not a rescue — and that impious telling (Jubilees, 2nd c. BC) is roughly two centuries older than Josephus's pious one. The verdict is plausible. The descriptive parallel is sound — the summary's "impious revelation vs. righteous discovery" pairing is realized in a shared physical device with a fixed order, impious first — and later tradition (the medieval Sefer ha-Yashar) even collapses the two Cainans into one stone-inscribing sage. But the stronger claim, that Josephus's legend is a pious inversion of the Cainan-stone tradition, is underdetermined: the mirror is partial (Cainan merely finds the stone; he designs no dual-catastrophe archive), and a shared motif does not prove dependence — the same limit the Enochic-displacement thesis meets, of which this is the dark twin.12
