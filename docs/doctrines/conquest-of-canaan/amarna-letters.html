The Amarna Letters — the Habiru, and Shechem handed over — on the Conquest of Canaan

Dates: the Amarna archive, c. 1360–1332 BC (the reigns of Amenhotep III and Akhenaten) · Wikipedia
Summary: Roughly 380 clay cuneiform tablets, found in 1887 at Tell el-Amarna (ancient Akhetaten) in Egypt, preserve the diplomatic correspondence between Pharaoh and his Canaanite and Syrian vassal city-kings in the fourteenth century BC. In letter after letter the kinglets of the land beg Egypt for archers against roving ʿApiru (Habiru) bands who are seizing towns "by force and guile" — ʿAbdi-Ḥeba of Jerusalem reporting that "the land of the king deserted to the Ḫapiru," and accusing Labʾayu of Shechem of handing his city over to them. The letters are a rich window onto Canaan's political collapse in the very century an early-date chronology assigns to the conquest. What they are — the letters, the Habiru, the peaceful transfer of Shechem — is set out here; whether those Habiru are the biblical Hebrews, and whether Shechem's handover answers to Joshua 8 and 24, are contested inferences weighed in the Arguments section, not asserted here.

Claims & evidence

An Egyptian diplomatic archive of the fourteenth century BC

In 1887 a peasant woman digging for decayed mud-brick (sebakh) at Tell el-Amarna, midway between Memphis and Thebes, turned up the first of what became some 380 inscribed clay tablets — the archive of the "Foreign Office" of Akhenaten's short-lived capital, Akhetaten. Written in Akkadian, the diplomatic lingua franca of the age, they are letters to and from the Egyptian court over roughly three decades, c. 1360–1332 BC. A large block of them are appeals from the mayors and kinglets of Canaan and the Levantine coast — Jerusalem, Shechem, Gezer, Lachish, Megiddo, Byblos, and others — pleading for Egyptian troops as their towns fall to raiders and to each other. They are the single richest external source for the political condition of Canaan in the Late Bronze Age.

— Standard edition: William L. Moran, The Amarna Letters (Baltimore: Johns Hopkins University Press, 1992); overview at Wikipedia, "Amarna letters" (article).

"The land of the king deserted to the Ḫapiru" (EA 290)

Here is the deed against the land that Milkilu and Shuardata did: against the land of the king, my lord, they ordered troops from Gazru, troops from Gimtu, and troops from Qiltu. They seized Rubutu. The land of the king deserted to the Ḫapiru.

And, later in the same short letter, the threatened repeat:

If there are no archers, the land of the king will desert to the Ḫapiru.

— ʿAbdi-Ḥeba, king of Jerusalem, to Pharaoh, EA 290 (Moran translation), quoted at Wikipedia, "Amarna letter EA 290" (article). "Ḫapiru" / "ʿApiru" (also written Habiru, or logographically SA.GAZ) is the recurring term for the marauding bands the Canaanite kings say are stripping them of the king's towns.

"The ʿApiru have plundered all the lands of the king" (EA 286)

(The) ʿApiru have plundered all the lands of the king.

Abdi-Ḥeba caps the report with the standing demand that runs through the whole Canaanite correspondence:

If there are archers this year, the lands of the king, my lord, will remain.

— ʿAbdi-Ḥeba of Jerusalem to Pharaoh, EA 286 (Moran translation), quoted at Wikipedia, "Amarna letter EA 286" (article). In the same letter Abdi-Ḥeba insists his throne is Pharaoh's gift, not an inheritance — "neither my father nor my mother put me in this place, but the strong arm of the king brought me into my father's house."

Shechem changes hands without an Egyptian battle: Labʾayu "giving the land of Šakmu to the Habiru" (EA 289, 287)

The most striking datum for a conquest reader is that Shechem — Akkadian Šakmu, the great central-hill city (Tell Balata, by modern Nablus) — is never reported besieged. It is reported handed over. Abdi-Ḥeba of Jerusalem denounces its ruler Labʾayu for exactly that:

Are we to act like Labaya when he was giving the land of Šakmu to the Hapiru?

— ʿAbdi-Ḥeba of Jerusalem to Pharaoh, EA 289 (Moran translation), quoted at Wikipedia, "Amarna letter EA 289" (article).

A companion letter lays the collapse of the king's lands at the door of Milkilu (of Gezer) and Labʾayu's sons together:

This is the deed of Milkilu and the deed of the sons(=cohorts) of Lab'ayu, who have given the land of the king (to) the ['Apiru].

— ʿAbdi-Ḥeba of Jerusalem to Pharaoh, EA 287 (Moran translation), quoted at Wikipedia, "Amarna letter EA 287" (article). Labʾayu was a powerful, semi-independent ruler of Shechem known only from this corpus, named in some fourteen letters; the king of Megiddo (EA 246) likewise charges that "two sons of Labʾayu have given their money to the ḧabiru." Labʾayu himself answers the accusation in his own letters (EA 252–254), protesting his loyalty — so the territory of Shechem passing to the Habiru is attested from more than one side of the correspondence.

City-states falling one by one

The Jerusalem letters are part of a wider drumbeat of loss. In EA 288 Abdi-Ḥeba reports named local rulers being killed and towns going over — Turbazu slain in a city gate, Zimredda of Lachish struck down by men "joined to the ʿApiru," Yaptih-Hadda slain — and describes his own isolation as being "a ship in the midst of the sea," with "the ʿApiru" taking "the very cities of the king." From the coast, Rib-Hadda of Byblos fills scores of letters (EA 68–138) with the same complaint, and Milkilu of Gezer (EA 271) begs relief against "the power of the ʿApiru." Whatever the ʿApiru were, the archive testifies to a real, broad breakdown of the Egyptian vassal order in Canaan across the mid-fourteenth century.

— EA 288 (Moran translation), Wikipedia, "Amarna letter EA 288" (article); EA 271 (article).

Notes

  • "Habiru / ʿapiru" is a social designation, not an ethnonym. This is the pivot on which the whole synchronism turns, and it must be stated plainly: mainstream scholarship does not equate the Habiru with Israel. The term (written logographically SA.GAZ) is used across the whole ancient Near East for most of the second millennium — at Mari, Nuzi, Ugarit, and in Egyptian sources — for uprooted, stateless, or outlaw people: fugitives, runaways, brigands, dispossessed persons, and mercenaries on the fringe of the settled city-state order, hiring out or raiding on any side. In the Amarna letters specifically, to brand a rival a "ʿApiru" is largely a political smear meaning "rebel against Pharaoh." The proposed link between the word ʿapiru and Hebrew ʿibrî is possible but debated; even if sound, it would connect a word, not identify these bands with the nation of Joshua. So the letters are common ground; the step from "Habiru" to "the Hebrews of the conquest" is a separate, contested inference — weighed on its own argument page, not here.
  • The Habiru as despised outsiders. The Amarna kings speak of the ʿApiru with contempt, likening them (as of Aziru of Amurru, EA 67) to a "stray dog" — the stock image for a rootless, masterless raider — which fits the "landless outlaw" sense of the term rather than any single people.
  • Two contested synchronisms are split off, by design. This page states the data; the two load-bearing inferences are adjudicated adversarially in the summary's Arguments section (those argument pages are being added): (a) that the Habiru of these letters are the biblical Hebrews, and (b) that Shechem's handover to the Habiru answers to Shechem's un-besieged place in Joshua and to the covenant assembly of Joshua 8 and 24. Both are favored by early-date apologists and doubted by the mainstream; the honest verdicts belong on those pages.
  • The force of the correlation rests on an early-date chronology. The letters date to c. 1360–1332 BC. They line up with a conquest only on the early-date (~1400 BC) scheme for the Exodus and conquest — itself the minority position, and itself weighed on its own page, Does the conquest date to c. 1400 BC or c. 1250 BC? (contested). On the more widely held late-date (~1250 BC) scheme the archive predates the conquest, and on the dominant secular reconstruction Joshua is a later origin narrative rather than reportage. The datum underdetermines the larger question.
  • "Kings of Canaan." The corpus addresses and names the local rulers as "kings" and even uses the phrase "kings of Canaan" (e.g. EA 30; cf. EA 8, 109) — the same idiom Scripture uses of the Canaanite rulers Israel faced (Judges 5:19; cf. Joshua 5:1). It is a shared vocabulary of the period, not by itself a proof of the conquest.
