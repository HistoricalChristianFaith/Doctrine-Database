Does the conquest date to c. 1400 BC (the early date) or c. 1250 BC (the late date)?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — a genuine, unresolved dispute in which biblical chronology pulls one way and Egyptology and field archaeology mostly pull the other: read literally, 1 Kings 6:1 and Judges 11:26 place the conquest c. 1400 BC, while the store-city name "Rameses" (Exodus 1:11) and the thirteenth-century clustering of Canaanite destruction layers place it c. 1250 BC; neither case is decisive, and beneath both lies a still deeper mainstream doubt that any invasion happened at all · advanced for the early date by Bryant Wood and Associates for Biblical Research, Titus Kennedy, and Douglas Petrovich; for the late date by Kenneth Kitchen and the broad Egyptological consensus

Why this page comes first. This is the linchpin of the whole doctrine. The tightest correlations the timeline offers — the identification of the Amarna Habiru with the Hebrews, and the reading of Shechem's peaceful handover against Joshua — line up only on the early date. On the late date the Amarna archive (c. 1350 BC) sits a century before the conquest, and the synchronism dissolves. So the strength of those arguments is conditional on the answer weighed here; that dependency is the single most important thing this page establishes.

The claim

"1 Kgs 6:1 and 1 Chr 6:33–37 converge on a date of 1446 BC for the exodus and the Jubilees data and Judges 11:26 independently converge on a date of 1406 BC for the beginning of the conquest."

— Associates for Biblical Research, "The Biblical Date for the Exodus Is 1446 BC". Against this, Kenneth Kitchen — while defending the substantial reliability of the Old Testament — holds that the Exodus belongs to the thirteenth century, under Ramesses II, and that the 480 years are a schematic figure, not a literal span.1 The question this page adjudicates is not "who is more pious" but which reconstruction the evidence, taken whole, best supports.

Who advances it

The early date (Exodus c. 1446 BC, conquest c. 1406 BC) is the traditional reading, defended today chiefly by conservative evangelicals: Bryant Wood and Associates for Biblical Research, Titus Kennedy, Douglas Petrovich, John Bimson, and Eugene Merrill. The late date (Exodus c. 1260–1250 BC, conquest c. 1210 BC) is the majority position among those who grant a historical Exodus at all; its most formidable defender is the Egyptologist Kenneth Kitchen, who is otherwise the leading conservative champion of Old Testament historicity — the late date is precisely where he parts from the traditional camp. Standing behind and beneath both is a third position — the dominant one in academic archaeology — associated with Israel Finkelstein and William Dever's more skeptical readers, which doubts that any external conquest took place, and for which the whole early-vs-late question is downstream of a prior doubt.2

The evidence for

For the early date. The backbone is 1 Kings 6:1: Solomon began the Temple "in the four hundred and eightieth year after the people of Israel came out of the land of Egypt," in his fourth regnal year. With that year fixed near 967–966 BC, counting back 479 years yields an Exodus c. 1446 BC and, after the forty years' wandering, a conquest c. 1406 BC. A second, independent biblical datum appears to converge: in Judges 11:26 Jephthah tells the Ammonites that Israel had held Heshbon and the Arnon towns "three hundred years" — a figure that, added to the later judges and the early monarchy, points back toward a fifteenth-century entry rather than a thirteenth. Some early-date proponents add an archaeological prop: Bryant Wood re-dated the fallen walls of Jericho City IV to c. 1400 BC (against Kathleen Kenyon's c. 1550), arguing the destruction fits Joshua 6 — a claim weighed on its own at the Jericho-dating argument (assessment: contested, leaning against the c. 1400 date).3 The decisive attraction, for this wiki's purposes, is that only on this chronology do the Amarna synchronisms line up: a conquest c. 1400 BC puts the fall of the Canaanite city-states and the Habiru turmoil of the 1350s BC squarely in the era of Joshua and the judges.

For the late date. The backbone is Exodus 1:11: the Israelites built "the store cities, Pithom and Rameses," for Pharaoh. The delta city Pi-Ramesses (modern Qantir / Tell el-Dabʿa) was the residence built by Ramesses II (reigned 1279–1213 BC), the nineteenth-dynasty king; it flourished only in his reign and was abandoned by c. 1069 BC. Naming it as a live construction project points the oppression, and so the Exodus, to the thirteenth century. Kitchen adds that the 480 years of 1 Kings 6:1 are best read as twelve schematic generations (12 × 40) — and that twelve actual generations at a realistic ~25 years each total ~300 years, landing the Exodus back in the thirteenth century, not the fifteenth. Field archaeology is generally read as reinforcing this: the destruction layers at Hazor, Lachish, Bethel, and other candidate sites cluster in the late thirteenth century, and the first firm external anchor for Israel — the Merneptah Stele (c. 1208 BC) — sits at the tail of that same century.4

Adversarial assessment

What holds on each side. The early date has the plain sense of the numbers: 1 Kings 6:1 gives a total, not a genealogy, and Judges 11:26 puts a specific "three hundred years" in a character's mouth for a specific rhetorical purpose — both resist being explained away as mere symbolism, and their rough convergence is a real datum. The late date has the toponym and the dirt: "Rameses" is a nineteenth-dynasty name, the excavated destruction horizons do cluster in the thirteenth century, and the Merneptah Stele fixes Israel in Canaan by c. 1208 BC. Neither foundation is soft; that is precisely why the dispute persists.

Where each side is overstated. The early date's archaeological prop is its weakest link: Wood's re-dating of Jericho is a minority position, and the tell is so eroded that a Late-Bronze city there at all is disputed — the biblical chronology, not the spade, is doing the real work. The late date's reading of "Rameses" is likewise not airtight: early-date scholars note the name already appears for a region in Genesis 47:11 (Joseph's day), and argue that the form in Exodus 1:11 may be an editorial updating of an older place-name for later readers — a real possibility, though it cannot be simply assumed. And Kitchen's twelve-generation reading of the 480 has been challenged on the ground that 1 Chronicles 6:33–37 lists roughly eighteen generations from the Exodus to the Temple singers, not twelve — so the schematic reading is not obviously the natural one. Crucially, neither literal proof-text is independent of the other's rebuttal: the whole case is a weighing of probabilities, not a proof.5

The deeper doubt, stated fairly. Much of academic archaeology — Israel Finkelstein most prominently — doubts a historical conquest of either date, reading the Joshua narrative as a seventh-century ideological composition and the Iron I highland villages as the settling of a largely indigenous Canaanite population rather than the arrival of invaders. This is not a naturalistic prejudice against the miraculous; it is a reading of the survey and pottery data, and it must be met on that ground, not dismissed. Against it, Kitchen presses a point that softens the sharpest objection: Joshua and Judges themselves describe a conquest that was gradual and partial — raids and campaigns, not a single total devastation with towns left standing "on their mounds" (Joshua 11:13) — so the absence of a uniform destruction layer is not by itself the refutation it is often taken to be. That defends historicity in general; it does not, by itself, decide between the early and late dates.6

Assessment

Contested. This is a live dispute with no clear winner, and it is the textbook case of a claim that is strong by one discipline's lights and weak by another's: biblical chronology, read at face value, favours the early date (c. 1406 BC); Egyptology and the balance of field archaeology favour the late date (c. 1210 BC); and the dominant secular reconstruction doubts an invasion of either date. Each camp's proof-text carries real weight and each has a real answer, so no honest reckoning declares a winner. What this wiki can state without hedging is the dependency the timeline turns on: the force of the Amarna arguments (Habiru = Hebrews; Shechem's handover) is conditional on the early date. Grant the early date and they become a striking synchronism; take the mainstream late date and they lapse, because the Amarna turmoil then precedes the conquest by a century. Those arguments should therefore be read as saying, "if the early date holds, then …" — not as independent proof that it does. The Merneptah Stele is the one fixed point both sides share: Israel was a people in Canaan by c. 1208 BC, which is a terminus after both schemes and so cannot itself break the tie.
