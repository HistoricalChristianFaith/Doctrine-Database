Is the Iron I structure on Mount Ebal the covenant altar of Joshua 8?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — the interpretation splits cleanly at two joints, and the split is the honest verdict. That the Mt. Ebal structure is a genuine early Iron I cultic installation is well grounded and near-consensus (accepted, at least in outline, even by scholars who reject Zertal — Mazar, and reportedly Finkelstein). That it is specifically the altar of Joshua 8:30–35 / Deuteronomy 27 is genuinely disputed, with no clear winner: the correspondences are real and, unlike Jericho and Ai, do not depend on the contested early date — an Iron I altar sits comfortably on the mainstream late date — but the leap from "a highland cult place c. 1200 BC" to "Joshua's altar" is drawn from the biblical text, not compelled by the ground · advanced by Adam Zertal (excavator) and taken up by conservative writers (Bryan Windle / Bible Archaeology Report, Answers in Genesis, Titus Kennedy); the leading dissents are Aharon Kempinski (watchtower), Israel Finkelstein and Nadav Na'aman (a cult site, but not Joshua's altar), and Anson Rainey (farmstead)

What this page does and does not decide. This weighs an identification — is the excavated installation on Mt. Ebal the altar Joshua 8 says Joshua built there? — on the archaeology and the text. Two questions are kept apart throughout, because they get different answers: (1) is the structure a cult installation at all? (widely accepted), and (2) is it the specific altar of the covenant ceremony? (disputed). Unlike its neighbours on this doctrine — the dating of Jericho's City IV and the identification of Ai — this case is not conditional on the early (~1400 BC) chronology: the structure is Iron I (c. 1200 BC), so it fits the mainstream late date without strain, which is a point in its favour, not against it. The agreed physical facts are set out on the witness page; this page weighs what they do and do not establish. The wiki's rule against ruling out the supernatural is not really engaged here: nothing in the altar claim requires a miracle, only that a particular people built a particular structure.

The claim

Has Joshua's altar been found on Mt. Ebal?

— the title under which Adam Zertal, the excavator, put the case in 1985. The structure he had uncovered on the mountain of the covenant curses answered, he argued, point for point to the altar the law commands to be built there: an altar of whole, unhewn stones untouched by iron (Deuteronomy 27:5–6; Exodus 20:25), approached by a ramp and not steps (Exodus 20:26, "Neither shalt thou go up by steps unto mine altar"), on Mt. Ebal itself (Deuteronomy 27:4; Joshua 8:30), used for burnt offerings of the permitted animals — and, Zertal added, of a plan resembling the great altar later described for the Jerusalem temple. On this reading the site is not merely a cult place but the covenant altar of Joshua 8:30–35, Israel's central sanctuary before Shiloh.1

The agreed datum

The starting facts, set out in full on the witness page, are not seriously in dispute. On the north-eastern slope of Mt. Ebal, Zertal excavated (1982–1989) a rectangular structure of undressed field-stones, roughly 9 × 7 m, filled with earth and ash rather than floored, approached by a stone ramp, built over an earlier round installation and then deliberately buried around 1140 BC after less than a century of use. In and around it lay a heavy ash layer and more than three thousand burnt animal bones — young, mostly male sheep, goats, and cattle, with no pig — analysed by Liora Kolska Horwitz as the debris of sacrifice. The pottery and two Egyptian scarabs place it in the Late Bronze–Iron I transition, c. 13th–12th century BC. That this is an early Iron I installation, and that it is cultic rather than domestic, is the common ground on which the whole dispute rests.2

The case for the identification (steelmanned)

Set at its strongest, the altar identification rests on a convergence of specifics that is genuinely hard to dismiss, and on one structural advantage the parallel conquest arguments lack. First, the correspondences are unusually precise. The biblical altar is defined by three peculiar, non-generic requirements — unhewn stone, no steps (hence a ramp), and the location on Mt. Ebal — and the structure satisfies all three at once, on the named mountain, in the right period, with a sacrificial bone deposit to match the burnt offerings the text prescribes. It is not one loose fit but several tight ones stacked on the one site the text points to. Second, the cultic reading is not fringe. That the installation is a cult place is accepted well beyond the conservative camp: Amihai Mazar found it "tempting to accept … the basic cultic nature of the site and its possible relationship to the Biblical tradition," and Israel Finkelstein — no friend of maximalist readings — is reported to grant its ritual character. The dispute is not whether Israelites worshipped here but which worship this was. Third, and decisively for its standing among the conquest arguments, it does not need the early date. An Iron I altar of c. 1200 BC sits squarely within the mainstream late-date horizon and within the highland settlement already on this timeline — so, unlike Jericho and Ai, it is not hostage to a contested chronology. And the deliberate burial of the complex, rather than its slow decay, preserved it and argues that its builders held it sacred.3

The counter-readings

Two kinds of dissent must be weighed, and they are very different in strength. The first denies the cultic reading altogether. Aharon Kempinski, in a direct 1986 rebuttal, argued the remains are an ordinary Iron I settlement topped by a watchtower, the "altar" and "ramp" being misread building phases; Anson Rainey proposed a farmstead. Both have worn badly: a watchtower set back from the ridge, with a footprint larger than known watchtowers and a fill of thousands of burnt sacrificial bones, is a poor fit, and the farmstead reading stumbles on the absence of the sickle blades that saturate farming sites. These are now minority views, and they cut against the cultic reading that most scholars accept.

The second, and far more serious, dissent grants the cult place but denies it is Joshua's altar. This is the live objection. Finkelstein holds that the biblical picture of a single central covenant altar "reflects a later reality," so that reading Joshua 8 onto an Iron I highland shrine is anachronistic; Nadav Na'aman identifies the site as the early cult place of Shechem, the great city at the mountain's foot, rather than a national covenant altar. On this reading the ground gives a highland Israelite (or proto-Israelite) sanctuary c. 1200 BC — real, and consonant with an Israel present in Canaan — but the further identification with the specific ceremony of Joshua 8:30–35 is supplied by the text, not demonstrated by the dirt. Three particulars press the point. (1) The identification is text-driven. Without Deuteronomy 27 and Joshua 8 in hand, no excavator would read this platform as Joshua's altar in particular; the Bible supplies the name. (2) The location is awkward for the ceremony. The structure sits on the northern slope, whereas the antiphonal blessing-and-cursing rite of Deuteronomy 27 sets the tribes facing across the valley between Ebal and Gerizim to the south — a tension for staging the covenant assembly here. (3) The fauna are not a perfect fit. The fallow deer among the bones is kosher for eating but is not a sacrificial species in the Levitical list, so the deposit, while overwhelmingly sacrificial in profile, is not a clean match to altar law and may include sacred-meal remains.4

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The cultic identification is on firm ground — arguably sound. A ramp-approached, unhewn-stone platform of the right period, filled with the burnt bones of young male sheep, goats, and cattle and no pig, on a mountain of covenant significance, is a cult installation by any reasonable reading, and the leading alternatives to that much (watchtower, farmstead) do not survive contact with the bone deposit. And the site carries a real advantage its neighbours lack: it needs no chronological special pleading, fitting the mainstream late date and the Iron I highland horizon without adjustment. A skeptic who granted only that "Israelites (or their forerunners) built and used a sacrificial altar in the central hill country around 1200 BC" would already have conceded something that dovetails with the biblical picture and with the Merneptah "Israel."

Where the case does not carry. The specific step — that this is the altar of the covenant ceremony of Joshua 8 — is where the argument thins from demonstration to plausible inference. Possibility is not proof: that Israel built highland altars, and that this is one, does not establish that it is the one Joshua 8 records. The identification is drawn from the text (nothing in the stones says "Joshua"), the northern-slope location sits awkwardly against the Deuteronomy 27 staging, and the fauna are a strong but imperfect match to sacrificial law. None of these is fatal — a national altar could be reused local shrine, ceremonies can be relocated in memory, sacred meals leave mixed bone assemblages — but together they mean the "Joshua's altar" label is a reasonable reading of a genuinely cultic site, not a compelled one.

The shape of the disagreement. Almost no one now disputes that Mt. Ebal holds an Iron I cult installation; the argument is whether the ground has yielded Joshua's altar or a highland altar that the text later names as Joshua's. That is a real, unresolved divide between serious readers of the same evidence — strong by the lights of one discipline (the physical fit to the altar law is striking) and weak by another's (the national-ceremony identification is text-supplied and archaeologically underdetermined). It is the textbook profile of a contested claim.

A separate, weaker sub-claim: the Mount Ebal "curse tablet"

One further item is often folded into the Mt. Ebal case and must be kept firmly apart from it, because leaning the altar argument on it would only weaken a stronger position. In 2019 a team led by Scott Stripling recovered a small folded lead object by wet-sifting the discarded excavation dump from Zertal's 1980s dig; announced in 2022, it was claimed (chiefly by Gershon Galil) to bear an early proto-alphabetic Hebrew curse invoking the divine name YHW — potentially the oldest such inscription. The claim is weak, verging on unsupported. The object comes from unstratified dump material, severing it from any excavation context; and a majority of epigraphers who have examined the published images — Christopher Rollston and Aren Maeir prominent among them — hold that there is no legible inscription at all, only striations in soft lead ("there are no actual discernible letters," Rollston), a verdict pressed in formal rebuttals in the Israel Exploration Journal and Heritage Science. Even the object's own promoters disagreed among themselves about how many letters it bears. Whatever it is, it is no part of the altar attestation, and this page does not rest a gram of the altar case on it.5

Assessment

Contested — sound as a cult installation, disputed as Joshua's altar. The interpretation divides at a clean seam, and the honest verdict tracks the seam. On the first question — is this a genuine early Iron I cultic installation? — the case is strong and broadly accepted, and the rival "watchtower" and "farmstead" readings do not hold against the sacrificial bone deposit. On the second — is it specifically the covenant altar of Joshua 8:30–35? — the case is a coherent, non-trivial, and (unusually for these arguments) chronology-independent reading, but it is text-supplied rather than compelled: the northern-slope location, the imperfect faunal fit, and the plain fact that the identification is read into the site from Deuteronomy 27 and Joshua 8 keep it short of demonstration, and serious scholars (Finkelstein, Na'aman) grant the cult place while denying the specific altar. So the interpretation is best rated contested — a genuine, impressive correlation with the text, resting on a firmly cultic site, that nonetheless cannot itself close the gap between "a highland altar c. 1200 BC" and "Joshua's altar." It is stronger than the Ai relocation (which needs the contested early date and an ad hoc toponym-migration) and stands apart from the Jericho dating fight; its weakness is not a broken chronology but the ordinary ceiling these correlations share — the ground can show that Israel worshipped here, but that this is the altar of the covenant ceremony comes from Joshua 8, not from the stones. The curse-tablet sub-claim is separately rated weak and is no part of the altar case.
