Does Soleb's "Shasu of Yhw" attest the divine name — and early Israel?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — the argument has two joints and they part company. That the Soleb name-ring tŕ šŕsw yhwŕ preserves the divine name YHWH — the oldest extra-biblical attestation of it anywhere, c. 1400 BC — is plausible, arguably sound: the mainstream reads it as a toponym named after the god, and Astour, van der Toorn, and Redford accept the Yahweh connection. But the leap from "Shasu of Yhw" to Israel or the conquest is the weak joint: the ring names Shasu bedouin of the Edom–Midian–Negev fringe, not Israel, and the very same datum feeds the rival Midianite/Kenite thesis in which Yahwism reaches Israel from the desert Shasu. Keep the two apart: external attestation of the name (strong) is not attestation of the conquest (weak) · advanced in its strong form by early-date writers (Titus Kennedy; the Armstrong Institute)

The claim

Who could "the nomads of Yahweh" refer to, other than the Israelites of the Hebrew Bible?

— Armstrong Institute of Biblical Archaeology, "The Soleb Inscription: Earliest Discovered Use of the Name Yahweh." The argument runs in two steps. First, that the name-ring on Amenhotep III's temple at Soleb (c. 1400 BC) reading tŕ šŕsw yhwŕ — "the land of the Shasu of Yhw" — preserves the divine name YHWH, some four centuries before any biblical text. Second, that since the only ancient people known to have worshipped a deity named Yahweh were the Israelites, the "Shasu of Yhw" must be identified with early Israel, corroborating an early (~1400 BC) Exodus and a Yahweh-worshipping people already in the patriarchs' zone.1

Who advances it

The strong two-step form is a staple of early-date apologetics: the Armstrong Institute, and the archaeologist Titus Kennedy, whose study of the Soleb ring reads it as the earliest attestation of the name and ties it to an early Israel. Behind them stands a genuine scholarly consensus on the first step alone: Michael Astour held the hieroglyphic rendering "corresponds very precisely to the Hebrew Tetragrammaton YHWH," and K. van der Toorn and Donald B. Redford accept the Yahweh connection. But those same scholars part from the apologists at the second step — van der Toorn concluding that "by the 14th century BC… groups of Edomites and Midianites worshipped Yahweh as their god," and Redford taking early Israel to be a Shasu enclave — and build from the datum a Midianite/Kenite origin of Yahwism that runs opposite to the apologetic reading.2

The evidence for

The reading itself is relatively strong. The ring is grammatically a place-name — "the land of the Shasu of Yhw," in the same construction as "the land of the Shasu of Seʻr" (Seir) beside it — so yhwŕ is a toponym named after the god who was worshipped there, exactly the pattern one expects for a divine name entering a topographical list. The identification does not rest on one scribe: a second, abbreviated occurrence of the same toponym turns up in a comparable prisoner-list at the related temple of Amarah West under Ramesses II. Astour noted it predates the Moabite Mesha Stele — long the earliest firmly-dated extra-biblical occurrence of the name — by roughly five centuries. So as external, independently-dated evidence that a Semitic group tied to the name Yahweh existed in the Late Bronze Levant, the datum is solid and widely accepted.3

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The first step. That the Soleb ring attests the divine name YHWH in the fourteenth century BC is the mainstream reading and is accepted across camps that otherwise disagree sharply — a rare point of consensus. On any account this is the oldest extra-biblical appearance of the name, and it independently places a Yahweh-connected Semitic group in the right region at the right period; the conservative reading of it as corroboration of a people the Bible places in that zone is legitimate as far as it goes.

What is overstated. The second step — the jump to Israel and the conquest — is where the argument breaks:

(1) "Shasu of Yhw" names Shasu, not Israel. The Egyptian sources place the Shasu in the Edom–Seir–Midian–Negev fringe; the ring names a nomadic people associated with the divine name, and it does not say "Israel." The inference "worshipped Yahweh, therefore Israel" assumes what it needs to prove — that no one but Israel could bear the name — which is exactly the point in dispute.

(2) The same datum feeds the opposite thesis. Precisely because the Shasu are desert nomads and not Israel, van der Toorn and Redford build from Soleb a Midianite/Kenite hypothesis: that Yahweh originated among these non-Israelite Shasu and was later adopted by Israel — the name coming to Israel from the desert rather than the reverse. The monument is common ground; only the direction of borrowing is disputed, and it does not by itself decide between a Yahweh-worshipping proto-Israel and a Yahweh-worshipping Midian that Israel later drew on. (3) Even granting a proto-Israelite reading, it does not deliver a conquest. A Yahweh-named group in the Edomite fringe c. 1400 BC is at most a datum about origins and the divine name, not a battle report or a synchronism with Joshua; the conquest inference rests on the same contested early date as the Amarna arguments and adds nothing the name-attestation itself supplies. And a minority still judges the evidence too scanty to tie yhwŕ to the divine name at all, treating it as a bare toponym.4

Assessment

Contested, because the argument's two halves earn different verdicts and must be stated separately. The name-attestation — that Soleb preserves the earliest extra-biblical occurrence of YHWH, c. 1400 BC — is plausible, arguably sound, the mainstream reading accepted by Astour, van der Toorn, and Redford alike. The Israel/conquest inference — that the "Shasu of Yhw" are early Israel and so corroborate the conquest — is weak: the ring names Shasu bedouin, not Israel; the "only Israel worshipped Yahweh" premise begs the question; and the identical datum grounds the rival Midianite/Kenite origin of Yahwism, which points away from an Israelite reading rather than toward one. This is not a verdict against the conquest's historicity or against the supernatural — the name-datum stands, and it is a striking one — only that this particular inscription attests a divine name in the Late Bronze Levant, which is a real and important thing, and not Israel's invasion of Canaan, which it is repeatedly asked to prove. Proponents who collapse the two steps into one should be read as affirming the first and asserting the second.
