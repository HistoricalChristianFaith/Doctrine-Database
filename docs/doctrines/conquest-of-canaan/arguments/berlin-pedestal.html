Does the Berlin pedestal relief (ÄM 21687) name "Israel" before Merneptah?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — as external proof of an Israel attested before the Merneptah Stele (c. 1208 BC) the reading does not hold on its best available case. Every load-bearing element is contested at once: the name sits in a broken ring whose "Israel" reading requires reconstructing a destroyed sign; its sibilant is š (shin), not the ś (sin/samekh) of Merneptah's securely-read Israel; the object is unprovenanced (a 1913 dealer purchase, no find-spot), so the orthographic dating that alone makes it "pre-Merneptah" is uncontrolled; and a senior Egyptologist (Robert Ritner) has refuted the reading outright, taking the name instead as Assyria. Not false — it is a live minority reading defended by real scholars, and the proponents concede it is not decisive — but it cannot bear the weight of "earliest Israel" · advanced by Manfred Görg, Peter van der Veen & Christoffer Theis (welcomed by Bryant Wood)

The claim

Israel in Canaan. (Long) Before Pharaoh Merenptah? A fresh look at Berlin statue pedestal relief 21687

— Peter van der Veen, Christoffer Theis & Manfred Görg, Journal of Ancient Egyptian Interconnections 2:4 (2010) — the article's title states its thesis. The broken third name-ring on the Berlin pedestal, they argue, once read an archaic form of Israel — reconstructed Yŕ-šr-il ("Ishrael"), "a name very close to Biblical ysr'l" — and, on orthographic grounds, was carved (or copied from a list) in the Eighteenth Dynasty, some two centuries before the Merneptah Stele. If so, it would be the earliest extra-biblical mention of Israel and would place a people called Israel in Canaan well before c. 1208 BC, corroborating an early presence and (for its early-date users) an early conquest.1

Who advances it

The reading originates with the German Egyptologist Manfred Görg, who in 2001 proposed that the long-catalogued but little-examined third ring be restored and read "Israel." It was republished in English, and defended at length, by Peter van der Veen and Christoffer Theis with Görg in the 2010 JAEI study above. On the biblical-archaeology side Bryant Wood (Associates for Biblical Research) welcomed it and folded it into his early-date case — an Exodus c. 1446 BC and a conquest c. 1406–1400 BC — as did the Armstrong Institute, which bills the pedestal as the "earliest mention of Israel." Against it stand James Hoffmeier (who rejected Görg's reading on linguistic grounds as early as 2007) and, decisively, Robert Ritner, whose 2020 study is a point-by-point refutation; other Egyptologists (Kai Adrom and Matthias Müller) have likewise declined the identification.2

The evidence for

The steelman is real and worth stating plainly. The pedestal (Egyptian Museum, Berlin, inv. ÄM 21687, a granite fragment c. 40 × 46 cm) preserves three bound captives, each over a name-ring: the first reads jsqrwn = Ashkelon, the second kynʿŕnnw = Canaan, and the third is broken. That the two intact rings name Ashkelon and Canaan is striking, because those are exactly two of the three Levantine names — Ashkelon, Canaan, Israel — that recur together on the Merneptah Stele; the sequence invites completing the damaged ring the same way. Görg proposed that the destroyed portion held a vulture sign (Gardiner G1, Egyptian ŕ), which yields Yŕ-šr-il; van der Veen and Theis report that close re-examination — specially-lit photography and 3D scanning of the surviving surface — recovered traces they read as that vulture (a beak, a leg and claw, a belly outline), lending the restoration physical support rather than mere conjecture. And the letter-forms, they argue, match Eighteenth-Dynasty rather than Ramesside orthography, pointing to a date around Amenhotep II–III (c. 1450–1400 BC), or at least to an Eighteenth-Dynasty list later copied.3

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The two intact rings really do read Ashkelon and Canaan, and their pairing with a Merneptah-like Levantine sequence is a genuine hook; the proponents are serious scholars, not fringe voices, and Görg's original observation — that the third ring had never been properly studied — was fair. The object is authentic and its two firm names are not in dispute. So the question is legitimate; it is the answer that the evidence will not carry.

What is overstated or imprecise. Four independent problems each cut against the reading, and they compound:

(1) The sibilant is wrong. The Berlin name is written with š (shin), whereas the Merneptah Stele's securely-read ysr'l uses ś (sin/samekh) — so the two spellings do not straightforwardly match, and on this ground Hoffmeier rejected the equation with biblical Israel. The proponents answer that Egyptian–West-Semitic sibilant correspondences were not fixed (appealing to James Hoch's transcription scheme), but Hoch's very scheme was queried and partly refuted by Anson Rainey — so the rebuttal rests on a contested premise rather than settling the point.

(2) The reading needs a reconstructed sign. "Israel" appears only once the broken part is restored as the G1 vulture; the surviving traces are ambiguous (the same remains have been read as an M23 sw-plant or a U33 t-pestle instead), and even with the vulture the resulting spelling of "Israel" would be, as Görg conceded, unique in the Egyptian record. A reading that exists only after one supplies the decisive sign, and is then without parallel, is a conjecture, not an attestation.

(3) No provenance — so the dating is uncontrolled. The fragment (with its companion ÄM 21688) was bought from an antiquities dealer (M. Nachman) by Ludwig Borchardt in 1913, with no find-spot and no archaeological context. The entire "before Merneptah" claim rests on orthographic dating of that unprovenanced object — and orthographic dating is precisely what an unstratified piece cannot secure. The pre-Merneptah date is thus argued from the same letter-forms whose reading is in dispute; other scholars date the object as late as Horemheb or Ramesses II, which would erase the "two centuries earlier" claim entirely.

(4) A direct refutation, with a rival reading. In 2020 the Egyptologist Robert Ritner published a point-by-point refutation and read the contested name not as Israel but as a writing of Assyria (Aššur) — a competing identification from a senior specialist that removes even the bare "most logical candidate" standing the reading is often given. For the proponents themselves grant that the identification is not demonstrative: van der Veen's defence that "'Israel' remains the most logical candidate" is an argument from the absence of a better option — and Ritner's "Assyria" is precisely such an option.4

Assessment

Weak — as a claimed pre-Merneptah attestation of Israel the reading does not hold on its best available case, though it is a live minority position and not a falsehood. Four contingencies stack on one broken, unprovenanced ring: the "Israel" reading requires reconstructing a destroyed sign (which, even restored, gives a spelling unique in Egyptian); the sibilant is š where Merneptah's Israel has ś, and the rebuttal to that leans on a transcription scheme (Hoch's) that was itself contested (Rainey); the "pre-Merneptah" date is orthographic dating of an object with no find-spot; and a senior Egyptologist (Ritner) has refuted the reading and offered a rival identification (Assyria). This is not a verdict against Israel's presence in Canaan — the Merneptah Stele firmly attests that by c. 1208 BC — nor against the early date as such, which is weighed on its own terms at the early-date vs. late-date argument. It is only that this inscription cannot be made to carry the extraordinary claim placed on it: a possible-but-unproven reading of a broken sign is not the earliest name of Israel. Proponents who present the pedestal as an established "earliest Israel" overstate a genuinely contested reconstruction; the honest statement is that the third ring may read Israel, on a chain of assumptions that mainstream Egyptology has largely declined.
