Does the conquest date to c. 1400 BC (the early date)?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — read literally, 1 Kings 6:1 (the 480 years) and Judges 11:26 (Jephthah's 300 years) place the Exodus c. 1446 BC and the conquest c. 1406 BC; the plain sense of these numbers is the case's real strength and does not dissolve into mere symbolism, but the reading is a minority position, its one archaeological prop (Wood's re-dated Jericho) is weak, and it stands against the Egyptological consensus that weighs behind the late date; beneath both dates lies a still deeper mainstream doubt that any invasion happened at all · advanced by Bryant Wood and Associates for Biblical Research, Titus Kennedy, Douglas Petrovich, John Bimson, and Eugene Merrill

Related arguments: the conquest date — see also Does the conquest date to c. 1250 BC (the late date)?

Why this argument comes first. This is the linchpin of the whole doctrine. The tightest correlations the timeline offers — the identification of the Amarna Habiru with the Hebrews, and the reading of Shechem's peaceful handover against Joshua — line up only on the early date. On the late date the Amarna archive (c. 1350 BC) sits a century before the conquest, and the synchronism dissolves. So the strength of those arguments is conditional on the answer weighed here; that dependency is the single most important thing this page establishes.

The claim

"1 Kgs 6:1 and 1 Chr 6:33–37 converge on a date of 1446 BC for the exodus and the Jubilees data and Judges 11:26 independently converge on a date of 1406 BC for the beginning of the conquest."

— Associates for Biblical Research, "The Biblical Date for the Exodus Is 1446 BC".1 The question this page adjudicates is not "who is more pious" but whether the evidence, taken whole, best supports the fifteenth-century entry.

Who advances it

The early date (Exodus c. 1446 BC, conquest c. 1406 BC) is the traditional reading, defended today chiefly by conservative evangelicals: Bryant Wood and Associates for Biblical Research, Titus Kennedy, Douglas Petrovich, John Bimson, and Eugene Merrill. It is the minority position within scholarship: the majority of those who grant a historical Exodus at all prefer the late date, whose most formidable defender is the Egyptologist Kenneth Kitchen — otherwise the leading conservative champion of Old Testament historicity, and the early date is precisely where he parts from the traditional camp. Standing behind and beneath both is a third position — the dominant one in academic archaeology — associated with Israel Finkelstein and William Dever's more skeptical readers, which doubts that any external conquest took place, and for which the whole early-vs-late question is downstream of a prior doubt.2

The evidence for

The backbone is 1 Kings 6:1: Solomon began the Temple "in the four hundred and eightieth year after the people of Israel came out of the land of Egypt," in his fourth regnal year. With that year fixed near 967–966 BC, counting back 479 years yields an Exodus c. 1446 BC and, after the forty years' wandering, a conquest c. 1406 BC. A second, independent biblical datum appears to converge: in Judges 11:26 Jephthah tells the Ammonites that Israel had held Heshbon and the Arnon towns "three hundred years" — a figure that, added to the later judges and the early monarchy, points back toward a fifteenth-century entry rather than a thirteenth. Both resist being explained away as mere symbolism: 1 Kings 6:1 gives a total, not a genealogy, and Judges 11:26 puts a specific "three hundred years" in a character's mouth for a specific rhetorical purpose — and their rough convergence is a real datum.

Some early-date proponents add an archaeological prop: Bryant Wood re-dated the fallen walls of Jericho City IV to c. 1400 BC (against Kathleen Kenyon's c. 1550), arguing the destruction fits Joshua 6 — a claim weighed on its own at the Jericho-dating argument (assessment: contested, leaning against the c. 1400 date).3 The decisive attraction, for this wiki's purposes, is that only on this chronology do the Amarna synchronisms line up: a conquest c. 1400 BC puts the fall of the Canaanite city-states and the Habiru turmoil of the 1350s BC squarely in the era of Joshua and the judges.

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The early date has the plain sense of the numbers, and that is not a soft foundation: two independent biblical figures, neither easily dissolved into symbolism, converge on a fifteenth-century entry. That convergence is precisely why the traditional reading has never simply gone away, and why serious scholars still defend it.

Where it is overstated. The archaeological prop is the weakest link: Wood's re-dating of Jericho is a minority position, and the tell is so eroded that a Late-Bronze city there at all is disputed — the biblical chronology, not the spade, is doing the real work. The reading must also answer the late date's two strongest props head-on. First, "Rameses" (Exodus 1:11) is a nineteenth-dynasty delta-city name, which points the oppression to the thirteenth century; early-date scholars reply that the name already appears for a region in Genesis 47:11 (Joseph's day) and that Exodus 1:11 may be an editorial updating of an older place-name for later readers — a real possibility, though it cannot simply be assumed. Second, Kitchen reads the 480 years as twelve schematic generations (12 × 40) that at a realistic ~25 years each total only ~300 years, landing the Exodus back in the thirteenth century; against this the early-date camp notes that 1 Chronicles 6:33–37 lists roughly eighteen generations from the Exodus to the Temple singers, not twelve — so the schematic reading is not obviously the natural one. Neither literal proof-text is independent of the other's rebuttal: the whole case is a weighing of probabilities, not a proof.4

The deeper doubt, stated fairly. Much of academic archaeology — Israel Finkelstein most prominently — doubts a historical conquest of either date, reading the Joshua narrative as a seventh-century ideological composition and the Iron I highland villages as the settling of a largely indigenous Canaanite population rather than the arrival of invaders. This is a defeater to the early and late dates alike, not to one only, and it is not a naturalistic prejudice against the miraculous; it is a reading of the survey and pottery data, and it must be met on that ground, not dismissed. Against it, Kitchen presses a point that softens the sharpest objection: Joshua and Judges themselves describe a conquest that was gradual and partial — raids and campaigns, not a single total devastation with towns left standing "on their mounds" (Joshua 11:13) — so the absence of a uniform destruction layer is not by itself the refutation it is often taken to be. That defends historicity in general; it does not, by itself, decide between the early and late dates.5

Assessment

Contested. The early date is the textbook case of a claim that is strong by one discipline's lights and weak by another's: biblical chronology, read at face value, favours it, while Egyptology and the balance of field archaeology favour the late date, and the dominant secular reconstruction doubts an invasion of either. The literal numbers carry real weight and each has a real answer, so no honest reckoning declares a winner. What this wiki can state without hedging is the dependency the timeline turns on: the force of the Amarna arguments (Habiru = Hebrews; Shechem's handover) is conditional on the early date. Grant the early date and they become a striking synchronism; take the mainstream late date and they lapse, because the Amarna turmoil then precedes the conquest by a century. Those arguments should therefore be read as saying, "if the early date holds, then …" — not as independent proof that it does. The Merneptah Stele is the one fixed point both dates share: Israel was a people in Canaan by c. 1208 BC, which is a terminus after both schemes and so cannot itself break the tie.
