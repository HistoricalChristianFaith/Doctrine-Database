The Mount Ebal structure — an Iron I altar-like installation with a sacrificial bone deposit — on the Conquest of Canaan

Dates: a rectangular unhewn-stone installation on Mt. Ebal, above Shechem, in use in the early Iron Age I — an earlier round installation c. 1250 BC, the large rectangular structure over it c. 1200 BC, the whole deliberately buried c. 1140 BC (pottery c. 1220–1000 BC) · Wikipedia
Summary: On the northern slope of Mt. Ebal, the mountain of the covenant curses (Deuteronomy 27; Joshua 8:30–35), Adam Zertal of the University of Haifa excavated, across eight seasons (1982–1989), a large rectangular structure of unhewn field-stones filled with earth, ash, and stones, approached by a stone ramp rather than steps, set within enclosure walls and built over an earlier round installation. In and around it lay a heavy ash deposit and more than three thousand burnt animal bones — young, mostly male sheep, goats, and cattle (plus fallow deer), the profile of sacrifice, not of a kitchen midden. Two features are agreed by nearly all who have weighed the site: it dates to early Iron I (c. 13th–12th century BC), and it is a cultic installation, not an ordinary dwelling. What is not agreed — and is the whole question of whether this is the altar of Joshua 8 — is the identification: is this Joshua's covenant altar of Deuteronomy 27 / Joshua 8, a more generic highland cult place, or (on the minority reading it once drew) a watchtower or farmstead? That identification is weighed at the Mount-Ebal-altar argument, not here; this page carries the agreed attestation and its honest complications.

Claims & evidence

The site: a monumental structure on the mountain of the curse

Zertal discovered the site in 1980 during the Manasseh Hill Country Survey and excavated it over eight seasons, 1982–1989, for the University of Haifa and the Israel Exploration Society. It sits on a rocky terrace on the north-eastern slope of Mt. Ebal — the mountain Deuteronomy 27 assigns to the covenant curses and on which Joshua 8:30 has Joshua build an altar. The centrepiece is a rectangular structure of large, undressed field-stones, roughly 9 by 7 m, its walls about 1.4 m thick and preserved up to some 3 m high, solidly filled with layers of earth, ash, and stone rather than floored as a room — it has no proper entrance and no living floor. A stone ramp some 7 m long rises to its top, and two lower stone ledges and a surrounding enclosure wall complete the complex.1

The two phases: a round installation, then the rectangular structure, then deliberate burial

The excavation found two building phases, both early Iron I. The earlier (Zertal's Stratum II, c. 1250 BC) was a small round installation, roughly 2 m across, set on bedrock, holding a fill of ash and charred bone; the later (Stratum IB, c. 1200 BC) is the large rectangular structure raised directly over it. The whole complex was then, strikingly, deliberately covered over with stones and earth and abandoned around 1140 BC, after a life of less than a century — a burial that sealed and preserved it. The pottery from the site runs c. 1220–1000 BC, and two Egyptian scarabs were recovered from the ash and refuse; their dating is itself disputed (Baruch Brandl assigned one to the reign of Ramesses II, while Daphna Ben-Tor and Pieter van der Veen reassign it toward the era of Thutmose III), but they place the installation firmly in the Late Bronze–Iron I transition.2

The bone deposit: burnt, young, male, and (almost entirely) sacrificial species

The find that turns the structure from a curiosity into a probable cult place is the fauna. The zooarchaeologist Liora Kolska Horwitz analysed the assemblage and published it in Tel Aviv (1986–87): more than three thousand animal bones, many burnt on an open flame and bearing butcher marks. The species are overwhelmingly the animals of Israelite sacrifice — sheep and goat (about two-thirds of the bones, most of them burnt), cattle (about a fifth), with a component of fallow deer — and the sheep, goat, and cattle are disproportionately young males, exactly the profile the sacrificial law prescribes for burnt offerings (cf. Leviticus 1). Conspicuously, there are no pig bones. This is not the debris of ordinary eating: the concentration of burnt, young, male, permitted animals in and around a ramp-approached stone platform is the material signature of sacrifice.3

The correlation with Joshua 8 and Deuteronomy 27

The convergence Zertal pressed is between the physical structure and the peculiar terms of the biblical altar command. The law requires an altar on Mt. Ebal, of whole (unhewn) stones untouched by iron, approached without steps, on which burnt and peace offerings are made:

Then Joshua built an altar unto the LORD God of Israel in mount Ebal, As Moses the servant of the LORD commanded the children of Israel, as it is written in the book of the law of Moses, an altar of whole stones, over which no man hath lift up any iron: and they offered thereon burnt offerings unto the LORD, and sacrificed peace offerings.

— Joshua 8:30–31 (KJV; cf. Deuteronomy 27:4–8 and the ban on hewn stone and on steps at Exodus 20:25–26). The Mt. Ebal structure answers point for point to the unusual details: undressed field-stones, a ramp instead of steps, and a deposit of burnt sacrificial animals — on the very mountain the text names. That the ground on Mt. Ebal preserves a burnt, ramp-approached, unhewn-stone cult installation of the right period is the agreed datum this page records. Whether that installation is Joshua's altar in particular — the single question on which the correlation turns — is taken up under Arguments, not settled here.4

Notes

  • The cultic reading is broadly accepted; the "Joshua's altar" label is not. Even scholars who reject Zertal's specific identification largely grant that the site is a cult place. Amihai Mazar's often-quoted verdict — "Zertal may be wrong in the details of his interpretation, but it is tempting to accept his view concerning the basic cultic nature of the site and its possible relationship to the Biblical tradition" — captures the middle ground; Israel Finkelstein, who surveyed the neighbouring hill country, is reported to accept a ritual character for the site while doubting that it is the central altar of Joshua. The two questions this page keeps apart — is it cultic? (widely, yes) and is it Joshua's altar? (disputed) — are exactly the two joints of the argument.5
  • The dissenting readings — watchtower and farmstead. The sharpest early rebuttal, from Aharon Kempinski, read the remains not as an altar but as an ordinary Iron I settlement crowned by a watchtower; Anson Rainey proposed a farmstead. Both have lost ground: a watchtower set back from the ridge, with a base larger than known watchtowers and thousands of burnt sacrificial bones, is hard to sustain, and the farmstead reading founders on the absence of the sickle blades ubiquitous at farming sites. These are weighed on the argument page; they matter here only to show that even the cultic reading, though dominant, is not unanimous.
  • The Mt. Ebal "curse tablet" is a separate, later, and disputed find — not part of this attestation. A small folded lead object, recovered in 2019 by Scott Stripling's team by wet-sifting Zertal's discarded excavation dump and announced in 2022 as bearing an early Hebrew curse invoking the divine name, is not evidence weighed on this page. It comes from unstratified dump material, and a majority of epigraphers (Christopher Rollston, Aren Maeir, and others) dispute that it carries any legible inscription at all. It is treated — and rated weak — only as a flagged side-note on the argument page; the altar case does not rest on it.

Editions and background: Zertal's own case is Adam Zertal, "Has Joshua's Altar Been Found on Mt. Ebal?", Biblical Archaeology Review 11/1 (1985): 26–43, and his book A Nation Born: The Altar on Mount Ebal and the Birth of Israel (English ed., 2018); the full excavation report is published as Ralph K. Hawkins, The Iron Age I Structure on Mt. Ebal: Excavation and Interpretation (Winona Lake: Eisenbrauns, 2012). The faunal study is Liora Kolska Horwitz, "Faunal Remains from the Early Iron Age Site on Mount Ebal," Tel Aviv 13–14/2 (1986–87): 173–189. The leading dissent is Aharon Kempinski, "Joshua's Altar — An Iron Age I Watchtower," Biblical Archaeology Review 12/1 (1986): 42–53.
