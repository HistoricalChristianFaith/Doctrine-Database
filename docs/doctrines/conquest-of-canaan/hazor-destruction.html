The destruction of Hazor — the one city Joshua "burned with fire" — on the Conquest of Canaan

Dates: the final Late Bronze destruction of Canaanite Hazor, c. 1230 BC (Stratum XIII of the upper city / Stratum 1A of the lower city); a distinct, earlier Late Bronze I destruction (~15th century BC) is also attested at the site · Wikipedia
Summary: Tel Hazor (Tell el-Qedah), in Upper Galilee, was by far the largest Canaanite city in the land — a huge upper acropolis over a sprawling lower city, precisely the "head of all those kingdoms" of Joshua 11:10. Two long excavations — Yigael Yadin (1955–58, 1968) and, from 1990, Amnon Ben-Tor — found that Late Bronze Hazor ended in a single, catastrophic fire so intense it cracked the city's basalt masonry, after which the great lower city was permanently abandoned. Statues of Canaanite gods and Egyptian kings had been deliberately decapitated and their hands cut off. This is the one thing the biblical text predicts of Hazor and of Hazor alone: of all the northern cities, "Israel burned none of them, save Hazor only" (Joshua 11:13). The agreed datum — a violent, human-caused Late Bronze burn at the one city Scripture singles out to be burned — is set out here; the two contested steps (who caused it, and which Late Bronze destruction answers to Joshua) are weighed in the Arguments section, not settled here.

Claims & evidence

The site: "the head of all those kingdoms"

Hazor was not one Canaanite town among many but the metropolis of the north. Its mound covers some 200 acres — a fortified upper city on the tell and, below it, a vast walled lower city — making it many times the size of Jericho, Ai, or any other site in the land, and the only Canaanite settlement whose scale answers to the Amarna and Mari archives, where "Hazor" is named as a royal city trading with Babylon and Egypt. When Joshua 11 reaches the northern campaign it stops to note exactly this pre-eminence: "for Hazor beforetime was the head of all those kingdoms."1 Yigael Yadin excavated the site in 1955–58 and again in 1968, uncovering Middle and Late Bronze temples, palaces, and fortifications; Amnon Ben-Tor's Selz Foundation expedition took up the work in 1990 and has dug there ever since.2

The burn layer: a fire that cracked basalt

Both excavations found that Late Bronze Hazor ended in an exceptionally violent conflagration. A layer of ash up to roughly a metre deep in places sealed the monumental buildings; excavators recovered burnt cedar beams, a collapsed ceiling, bricks cemented together by the heat, and soot on the walls. The fire was hot enough to crack the city's basalt architecture — a temperature well over 1200 °C. As Jennie Ebeling summarizes the ceremonial palace on the acropolis, it was destroyed in

a fire so intense that it cracked the basalt architectural elements of the palace, the gate shrine, and other structures.

— a blaze the excavators of the lower city likewise describe as "memorable for its intensity." A cache of storage jars in the palace, discovered in 2012, held some 3,400-year-old scorched wheat that sealed and helps date the destruction; the oil stored alongside it is thought to have driven the palace fire to around 2350 °F (≈1290 °C). After this destruction the great lower city was never rebuilt — it lay effectively abandoned for roughly two centuries before an ephemeral Iron Age resettlement.3

The mutilated gods: deliberate, not accidental

The single feature that fixes the destruction as an act of war rather than an accident is the treatment of the statues. Across the temples of both the acropolis and the lower city, images of Canaanite deities and rulers — and Egyptian royal statuary too — had been methodically beheaded and had their hands hacked off, the mutilation carried out before the fire. Ben-Tor, who directed the excavation, put the reasoning plainly in a 2013 article:

Hazor was not destroyed by an accidental fire, an earthquake or any other natural catastrophe. The destruction was clearly the result of human activity, as indicated by the large number of statues of deities and rulers that were intentionally disfigured by cutting off their heads and hands. Since we have no record mentioning the destruction other than the book of Joshua, the only way to go about answering the question of who destroyed Hazor is to consider all those peoples who were around at the time, examining whether any of them can be regarded as a possible candidate for the city's destroyer.

— Amnon Ben-Tor, "Who Destroyed Canaanite Hazor?", Biblical Archaeology Review 39/4 (2013). A much-cited instance is a seated basalt figure of a Canaanite dignitary, about 15 inches high, found decapitated where it sat, its severed head lying nearby — one of several torsos whose heads Ben-Tor's team was able to match. The selective targeting of divine and royal images, rather than random destruction, is the hallmark of an ideological assault on the city's gods and kingship.4

Who did it? Ben-Tor's argument by elimination

Because no text but Joshua names a destroyer, Ben-Tor proceeds by ruling candidates out. He judges the deliberate smashing of statues — Egyptian ones included — to make an Egyptian campaign unlikely; he sees no nearby Canaanite city-state powerful enough to have overthrown so great a city; and he notes that not a single sherd of the distinctive Sea-Peoples pottery has turned up at Hazor, which lies far inland of the coastal sites they are known to have taken. As Archaeology magazine summarizes his case:

Yet Ben-Tor believes that the intentional smashing of statues at Hazor, particularly those of the Egyptian kings, makes these possibilities unlikely. He also dismisses the likelihood of destruction at the hands of a rival Canaanite city-state because of the apparent absence of nearby cities powerful enough to attack Hazor. As for the Sea Peoples, Ben-Tor notes that not a single sherd of their distinctive decorated pottery has been found in the city, which is much further inland than the sites they are known to have conquered. That leaves the Israelites.

Reviewing all the finds — five cuneiform tablets, cylinder seals, ivories, bronze weapons and figurines, and the largest basalt statue of a Canaanite god ever found at a biblical site in Israel — the Biblical Archaeology Society concludes that "Amnon Ben-Tor convincingly concludes that it was indeed the Israelites who conquered the Biblical Canaanites' city at Hazor." That this is an inference reached largely by elimination, rather than a direct attestation, is stated honestly in the note below.5

The correlation with Joshua 11: the one city burned

What makes the Hazor burn layer more than a generic destruction is the specificity of the biblical claim. Joshua 11 reports that in the northern campaign Israel took many cities but burned almost none of them — the towns "that stood still in their strength" were left standing and reoccupied. Hazor is the sole, named exception:

And Joshua at that time turned back, and took Hazor, and smote the king thereof with the sword: for Hazor beforetime was the head of all those kingdoms… and he burnt Hazor with fire… But as for the cities that stood still in their strength, Israel burned none of them, save Hazor only; that did Joshua burn.

— Joshua 11:10–13 (KJV). So Hazor is the one place in the whole conquest narrative where the text predicts an archaeological burn layer at the largest Canaanite city — and a massive, human-caused burn layer at the largest Canaanite city is exactly what excavation found. The correlation is genuine and worth stating plainly; it is also, on its own, silent about the two questions the Arguments section takes up.6

Notes

  • The burn is agreed; the destroyer is an inference. Every excavator agrees Late Bronze Hazor died by a deliberate human fire. But "who?" is reached by elimination, not by a text or an inscription naming Israel — a real and careful inference, yet not airtight: it depends on the survey of who else could have done it, and on reading the mutilated Egyptian statuary as ruling Egypt out. Whether the destroyer was Israel — and whether, as Ben-Tor's own co-director Sharon Zuckerman argued, the fire was instead an internal collapse rather than an invasion — is weighed on its own page, "Who destroyed Canaanite Hazor?"; this page carries only the agreed attestation and the Joshua-11 correlation.
  • There is more than one Late Bronze destruction — and that is where the chronology turns. Yadin attributed the final Late Bronze city's destruction to Joshua and dated it c. 1230 BC (his own words: the destruction "is doubtless to be ascribed to the Israelite tribes, as related in the Book of Joshua"). But the site also shows an earlier Late Bronze I destruction, and early-date defenders (Douglas Petrovich, the Associates for Biblical Research) reverse the assignment: they take the earlier (~15th-century) burn as Joshua's and read the 13th-century destruction as the work of Deborah and Barak (Judges 4) or an Egyptian raid — preserving a ~1400 BC conquest. Bryant Wood's formula, which Petrovich endorses, states the conservative reconstruction crisply: "The simple (and biblical) solution is that Joshua destroyed an earlier city at Hazor in ca. 1400 BC, while Deborah and Barak administered the coup de grâce in ca. 1230 BC." Crucially, Judges 4 — the war with Jabin of Hazor — does not say Israel burned the city, so neither dating strictly contradicts Scripture; which Late Bronze burn is Joshua's is bound up with the early-date–vs–late-date question weighed under Arguments.7
  • Why this sits on the timeline at c. 1230 BC. The best-attested, most fully excavated destruction — the one Yadin and Ben-Tor both worked on and the one the mainstream calls "the" destruction of Canaanite Hazor — is the 13th-century burn, so it is slotted here at that date, just before the Merneptah Stele's c. 1208 BC naming of Israel. Readers who hold the early date should read the block as attesting the earlier Late Bronze I destruction instead; the datum — a human-caused fire at the one city Scripture says was burned — is the same either way.

Editions and background: Yadin's own account is Hazor: The Rediscovery of a Great Citadel of the Bible (New York: Random House, 1975); Ben-Tor's popular statement of the case is "Who Destroyed Canaanite Hazor?", Biblical Archaeology Review 39/4 (2013): 26–36, and his final report volumes appear in the Hazor excavation series. A readable overview is Jennie Ebeling, "Hazor," Bible and Interpretation; the journalistic account is Abraham Rabinovich and Neil Asher Silberman, "The Burning of Hazor," Archaeology 51/3 (May/June 1998). The early-date rebuttal is Douglas Petrovich, "The Dating of Hazor's Destruction in Joshua 11 via Biblical, Archaeological, and Epigraphical Evidence," JETS 51/3 (2008): 489–512.
