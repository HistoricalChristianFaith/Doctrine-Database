The Soleb Inscription — "the land of the Shasu of Yhw" — on the Conquest of Canaan

Dates: carved c. 1400–1390 BC, in the reign of Amenhotep III (18th Dynasty) · Wikipedia
Summary: A topographical list carved on a column of Amenhotep III's temple at Soleb in Nubia names, among six "lands of the Shasu" (the semi-nomads of the southern Levant), one written tŕ šŕsw yhwŕ — "the land of the Shasu of Yhw." It is widely held to be the earliest extra-biblical appearance of the divine name YHWH anywhere, roughly four centuries before any biblical text was written — external, independently-dated evidence that a Semitic group tied to the name Yahweh existed in the right geographical zone in the Late Bronze Age. It is the earliest datum on this timeline; what it does and does not establish about early Israel and the conquest is weighed in the Arguments section, not here.

Claims & evidence

A name-ring at Soleb reads "the land of the Shasu of Yhw"

The temple at Soleb, north of the third cataract of the Nile in what is now Sudan, was built by Amenhotep III (reigned c. 1390–1353 BC) and dedicated to Amun-Re and to the deified king himself. In its hypostyle hall, a series of columns carries reliefs of bound prisoners, each drawn above an oval "name-ring" naming a land the pharaoh claims among his conquests. One sector lists six "lands of the Shasu" — the Egyptian term for the semi-nomadic peoples the same sources place in Edom, Seir, Midian, and the Sinai–Negev fringe. Alongside Seʻr (Seir) and four others, one ring is transliterated tŕ šŕsw yhwŕ: "the land of the Shasu of Yhw." The reading is grammatically a place-name — the land of the people of Yhw, in the same construction as "the land of the Shasu of Seir" beside it — so that already in the late fourteenth century BC an identifiable Semitic group in the Edom–Midian region was being named by this divine name. The name is generally vocalized Yahwa or Yehua. The relevant sector had collapsed and been buried under sand, and was recovered and reconstructed in the 1957–1963 excavations led by Michela Schiff Giorgini, published in her multi-volume Soleb.

— Wikipedia, "Soleb" (article); the scholarly treatment is Titus Kennedy, "The Land of the šŕsw (Nomads) of yhwŕ at Soleb," Dotawo: A Journal of Nubian Studies 6 (2019): 175–192 (open access).

It is generally taken as the oldest extra-biblical attestation of the name YHWH

Michael Astour observed that the hieroglyphic rendering "corresponds very precisely to the Hebrew Tetragrammaton YHWH," and that it predates the Moabite Mesha Stele — long the earliest firmly-dated extra-biblical occurrence of the name — by roughly five centuries. A second, abbreviated occurrence of the same toponym tŕ šŕsw yhwŕ appears in a comparable prisoner-list at the related temple of Amarah West, some 50 km to the north, carved under Ramesses II (13th century BC) — so the reading is not a single scribe's oddity but recurs across New Kingdom sources.

— Michael C. Astour, "Yāhweh in Egyptian Topographic Lists," in Festschrift Elmar Edel (Bamberg, 1979), 17–34; summarized at Wikipedia, "Shasu" (article).

Notes

  • "Shasu of Yhw" is a people-and-place, not "Israel" as such. The inscription names a nomadic group associated with the divine name, in the Edom–Midian zone; it does not say "Israel," and the step from "Shasu of Yhw" to early Israel or the conquest is an interpretive one, favored by proponents of an early-date (~1400 BC) Exodus and debated among scholars. The sober datum — a Yahweh-named Semitic group in the right region in the Late Bronze Age — is shared ground; the inference from it divides. That inference is adjudicated in the Arguments section, not asserted here.
  • The same datum feeds an opposite thesis. Where apologetic writers read Soleb as outside corroboration of a people the Bible already places in the region, other scholars — K. van der Toorn, who concluded that "by the 14th century BC… groups of Edomites and Midianites worshipped Yahweh as their god," and Donald B. Redford, who took early Israel to be a Shasu enclave out of Moab and Edom/Seir — build from it a Midianite/Kenite origin for Yahwism, in which the name comes to Israel from the desert rather than the reverse. The monument is common ground to both camps; only the direction of borrowing is disputed. This wiki takes the conservative reading as corroboration of a name and people, not as the source of the name, while noting the datum underdetermines the larger question.
  • Not all accept even the identification. Some judge the evidence too scanty to tie yhwŕ to the divine name at all, treating it as a bare toponym of uncertain sense; the mainstream reading is the Yahweh identification, but it is not unanimous. The name "Shasu" itself is of debated origin — Raphael Giveon and others derive it from an Egyptian root šŕs, "to wander / move on foot," fitting the semi-nomadic life the Egyptian sources ascribe to them.
