The destruction of Jericho — a fortified city burned, its granaries full — on the Conquest of Canaan

Dates: the destruction of the fortified Bronze Age city at Tell es-Sultan known as "City IV"; mainstream dating (Kenyon; radiocarbon on the burnt grain, Bruins & van der Plicht 1995) places its fall at the end of the Middle Bronze Age, c. 1550 BC, while early-date defenders (Garstang, then Bryant Wood) place it c. 1400 BC — the date is the whole dispute · Wikipedia
Summary: Tell es-Sultan, by the springs above the Jordan valley, is biblical Jericho beyond dispute. Excavation there — John Garstang (1930–36) and, decisively, Kathleen Kenyon (1952–58) — found that a heavily fortified Bronze Age town (the stratum labelled "City IV") ended in a fierce fire: walls and floors blackened, every room filled with fallen brick and timber, a burnt-debris layer a yard or more thick across the whole dig. Two features of that burn are what conquest-readers press: a stretch of the mudbrick city wall found fallen outward down the slope, and storage jars still full of grain — a city taken quickly and, strikingly, not plundered. Those destruction facts are common ground; what they do not settle — and what the excavators divide bitterly over — is the date, which is the crux of whether this is Joshua's Jericho at all. That question is weighed in the Arguments section, not here; this page carries the agreed attestation and the honest complications that come with it.

Claims & evidence

The site: Tell es-Sultan is Jericho

Unlike Ai or even Hazor, the identification is not in doubt: the mound of Tell es-Sultan, beside the perennial spring of ʿAin es-Sultan on the western edge of the Jordan valley, is Old Testament Jericho. It is also one of the most excavated sites in the Levant. Ernst Sellin and Carl Watzinger dug it in 1907–11; John Garstang excavated in 1930–36 and found a violently burnt Bronze Age city he labelled "City IV," dating its fall to about 1400 BC and linking it to Joshua; Kathleen Kenyon re-excavated in 1952–58 with the more rigorous stratigraphic method that bears her name, and an Italian–Palestinian expedition under Lorenzo Nigro and Nicolò Marchetti has worked the tell since 1997. The debate that follows is not about whether a fortified city there burned, but when.1

The burn layer: an intense, city-wide fire

City IV ended in fire, and both Garstang and Kenyon documented it. The conflagration reddened and blackened the mudbrick, brought down ceilings and stored timber, and left a thick blanket of ash and collapse across the excavated area. Bryant Wood, summarizing Kenyon's own field notes, describes the destruction stratum:

Walls and floors were blackened or reddened by fire, and every room was filled with fallen bricks, timbers, and household utensils; in most rooms the fallen debris was heavily burnt.

— a burnt layer, he notes, "a yard or more thick across the entire excavation area." Whatever its date, this was a total, deliberate destruction by fire, not a slow decline.2

The fallen walls: mudbrick collapsed outward

Garstang reported, and Kenyon's sections confirmed, a stretch of the mudbrick city wall that had toppled outward and down the slope, heaping against the base of the stone revetment (retaining) wall. Wood draws the correlation with Joshua 6:20, where the walls "fell down flat" so the attackers went "up into the city, every man straight before him":

When the wall was deposited in this fashion at the base of the tell, the collapsed mudbricks themselves formed a ready ramp for an attacker to surmount the revetment wall.

This is the most-cited — and the most contested — correlation, and it must be stated with its complication (see the Notes below): the great fortification lines Garstang once assigned to Joshua's Jericho are now generally dated to the earlier Early–Middle Bronze system, so which wall fell, and when, is bound up with the same dating dispute as everything else on the site.3

The granaries: full jars, a city not plundered

The most unusual find is what was not taken. In the burnt debris both excavators recovered storage jars still full of grain — Kenyon retrieved some six bushels in a single season, an amount Wood calls "unique in the annals of Palestinian archaeology." Its significance is twofold. First, the grain dates the fall to just after harvest and shows the city fell quickly: "The ample food supply at Jericho indicates that it succumbed quickly, not after a long siege." Second, and more pointedly, a victorious army would ordinarily have carried off so valuable a store; that it was left to burn fits a city taken under a ban on plunder — exactly what Joshua 6:17–18 lays on Jericho, where the city and all in it were "accursed" (devoted) to the LORD and the Israelites forbidden to take spoil:

Under normal circumstances, valuables such as grain would have been plundered by the conquerors. Why was the grain left at Jericho? The Bible provides the answer: Joshua commanded the Israelites that the city and all that is in it were to be dedicated to the Lord.

— Bryant Wood. This is a genuine and striking correlation with the shape of the Joshua 6 narrative; like the fire and the fallen wall, it says nothing on its own about the destruction's date.4

The correlation with Joshua 6

Taken together the three features — a fortified town burned to the ground, a mudbrick wall fallen outward, and untouched granaries — line up point-for-point with the peculiar details of the Jericho story: walls down, city burned "with fire," nothing taken as spoil.

And they burnt the city with fire, and all that was therein… And the people shouted with a great shout, that the wall fell down flat, so that the people went up into the city, every man straight before him, and they took the city.

— Joshua 6:24, 20 (KJV). That the ground at Tell es-Sultan preserves precisely this profile of destruction is the agreed datum this page records. Whether the destruction that fits it is Joshua's destruction — the single question on which the whole correlation turns — depends entirely on the date, taken up under Arguments, not settled here.5

Notes

  • The burn is agreed; the date is the whole dispute. A fortified Bronze Age city at Tell es-Sultan burned fiercely — on that, Garstang, Kenyon, Wood, and the current excavators agree. But Garstang dated City IV's fall to c. 1400 BC and tied it to Joshua; Kenyon redated it to c. 1550 BC (the end of the Middle Bronze Age) and found essentially no walled city standing at c. 1400, which on the early-date conquest scheme is fatal — there would be no Late Bronze Jericho for Joshua to take. Decisively for the mainstream view, radiocarbon dating of short-lived charred grain from the very destruction layer (Bruins & van der Plicht, in Radiocarbon, 1995) returned a date of roughly 1562 ± 38 BC — independent, sample-based confirmation of Kenyon's Middle Bronze figure, against a 1400 BC destruction. Bryant Wood's 1990 BAR reversion to Garstang's c. 1400 BC date (on pottery and stratigraphic grounds) is the leading early-date rebuttal; Titus Kennedy's 2023 restatement of a c. 1400 destruction appears in an MDPI Religions "Biblical Perspective" special issue — keep the venue and advocacy standpoint visible when weighing it. The dating fight is adjudicated under Arguments; this page asserts only the agreed destruction, not that it is Joshua's.6
  • The walls Garstang gave to Joshua are now generally assigned to an earlier system — a real complication. The imposing double fortification (a stone revetment supporting a mudbrick rampart) that Garstang attributed to the city Joshua took is today generally dated to the Early–Middle Bronze defensive system, centuries before any proposed conquest date. So the honest form of the fallen-wall correlation is that a mudbrick wall at Jericho collapsed outward in a destruction — not that the specific wall Garstang labelled has been securely dated to 1400 BC. This is exactly the kind of overstatement flagged where it is made, and it is part of why the date question, not the destruction, is where the case stands or falls.
  • Why this sits on the timeline at c. 1550 BC. The best-attested, sample-dated figure for City IV's fall is the Middle Bronze date around 1550 BC (Kenyon; the 1995 radiocarbon on the burnt grain), so the block is slotted at that date — the earliest external datum on the page — rather than at the contested c. 1400 BC that the early-date reading requires. Readers who hold the early date should read the block as attesting a c. 1400 BC destruction instead; the datum — a fortified city burned, its granaries full, a mudbrick wall fallen outward — is the same either way, and it is precisely the date that decides whether it is Joshua's Jericho.

Editions and background: the early-date case is Bryant G. Wood, "Did the Israelites Conquer Jericho? A New Look at the Archaeological Evidence," Biblical Archaeology Review 16/2 (1990): 44–58 (opening text; full text at Associates for Biblical Research); the neutral radiocarbon anchor is Hendrik J. Bruins and Johannes van der Plicht, "Tell es-Sultan (Jericho): Radiocarbon Results of Short-Lived Cereal and Multiyear Charcoal Samples from the End of the Middle Bronze Age," Radiocarbon 37/2 (1995): 213–220 (PDF); a recent early-date restatement is Titus Kennedy, "The Bronze Age Destruction of Jericho, Archaeology, and the Book of Joshua," Religions 14/6 (2023): art. 796 (MDPI). Kathleen Kenyon's own account is Digging Up Jericho (London: Benn, 1957).
