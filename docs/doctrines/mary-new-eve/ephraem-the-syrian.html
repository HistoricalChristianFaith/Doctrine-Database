Ephraem the Syrian on Mary as the New Eve

Dates: c. 370 (Ephraem — Ephrem the Syrian, c. 306–373 — deacon of Edessa and the foremost Syriac hymnographer; the Hymns on the Nativity belong to his Edessene decade [363–373] and cannot be dated closely within it, so they are anchored to c. 370 for the timeline) · Wikipedia
Summary: In the Hymns on the Nativity of Christ in the Flesh the Eve–Mary parallel becomes song. Ephraem sets “Eve our mother” against “Eve’s daughter” the Virgin, weaving the antithesis into the wider Adam–Christ and virgin-earth typology; and he pictures Eve herself lifting her eyes from Sheol to rejoice that “the Son of her daughter… came down to raise up the mother of His mother.” With him the reading passes from the Greek and Latin apologists into the Syriac liturgical tradition.

Claims & evidence

Eve our mother and Eve’s daughter: the Virgin bears the Adam who is Head over the heavens

Travail Adam on the woman brought, that from him had come forth. She to-day her travail ransomed, who to her a Saviour bare! To Eve our mother a man gave birth, who himself had had no birth. How much more should Eve’s daughter be believed to have borne a Child without a man! The virgin earth, she bare that Adam that was head over the earth! The Virgin bare to-day the Adam that was Head over the Heavens.

— Hymns on the Nativity, Hymn I, source. Ephraem folds the Eve–Mary antithesis into the older Pauline frame: as the virgin earth from which the first Adam was formed prefigures the Virgin from whom the second Adam is born, so “Eve our mother” — through whom travail came — is answered by “Eve’s daughter,” the Virgin who bears the Saviour that ransoms Eve’s travail. The parallel is drawn to defend the virgin birth (the surrounding stanza reasons from the miracle of the first creation to the greater miracle of the second), not as a devotional aside.

Eve lifts her eyes from Sheol: the Son of her daughter raises up the mother of His mother

Eve lifted up her eyes from Sheol and rejoiced in that day, because the Son of her daughter as a medicine of life came down to raise up the mother of His mother. Blessed Babe, that bruised the head of the Serpent that smote her!

— Hymns on the Nativity, Hymn VIII, source. Here the typology turns explicitly redemptive: Mary’s Son is “a medicine of life” who reverses the death Eve brought, and Ephraem ties the reversal to Genesis 3:15 — the “Blessed Babe” who bruised the Serpent’s head. As in Irenaeus, the head-crusher is the seed (Christ) rather than the woman; Mary’s role is to be the daughter of Eve through whom that seed is given.

Notes

  • Ephraem carries the second-century reading — Justin, Irenaeus, Tertullian — into the Syriac liturgical tradition, where it is sung rather than argued. Later writers cite him as a bridge toward Mary’s sinlessness (Eve before the fall as the measure of the second Eve); that development is traced on the Immaculate Conception.
  • Ephraem’s prose Commentary on Genesis reads 3:15 soberly: the enmity is between the serpent and the woman “and her children,” whom the serpent had “subjected to death” — a plain account of the curse, without the later Latin identification of the woman as Mary. His Marian development is in the hymns, keyed to the virgin birth, not to a Mariological reading of the Genesis oracle.
  • Ephraem appears on this wiki also as a witness on the perpetual virginity of Mary.
