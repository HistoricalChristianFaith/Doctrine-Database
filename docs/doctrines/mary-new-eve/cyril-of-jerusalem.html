Cyril of Jerusalem on Mary as the New Eve

Dates: c. 350 (Cyril, later bishop of Jerusalem, c. 313–386; the New-Eve statement is in his Catechetical Lectures, delivered to baptismal candidates at Jerusalem c. 348–350) · Wikipedia
Summary: In the twelfth of his catechetical lectures — ordinary instruction to those preparing for baptism, not polemic against a named heresy — Cyril lists reasons the Word was made man and gives, as the “second,” the Eve–Mary antithesis in its earliest Greek form: “Through Eve yet virgin came death; through a virgin… must the Life appear.” He pairs it with the serpent–Gabriel antithesis, matching Justin’s earliest structure, and so stands as a Greek link in the chain between Tertullian (c. 210) and Ephraem (c. 370).

Claims & evidence

“Through Eve yet virgin came death; through a virgin… must the Life appear”

This then is one cause; but there is also a second. Through Eve yet virgin came death; through a virgin, or rather from a virgin, must the Life appear: that as the serpent beguiled the one, so to the other Gabriel might bring good tidings.

— Catechetical Lecture 12 (On the Words Incarnate, and Made Man), §15, trans. Edwin Hamilton Gifford, NPNF Series II, vol. 7 (source; cf. New Advent). Cyril is answering the question why the eternal Word took flesh. His “second” cause is the recapitulation motif: death entered through Eve — herself still a virgin in paradise — so life must enter through a virgin, and as the serpent’s word beguiled the first, so Gabriel’s good tidings come to the second. The parallel runs virgin Eve / serpent’s word ↔ virgin Mary / angel’s word — the same two-membered structure Justin gives as the earliest surviving form of the reading. In the sentences just after, Cyril extends the “same weapons” logic to the whole economy: “By the very same weapons… wherewith the Devil used to vanquish us, have we been saved.”

Notes

  • The setting is worth weighing: this is catechesis — standard baptismal instruction at Jerusalem — not a treatise aimed at a particular error. That the Eve–Mary antithesis appears here, unargued and in passing, as one item in a list of reasons for the Incarnation, shows the typology had already become part of ordinary mid-fourth-century teaching in the Greek East, not merely an apologist’s device against Trypho or Marcion.
  • Cyril’s emphasis, like the earlier witnesses’, is Christological: the point of the antithesis is the reality and fitness of the Incarnation (“that the Life appear”), with Mary the virgin counter-term to the virgin Eve. He does not press it toward any independent Marian claim — the reversal turns on the coming of Life through her, answered to the coming of death through Eve.
  • Cyril appears on this wiki also as a witness on the real presence, from the Mystagogic Catecheses that close the same lecture series. (The authorship of those final five lectures is sometimes disputed between Cyril and his successor John of Jerusalem; Lecture 12, part of the main catechetical series, is not subject to that question.)
