Proclus of Constantinople on Mary as the New Eve

Dates: c. 430 (Proclus, then bishop of Cyzicus and later archbishop of Constantinople, c. 385–446; his Homily 1, On the Holy Virgin Theotokos was preached in the Great Church at Constantinople while Nestorius sat in the see, on the eve of the controversy that led to the Council of Ephesus in 431) · Wikipedia
Summary: In the most celebrated Marian sermon of the age — delivered in Nestorius’s own church in defense of the title Theotokos — Proclus folds the Eve–Mary reversal into the case for a real Incarnation. He states the antithesis in the Greek “conception through hearing” form: “Through ears that disobeyed, the serpent poured in his poison; through ears that obeyed, the Word entered.” The homily thus carries the New-Eve typology directly into the Ephesus-era Christology, tying this timeline to the Theotokos debate, and stands as a Greek witness between Augustine (c. 396) and John of Damascus (c. 730).

Claims & evidence

“Through ears that disobeyed, the serpent poured in his poison; through ears that obeyed, the Word entered”

Through ears that disobeyed, the serpent poured in his poison; through ears that obeyed, the Word entered in order to build a living temple. From the place where Cain, the first disciple of sin, emerged, from there also did Christ, the redeemer of the race, sprout unsown into life.

— Homily 1 (On the Holy Virgin Theotokos), §2, trans. Nicholas Constas, Proclus of Constantinople and the Cult of the Virgin in Late Antiquity: Homilies 1–5, Texts and Translations (Supplements to Vigiliae Christianae 66; Brill, 2003), p. 139 (full translation here). The reversal is the aural one that runs back to Justin and Irenaeus: the serpent’s word crept in through the ear of the first virgin, Eve, to breed disobedience and death; the Word of God enters through the ear of the second virgin, Mary, who believes and so conceives — “a living temple.” Proclus makes the two women the twin gates of poison and of life, and does so precisely to insist that the one born of Mary is God made truly man — the point of the whole sermon against Nestorius.

The “ancient fleece of Adam” woven into new flesh

… the awesome loom of the divine economy upon which the robe of union was ineffably woven. The loom-worker was the Holy Spirit; the wool-worker the overshadowing power from on high. The wool was the ancient fleece of Adam; the interlocking thread the spotless flesh of the Virgin.

— Homily 1, §1 (Constas, pp. 137–39; full translation here). Proclus’s signature image of the Virgin’s womb as a loom sets the New-Eve reading inside the second-Adam frame that underlies it: the “ancient fleece of Adam” — fallen humanity — is taken up and rewoven into the “spotless flesh of the Virgin,” the robe the incarnate God puts on. The old stock is not discarded but repaired, which is just the logic of recapitulation on which the Eve–Mary antithesis depends.

“The pride of women and the glory of the female”

What we celebrate is the pride of women and the glory of the female, thanks to the one who was at once both mother and virgin… where sin increased, grace abounded yet more.

— Homily 1, §1 (Constas, p. 137; full translation here). The sermon opens by declaring that the Virgin’s feast honors womankind — the “pride of women,” the “glory of the female” — and glosses it at once with Romans 5:20, the verse of the second Adam. The honor Eve forfeited is restored in Mary; the reversal of the first woman’s shame is the frame within which Proclus then states the serpent/ear antithesis.

Notes

  • The occasion sharpens the point. Proclus preached this while Nestorius, who objected to calling Mary Theotokos (“God-bearer”), presided over the church; the sermon is a public defense of the title and of the “coupling of natures,” divine and human, in the one Christ. The Eve–Mary typology is thus deployed, as in the earliest witnesses, in the service of Christology — here the reality of the Incarnation the Theotokos title guards — not as a free-standing Marian claim.
  • Proclus does not use the Latin tag mors per Evam, vita per Mariam; his form is the Greek “conception through hearing” motif (the disobedient versus the obedient ear), the same structure Justin, Irenaeus, and Cyril of Jerusalem give — the serpent’s word to Eve answered by the angel’s word to Mary. The antithesis is carried by that aural contrast rather than by a matched “death/life” couplet.
  • This homily is a cornerstone of the Theotokos timeline as well; on the New-Eve timeline it shows the typology feeding directly into the Ephesine debate over Mary’s title in the year before the council met.
