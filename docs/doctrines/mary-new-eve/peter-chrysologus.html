Peter Chrysologus on Mary as the New Eve

Dates: c. 440 (Peter Chrysologus, bishop of Ravenna, c. 380–450, “the golden-worded” preacher; his Sermon 99, on the parable of the leaven, is dated to the 430s) · Wikipedia
Summary: Preaching in Latin at Ravenna in the years around the Council of Ephesus, Peter Chrysologus gives the Eve–Mary antithesis its terse Latin couplet — sicut per Evam venit ad omnes mors, ita per Mariam rediret omnibus vita, “as through Eve death came to all, so through Mary life returned to all” — and, reading the woman of the leaven parable as Mary, casts her as the true “mother of all the living” who reverses Eve, “the mother of all the dead.” He stands as a fifth-century Latin witness alongside the Greek homilists of the Nestorian milieu (Proclus, Cyril of Alexandria) and just after Augustine.

Claims & evidence

“As through Eve death came to all, so through Mary life returned to all”

Ob hoc namque Christus nasci voluit, ut sicut per Evam venit ad omnes mors, ita per Mariam rediret omnibus vita.

— Sermon 99 (On the Parable of the Leaven, De parabola fermenti; Latin as printed in Patrologia Latina 52, from the critical text ed. A. Olivar, CCSL 24), Latin Wikisource. In the English of William B. Palardy: “For it was indeed for this reason that Christ willed to be born, that just as death came to all through Eve, so life might return to all through Mary” (St. Peter Chrysologus: Selected Sermons, vol. 2, trans. W. B. Palardy, Fathers of the Church 109; CUA Press, 2004). This is the “death through Eve, life through Mary” antithesis in its compact Latin form — the same reversal Jerome had coined a generation earlier (mors per Evam, vita per Mariam) — here made the very reason of the Incarnation (“for this Christ willed to be born”).

Mary the “mother of all the living,” reversing Eve the “mother of all the dead”

… et esset omnium viventium mater vera per Christum, quae erat in Adam mater omnium mortuorum.

— Sermon 99 (Latin Wikisource); Palardy: “and might truly be, through Christ, the mother of all the living, she who in Adam was the mother of all the dead.” Reading the woman who hides leaven “in three measures of meal” (Matthew 13:33) as an image of Mary working the leaven of faith through the ages of history, Chrysologus turns Eve’s own title inside out: the name “mother of all living” that Genesis gave the first woman (Genesis 3:20) belongs in truth to Mary, through whom life comes, while Eve — in Adam — proved the mother of all the dead. The move is the same one Epiphanius made in Greek: Eve’s title points past Eve to the Virgin who bears the Living One.

Notes

  • The couplet belongs to the same Latin lineage as Jerome’s mors per Evam, vita per Mariam and Augustine’s “through Eve, ruin; through Mary, salvation.” Chrysologus, preaching at Ravenna in the decade of the Council of Ephesus (431), carries it into the fifth-century Latin West at the moment the Greek East was fighting over the title Theotokos.
  • The antithesis is not a stray devotional line but the hinge of an extended allegory: the woman of the leaven parable is read as Mary, the leaven as faith, the “three measures” as the ages from Adam to Christ — so that the whole lump of fallen humanity, corrupted in Eve, is leavened anew to life through Mary. The typology is Christological at root (life comes “through Christ”), with Mary the instrumental counter-term to Eve.
  • The primary text quoted here is the Latin of Patrologia Latina 52 (critical edition CCSL 24, ed. Olivar); the English is Palardy’s Fathers of the Church rendering. Older selections render fermentum as “leaven” rather than “yeast.”
