Justin Martyr on Mary as the New Eve

Dates: c. 160 (the Dialogue with Trypho is usually dated c. 155–165, after the two Apologies) · Wikipedia
Summary: The earliest surviving statement of the Eve–Mary parallel: Christ became man through the Virgin “in order that the disobedience which proceeded from the serpent might receive its destruction in the same manner in which it derived its origin.” Eve, a virgin, conceived the serpent’s word and bore disobedience and death; Mary, a virgin, received faith and joy at Gabriel’s word and bore the Son of God.

Claims & evidence

The disobedience of the virgin Eve is undone through the Virgin Mary

…and that He became man by the Virgin, in order that the disobedience which proceeded from the serpent might receive its destruction in the same manner in which it derived its origin. For Eve, who was a virgin and undefiled, having conceived the word of the serpent, brought forth disobedience and death. But the Virgin Mary received faith and joy, when the angel Gabriel announced the good tidings to her that the Spirit of the Lord would come upon her, and the power of the Highest would overshadow her: wherefore also the Holy Thing begotten of her is the Son of God; and she replied, ‘Be it unto me according to thy word.’

— Dialogue with Trypho, ch. 100, source. Justin sets the parallel inside a wider argument that Christ is the second Adam who reverses the fall “in the same manner” it began: as a virgin’s ear received the serpent’s word, so a virgin’s assent receives the angel’s. The typology is Christological — its point is the recapitulation of the fall in Christ — but it fixes Mary’s obedient reply (Luke 1:38) as the hinge on which the reversal turns.

Notes

  • Justin gives the parallel in seed form — a single antithesis (Eve believed the serpent / Mary believed the angel) — without the developed “recapitulation” architecture Irenaeus would build on it a generation later, or the “knot loosed” imagery.
  • The reading is soteriological and typological, not biographical: it says nothing about whether Mary remained ever-virgin (the separate question traced on the perpetual virginity of Mary). What matters here is the obedience of the second virgin over against the disobedience of the first.
