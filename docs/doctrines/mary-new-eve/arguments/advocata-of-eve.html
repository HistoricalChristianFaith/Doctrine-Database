Does Irenaeus’s advocata make Mary the advocate (intercessor) of Eve?

Type: interpretive argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak as a proof of intercessory Marian mediation from Irenaeus — it hangs on a single Latin word (advocata) rendering a Greek original that is lost, in a passage whose plain sense is a balance-scale of two women’s obedience; the sense the context supports, Mary the counterpart / helper of Eve, is sound, but the juridical “advocate who pleads” is a later meaning read back into the word. Advanced by the medieval and modern Marian tradition (the Salve Regina’s “advocata nostra”; O’Carroll).

Related arguments: the reach of the typology — see also is the parallel a Christological point or a Marian exaltation? · does the typology entail Mary’s sinlessness or co-redemption?

The claim

And if the former did disobey God, yet the latter was persuaded to be obedient to God, in order that the Virgin Mary might become the patroness (advocata) of the virgin Eve. And thus, as the human race fell into bondage to death by means of a virgin, so is it rescued by a virgin; virginal disobedience having been balanced in the opposite scale by virginal obedience.

— Irenaeus, Against Heresies V.19.1.1 The Latin runs uti virginis Evae virgo Maria fieret advocata — “that the virgin Mary might become the advocata of the virgin Eve.” The strong Marian reading takes advocata in its full later sense — advocate / intercessor, one who pleads another’s cause — and finds here an early patristic warrant for Mary’s intercessory office, Eve’s cause pleaded by Mary and a seed of the later doctrine of Marian mediation. The deflationary reading takes it as patroness / counterpart / helper — the answering counter-term in a typological pairing (as Christ is to Adam, so Mary is to Eve), carrying no claim about intercessory prayer. The dispute turns almost entirely on one word.

Who advances it

The intercessory reading is the settled Latin devotional one: from the twelfth century advocata names Mary’s power of intercession — it stands in the Salve Regina (“advocata nostra”) and is pressed by Bernard of Clairvaux (“our Advocate, reconcile us to your Son”) — and modern Marian theology reaches back through it to Irenaeus as the first witness, taking the Latin advocata to translate a Greek paraklētos, the Johannine word for the Advocate–Paraclete.2 The question here is not whether the medieval title is licit, nor whether Mary intercedes, but the narrower historical one the lead isolates: does Irenaeus, in this sentence, assert an intercessory role — or is the intercessory sense a freight the word gathered centuries downstream and was then read back into him?

The philology: a translator’s word for a lost original

The decisive fact is that advocata is not Irenaeus’s word. Irenaeus wrote Against Heresies in Greek, but the Greek of Book V is lost; the book survives whole only in an early, very literal Latin translation, and advocata is the Latin translator’s rendering of a Greek term we no longer possess.1 What that term was is a genuine and unsettled scholarly dispute, and two candidates frame it:

  • paraklētos (παράκλητος) — the usual older assumption, the word John uses of the Spirit, the “Advocate.” It means both “advocate” and “comforter / consoler,” so even on this reconstruction the intercessory sense is not fixed — the ambiguity is baked into the very word.2
  • antilēpsis (ἀντίληψις) — the conjecture flagged by the Ante-Nicene Fathers editor at this very line: “a person called in to help, or to take hold of the other end of a burden,” so that “Mary was thus the counterpart or balance of Eve.” The same note warns that the text here “is most uncertain and obscure.”3

Between the two the meaning shifts from “intercessor” to “helper who takes the other end of the load,” and no surviving evidence decides which Irenaeus wrote. The honest verdict of the specialists is that stated in the standard reference: “in view of the differences of interpretation proposed by expert patrologists, it is difficult to decide what exactly St. Irenaeus meant by the word.”2 A doctrine of intercession cannot be founded on a word whose original form is unknown and whose two candidates point in different directions.

The parallel in Irenaeus’s other work is often cited as corroboration — and it does confirm the idea, but not the word. His Demonstration of the Apostolic Preaching §33 restates the same Eve–Mary reversal; but the Demonstration too survives only in translation (an Armenian version recovered in 1904), and its first English translator, J. Armitage Robinson, renders the clause “Eve summed up in Mary, that a virgin should be a virgin’s intercessor,” cross-referencing the Latin advocata of Book V.4 So the second witness runs through the same bottleneck — a translator’s choice of word for a Greek original neither text preserves. Two translations of a lost term are not two independent attestations of “intercessor.”

The context: a balance-scale, not a courtroom

Set the word back in its sentence and the plain sense favours counterpart. The whole passage is Irenaeus’s recapitulation antithesis: as Adam is summed up and reversed in Christ, so Eve is summed up and reversed in Mary. The controlling image is a pair of scales — “virginal disobedience having been balanced in the opposite scale by virginal obedience” — in which Mary is the answering weight to Eve, the term that rights what the first virgin’s unbelief overturned.1 Nothing in the surrounding argument is about pleading, petition, or prayer; it is about two obediences weighed against two disobediences. On that reading advocata is the structural counterpart — the one “called in” to take Eve’s opposite place on the balance — and the juridical “advocate who pleads a client’s cause” is precisely the sense the immediate imagery does not supply. The word leaves a door ajar; the sentence does not walk through it.

Adversarial assessment

What holds — don’t over-deflate. A bare “mere metaphor” dismissal undersells Irenaeus. He does give Mary a genuinely active place in the economy: in the companion passage she “by yielding obedience, become[s] the cause of salvation, both to herself and the whole human race” (AH III.22.4), and the advocata image itself makes her an agent on the scale, not the vessel of an agent.5 The typology only works because Mary’s free assent is treated as really answering Eve’s free refusal, term for term. So “Irenaeus assigns Mary an active, honoured role” is true and is a real datum the later tradition legitimately draws on (this is the surplus weighed in the companion argument).

What is overstated. But that active role is located at the Annunciation — Mary’s obedient “yes” that lets the Word take flesh — not in post-mortem intercession, and the two must be kept distinct. To read the developed medieval advocata (Mary enthroned in glory, pleading sinners’ causes before her Son) back into Irenaeus’s second-century word is anachronism: it imports the reception into the source. Three things block the intercessory proof: the Greek Irenaeus actually wrote is lost; its two likeliest forms include one (antilēpsis) that means “helper,” not “advocate”; and the sentence’s own image is a balance of obediences, not a plea. That the word later carried the intercessory sense — certainly by the Salve Regina — tells us what twelfth-century Latin devotion meant by it, not what a second-century Greek bishop asserted. Gambero’s own summary of the patristic evidence is that Irenaeus deploys the Eve–Mary contrast “in a completely Christocentric context” to safeguard the reality of the redemptive Incarnation — the setting of a Christological argument, not a treatise on Marian prayer.6

Assessment

Weak as a proof of intercessory Marian mediation from Irenaeus; the softer, typological reading — Mary the counterpart / helper of Eve — is sound. The intercessory case rests on a single Latin word, advocata, rendering a Greek original that does not survive, in a passage whose plain sense is a balance-scale of two women’s obedience; the two best guesses at that lost Greek (paraklētos, antilēpsis) diverge exactly on the point in dispute, and even paraklētos means “comforter” as readily as “advocate.” The Demonstration §33 parallel corroborates the reversal-motif but runs through the same translational bottleneck, so it adds no independent purchase on the word. What Irenaeus genuinely gives Mary — an active, honoured role in the reversal of the fall — belongs to her obedient assent at the Annunciation, and should not be silently upgraded into a developed office of heavenly intercession. The reader should keep two things apart: the intercessory advocata is a licit later development of Marian devotion, but as an exegesis of what Irenaeus asserts it is a reading the evidence underdetermines — the counterpart sense is what the text will bear.
