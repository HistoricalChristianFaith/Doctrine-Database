Does Genesis 3:15 make Mary the serpent-crusher? (the Vulgate ipsa vs. the Hebrew hû)

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — the reading that makes Mary herself the one who crushes the serpent’s head rests on the Latin ipsa conteret (“she shall crush”), and that pronoun is an interpretive Latin variant, not the text: the Hebrew hû is masculine, the Greek autos is masculine, the crusher is the seed (Christ), Jerome the Vulgate’s own translator glossed it masculine, and the Nova Vulgata dropped ipsa for the neuter ipsum. The lesser, typological reading — that Mary, as the New Eve and mother of the victorious seed, is “the woman” God sets at enmity with the serpent — is a separate and plausible claim, but it is a retreat from what the proof-text is enlisted to assert · advanced, magisterially, by Ineffabilis Deus (Pope Pius IX)

The claim

The Lord said to the serpent: I will put enmities between you and the woman, and your seed and her seed; she shall crush your head, and you shall lie in wait for her heel. … Bernard says of the glorious Virgin: “She crushed the head of the ancient serpent, when she reduced to nothing every suggestion of the evil one.”

— Bonaventure, Collationes de Septem Donis, Collation 6, citing Bernard of Clairvaux.1 The claim reads the promise of Genesis 3:15 — “she shall bruise thy head” — as identifying the woman herself, understood as Mary, as the appointed conqueror of the serpent. So read, it is the load-bearing Old-Testament proof-text behind both the New-Eve typology and, later, the scriptural warrant of the Immaculate Conception: the Protoevangelium, the “first gospel,” in which Mary stands wholly and from the first at odds with Satan.

Who advances it

The reading is carried by the Latin text of Genesis 3:15. Where the Hebrew and Greek make the serpent’s conqueror masculine, the Vulgate as it came to circulate reads ipsa conteret caput tuum — “she shall crush your head” — and on that feminine pronoun the medieval Latin devotion built. Bernard of Clairvaux and Bonaventure apply the “she crushed the serpent’s head” to the Virgin by name; the reading passes into Marian art (Mary treading the serpent) and reaches its magisterial form in Pius IX’s Ineffabilis Deus (1854), which gathers the Genesis 3:15 “woman” into the case for the definition.2 The argument therefore stands or falls on a text-critical question — what Genesis 3:15 actually says about who crushes the serpent — and is best weighed as one.

The evidence for

Two things can be granted at once. First, the enmity really is set “between the serpent and the woman”: the woman is a named party to the promise, not a bystander, and on the New-Eve reading that woman finds her fulfilment in Mary as surely as the second Adam finds his in Christ. Second, a feminine Latin text of the verse is genuinely old: Ambrose already reads “she shall watch for your head,” and Bede comments on “she will crush your head” — so the medieval preachers did not invent the pronoun.3 On top of that, the old Catholic Encyclopedia defends the Vulgate’s “she” not as a blunder but as an “explanatory version, expressing explicitly… Our Lady’s part in the victory over the serpent, which is contained implicitly in the Hebrew original.” So stated — the woman implicated in the seed’s victory — the Marian application is not frivolous.4

Adversarial assessment

What holds. Mary is the woman of Genesis 3:15 on any New-Eve reading, and the enmity between that woman and the serpent is real and total — the point the typology genuinely secures. Nothing below disturbs that. What is at issue is the stronger and more specific claim the proof-text is usually enlisted to carry: that Mary is the one who does the crushing.

What is overstated. On that specific point the case fails, and it fails on the text itself.

(1) The subject of “shall crush” is the seed, not the woman. In the Hebrew the pronoun is hû (הוּא), masculine, reaching back to the masculine noun zera‘ (“seed, offspring”); the verb yəshûpəkā is third-person masculine singular. The crusher is “her seed,” not she. The Septuagint keeps the masculine deliberately — autos sou tērēsei kephalēn, “he shall watch thy head” — choosing the masculine autos even over the neuter its own word sperma (“seed”) would call for, because the offspring in view is a person. Every early reader who works from the Greek or the Hebrew takes the conqueror to be that offspring: Irenaeus reads “He who should be born of a woman… keeping watch for the head of the serpent” — the seed, whom he names as Christ; Ephrem hails “the Blessed Babe, that bruised the head of the Serpent”; Cosmas Indicopleustes glosses “thou for his head” of the man who treads the serpent down. The head-crusher of Genesis 3:15, read grammatically, is Christ.5

(2) Jerome — the Vulgate’s own translator — glosses it masculine. The decisive internal witness against the Marian pronoun is the man who made the Latin Bible. In his Hebrew Questions on Genesis Jerome renders the clause “He will crush your head, and you will crush his heel,” and adds that this “is better in Hebrew” — “for our steps are hindered by the serpent, and the Lord will quickly crush Satan under our feet.” Jerome plainly did not read the verse as “she,” and did not think the Hebrew allowed it.6 The ipsa of the circulating Vulgate is thus a transmissional/interpretive variant that arose after Jerome, not his translation of the Hebrew — and the point is now conceded from within: the Catholic Encyclopedia grants that the “she” rendering “originated after the fourth century, and cannot be defended critically,” that “the conqueror… who should crush the serpent’s head, is Christ,” and Mary only “the woman at enmity with the serpent”; and the 1979 Nova Vulgata abandons ipsa altogether, printing the neuter ipsum conteret caput tuum to agree with semen (“seed”).7

(3) Even the old Latin “she” was not read of Mary crushing. The feminine text existed, but the Latin fathers who had it did not draw the conclusion the argument needs. Ambrose turns “she shall watch for your head” into a moral about not walking in earthly things and putting on “the sandals of the gospel”; Bede reads “she will crush your head” of “the holy Church” that detects and scatters the devil’s wiles — not of Mary. The specific identification “she = Mary, who crushes” crystallizes only later, with Bernard and Bonaventure, on a pronoun that by then was already a known peculiarity of the Latin.8

Assessment

Weak — as a claim that Genesis 3:15 identifies Mary as the crusher of the serpent’s head. That reading depends on the Latin ipsa (“she shall crush”), and the pronoun does not carry the weight: the Hebrew hû and the Greek autos are masculine, the crusher is “her seed” (Christ), the fathers who read the Greek or Hebrew say as much, Jerome the Vulgate’s own translator glossed it masculine and thought the Hebrew required it, and the Nova Vulgata has since dropped ipsa for the neuter ipsum. This grammatical overreach should be flagged wherever the proof-text is pressed to prove Mary the serpent-slayer. What survives — and it is a real thing, not nothing — is the softer typological reading: Mary is “the woman” set at enmity with the serpent, the New Eve whose seed does the crushing; that claim needs no ipsa, coheres with the whole New-Eve pattern traced on the home timeline, and is independently plausible — even the Catholic Encyclopedia rests the Marian application there rather than on the pronoun. The two must be kept apart: Mary as the woman of the Protoevangelium is well-grounded; Mary as its serpent-crusher is a reading the text will not bear.
