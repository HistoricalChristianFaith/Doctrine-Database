Hesychius of Jerusalem on Mary as the New Eve

Dates: c. 440 (Hesychius, presbyter and preacher of Jerusalem, d. after 451; his festal homilies in praise of the Mother of God belong to the second quarter of the fifth century) · Wikipedia
Summary: In a festal homily praising the Virgin, Hesychius of Jerusalem folds the Eve reversal into a dense typological encomium: the Virgin is the one “who freed Eve from her shame and Adam from the curse,” who “cut off the bold insolence of the dragon.” The line is compressed — a single clause inside an Ark-and-gem litany rather than an extended “through Eve… through Mary” parallel — but it states the New-Eve motif plainly, a Greek witness of the Ephesus generation alongside Proclus and Cyril of Alexandria.

Claims & evidence

The Virgin “who freed Eve from her shame and Adam from the curse”

Behold a Virgin. Who is she? The most noble of women, the elect among virgins, the splendid ornament of our nature, the glory of our mould, who freed Eve from her shame and Adam from the curse, who cut off the bold insolence of the dragon, she whom the smoke of concupiscence touched not, nor the worm of pleasure harmed.

— Hesychius of Jerusalem, Oration on the Praises of the Holy Mother of God (In sanctam Mariam Deiparam; the homily is edited by Michel Aubineau, Les homélies festales d’Hésychius de Jérusalem, vol. 1, Subsidia Hagiographica 59, Brussels 1978; Greek in Patrologia Graeca 93), trans. Thomas Livius, The Blessed Virgin in the Fathers of the First Six Centuries (London: Burns & Oates, 1893), p. 89. Mary is set against Eve in one dense turn: she “freed Eve from her shame” — the shame of the fall — and Adam from the curse, and “cut off the bold insolence of the dragon,” the serpent of Genesis 3. The reversal is stated as accomplished fact and paired with the crushing of the serpent, the New-Eve motif in miniature.

Notes

  • The witness is real but compressed. Where Irenaeus and Cyril of Jerusalem lay out a two-membered “virgin Eve / virgin Mary” parallel, Hesychius packs the reversal into a single relative clause within a rolling encomium (the Virgin as Ark, as gem, as the fulfilment of Isaiah 7:14). The Eve motif is present and unmistakable, but it is one jewel in a litany rather than a developed argument.
  • The only widely accessible English is the nineteenth-century rendering of Thomas Livius, made from the old Bibliotheca Patrum Graeco-Latina; the critical Greek text is Aubineau’s (with a French translation, in print). The homily is one of Hesychius’s two authentic Marian homilies; its Ark passage is cited as genuine Hesychius (Patrologia Graeca 93, col. 1464) in Paul VI’s Marialis Cultus (1974), n. 78.
  • Hesychius preached at Jerusalem in the decades around the Council of Ephesus (431); his Marian homilies belong to the same fifth-century festal flowering as Proclus at Constantinople, when the New-Eve typology and the Theotokos title were being sung together across the Greek East.
