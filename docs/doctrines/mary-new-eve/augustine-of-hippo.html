Augustine of Hippo on Mary as the New Eve

Dates: c. 396 (the terse antithesis — “through a woman death, through a woman life” — in De agone Christiano 22.24, an instructional treatise of the late 390s; restated more fully c. 410–418 in Sermon 51; Augustine 354–430) · Wikipedia
Summary: The great Latin doctor folds the Eve–Mary antithesis into his standing defence of the goodness of the body and the female sex against the Manichaeans: as death entered the world through a woman (Eve, corrupted by the deceiver), so life came through a woman (Mary, who bore the Saviour). The contrast turns on the two women’s choices — Eve deceived and disobedient, Mary uncorrupted and believing — a Christological corollary of the second-Adam recapitulation, marshalled to defend the reality of the Incarnation and the dignity of women against a sect that scorned birth from a woman as defiling.

Claims & evidence

“Through a woman came death; through a woman, life: through Eve, ruin; through Mary, salvation”

Through a woman came death; through a woman, life: through Eve, ruin; through Mary, salvation. The former, corrupted, followed the deceiver; the latter, uncorrupted, gave birth to the Saviour.

— The Christian Combat (De agone Christiano) 22.24, trans. Robert P. Russell, in Christian Instruction; Admonition and Grace; The Christian Combat; Faith, Hope and Charity (Fathers of the Church 2; CUA Press, 1947). Written for unschooled believers in the late 390s, the treatise gives the antithesis in its sharpest, most balanced Latin form — the same two-membered mors / vita, Eva / Maria pairing that Jerome had coined a decade earlier and that Lumen Gentium 56 would later ascribe to the fathers. Augustine grounds it not in Mary’s nature but in the women’s opposite responses: Eve “corrupted, followed the deceiver,” Mary “uncorrupted, gave birth to the Saviour.”

“Through a woman came the poison, through a woman came the salvation” — against the Manichaeans

Christ wished to be born of a virgin. Through a woman came the poison, through a woman came the salvation.

By deceiving, poison was offered to man through a woman: by repairing, salvation is offered to man through a woman. Let a woman compensate for the sin of the man deceived through her, by giving birth to Christ. Hence, also, women first announced the resurrection of God to the Apostles. A woman announced death to her husband in paradise: and women announced salvation to men in the Church.

— Sermon 51 (on the harmony of the genealogies), §§2–3, source. Here Augustine is answering the Manichaean taunt “ut quid elegerit feminam?” — why should Christ deign to be born of a woman at all? — and turns the sneer with the Eve–Mary reversal: precisely because the first man “was deceived by a woman,” women would else “despair about themselves,” so Christ was born of a woman that “in no sex of human creation should there be despair about Himself.” The sex through which the poison came is the sex through which the antidote comes; the “woman… in paradise” who announced death is answered by the women who announce the resurrection.

Notes

  • An anti-heretical, not devotional, setting — as with the earliest fathers. Sermon 51 deploys the parallel inside a defence of the Incarnation against Manichaean disdain for birth and the body; the point of “salvation… through a woman” is that the female sex is God’s good creature and no bar to the Saviour, “so that in no sex of human creation should there be despair.” The typology is, as with Justin, Irenaeus, and Tertullian, a Christological corollary put to a further use — here the goodness of the body and the equal standing of women as both sinners and instruments of salvation. On the scholarly setting and the disputed date (the majority place the sermon c. 410–412, Verbraken c. 417/418), see Anthony Dupont, “‘Ut quid elegerit feminam?’: Manichaeans Questioning the Usefulness of Mary in the Perspective of Augustine’s Sermon 51.”
  • A contrast of choices, not of natures. Augustine’s antithesis turns on Eve’s being “corrupted” and deceived against Mary’s obedient, “uncorrupted” bearing of Christ — the two women’s responses, not a claim about Mary’s sinless nature. The step from “obedient New Eve” to an immaculate conception is a later development, not an entailment of the typology — see the entailment argument.
  • Do not read an immaculate Augustine into this. Augustine appears on this wiki also on the Immaculate Conception timeline, where his famous reservation — “the holy Virgin Mary, concerning whom I wish to raise no question when it touches the subject of sins, out of honour to the Lord” (On Nature and Grace 36) — is reticence about attributing actual sins to her, framed by his conviction that all others are born under original sin. His New-Eve typology here is a distinct thread, resting on Mary’s believing obedience, and should not be conflated with a doctrine of her sinless conception.
