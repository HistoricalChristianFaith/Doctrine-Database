John of Damascus on Mary as the New Eve

Dates: c. 730 (John of Damascus, monk of Mar Saba and the last of the Greek fathers, c. 675/676–749; the doctrinal statement is in An Exposition of the Orthodox Faith, and the fullest typological development in the three homilies on the Dormition preached at Gethsemane, all placed in his mature Palestinian period) · Wikipedia
Summary: John gathers the patristic reading into a settled formula: Mary, giving her flesh to the Son of God, is “satisfying the debt of the first mother” and bringing forth “the new Adam.” In the Dormition homilies he lets Adam and Eve themselves address her as “our blessed daughter, who hast removed the penalty of our disobedience,” through whom “good” comes “from sorrow.”

Claims & evidence

Mary satisfies the debt of the first mother and brings forth the new Adam

She ministered to the Creator in that He was created, to the Fashioner in that He was fashioned, and to the Son of God and God in that He was made flesh and became man from her pure and immaculate flesh and blood, satisfying the debt of the first mother.

For just as the latter was formed from Adam without connection, so also did the former bring forth the new Adam, who was brought forth in accordance with the laws of parturition and above the nature of generation.

— An Exposition of the Orthodox Faith, IV.14, trans. S. D. F. Salmond, source. “The first mother” is Eve; Mary repays her debt. John keeps the reversal anchored in the Incarnation — the new Adam is formed from the second Eve as the first Adam from the virgin earth — the same frame drawn by Ambrose and Ephraem.

Adam and Eve address Mary: “through thee good from sorrow”

Thou blessed daughter of ours, who hast removed the penalty of our disobedience! Thou, inheriting from us a mortal body, hast won us immortality. Thou, taking thy being from us, hast given us back the being in grace. Thou hast conquered pain and loosened the bondage of death. Thou hast restored us to our former state. We had shut the door of paradise; thou didst find entrance to the tree of life. Through us sorrow came out of good; through thee good from sorrow. How canst thou who art all fair taste of death? Thou art the gate of life and the ladder to heaven. Death is become the passage to immortality.

— Second Homily on the Dormition, trans. Mary H. Allies (1898), source. John puts the New-Eve reading into the mouths of Adam and Eve, who greet Mary as the daughter who reverses their fall — the shut door of paradise reopened, the tree of life regained. The phrase “through us sorrow came out of good; through thee good from sorrow” is the antithesis in its most compressed Byzantine form.

Notes

  • By John’s day the typology has hardened into a fixed liturgical topos; the Second Vatican Council would later cite him among the fathers for the New-Eve reading (Lumen Gentium 56). His homilies also make him a principal witness to the Dormition, traced on the Assumption / Dormition, where he appears again.
  • The reversal remains, as in the earliest witnesses, a work God accomplishes through Mary in the Incarnation, not a redemptive act of her own; John frames her as the one who “ministered” and gave her flesh, the vessel of the new Adam.
