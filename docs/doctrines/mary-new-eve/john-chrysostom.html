John Chrysostom on Mary as the New Eve

Dates: c. 390 (John Chrysostom, c. 349–407, then a presbyter preaching at Antioch; the Nativity homily quoted here belongs to his Antiochene years) · Wikipedia
Summary: Chrysostom’s Mariology is famously reserved — he most often pairs Eve not with Mary but with fallen humanity or with virgins in general. But in a Nativity homily transmitted among his works he gives the full New-Eve antithesis, and names Mary: the Devil deceived the virgin Eve, so Gabriel brought good news to the virgin Mary; Eve’s word bred death, Mary bore the Word who is life; Eve’s word pointed to the tree that cast Adam from Paradise, Mary’s Word to the Cross that led Adam back in. He stands as a Greek witness between Ambrose (c. 389) and Augustine (c. 396).

Claims & evidence

Virgin Eve deceived / virgin Mary evangelized; the tree answered by the Cross

And why is He born from a virgin, and keeps her virginity undefiled? Because, long ago, the Devil deceived Eve while a virgin; for this reason Gabriel shared the good news with Mary while a virgin. But Eve, when she was deceived, on the one hand, gave birth to a word [that was the] cause of death. But, in contrast, after Mary was told the good news, she bore the Word in flesh, the agent of our eternal life. Eve’s word pointed out a tree, through which she thrust Adam from Paradise. But the Word from the virgin pointed out the Cross, through which He led the bandit (representing Adam) into Paradise.

— Second Homily on the Birthday of Our Savior. This is the complete New-Eve structure and it names Mary throughout: the two virgins (Eve, Mary) addressed by two spirits (the Devil, Gabriel); the two “words” (Eve’s word of death, the Word Mary bears); and the two trees (the tree of the fall, the Cross), with Adam expelled through the one and the penitent thief — “representing Adam” — brought in through the other. The reversal is the same one Justin and Irenaeus drew, worked out here in a matched, four-fold antithesis.

The Virgin “paying off the debt owed… on Eve’s behalf”

… for this reason today the virgin gave birth without a husband, paying off the debt owed to men on Eve’s behalf.

— Second Homily on the Birthday of Our Savior. Earlier in the same sermon Chrysostom frames Mary’s childbearing as the settling of a debt Eve incurred: as Adam produced a woman without a wife, so now a virgin bears a man without a husband, and womankind repays what it owed — Mary discharging, on Eve’s behalf, the debt of the first woman.

Notes

  • A reserved Mariology. Across his securely-genuine works — the homilies On Matthew (including the nativity homilies), On Romans (even Homily 10 on Adam and Christ in Romans 5), On John — Chrysostom keeps Eve paired with fallen humanity or with women generally, not with Mary; he even speaks sharply of Mary’s ambition at Cana and Capernaum. The explicit Eve–Mary parallel is confined to this Nativity homily, which is worth stating plainly: this is not a note he sounds often.
  • On the homily’s authorship. The sermon is transmitted among Chrysostom’s works and preached in his manner, but it is a different piece from his securely-attested Christmas sermon On the Day of the Birth of Our Saviour (which argues the December 25 date and lacks this passage), and the authenticity of the several Nativity homilies under his name is debated. The New-Eve typology quoted here is genuine and verbatim as transmitted; the open question is only whether Chrysostom himself or a preacher of his school and century composed it. Either way it is a Greek witness of the late fourth or early fifth century in the Antiochene tradition.
  • The form is the Greek “word/hearing” antithesis — the serpent’s word to Eve answered by Gabriel’s word to Mary — that runs from Justin and Irenaeus through Cyril of Jerusalem and on to Proclus, here joined to the tree/Cross pairing that Ambrose was drawing in Latin at the same date.
