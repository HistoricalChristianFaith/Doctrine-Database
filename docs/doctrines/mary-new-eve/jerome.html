Jerome on Mary as the New Eve

Dates: c. 384 (the antithesis is coined in Letter 22 to Eustochium, Jerome’s great treatise-letter on virginity, written at Rome in 384; Jerome c. 347–420) · Wikipedia
Summary: Jerome gives the Eve–Mary antithesis its terse, quotable Latin form — mors per Evam, vita per Mariam, “death came through Eve, but life has come through Mary” — the very phrase the Second Vatican Council would later cite as a saying of the fathers. He deploys it not as detached devotion but inside an exhortation to consecrated virginity: because life came into the world through the Virgin Mary, the gift of virginity is now bestowed most richly upon women, “seeing that it has had its beginning from a woman.” He ties the reversal to the older virgin-Eve motif — Eve was a virgin in paradise and married only after the fall — that Justin, Tertullian, and Epiphanius also use.

Claims & evidence

“Death came through Eve, but life has come through Mary”

But now that a virgin has conceived in the womb and has borne to us a child of which the prophet says that “Government shall be upon his shoulder, and his name shall be called the mighty God, the everlasting Father,” now the chain of the curse is broken. Death came through Eve, but life has come through Mary. And thus the gift of virginity has been bestowed most richly upon women, seeing that it has had its beginning from a woman.

— Letter 22 (to Eustochium), §21, trans. W. H. Fremantle, source. The Latin tag — mors per Evam, vita per Mariam — is Jerome’s own coinage and the sharpest Western form of the antithesis; it is this saying that Lumen Gentium 56 attributes to “the holy Fathers” (“death through Eve, life through Mary”). Jerome frames it Christologically — the curse is undone because the Virgin bore “the mighty God” — and turns it at once to his ascetical purpose: the Incarnation through a woman is what has now exalted virginity among women.

Eve a virgin in paradise, wedded only after the fall

In paradise Eve was a virgin, and it was only after the coats of skins that she began her married life.

— Letter 22 (to Eustochium), §19, trans. W. H. Fremantle, source. Here Jerome joins the Eve–Mary reversal to the older virgin-Eve motif shared by Justin, Tertullian, and Epiphanius: because Eve fell while still a virgin (marrying only after being clothed in the “coats of skins” of Genesis 3:21), the disobedient virgin is properly answered by the obedient Virgin. Virginity, for Jerome, is the paradisal state to which Mary restores women — which is why the antithesis serves his exhortation to Eustochium to keep her virginity.

Notes

  • Letter 22 is an exhortation to consecrated virginity, so Jerome deploys the Eve→Mary reversal to exalt virginity rather than to expound Mariology for its own sake: the point of “life through Mary” is that “the gift of virginity has been bestowed most richly upon women.” The typology is, as with the earlier fathers, a Christological corollary put to a further use — here ascetical rather than anti-heretical.
  • Jerome supplies the Latin phrase the magisterium later canonized, yet on the related Genesis 3:15 question he cuts against the maximal Marian reading: as the Vulgate’s own translator he glossed the serpent-crusher as masculine (“he will crush”) and judged the Hebrew required it — see the Genesis 3:15 “ipsa” argument.
  • Jerome appears on this wiki also as a witness on the perpetual virginity of Mary (his Against Helvidius, c. 383), among other timelines.
