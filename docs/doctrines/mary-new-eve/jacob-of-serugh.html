Jacob of Serugh on Mary as the New Eve

Dates: c. 500 (Jacob of Serugh — Mar Jacob, c. 451–521 — bishop of Batnae in the district of Serugh, east of Edessa; the greatest Syriac poet-theologian after Ephraem, remembered as “the Flute of the Holy Spirit.” His verse homilies [mêmrê] on the Mother of God belong to a long preaching career and cannot be dated closely, so they are anchored to c. 500 for the timeline) · Wikipedia
Summary: A century and a half after Ephraem, Jacob carries the Syriac Eve–Mary antithesis into the sixth century in the same sung, typological key. In his homilies On the Mother of God he sets Eve’s ear, bent to the serpent’s hiss, against Mary’s ear, into which the angel instills salvation, and pictures the Spirit restoring Mary to the very state of Eve before the fall — the New-Eve reading developed east of the Greek and Latin fathers, with the “conception through the ear” motif at its centre.

Claims & evidence

Eve’s ear bent to the serpent, Mary’s ear filled with salvation

See how Eve’s ear inclines and hearkens to the voice of the deceiver when he hisses deceit to her. But come and see the Watcher instilling salvation into Mary’s ear and removing the insinuation of the serpent from her and consoling her.

— On the Mother of God, first homily, trans. Mary Hansbury, pp. 29–30.1 The antithesis turns on the ear: as the serpent’s word entered the virgin Eve through hearing and bore disobedience, so the angel’s word enters the virgin Mary through hearing and bears salvation — the conceptio per aurem that Tertullian (“the ensnaring word… crept into the ear”) and Proclus (“through ears that obeyed, the Word entered”) had already made a fixture of the reading, here in its Syriac voice. Jacob does not merely juxtapose the two women; he has the same organ that betrayed the first Eve become the door of rescue for the second.

The Spirit restores Mary to Eve’s state before the fall

He bestowed on her that first grace which her mother had, until she ate from the tree which was full of death. The Spirit who came made her like Eve of old, though she did not hear the counsel of the serpent nor his hateful speech. In that condition where Eve and Adam were placed, before they sinned, He placed her and then descended in her.

— On the Mother of God, first homily, trans. Mary Hansbury, p. 36.2 Where Justin and Irenaeus match the two women by their choices — Eve’s unbelief against Mary’s faith — Jacob’s Syriac development leans further, toward Mary’s restoration to the unfallen purity Eve once held: the Spirit gives her back “that first grace which her mother had” and sets her in the garden-state “before they sinned.” Elsewhere in the same homily he says the Spirit came “to let loose from her the former sentence of Eve and Adam” and “freed her from that curse of sufferings on account of Eve, her mother.” This is the New-Eve typology tilting toward the later theme of Mary’s sinlessness — Eve before the fall as the measure of the second Eve — the same bridge Ephraem is read to open (see the Immaculate Conception).

Notes

  • Jacob extends the Syriac strand begun by Ephraem (c. 370): both sing the Eve–Mary antithesis rather than argue it, and both make Eve’s daughter the one through whom Eve’s loss is repaired. Jacob’s distinctive stress is the ear — the organ of Eve’s undoing turned into the organ of Mary’s conceiving — a motif he shares with the Greek fathers of the Ephesus era and presses harder than Ephraem does.
  • The Marian homilies survive in Syriac (ed. P. Bedjan) and are given here from Mary Hansbury’s English translation, Jacob of Serug: On the Mother of God (Crestwood, NY: St Vladimir’s Seminary Press, 1998), with an introduction by Sebastian Brock — the standard English edition of the four homilies.
