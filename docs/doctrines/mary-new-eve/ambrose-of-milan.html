Ambrose of Milan on Mary as the New Eve

Dates: c. 389 (Ambrose, bishop of Milan c. 374–397; the fullest Eve–Mary statement is in his Exposition of the Gospel of Luke, worked up from homilies and completed c. 385–389, with related passages in a letter of c. 396 and the treatise On the Instruction of a Virgin of c. 391–392) · Wikipedia
Summary: Ambrose gives the typology its influential Latin form. Reading Christ’s temptation against Adam’s fall, he sets “the virgin earth” from which Adam was formed beside the Virgin from whom Christ is born, and coins the antithesis Latin preaching would repeat: “Through a woman came folly, through a virgin came wisdom.” Where the fall expelled our flesh from paradise, a Virgin joins it again to God; and he can summon Eve herself to be renewed in Mary.

Claims & evidence

The virgin earth and the Virgin: “through a woman came folly, through a virgin came wisdom”

From the virgin earth Adam, Christ from the virgin: the former made in the image of God, the latter the image of God: the former placed above all irrational animals, the latter preceded by all living creatures. Through a woman came folly, through a virgin came wisdom. Death came through a tree, life through a cross.

— Exposition of the Gospel of Luke, IV.7, source. The parallelism is Pauline at root — the second Adam undoing the first — but Ambrose runs the “woman / virgin” and “tree / cross” couplets alongside it, so that Eve and Mary become the two poles of the reversal. His terse Latin (per mulierem stultitia, per virginem sapientia) proved memorable and shaped later Western statements of the parallel.

Our flesh, cast out of paradise by man and woman, is joined to God through a Virgin

A Virgin conceived the salvation of the world, a Virgin brought forth the life of all… Further, our flesh was cast out of paradise by a man and woman and was joined to God through a Virgin.

— Letter 63 (to the Church at Vercellae), §33, trans. H. de Romestin, source. The fall was worked “by a man and woman” (Adam and Eve); the restoration comes “through a Virgin.” Ambrose keeps the reversal Christological — it is our flesh that is rejoined to God in the Incarnation — while giving the Virgin the pivotal place opposite Eve.

“Come, therefore, Eve, now Mary”

Come, therefore, Eve, now Mary, who brought us not only the incentive of virginity but also God.

— On the Instruction of a Virgin, ch. V, source. In a rhetorical crescendo Ambrose bids Eve be “now” sober, “now” Sarah, “now” Mary — Eve is not discarded but taken up and rehabilitated in the second Eve, so that the woman once excluded from paradise is “taken up into heaven.”

Notes

  • Ambrose is the hinge by which the Greek-and-African typology of Justin, Irenaeus, and Tertullian passes into the Latin West, where his pupil Augustine and later medieval writers develop it. His own Genesis commentary, by contrast, reads the enmity of Genesis 3:15 soberly as between the serpent and the woman with her seed, without the later Latin identification of the woman there as Mary.
  • Ambrose’s abiding concern in these works is Mary’s virginity; the New-Eve reading serves that argument, and he appears on this wiki also on the perpetual virginity of Mary.
