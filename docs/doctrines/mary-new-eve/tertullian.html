Tertullian on Mary as the New Eve

Dates: c. 210 (the North-African advocate flourished c. 197–220; On the Flesh of Christ is placed c. 206–212) · Wikipedia
Summary: Arguing against Marcion that Christ took real human flesh from the Virgin, Tertullian restates the Eve–Mary antithesis as a “contrary operation”: the deceiving word entered the ear of the virgin Eve to build “the edifice of death,” and so the Word of God must enter a virgin’s soul to raise “the fabric of life” — “that what had been reduced to ruin by this sex might by the selfsame sex be recovered to salvation.”

Claims & evidence

By a contrary operation the ruin wrought through the virgin Eve is recovered through the Virgin Mary

And even reason here maintains the same conclusion, because it was by just the contrary operation that God recovered His own image and likeness, of which He had been robbed by the devil. For it was while Eve was yet a virgin, that the ensnaring word had crept into her ear which was to build the edifice of death. Into a virgin’s soul, in like manner, must be introduced that Word of God which was to raise the fabric of life; so that what had been reduced to ruin by this sex, might by the selfsame sex be recovered to salvation. As Eve had believed the serpent, so Mary believed the angel. The delinquency which the one occasioned by believing, the other by believing effaced.

— On the Flesh of Christ, ch. 17, source. Tertullian’s formula — “As Eve had believed the serpent, so Mary believed the angel” — is the tersest patristic statement of the parallel, and he presses it to the polemical point at hand: the reversal only works if Mary’s flesh (and so her Son’s) is genuinely human, against Marcion’s phantom-body Christ. The typology thus serves his argument for the reality of the Incarnation.

Notes

  • Tertullian stands third in the second-century spine of the New-Eve reading, after Justin Martyr and Irenaeus, and shares their “word into the ear / assent of the will” structure. His contribution is to tie the typology explicitly to the recovery of the image and likeness of God lost in the fall.
  • On the separate biographical question — whether Mary bore other children after Jesus — Tertullian is the father usually reckoned the earliest Helvidian witness; that dispute is traced on the perpetual virginity of Mary and does not bear on his New-Eve typology, which turns only on Mary’s believing assent at the Annunciation.
