The Akathist Hymn on Mary as the New Eve

Dates: c. 500 (an anonymous Greek kontakion; the date is genuinely disputed — scholars have placed the hymn anywhere from the first half of the fifth century to the sixth, and it has been ascribed, without proof, to Romanos the Melodist) · Wikipedia
Summary: The New-Eve typology the earlier fathers built in prose passes here into the Church’s worship. The Akathist Hymn to the Theotokos — still sung in the Byzantine rite through Lent — enshrines the Eve–Mary reversal in a salutation the whole Greek Church prays: greeting the Virgin at the Annunciation, the archangel hails her as “the recall of fallen Adam… the redemption of the tears of Eve.” It does not argue the typology; it presupposes it, evidence that by this date the reading had become devotional common property rather than a theologian’s thesis.

Claims & evidence

The Virgin reverses the fall — Adam recalled, Eve’s tears redeemed

In the first oikos (stanza) of the hymn the archangel Gabriel, sent to greet the Theotokos, addresses her with a chain of salutations each opening “Hail” (or “Rejoice”; Greek Chaire). Among the first of them the fall is undone member by member — the curse annulled, Adam recalled, Eve’s tears redeemed:

Hail, O you through whom joy will shine forth; Hail, O you through whom the curse will disappear! … Hail, O Restoration of the fallen Adam; Hail, O Redemption of the tears of Eve!

— The Akathist Hymn to the Most Holy Theotokos, Ikos 1, source.1 The Greek makes the Eve wordplay plain:

… Χαῖρε, δι᾿ ἧς ἡ χαρὰ ἐκλάμψει· χαῖρε, δι᾿ ἧς ἡ ἀρὰ ἐκλείψει. Χαῖρε, τοῦ πεσόντος Ἀδὰμ ἡ ἀνάκλησις· χαῖρε, τῶν δακρύων τῆς Εὔας ἡ λύτρωσις.

— the original Greek of Ikos 1.2 The paired antitheses are the very ones the fathers had drawn: the curse (ara, Genesis 3) undone by the Virgin through whom joy (chara) dawns; the fallen Adam “recalled” (anaklesis) as Christ the new Adam takes flesh in her; and Eve, whose sentence was sorrow and tears (Genesis 3:16), “redeemed” (lytrosis) in the second Eve. The hymn nowhere reasons to the parallel — it sings it as settled praise, the typology already assumed as the reader’s shared inheritance.

Notes

  • A liturgical, not an argumentative, witness. Unlike Justin, Irenaeus, or Tertullian — who forge the Eve–Mary parallel inside polemic for a real Incarnation — the Akathist simply presupposes the developed typology and hymns it. That is precisely its evidential value: the reading has passed from the fathers’ prose into the Church’s common prayer, sung antiphonally by a whole congregation. What the theologians argued, the worshippers now confess as doxology.
  • Dating — hedged. The Akathist is anonymous, and its date is genuinely contested. The older consensus, following Egon Wellesz, placed it in the sixth or seventh century, and a persistent tradition credits Romanos the Melodist (fl. c. 540) — but the attribution is undemonstrated. More recent work by Leena Mari Peltomaa dates the hymn to the first half of the fifth century, arguing that its Christology reflects the Council of Ephesus (431) and shows no trace of Chalcedon (451); Mary Cunningham accepts the theology but, weighing the hymn’s highly developed poetic form, leans to the late fifth or early sixth century. The c. 500 placed here is a deliberate midpoint across that range.3
  • The proem is later. The famous prologue “To thee, the Champion Leader” (Têi Hypermachôi), which hails Mary as defender of the city, is a separate and later addition, traditionally tied to the deliverance of Constantinople in the siege of 626 (and ascribed to Patriarch Sergius) — not part of the original hymn body that carries the Eve typology quoted above.
  • The reversal sung here is the same one Proclus of Constantinople preached in the Theotokos debate a generation before, and which John of Damascus would fix into formula in the eighth century — the typology moving, across these witnesses, from sermon to hymn to settled dogma.
