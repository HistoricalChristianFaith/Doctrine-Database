The Place of Christian Worship — house churches to church buildings

"Why aren't we breaking bread in homes anymore — why is it only in church?" The earliest Christians had no buildings set apart for worship: they met in private houses, and the New Testament greets "the church that is in their house."1 Only from roughly the close of the second century do dedicated meeting-places appear, and after the Empire made peace with the Church in the fourth century these grew into the spacious basilicas that are the ancestors of the modern church building. This page traces that development — and weighs the question it raises: was the early absence of church buildings a principle of the faith (a conviction that worship belongs in homes, or that God is not to be housed in sacred buildings at all), or a necessity of the Christians' position as an illegal and persecuted body?

The Scriptural and historical backdrop

Three strands of evidence frame the question:

  • The house churches of the New Testament — Christians assembling in private homes: "the church that is in their house" (Romans 16:5; 1 Corinthians 16:19; Colossians 4:15; Philemon 2), and the believers "breaking bread from house to house" (Acts 2:46).1
  • The de-sacralization of place — the theme that God is not confined to buildings: "the Most High dwelleth not in temples made with hands" (Acts 7:48; 17:24), and the hour coming when true worshippers will worship "neither in this mountain, nor yet at Jerusalem … but … in spirit and in truth" (John 4:21–24); the apologists turned this against the pagan taunt that Christians had "no temples, no altars."2
  • The material record — no securely identified building set apart for Christian worship before the early-to-mid third century (the converted house-church at Dura-Europos, c. 233, is the earliest physically attested), followed by the rapid raising of "spacious" purpose-built churches once the persecutions relented, and the great basilicas after Constantine.3

Whether these strands add up to a positive house-church principle or only to the circumstances of an unrecognized cult is the disputed question, weighed on its own page in the Arguments below.

Justin Martyr (c. 165)

Tried at Rome under the prefect Rusticus and asked point-blank where the Christians gathered, Justin first denied the premise of a single cult-site — "the God of the Christians is not circumscribed by place" — and then, pressed for a location, could name only the private lodging he stayed in: "I live above one Martinus, at the Timiotinian Bath … I am unaware of any other meeting than his" (Acts of Justin 2).4 A generation before the first physically attested church buildings, the church in the imperial capital is still meeting in a private house — the clearest narrative datum behind the "necessity, not principle" question weighed below.

Arguments

The cross-cutting dispute adduced for and against a house-church "principle," weighed on its own page.

Was the early church's lack of dedicated buildings a principle of the faith, or a necessity of their position? (assessment: sound)

J. B. Lightfoot's "necessity, not principle" thesis — that the first Christians built no houses of worship not because their faith forbade it but because, as an unrecognized corporation, they could hold no corporate property and prudence deterred any display that "might arouse the fury of the populace." The reading holds well: Roman law and the persecutions account for the absence, and the moment the Church was tolerated it acquired and built dedicated churches with no recorded objection of principle — which tells against the popular claim that the New Testament mandates meeting in homes. What needs flagging is only the overstatement that the absence was purely circumstantial: a genuine theological strand (God "dwelleth not in temples made with hands") did color it, though that strand targets pagan temple-cult and the localizing of God, not the existence of an assembly building.5
