The Place of Christian Worship — house churches to church buildings

"Why aren't we breaking bread in homes anymore — why is it only in church?" The earliest Christians had no buildings set apart for worship: they met in private houses, and the New Testament greets "the church that is in their house."1 Only from roughly the close of the second century do dedicated meeting-places appear, and after the Empire made peace with the Church in the fourth century these grew into the spacious basilicas that are the ancestors of the modern church building. This page traces that development — and weighs the question it raises: was the early absence of church buildings a principle of the faith (a conviction that worship belongs in homes, or that God is not to be housed in sacred buildings at all), or a necessity of the Christians' position as an illegal and persecuted body?

The Scriptural and historical backdrop

Three strands of evidence frame the question:

  • The house churches of the New Testament — Christians assembling in private homes: "the church that is in their house" (Romans 16:5; 1 Corinthians 16:19; Colossians 4:15; Philemon 2), and the believers "breaking bread from house to house" (Acts 2:46).1
  • The de-sacralization of place — the theme that God is not confined to buildings: "the Most High dwelleth not in temples made with hands" (Acts 7:48; 17:24), and the hour coming when true worshippers will worship "neither in this mountain, nor yet at Jerusalem … but … in spirit and in truth" (John 4:21–24); the apologists turned this against the pagan taunt that Christians had "no temples, no altars."2
  • The material record — no securely identified building set apart for Christian worship before the early-to-mid third century (the converted house-church at Dura-Europos, c. 233, is the earliest physically attested), followed by the rapid raising of "spacious" purpose-built churches once the persecutions relented, and the great basilicas after Constantine.3

Whether these strands add up to a positive house-church principle or only to the circumstances of an unrecognized cult is the disputed question, weighed on its own page in the Arguments below.

Justin Martyr (c. 165)

Tried at Rome under the prefect Rusticus and asked point-blank where the Christians gathered, Justin first denied the premise of a single cult-site — "the God of the Christians is not circumscribed by place" — and then, pressed for a location, could name only the private lodging he stayed in: "I live above one Martinus, at the Timiotinian Bath … I am unaware of any other meeting than his" (Acts of Justin 2).4 A generation before the first physically attested church buildings, the church in the imperial capital is still meeting in a private house — the clearest narrative datum behind the "necessity, not principle" question weighed below.

The church at Edessa (c. 202)

The earliest literary notice of a building set apart for Christian worship. Cataloguing the edifices swept away when the river Daysan inundated Edessa in the reign of Abgar the Great, the Chronicle of Edessa names among them "the temple of the church of the Christians" — a building of some standing, itemized alongside the royal palace.5 Edessa lay beyond the Roman frontier under kings who had early embraced Christianity, so the legal restraints that kept Christians inside the Empire from owning corporate property did not apply: a church building appears first exactly where the constraints are absent — the load-bearing point of the "necessity, not principle" thesis weighed below.

The Dura-Europos house-church (c. 233)

The earliest physically surviving Christian building: an ordinary courtyard house in the Roman garrison town of Dura-Europos, on the Euphrates, that a Christian community remodeled into a dedicated meeting-house — knocking two rooms into a single assembly hall and converting another into a frescoed baptistery — while the faith was still illegal. It was buried, and so preserved, when the city wall was packed with an earthen rampart during the Sasanian siege of 256.6 A converted, unobtrusive house on the Empire's edge, put to dedicated use the moment the community had the means, it is the standard archaeological anchor for the move from house church to domus ecclesiae that this page traces.

The rescript of Gallienus (c. 260)

The first imperial act to recognize the Christian body's right to its own places of worship. Ending his father Valerian's persecution, Gallienus addressed a rescript to the bishops — preserved by Eusebius under the heading "The Peace Under Gallienus" — directing that their "places of religious worship" be restored to them, with a further decree returning the Christians' cemeteries.7 The order to restore these places presupposes that the Church already held recognized meeting-places and burial grounds, confiscable in persecution; with the legal disability lifted, Lightfoot notes, "all obstacles to the multiplication of churches were at length removed" — the documented hinge of the "necessity, not principle" question weighed below.

The Edict of Milan (313)

The settlement of Constantine and Licinius that ended the persecutions for good. Beyond granting universal religious liberty, the edict — preserved in Latin by Lactantius and in Greek by Eusebius — twice orders the restitution, without charge, of confiscated Christian property: first the places "appropriated for their religious assemblies," then everything belonging "to their society in general, that is, to their churches." Licinius then "exhorted the Christians to rebuild their religious edifices."8 The clauses restore property the Church already owned and lost under Diocletian (303); once the disability was gone the churches multiplied, with no recorded objection that dedicated buildings betrayed a principle — the building boom tracking legalization, not a doctrinal shift.

Arguments

The cross-cutting dispute adduced for and against a house-church "principle," weighed on its own page.

Was the early church's lack of dedicated buildings a principle of the faith, or a necessity of their position? (assessment: sound)

J. B. Lightfoot's "necessity, not principle" thesis — that the first Christians built no houses of worship not because their faith forbade it but because, as an unrecognized corporation, they could hold no corporate property and prudence deterred any display that "might arouse the fury of the populace." The reading holds well: Roman law and the persecutions account for the absence, and the moment the Church was tolerated it acquired and built dedicated churches with no recorded objection of principle — which tells against the popular claim that the New Testament mandates meeting in homes. What needs flagging is only the overstatement that the absence was purely circumstantial: a genuine theological strand (God "dwelleth not in temples made with hands") did color it, though that strand targets pagan temple-cult and the localizing of God, not the existence of an assembly building.9
