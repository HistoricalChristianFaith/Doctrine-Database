The Ministerial Priesthood — why only ordained ministers confect the Eucharist

"Why can the Eucharist only be given by priests?" The historic answer turns on a chain of convictions: that the Eucharist is not merely a fellowship meal but a true offering — the "pure offering" the prophet Malachi foresaw being made "in every place" among the nations; that Christ instituted it at the Last Supper and there charged the apostles, "this do in remembrance of me"; and that what he committed to the apostles passed to those they ordained — bishops and presbyters — so that the offering of the Eucharist belongs to the ordained ministry and not to every believer at large. This page traces how that conviction was held and expressed across time, earliest voice first, and weighs the disputes it raises.

Related doctrines: The Eucharist and the ministry — see also the Real Presence in the Eucharist

The Scriptural texts

The doctrine is built from four strands of biblical text:

  • The institution at the Last Supper — "when the hour was come, he sat down, and the twelve apostles with him … And he took bread, and gave thanks, and brake it, and gave unto them, saying, This is my body which is given for you: this do in remembrance of me" (Luke 22:14–19; cf. Matthew 26:26–28; Mark 14:22–24). The command "this do" is read as addressed to the apostles present.1
  • The commission and delegated authority — the risen Christ's "as my Father hath sent me, even so send I you … Receive ye the Holy Ghost: Whose soever sins ye remit, they are remitted unto them" (John 20:21–23), and the charge to the seventy, "He that heareth you heareth me; and he that despiseth you despiseth him that sent me" (Luke 10:16; cf. Luke 10:1) — read as Christ committing his own authority to chosen men.2
  • The prophesied pure offering — "from the rising of the sun even unto the going down of the same my name shall be great among the Gentiles; and in every place incense shall be offered unto my name, and a pure offering" (Malachi 1:10–11), read from the Didache onward as the New-Covenant sacrifice the Eucharist fulfils.3

Whether these texts establish a distinct ordained priesthood that alone may offer the Eucharist, or whether the New Testament's "priesthood of all believers" leaves the act to the whole body, is the central dispute; it is weighed on its own page in the Arguments below.

Timeline

The witnesses are listed earliest first. This timeline is in its early stages and will be filled out as the doctrine's development across the centuries is traced.

Clement of Rome (c. 96)

Writing from Rome to quell a revolt at Corinth that had ejected its presbyters, Clement gives the earliest explicit witness that the church's offering belongs to a divinely ordered, appointed ministry rather than to every member at large. God himself, he argues, "has fixed by His own supreme will" both "where and by whom" the offerings are to be presented and at what times; as the old covenant assigned distinct ministrations to high priest, priests, and Levites while "the layman is bound by the laws that pertain to laymen," so each Christian must keep "the rule of the ministry prescribed to him" and not go beyond it (1 Clement 40–41).4 He traces the ministry's authority through an unbroken chain — God sent Christ, Christ the apostles, and the apostles, "preaching through countries and cities, appointed the first-fruits… to be bishops and deacons of those who should afterwards believe" (1 Clement 42).5 Foreseeing "strife on account of the office of the episcopate," the apostles further "gave instructions, that when these should fall asleep, other approved men should succeed them in their ministry" — so the deposed Corinthian presbyters, standing in that succession, "cannot be justly dismissed from the ministry" (1 Clement 44).6 Clement does not yet name the Christian minister a "priest" or expound the Eucharist's content — the Levitical priesthood is his analogy, and he uses "bishop" and "presbyter" interchangeably, attesting a two-order ministry rather than the later monarchical episcopate.7 What he supplies is the structural plank the doctrine rests on: an appointed, succeeding order that alone presents the offerings, with the layman expressly outside that station.

The Didache (c. 100)

The earliest church-order manual gives the first post-apostolic text to call the Sunday Eucharist a sacrifice and to read it as the fulfilment of Malachi's "pure offering." The assembly gathers "every Lord's day" to "break bread and give thanksgiving," confessing its transgressions and being reconciled first, "that your sacrifice may be pure… For this is that which was spoken by the Lord: In every place and time offer to me a pure sacrifice; for I am a great King, saith the Lord, and my name is wonderful among the nations" (Didache 14).8 The very next chapter directs the church to "appoint… bishops and deacons worthy of the Lord… for they also render you the service of the prophets and teachers" (Didache 15) — setting the appointed ministry beside the offering, though without yet saying the offering belongs to them alone.9

Ignatius of Antioch (c. 108)

Writing to the churches of Asia Minor on his way to martyrdom at Rome, Ignatius gives the earliest explicit rule that a Eucharist is valid only when the bishop, or one he authorizes, presides. Where Clement grounded an ordered, succeeding ministry and the Didache named the offering a sacrifice, Ignatius fastens the two together: "Let that be deemed a proper Eucharist, which is administered either by the bishop, or by one to whom he has entrusted it… It is not lawful without the bishop either to baptize or to celebrate a love-feast" (Smyrnaeans 8).10 The congregation is to keep "but one Eucharist… one altar; as there is one bishop, along with the presbytery and deacons" (Philadelphians 4), so that the one offering answers to the one ordained ministry.11 Unlike Clement, Ignatius presupposes the three-order, monarchical episcopate — one bishop over a presbytery and deacons in each city — and it is on that single bishop that he hangs the Eucharist's validity. This is the most direct ante-Nicene statement that the offering belongs to the ordained ministry and not to the body at large; the clause "or by one to whom he has entrusted it" already supplies the principle of delegation on which presbyteral celebration rests.

Justin Martyr (c. 155)

Justin gives the earliest detailed description of the rite in practice, and in it the offering is plainly the act of a single presiding minister, not of the congregation at large. At the Sunday assembly the people's gifts of bread and "a cup of wine mixed with water" are "brought to the president of the brethren," who "offers prayers and thanksgivings, according to his ability," the people answering only "Amen"; then "those who are called by us deacons" distribute the elements "and to those who are absent a portion is sent" (First Apology 65, 67).12 The food so blessed is "not common bread and common drink" but "the flesh and blood of that Jesus who was made flesh," and no one is "allowed to partake" but the baptized believer in right standing (First Apology 66) — a realism and a reserve that together explain why its confecting is guarded.13 In the Dialogue with Trypho he reads Malachi's "pure offering… in every place" as fulfilled "in the Eucharist of the bread and the cup… presented by Christians in all places throughout the world," against the rejected sacrifices of the Jewish priests (Dialogue 117).14 Justin never calls the president a "priest" or "bishop" — that identification is drawn from the threefold ministry Ignatius already attests, not from this text — and he can equally call the whole believing people "the true high priestly race of God" (Dialogue 116); what is not in doubt is that the eucharistic offering, in each assembly, is made through one who presides.

Irenaeus of Lyons (c. 189)

Writing against the Gnostics, Irenaeus joins the two convictions the doctrine rests on. He names the Eucharist "the new oblation of the new covenant" — the "pure sacrifice" Malachi foretold — "which the Church receiving from the apostles, offers to God throughout all the world," and presses that "the class of oblations in general has not been set aside… sacrifices there are, too, in the Church: but the species alone has been changed, inasmuch as the offering is now made, not by slaves, but by freemen" (Against Heresies IV.17–18).15 And he makes apostolic succession the public test of an authentic ministry: one can "reckon up those who were by the apostles instituted bishops in the Churches, and [to demonstrate] the succession of these men to our own times" (III.3), so that "it is incumbent to obey the presbyters… those who… possess the succession from the apostles… [and] have received the certain gift of truth" (IV.26).16 Where Clement of Rome had asserted the apostles' orderly appointment of successors, Irenaeus turns that succession into an explicit rule of faith and binds the Church's eucharistic offering to it. Yet, like Justin before him and Tertullian after, he can still call "all the righteous" and "all the apostles of the Lord" priests (IV.8.3) — affirming the reserved offering and the corporate priesthood together, without yet marking off a distinct sacrificing hiereus-class.17

Tertullian (c. 208)

Tertullian gives the earliest Latin statement of a settled distinction between the ordained "Order" (ordo) and the laity — and, in the same breath, its sharpest early qualification. Arguing that clergy must be men of one marriage, he anticipates the objection that what is barred to priests is open to laymen: "Are not even we laics priests?… It is the authority of the Church, and the honour which has acquired sanctity through the joint session of the Order, which has established the difference between the Order and the laity. Accordingly, where there is no joint session of the ecclesiastical Order, you offer, and baptize, and are priest, alone for yourself. But where three are, a church is, albeit they be laics" (On Exhortation to Chastity 7).18 In On Baptism he ranks the right to confer the sacrament: "the chief priest (who is the bishop) has the right: in the next place, the presbyters and deacons, yet not without the bishop's authority… Beside these, even laymen have the right" — but only in necessity, and never to usurp "the specific function of the bishop," for "emulation of the episcopal office is the mother of schisms" (On Baptism 17).19 Tertullian thus supplies Latin theology its very word for the clergy — ordo — and the earliest Western account of the reserved ministry as the Church's settled discipline; yet he grounds the distinction in the Church's authority and honour rather than a separate consecration, and concedes both the priesthood of all believers and the layman's emergency right to offer and baptize. He is a doubled witness, cited on both sides of the framing question in the Arguments.

Hippolytus of Rome (c. 215)

The Apostolic Tradition preserves the earliest surviving ordination prayers, and they grade the clergy precisely by their relation to the offering. The bishop is consecrated in openly high-priestly terms — God is asked to grant him "to serve as thy high priest without blame… to offer thee the gifts of thy holy church," and "by the Spirit of high-priesthood to have authority to remit sins" (Apostolic Tradition 3).20 The deacon, by contrast, "is not ordained to the priesthood, but to serve the bishop… He does not receive that Spirit that is possessed by the presbytery, in which the presbyters share"; presbyters join in laying hands on a new presbyter "because of the common and like Spirit of the clergy," while the deacon is ordained by the bishop alone (Apostolic Tradition 8–9).21 Where Ignatius had asserted that the valid Eucharist hangs on the bishop, here the Church's own liturgy confers the offering-power by ordination in sacerdotal words — bishop and presbyter share the priestly Spirit; the deacon, ordained only to service, does not. (The church-order is traditionally ascribed to Hippolytus and dated c. 215; modern scholarship treats it as a layered compilation of uncertain authorship, while agreeing its ordination material is very early.)

Cyprian of Carthage (c. 253)

Cyprian gives the most explicit ante-Nicene statement that the eucharistic minister acts in the place of Christ. Correcting those who offered water alone in the cup, he reasons that since Christ "is Himself the chief priest of God the Father, and has first offered Himself a sacrifice… and has commanded this to be done in commemoration of Himself, certainly that priest truly discharges the office of Christ, who imitates that which Christ did; and he then offers a true and full sacrifice in the Church to God the Father" (Epistle 62, to Caecilius).22 The rite's form is "divinely appointed" and may not be "changed by human tradition into anything else," for "we may not break even the least of the Lord's commandments" — so the priest's office is to reproduce, not recompose, what Christ did.23 Where Ignatius had hung the valid Eucharist on the bishop's authority, Cyprian supplies the rationale: the celebrant offers because he "discharges the office of Christ," standing in the place of the one high priest. He draws the ecclesial corollary in On the Unity of the Church: because "the flesh of Christ… cannot be sent abroad, nor is there any other home to believers but the one Church," there is one Eucharist within the one episcopal communion and none in schism.24 This is the bridge from Ignatius's bishop-bound Eucharist to the developed Latin doctrine of the priest who offers in persona Christi.

The First Council of Nicaea (325)

The first ecumenical council gives the earliest conciliar witness that the offering of the Eucharist is reserved to the higher orders. Canon 18 corrects districts where deacons had been administering the Eucharist to the presbyters, and even handling it before the bishops: "neither canon nor custom permits that they who have no right to offer should give the Body of Christ to them that do offer." The deacons are to "remain within their own bounds, knowing that they are the ministers of the bishop and the inferiors of the presbyters," receiving the Eucharist after them, so that "either the bishop or the presbyter administer to them" (Canon 18).25 The canon does not argue the reservation but presupposes it as already fixed "by canon" and "by custom" — distinguishing the act of offering (the bishop's and presbyter's right) from distributing (the deacon's subordinate role), the very line Justin had drawn between the "president" who offers and the deacons who distribute. That an ecumenical council treats a deacon's offering as a disciplinary scandal, correctable by deposition, attests that by AD 325 the conviction of Ignatius, Justin, and Cyprian was the settled, universal order of the church.

Aerius of Sebaste (c. 355)

Aerius gives the timeline its earliest explicit denial that bishop outranks presbyter — the position Jerome would argue more moderately a generation later, here pushed to the point of schism. A presbyter of Sebaste passed over for its bishopric, Aerius held that "a bishop is no different from a presbyter… one order, and one honor and one rank," reciting the functions the two share: "A bishop lays on hands, but so does a presbyter… The bishop performs the eucharistic liturgy, the presbyter likewise" (Panarion 75.3, as Epiphanius quotes him to refute him).26 Tellingly for this doctrine, every function he lists belongs to presbyter and bishop alike — none to the layman: in the same breath that levels the two clerical ranks he takes for granted that the offering is the act of an ordained minister. Aerius levels the ranks within the ministry; he does not hand the Eucharist to the people. His quarrel is with the gradation of the clergy, not the reservation of the offering to it — the same seam Jerome would later cut along. Epiphanius answers by making ordination the bishop's distinguishing power (the episcopate alone "generates fathers… since he is not ordained for the purpose of ordaining, how could a presbyter consecrate a bishop?") — the very hinge Jerome grants from the other side, that ordination is the one function reserved to a bishop. A dissenting voice, catalogued as the seventy-fifth heresy and bracketed by the settled practice he disputes.27

Ambrose of Milan (c. 390)

Ambrose gives the developed Latin account of why the offering belongs to the priest: because the consecration is effected by Christ's own words of institution, and it is the priest who speaks them over the elements. Catechizing the newly baptized, he points them to the threefold ministry they have just seen — "You saw there the deacon, you saw the priest, you saw the chief priest [i.e. the bishop]" (On the Mysteries 2.6).28 The change, he insists, is by no natural process nor the minister's own authority but by the dominical word: "that sacrament which you receive is made what it is by the word of Christ… Before the blessing of the heavenly words another nature is spoken of, after the consecration the Body is signified" (On the Mysteries 9.52–54); and more pointedly, at the consecration "the priest no longer uses his own words, but he uses the words of Christ. Therefore, the word of Christ completes this sacrament" (On the Sacraments 4).29 Where Cyprian said the priest "discharges the office of Christ," Ambrose locates the act in the priest's speaking Christ's words — the very point Aquinas will systematize as consecration in persona Christi. His change-of-nature language is also a major witness on the Real Presence page; it is cited here for the ministerial point — that the offering is confected by the ordained who speak the dominical word.

John Chrysostom (c. 392)

Chrysostom's Treatise on the Priesthood gives the office its most exalted patristic expression. The priesthood "is indeed discharged on earth, but it ranks amongst heavenly ordinances," for "neither man, nor angel, nor archangel… but the Paraclete Himself, instituted this vocation"; and at the altar "there stands the priest, not bringing down fire from Heaven, but the Holy Spirit," so that grace descends on the sacrifice (On the Priesthood 3.4).30 From this he draws the reservation at full stretch: priests "have received an authority which God has not given to angels or archangels" — to bind and loose, to forgive sins, and to confect the Eucharist — so that "what priests do here below God ratifies above"; and since regeneration and "the flesh of the Lord" are "accomplished only by means of those holy hands, I mean the hands of the priest," salvation itself passes through the ordained ministry (3.5–6).31 Where Cyprian had said the priest "discharges the office of Christ," Chrysostom — freely calling the Christian minister a "priest" (hiereus), which Clement had pointedly declined to do — grounds the office on the Spirit-given powers to offer, absolve, and beget to God. His is the developed Greek form of the conviction, the who-may-offer counterpart to the eucharistic realism of the same treatise weighed on the Real Presence page.

Jerome (c. 393)

Jerome gives the timeline its sharpest ancient qualifier — not a denial of the reserved ministry, but a denial that its later hierarchy is apostolic. Expounding Titus and again in Letter 146 to Evangelus, he presses the New Testament's own interchange of titles: "the presbyter is the same as the bishop," since Paul calls the same men both (Philippians 1:1; Acts 20:28; Titus 1:5–7). The single bishop set over the presbyters is, he argues, a post-apostolic remedy: earlier "the governance of the church was conducted by the joint counsel of the presbyterate," until "one chosen from among the presbyters should be placed in charge of the others… so that the seeds of schism might be rooted out" — bishops are greater than presbyters only "by the custom of the Church," and the one power reserved to a bishop is ordination.32 Yet Jerome is no congregationalist and never touches the doctrine's core: the occasion of Letter 146 is his fury that a deacon — "a mere server of tables" — should exalt himself over the presbyters "through whose prayers the body and blood of Christ are produced," the Eucharist being confected only by ordained hands.33 So where Ignatius and Cyprian read the monarchical episcopate as apostolic, Jerome historicizes it while keeping the reservation of the offering to the ordained — a witness for the ministerial priesthood who prunes one of its later growths.

Augustine of Hippo (c. 400)

Forced by the Donatist controversy to say why a sacrament from an unworthy minister is still valid, Augustine supplies the principle that anchors the reserved offering in the office, not the minister's holiness. The sacrament's whole power is Christ's: "although many ministers, be they righteous or unrighteous, should baptize, the virtue of baptism would be attributed to Him alone… Peter may baptize, but this is He that baptizeth… Judas may baptize, still this is He that baptizeth" (Tractates on John 6).34 From the same controversy he draws the parallel between baptism and orders that seeds the later doctrine of an indelible priestly character: as a validly baptized man who secedes is not rebaptized on return, so those "ordained before their secession, are certainly not ordained again… they retain the sacrament of their ordination; and hence… they are not ranked with the laity" (On Baptism I.1).35 And in the City of God he names the offering the Church's own sacrifice, made through the one High Priest — "the sacrifice which the Church continually celebrates in the sacrament of the altar… in which she teaches that she herself is offered" (X.6).36 Where Cyprian said the priest "discharges the office of Christ," Augustine guards that same conviction against the Donatist inflation of it: because the minister acts in Christ's stead, it is Christ's holiness, not the man's, on which the offering rests, and the power to celebrate abides in the ordained by a mark ordination imprints — the exact point Aquinas will systematize as a power "bestowed upon the priest at his ordination." (His change-of-nature and eucharistic-sacrifice language is also a major witness on the Real Presence page; it is cited here for the ministerial point.)

Leo the Great (c. 450)

Preaching year by year on the anniversary of his consecration, Pope Leo I gathers the tradition into the classic Roman synthesis: the priesthood is a Christ-derived office handed on in perpetuity, not a human inheritance. Reading "Thou art a priest for ever after the order of Melchizedech" of Christ, "in whom was prefigured the eternal High Priest," he presses that "now that the mystery of this Divine priesthood has descended to human agency, it runs not by the line of birth… those men are received by the Church as its rulers whom the Holy Ghost prepares: so that in the people of God's adoption, the whole body of which is priestly and royal, it is not the prerogative of earthly origin which obtains the unction, but the condescension of Divine grace which creates the bishop" (Sermon 3).37 He gives at once the fathers' fullest statement of the corporate priesthood and its clearest limit: all the regenerate "are made kings by the sign of the cross… consecrated priests by the oil of the Holy Spirit, so that beyond the special service of our ministry as priests, all… are a royal race and are sharers in the office of the priesthood" (Sermon 4) — the common priesthood affirmed in full, yet set alongside, not in place of, the ministry of the altar.38 And on that altar he sets the New-Covenant sacrifice: with "the variety of fleshly sacrifices" now ceased, "the one offering of… Body and Blood fulfils all those different victims," presented by "a nobler rank of Levites… priests of holier anointing" (Sermon 59).39 Where Augustine grounded the offering in the office rather than the minister's merit, Leo names the office's source — the Spirit's grace at consecration — and hands the whole, corporate priesthood and reserved ministry together, to the medieval schools Aquinas will systematize. Because he holds the two priesthoods in one sentence, he is cited on both sides of the framing question in the Arguments.

Thomas Aquinas (c. 1274)

Aquinas gives the doctrine its mature scholastic form, recasting the patristic conviction as a systematic mechanism. Asking "whether the consecration of this sacrament belongs to a priest alone," he reasons that because the Eucharist "is performed only as in the person of Christ," and "whoever performs any act in another's stead, must do so by the power bestowed by such a one," the lay believer receives at baptism only the power to receive the sacrament, whereas "the power of consecrating this sacrament on Christ's behalf is bestowed upon the priest at his ordination: for thereby he is put upon a level with them to whom the Lord said (Luke 22:19): 'Do this for a commemoration of Me'" (Summa Theologiae III, Q. 82, a. 1).40 The conclusion is flat: "it must be said that it belongs to priests to accomplish this sacrament."41 Where Cyprian had said the priest "discharges the office of Christ," Aquinas supplies the metaphysics behind it: the consecration is an act done in persona Christi by an instrumental power Christ confers at ordination, so the reservation of the offering to the ordained follows from what the sacrament is, not from church order alone. He fastens the whole on the Last-Supper command, reading "Do this in remembrance of me" (Luke 22:19) as addressed to the apostles and carried to the ordained by ordination — the institution-narrative strand weighed in the Arguments.42

The Council of Trent (1563)

Trent's twenty-third session gives the Roman dogmatic terminus of the doctrine this timeline traces, defining against the Reformers exactly what the fathers had held. Because the church has received "the holy visible sacrifice of the Eucharist," the council reasons, "it must needs also be confessed, that there is… a new, visible, and external priesthood," instituted by Christ, "and… to the apostles, and their successors in the priesthood, was the power delivered of consecrating, offering, and administering His Body and Blood, as also of forgiving and of retaining sins" (Decree on the Sacrament of Order, ch. 1).43 Canon 1 anathematizes the Reformed reduction of that office to preaching — denying "that there is not in the New Testament a visible and external priesthood… but only an office and bare ministry of preaching the Gospel" — while the canons that follow define order as a true sacrament that imprints an indelible character (so that "he who has once been a priest" cannot "again become a layman," the point Augustine had pressed) and affirm "a hierarchy by divine ordination instituted, consisting of bishops, priests, and ministers," with the bishop "superior to priests" in the reserved power of ordaining — closing on the side of gradation the seam Aerius and Jerome had cut along.44 The council presents all this not as innovation but as what "the tradition of the Catholic Church has always taught," pointing back up the timeline; what it adds is definition and anathema, in answer to a denial the earlier centuries had not faced.

Pius XII (1947)

In the encyclical Mediator Dei, Pius XII gives the modern papal statement of the doctrine in the idiom the tradition had been moving toward: the priest offers in the person of Christ. Explaining why the Mass is the one sacrifice of Calvary, he locates the identity in the celebrant — "The priest is the same, Jesus Christ, whose sacred Person his minister represents," for the minister, "by reason of the sacerdotal consecration which he has received, is made like to the High Priest and possesses the power of performing actions in virtue of Christ's very person" (Mediator Dei 69) — and grounds the reservation of that power in the apostolic succession Clement and Irenaeus had traced: "Only to the apostles, and thenceforth to those on whom their successors have imposed hands, is granted the power of the priesthood" (§ 40).45 Against a modern tendency to dissolve the ordained priesthood into the priesthood of all believers, he holds the two apart as Leo had — the faithful truly "offer," yet "the sacrament of holy orders sets the priest apart from the rest of the faithful who have not received this consecration" (§ 43) — the immediate papal antecedent of Vatican II's formula.46

The Second Vatican Council (1964)

The dogmatic constitution Lumen Gentium gives the doctrine its settled modern shape and its most precise phrase. It affirms the common priesthood of all the baptized in full — the regenerate are "consecrated as a spiritual house and a holy priesthood… to offer spiritual sacrifices" — and then distinguishes it from the ordained ministry not by degree but by kind: "Though they differ from one another in essence and not only in degree (essentia et non gradu tantum), the common priesthood of the faithful and the ministerial or hierarchical priesthood are nonetheless interrelated: each of them in its own special way is a participation in the one priesthood of Christ." The ministerial priest, "acting in the person of Christ… makes present the Eucharistic sacrifice, and offers it to God in the name of all the people," while the faithful "in virtue of their royal priesthood, join in the offering" (Lumen Gentium 10).47 This holds together in one article the two things the whole timeline has traced — the priesthood of all believers affirmed without reserve, and the ministerial priesthood marked off as differing "in essence," Cyprian's and Pius XII's in persona Christi carried into the council's own words — and it is the text the framing question in the Arguments below most directly engages.

Arguments

The cross-cutting disputes adduced for and against the doctrine are weighed here, each on its own page. Chief among them is the framing question the doctrine turns on.

Related arguments — Christ's commission to the apostles

Two readings of the dominical texts that ground the reserved ministry: that at the Supper Christ charged the apostles to "do this," and that the risen Christ delegated his own authority to them — both weighed against the objection that the words commission the apostles without, of themselves, reserving the act to an ordained line of successors.

Was "Do this in remembrance of me" a commission to the apostles alone? (contested)

Was "Do this in remembrance of me" a commission to the apostles alone? (Luke 22:19; 1 Corinthians 11) — contested. Christ spoke the command to the Twelve at the Supper, and reading it as their priestly commissioning fits the early church's uniformly reserved celebration; but that the words by themselves reserve the act to the ordained is an inference the bare text underdetermines, since Paul hands the very same command to the whole Corinthian congregation as the church's standing practice.

Did Christ delegate his own authority to the apostles, to be handed on to ordained successors? (contested)

Did Christ delegate his own authority to the apostles, to be handed on to ordained successors? (John 20:21–23; Luke 10:16) — contested. "As the Father hath sent me, even so send I you … Receive ye the Holy Ghost: Whose soever sins ye remit, they are remitted" plainly delegates a Christ-derived authority, and orderly succession is attested within living memory of the apostles (Clement of Rome, Irenaeus); but that this authority descends only through episcopal ordination, as a sacramental power to confect the Eucharist, is an inference the texts do not name — the Reformers read it as the gospel-ministry given to the whole church, the episcopal channel is a second-century development, and even Catholic theology concedes the succession is selective.

The remaining arguments stand on their own:

  • Does the New Testament institute a distinct ministerial priesthood, or only the priesthood of all believers? — contested. An ordered ministry that alone presided at the Eucharist is well-attested from the sub-apostolic age, but a distinct sacrificing priesthood — a New-Covenant hiereus-class — is a development the New Testament's own vocabulary does not supply, and that half of the dispute turns on whether the Eucharist is a sacrifice.
  • Is Malachi 1:11's "pure offering among the nations" fulfilled in the Eucharist? (Malachi 1:11) — contested. The fathers read the prophecy of a worldwide "pure offering" as the Eucharist from the Didache onward, and the universal, non-Temple language fits a Christian rite well; but the Hebrew is present-tense participial with no future verb, and the rebuke's near-context reads "pure offering" most naturally as sincere worship — so the verse fulfils the eucharistic reading better than it predicts it, and cannot settle the sacrifice question alone.
  • Was Ignatius the child Jesus took in His arms? (Matthew 18:2–4) — weak. A medieval Greek tradition identified Ignatius as the child Christ embraced as a model of humility — in its earliest form a claim exalting the bishop's office ("not even the apostles dared lay hands on him again"). But no writer before Anastasius the Librarian (c. 878) knows it, Ignatius never claims it, and his epithet Theophoros was anciently read in the active sense ("God-bearer"), not the passive one the legend needs — so it is a late fable, though its lateness tells nothing against the genuineness of his letters.
