The Immaculate Conception of Mary

The Immaculate Conception is the belief that Mary was preserved free from original sin from the first instant of her conception — not merely that she led a sinless life, but that the inherited stain every other child of Adam contracts at conception never touched her. Defined as Catholic dogma only in 1854, it is one of the latest-developing of all Marian doctrines, and its road to definition ran against the greatest doctors of the medieval church: Bernard of Clairvaux, Bonaventure, and Thomas Aquinas each rejected it, and the deadlock broke only when the Franciscan John Duns Scotus reframed the question around a “preservative” redemption.

Everything on this page turns on a distinction the reader must keep in view, because the two claims are constantly confused:

  • (a) Mary’s sinlessness in life — that she committed no actual sin. This is early and nearly consensual: Augustine already brackets her, “out of honour to the Lord,” from any discussion of sins (c. 415).
  • (b) the Immaculate Conception proper — freedom from original sin at the very instant her soul began to exist. This is the distinct, late, and long-disputed development. Most of the patristic and early-medieval witnesses commonly cited “for” the doctrine testify only to (a); the specific claim (b) is what the scholastics fought over.

The timeline below is set out earliest first, and follows claim (b) — the conception itself — through the fathers who praised Mary’s purity without ever raising it, the Greek “prepurified” and “all-holy” witnesses who came closest to it in words without defining freedom from inherited guilt, the Eastern feast that kept the day of her conception without defining it, the Canterbury monks who first framed the case for it, the scholastic giants who denied it, Scotus’s turn and the Franciscan school that carried it, the Dominican Thomists who kept up the denial into the sixteenth century (with one outlier of their own who leaned the other way), the institutional war between the two orders that erupted alongside the theology (a Dominican maculist condemned and his order expelled from Paris, the university oaths that later enforced the doctrine, and the fraud and burnings of the Jetzer affair), the institutional acts that froze the dispute (a schismatic council, two hedging papal bulls, and Trent’s deliberate exemption), Alexander VII’s bull stating the doctrine’s full content two centuries before it was defined, the 1854 definition, and the Eastern Orthodox dissent that outlasts it. The great irony it records is that the medieval opposition rested precisely on Augustine’s own doctrine of original sin, transmitted by ordinary generation: if Christ is the universal Redeemer of all, how could his mother alone owe him nothing?

Timeline

Ephrem the Syrian (c. 361)

Ephrem the Syrian supplies the earliest text later apologists press into service — a hymn stanza praising Mary’s purity: “Thou and Thy Mother are the only ones who are totally beautiful in every respect; for in Thee, O Lord, there is no spot, and in Thy Mother no stain.”1 It is poetic praise of claim (a) — her purity in life — and says nothing of her conception; and Ephrem elsewhere reads the wedding at Cana as a rebuke of Mary, which tells against reading an immaculate origin back into the line. A genuine but slender datum, over-read by those who cite it for claim (b).

Gregory of Nazianzus (c. 380)

Gregory of Nazianzus is the Eastern keystone. Preaching Christ’s nativity, he says the Virgin was “first in body and soul… purified by the Holy Ghost” — a single Greek word, prokathartheisa (“prepurified”), that became the seed of the whole Eastern debate over Mary’s purity and the fountainhead of every later Greek “prepurified / all-holy” witness.2 The word is genuinely double-edged: modern Catholic scholarship reads the prefix pro- as an already-pure nature raised higher (a Greek antecedent of claim (b)), while the mainstream and Orthodox reading takes its plain sense — a purification worked at the conceiving of Christ, presupposing a nature still “subject to impurity,” which if anything tells against a prior immaculacy. Unlike the Western (a)-texts, his word bears on Mary’s origin and nature, not merely a sinless life; it is weighed on its own argument page (assessment: contested).

Ambrose of Milan (c. 389)

Ambrose of Milan is the Western text later Latin theology treats as its anchor: he praises Mary as “an incorrupt virgin — but a virgin through grace, free from every stain of sin” (virgo per gratiam ab omni integra labe peccati), and hails her as uniquely “full of grace… which no other deserved.” But the operative words are per gratiam — purity bestowed by grace, not a stainless nature or conception — and Ambrose’s own doctrine of original sin cuts against the later reading: to be immaculate “from the beginning… is impossible for human nature,” Christ “alone is without sin,” and the “stain of original sin” is the common inheritance of the whole line from which Christ took flesh. Praise of claim (a), her purity in life, later pressed into service for claim (b); scholarship is explicit he “never stretched his claim… as far as the Immaculate Conception.”3

Augustine of Hippo (c. 415)

Augustine gives the anchor for claim (a), not (b). Answering Pelagius’s list of the sinless, he sets Mary apart: “We must except the holy Virgin Mary, concerning whom I wish to raise no question when it touches the subject of sins, out of honour to the Lord.”4 The exception is about actual sin overcome by grace, not about her conception; and Augustine’s own teaching that original sin is transmitted through ordinary generation would become the chief obstacle every later objector raised against the Immaculate Conception.

Proclus of Constantinople (c. 430)

Proclus of Constantinople is one of two Ephesus-era Greek preachers whose lavish “spotless” Marian rhetoric later apologetics presses toward claim (b). His famous Homily 1, On the Holy Virgin Theotokos — the sermon that lit the Nestorian fuse — hymns Mary as “the spotless flesh of the Virgin,” “the awesome loom of the divine economy,” “the purest fleece”; the standard reference presses one further phrase, rendering him as saying she “was formed without any stain.”5 But the register is Christological-typological praise of Mary’s bodily purity as the fit vessel of the Incarnation — claim (a) — not a claim about freedom from original sin at her conception; the homily nowhere raises the question the dogma answers.

Theodotus of Ancyra (c. 431)

Theodotus of Ancyra, an anti-Nestorian theologian at the Council of Ephesus, shows the same “spotless” register with one touch that leans further. His Oration on the Mother of God praises Mary as “a virgin innocent, without spot… holy in body and in soul… untaught the ills of Eve” — Eve-typology purity, claim (a) — but adds that she, “when not yet born, was consecrated to God.”6 That prenatal consecration is a genuine proto-(b) note — yet on the model of Jeremiah and John the Baptist it is sanctification in the womb, not preservation from inherited guilt at conception (the very distinction on which Bernard would later grant the womb-sanctification while denying the immaculate conception proper). The closest this era’s Greek idiom comes to (b), still stopping short of it.

Sophronius of Jerusalem (c. 634)

Sophronius of Jerusalem carries Gregory Nazianzen’s prokathartheisa to its most exalted pre-Damascene pitch. Preaching the Annunciation he greets Mary as “the Immaculate,” on whom the Spirit descends “to make you purer,” filled with a grace “granted to you alone” and surpassing every saint and rank of angels; his Synodical Letter of 634 — read into the acts of the Sixth Ecumenical Council — confesses her “prepurified (prokathartheisēs) in body and soul.”7 Modern Catholic scholarship reads the “already-Immaculate, then made purer” language as the clearest Greek antecedent of claim (b); the Orthodox reading takes the same “prepurified” word in its plain sense — a purification worked at the Annunciation, presupposing a nature still “subject to impurity.” Proto-(b) at most, and weighed with Gregory’s word on its argument page (assessment: contested).

The Life of the Virgin — attributed to Maximus the Confessor (early 7th c.?)

The Life of the Virgin, the earliest complete biography of Mary, heaps Byzantine purity-titles on the “all-holy, immaculate, and most blessed Theotokos,” “ever immaculate: before birth, in birth, and after birth.”8 Read closely it tells against claim (b) rather than for it: its one passage reasoning explicitly about a conception free of “the original sin of Adam and Eve” is applied to Christ, not Mary, and its narrative of Mary’s own conception is the plain miracle of the barren Anne. Its translator, Stephen Shoemaker, cautions against retrojecting later Latin Marian dogma onto it — and its authorship and date are themselves contested, with a leading study placing the Greek prototype as late as the 10th century. Honorific all-holiness, not preservation from inherited guilt.

The Eastern feast of Mary’s conception — Andrew of Crete and Germanus (7th–8th c.)

Before there was a doctrine there was a feast: the conception of Mary by her mother Anne, kept in the Christian East from the seventh century, its oldest witness the liturgical Canon of Andrew of Crete. Andrew and Germanus of Constantinople hymn Mary as “undefiled… most pure” and “wholly immaculate.”9 But the standard reference itself draws the needed line — the older feast of Mary’s conception and the modern feast of the Immaculate Conception “are not identical in their object”: the East kept the day of her conception; it did not define her freedom from original sin. Testimony to claim (a), not (b).

John of Damascus (c. 730)

John of Damascus is the high-water mark of the Greek “all-holy” (Panagia) strand — the closest a father comes to claim (b) without teaching it. Preaching on Mary’s own nativity he sanctifies not just her life but her very conception and her parents: “O most all-blessed loins of Joachim, from which a wholly unblemished seed was sent forth! O renowned womb of Anna, in which… an all-holy infant grew,” a birth at which “nature did not dare to anticipate the germ of grace.”10 Yet his frame is foreknowing sanctification and predestination (God, “having known in advance your worth… predestined you”), not the preservation of Mary from an inherited guilt — the Latin category the Greek East, which does not hold Adam’s guilt to be inherited, construes differently. The culmination of the “all-holy / predestined” development, and precisely the figure the modern Orthodox rejection reaches back to and reads the other way; claimed by both sides because it stands just short of the distinction 1854 would draw.

Anselm of Canterbury (c. 1098)

Anselm of Canterbury stands on the hinge. He denies the immaculate conception in the later sense — Mary “was of the number of those who were cleansed from their sins before” Christ’s birth, purified after her conception, not preserved at it11 — yet he supplied the doctrine’s raw materials: the argument from “fittingness” (it was fitting Mary shine with “a purity than which, under God, none greater can be conceived”) and an account of original sin as objectively transmitted. He declined the conclusion his tools would later be used to reach.

Eadmer of Canterbury (c. 1125)

Eadmer of Canterbury, Anselm’s disciple and biographer, wrote the first sustained case for claim (b). Pressing his master’s fittingness argument from Mary’s purification all the way to her preservation, he reasoned that God “clearly could and willed it; if therefore he willed it, he did it” (Potuit plane et voluit; si igitur voluit, fecit) — a two-century anticipation of the Scotist potuit, decuit, ergo fecit, pressed home with the image of a chestnut grown unharmed amid its thorns.12

Bernard of Clairvaux (c. 1140)

Bernard of Clairvaux, writing to the canons of Lyons to rebuke their newly adopted Feast of the Conception, is the first great voice to deny claim (b) outright. Holiness presupposes existence, he argues, so Mary could not have been sanctified before she was conceived, nor in the conception itself; rather “she received sanctification when existing in the womb after conception, which, by excluding sin, made her birth holy, but not her conception” — sanctified in the womb like Jeremiah and John the Baptist.13

Peter Lombard (c. 1155)

Peter Lombard is the frame-setter the whole later dispute presupposes. His Sentences became the standard theology textbook of the medieval schools, and the scholastic question of Mary’s sanctification was argued on one of its distinctions — Book III, distinction 3. There Lombard teaches that the flesh from which Christ’s body was formed was “subject to sin” (obnoxia peccato) “like the rest of the Virgin’s flesh,” and that the Holy Spirit “wholly purged” Mary from sin and freed her from its “tinder” (fomes peccati) — the decisive purgation worked at Christ’s conception.14 That is the default of claim (a) — sanctification after conception, of a flesh already under sin — not the preservation at the first instant that claim (b) asserts, a category not yet formulable in his frame. It is the very default Bonaventure and Aquinas would operate within and Scotus would finally break.

Bonaventure (c. 1255)

Bonaventure, in his commentary on the Sentences, holds the same line as the “more common” opinion of his day: Mary’s soul contracted original sin and was sanctified only shortly after its infusion (animation), not preserved from it at conception.15 He judged the sanctification-after-contraction view the safer and more reasonable, more consonant with Christ’s office as universal Redeemer.

Thomas Aquinas (c. 1270)

Thomas Aquinas gives the opposition its most influential form. Mary “was sanctified after animation,” her soul having “contracted original sin” but being “cleansed therefrom before her birth from the womb.”16 A conception wholly free of the stain, he reasoned, would be “derogatory to the dignity of Christ, by reason of His being the universal Saviour of all” — for then Mary alone would not have needed the redemption Christ won for every human being.17

William of Ware (c. 1290)

William of Ware, an English Franciscan and Scotus’s own master, is the first university theologian to argue the case for claim (b) in the schools. Asking “whether the Blessed Virgin was conceived in original sin,” he sets out the very argument-form his pupil would make famous: since her Son “is purity itself, it was fitting that he should make his Mother as pure as he could” — and so “not merely cleanse her but preserve her from all impurity” (ab omni immunditia praeservaret); “what he could do, it was fitting that he did, and… it follows that he did so.”18 The decisive word is preserve, not cleanse — the claim (b) turn. Two cautions belong on the record: the pithy slogan potuit, decuit, ergo fecit and the celebrated maxim that “the more excellent is to be attributed to Mary” are Scotus’s crystallizations, not Ware’s own words, and which of the two reached the immaculist position first is disputed.

Ramon Llull (c. 1298)

Ramon Llull, the Majorcan lay Franciscan and philosopher, argued for Mary’s freedom from original sin at Paris in the autumn of 1298 — roughly contemporary with, and by some reckonings just before, Scotus. Reasoning in the demonstrative style of his “Art,” he held that God and sin cannot be united in one and the same subject, so the mother of the incarnate Son did not contract the stain but was sanctified in the very matter from which she was formed.19 A genuine early affirmation of claim (b), reached by a mechanism of his own that pre-dates Scotus’s preservative redemption; the popular claim that Llull coined the phrase “Immaculate Conception” is an apologetic commonplace the reference literature does not support.

John Duns Scotus (c. 1300)

John Duns Scotus broke the deadlock by turning Aquinas’s objection on its head. The most perfect Mediator, he argued, performs the most perfect act of mediation, and “he who prevents lest one offend, is a more perfect mediator than he who merely reconciles after the offense”; so Mary was redeemed more excellently, not less, by being preserved from ever contracting original sin — “she would have contracted original sin… unless this had been prevented by the grace of a Mediator.”20 This “preservative redemption” reconciled the privilege with Christ’s universal saviourhood and became the case the doctrine would rest on.

Peter Auriol (1314)

Peter Auriol, a Franciscan lector at Toulouse, gave the doctrine its first dedicated monograph. He defended it in a public disputation at Toulouse in 1314, finished his Tractatus de conceptione beatae Mariae Virginis on 20 December 1314, and answered his critics the next year in the Repercussorium. His case runs in the Scotist order — God could preserve Mary from contracting original sin, it was of the “highest fittingness” that he do so, and it “can be held without danger to faith… that God did in fact preserve her” — but is deliberately cautious, expressly leaving the matter open “until the Church shall have determined” it.21 His own contribution lies in the question of the debitum peccati: whether Mary was ever under any debt or obligation to contract the stain at all, original sin construed as an inherited legal liability from which a singular exemption might be granted.

Francis of Mayronis (c. 1320)

Francis of Mayronis, a leading early Scotist (princeps Scotistarum), became with Auriol one of the doctrine’s “most fervent champions” in the generation after their master. Where Scotus advanced preservative redemption tentatively, Mayronis “threw Duns’ caution into the wind… arguing forcefully for Mary’s divine ‘privilege’ or exemption from original sin” — asserting as a certainty what Scotus had left as the more probable.22 He built it out with two arguments of his own: extending Scotus’s doctrine of Christ’s absolute predestination to Mary, and reading original sin through the categories of Roman law as an inherited debt on which her exemption is a juridically granted privilegium.

Durandus of Saint-Pourçain (c. 1320)

Durandus of Saint-Pourçain, a Dominican famous for thinking against the grain of his own order, is the one early-fourteenth-century friar of the Preachers who leaned toward affirming claim (b). Against the maculist consensus of his brethren he held that Mary was conceived without original sin, reasoning that the union of her soul with her body and the gift of sanctifying grace fell at one and the same instant, leaving no interval in which the stain could be contracted — and he reached it by his own route, from Augustine, Anselm, and Richard of St-Victor, not from Scotus, whom he does not cite. A lone Dominican voice in the immaculist current, against enforcers like Herveus Natalis (the order’s Master General, 1318–23) who denied it outright; the attribution rests on modern scholarship, the primary text not yet examined here.23

John of Montson condemned at Paris (1387)

The condemnation of John of Montson is the sharpest maculist statement of the whole dispute and its great institutional turning point. An Aragonese Dominican and Paris master, Montson taught that Mary was conceived in original sin like all Adam’s descendants and that the immaculist opinion was heretical; the Paris theology faculty condemned fourteen of his propositions, and the Bishop of Paris excommunicated any who should defend them.24 Montson appealed to Avignon and then fled, and the Order of Preachers was expelled from the University — the delegates against him (among them Pierre d’Ailly and Jean Gerson) resting their case not on the doctrine’s truth but on the university’s right to police its teachers. The plainest denial of claim (b) by a Dominican, it fixed Paris as an immaculist bastion and is the root of the 1497 oath.

John Capreolus (c. 1430)

John Capreolus, the “Prince of Thomists” (princeps thomistarum), gave the maculist denial its most thorough late-medieval defense. His Defensiones — a point-by-point vindication of Aquinas against Scotus and Auriol — concludes at Book III that “the Blessed Mary was conceived in original sin” (beata Maria fuit concepta in peccato originali), a thesis he credits to Aquinas and backs with Bernard’s letter to Lyons.25 Claim (a), not (b) — sanctification after animation, not preservation at conception — restated with full force on the eve of Basel.

The Council of Basel (1439)

The Council of Basel was the first conciliar body to declare the doctrine “pious, and consonant to the Catholic faith,” forbidding preaching against it.26 But the decree bound no one: by September 1439 Basel had defied Pope Eugene IV and gone into schism — it had just purported to depose him and would elect an antipope — and its sessions from this period are not reckoned ecumenical. An Immaculist victory won from a body that had put itself outside the Church’s authority; the question stayed open.

Pope Sixtus IV (1476–1483)

Pope Sixtus IV froze the dispute rather than settling it. His Cum praeexcelsa (1476) approved the feast and its Office — while pointedly calling the conception “miraculous,” not “immaculate,” and defining nothing about original sin. His Grave nimis (1483) then forbade either party from branding the other heretical, on the express ground that the point had “not yet [been] decided by the Roman Church and the Apostolic See.”27 The controversy would continue as a permitted difference between Dominicans and Franciscans, not a matter of orthodoxy.

The University of Paris oath (1497)

The University of Paris oath marks the doctrine’s deepest institutional entrenchment. In 1497 Paris decreed that no one could be “admitted a member of the university, who did not swear that he would do the utmost to defend and assert the Immaculate Conception of Mary” — a requirement born of the same Franciscan–Dominican disputes the Montson affair had inflamed, and quickly imitated at Cologne (1499), Vienna (1501), and a long roll of studia from Oxford and Cambridge to Salamanca and Louvain.28 An emphatic affirmation of claim (b) — but by statute and academic oath, not magisterial definition, and running well ahead of a Rome that Sixtus IV had just committed to leaving the question open.

The Jetzer affair at Bern (1507–1509)

The Jetzer affair is the most notorious scandal of the war over the doctrine. In the Dominican convent at Bern, apparitions of the Virgin were staged for a lay brother, Hans Jetzer, in which “Mary” declared she had been conceived in original sin — scripted propaganda for the maculist side against the Franciscan immaculist cause. The fraud was exposed, and after trials that turned on torture-extracted confessions four Dominicans were burned at Bern on 31 May 1509.29 The affair is handled here with caution: the executions are certain, but the guilt is genuinely contested — the verdict rested on confessions drawn out under torture, and modern scholarship (Paulus, 1897; Utz Tremp, 2022) reads Jetzer as more likely the dupe than the mastermind. It belongs on the timeline not as a doctrinal witness but as a measure of how bitter the dispute had become.

Cardinal Cajetan (1515)

Cardinal Cajetan (Thomas de Vio), the age’s great commentator on Aquinas and Master General of the Dominicans, carried the maculist line into the sixteenth century. In a treatise addressed to Leo X while the Fifth Lateran Council sat, he framed the question as whether God “preserved” Mary from original sin or “cleansed” her from it — and ranked the cleansing (claim (a)) as “probable,” the preservation (claim (b)) as at best “tolerable,” branding the strong form — a total exemption that would place Mary outside those for whom Christ died — “manifest heresy,” and dismissing the Council of Basel’s contrary decree as the act of a schism, “the synagogue of Satan.”30 The last great scholarly denial before Trent; it provoked sharp Dominican rebuttals, and on one reading marked the turning point at which the order first conceded the properly-framed preservation as tolerable rather than heretical.

The Council of Trent (1546)

When the Council of Trent defined the doctrine of original sin against the Reformers, it faced the question of Mary and stepped around it: it “declare[d], that it is not its intention to include in this decree, where original sin is treated of, the blessed and immaculate Virgin Mary,” and ordered Sixtus IV’s constitutions kept.31 This is an exemption — Mary carved out of the scope of the original-sin decree — not a positive definition of claim (b), which still awaited 1854.

Pope Alexander VII — Sollicitudo omnium ecclesiarum (1661)

Pope Alexander VII gave the doctrine its fullest papal statement before 1854. His bull Sollicitudo omnium ecclesiarum spells out claim (b) entire — the faithful believe that Mary’s soul, “from the first instant of its creation and infusion into her body, was preserved immune by a special grace and privilege of God from the stain of original sin, in view of the merits of her Son, Jesus Christ, the Redeemer of our human race” — renews the constitutions of Sixtus IV (with Paul V and Gregory XV), and bars all further attack on the pious view.32 Every element of Pius IX’s definition is already here, almost word for word — the “first instant,” the preservation from the stain, the Scotist “merits of her Son.” But it stops short of a formal definition: it protects the doctrine’s content and silences its opponents without declaring it a truth revealed by God. The doctrinal high-water mark of the pre-definition era — content without dogma.

Pope Pius IX polls the world’s bishops — Ubi primum (1849)

Before defining anything, Pius IX asked the whole Church what it believed. Writing from exile at Gaeta after fleeing the Roman revolution of 1848, he issued the encyclical Ubi primum on 2 February 1849, putting two questions to every bishop: how great the devotion of their clergy and people to the Immaculate Conception and their desire that it be defined, and — “especially” — “what you yourselves, in your wise judgment, think and desire on this matter.”33 Of the roughly six hundred bishops who replied, some 536 favoured the definition; four or five (among them the Archbishop of Paris) opposed it outright, and about three dozen, mostly from Protestant countries, judged only that the moment was inopportune. This near-unanimous canvass of the entire episcopate — the doctrine made explicit only after consulting the bishops and the faithful — is the immediate run-up to Ineffabilis Deus, and a datum the late-development question weighs.

Pope Pius IX — Ineffabilis Deus (1854)

Pope Pius IX closed the long dispute by definition, declaring “that the most Blessed Virgin Mary, in the first instant of her conception, by a singular grace and privilege granted by Almighty God, in view of the merits of Jesus Christ, the Saviour of the human race, was preserved free from all stain of original sin, is a doctrine revealed by God.”34 The bull grounds the privilege partly in the Genesis 3:15 reading of Mary as the woman set against the serpent — the New-Eve typology whose patristic development is traced on its own timeline — and Scotus’s preservative redemption supplied the answer to the objection that had held the great scholastics back.

The Eastern Orthodox rejection (modern)

The dogma of 1854 did not end the story but divided it: the Christian East, which venerates Mary as Panagia (“all-holy”) and free of every personal sin, rejects the Roman definition. The reason is a different doctrine of the Fall. The East does not hold that Adam’s guilt is inherited — only his mortality and corruption — so, as Vladimir Lossky puts it, it “does not admit the idea that the Holy Virgin was thus exempted from the lot of the rest of fallen humanity.”35 With no inherited guilt to be preserved from, the immaculate conception answers a question the East never asked — a live dissent that outlasts the definition, and a mirror on the Augustinian premise that made the whole Western dispute possible.

Arguments

Sub-claims marshaled around the controversy, which cut across the timeline rather than sitting at one date — each weighed adversarially on its own page, with an assessment of the interpretation it serves.

Related arguments — universal redemption

The doctrine’s central dispute is a single objection and its single reply, read best as a pair. The objection — that exempting Mary from original sin breaks the universality of sin and of Christ’s redemption — is the reasoned ground on which the greatest medieval doctors rejected the doctrine; Scotus’s praeredemptio is the reply that broke the deadlock and that Rome built on in 1854.

Does the Immaculate Conception contradict Christ’s universal redemption? (assessment: contested)

The universal-redemption objection — that an immaculate conception is incompatible with the “all have sinned” of Scripture (Romans 3:23, 5:12) and with Christ’s being the Saviour of all: if Mary contracted no stain she owed no debt, and then either she needed no redemption (so Christ is not the Saviour of all) or the universal is broken. The reading is contested. On the pre-Scotist definition of redemption — a cure applied after the stain is contracted — the objection is near-decisive and unrefuted on its own terms, which is exactly why Bernard, Bonaventure, and Aquinas denied the doctrine (Aquinas: an immaculate conception would be “derogatory to the dignity of Christ… the universal Saviour of all”). But its whole force rests on that one definition of redemption; Scotus’s preservative-redemption reply, later adopted at Rome, answers it — so the question stays genuinely disputed, strong by one account of redemption, answerable by another.36

Does Scotus’s “preservative redemption” dissolve the objection? (assessment: plausible)

The praeredemptio argument — that Scotus’s move (“he who prevents lest one offend, is a more perfect mediator than he who merely reconciles after the offense”) turns the objection inside out: Mary is not exempted from redemption but its supreme beneficiary, “pre-redeemed” by the Passion’s merits applied proleptically by a God outside time, sanctified in the order of nature not of time. As a mechanism the reading is plausible — coherent, hard to rule out for an omnipotent God, and the doctrine’s strongest single move (the one Ineffabilis Deus leaned on). But coherence is not actuality: the completing inference potuit, decuit, ergo fecit (“God could, it was fitting, therefore he did”) is a fittingness argument, not a proof — flagged “doubtful” even by friends of the doctrine, and used by Scotus only illustratively — and the whole machinery presupposes a Franciscan account of grace a Thomist may decline while granting the bare possibility. The mechanism answers the objection; it does not settle whether it is true.37

Did Aquinas actually deny the Immaculate Conception? (assessment: sound)

The claim that Thomas Aquinas denied the doctrine — often softened by his admirers — is sound for the point that matters: in his mature works Aquinas denies the immaculate conception proper (claim (b)) in plain words. On the Angelic Salutation teaches that “the Blessed Virgin was conceived in original [sin], but not born [in it]… from this she was cleansed in the womb,” and the Summa that her soul “did indeed contract original sin, but was cleansed therefrom before her birth from the womb” — sanctification after animation, not preservation at conception. He affirmed her sinless life (claim (a)) as warmly as anyone. A distinct and weaker claim — that he would have accepted the doctrine given the distinctions Scotus later drew (nature-vs-time priority, the shared delayed-animation embryology, the redemption worry Scotus answered), so that his principles vindicate rather than oppose it — is a genuine Thomist position, but a plausible counterfactual about what he might have thought, not a correction of what he wrote.38

Does “full of grace” (Luke 1:28) prove the Immaculate Conception? (assessment: weak)

The central New-Testament proof-text — that Gabriel’s greeting κεχαριτωμένη (“full of grace,” Luke 1:28), a perfect passive participle of “to grace/favour,” proves Mary’s freedom from original sin from her conception. As strict proof the reading is weak. The grammar is real — a completed, enduring, God-given state of favour, Mary addressed almost by a title — but it specifies neither the content of the grace (original sin is not mentioned) nor its terminus: a perfect tense fixes a settled state at the Annunciation, not at conception, and the same verb is used of all believers in Ephesians 1:6. The verb means “favoured” (so the Catholic New American Bible now renders “favored one,” and Fitzmyer and Brown caution against the Vulgate’s over-reading gratia plena); and decisively, the witnesses who expound “full of grace” most richly draw no immaculate conception from it — Bede ties it to the divine maternity, and Bonaventure, who lavishes praise on the phrase, still held Mary contracted the stain. Even the Catholic Encyclopedia grants the word “serves only as an illustration, not as a proof of the dogma.” The softer reading — the salutation as a datum consonant with a unique plenitude of grace, on which the doctrine builds by fittingness — is a separate and plausible claim, all the verse actually supplies.39

Does Gregory Nazianzen’s prokathartheisa testify to the Immaculate Conception? (assessment: contested)

The claim that Gregory of Nazianzus’s word prokathartheisa testifies to the Immaculate Conception — pressed by modern Catholic scholarship (Christiaan Kappes) as the Greek patristic antecedent of Mary’s freedom from original sin: the prefix pro- marks a purity Mary already possessed, later “prepared” rather than cleansed of stain (as Christ is “purified” in baptism though sinless), founding the Eastern “prepurified / all-holy” vocabulary that runs to John of Damascus and that Rufinus rendered into Latin as immaculata. The reading is contested. It has a real philological hook and a genuine reception-history; but on the plain grammar the participle names a purification worked at the conceiving of Christ — not at Mary’s own conception, the precise object of the dogma — and purification presupposes something to purify, so the mainstream and Orthodox tradition reads the same text for a fallen womb “subject to impurity,” its own clearest patristic ground for rejecting the Roman definition. The word underdetermines the verdict, which is why one participle seeded the entire Eastern debate: that Gregory attests Mary’s singular, Spirit-wrought holiness is sound; that his word attests her immaculate conception is strong by philology and reception, answerable by the plain grammar and the timing.40

Is the Immaculate Conception an unwarranted late development, unknown to the first millennium? (assessment: contested)

The charge that 1854 defined a novelty — that no unambiguous first-millennium father teaches the immaculate conception proper (claim (b)); the patristic texts attest Mary’s sinless life (claim (a)) or the Eastern feast of her conception, not freedom from original sin at conception; and the greatest scholastics (Bernard, Bonaventure, Aquinas) denied it — so the definition is a late accretion, not an apostolic deposit made explicit. The charge splits in two. Its factual kernel is sound and conceded even within Catholic scholarship (the standard reference grants “no direct or categorical and stringent proof… from Scripture” and that “the Greek Fathers never formally or explicitly discussed the question”), and it is materially stronger than the same “late invention” charge against the perpetual virginity, which fails because that doctrine is second-century. But the conclusion — that the development is therefore unwarranted — is contested: it turns on the development-of-doctrine question (may the church make explicit what was implicit in Mary’s holiness and the New-Eve typology, once Scotus’s distinction dissolved the doctors’ stated reason?), which is the disputed premise, not a settled answer. Strong by the measure of explicit patristic attestation; answerable by the measure of legitimate development — with a real disanalogy (the doctrine was denied, not merely undefined, and the East that shares the premises declines the conclusion) keeping the reply from settling it, and Rome’s own “always existed in the Church” framing overreaching the record its scholars concede.41
