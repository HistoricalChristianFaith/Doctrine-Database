Was Origen's subordination of the Son a pre-Nicene idiom — so that reading him as an Arian is anachronistic?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — the anachronism at the core is real and is not seriously disputed. Origen wrote three generations before Nicaea, in an idiom every ante-Nicene theologian shared, and he is the ascertained source of the one formula Nicene orthodoxy could not do without: the Son's eternal generation, “never was it not,” which Athanasius quoted against the Arians by name. To read the “second God” language back through 325 as covert Arianism is a retrojection, and the fourth century itself read him both ways. But two things keep the defence from carrying the whole charge. Some of Origen's formulations are not merely undeveloped but positively at odds with what was later defined — the Father alone is God-of-himself, prayer in the strict sense goes to the Father and “not even to Christ himself,” the Spirit is “made through the Word” and “inferior” — and anachronism does not dissolve them; it dates them. And the retrojection cuts both ways: if the Arian claim on Origen is one-sided, so is the Athanasian rescue, which works only by sorting his exploratory sentences from his settled ones. The reading that survives is the narrow one — his Trinitarian language cannot be indicted by a rule defined after his death, and Arianism proper is not his — not the sweeping one, that the subordination is a verbal accident later readers invented. See also was the pre-existence of souls an open question when Origen taught it?, the same anachronism claim pressed on a different charge, and was Origen condemned by a standard that did not exist in his lifetime?, the umbrella fairness question this is one instance of.

The claim

The charge answered here is that Origen subordinated the Son (and the Spirit below the Son) to the Father in a way the Church would later declare heretical. The texts cited for it are genuine. In the Commentary on John the Father alone is “Very God”:

God on the one hand is Very God (Autotheos, God of Himself)… but… all beyond the Very God is made God by participation in His divinity, and is not to be called simply God (with the article), but rather God (without article). And thus the first-born of all creation, who is the first to be with God, and to attract to Himself divinity, is a being of more exalted rank than the other gods beside Him.

— Origen, Commentary on John II.2 (c. 231).1 In On Prayer prayer “in its full meaning” is owed to the Father alone, “not even to Christ himself”;2 in Against Celsus the Son is a “second God”;3 and Jerome's inventory of the lost Greek Peri Archōn renders Origen flatly: “The Son is inferior to the Father, inasmuch as He is second and the Father first; and the Holy Spirit which dwells in all the saints is inferior to the Son.”4

The interpretation weighed here is the defence against that charge, in its advocated form: that this language is pre-Nicene and cannot be measured by a standard the Church defined some seventy years after Origen's death; that Origen is in fact the source of the eternal generation Nicene orthodoxy needed; and that the charge of Arianism against him is a retrojection, a fourth-century category read backward onto a third-century man.

Who advances it

It is the settled view of Origen scholarship. Henri Crouzel, Peter Widdicombe, and Mark Edwards each argue, from different angles, that Origen's gradation of Father, Son, and Spirit is the common ante-Nicene idiom rather than a first draft of Arianism, and that his doctrine of the Son's eternal generation is one of the genuine sources of the Nicene position — Widdicombe's The Fatherhood of God from Origen to Athanasius tracing precisely the line from Origen's eternal Father-Son relation to Athanasius's use of it, Edwards's Origen against Plato resisting the Platonizing, proto-Arian Origen of older textbooks.5 The point has a fourth-century pedigree of its own: Athanasius, the architect of Nicene orthodoxy, cited Origen not as an adversary but as a witness, and did so knowing full well that the Arians were quoting him too.

The evidence for

Origen is the source of eternal generation, and Athanasius quoted him for it against the Arians. The formula that decides the Arian question — that there was no “when” in which the Son did not exist — is Origen's before it is anyone's. In Peri Archōn he writes that the Son's “generation is as eternal and everlasting as the brilliancy which is produced from the sun… not by receiving the breath of life… by any outward act, but by His own nature.”6 Athanasius, defending the Council of Nicaea in De Decretis, adduces Origen among the authorities who taught the faith before it — carefully setting aside “what he has written as if inquiring and by way of exercise” and quoting only “what he definitely declares”: “as being the likeness of the Father, never was it not… let him understand well who dares to say, ‘Once the Son was not,’ that he is saying, ‘Once Wisdom was not.’”7 “Once the Son was not” is the Arian slogan in as many words; Athanasius produces Origen to refute it. A man whose sentence is the standing answer to Arianism cannot without strain be filed as its ancestor.

The idiom is pre-Nicene, and universal in its age. The vocabulary the charge fastens on — the Son as “second,” God “by participation,” ranked below the Father — is not peculiar to Origen. It is the common speech of every theologian before the Arian crisis forced the questions to precision: Justin's Son who is “another God” numerically distinct and “subordinate to the Maker of all things,” Tertullian's Son as a “portion” who makes the Father “greater,” Novatian and Dionysius of Alexandria in the same key. The homoousion, co-equality, and the technical distinction of “begotten” from “made” that would sort the orthodox from the Arian did not yet exist as tests; the language that fails those tests fails them everywhere in the third century, and historians of doctrine do not on that account call Justin or Tertullian Arians. To single Origen out is to apply to one man a rule that would condemn the whole ante-Nicene church.

The fourth century read him in both directions — which is the anachronism made visible. That Arians and Eusebians claimed Origen while Athanasius claimed him is not a puzzle to be resolved in one party's favour; it is the direct symptom of a body of writing composed before the dividing line was drawn. His texts contained a real eternal generation and a real gradation of ranks, and each side quarried the half it wanted. The lesson the two-way reception teaches is the argument's own: a corpus that can be read as Arian and as Nicene with equal textual warrant is a corpus written before either category applied.

Origen defends the worship of the Son. The strict-prayer passage is often quoted as though Origen denied the Son religious honour altogether. He does not. Against Celsus he insists that “we worship one God, the Father and the Son,” and that the Son has “the right to be honoured… given to Him by God, ‘that all may honour the Son, even as they honour the Father.’”8 The distinction in On Prayer is a distinction about the grammar of address — that petition in the fullest sense is offered to the Father through the Son — not a demotion of the Son out of the object of worship. Read whole, Origen is harder to enlist for Arianism than the excerpted lines suggest.

Adversarial assessment

The subordination is real and ontological, not merely a verbal accident. The steelman of the defence has to concede what the excerpts show. “Very God” belongs to the Father alone; the Son is God “by participation,” “not to be called simply God (with the article)”; the Spirit is placed “third in dignity” and, in the Commentary on John, is “made through the Word” and reckoned “one of the ‘all things’ which are inferior to their Maker”9 — a sentence Origen calls “too firmly settled to be disturbed.” These are not undeveloped anticipations of Nicaea that later precision would have tidied; they run against it. The homoousion asserts one identical Godhead of Father, Son, and Spirit; Origen asserts a hierarchy of participated divinity, and the First Council of Constantinople's confession of the Spirit as Lord and co-worshipped is exactly what “the Spirit was made through the Word” denies. Anachronism explains why Origen wrote so; it does not turn the propositions into orthodoxy. Dating a difficulty is not dissolving it.

The retrojection charge cuts both ways. If it is anachronistic for the Arians to enlist Origen, it is equally anachronistic for the Nicenes — and Athanasius's own procedure admits as much. He can produce the orthodox Origen only by first setting aside everything written “as if inquiring and by way of exercise” and keeping what Origen “definitely declares.” That is a hermeneutic of rescue: it presupposes that the corpus contains material an Arian could fairly cite, and manages it by triage. The honest description is not “Origen was Nicene and the Arians misread him,” but “Origen was pre-Nicene, and both later parties read their own settlement back into him.” The defence is entitled to the second sentence; it overreaches when it slides to the first.

The charge that condemned him was not this one. The defence is strongest and least necessary here, because subordinationism was never the operative ground of Origen's condemnation. The Trinitarian complaint appears in the record — Theophilus's synod of 400 charged that “the Son compared with the Father is falsehood,” and Epiphanius reported that on Origen's view “the Son cannot see the Father, and the Holy Spirit cannot see the Son”10 — but it is never the core, and it is absent from the enforced anathemas. What Justinian's edict of 543 and the fifteen anathemas of 553 actually condemn is the pre-existence of souls, their pre-cosmic fall, the apokatastasis, the spherical resurrection body, and the pre-incarnate soul of Christ — not the ranking of the persons. So the “judged by Nicaea” framing partly answers a charge the councils did not press. It rebuts the popular slander that Origen was “really an Arian”; it does little to lift the condemnation that was in fact imposed, which rests elsewhere and is weighed on its own pages.

“Cannot be heretical by a later standard” conflates two claims. As with the parallel argument on pre-existence, the exculpatory move slides between “not yet defined” and “not false.” That Nicaea post-dates Origen by seventy years bears on his culpability — he cannot have transgressed a rule not yet made — and not on whether “the Spirit was made” is true. The council did not invent a truth in 325 that was a falsehood in 253; on the Church's own account it declared what had always been the case. Origen is exculpable for using the language of his age; the language can still be, by the later reckoning, mistaken.

Assessment

contested. By the lights of the history of doctrine the argument is strong: Origen's Trinitarian idiom is the shared ante-Nicene idiom, he is a genuine source of the eternal generation that Nicaea required, Athanasius cited him against the Arians by name, and to brand him an Arian is a retrojection that would equally brand Justin and Tertullian. On that the defence holds. By the lights of systematic theology it falls short of clearing him: the subordination is ontological and not merely verbal — the Father alone God-of-himself, the Spirit “made through the Word” and “inferior” — and these positively contradict what Nicaea and Constantinople defined, so that dating them does not baptize them; the two-way fourth-century reception shows the Nicene rescue to be as selective as the Arian appropriation; and the anachronism, real as it is, answers a charge the councils that condemned him did not finally press. The reading that survives the counter-evidence is the modest one: Origen's Trinitarian theology cannot be indicted as Arianism by a rule defined after his death, and he did more to found Nicene orthodoxy than to undermine it — but his subordinationism is a real feature of his thought, not an optical illusion of later readers, and “anachronism” ends the trial for heresy without settling the theology.
