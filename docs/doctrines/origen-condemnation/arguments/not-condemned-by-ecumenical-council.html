Was Origen himself anathematized by an ecumenical council?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — the text-critical case is genuinely strong at its narrowest and overreaches at its widest. That the fifteen anathemas against Origen were not an act of the council’s formal sessions is now the majority editorial view (Diekamp, Tanner, Crouzel), and that Origen’s name in the council’s eleventh canon may be a later insertion is arguable (Ramelli). But the popular conclusion drawn from these — that no ecumenical council ever anathematized the man — overstates. On every reconstruction short of forgery the fifteen were issued by the same bishops gathered at Constantinople for the council, acting as a synod; the earliest narrative witness says the assembled fathers condemned him by name; the Latin acts (the only full text) carry his name in the eleventh canon; and two later, undisputed ecumenical councils — Constantinople III (680–681) and Nicaea II (787) — name Origen personally and refer his condemnation to the fifth synod, supplying conciliar reception whatever the disputed 553 text contained. The reading that survives is the narrow one: the formal, in-session ecumenical anathema of Origen the man is not securely documented, but a conciliar condemnation of him is.

The claim

Recent studies have shown that these anathemas cannot be attributed to this council.

— Norman P. Tanner’s standard edition of the conciliar decrees, printing the acts of 553 without the fifteen anathemas against Origen, link.1 The interpretation weighed here is the canonical corollary usually drawn from that editorial judgement: that the Second Council of Constantinople — the Fifth Ecumenical Council — did not, as an act of the council in session, anathematize Origen. Two textual facts carry it. The fifteen anathemas, the fullest statement of what was condemned, belong (on this view) to a preliminary or separate synod, not to the eight numbered sessions; and Origen’s name in the eleventh of the council’s own anathemas is a later insertion. If both hold, then the one thing everyone “knows” about Origen — that an ecumenical council condemned him — is, in its strict canonical form, undocumented.

Who advances it

The scholarly spine of the argument is Franz Diekamp’s study of 1899, which reconstructed the fifteen anathemas as the work of the bishops gathered at Constantinople in the weeks before the council opened on 5 May 553, rather than of the council itself; this has been the prevailing view since. Norman Tanner’s Decrees of the Ecumenical Councils (1990) accordingly omits them from the acts, and Henri Crouzel — whose Origène remains the mature synthesis — concluded that Origen was never officially condemned by the church, a judgement about the canonical form of the proceedings rather than about the doctrines’ merit. Ilaria Ramelli presses the point onto the eleventh canon, arguing that Origen’s name there is an interpolation. 2 In its popular form the thesis is pressed harder still — into the flat claim that “the Fifth Council never condemned Origen” (and, in the apokatastasis debate, that it therefore never condemned universal salvation) — and that stronger form is what the assessment below tests. Richard Price, the modern editor of the acts, holds the moderate version of the source-critical point while resisting the sweeping conclusion, as noted below.

The evidence for

Origen is absent from the council’s stated business. The council was summoned to endorse Justinian’s condemnation of the Three Chapters — Theodore of Mopsuestia, Theodoret of Cyrrhus, Ibas of Edessa. Origen, dead three centuries and none of the Three Chapters, is not named in the letter of summons or in any of the correspondence around the council. The nineteenth-century editors of the acts put this as the decisive difficulty: “the chief difficulty in supposing these anathematisms to have been adopted by the Fifth Ecumenical is that nothing whatever is said about Origen in the call of the council, nor in any of the letters written in connexion with it.”3

The fifteen anathemas hang on a single late caption. The text everyone quotes as “the condemnation of Origen” survives because Peter Lambeck, the imperial librarian at Vienna, found it in the seventeenth century under a heading ascribing it to “the 165 holy Fathers of the holy fifth Synod.” That caption is the whole of the manuscript case for attaching the fifteen to the council, and editors have doubted it since Walch and Döllinger. Diekamp’s pre-conciliar dating displaced it; Tanner’s edition prints the acts without the fifteen; Crouzel observed that they “do not appear in the official minutes of that Council.” The council’s eight sessions, as transmitted, contain no sitting at which the fifteen were put and passed.4

The eleventh canon’s naming of Origen looks secondary. The council’s own eleventh anathema does contain his name — “If anyone does not anathematize Arius, Eunomius, Macedonius, Apollinarius, Nestorius, Eutyches and Origen, as well as their heretical books… let him be anathema” — but three features tell against its originality. Origen stands last, out of the chronological order the other six observe: Arius to Eutyches run from the 320s to the 440s, and the man from the 230s is placed after all of them. The parallel roll in Justinian’s Homonoia, the draft of the anathemas from the imperial chancery, does not have him. And Pope Vigilius’s own documents never name Origen. Ramelli takes the three together as evidence that the name “did not belong to the official acts of the council, but w[as] interpolated later” — the more so because the Greek acts of 553 are lost and the council survives only in a Latin version, and the fathers of 680 already suspected the Greek acts had been tampered with.5

Even the earlier “condemnations” were not conciliar. The nine anathemas of 543, often called the Synod of Constantinople’s, were in fact issued as an imperial edict of Justinian and sent to the patriarchs; their authority, in Price’s words, was “imperial rather than synodal.” So the document that first put the charges in numbered, canonical form was the emperor’s, not a council’s — which is exactly the pattern the argument says continued in 553.6

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The narrow, source-critical claim is well grounded and now mainstream. The fifteen anathemas cannot be shown to have been passed in the council’s recorded sessions; the manuscript attribution is a seventeenth-century caption; the standard critical edition omits them; and the naming of Origen in the eleventh canon is at least open to the interpolation charge. Anyone who says flatly “the eight sessions of 553, as transmitted, contain a formal ecumenical anathema of Origen the man” is asserting more than the documents support. To that extent the interpretation is sound.

“Not the council” is not “not the bishops.” The prevailing reconstruction does not make the fifteen a private or freelance document. On Diekamp’s and Price’s account they were the work of the súnodos endemousa — the home synod of bishops resident at Constantinople, essentially the same episcopate that then constituted the ecumenical council — acting at Justinian’s initiative in the same weeks and the same city. That relocates the anathemas from the numbered sessions to a synodical sitting of the conciliar fathers; it does not make them the act of no synod at all. Price, who holds the home-synod dating, resists the apologetic leap from it: the condemnation of Origenism in 553 was a real synodical act of the assembled bishops, not a fiction to be dissolved by a technicality about which sitting recorded it.7

The earliest narrative witness says the fathers condemned him. Cyril of Scythopolis, a Judaean monk writing within five years of the council, closes his Life of Sabas by reporting that the assembled fathers issued a common and universal anathema against Origen along with Theodore, and against the teaching of Evagrius and Didymus, with the four patriarchs consenting. He is a partisan of the party that had petitioned for exactly that outcome — a summary of what one side believed it had won — but he is also the earliest and best-placed source for what the assembly did, and he took it for granted that the fathers of the council had condemned Origen personally.8

The interpolation charge on the eleventh canon is possible, not proven. The Latin acts — the only full text there is — carry Origen’s name; the older editors saw “no possible reason to doubt” that it stood there in 553. Absence from the imperial Homonoia draft and silence in Vigilius’s polemics are consistent with interpolation but do not require it: a loyalty formula listing already-condemned heretics need not match a chancery draft word for word, and Vigilius’s Constituta were arguing a different quarrel (the Three Chapters), where Origen had no place. The name being last and out of order is the one real anomaly, and it is as compatible with a deliberate appendage by the council as with a later hand. So the eleventh canon yields a genuine doubt, not a demonstrated forgery.9

Two undisputed ecumenical councils supply the reception the 553 text is short of. Whatever the acts of 553 contained, Constantinople III (680–681) refers to “the fifth holy synod… which was gathered here against Theodore of Mopsuestia, Origen, Didymus and Evagrius,” and Nicaea II (787) anathematizes “the mythical speculations of Origen, Evagrius and Didymus, as did the fifth synod.” Both are ratifications by reference rather than fresh trials, and neither text is in doubt. They establish that from the seventh century two ecumenical councils named Origen personally and understood the fifth to have condemned him — so the claim “no ecumenical council ever anathematized Origen” is false even if the whole of the 553 apparatus is set aside. (Ramelli notes that 680 itself already suspected the 553 acts of interpolation, which cuts both ways: it weakens 553’s text as a witness and shows the seventh century reading Origen’s personal condemnation as settled all the same.)10

Assessment

contested. The argument is strong exactly where it is narrow and fails where it is made to prove too much. It is true, and now the majority editorial position, that the fifteen anathemas against Origen cannot be attributed to the recorded sessions of the Fifth Ecumenical Council, and arguable that Origen’s name in the eleventh canon is secondary — so the tidy statement that the council in formal session personally anathematized the man is not securely documented. But the popular conclusion built on this — that no ecumenical council ever condemned Origen, and by extension that his condemnation (and that of universal salvation) can be waved away — does not follow. On every reconstruction short of outright forgery the fifteen were issued by the same bishops assembled for the council, sitting as a synod; the earliest narrative witness reports the fathers condemning him by name; the only full text of the eleventh canon carries his name; and two later, undisputed ecumenical councils name him personally and attribute his condemnation to 553. What would change the verdict is concrete and known: the recovery of the lost Greek acts of 553, which would show whether the fifteen were put to a session and whether the eleventh canon named Origen in the original. Until then the defensible reading is the moderate one — a formal in-session ecumenical anathema of Origen the man is not on the record, but a conciliar condemnation of him is, and the church’s ecumenical tradition has treated it as such since the seventh century. The distinction is real and worth keeping; the exculpation usually squeezed from it is not.
