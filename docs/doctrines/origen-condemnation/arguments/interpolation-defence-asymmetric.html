Did the “heretical interpolation” defence cut both ways — invoked to keep Gregory of Nyssa orthodox, refused to keep Origen’s book his own?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — the comparative, philological form of the fairness question. The same textual move — the offending passages are not authentic — was pressed once to keep a canonized saint orthodox and once (and rejected) to keep a condemned man’s book his own. To rescue Gregory of Nyssa from his own apokatastasis, Germanus of Constantinople argued that every universal-restoration passage in Nyssa had been interpolated by Origenist heretics; to convict Origen, the mirror plea — that his heresies were the interpolations — was refused. The asymmetry is real and vivid, and it is genuine evidence that the tradition’s judgement fell by person rather than by text: the same doctrine, drawn from the same verse, cost Origen an anathema and cost Nyssa nothing. But whether it is a philological double standard overstates, because neither interpolation thesis actually held — Germanus’s is rejected by the same scholarship that rejects Rufinus’s, Nyssa’s survival never in fact depended on it, and if his text was tampered with at all the tampering ran the other way, toward minimizing his universalism. So the comparandum sharpens the person-not-proposition finding without adding a distinct verdict of textual favouritism.

See also its home question, was Origen singled out? (the condemned views measured against who else held them — of which this is the philological instance), and, across the Latin quarrel it mirrors, were Origen’s heterodox passages heretical interpolations? (Rufinus’s thesis, rated weak). On whether Nyssa’s apokatastasis is itself sound and was condemnable in Origen’s day, see the restoration argument.

The claim

Gregory of Nyssa taught the final restoration of all rational creatures, the devil included, and was never condemned for it — he was crowned “father of the fathers” by the seventh ecumenical council. Origen taught the same restoration, from the same verse (1 Corinthians 15:28, “that God may be all in all”), and was anathematized for it. Faced with the embarrassment of a saint who held a condemned doctrine, Germanus, patriarch of Constantinople (d. 733), wrote a treatise — the Antapodotikos, now lost but summarized by Photius in the Bibliotheca — arguing that Nyssa never really held it: the offending passages were forgeries planted by Origenists.

… those who held that the devils and lost souls would one day be set free had dared “to instill into the pure and most healthful spring of his [Gregory’s] writings the black and dangerous poison of the error of Origen, and to cunningly attribute this foolish heresy to a man famous alike for his virtue and his learning.”

— Germanus of Constantinople, Antapodotikos, as reported by Photius, Bibliotheca cod. 233, and quoted in the Catholic Encyclopedia, “Apocatastasis”.1 Photius’s own summary preserves the same charge: the detractors “have attempted to mix into his works, full of the light of salvation… troubled and disastrous ideas from the dreams of Origen”, and “sometimes by faked additions, sometimes by their relentless efforts to pervert correct thinking, they have attempted to falsify many of his works which were beyond reproach.”2 The claim weighed here is not Germanus’s text-criticism as such — that is easily settled — but the comparative one it exposes: that the church reached for “interpolation” to protect a venerated figure and declined it for a condemned one, which is evidence of a person-driven rather than a text-driven judgement.

Who advances it

The interpolation-for-Nyssa thesis is Germanus’s, and it had a long afterlife: it “originated with Germanus… in harmony with a favorite mode of opposition to Universalism” and was still being repeated in the nineteenth century as the standard way of squaring a canonized saint with a condemned doctrine. The mirror plea — that Origen’s heresies, not his orthodoxy, were the graft — is Rufinus’s, pressed a generation into the Latin controversy and rated weak on its own page. Setting the two side by side is the move modern historians of the controversy make: the same philological instrument, turned to opposite ends by which reputation it served. Ilaria Ramelli frames the underlying puzzle directly — “Why was St Gregory of Nyssa never condemned for his doctrine of apokatastasis?” — and answers it without any recourse to Germanus’s forgery-thesis.3

The evidence for

The parallel is exact, and the outcome opposite. Both theses make the identical structural claim — a revered author cannot have written the heresy that stands in his book, so a heretic must have written it — and both name the same heresy (the restoration of all, the salvation of the devil) and the same culprit (Origenists). Yet the one protected a saint from a doctrine and the other failed to save a condemned man’s book. And the asymmetry it points to is not manufactured: Nyssa and Origen taught the same apokatastasis from the same Pauline verse, and the tradition treated them in flatly opposite ways — Origen anathematized by name at Constantinople in 553, Nyssa honoured as a doctor and cited as an authority by the very councils that condemned Origenism. That much of the person-not-proposition finding is independently established: enforcement fell on Origen the man and spared Nyssa the man, though their doctrine was one. Germanus’s treatise is the sharpest single illustration of it — a patriarch driven to invent a forgery rather than admit that a saint held what a council had condemned.

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The comparandum is genuine and does real work. The tradition’s verdict on the restoration of all did not track the doctrine, which was constant; it tracked the reputation of who held it. Origen, who died in communion but was posthumously anathematized, is convicted of it; Nyssa, whose authority was by the eighth century unassailable, is defended from it by any means available — including the invention of an interpolating heretic. That the same philological device could be deployed to opposite verdicts, depending only on whose orthodoxy needed saving, is a vivid confirmation that the judgement was person-driven. It also lands a fair hit on the interpolation defence generally: an instrument that can absolve a saint of a real doctrine can convict a text of a forged one, and neither result is controlled by the evidence.

What is overstated or imprecise. But a philological double standard — the church believed the interpolation move for Nyssa and disbelieved it for Origen — is not what happened, on three counts.

Both theses failed alike as text-criticism. Germanus’s forgery-thesis is rejected by the same scholarship that rejects Rufinus’s: the Catholic Encyclopedia grants that “the explanation given by St. Germanus… cannot hold”, and modern editors treat Nyssa’s apokatastasis as integral and authentic, woven through the De anima et resurrectione, the Catechetical Oration and the In illud: Tunc et ipse Filius, exactly as Origen’s is woven through his authenticated Greek. The move was wrong in both cases, for the same reason — the doctrine is load-bearing, not intrusive.3 So the church did not reward one interpolation thesis and punish the other; it (eventually) recognized both as false.

Nyssa’s survival never depended on Germanus. Germanus wrote in the early eighth century, long after Nyssa’s reputation was settled; his was a private apologetic rationalization, never a conciliar ruling, and the real reasons Nyssa escaped condemnation are the ones Ramelli marshals — his towering authority as the theologian behind the Constantinopolitan settlement of 381, and the assimilability of his restoration-hope to the developing doctrine of purgatorial purification. A saint who was never going to be condemned did not need a forgery-thesis to save him; Germanus supplied one anyway, which tells us about the discomfort of later readers more than about any official leniency toward the interpolation argument.3

Nyssa’s text was tampered with — in the opposite direction. The one grain of textual truth cuts against Germanus, not for him. If Nyssa’s manuscripts suffered corruption on this doctrine, it was at the hands of copyists who minimized his universalism, inserting glosses to explain that he did not really teach apokatastasis — the reverse of the heretical magnifying Germanus alleged. The transmission history, so far as it is disturbed, disturbed Nyssa toward orthodoxy, not away from it; the “interpolation” that actually occurred is the pious kind, and it undercuts the very thesis Germanus built.3

Assessment

Contested. The comparandum is striking and true as far as it goes: the same “the offending passages are not authentic” move was reached for to keep Gregory of Nyssa orthodox and refused to keep Origen’s book his own, and the underlying asymmetry — one doctrine, one verse, opposite verdicts turning on whose reputation was at stake — is real evidence that the tradition judged the restoration by person, not by text. That much belongs to, and reinforces, the singling-out finding. But read as a philological double standard — the church crediting interpolation for the saint while denying it for the heretic — it overstates. Neither interpolation thesis held: Germanus’s is rejected as squarely as Rufinus’s, Nyssa’s escape rested on his authority and the purgatory-analogy rather than on any accepted forgery-claim, and the real tampering in Nyssa’s transmission ran toward suppressing his universalism, not planting it. So the case is genuinely double-edged — a vivid, sound illustration of person-driven judgement, but not an independent verdict of textual favouritism — which is why it rates contested rather than sound.
