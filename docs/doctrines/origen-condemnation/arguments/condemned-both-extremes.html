Did 553 condemn a school, or both extremes at once? Origen and Theodore in the same council

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — a corrective to the reading of 553 as an anti-Alexandrian, anti-allegory purge that swept up Origen. The council that reached Origen the allegorist condemned, in the very next of its own fourteen anathemas, Theodore of Mopsuestia — the leading Antiochene literalist, Origen's exact methodological opposite, the exegete who “rejected the allegorical interpretation” the Alexandrians prized. The two were condemned on unrelated charges (Origen for his cosmology, Theodore for a Christology read as Nestorian), and the datum cuts two ways. It tells decisively against the allegory-hunt reading: a sentence that falls on the arch-allegorist and the arch-literalist in the same sessions is not a faction settling scores with a school. And it reinforces the shared anachronism critique: like Origen, Theodore died in the Church's communion and honour and was condemned more than a century after his death, measured against a standard fixed after he was gone. What the observation does not establish is that the two fell “by the same measure”: Theodore's condemnation was the council's primary business, driven by the Nestorian crisis and Justinian's Three Chapters campaign, while Origen's was a marginal, largely extra-conciliar affair driven by the monastic Origenist war — two separate movements converging on one council, not one even-handed house-cleaning.

See also, on the other forms of the same fairness question: the comparative form (was Origen singled out, measured against who else held his views?) — its closest neighbour, where Theodore appears among the condemned company · the umbrella anachronism question (was he condemned by a standard that did not exist in his lifetime?) · the political form (was the condemnation driven by ecclesiastical politics rather than doctrine?) · the canonical form (was he anathematized by an ecumenical council at all?) · the philological form (did the fifteen anathemas of 553 condemn a sixth-century Evagrian system rather than Origen?).

The claim

If anyone does not anathematize Arius, Eunomius, Macedonius, Apollinarius, Nestorius, Eutyches and Origen, as well as their heretical books… let him be anathema.

If anyone defends the heretical Theodore of Mopsuestia, who said that God the Word is one, while quite another is Christ… let him be anathema.

— Second Council of Constantinople (553), the eleventh and twelfth of its fourteen anathemas.1 Back to back in the council's own capitula stand the two names this page sets side by side: Origen of Alexandria, whose method was the allegorical reading of Scripture pressed to its boldest reach, and Theodore of Mopsuestia, the Antiochene who built his exegesis on the opposite principle and “rejected the allegorical interpretation of the Alexandrians, and adhered to the literal sense.” The interpretation weighed here is the one that reads that juxtaposition as evidence: 553 is often described as an anti-allegorical, anti-Alexandrian reaction that caught Origen up in a purge of a whole school — but the same council anathematized the school's sharpest enemy, on charges that have nothing to do with allegory. So (the argument runs) 553 was no allegory-hunt and no anti-Origen vendetta, but a broad posthumous house-cleaning that condemned the allegorist and the anti-allegorist alike; and since both had died in communion and were reached only long after death, the unfairness of anachronism the timeline presses against the sentence on Origen is not peculiar to him but structural to how this council worked.

Who advances it

The observation is a commonplace of the modern reassessment, made whenever the fifth council's even-handed severity is contrasted with the picture of it as an Alexandrian or an anti-Origenist affair. Justinian's Three Chapters edict and the council that ratified it aimed to conciliate the Miaphysites by condemning the Antiochene figures they most detested — Theodore first among them — so the same emperor whose 543 edict furnished the anti-Origenist anathemas presided over the sentence on Origen's methodological opposite. Frederick McLeod, who has worked on the seventy-one extracts of Theodore read at the council, states the symmetry of fate plainly: Theodore “was acclaimed in the late-fourth and early-fifth centuries as the preeminent exegete of the School of Antioch as well as one of the most respected theologians of his day… One hundred and twenty-five years later in A.D. 553 his christological writings and person were condemned at the Second Council of Constantinople as impious and heretical.”2 The point is used, in its strong form, to argue not merely that 553 was even-handed but that it condemned Origen and Theodore “by the same measure” — a broad, consistent posthumous discipline; that stronger form is weighed below.

The evidence for

The council condemned opposite methods in adjacent breaths. This is the load-bearing datum and it is not in dispute. In the fourteen anathemas the council issued in its own recorded sessions, the eleventh appends Origen's name to the roll of arch-heretics and the twelfth condemns Theodore of Mopsuestia by name and person as the first of the Three Chapters. Origen was the exegete who dehistoricized Eden by a stated rule of divinely-planted “impossibilities” and allegorized paradise, the heavens, and much else; Theodore was the exegete whose commentaries show an “almost exclusively grammatico-historical and realistic explanation,” who denied that the Song of Songs was allegory at all and read the Psalter for its plain historical occasion. If 553 had been an allegory-hunt — a reaction of the literal-minded against Alexandrian excess — the last name one would expect to find in its anathemas is the champion of the literal sense.3 That it condemned him in the same list is the plainest possible evidence that the council was not enforcing a method or purging a school. It was reaching, on unrelated grounds, whatever its own quarrels put in front of it.

The anachronism runs exactly parallel. The complaint the whole timeline presses against the sentence on Origen — that he was judged by a rule of faith fixed after his death — applies to Theodore almost verbatim, and from the opposite side of every controversy. Theodore died in 428, the year Nestorius became bishop of Constantinople, and “during his lifetime Theodore was always regarded as orthodox and as a prominent ecclesiastical author.” His Christology was condemned when it was “measured against the christological stand taken by the Fathers at the Council of Ephesus in 431 and at the Council of Chalcedon in 451” — standards formulated three and more years after he was in his grave, in controversies he did not live to see. Cyril of Alexandria, his fiercest doctrinal opponent, had refused to anathematize him for exactly this reason: he “was, however, unwilling to condemn Theodore, as he had died in peace with the Church.”4 The pattern that the anachronism page traces in Origen — a man honoured in life and condemned generations later for failing a test set after his death — is therefore not a peculiarity of the Origenist quarrel but the recurring shape of the fifth council's posthumous verdicts. Two of its principal victims were dead more than a century before it sat, and one of them the other's exact contrary in method.

Diodore behind Theodore: the same shape a generation earlier. The pattern reaches back one teacher further, though here the evidence thins and must be stated with care. Diodore of Tarsus (d. c. 390), Theodore's master and John Chrysostom's, was the founder of the Antiochene literal school who “rejected the allegorical interpretation of the Alexandrians, and adhered to the literal sense,” and in his own lifetime a byword for orthodoxy: in the decree ratifying the First Council of Constantinople (381), Theodosius I named him a “champion of the faith” and a norm of communion for the whole East. Yet his Christology was posthumously charged with the same Nestorian tendency — a local synod at Constantinople reported for about 499 condemned his views as Nestorian — and his enormous body of writing was so thoroughly suppressed that it survives only in scattered fragments of uncertain provenance. The lifetime standard of orthodoxy became, after death, the fountainhead of the condemned error.5

Adversarial assessment

The both-extremes datum is sound, and it does defeat the allegory-purge reading. The narrow claim — that 553 anathematized the arch-allegorist and the arch-literalist in the same capitula, on unrelated charges — is simply true, and it disposes cleanly of the picture of the fifth council as a reaction against Alexandrian allegory that caught Origen in a methodological net. No reading of the anathemas survives the presence of Theodore's name in them: a council hunting allegory does not condemn the man who spent his life refuting it. To that extent the argument holds without qualification, and it is a genuine correction of a common misdescription.

“By the same measure” overstates a convergence of two separate campaigns. What the even-handedness does not show is that the two condemnations were one consistent act of discipline applied impartially to both flanks. They were not. Theodore's condemnation was the council's declared and primary business: the Three Chapters were Justinian's instrument for reconciling the Miaphysites, Theodore was the first Chapter, condemned by name and person in the recorded sessions, his seventy-one extracts read into the acts and his whole literary output ordered destroyed after the council.6 Origen's condemnation was, by comparison, a marginal and largely extra-conciliar affair: his name appears in the council proper only as the last, out-of-order entry appended to a stock list of old heretics in the eleventh canon, while the fifteen detailed anti-Origenist anathemas usually cited — the ones that describe an actual system — “cannot be attributed to this council” on the current scholarly reading and belong to a preliminary synod, as the canonical page sets out. The two sentences sprang from two unrelated crises — the Nestorian controversy and the imperial politics of the Three Chapters on one side, the monastic Origenist war in the Judaean desert on the other — that happened to converge on the same assembly for reasons of their own. “The council condemned both extremes” is true; “the council condemned both extremes by one even-handed measure” reads a coherence into the convergence that the history does not support. The even-handedness is a fact about the net, not about a principle.

Diodore is the weakest leg, and reported rather than established. The Diodore parallel is suggestive but must not bear weight. The c. 499 synod is thinly attested; and whether Diodore was condemned at 553 at all is disputed at the source: the standard reference argues that “the damning passages cited by [Marius] Mercator and Leontius seem… to belong to a work of Theodore, not of Diodorus; nor was the latter condemned when Theodore and passages of Theodoret and Ibas (the Three Chapters) were condemned by the Fifth General Council (553).” So the sharpest form of the Diodore case — that the founder of the literal school was condemned alongside Origen the allegorist — may rest on a misattribution, the “Diodoran” passages being really Theodore's. Taken at its careful minimum the parallel still holds — a lifetime champion of orthodoxy whose Christology was posthumously suspected and whose works were lost — but it does not add a third condemned extreme to the two the council's own anathemas supply.7

What the observation establishes, and what it does not. Granted its sound core, the argument yields two real conclusions and forecloses a third. It establishes that 553 was not an anti-allegory or anti-Alexandrian purge — the arch-literalist fell in the same list — and that the anachronism the timeline charges against the sentence on Origen is a recurring feature of the council's posthumous verdicts rather than a grievance peculiar to him. It does not establish that Origen and Theodore were condemned by one impartial measure, for their sentences came from separate movements of unequal weight in the council's business; and it does not, of course, touch the truth of the propositions either man was condemned for. The observation corrects a caricature of the council's motive; it leaves the merits of its verdicts exactly where the charge-pages find them.

Assessment

contested. The core observation is sound and worth making: the council of 553 anathematized Origen the allegorist and Theodore of Mopsuestia the literalist in adjacent anathemas of its own capitula, on wholly unrelated charges, so the reading of 553 as an anti-allegory or anti-Origen purge of an Alexandrian school cannot stand — a council hunting allegory does not condemn its greatest enemy. And the anachronism critique the timeline presses for Origen applies to Theodore verbatim and from the opposite camp: he died honoured and in communion in 428 and was condemned a hundred and twenty-five years later against a Christology fixed at Ephesus and Chalcedon after his death, so the unfairness of posthumous judgement is structural to how the fifth council worked, not a grievance unique to Origen. What overstates is the strong “by the same measure” form: the two condemnations were not one even-handed act but two separate campaigns — the Three Chapters and the Nestorian crisis behind Theodore, the monastic Origenist war behind Origen — converging on one council, with Theodore's condemnation its central business and Origen's a marginal, largely extra-conciliar appendix. The Diodore parallel, which would extend the pattern back a generation, is reported and contested — the passages that would prove his condemnation may belong to Theodore — and cannot carry weight. The even-handedness is a fact about what the council's net caught, not proof of an impartial principle; it refutes the vendetta reading without showing the verdicts just.
