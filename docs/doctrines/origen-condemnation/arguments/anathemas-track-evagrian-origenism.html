Did the fifteen anathemas of 553 condemn sixth-century Evagrian Origenism rather than Origen?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — the philological core is strong and now widely accepted: Antoine Guillaumont's recovery of the unexpurgated Syriac Kephalaia Gnostica (the S2 recension) showed the fifteen anathemas tracking the propositions of Evagrius of Pontus and the sixth-century Palestinian monks — the Isochristoi and Protoktistoi — almost clause for clause, and quoting Peri Archōn nowhere. The spherical resurrection body, the nous-Christ, the final equality of every spirit with Christ: these are Evagrian and monastic, not third-century Origen. But the strong inference — that the man was condemned for a system that was simply “not his” — overstates on three counts. The list opens with the pre-existence of souls and closes with the restoration of all, both of which Origen really did canvass; its third anathema, the ensouled, rational stars, he argues at length in Peri Archōn I.7 and Against Celsus V, so it too is his; the Evagrian system is a radicalizing development of Origen, not an unrelated import, so “not his” understates the continuity; and Justinian's edict of 543 quotes Origen's own Greek at length, so the sixth century was not working only from monastic slogans. The reading that survives: the particular propositions anathematized in 553 are a sixth-century monastic system read back onto Origen's name, but the system's root is genuinely his, and several of the charges reach him directly.

The claim

X. If anyone shall say that after the resurrection the body of the Lord was ethereal, having the form of a sphere, and that such shall be the bodies of all after the resurrection…: let him be anathema.

XII. If anyone shall say that the heavenly Powers and all men and the Devil and evil spirits are united with the Word of God in all respects, as the Nous which is by them called Christ and which is in the form of God, and which humbled itself as they say…: let him be anathema.

XIV. If anyone shall say that all reasonable beings will one day be united in one, when the hypostases as well as the numbers and the bodies shall have disappeared… moreover, that in this pretended apocatastasis, spirits only will continue to exist…: let him be anathema.

— three of the fifteen anathemas against Origen (553), link.1 The interpretation weighed here is the load-bearing modern thesis about the condemnation: that the fifteen anathemas — the fullest and most explicitly Christological statement of what was condemned, and the text most often quoted as “the” condemnation of Origen — describe not the third-century book but the system of the Palestinian Origenist monks whose theology derived from Evagrius of Pontus's Kephalaia Gnostica. On this reading, Origen was anathematized for a scheme assembled three centuries after him, so that the sentence, whatever else it settled, did not fall on his own text.

Who advances it

The thesis is Antoine Guillaumont's. In 1958 he published the editio princeps of the unexpurgated Syriac of the Kephalaia Gnostica — a recension (S2, from a single British Library manuscript) beside which the long-known version (S1) proved to be a doctrinally cleaned-up copy — and in a paper of 1961 and a book of 1962 argued that the fifteen anathemas match propositions standing in S2 point by point, and cite Peri Archōn at no point.2 The demonstration has organized the field since. Henri Crouzel drew the conclusion that the fifteen “do not appear in the official minutes of that Council” and describe a later system; Ilaria Ramelli, who published a fresh English translation of S2 in 2015, states that in the anathemas “Origen is considered to be the inspirer of the so-called Isochristoi”; and Richard Price, editing the acts of 553, stresses that the council's real quarry was a living sixth-century monastic movement with Origen's name attached to it.3 In its popular form the claim is pressed harder — into the flat verdict that 553 “did not condemn Origen at all” — and that stronger form is weighed below.

The evidence for

The fifteen describe a system, and the system is Evagrian. Read straight through, the anathemas set out an interlocking scheme with a beginning, a fall, a graded cosmos, and a return: an original unity of undifferentiated minds (henad); a fall by degrees into graded bodies; ensouled heavenly bodies; a nous that did not fall and remained united to the Word, “by them called Christ”; a spherical, ethereal resurrection body; and an end in which every spirit becomes equal to Christ and hypostases, numbers and bodies alike disappear. That is the doctrine of the two Origenist parties Cyril of Scythopolis names from inside the Judaean lauras — the Protoktistoi or “first-created,” who held Christ's mind alone never fell, and the Isochristoi or “those equal to Christ,” who held that at the restoration all minds become his equals — and its textual source is Evagrius, not Origen.4 The spherical body, the nous-Christ, and the Isochrist equality are the clearest cases: none is set out in Peri Archōn, and each stands in the Kephalaia Gnostica. (The ensouled heavenly bodies are the exception that proves the point: their systematic setting is Evagrian, but the bare proposition — living, rational luminaries — Origen does argue in Peri Archōn I.7, so the anathema catches a genuine Origenist doctrine dressed in a later apparatus.) The anathemas, in short, are answering a book — but the book is largely Evagrius's.

Origen's own name is in none of them. The fifteen condemn propositions, not a man: from the first (“the fabulous pre-existence of souls”) to the fifteenth (the end “like to the life which was in the beginning”), Origen is nowhere named. Where his name does stand in the conciliar material — the eleventh canon's roll of arch-heretics — it is a bare loyalty formula that passes no charge and states no doctrine, and whether it stood there in 553 is itself disputed, since the parallel roll in Justinian's Homonoia omits him.5 The document that actually enumerates the condemned doctrines never once anchors them to Origen's text.

The philology reversed a printed illusion. The impression that the anathemas quote Origen accurately was itself partly an artefact. Paul Koetschau's 1913 edition of Peri Archōn, persuaded that Origen's opponents preserved what Rufinus had suppressed, filled the gaps in the Latin from Justinian's edict and from the anathemas of 553 — so that the prosecution's brief was printed inside the defendant's book, and for eighty years the standard English Peri Archōn read as though the two were one. John Behr's Oxford edition of 2017 undid the splice and relegated the disputed material to an appendix. A great deal of what looked like proof that the anathemas quoted Origen was the anathemas quoting themselves.6

The occasion was a monastic war, not a reading of Origen. The sixth-century revival was Palestinian and monastic, and its trigger was a fight between rival parties of monks, not anything in the third-century corpus. Sabas of the Great Laura went to Constantinople in 531 to ask for help against the Origenist current in the lauras; his successor expelled them in 537; they regrouped at the New Laura, and two of their party, Domitian and Theodore Askidas, were made bishops and stayed at court. The condemnation legislated against that present disorder and reached back for a name. It is the difference between disinterring a dead man to try him and outlawing a living movement under a dead man's banner.

Adversarial assessment

The list opens on ground that is genuinely Origen's. The first anathema condemns “the fabulous pre-existence of souls, and… the monstrous restoration which follows from it” — and the pre-existent descent of the soul is a doctrine Origen really taught, argued at length in Peri Archōn, and the restoration of all rational creatures is one he really canvassed, from the same proof-text (“that God may be all in all”) that Gregory of Nyssa used.7 Those are not Evagrian imports; they are the roots the Evagrian system grew from, and they are in the third-century book. The clean form of the thesis — “the anathematized doctrines are not Origen's” — is true of the spherical body and the Isochrist equality and false of the two propositions the list is built around. What can be said without qualification is narrower: the fifteen state Origen's premises in a systematized, sixth-century, Evagrian form he did not give them.

“Evagrian” is a continuity, not an alibi. Evagrius was a self-conscious Origenist; the Kephalaia Gnostica is a radicalization of Peri Archōn, not a rival import from outside the tradition. To show that the anathemas track Evagrius rather than Origen is therefore to show that they track a development of Origen — which relocates the authorship of the system by three centuries without severing it from its source. The move that clears Origen of the sixth-century formulations cannot clear him of the seeds, because the seeds are demonstrably his and the plant grew in a straight line. (The point cuts the other way too: the qualification pressed since Guillaumont — Daniel Hombergen's argument that anathemas framed a century and a half after Evagrius's death are a poor blueprint for Evagrius either, and Ramelli's that Evagrius does not belong with those who radicalized Origen — loosens the tie to Evagrius as much as to Origen, and leaves the condemned system harder than ever to pin on any one author.)8

The sixth century did read his actual text. The thesis is strongest for the fifteen anathemas and weakest for the proceedings as a whole, because the fifteen are not the only sixth-century document. Justinian's edict to Menas of 543 quotes Peri Archōn at length in the original Greek and appends nine anathemas drawn from it — and the first and ninth of those (the pre-existent fall, the temporary punishment of the wicked) reach doctrines Origen really held. The sixth-century authorities were not working only from monastic slogans; they had the book, cited it, and condemned things that were in it. The fifteen of 553 travel further into the monastic system than the nine of 543, but the campaign that produced both had the third-century text in front of it.9

Two questions are being run together. “Do the fifteen anathemas describe Origen's own teaching?” and “Was Origen justly condemned?” are different questions, and the popular form of the thesis slides from the first to the second. Even granting that the fifteen describe a later system, it does not follow that the Church condemned no doctrine of Origen's: it condemned pre-existence and restoration in 543 out of his own text, and it read 553 — rightly or not — as reaching him personally, as the later councils that name the Origen–Didymus–Evagrius triad show. The philology settles what the anathemas describe; it does not by itself settle whether the man behind the system was fairly caught by it. That larger question is the umbrella fairness argument, weighed on its own page.

Assessment

contested. The philological core is sound and is no longer seriously disputed: Guillaumont established that the fifteen anathemas of 553 track the Evagrian system of the sixth-century Palestinian monks and quote Peri Archōn nowhere, and the most distinctive condemned propositions — the spherical body, the nous-Christ, the final equality of every spirit with Christ — are demonstrably not in Origen's surviving text. To that extent the thesis holds, and it is the single most important thing modern scholarship has established about the condemnation. What it does not carry is the strong exculpatory form in which it usually circulates. The list is framed by the pre-existence of souls and the restoration of all, both of which Origen genuinely taught, and its third article, the ensouled, rational stars, he canvasses in his own words; the Evagrian system is a lineal development of his thought, not an unrelated graft, so “condemned for what was not his” understates the continuity; and Justinian's edict of 543 quotes Origen's own Greek and condemns doctrines that stand in it. The reading that survives is the moderate one: the particular propositions anathematized in 553 are a sixth-century monastic system read back onto Origen's name, and to that degree he was condemned for a scheme built after him — but the scheme's premises are his own, two of the charges reach him directly, and the sixth century had his book open while it judged. The anathemas misname the author of the system; they do not miss him entirely.
