Did Origen teach that God and all rational creatures are “of one and the same essence” — a creaturely consubstantiality that dissolves the line between Creator and creature?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — Jerome’s climactic charge in Letter 124 is a real quotation put to a false use. Origen did write that rational beings which share the “intellectual light” are “of one nature and of one essence,” and Jerome reproduces the sentence with fair accuracy. But the sentence is a step in an argument for the immortality of the soul, built on the word participation — and participation is the one relation that presupposes the participant is not identical with what it participates in. Origen marks a “diversity in the participation,” keeps the Trinity as the light in which creatures merely share, and elsewhere states flatly that rational beings are “not holy in their essence” but only “by participating” — the very Creator/creature distinction the charge says he erased. The “in some sort” hedge Jerome preserves and then mocks is doing exactly this work: it flags a generic kinship of the rational kind, not a numerical consubstantiality with God. The charge earns weak rather than unsupported only because it fastens on genuine Origenian material — the soul’s kinship as image, the pre-existent unity of minds and their fall from it, and “God all in all” as the term of restoration — that a hostile reader can push toward monism. This is a distinct charge from the subordination of the Son: there the complaint is that Origen ranked the persons of the Trinity too far apart; here it is that he brought God and creatures too close together — and Jerome presses the two as a paradox, that Origen denies the Son and Spirit are consubstantial with the Father yet grants the divine nature to angels and men.

The claim

The charge is Jerome’s, and he makes it the crescendo of his catalogue of Origen’s errors in Letter 124 to Avitus (c. 409–410), a section-by-section digest of the lost Greek Peri Archōn. Reaching the treatise’s closing recapitulation, Jerome renders Origen thus:

God and His only-begotten Son and the Holy Spirit are of an intellectual and reasonable nature. But so also are the angels, the powers, and the virtues, as well as the inward man who is created in the image and after the likeness of God. From which I conclude that God and they are in some sort of one essence.

— then pounces on the qualifier: “He adds in some sort to escape the charge of blasphemy.”1 Jerome then sharpens the paradox that makes the charge sting: Origen, he says, “will not allow the Son and the Holy Spirit to be of one substance with the Father, lest by so doing he should appear to make the divine essence divisible” — yet “here bestows the nature of God almighty upon angels and men.” The interpretation weighed here is that this is Origen’s own teaching: a creaturely consubstantiality or monism in which the difference between the uncreated God and the created rational soul dissolves — the henad from which minds fell, and to which they return, so nearly divine that pantheism is the honest name for it. The allied form of the charge, that Origen makes even Christ’s human soul “consubstantial with God,” Theophilus of Alexandria pressed in his festal letters and Jerome relayed in Letter 98.2

The evidence for

The quotation is genuine, and rare in being double-attested. Most of the sharpest lines in Origen’s dossier survive only in a hostile Greek excerpt or only in Rufinus’s softening Latin, and the two seldom overlap. This one does. Rufinus’s Latin of the same recapitulation reads, of rational natures that share the intellectual light: “human souls, have partaken of the same light and wisdom, and thus are mutually of one nature and of one essence.”3 Jerome, translating the Greek independently and with the opposite motive, arrives at the same “one essence.” Where the friendly and the hostile transmitter agree, the wording is as secure as anything in Peri Archōn — so the charge cannot be dismissed, as several others can, by pleading that we cannot check the words against Origen’s page.

Origen really does hold a native kinship of the rational soul with God. The soul is made “in the image and after the likeness of God”; the nous has a “capacity for God”; and in the pre-cosmic beginning all rational minds were created equal in a single unity, falling into their present diversity only by the cooling of their love — a scheme in which the creature’s original and final condition lies very near the divine. When Origen makes the term of all history “God all in all” (1 Cor 15:28), a reader can hear absorption. So the charge is not spun from nothing: it seizes a real center of gravity in Origen’s thought and names it consubstantiality.

Adversarial assessment

The sentence is a proof of immortality, and its engine is participation — the opposite of identity. Read in place, the recapitulation is arguing that the rational soul cannot perish. Its logic: whatever partakes of a thing is “of one nature” with what it partakes of; minds partake of the “intellectual light” that is the divine nature; that light is “incorruptible and eternal”; therefore the mind that shares it “should last for ever, and be incorruptible and eternal.”4 The whole inference runs on participation (metousia) — and to participate in something is by definition not to be it. The eye is “of one nature” with all it lights because it shares the sun’s light; it is not the sun. Origen presses the illustration precisely to insert degree and difference: “every eye does not equally share in the light,” and “a diversity is to be noted in the participation of Father, Son, and Holy Spirit, varying with the degree of zeal or capacity of mind.” A “one essence” that admits of more and less, and that one climbs toward by zeal, is not the indivisible, numerically single ousia that consubstantiality means. It is a generic unity — all rational beings belong to one kind, the kind made to receive God — which is exactly what the “in some sort” (quodammodo) flags. Jerome quotes the hedge and treats it as a guilty dodge; it is in fact the precise qualification the argument requires.

Origen states the Creator/creature distinction the charge says he erased. The reading collapses if one turns two chapters back, where Origen sets out the graded dependence of creatures on the Trinity: rational beings “are not holy in their essence” but “are rendered holy by participating” in the Holy Spirit; they “derive their existence from God the Father,” “their rational nature from the Word,” “their holiness from the Holy Spirit.”5 This is the inverse of monism: the Trinity is being, reason, and holiness and does not participate in them; the creature has them only by receiving them, and can lose them. In the very passage Jerome quarries, the Trinity is “the nature of Father, and Son, and Holy Spirit, whose intellectual light alone all created things have a share” — the source in which creatures share, categorically distinguished from the sharers. Origen draws the line between God and creation exactly where the charge says he rubbed it out.

Jerome’s “paradox” equivocates on the word essence. The debater’s point — that Origen denies the Son and Spirit are “of one substance” with the Father yet grants the divine nature to men — trades on two different senses of ousia. Origen shied from homoousios of the Son because in his day the word carried a materialist freight — it suggested dividing the Godhead into portions, as the Valentinians divided their emanations — a worry three generations of ante-Nicene writers shared before Nicaea repurposed the term. That is a claim about the internal unity of the Godhead. The creaturely “one essence” is a claim about the generic kinship of rational natures by participation. To set the two side by side as a contradiction is to make Origen say “same substance” in one sense and deny it in another, then charge him with inconsistency — a contradiction manufactured by the equivocation, not found in the text.

What survives is a real tendency, not the stated doctrine. The steelman of the charge is not the “one essence” sentence, which will not bear it, but the deeper architecture the sentence sits in: Origen’s pre-existent unity of minds, their fall from a near-divine likeness, and their restoration to it. That architecture does hold the creature perilously close to God at the beginning and the end of its story, and a monist could quarry it — as the later Evagrian “equality of every mind with Christ” in fact did. But Origen himself never draws the consubstantialist conclusion; he blocks it with participation, degree, free will, and the persistence of individual substances (“every substance which partakes of that eternal nature should last for ever” — last, not be dissolved into God). So the charge catches a genuine gravitational pull in his system and misnames it as his teaching. The pull is his; the doctrine of a creaturely consubstantiality is Jerome’s inference, hung on a hedge Origen inserted to prevent it.

Assessment

weak. The interpretation — that Origen taught God and all rational creatures to be “of one and the same essence,” a consubstantiality dissolving the Creator/creature line — does not hold on its best case. Its one hard proof-text is an argument for the soul’s immortality whose whole force is participation, the relation that presupposes the participant is not what it shares in; Origen builds degree and diversity into it, hedges it with “in some sort,” and two chapters earlier states plainly that creatures are “not holy in their essence” but only by receiving from a Trinity that alone is what they merely share — the Creator/creature distinction the charge denies him. Jerome’s sharpening paradox (homoousion refused to the Son, granted to men) survives only by equivocating on ousia. It earns weak and not unsupported because it fastens on a real feature — the soul’s native kinship as image, the pre-existent henad, and “God all in all” — that leans toward monism and that Origen’s successors did push there; but Origen himself blocks the step, and the “one essence” sentence is a hostile inference from a hedged proof of immortality, not the pantheism the charge names. Flag on the proponent’s side: Jerome quotes accurately and then reads against the plain sense of the argument he has just transcribed, converting a qualified generic kinship into consubstantiality by suppressing the participation-logic that governs it. See also was Origen’s subordination of the Son a pre-Nicene idiom?, the mirror charge (the persons ranked too far apart, not God and creatures too close), and was Origen condemned by a standard that did not exist in his lifetime?, the umbrella fairness question — noting that this charge, unlike most, fails not on anachronism but on the reading of the text itself.
