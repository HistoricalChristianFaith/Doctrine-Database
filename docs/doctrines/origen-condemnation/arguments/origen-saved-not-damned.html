Is it more reasonable to believe Origen saved than damned?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — the question turns on something no evidence can settle, a soul’s final state, so what is really being weighed is which belief is more reasonable; and there the case is genuinely divided. On one side the personal facts are strong and undisputed: Origen died in communion with the Church, a tortured confessor who did not recant, and no church excommunicated him for his teaching in his lifetime; his gravest speculations were offered where the rule of faith of his day had defined nothing; heresy in the technical sense requires pertinacity, and no one can show he ever thought he had erred; and the conciliar sentence three centuries later fell on propositions only loosely his, in a text whose attribution to the council is itself contested. On the other side stands the Church’s determination as a determination — Pico’s own censors called his thesis “rash… savouring of heresy, and contrary to the determination of the universal Church” — together with the posthumous anathema of the man in the eleventh canon of 553, which, if genuine, is not nothing. Neither side can convert its case into a demonstration: possibility that he is saved is not proof, and a partisan or later condemnation is not disproof. The modest, comparative form Pico actually stated — that it is more reasonable to believe him saved than damned — is the defensible one; the maximal form, a positive certainty of his salvation, is not available to anyone. Five centuries of Catholic and Protestant disputants on both sides, with no resolution and none possible on the evidence, is what “contested” names.

Related arguments: the verdict on the man, not the doctrine — see also Was Origen himself anathematized by an ecumenical council? · Did Rome in 400–401 condemn Origen’s books only, and not the man?. The umbrella fairness question this presupposes is whether Origen was condemned by a standard that did not exist in his lifetime; the plea that his speculations were inquiries, not assertions, is weighed on its own page.

The claim

It is more reasonable to believe that Origen is saved than to believe that he is damned.

— Giovanni Pico della Mirandola, the last of the twenty-nine theses he offered on his own theological opinion in the nine hundred Conclusiones published for public disputation at Rome in December 1486 (“Rationabilius est credere Origenem esse salvum, quam credere ipsum esse damnatum”).1 The interpretation weighed here is the one Pico stated and defended, and it is worth stating precisely, because its force lies in its modesty. It is not the claim that Origen’s condemned doctrines are true; almost no one has held that, and this wiki does not. Nor is it the claim, which no one could establish, that his salvation is a demonstrable fact. It is a comparative claim about which belief the evidence makes more reasonable — that a man who died in the Church’s communion, honoured as a confessor, whose errors were speculations in questions the Church had not yet closed and who was never shown to have held them obstinately, is more reasonably thought saved than damned. The whole timeline of the condemnation is, on this reading, a series of verdicts on his books and his doctrine; the verdict on the man’s eternal state is a separate question, and one the evidence tilts the other way.

Who advances it

Pico put the personal question to a formal disputation and was censured for it — the one point in this whole history where that happened. A commission appointed by Innocent VIII in February 1487 examined the nine hundred theses and indicted thirteen: six it graded “suspect,” seven it condemned, and the Origen thesis was among the condemned. Pico reports the censure in his censors’ own words — the conclusion was “rash and reprehensible, and savouring of heresy, and contrary to the determination of the universal Church” (“temeraria et reprehendenda, et haeresim sapiens, et contra determinationem universalis Ecclesiae”) — and the grade matters: haeresim sapiens, “savouring of heresy,” is the suspect grade, not a flat declaration that the thesis is heretical. He answered it with a treatise, the Disputatio de salute Origenis, the longest section of his 1487 Apologia, laid out in seven articles of which the fifth is the question itself.2 Innocent VIII then condemned all nine hundred theses together in a bull of 4 August 1487 — which does not enumerate them and does not name Origen — and Alexander VI absolved Pico in 1493.3 The reading did not begin or end with him: it is the same judgement Henry More would make in 1662, disposing of the fifth council in a sentence that concedes its authority while voiding its verdict, and the same one the Jesuit Pierre Halloix pressed in the Origenes defensus of 1648 — a book Rome put on the Index — so that the roster of Origen’s early-modern defenders is, tellingly, a list of Catholics.9

The evidence for

He died in the Church’s communion, a confessor. This is the fact everything else rests on. In the Decian persecution Origen was arrested and tortured — “bonds and bodily tortures… under the iron collar and in the dungeon” — under a judge who strove to keep him alive so that he would recant, and he did not. He survived, broken, and died a few years later at Tyre, where he was buried. No church had excommunicated him for his teaching; he was never tried in his lifetime for the doctrines later condemned; and the next generation of Greek theologians were his pupils. Whatever the later verdicts on his books, the man himself ended inside the Church, holding the faith under torture.4

Anachronism: he was not bound to a rule that did not yet exist. Pico’s first argument is the one the whole timeline poses. On the point that mattered most, the origin of the soul, “Origen was not bound in his own time to believe explicitly all that we are now bound to believe about the soul, because it was neither set down plainly in Scripture nor yet determined by the Church as it was afterwards” (“Origenes non omnia tenebatur suo tempore explicite de anima credere, quae nos nunc tenemur”). Culpability for holding an opinion the Church later closed is measured by the standard in force when it was held, and on the soul’s origin, the risen body, the fate of the damned and the inner relations of the Trinity the rule of faith of the third century had defined nothing — a point argued in its own right as the umbrella fairness question.5

Non-obstinacy: heresy requires pertinacity, and none is shown. Pico’s second argument is the technical one, and it is the strongest. In the settled definition a heretic is not merely one who holds an erroneous opinion but one who holds it pertinaciter — obstinately, against the known judgement of the Church. No one can produce evidence that Origen repented of what he wrote, Pico grants; but neither can anyone produce evidence that he ever thought he had erred, and without that there is no pertinacity to convict. The preface to Peri Archōn supplies the positive side of the same point: Origen there divides the plainly-delivered faith from the questions the apostles left open, assigns his hardest speculations — the origin of the soul, the nature of the devil — to the second class, and frames them as investigations rather than fixed decisions. A man who labels his own speculations as inquiries into open questions is, by the Church’s own definition of heresy, not a heretic in the culpable sense — the genre plea weighed separately.6

The conciliar sentence fell on doctrine, and its attribution to the man is contested. The propositions anathematized in 543 and 553 are only loosely Origen’s — the fullest list, the fifteen anathemas, describes a sixth-century Evagrian system nowhere set out in his surviving text — and the fifteen cannot be shown to have been passed in the recorded sessions of the fifth council at all, while Origen’s name in its eleventh canon is arguably a later insertion. So the one act usually taken to settle the man’s status condemns books and opinions, and its personal reach is exactly what is in dispute. The same distinction runs back through the whole story: what the synods of 400 condemned was “the books of Origen,” and where Rome added “and their author” the pope who signed it wrote that he neither knew nor wished to know who Origen was.

Adversarial assessment

The question is, in the strict sense, undecidable — which is why the modest form is the only defensible one. No one, on either side, can demonstrate the eternal state of a soul; that is God’s judgement, not a datum of the historical record. This cuts against the maximal reading before it touches the counter- arguments: “Origen is saved,” asserted as a fact, claims more than any evidence can carry, exactly as “Origen is damned” does. What makes Pico’s thesis serious is that he did not assert the fact. He asserted a comparison — rationabilius, “more reasonable” — and a comparison of reasonableness is something the evidence can bear on. Read as the comparison it is, the thesis is strong; read as the certainty its enemies attacked and some of its friends implied, it is unavailable. Half the five-century quarrel is the two readings talking past each other.

A determination of the Church is still a determination. The heaviest objection is the one Pico’s censors made and Pedro García, commissioned by the curia, worked out at length in the Determinationes magistrales of 1489: the Church has pronounced, and its pronouncement is not dissolved by the observation that it came late. Anachronism bears on Origen’s personal culpability in his own day; it does not unmake the later judgement that the propositions are errors, nor license a private verdict that overrides the public one. García pressed that the conclusion accords with neither reason nor Catholic piety — that to prefer one’s own estimate of a man’s salvation to the Church’s determination is precisely the temerity the censure named. The force of this is real, and it is why the thesis is contested rather than sound; but it has a limit. It establishes that the Church condemned Origen’s doctrine; it does not establish that the Church damned the man, which no ecclesiastical act claims to do.7

The posthumous anathema, if genuine, is not nothing. The eleventh canon of 553 anathematizes Origen by name alongside Arius, Eunomius and the rest, and two later ecumenical councils (680–681, 787) name him and refer his condemnation to the fifth synod. Even granting that the fifteen anathemas were a home-synod act and that the name in canon 11 is doubtful, the Church’s ecumenical tradition has, since the seventh century, treated Origen the man as condemned. That tells against the sanguine reading. What it does not do is settle the soteriological question in the other direction: a conciliar anathema is a judgement on a man’s teaching and his standing in the Church’s memory, not a revelation of his lot before God — the councils anathematize, they do not consign to hell, and the distinction is one the tradition itself keeps. So this consideration lowers the confidence of the “saved” reading without supplying a “damned” one.8

Non-obstinacy is the argument that survives the objections best. Of Pico’s two grounds, the anachronism plea is answered in part — a few charges, the resurrection of the flesh and the end of hell, did meet a standard some contemporaries (Methodius) already applied — but the pertinacity plea is much harder to answer. Heresy in the culpable sense is a settled definition, and it turns on obstinacy against a known judgement of the Church. No source shows Origen defying such a judgement on the points condemned; on the contrary, he flagged them as open questions, submitted his work to the Church’s correction in the very preface, and died before any of the rules he is measured by existed. That a man’s opinions were later judged erroneous is compatible with his having held them without the obstinacy that makes error damnable — and it is the second, not the first, that bears on his eternal state. This is the load-bearing point in Pico’s favour, and none of his censors met it directly.

Assessment

contested. The interpretation is genuinely disputed, with strong and irreducible cases on both sides, and — uniquely among the questions on this timeline — it concerns something no evidence can decide. Taken as Pico stated it, a comparison of reasonableness, it is well grounded: Origen died in communion, a confessor unbroken under torture, never tried in life for the doctrines later condemned; his gravest speculations were offered in questions the Church had not yet closed and were flagged by him as inquiries; heresy in the culpable sense requires an obstinacy no one has shown in him; and the conciliar sentence fell on his books and doctrine, in a text whose personal reach is itself contested. Against that stands the plain fact that the Church did determine the propositions to be errors and did, in its ecumenical tradition, name the man — and a determination is not undone by being late, nor by being entangled (as every stage of this condemnation was) in politics. Neither case can be converted into a proof: the possibility of his salvation is not its demonstration, and a partisan or posthumous condemnation is not its refutation. What tips the balance toward the modest reading, without settling it, is the pertinacity point — the one argument Pico’s opponents never squarely answered — and what holds the verdict short of “sound” is that a man’s final state is not ours to read off the record. So the defensible form is Pico’s own: it is more reasonable to hope, and to believe, Origen saved than damned; the certainty that the label “heretic” is sometimes taken to imply is available to no one, in either direction.
