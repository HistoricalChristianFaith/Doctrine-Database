Were Origen's condemned opinions offered as inquiry rather than assertion, so that the anathemas condemn a genre error?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — the descriptive half is strong and well-grounded: Peri Archōn really is written as an exercise in inquiry (zētēsis). Origen divides the faith into what the apostles delivered plainly and what they left open, assigns his most notorious speculations to the second class, and flags them as he treats them — “investigation and discussion” rather than “fixed and certain decision” — so that reading a hedged hypothesis as a bald dogma does misdescribe the book, and his accusers, who circulated the propositions stripped of their qualifications, did exactly that. The normative half — that the anathemas therefore condemn a genre error, sentencing as doctrines what were never asserted as doctrines — overstates. The disclaimers are uneven: the same book argues the restoration and the succession of ages as settled conclusions, not open questions. A hypothesis, once published in a widely-copied handbook and argued as the necessary reading of Scripture, is still a proposition the Church can judge; the councils were not making a category mistake when they weighed the propositions and found them false. And even a hostile contemporary who preserved the disclaimer — Jerome — judged it no cover for the doctrine under it. The reading that survives is the narrower one: the genre plea is a real and partly-successful mitigation of Origen's culpability, not a demonstration that the propositions escaped judgement on a technicality.

The claim

The cause of the diversity of opinion on these subjects is this, viz., that those subjects on which we discourse are treated by us with great solicitude and caution, in the manner rather of an investigation and discussion, than in that of fixed and certain decision.

— Origen, De Principiis I.6.1 (c. 229), link.1 Origen introduces the restoration of all things — the doctrine the ninth anathema of 543 would condemn — with that sentence, and warns in the same breath that a reader “full of preconceptions and prejudices” may “judge these to be heretical… yielding… not so much to the convictions of reason as to the dogmatism of prejudice.” The interpretation weighed here is the one his defenders have pressed from Pamphilus to the present: that Peri Archōn is an exercise in inquiry, that its author submitted its hard speculations to the Church's judgement rather than asserting them as dogma, and that anathematizing those propositions as doctrines Origen taught mistakes the very genre of the book — condemning a question as though it were an answer.

Who advances it

The defence is as old as the attack. Pamphilus of Caesarea, writing his Apology for Origen from prison around 308–310, made it the hinge of his method. Answering the charges out of Origen's own mouth, he observed that

In the process of explaining these things, his frequent custom is to add an admission that he is not declaring these things by a definitive pronouncement, nor is he defining them as secure dogma, but he is investigating to the best of his ability and discussing the meaning of the Scriptures.

— a reading Pamphilus grounds, explicitly, in the preface to Peri Archōn.2 A second, anonymous apology that Photius read alongside Pamphilus's pressed the same case further, taking “great trouble to prove that they were only intended as a rhetorical exercise.”3 In modern scholarship the genre reading is the framing of Henri Crouzel and Brian Daley, who treat Peri Archōn as a work of theological exploration rather than a system of dogmas, and read its boldest conclusions as inquiries offered under the caution its author announced.4

The evidence for

Origen builds the book on an explicit division between fixed and open teaching. The preface sets it out as a programme. On some points “the holy apostles… delivered themselves with the utmost clearness… leaving, however, the grounds of their statements to be examined into by those who should deserve the excellent gifts of the Spirit”; on others they stated only that a thing is so, leaving the rest “to be investigated.” Into that second class Origen puts, by name, the very matters he was later condemned for canvassing: the origin of the soul, “not distinguished with sufficient clearness in the teaching of the Church”; the nature of the devil and his angels, of whom “the teaching of the Church has laid down that these beings exist indeed; but what they are, or how they exist, it has not explained with sufficient clearness.”5 The speculations are not smuggled in; they are announced as investigations of questions their author took the Church to have left open.

He flags the inquiry as he conducts it, and asks the reader to judge. The reservations are not confined to the preface. Introducing the restoration in I.6.1 he calls the whole subject a matter of “investigation and discussion” rather than “fixed and certain decision.” Having laid out three views of the world's end and the resurrection body in Book II, he refuses to choose for the reader: “let each one of our readers determine for himself, with care and diligence, whether any one of them can be approved and adopted.”6 That is the grammar of a man setting out possibilities for judgement, not handing down articles of faith. Even the closing of the preface describes his aim as constructive rather than dogmatic: to take the Church's fixed points and “form a connected series and body of truths… by means of illustrations and arguments” — a reasoned reconstruction offered for testing, not a decree.7

A hostile witness confirms the disclaimer was really there. The strongest evidence that the hedging is Origen's and not a later apologist's decoration is that his fiercest Latin accuser preserved it while brushing it aside. Jerome, cataloguing the errors of Peri Archōn for Avitus, reports that Origen, having reasoned his way toward the transmigration of souls, “winds up the wicked reasoning… by saying: I must not be taken to make dogmas of these things; they are only thrown out as conjectures.”8 Jerome translated literally and meant to convict; that he nonetheless records the saving clause shows the clause stood in the Greek. The genre reading does not depend on trusting Origen's friends.

His accusers worked from the excerpts, not the frame. The mechanism the defence describes is visible in the record. What circulated against Origen, from the fourth century on, was an anthology of the offending sentences lifted out of the book that hedged them — a practice Pamphilus already complained of, that the malicious “learn with utmost zeal” the passages “that lead to calumny” and disdain the rest.9 The numbered anathemas of 543 and 553 quote propositions; they do not reproduce the announced caution under which the propositions were framed. To that extent the charge is fair: the enforced form of the doctrine is more assertive than the book it came from.

Adversarial assessment

The disclaimers are uneven, and the boldest doctrines are argued as conclusions. The caution is genuine but not uniform, and the defence that rests the whole case on “he was only asking” has to reckon with the stretches where he plainly is not. The same Peri Archōn states the restoration as the settled term of a process: “all things shall be re-established in a state of unity, and… God shall be all in all” — a result “brought about, not suddenly, but slowly and gradually… during the lapse of countless and unmeasured ages.” That is a description of what Origen takes will happen, not a question left for the reader.10 The honest account is that his hardest speculations are announced under a general caution and then argued as the necessary reading of Scripture — more than a bare conjecture, if less than a fixed definition. Where his own reserve lapses, the plea that his critics manufactured his confidence lapses with it.

A submitted hypothesis is still a condemnable proposition. The genre plea proves too much. The Church's doctrinal judgement falls on propositions — on whether a thing said is true — not only on the grammatical mood in which they are put; a false teaching offered as a “conjecture” and copied across the Greek and Latin worlds is not thereby placed beyond assessment. This was the settled ancient answer, and it was not obtuse: Jerome's retort to the disclaimer — that naming a poison a conjecture does not make it safe to drink — is the same principle a court applies when it holds a published speculation to account for its content. The councils weighed the propositions and judged them false; whether Origen asserted them or floated them bears on his guilt, not on the propositions' standing. On that distinction the umbrella anachronism argument turns, and it holds here too: “offered as inquiry” is a point about culpability, not about truth.

The same speculations recur beyond the one hedged book. The reservations are a feature of Peri Archōn in particular; the doctrines are not confined to it. The pre-existent fall of souls is the working premise of Origen's exegesis elsewhere — his reading of the disparities of birth, of Jacob loved and Esau hated before either had done anything, of John the Baptist — where it functions as an explanatory assumption rather than a flagged conjecture. And the continuity he lodges in a transferable prophetic spirit to keep clear of transmigration is argued, not merely floated (weighed on its own page). A doctrine that surfaces as a load-bearing premise across a man's commentaries is not adequately described as a hypothesis he merely raised and set down.

The Latin caution may be partly the translator's, but the point cuts both ways. Rufinus, who gave the West its Peri Archōn, openly translated so as to “purge and pare away… all the causes of stumbling” he found in the Greek, and a translator with that policy may well have firmed up Origen's hedges as he softened his heresies — a question of translation ethics pursued there. If so, the surviving Latin overstates the density of the disclaimers, and the genre defence leans on a text partly of Rufinus's making. But the same fact rescues the descriptive core from the opposite charge: Jerome's independent, hostile, literal Latin preserves a disclaimer too, so the hedging is not a Rufinian invention. The translator may have thickened it; he did not conjure it.

Assessment

contested. The descriptive half of the interpretation is sound and should be granted without grudging: Peri Archōn is a self-consciously exploratory book. Origen divides the faith into the plainly-delivered and the left-open, assigns his most-condemned speculations to the second class in the preface, flags them as “investigation and discussion” as he treats them, and expressly asks the reader to judge — and a hostile witness confirms the disclaimer stood in the Greek. His accusers, circulating the propositions as an anthology of bald assertions, did flatten that genre, and to that extent the sentence misrepresents the book. What the interpretation cannot carry is its normative conclusion — that the anathemas therefore condemn a mere genre error. The disclaimers are uneven: the same book argues the restoration and the succession of ages as settled conclusions. A hypothesis broadcast in a widely-copied handbook, argued as the necessary reading of Scripture, and recurring as a working premise across his exegesis is still a proposition the Church may weigh and reject; the councils did not mistake a question for an answer so much as judge the answer Origen's questions kept arriving at. The genre plea is best read for what Pamphilus first made it: a strong mitigation of Origen's culpability — he broke no defined rule and disowned the dogmatic mood — not a demonstration that the propositions escaped judgement on the technicality of how they were phrased.
