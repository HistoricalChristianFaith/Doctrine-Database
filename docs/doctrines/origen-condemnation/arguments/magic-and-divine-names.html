Did Origen teach that “magic is not an evil” — that certain names have a real, natural power?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — strong by philology, weak by the moral reading it asserts. The charge fastens on something real. Origen does hold, and argue at length against Celsus, that names are “not conventional but natural,” so that the Hebrew divine names — Sabaoth, Adonai, the God of Abraham, of Isaac, of Jacob — carry a power lodged in the very sounds and lose it when translated; and in the same breath he defends “the so-called magic” as “not… an altogether uncertain thing, but… a consistent system,” a real art rather than the empty superstition Epicurus and Aristotle took it for. A hostile reader who wanted a charge could find it there. But the proposition the charge-lists actually assert — that magic is not an evil, that the art is morally licit — misnames an epistemological and philological claim (magic is a real system; names have natural force) as a moral one (magic is permissible), and it runs flat against Origen’s own words: he condemns sorcery by the law of Moses (“Neither seek after wizards, to be defiled by them”), assigns the working of magic to “wicked men under the influence of an evil spirit,” and calls the Magi’s art “the falsity of their sorcery,” broken at the birth of Christ. So the charge catches his doctrine of names and his concession that magic is a coherent system, and inflates both into an endorsement he nowhere gives and repeatedly refuses.

The claim

Two of the standing charge-lists against Origen accuse him of holding, flatly, that magic is not an evil. In Photius’s ninth-century summary of an anonymous defence of Origen, the seventh of the fifteen counts the defender tries to answer is the charge “that magic is not an evil”;1 and the synodical letter of the Alexandrian council of 400, translated into Latin by Jerome, lists among Origen’s eight chief errors the proposition “Magic, if real, is not evil” — set, tellingly, between the charge that “the stars are conscious of their own movements, and the demons know the future by their courses” and the charge that Christ “will suffer again for the demons.”2 The interpretation weighed here is the one that lies behind the charge: that Origen taught a real, intrinsic power in certain names — above all the divine names — and that this doctrine amounts to, or endorses, magic. The place to test it is Against Celsus, where the “power of names” material is set out, read alongside the passages where Origen condemns sorcery outright.

Who reports it, and where it comes from

The charge’s root is not obscure. Celsus had gibed that it makes no difference whether the supreme God is called Zeus by the Greeks or Ammon by the Egyptians or Sabaoth by the Jews — one God under interchangeable labels. Origen’s reply is a sustained theory of the nature of names, and it is here, defending the untranslatable force of the Hebrew divine names, that he says the things his accusers turned into “magic is not an evil.” The doctrine is genuinely his, argued in his own voice across two books of his greatest apologetic work; the accusers did not invent it. What is in question is whether they described it fairly — whether a claim about language and about the reality of an art is the same as a claim that the art is good.

The evidence for — the doctrine the charge fastens on

Names are natural, not conventional, and the divine names cannot be translated without loss. Against Celsus’s “it makes no difference,” Origen sides with the Stoics and Epicurus against Aristotle: names were given “by nature,” not “by arrangement.” From this he draws the conclusion that the sacred names are not floating labels. “The name Sabaoth, and Adonai, and the other names treated with so much reverence among the Hebrews, are not applicable to any ordinary created things, but belong to a secret theology which refers to the Framer of all things. These names, accordingly, when pronounced with that attendant train of circumstances which is appropriate to their nature, are possessed of great power.” He extends the point to the names of angels — Michael, Gabriel, Raphael, each named “appropriately to the duties which they discharge” — and above all to the name of Jesus, which “has already been seen… to have expelled myriads of evil spirits.”3 The fifth book presses the untranslatability directly: when names “which in a certain language were possessed of a natural power were translated into another, they were no longer able to accomplish what they did before.” “The God of Abraham,” “of Isaac,” “of Jacob” will “produce certain effects… since even demons are vanquished and become submissive to him who pronounces these names”; render them into Greek and “the mention of the name is attended with no result.”4 That a real, natural power resides in the very sounds of the divine names is, past dispute, Origen’s teaching.

Magic is a real, coherent system — not empty superstition. The sharpest edge of the charge is that, in laying this foundation, Origen expressly rehabilitates “magic” as a category. Establishing the power of names, he writes, requires showing “that the so-called magic is not, as the followers of Epicurus and Aristotle suppose, an altogether uncertain thing, but is, as those skilled in it prove, a consistent system, having words which are known to exceedingly few.”3 Different names, he goes on, are “efficacious against certain demons who can only do certain things,” each demon bearing “a name appropriate to the several dialects of place and country.” And elsewhere, answering Celsus’s attempt to level Jesus’s miracles with the tricks of Egyptian conjurers, Origen lets the comparison stand far enough to concede the point his enemies seized: “You see that by these expressions he allows, as it were, the existence of magic.”5 He does not treat magic as nothing; he treats it as a real traffic with real spirits, worked through names that really bind them. That concession — that the art is not empty — is the true kernel the charge-lists compressed into “magic is not an evil.”

The evidence against — what Origen actually says about the practice

He condemns sorcery by the law of Moses. The same book that theorizes the power of names condemns the working of magic as a defilement forbidden by God. Answering the slander that the Jews “are addicted to sorcery, in which Moses was their instructor,” Origen throws the law back: let Celsus show where Moses taught it, “who… read the injunction, ‘Neither seek after wizards, to be defiled by them.’” The divinatory arts of the nations — “oracles… omens… birds… ventriloquists… those who profess the art of sacrifice… Chaldean genealogists” — are, he says, precisely “all which practices were forbidden to the Jews.”6 The reality of an art and its licitness are, for Origen, two different questions, and on the second his answer is the law’s: forbidden, defiling.

He assigns the working of magic to evil spirits and wicked men. Where Origen grants that magic “exists,” he immediately fixes its source in the demonic. Celsus’s conjurers do what they do, he allows, but they are to be reckoned “the proceedings of wicked men under the influence of an evil spirit.”5 The Magi of the nativity are his clearest case: they were “on familiar terms with evil spirits, and invoking them for such purposes as their knowledge and wishes extend to” — and at the birth of Christ “the evil spirits… became feeble, and lost their strength, the falsity of their sorcery being manifested, and their power being broken.” Their art fails precisely as “sorcery,” overwhelmed by “the light of divinity.”7 A doctrine that makes magic a real commerce with evil spirits, exposed as falsehood by the coming of God, is not a doctrine that magic is not an evil.

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The philological half of the charge is accurate, and importantly so. Origen really does teach that names have a natural, not a conventional, power; that the divine and angelic names carry a force lodged in their very sounds; and that they lose that force in translation. He really does, in the course of it, defend “the so-called magic” as a coherent system rather than an empty one, and concede that magic “exists.” This is not a distortion foisted on him by hostile excerptors; it is set out at length and in his own voice in Against Celsus, and it sits close enough to contemporary theurgy and the name-magic of the papyri that a suspicious reader’s alarm is intelligible. The charge is rooted in a real feature of his thought.

What is overstated. The proposition the lists assert — that Origen held magic not to be an evil — misreads an epistemological claim as a moral one. Origen’s point about “the so-called magic” is that it is real, a consistent system with genuine effects, against philosophers who dismissed it as delusion; it is not that it is good, or permitted. On permissibility his verdict is the opposite of the charge: sorcery is forbidden by the law, a defilement; its power is demonic; its practitioners are “wicked men under the influence of an evil spirit”; and the paradigm magic of his own Gospel narrative, the Magi’s, is “falsity” broken by Christ. The charge slides from “magic is a real art” and “names have real power” to “magic is not an evil,” and the middle term — that the power Origen locates in the divine names is the same thing as the sorcerer’s art — is exactly what he denies: the names of the true God vanquish the demons the sorcerer courts. What the accusers have done is to take his defence of the reality of names, strip the moral frame in which he everywhere sets it, and re-describe the residue as an apology for the magical arts.

Why the charge kept its place anyway. The company the accusation keeps on the lists explains its staying power. It stands next to the charge that the stars are conscious and the demons foretell the future by their courses — astral determinism — a doctrine Origen in fact argued against, as this wiki’s astrology timeline records; the two charges together read Origen’s serious interest in the cosmos of spirits, names, and powers as a single occult tendency. His doctrine of the natural power of names really does give a theory that ancient magic could and did use, and a churchman compiling errors from Peri Archōn and Against Celsus was not wrong to feel the proximity. But proximity is not endorsement: the same theory, in Origen’s hands, is a weapon against the demons, an account of why the name of Jesus casts them out, and it comes wrapped in an explicit condemnation of the sorcerer’s practice. The charge convicts him of the neighbourhood, not of the crime.

Assessment

contested. That Origen taught a real, natural power in certain names — the divine and angelic names above all, untranslatable without loss — is beyond dispute and argued in his own words (Against Celsus I.24–25, V.45), and he does there defend “the so-called magic” as a coherent system rather than an empty superstition. To that extent the charge is grounded, strong by the lights of philology and history-of-religions. But the specific proposition the charge-lists assert — that magic is not an evil, that the art is morally licit — misnames a claim about the reality of names and arts as a claim about their goodness, and it is refuted by Origen’s own repeated condemnation of sorcery as demonic, defiling, and forbidden by the law of Moses. So the reading is genuinely two-sided: the doctrine of the power of names is his, and it is magic-adjacent enough to alarm; the endorsement of magic is not his, and his text condemns the practice the charge says he excused.
