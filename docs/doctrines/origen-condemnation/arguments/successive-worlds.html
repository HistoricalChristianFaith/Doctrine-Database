Did Origen teach an endless succession of worlds — and does it entail a second fall?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — the interpretation weighed here has a strong core and an overreaching edge. Its core is well-evidenced: Origen did propose a succession of ages (aiōnes), worlds before this one and worlds after it, but he built the succession as an account of God’s ceaseless providence and of free will’s history, and he expressly repudiated the one thing the anathemas fasten on him — the Stoic wheel of identical recurrence — aiming the whole sequence at a real terminus in which God is “all in all” and diversity ceases. That much holds. The overreaching edge is the further claim that a second fall is therefore no part of his scheme, and that the “endless recurrence” is purely the sixth-century Origenists’ system read back onto him. Origen’s own non-negotiable free will, and his principle that “the end is always like the beginning,” leave the final rest insecure — a difficulty Augustine and the councils pressed as a fair inference from his premises, not a bare slander. So the reading is strong by the lights of what Origen intended and repudiated, and weak by the lights of what his premises will bear.

Related arguments: the fall and restoration of the rational minds — see also Was the pre-existence of souls an open question when Origen taught it? · Did Origen teach universal restoration as a doctrine?

The claim

…not then for the first time did God begin to work when He made this visible world; but as, after its destruction, there will be another world, so also we believe that others existed before the present came into being… It is not, however, to be supposed that several worlds existed at once, but that, after the end of this present world, others will take their beginning.

— Origen, De Principiis III.5.3 (c. 229), link.1 Three centuries later the second anathema of Justinian’s edict of 543 and the fifteen anathemas of 553 turned this into a charge: the last of the fifteen condemns anyone who says “that the life of the spirits shall be like to the life which was in the beginning… so that the end and the beginning shall be alike, and that the end shall be the true measure of the beginning”;2 and Justinian’s ninth condemns holding that the punishment of the impious “is temporary and that it will have an end at some time.”3 Read together, the accusers took Origen to have made salvation a wheel: an endless series of worlds in which the restored fall, are punished, and are restored again, world without end. The interpretation weighed here is the one Origen’s defenders set against that: that his succession of ages is a bounded, providential sequence ending in a final restoration, not an eternal cycle, and that the “endless recurrence” is the sixth-century Origenist system — and Jerome’s hostile digest — rather than anything he wrote.

Who advances it

The distinction is Origen’s own before it is his defenders’: the case rests first on the fact that he attacked the Stoic doctrine of recurrence by name (below). Among modern scholars it is the reading of Henri Crouzel, who treats Peri Archōn as a work of inquiry rather than a closed system and denies that Origen taught a Stoic-style eternal return; of Mark Julian Edwards, whose Origen against Plato argues that Origen’s cosmology is built precisely against the cyclical determinism of the philosophers, though Edwards grants that Origen “allowed the possibility of diverse and non-identical cycles”; and, at greatest length, of Panagiotes Tzamalikos, whose Origen: Cosmology and Ontology of Time argues that Origen’s ages are irreversibly linear and teleological, the antithesis of the pagan wheel. John Behr’s 2017 edition presses the wider point on which the reading depends — that the systematic “Origenism” condemned in 553 is a construction later than Origen, to be distinguished from what the text of On First Principles actually says.4

The evidence for

Origen repudiates the Stoic wheel by name. The one doctrine the anathemas describe — an identical world recurring after each conflagration — is the doctrine Origen ridicules at length. In Contra Celsum he mocks the Stoics for holding that after the world’s burning “there will be… an arrangement of things in which everything will be unchanged, as compared with the former arrangement”, so that “Socrates will be again the son of Sophroniscus, and a native of Athens”, accused once more by men indistinguishable from Anytus and Meletus and condemned again by the same court — a scheme he calls not merely false but ridiculous.5 Against it he sets the Christian account: all things “are administered by God in proportion to the relation of the free-will of each individual, and are ever being brought into a better condition.”6 Whatever his succession of ages is, it is expressly not the mechanical repetition the sixth-century canons ascribe to him: it is providential, moral, and non-identical, driven by the free choices of creatures rather than by fate.

The succession is bounded by a terminus, not closed into a loop. The ages exist because God’s goodness and power are never idle — his answer to the objection “what was God doing before the world began?” is that God did not “then for the first time… begin to work”, there being worlds before this one.1 But the sequence runs toward an end that is a genuine rest, not another turn. At the consummation God is “all and in all”; when “all may be one, as the Father is one with the Son… there will no longer be any diversity”; the last enemy, death, is destroyed, so that “there may not be anything left of a mournful kind… nor anything that is adverse when there is no enemy.”7 On this reading the plurality of worlds is the long pedagogy by which fallen minds are trained back to unity, and the restoration is precisely where the pedagogy stops — the end of the succession, the state the whole sequence was for.

The worlds differ; they do not repeat. Origen is explicit that the ages are not interchangeable copies. There is a world before and a world after, but “it is not… to be supposed that several worlds existed at once”, and the diversity of any given world springs from “the movements and declensions of those who fell from that primeval unity” — that is, from an unrepeatable moral history, each age shaped by the merits and demerits carried into it.8 Isaiah’s “new heavens and a new earth” are for Origen a genuinely new state, not a reprint of the old. This is the opposite of Stoic recurrence, where each cycle reproduces the last down to the marriage of Socrates and Xanthippe.

The fully systematized “wheel” is later than Origen. The condemnation of 553 describes a whole apparatus — an original undifferentiated unity of minds, a soul of Christ united to the Word before the incarnation, spherical risen bodies — that belongs to the sixth-century Evagrian Origenists and is nowhere in Origen’s surviving text, as the summary timeline and the resurrection-body page note. The “endless recurrence of identical worlds” travels in that same company: it is the tidy system the anathematizers needed, and the shape Jerome’s hostile summary gave the doctrine when he had Origen turning archangels into devils and back “time after time” across worlds without number.9 Distinguishing Origen from Origenism is the burden of most recent scholarship, and on this point the distinction has real purchase.

Adversarial assessment

“No second fall” overstates what Origen secured. The reading is right that Origen aimed at a final rest and rejected the pagan cycle; it is wrong to conclude that a fresh fall is therefore excluded from his system. Two of his own commitments pull the other way. The first is his principle of symmetry: “the end is always like the beginning”, and “a new beginning springs from the end.” The second is his non-negotiable doctrine of free will, which he will not surrender even at the consummation — rational natures possess goodness “as an accidental and perishable quality”, never by essence. Put the two together and the terminus is not obviously stable: if the restored keep the freedom by which they first fell, and if every end is the seed of a new beginning, then nothing in the machinery guarantees that the restored will not fall again — and if they can, the succession need not stop. Origen sometimes writes as though the final unity were unlosable, but he does not demonstrate it, and in places he leaves the question open rather than closing it.10

The cyclical charge is a fair inference, not a pure fabrication. Because the difficulty is generated by Origen’s own premises, his sharpest critic answered it with an argument rather than an anathema. In City of God XXI.17 Augustine fastens not on some invented doctrine but on “his theory of the ceaseless alternation of happiness and misery” — the version in which the freed fall and are restored without end — and observes that in it Origen “lost even the credit of being merciful”, since a blessedness that can be lost again is no blessedness.11 Augustine had read Peri Archōn; the alternation he attacks is drawn from it, not fabricated against it. When the fifteenth anathema of 553 condemns the doctrine that “the end and the beginning shall be alike”, it is condemning a principle Origen actually stated, whatever he meant to build on it. The reading’s claim that the recurrence is “entirely the sixth century’s” will not carry the weight put on it: the systematized cosmology is later, but the vulnerability that the system exploits is Origen’s.

Rejecting the wheel of the same is not securing a final rest. The strongest evidence for the reading — Origen’s ridicule of the Stoics — establishes less than it seems. What he refutes there is the recurrence of identical events by fate; that leaves untouched the possibility of a non-identical and open succession driven by free will, which is arguably just what his own scheme yields, and which Edwards concedes he “allowed.” A universe in which creatures endlessly fall and are restored in ever-varying ways is not the Stoic wheel — each turn is new — but it is still a wheel, and it is still without a guaranteed end. Origen’s repudiation of Stoic determinism is genuine and should be credited; it does not by itself deliver the stable terminus the defence needs, and the defence tends to treat the two as if they were one.

What the succession itself was condemnable for is narrower than the whole scheme. As with the companion questions, the timing point holds: no rule of faith had defined the origin or the future of the world when Origen canvassed these ages, and the plurality of worlds is a speculation he grounds in Scripture (Ecclesiastes 1:9–10; Isaiah 65:17) and offers under the same announced caution as the rest of the open questions — weighed on the pre-existence and restoration pages. What drew fire, and what the anathemas actually reach, is not the bare idea of ages before and after but the cyclical version — the insecure terminus and the second fall — which is where the interpretation is at its weakest, not its strongest.

Assessment

contested. The core of the interpretation is sound and well-evidenced. Origen did teach a succession of ages, but he built it as providential and moral rather than fated, he ridiculed the Stoic doctrine of identical recurrence by name, and he aimed the whole sequence at a genuine terminus — God “all in all”, the last enemy destroyed, an incorporeal unity in which “there will no longer be any diversity.” The systematized “wheel of identical worlds” that the councils describe, with its spherical risen bodies and pre-incarnate soul of Christ, is a sixth-century elaboration, and Jerome’s digest sharpened it further; to read that back onto Origen unmodified is anachronistic. But the reading overreaches when it concludes that a second fall is no part of his scheme at all. His principle that “the end is always like the beginning” and his refusal to surrender free will even at the consummation leave the final rest insecure, and a fresh fall a live possibility — a difficulty his own premises generate, which Augustine pressed as an argument and the fifteenth anathema of 553 condemned as a stated principle, not a slander. Rejecting the recurrence of the same is not the same as securing a rest that cannot be lost. So the interpretation is strong by the lights of what Origen intended and repudiated, and weak by the lights of what his premises will bear: his succession is not the Stoic wheel, but neither did he close the circle he left open.
