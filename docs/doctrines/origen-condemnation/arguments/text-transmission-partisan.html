Can Origen be fairly judged at all, given how his text reaches us?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — the predicament is real and one-sided in a way that genuinely limits confidence: the book the whole condemnation turns on, Peri Archōn, survives whole only in an avowedly softening Latin translation, while its Greek reaches us mainly through the briefs of its prosecutors, so the precise wording of the condemned propositions — above all the spherical body and the rest of the fifteen anathemas — cannot be checked against Origen’s own page. But the strong form of the thesis — that the evidence is so shaped by partisans that no confident verdict on his teaching is recoverable — overstates: the divergence is measurable, not unknowable, because the Greek that survives lets Rufinus be checked; Origen’s vast authenticated exegetical corpus gives independent access to his mind; and an unbounded appeal to textual uncertainty proves too much, immunising every author against every charge alike. So the skepticism is sound as a caution about the letter of the condemned system, weak as a verdict on the substance of his theology.

(the philological form of the fairness question) · was Origen condemned by a standard that did not exist in his lifetime? (the umbrella fairness question). The translator’s side of the problem — whether Rufinus’s “smoothing” recovered or rewrote his author — is weighed across the Origenist controversy on the interpolation thesis, Rufinus’s translation ethics, and whether his draft was interpolated before it reached Rome.

The claim

…he “added or struck out whatever he chose, and changed things as he thought right, and published it abroad as if it were my work.”

— Origen, in a letter “to certain intimate friends at Alexandria” complaining of the falsification of the record of a disputation, preserved by Rufinus, On the Falsification of the Works of Origen, link.1 The interpretation weighed here generalises Origen’s own alarm into a verdict on the whole controversy: that the surviving evidence for what he taught is so systematically shaped by partisans on both sides — a defender who softened him, prosecutors who sharpened him — that no confident judgement on his precise teaching is recoverable, and the condemnations therefore rest on a text no one can check.

Who advances it

As a bounded caution the point is a scholarly commonplace, and a sound one: every serious modern editor of Peri Archōn opens by warning that its transmission is exceptionally compromised. The whole work survives only in the Latin Rufinus made about 398, who told his readers in the preface that where he found something “contrary to the truth concerning the Trinity” he had “either omitted it… or set it down in terms agreeing with the rule of faith”; the Greek survives only in fragments — the friendly excerpts Basil and Gregory of Nazianzus gathered into the Philocalia, and the hostile ones quoted to convict him: Justinian’s edict to Menas, Jerome’s Letter 124 to Avitus, Epiphanius. The book, in short, reaches us from its friends abridged and from its enemies weaponised.2 The bounded caution becomes an advocated thesis when it is pushed — in popular and apologetic retellings of the condemnation — into the stronger claim that the whole verdict is worthless because the text behind it is unrecoverable: we cannot know what Origen really taught, so the Church condemned a phantom. It is that stronger claim, not the editors’ caution, that is assessed here.

The evidence for

The transmission really is partisan, and asymmetrically so. This is not a general worry about ancient books but a specific and documented one about this book. Its only complete witness is a translator who announced in advance that he had corrected his author toward orthodoxy; its Greek survives chiefly in the very documents drawn up to condemn it. Origen himself, in the letter above, testifies that he had watched an opponent doctor the transcript of a debate and circulate it under his name, comparing his fate to the forged letter that alarmed the Thessalonians and to Marcion’s mutilation of the Gospels. A defendant whose defender edits him and whose accusers are the ones who preserve his words is, on the face of it, badly placed to receive a fair hearing.1

The condemned wording cannot be checked against his page. The sharpest charges of 553 — the spherical resurrection body, the ensouled stars, the nous-Christ, the final equality of every spirit with Christ — are set out in that form nowhere in Origen’s surviving text, and the modern demonstration that they track a later, Evagrian system depends on comparing the anathemas with the recovered Syriac of Evagrius, not with Peri Archōn, which has no complete Greek to compare. To that extent the strong thesis has a true and load-bearing kernel: on the exact words of the most-quoted condemnation, the accused’s own text is missing.3

A printed edition really did fuse the two sides. The clearest proof that partisan transmission can corrupt the record is that within living memory it did. Paul Koetschau’s 1913 critical edition of Peri Archōn, convinced that Origen’s opponents had preserved what Rufinus suppressed, broke up the Latin and spliced into its flow the hostile fragments from Justinian, Jerome’s Letter to Avitus, and the anathemas of 553 — and G. W. Butterworth’s 1936 English version rubricated the insertions “Greek,” as if they were more authentic than the surrounding text. For some eighty years the standard English Origen thus read the prosecution’s brief into the defendant’s book. John Behr’s Oxford edition of 2017 undid the splice and relegated the disputed matter to an appendix. The skeptic’s worry that the record has been shaped by its enemies is, in this instance, simply what happened.4

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The bounded claim is sound. The transmission of Peri Archōn is genuinely one of the worst in the patristic corpus, it is skewed by partisans in both directions, and it does place the precise formulation of the condemned propositions — especially the fifteen anathemas — beyond direct check against Origen’s own words. Anyone who quotes the spherical body as a verbatim teaching of the historical Origen is quoting a text that is not there. So far the skepticism is not merely defensible but correct, and Koetschau’s edition is the standing demonstration of it.

The asymmetry cuts the other way once it can be measured. The thesis treats the two-sided distortion as leaving the truth unrecoverable; but distortion you can measure is not distortion that hides the truth. Wherever the Greek survives — the substantial Philocalia excerpts from Books 3 and 4, the quotations in Justinian, the literal fragments in Jerome’s Letter 124 — Rufinus can be laid beside his original, and the comparison has been done: the pattern is that he softened Origen toward orthodoxy rather than removing intrusions, and modern scholarship (Crouzel, Behr) has on that basis rehabilitated him as a broadly faithful translator against the older wholesale distrust. A softening whose direction and rough magnitude are known is a correction one can reason across, not a veil. The controls turn “we cannot know” into “we can estimate, and adjust.”5

One book is not the whole man. The thesis silently equates “what Origen taught” with “the text of Peri Archōn.” But Origen is among the most voluminous of the fathers, and much of his exegetical and apologetic work survives in undisputed Greek: the whole of the Contra Celsum, large stretches of the Commentary on John, homilies and commentaries, the treatise On Prayer and the Exhortation to Martyrdom — and the papyri recovered at Tura, near Cairo, in 1941 added still more in his own Greek, including the Dialogue with Heraclides, the treatise On the Pascha, and earlier texts of the Contra Celsum and the Commentary on Romans. The pre-existence and fall of souls, the subordination of the Son, the restoration of all things and his allegorical method are all attested across this corpus, independently of the compromised book. His mind is not locked inside the one text his enemies excerpted; it is legible across thousands of pages they never touched.6

Unbounded, the skepticism proves too much. If a text’s partisan transmission suffices to bar any verdict on its author, the rule does not stop at Origen: it acquits every heresiarch preserved by his opponents and convicts no one, since the offensive lines can always be laid to a corrupter. Jerome saw the structure of the move and named it against Rufinus’s narrower version of it:

If, I reply, we admit that everything in a book which is offensive is corruptly inserted by others, nothing will remain belonging to the author under whose name the book passes, but everything can be assigned to those by whom it is supposed to have been corrupted.

— Jerome, Apology against Rufinus II.17. An epistemic principle that dissolves all authorship on demand is not a finding about Origen but a refusal to reach one, and it is as unfalsifiable applied to the whole transmission as it was applied to single lines.7

Assessment

contested. The skepticism is right about the letter and wrong about the substance, and the two verdicts are both firm, which is what makes it genuinely disputed rather than simply mistaken. On the exact wording of the condemned system — the fifteen anathemas, the spherical body, the propositions quoted as if lifted from his page — the caution is sound: Peri Archōn survives whole only in a translation that announced its own softening, its Greek reaches us largely through the prosecution, and a printed edition within living memory fused the two, so the accused’s precise words often cannot be produced. But the strong, circulating form — that the record is so partisan that no confident verdict on his teaching survives, and the condemnation rests on nothing checkable — does not hold: the surviving Greek makes Rufinus’s divergence measurable rather than unknowable, the vast authenticated corpus (enlarged by the Tura papyri of 1941) gives independent access to every doctrine at issue, and an unbounded textual skepticism would immunise every author against every charge. The honest position is the bounded one the editors actually hold: treat the exact formulae of the condemned propositions as uncertain and often not his, and Origen’s substantive theology as well attested — which is why the neighbouring pages can still say, with confidence, what he did and did not teach.
