Pamphilus of Caesarea on the condemnation of Origen

Dates: c. 240 – 16 February 310, martyred · Wikipedia
Summary: Pamphilus, presbyter of Caesarea and keeper of the library Origen's books had built, was arrested in November 307 and spent the twenty-seven months before his beheading writing, with his pupil Eusebius, a defence of Origen in five books; Eusebius added a sixth after his death. Only the first survives, in Rufinus's Latin of 397. Its value for this timeline is that it opens with a list — the earliest surviving inventory of what Origen was being accused of, drawn up by a man who had the whole of Origen in front of him and who wrote it in a cell. This page covers his share of the timeline of verdicts passed on Origen: the nine charges of c. 310 and how they compare with the lists that came after, the method he answered them with, and the long quarrel over whether he wrote the book at all.

The man and the library

Born at Beirut of a wealthy family and trained at Alexandria under Pierius — a teacher of the next generation whom Jerome nicknamed “Origen Junior” — Pamphilus settled at Caesarea, was ordained presbyter there, and made the school and library his life's work. Jerome, who owned some of the results, is the best witness to what that meant:

Pamphilus the presbyter, patron of Eusebius bishop of Cæsarea, was so inflamed with love of sacred literature, that he transcribed the greater part of the works of Origen with his own hand and these are still preserved in the library at Cæsarea. I have twenty-five volumes of Commentaries of Origen, written in his hand, On the twelve prophets which I hug and guard with such joy, that I deem myself to have the wealth of Croesus.1

Photius, reading the Apology in the ninth century, records the same two facts in a sentence: “Pierius was the teacher of Pamphilus… The holy Pamphilus was a presbyter, and is said to have copied most of Origen's commentaries on Scripture with his own hand.”2 It is worth registering what that copying was for. A great deal of Origen survives at all because Pamphilus listed and transcribed it; the man who compiled the fullest defence of Origen is also the reason there was anything left to prosecute. His pupil Eusebius — the church historian — took his name in his own, signing himself Eusebius Pamphili, and wrote a three-book life of him that is now lost.3

Written in prison, between November 307 and February 310

Eusebius, who visited him there, dates the arrest exactly: in the fifth year of the persecution, on the Nones of November, the governor Urbanus sent some to the mines and others to prison, and

Among these was my dearest friend Pamphilus, who was by reason of every virtue the most illustrious of the martyrs in our time. Urbanus first tested him in rhetorical philosophy and learning; and afterwards endeavored to compel him to sacrifice. But as he saw that he refused and in nowise regarded his threats, being exceedingly angry, he ordered him to be tormented with severest tortures.4

He was not killed. He was put with the confessors in prison, where he stayed — “after these persons had continued in prison for two entire years” — until a second batch of Egyptian arrests brought the matter back before the judge. He died with eleven others, a company Eusebius counts as “twelve in all; being counted worthy of apostolic grace and number”, on “the nineteenth of the month Peritius, according to the Roman reckoning the fourteenth before the Kalends of March” — 16 February 310.5 The Apology therefore belongs to those twenty-seven months, and it was not written for scholars. Photius preserves the dedication: it was “addressed to those who were condemned to the mines for the sake of Christ, the chief of whom was Patermythius, who shortly after the death of Pamphilus ended his life at the stake with others.” Photius also gives the division of labour — six books, “five of which were written by Pamphilus when in prison in the company of Eusebius. The sixth is the work of Eusebius alone.”6 Condemned men, that is, were writing to condemned men about a third condemned man; the audience was people with weeks to live who wanted to know whether their teacher's teacher had been orthodox.

The charges of c. 310

The first half of Book I sets out Origen's own summary of the Church's preaching and assembles a dossier on the Trinity from his writings. The second half turns to the accusations, and Pamphilus begins by listing them. This enumeration is the earliest surviving inventory of the case against Origen:

So let us consider those very charges that are leveled by his malevolent accusers: The first is that they claim that he says the Son of God was not born. The second is that they claim that he says, in agreement with the fictitious ideas of Valentinus, that the Son of God came into existence as an emission. The third, which completely contradicts all of the above, is that they claim that he says, in agreement with Artemas and Paul of Samosata, that Christ the Son of God is a mere man, that is, that he is not also God. After this comes the charge that is opposed to all the preceding—for malice is blind—that they claim that he says that all the Savior's actions were done δοκήσει, that is, only in appearance, and by allegory, but not also in accordance with the things that are reported in the historical narratives. There is another charge too, according to which they assert that he proclaims two Christs. They add this one too: that he completely denies the literal historical narratives that are reported throughout all of Holy Scripture concerning the deeds of the saints. Moreover, with respect to the resurrection of the dead and the punishments of the ungodly, they impugn him with no trivial calumny, [that is,] that he denies that penalties will be inflicted upon sinners. Moreover, some find fault with his discussions and opinions in which he treated the condition and divine management of the soul. The final charge against him is the one that is spread abroad with complete shamelessness, namely, [the doctrine] of μετενσωματώσις, that is, that Origen asserts that human souls after death transmigrate into speechless animals, for example, into snakes and cattle, and that even the very souls of speechless animals are rational.7

Nine charges, then, in the order he takes them:

  1. that the Son of God was not born (innatus);
  2. that the Son came into being as an emission (per prolationem), as Valentinus taught;
  3. that Christ is a mere man (purus homo), as Artemas and Paul of Samosata taught;
  4. that the Saviour's acts were done only in appearance and by allegory;
  5. that he preaches two Christs;
  6. that he denies the literal historical narratives of Scripture (historias corporales);
  7. that he denies that the wicked will be punished, and with it the resurrection of the dead;
  8. that his account of the origin and government of the soul is erroneous;
  9. that he teaches the transmigration of human souls into animals.8

Pamphilus's first move against them is to point out that they cannot all be true at once: charges 1–3 are the Son uncreated, the Son emitted, and the Son a mere man, and the fourth — that everything Christ did was appearance only — is, he says, “opposed to all the preceding—for malice is blind.” The list is not a doctrine; it is what was in the air, contradictions included. The material behind charges 6 and 7 is the resurrection controversy that Methodius of Olympus had been prosecuting in the same decade — Pamphilus answers him at length without ever naming him.

The method: defend him out of his own mouth

The book's design is a decision about evidence, and Pamphilus states it before he uses it. He will not vouch for Origen; he will quote him, on the ground that a partisan's summary of a dead man's views is worth nothing beside the dead man's own sentences:

In pursuing this goal it seemed more proper to us to prepare a defense not in our own words or assertions, but from Origen's own utterances, where he himself testifies in his own words that he is a stranger to those things that his detractors raise as objections… For it could possibly seem suspicious if we intend to assert these things in our own words, since out of love for him we may have concealed passages where his thinking was corrupt. But when we use the utterances of the very man who is being accused and defend him against all the objections of his accusers by his own words, not by our assertions, what pretext for incrimination can further remain…? And since the present words concern one who, at least among men, has died, what could have greater reliability and force among human judges on behalf of one who has died than the literature and written works of the deceased?9

He goes to the disputed book on purpose rather than around it: “we will assemble testimonies chiefly from those from which his accusers mainly raise their charges… The majority of the objections raised by his accusers are chiefly based on the books that he entitled Peri archon.”10 And the substantive defence, when it comes, is one Origen had made for himself in the preface to that same book: that the disputed propositions were offered as inquiries, not as dogma. “In the process of explaining these things,” Pamphilus writes, “his frequent custom is to add an admission that he is not declaring these things by a definitive pronouncement, nor is he defining them as secure dogma, but he is investigating to the best of his ability and discussing the meaning of the Scriptures.”11

What he thought was actually happening

Two complaints in the opening run are worth having in full, because they are the earliest description of the mechanism by which the condemnation worked. The first is that Origen's accusers have not read him:

Sometimes his accusers are those who do not know Greek: some are utterly ignorant of it; others, though they seem to have some competence in it, are nevertheless insufficiently qualified to undertake the task of reading his works… One can find many who, if you ask them in which books or in which passages Origen said the things [of] which they are accusing [him], will admit that they do not even know. The things about which they are making their assertions are things they have never read, but they have heard others talking about them.12

The second is that what circulates is an anthology of the offensive bits, assembled for the purpose:

Very rarely and infrequently do we find passages by which perhaps those with less knowledge, or, to speak more truly, the malicious, are able to see themselves offended. Yet some destroy and disdain everything Origen has written, even the things that they themselves confess to be catholic… Instead, those things alone that lead to calumny are the things they learn with utmost zeal… The reason they do this is, to be precise, to be able more readily to concoct more effective calumnies by setting these things forth.13

He offers a demonstration, and it is the most quoted paragraph in the book:

it usually happens, whether by accident or, sometimes, on purpose, that something of Origen's is read in the hearing of his detractors as if it were from another commentator (when Origen's name is not found in the heading of the manuscript). The reading pleases the audience and is praised and is held in all admiration for as long a time as the name of the author remains unknown. But when it becomes known that what was pleasing was from Origen, all at once it becomes displeasing, all at once the reading is said to be heretical, and the things that a little while before were being extolled to heaven, by the same voices and by the same tongue are plunged into hell.14

Whether the anecdote records an actual experiment or is a rhetorical figure cannot be settled from the text. What it establishes is that by 310 the name was already doing work independent of the sentences — which is the whole subject of the timeline this page belongs to.

How the indictment grew

Set the nine charges beside the lists that followed and the growth is visible, and it is not gradual: it happens in the fourth century.

c. 310 — Pamphilus, nine charges. Four of them Christological in a pre-Nicene idiom (unbegotten Son, Valentinian emission, Samosatene mere man, docetism); then two Christs, the dissolving of biblical history into allegory, the denial of punishment and resurrection, the origin of the soul, and transmigration.

375–394 — Epiphanius. The list has changed shape. It is now headed by a charge that could not have existed in 310, that Origen fathered Arianism — “you ought not to eulogize one who is the spiritual father of Arius, and the root and parent of all heresies” — and it has acquired its permanent furniture: that “the Son, he tells us, cannot see the Father, and the Holy Spirit cannot see the Son”; that souls “were once angels in heaven, and that having sinned in the upper world, they have been cast down into this, and have been confined in bodies as in barrows or tombs”; and that “the devil will once more be what he at one time was.”15 The restoration of the devil, the fall of souls from a heavenly state, and the paternity of Arianism are all absent from Pamphilus, and the first two are the doctrines the sixth century would anathematize.

c. 400 — a second, anonymous defence. Photius read two apologies for Origen and summarized them in consecutive entries. The second (cod. 118) is Pamphilus and Eusebius; the first (cod. 117) is an anonymous work in five books that answers fifteen counts, among them that prayer should not be offered to the Son, that the Son is not absolutely good, that He does not know the Father as He knows Himself, that the soul of the Saviour was the same as the soul of Adam, that magic is not an evil, that astrology causes events, and that the Cherubim are the ideas of the Son.16 Éric Junod's argument that these are two different works, and that the anonymous fifteen answer concerns belonging to the age of Jerome and Theophilus rather than of Diocletian, is now the standard reading; it dates the second list to the end of the fourth century or the beginning of the fifth.17 On that reading the indictment goes from nine counts to fifteen in ninety years, and the six new ones are datable by their content to the quarrel that Jerome and Rufinus were then conducting.

543 and after — the anathemas. What Justinian's edict to Menas condemns, and what the fifteen anathemas condemn after it, is a system: pre-existent minds falling through satiety, a pre-existent soul of Christ, ensouled heavenly bodies, a spherical risen body, an end identical with the beginning.18 Exactly one of Pamphilus's nine survives into it intact — the charge of transmigration, which Justinian repeats and which had been in circulation continuously since 310.19 The four pre-Nicene Christological charges have vanished, having been overtaken by the Arian controversy that made them unaskable in that form. That is the single most useful thing this book supplies to a history of the condemnation: a fixed point in 310 against which the later lists can be measured, showing that the charge-sheet which was finally enforced is not the one Origen's own century brought against him.

Who wrote it — Jerome's reversal

Jerome first records the Apology as Pamphilus's without hesitation, in the catalogue of 393: “He wrote an Apology for Origen before Eusebius had written his, and was put to death at Cæsarea in Palestine in the persecution of Maximinus.” Under the entry for Eusebius he lists the “six books of Apology for Origen” separately, and explains the name: “His surname Pamphilus arose from his friendship for Pamphilus the martyr.”20 Then Rufinus translated the book into Latin in 397 and published it as a martyr's endorsement of Origen, and Jerome's position changed entirely:

The real fact is that Eusebius Bishop of Cæsarea… who was in his day the standard bearer of the Arian faction, wrote a large and elaborate work in six books in defense of Origen… The first of these six books you have translated and assigned it to the martyr… If that book is Pamphilus's, which of the six books is Eusebius's first? In the very volume which you pretend to be Pamphilus's, mention is made of the later books… You thought that men would believe the martyr, though they would have turned in abhorrence from the chief of the Arians.21

His evidence is a sentence of Eusebius's own lost Life of Pamphilus, quoted from the third book: “He himself however, wrote nothing whatever of his own, except private letters which he sent to his friends, so humble was his estimate of himself” — whence, Jerome concludes, “the martyr Pamphilus never wrote a single book.”22 Writing to Rome he pressed the same case with an argument from the internal evidence — that a work describing Origen as a supporter of the Nicene faith cannot be by a man who died fifteen years before Nicaea:

show me any other work of Pamphilus; you will nowhere find any, this is his only one. How then can I know that it is by Pamphilus?… How then could they have disagreed so fundamentally on this point, Eusebius in all his works proving Origen to be an Arian, and Pamphilus describing him as a supporter of the Nicene council, which had not yet been held?23

Later he explained his own earlier attribution as having been taken on trust from Rufinus, and set out what he had since concluded: that Rufinus had published Eusebius's first book as Pamphilus's, “only altering the opinion about the Son and the Holy Spirit”, and that “your object in circulating this book was to introduce heresy under the authority of a martyr.”24

The attribution to Pamphilus has long since been vindicated, and Jerome's denial is not now defended: he had himself assigned the work correctly before the quarrel, Photius — who had the Greek in front of him and no stake in the Latin dispute — calls it flatly “the Defence of Origen by Pamphilus the martyr and Eusebius”, and Eusebius's remark about his teacher's modesty will not bear the weight Jerome puts on it, since Eusebius is speaking of works published in Pamphilus's own name, not of a book they wrote together.25 Two qualifications survive the verdict, and both matter. Georg Röwekamp has argued that it was Rufinus who narrowed the joint attribution to Pamphilus alone, precisely in order to give the book a martyr's authority — so Jerome's account of the motive may be right even though his conclusion about the author is wrong. And the second concerns the text: what we have is Rufinus's Latin, made by a translator who elsewhere states that he corrected what he took to be heretical corruptions as he went. The critical editors flag particular passages in the Apology as probably Rufinus's own, and Rowan Williams has argued that the underlying Greek defended a more subordinationist Origen than the Latin now shows.26 The charge-list of §87 is the part least exposed to that worry — a translator with Rufinus's interests had no motive to invent accusations, and several of the nine were long obsolete by 397.

What the book does not contain

It is often said that the defence of Origen by interpolation — that heretics tampered with his books, so that the heterodox sentences in them are not his — begins with Pamphilus. It does not. Book I argues in one way only: it quotes Origen at length and lets the quotations answer the charge. The interpolation thesis belongs to the short treatise Rufinus attached to his translation in 397, On the Falsification of the Books of Origen, which is his own composition and argues from a series of parallels — Clement, Dionysius of Alexandria, Hilary, Cyprian — that orthodox writers' books were routinely doctored:

Whenever they found in any of the renowned writers of old days a discussion of those things which pertain to the glory of God so full and faithful that every believer could gain profit and instruction from it, they have not scrupled to infuse into their writings the poisonous taint of their own false doctrines; this they have done, either by inserting things which the writers had not said or by changing by interpolation what they had said.27

Rufinus's best evidence for it is a letter of Origen's own, which he quotes entire, complaining that a transcript of a public disputation had been altered and published under his name: “A certain promoter of heresy… procured the document from those who had written out the notes, and added or struck out whatever he chose, and changed things as he thought right, and published it abroad as if it were my work.”28 That argument is weighed on its own page. What belongs here is only the attribution: the two works travel together in the manuscripts and in Rufinus's own presentation of them, and the strategy of the second is not the strategy of the first. Pamphilus's answer to the charge that Origen taught transmigration is not that someone inserted it; it is that here are the passages where he denies it. The nearest thing in the Apology to Rufinus's thesis is a version of it that appears in the other, anonymous, later defence Photius read — whose author, Photius says, “takes great trouble to prove that they were only intended as a rhetorical exercise, or that they were foisted into his writings by certain heterodox persons.”29

Notes

  • Rufinus's preface to his translation states plainly whose judgement he is offering, and why he expects trouble for offering it: “It is true that it is not my opinion about him that you have asked for, but that of the holy martyr Pamphilus… nevertheless I do not doubt that there will be some who will think themselves aggrieved if I say anything in his defence even in the words of another man.”30 He was right; the translation is what began the Latin quarrel traced on the Origenist controversy.
  • Pamphilus is also the source, through Photius, for the only detailed account of the proceedings Demetrius of Alexandria brought against Origen in the 230s — two sittings, expulsion then deposition. Whether that account establishes the sentence was disciplinary rather than doctrinal is weighed on its own page, where the fact that our only witness is Origen's own defender is part of what has to be reckoned with.
  • Book VI of the Apology, Eusebius's own, contains the counter-attack on Methodius that Jerome would later find so convenient a precedent.
