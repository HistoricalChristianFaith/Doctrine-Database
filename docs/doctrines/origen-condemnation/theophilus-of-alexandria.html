Theophilus of Alexandria on the condemnation of Origen

Dates: died 15 October 412; patriarch of Alexandria from 385 · Wikipedia
Summary: Theophilus is the point at which the condemnation of Origen stops being the work of individual writers and becomes the act of sitting bishops. Between 399 and 403 he changed sides in the Egyptian monastic quarrel, drove the Origenist monks out of Nitria, convened a council at Alexandria that condemned Origen's books, procured a parallel condemnation from Epiphanius on Cyprus, circulated a synodical letter enumerating eight errors, and had the campaign echoed at Rome and in an imperial rescript. This page covers his share of the timeline of verdicts passed on Origen: what his synods actually decreed, whether it was the man or the books they condemned, what evidence of the proceedings survives, and what he did with Origen afterwards. The wider quarrel these years produced — his destruction of John Chrysostom — is told on its own timeline, and its Latin half on another.

The turn of 399

He had been on the other side

Egypt in the 390s was arguing whether God has a body and a human shape; the learned monks said no, the simpler said yes, and Theophilus had preached the learned view — the view Origen's books were used to support — in church and in his paschal letter. A crowd of desert ascetics came to Alexandria to kill him for it, and he bought his way out in a sentence:

‘If you really admit that God's countenance is such as ours, anathematize Origen's book; for some drawing arguments from them oppose themselves to our opinion. If you will not do this, expect to be treated by us as an impious person, and the enemy of God.’ ‘But as far as I am concerned,’ said Theophilus, ‘I will readily do what you require: and be ye not angry with me, for I myself also disapprove of Origen's works, and consider those who countenance them deserving of censure.’1

Socrates, who reports the exchange, adds that the concession was tactical only — “Now although Theophilus was really unchanged in sentiment, and had denounced those who thought that the divinity was human in form, yet on account of his hatred of others, he openly denied his own convictions.”2 Sozomen tells the same story and gives the same verdict. Whether that judgement is fair to Theophilus, and what else was driving the campaign, belongs to the Chrysostom timeline; what matters here is the sequence, which is not in dispute among the sources: the anathema on Origen was demanded of him by a mob and granted on the spot, and the synods followed.

Nitria (400)

He then went to the monasteries of Nitria in person and expelled the party he had been aligned with. His own account of it, written to Jerome at Bethlehem, is a single triumphant sentence: “we have cut down with the prophet's sickle certain wicked fanatics who were eager to sow broadcast in the monasteries of Nitria the heresy of Origen.” He asked Jerome to take up the same work in Latin: “Do you therefore on your part, as you hope to receive a share in this reward, make haste to bring back with scriptural discourses those who have been deceived.”3 Writing to Epiphanius of Salamis in the same year he cast it in the imagery of Ephesians: the church “not having spot or wrinkle or any such thing” has “with the sword of the gospel cut down the Origenist serpents crawling out of their caves, and has delivered from their deadly contagion the fruitful host of the monks of Nitria.”4 What the expulsion looked like from the other side — a night raid, burned cells, an old monk throttled — is told on the Chrysostom timeline from the contemporary who wrote Chrysostom's defence.

What the synods decreed

The Alexandrian council of 400 and its synodical letter

The council met at Alexandria in 400 and its findings were circulated as a synodical letter addressed — in two identically worded copies, whose lists of names are preserved — to the bishops of Palestine gathered at Jerusalem and to the bishops of Cyprus. Jerome translated it into Latin; the standard English edition gives it in epitome rather than in full, and the epitome runs: the writer has “personally visited the monasteries of Nitria” and found the heresy rife there; “the books of Origen have been read before a council of bishops and unanimously condemned”; and then eight articles, said to be drawn “mainly” from Peri Archōn:

1. The Son compared with us is truth, but compared with the Father he is falsehood. 2. Christ's kingdom will one day come to an end. 3. We ought to pray to the Father alone, not to the Son. 4. Our bodies after the resurrection will be corruptible and mortal. 5. There is nothing perfect even in heaven; the angels themselves are faulty, and some of them feed on the Jewish sacrifices. 6. The stars are conscious of their own movements, and the demons know the future by their courses. 7. Magic, if real, is not evil. 8. Christ suffered once for men; he will suffer again for the demons.5

Two features of the list matter for the arc of this timeline. It is an inheritance, not a discovery: its second article — that Christ's kingdom will end — stands word for word in the summary of Epiphanius's Panarion entry of some twenty-three years before, and its fourth is the resurrection charge Methodius had pressed a century before that. And the rest of the letter is not doctrinal at all: it complains that the Origenists have stirred up the pagans over the destruction of the Serapeum, have tried to shield two people accused of crimes, have seized the monastery church at Nitria, and have tried to murder him. The document that first makes Origen the subject of a conciliar finding is also, in its second half, a brief in a personal quarrel.

Cyprus, and the request Epiphanius did not quite grant

Theophilus did not want the Alexandrian sentence to stand alone, and the letter in which he asked Epiphanius for a second one states plainly what he was after:

As your excellency has often fought in contests of the kind before me, it is your present duty to strengthen the hands of those who are in the field and to gather together to this end the bishops of your entire island. A synodical letter should be sent to myself and the bishop of Constantinople and to any others whom you think fit; that by universal consent Origen himself may be expressly condemned and also the infamous heresy of which he was the author.6

The same letter names the three monks who had taken ship for Constantinople and asks that the decrees be sent there by a fast messenger — which is why the historians read the Cyprus synod as a move in the campaign against Chrysostom rather than a proceeding about Origen. Epiphanius did convene it: he “assembled the bishops of Cyprus together, and prohibited the examination of the books of Origen,” and wrote round to other bishops urging them to do the same; Theophilus, seeing it prosper, “passed a vote similar to that of Epiphanius, with the concurrence of the bishops under his jurisdiction.”7

The man, or the books?

Theophilus had asked that “Origen himself” be “expressly condemned.” That is not what the decree said. Socrates saw the document, carried to Constantinople in 403 and read out there, and describes it exactly:

he brought also with him a copy of the synodical decree in which they did not excommunicate Origen himself but condemned his books.8

The same distinction runs through every other report of these synods. Epiphanius on Cyprus “prohibited the examination of the books”; Theophilus at Alexandria “caused a like sentence of condemnation to be pronounced on the writings of Origen”; the synodical letter says that “the books of Origen have been read before a council of bishops and unanimously condemned”; Theophilus “wrote to the bishops of every city, condemning the books of Origen.”9 The synods of 400 condemned propositions and prohibited reading; they did not pass sentence on the person of a presbyter a hundred and fifty years dead. The step Theophilus asked for is taken elsewhere — by Epiphanius on his own authority in a Constantinople church, where he “condemned the books of Origen, excommunicated Dioscorus with his followers, and charged John with countenancing them” — and, finally and canonically, only in the sixth century.10 It is a distinction the later tradition stopped making, and it is one of the reasons the fifteen anathemas of the 550s and the eleventh canon of 553 look like a continuation of 400 when they are in substance a new departure.

What survives of the proceedings

No acts of either synod survive — no attendance list beyond the addressees, no minutes, no record of what was read out or by whom. What survives is the correspondence: the synodical letter in Jerome's Latin, the Palestinian bishops' reply, and Theophilus's private letters to Jerome and to Epiphanius. That the decrees were in circulation is well attested: Jerome wrote to Alexandria asking for them — “I beg that if you have any synodical decrees bearing upon the subject you will forward them to me, that, strengthened with the authority of so great a prelate, I may open my mouth for Christ with more freedom and confidence” — and Epiphanius carried a copy to Constantinople three years later.11 The Palestinian bishops answered that they had done everything asked of them, that Origenism was not to be found in their province, that the doctrines described in the letter were never heard there, and that they would receive no one Theophilus had excommunicated — a reply that grants the request and, read closely, disputes the premise.12

An eyewitness at Alexandria

The best independent account of what the Egyptian synods were doing comes from a Gallic traveller, Postumianus, who reached Alexandria in the middle of it and told the story afterwards to Sulpicius Severus. He is a sympathetic outsider — he thinks the objectionable doctrines really are Origen's — and his report is the most precise statement we have of what the bishops were asking for:

There we found a disgraceful strife raging between the bishops and monks, the cause or occasion of which was that the priests were known when assembled together often to have passed decrees in crowded synods to the effect that no one should read or possess the books of Origen… The bishops, struggling against these positions to the utmost extent of their power, insisted that what was quite correct in the writings of Origen should, along with the author himself, and even his whole works, be condemned, because those books were more than sufficient which the church had received. They also said that the reading was to be avoided of such works as would do more harm to the unwise than they would benefit the wise.13

Three further things are in his account. The defence the Origenist monks were running was the interpolation thesis — that the offending passages “had been inserted by the heretics” and that the rest should not fall with them — the argument Rufinus was pressing in Latin at the same moment and which is weighed on its own page. The particular passage that “especially excited bad feeling” when the bishops read their extracts aloud was the salvation of the devil: that Christ, having tasted death for men, “was, by the same course of suffering, even to render the devil a partaker of redemption” — the eighth article of the synodical letter, and the doctrine that would still be the ninth of Justinian's anathemas a hundred and forty years later. Theophilus pressed its sharpest form in his festal letter for 401, that Christ “should be affixed to the cross for demons” a second time — a charge he argues as a reductio (a passion for demons would make Christ “become a demon,” and to crucify Him afresh is what Hebrews 6:6 forbids) rather than quotes; whether Origen taught it is weighed on its own page, which finds it a hostile hardening of a speculation Origen floated and disowned. And the enforcement was civil: when the tumult could not be quelled, “the governor of the city was called upon to regulate the discipline of the church by a perverse precedent; and through the terror which he inspired, the brethren were dispersed, while the monks took to flight in different directions.”14 Postumianus, who is no friend of Origen, thought this went too far: “although perhaps it may seem that they ought to have obeyed the bishops, yet such a multitude of persons, all living in an open confession of Christ, ought not for that reason to have been persecuted, especially by bishops.”15

The dissent

The decree was not received without objection, and the objection is recorded in the words of the bishop who made it. When Epiphanius produced his copy at Constantinople and read it to the bishops resident there, he was “not being able to assign any reason for this judgment, than that it seemed fit to Theophilus and himself to reject them”; some subscribed out of respect for him, and many did not. Theotimus of Scythia answered him:

‘I neither choose, Epiphanius,’ said he, ‘to insult the memory of one who ended his life piously long ago; nor dare I be guilty of so impious an act, as that of condemning what our predecessors did not reject: and especially when I know of no evil doctrine contained in Origen's books.’ Having said this, he brought forward one of that author's works, and reading a few passages therefrom, showed that the sentiments propounded were in perfect accordance with the orthodox faith. He then added, ‘Those who speak evil of these writings are unconsciously casting dishonor upon the sacred volume whence their principles are drawn.’16

Sozomen reports the same speech in slightly different words — that “it was not right to cast insult on the memory of one who had long been numbered with the dead; nor was it without blasphemy to assail the conclusion to which the ancients had arrived on the subject, and to set aside their decisions” — and adds that Theotimus “drew forth a book of Origen's which he had brought with him” and read from it.17 Two objections are being made at once, and both recur for the next fifteen hundred years: that the man is dead and cannot answer, and that the church of the preceding century did not think these books heretical. Chrysostom's own position was procedural in the same way — he declined to subscribe, “saying that nothing ought to be done rashly before investigation by a general council.”18

The paschal letters, and the reach of the sentence

Theophilus kept the case in front of his province in the annual paschal letters that announced the date of Easter. Those for 401, 402 and 404 all turn from the festival to Apollinaris and Origen; Jerome translated each into Latin and sent them to Rome, and they survive only because he did. Forwarding the letter for 402 he took the trouble to enclose the Greek as well, “because in the preceding year complaints have been made that his translation was not accurate”; forwarding that for 404 he apologized that grief for Paula's death and five days of fever had delayed him.19 Jerome's covering letter of 402 also states, better than anything else written at the time, what the difference was between this condemnation and the first one:

Demetrius expelled Origen from the city of Alexander; but he is now thanks to Theophilus outlawed from the whole world.20

The claim was not empty. At Rome in 400, with Rufinus's Latin Peri Archōn in front of him, Pope Anastasius I held a synod at Theophilus's prompting and wrote to Milan that “everything written in days gone by Origen that is contrary to our faith is even by us rejected and condemned”; a year later he told John of Jerusalem that Origen's “object was to disintegrate our faith” and referred to the imperial decree “by which every one who serves God is warned against the reading of Origen.”21 Within two years of the Alexandrian council the sentence had been echoed in Palestine, Cyprus, Rome and the imperial chancery.

Afterwards

The campaign ended in 403 with Chrysostom deposed at the Synod of the Oak, where — as both historians note with some astonishment — Origen was not mentioned at all; and it ended for Theophilus a few months later with a flight home to Alexandria and the annulment of everything the Oak had decreed. Socrates then records what he did with the books:

And when he was asked why he thus countenanced what he had publicly condemned, he replied, ‘Origen's books are like a meadow enameled with flowers of every kind. If, therefore, I chance to find a beautiful one among them, I cull it: but whatever appears to me to be thorny, I step over, as that which would prick.’22

As a rule for reading a difficult author it is defensible, and it is close to what Origen's defenders had been saying; as the settled practice of the bishop whose synod had just made possessing those books an offence, it is the sharpest evidence in the whole record that the condemnation of 400 was framed for a purpose other than the one it stated.

Notes

  • This is the first condemnation of Origen by standing episcopal synods rather than by an individual writer or a single bishop's court. Demetrius's sentence of c. 231–232 was passed by an Alexandrian gathering too, but on a living presbyter and on his standing; Methodius wrote a dialogue and Epiphanius a catalogue. From 400 the charges have a conciliar form and travel by synodical letter, and the later tradition cites them as findings rather than as opinions.
  • The eight articles of 400 are not the list finally enforced. Four of Justinian's nine anathemas of 543 have no counterpart here, and the doctrines that dominate the sixth-century condemnation — the fall of minds from contemplation, the equality of the end with the beginning — are absent; what carries forward from this list is the end of Christ's kingdom, the resurrection body, and the salvation of the demons.
  • Theophilus held the see for twenty-seven years, died on 15 October 412, and is venerated as a saint in the Coptic and Syriac Orthodox churches; his nephew and successor was Cyril of Alexandria.23 He appears on this wiki also on the Theophilus–Chrysostom timeline, which has his life and career at length.
