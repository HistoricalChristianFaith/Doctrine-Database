Justinian and the edict to Menas (543)

Dates: c. 482 – 14 November 565; emperor from 527; the edict to Menas issued c. 543 · Wikipedia
Summary: This is the decisive move in the whole story, and the one most often skipped over in favour of the council ten years later. In or about 543 the emperor Justinian addressed to Menas, patriarch of Constantinople, a long treatise against Origen with a set of extracts from Peri Archōn and nine anathemas attached, and instructed him to have the bishops resident in the capital subscribe it. Here for the first time the charges against Origen take the numbered, canonical form in which they were handed down — and they do so by imperial law, on the prompting of a monastic quarrel in the Judaean desert that had very little to do with the third-century Alexandrian. This page covers that document's occasion, its text, its authority, and the odd afterlife it has had as a witness to Origen's own words. It is one block of the timeline of verdicts passed on Origen; the disputed question of what the council of 553 did with it is treated there.

The occasion: a quarrel in the Judaean desert

The revival of the Origenist question in the sixth century was monastic, Palestinian, and in the first instance a fight between rival parties of monks rather than a reaction to anything Origen had written. The narrative source is Cyril of Scythopolis, a monk of the Judaean desert writing in the 550s, who tells the story in the closing chapters of his Life of Sabas; he is a partisan of the anti-Origenist side and was himself among the monks who took over the New Laura after its Origenists were expelled, so his account is evidence of what one party believed its opponents held rather than a neutral record.1

The shape of it is not in dispute. A learned Origenist current, indebted less to Origen than to Evagrius of Pontus, had taken root in the Palestinian lauras, and its centre was the New Laura near Tekoa, where Nonnus and Leontius of Byzantium had settled around 520. The aged Sabas, founder of the Great Laura, travelled to Constantinople in 531 and asked Justinian, among other things, for imperial help against them; he was honoured, his requests were granted, and nothing effective was done. He died the following year. In 537 the new abbot of the Great Laura, Gelasius, had an anti-Origenist book read out to his monks; the Origenists among them were expelled and joined Nonnus at the New Laura, and the quarrel turned violent. Meanwhile two monks from the Origenist side, Domitian and Theodore Askidas, had come to a synod in the capital and been made bishops there — Domitian of Ancyra, Theodore of Caesarea in Cappadocia — and neither went to reside in his see. Evagrius Scholasticus, writing a generation later, describes what Theodore had instead: he was “constantly about the person of Justinian, as being trusty and highly serviceable to him.”2 The anti-Origenist party answered in kind. Gelasius and Sophronius drew up a memorandum against Origen for the patriarch of Jerusalem; it was put into the hands of the papal representative at Constantinople, the deacon Pelagius, who brought it before the emperor. The edict followed.

Two features of that background matter for reading the document it produced. The occasion was a dispute about jurisdiction and monastic discipline in Palestine, prosecuted at court by men with access to the emperor; and the doctrines complained of were those of living sixth-century monks, whom their opponents called Origenists and who called themselves nothing of the kind.

The document

What Justinian issued is a treatise, not a canon: a Liber adversus Origenem setting out the grounds of condemnation, followed by a series of excerpts from Peri Archōn — some two dozen of them — and then the anathemas. It was addressed to Menas as patriarch of the capital, with the instruction that the bishops then resident in Constantinople (the synodos endēmousa, the “home synod”) subscribe it; copies went to the other patriarchs, and Pope Vigilius, who was in Constantinople at the time, gave his adherence in writing.3

The nine anathemas

In Richard Price's translation they run:

1. If anyone says or holds that the souls of human beings pre-exist, as previously minds and holy powers, but that they reached satiety with divine contemplation and turned to what is worse and for this reason grew old in the love of God and are therefore called souls, and were made to descend into bodies as a punishment, let him be anathema.

2. If anyone says or holds that the Lord's soul pre-existed and came into being united to God the word before the incarnation and birth from a virgin, let him be anathema.

3. If anyone holds or says that the body of our Lord Jesus Christ was first formed in the womb of the holy Virgin and that afterwards both God the word and the soul, being pre-existent, were united to it, let him be anathema.

4. If anyone says or holds that the Word of God became like all the heavenly orders, becoming cherubim for the cherubim, seraphim for the seraphim, and becoming (in a word) like all the powers above, let him be anathema.

5. If anyone says or holds that at the resurrection the bodies of human beings will be raised spherical and does not profess that we shall be raised upright, let him be anathema.

6. If anyone says or holds that heaven, sun, moon, stars, and the waters above the heavens are ensouled and rational powers, let him be anathema.

7. If anyone says or holds that in the age to come Christ the Master will be crucified on behalf of demons as well as on behalf of human beings, let him be anathema.

8. If anyone says or holds that God's power is finite and that he created [only] what he could grasp and comprehend, or that creation is coeternal with God, let him be anathema.

9. If anyone says or holds that the punishment of demons and impious human beings is temporary and that it will have an end at some time, and that there will be a restoration of demons and impious human beings, let him be anathema.4

They are sometimes counted as ten rather than nine; the older Latin handbooks describe the edict as appending “twenty-four censurable texts” from Peri Archōn and “ten propositions to be anathematized.”5 The discrepancy is one of division rather than of substance.

Its authority

The edict is habitually cited as “the synod of Constantinople of 543”, and Cyril reports that Menas and the bishops signed. But subscription was not ratification. Richard Price, who edited and translated the acts of the council that followed, states the position exactly: the canons were issued as an imperial decree and sent to the patriarchs “not for their confirmation but for their circulation. Their authority was imperial rather than synodal.”6 No acts of any such synod survive; what survives is the emperor's document and the fact that everybody put their name to it — including, as it happens, Theodore Askidas, whose own party the edict was aimed at, and who went on being the most influential churchman at court for another fifteen years.

That is the sense in which 543, and not 553, is where the enumerated charge-sheet against Origen enters the record with force. Every previous condemnation on this timeline — Theophilus's eight articles of 400, Epiphanius's catalogue entry, Augustine's single doctrine — was a list of errors in a letter, a handbook, or a synodical epitome. From 543 the charges are numbered anathemas carrying legal force throughout the empire, and they are the form in which the fifteen anathemas of the 550s and the councils after them inherited the question.

Whose doctrines are these?

The nine do not track Peri Archōn. Some of them do reach an Origenian doctrine, if in a hardened form: the first states the fall of pre-existent minds cooled from contemplation, the ninth the temporary punishment and eventual restoration of demons — the two charges that had been running against Origen since Methodius and the nine accusations Pamphilus answered. The sixth — that heaven, sun, moon and stars are “ensouled and rational powers” — likewise reaches a genuine Origenian doctrine: he argues the living, rational heavens in his own words at Peri Archōn I.7 and Against Celsus V, though the anathema hardens his bold inquiry into flat assertion. Others have no clear counterpart in anything Origen wrote. The spherical resurrection body of the fifth and the pre-existent soul of Christ united to the Word before the incarnation in the second and third belong to the developed monastic system of the sixth century, whose sources are Evagrian rather than Origenian. The fourth — that the Word became cherubim for the cherubim — is a proposition of Palestinian Origenism, not a sentence of Origen's.

The acts of 553 preserve, in the libels the anti-Origenist monks submitted, a specimen of what the sixth-century party was actually saying. Evagrius Scholasticus quotes from the fifth head of the schedule appended to the synod's address to Justinian, giving “the blasphemous expressions uttered by private individuals belonging to what is called the New Laura”: Theodore Askidas, he reports, said “If the Apostles and Martyrs at the present time work miracles, and are already so highly honoured, unless they shall be equal with Christ in the restitution of things, in what respect is there a restitution for them?”7 That is a sixth-century monk arguing about the eventual equality of the saints with Christ. It is not a quotation from Peri Archōn, and nothing like it stands there. Whether the anathemas of 543 and the fifteen that followed strike Origen or only the men who had made a system out of him is the question the modern reassessment turns on, and it is weighed on the summary timeline.

The edict as evidence for Origen's text

There is a further consequence, and it runs the other way. Peri Archōn survives whole only in Rufinus's Latin of 398, which Rufinus admitted to having tidied; the substantial Greek of the work that survives is largely what hostile readers quoted in order to condemn it, and Justinian's edict is the single largest such deposit. That put a temptation in front of the editors. Paul Koetschau's critical edition of 1913 — the one G. W. Butterworth translated in 1936, and thus the De Principiis every English reader had for the next eighty years — treated Rufinus as systematically untrustworthy and Justinian, along with Jerome, as a reliable witness, and accordingly broke up the Latin to insert the Greek fragments where he judged Rufinus had suppressed something. The effect was that readers of Origen in English met the words of his prosecutors set out as Origen's own.8

The correction has been slow and is now general. Crouzel and Simonetti's edition argued that several of the passages Koetschau imported are not exact quotations of Peri Archōn at all, and that Justinian probably never read the book, working instead from the dossier the Palestinian monks supplied. John Behr's Oxford edition of 2017 restores Rufinus's text as the text and relegates the Justinianic material to an appendix, with an introduction explaining why it is not what Koetschau took it for.9 A hostile prosecutor's brief is a poor edition of the accused's book, and for most of the twentieth century it was serving as one.

Notes

  • Justinian is not, on this timeline, a theologian giving a verdict but a legislator enacting one. The edict of 543 is of a piece with the rest of his religious legislation, and it ends the way the others do — in subscription rather than debate. Evagrius Scholasticus, no friend of the emperor's later course, notes that Justinian afterwards “proceeded to compel the bishops in all quarters to give their assent” to a quite different and less orthodox edict of his own.10
  • The relation of these nine to the fifteen anathemas usually printed as the council's is disputed. The prevailing view, following Franz Diekamp, is that the fifteen were the work of bishops already gathered at Constantinople in the weeks before the council opened on 5 May 553, and that they restate the same targets in sharper and more explicitly Christological terms. Both sets are treated on the summary timeline.
  • Two ancient narratives of the affair survive and do not agree in their mechanics: Cyril of Scythopolis's Life of Sabas and Liberatus of Carthage's Breviarium. Where they diverge, the reconstruction above follows neither exclusively.
