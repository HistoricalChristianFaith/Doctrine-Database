Methodius of Olympus on the condemnation of Origen

Dates: d. c. 311, martyred · Wikipedia
Summary: Methodius is the first man known to have written at length against Origen's teaching rather than against his standing. In three works — a dialogue on the resurrection (the Aglaophon), a treatise on created things, and a lost book on the witch of Endor — he attacked the pre-existence of souls, the eternity of the world, and above all Origen's account of the risen body. He did it in the form of a philosophical dialogue, not a denunciation; he nowhere calls Origen a heretic, and in the middle of the attack he cites him approvingly on a point of exegesis. This page covers his share of the timeline of verdicts passed on Origen: what he actually charged, on what grounds, how Caesarea answered him, and how his dialogue became, seventy years later, the body of Epiphanius's heresy-entry.

The man, the see, and the silence of Eusebius

What Jerome records

The earliest account of him is Jerome's, in the catalogue of 392:

Methodius, bishop of Olympus in Lycia and afterwards of Tyre, composed books Against Porphyry written in polished and logical style also a Banquet of the ten virgins, an excellent work On the resurrection, against Origen and On the Pythonissa and On free will, also against Origen. He also wrote commentaries On Genesis and On the Song of Songs and many others which are widely read. At the end of the recent persecution or, as others affirm, in the reign of Decius and Valerianus, he was crowned with martyrdom at Chalcis in Greece.1

Three of the six titles Jerome lists are anti-Origen: On the Resurrection, On the Pythonissa (against Origen's reading of the witch of Endor in 1 Samuel 28), and On Free Will. Jerome's own alternative date is impossible — a man who answered Porphyry, writing in the 270s, cannot have died under Decius and Valerian in the 250s — and the martyrdom belongs where he first puts it, at the end of the last persecution, conventionally about 311.

Olympus, Patara, Tyre, Philippi

The see is the least secure thing about him. Jerome's transfer to Tyre is otherwise unattested, and the episcopal list for Tyre in those years is occupied; some manuscripts and later writers call him bishop of Patara, which is most likely borrowed from the dramatic setting of the Aglaophon rather than from any record; some name Philippi in Macedonia, which would suit Jerome's placing of the martyrdom in Greece. The standard reference article settles for Olympus and treats the rest as later confusion.2 His own writing supplies one small confirmation of the Lycian setting: describing a marvel he says he witnessed personally, he places it “on Olympus, which is a mountain of Lycia.”3

Eusebius does not mention him

The Church History, the fullest surviving record of the generation in which Methodius lived and died, has no entry for him at all — no notice of his works, and none of his martyrdom, though Eusebius catalogues the martyrs of that persecution at length. The omission is generally read as partisan: Eusebius, Origen's biographer and defender, had no wish to advertise the man who had attacked him.4 It is the first sign that by the early fourth century the question of Origen had already become a question of sides.

What he actually charged

That the body is not a fetter, and was not put on after a fall

The Aglaophon is a dialogue: Methodius argues against a named interlocutor, Aglaophon, who holds the Origenist position, and the tone throughout is that of an argument among Christians. The ninth-century epitome of it opens by naming the target:

Read the Book on the Resurrection by St. Methodius, Bishop and Martyr, of which that which follows is a selection, that the body is not the fetter of the soul, as Origen thought, nor are souls called by the prophet Jeremiah “fettered” on account of their being within bodies… That Origen said that the body was given to the soul as a fetter after the fall, and that previously it lived without a body; but that this body which we wear is the cause of our sins; wherefore also he called it a fetter, as it can hinder the soul from good works.5

His counter-argument is empirical rather than dogmatic. If the body was imposed as a restraint on evil, it plainly failed:

But surely we see that, at the beginning, Cain, clad in this body, committed murder; and it is evident into what wickedness those who succeeded him ran. The body is not, then, a fetter upon evil, nor indeed a fetter at all; nor was the soul clothed in it for the first time after the fall.6

Behind this stands a definition of man he thinks Origen has taken from Plato rather than from Scripture: “man, with respect to his nature, is most truly said to be neither soul without body, nor, on the other hand, body without soul; but a being composed out of the union of soul and body into one form of the beautiful. But Origen said that the soul alone is man, as did Plato.”7 That is the pre-existence charge in its earliest form — two and a half centuries before it was made the first of Justinian's anathemas, and made against a doctrine Origen had expressly filed among the questions he took the Church to have left open.

That the flesh itself rises, not merely its form

This is the heart of the dispute, and Methodius states his opponent's position with care before attacking it. Origen, he says, holds that the material of the body is in constant flux and so cannot be what rises; what persists and is raised is the form — the individuating shape — stamped now on a spiritual body:

Origen therefore thinks that the same flesh will not be restored to the soul, but that the form of each, according to the appearance by which the flesh is now distinguished, shall arise stamped upon another spiritual body; so that every one will again appear the same form; and that this is the resurrection which is promised.8

His reply is that a form has no existence apart from the matter it shapes, and perishes with it: melt a bronze statue and the shape does not survive somewhere, waiting to be re-imposed, for “the shape which disappears around the brass is separated from the melted statue, and has no longer a substantial existence.” Hence “it is, therefore, untenable to say, that the form shall arise in nowise corrupted, but that the body in which the form was stamped shall be destroyed.”9 And he presses a scriptural difficulty: if the resurrection is the appearing of the form only, then Moses and Elijah at the Transfiguration had already risen, and Christ is not “the first begotten of the dead.”10

In the surviving Greek of the dialogue itself he puts the same point without the technical apparatus, and the complaint is about method as much as doctrine — that the allegorizing is being driven by the conclusion:

For I cannot endure the trifling of some who shamelessly do violence to Scripture, in order that their opinion, that the resurrection is without flesh, may find support; supposing rational bones and flesh, and in different ways changing it backwards and forwards by allegorizing.11

He reads the “coats of skins” of Genesis 3:21 not as bodies but as mortality — God's remedy, not God's sentence: death was appointed “in order that, by the dissolution of the body, sin might be altogether destroyed from the very roots”, as a fig-tree rooted in a temple wall must be torn out before the stones can be reset.12 And he refuses the inference from “they are as the angels in heaven” to a fleshless resurrection: Christ “did not say ‘they shall be angels,’ but like angels,” in respect of not marrying — as one may say the moon shines like the sun without saying that the moon is the sun.13

That the world is not co-eternal with God

The second front is the treatise On Things Created, of which a synopsis survives. Here Methodius takes on the argument that God cannot have become a Creator, since a maker with nothing made is no maker — so the world must always have existed:

For he says, since there is no workman without some work, or maker without something made, so neither is there an Almighty without an object of His power… And so it must be, that these things were made by God from the beginning, and that there was no time in which they did not exist… And it will result that the unchangeable and unaltered God has altered and changed.14

Methodius answers it twice over. First, that it makes God's perfection depend on the world: if God is Almighty and Creator on account of what He rules and makes, then “He would by Himself be imperfect and have need of these things” — whereas “He is His own riches, and is by Himself rich and powerful.” Second, that the change-objection cuts both ways, since God rested from making: “If, then, the act of making, after not making, makes an alteration in God, does not His ceasing to make after making the same?” The interlocutor arguing the Origenist side is addressed in the dialogue as “O Centaur.”15

Error, not heresy

Nothing in what survives asks anyone to condemn Origen, and no ecclesiastical authority is invoked. Origen is treated as a living opponent in argument — addressed in the second person, “If then, O Origen, you maintain…” — not as a heresiarch. He is even cited approvingly in the middle of the attack, on the etymology of Hades: by hell is meant “the air, derived from aeïdēs, because it is invisible, as was said by Origen.”16 The point mattered enough to be noticed in the fifth century: Socrates, defending Origen, observes that his critics attacked different doctrines and none of them touched him on the Trinity, so that “those who have sought out whatever they deemed worthy of reprobation in him, and yet have never charged him with holding unsound views respecting the holy Trinity, are in this way most distinctly shown to bear witness to his orthodox piety.”17 Whatever Methodius was doing, he was not drawing up a charge-sheet.

The answer from Caesarea

Origen's own school replied, and replied in kind. In the sixth and last book of the Apology for Origen — the book Eusebius added after Pamphilus's execution — Eusebius rounded on Methodius. Jerome, who had the whole work in front of him a century later and was using it for a quite different quarrel, reports:

Eusebius the Bishop of Cæsarea, of whom I have made mention above, in the sixth book of his Apology for Origen makes the same complaint against Methodius the bishop and martyr, which you make against me in your praises of me.18

The complaint, as Jerome reports it, was one of inconsistency: how could Methodius write against Origen after having spoken as he had of his teaching? The charge is worth noting for what it concedes. Eusebius does not answer that Methodius has misdescribed Origen's doctrine of the risen body; he answers that Methodius has no standing to object, having drawn on him before. That is the shape the Origen quarrel would keep for the next two centuries — each side impugning the other's consistency — and it is already fully formed by 310.

What became of the attack

Methodius wrote in the ordinary genre of philosophical disagreement, and the text did not stay there. Two of his later readers preserved it, and one of them changed what it was for. Epiphanius of Salamis quarried the Aglaophon into Panarion 64, the entry that made Origen a heresy in his own right: the long stretch of the dialogue that survives entire in Greek — Methodius's own voice, not an epitome — survives precisely because Epiphanius copied it into a heresy-catalogue. The remainder comes through the ninth-century summaries of Photius, who read both the resurrection dialogue and On Things Created and abstracted them.19 A third-century disagreement about the physics of the resurrection thus reaches us in two containers, one of them a prosecution file.

Socrates, writing about 440 and defending Origen, put Methodius at the head of a list of four:

And first Methodius, bishop of a city in Lycia named Olympus, labored under this malady; next Eustathius, who for a short time presided over the church at Antioch; after him Apollinaris; and lastly Theophilus. This quaternion of revilers has traduced Origen, but not on the same grounds…20

He adds a report that is corroborated nowhere else: “Methodius indeed, when he had in various places railed against Origen, afterwards as if retracting all he had previously said, expresses his admiration of the man, in a dialogue which he entitled Xenon.”21 The Xenon is lost, and Socrates — who is arguing a case — is the only witness to the recantation; his own qualifier, “as if retracting,” is doing work. The safe conclusion is that a work of that name existed and that Socrates found admiration in it, not that Methodius withdrew his objections.

Notes

  • Two of the three positions Methodius attacked are the first and the tenth of the fifteen anathemas of the sixth century — the pre-existence of souls, and a risen body that is not this flesh. The charge-sheet is therefore older than the machinery that eventually enforced it by more than two hundred years, and was first assembled by a man who had no machinery and asked for none.
  • Whether those propositions are Origen's own or a hostile abstraction of them is the question the modern reassessment turns on, and Methodius is among the earliest evidence bearing on it: he was reading the Greek Peri Archōn and Origen's commentaries directly, a century before the text was translated, excerpted, and disputed. What he reports Origen as holding on the risen body is close to what the fourth-century accusers report — which tells against the strongest version of the interpolation thesis, weighed on its own page.
  • Origen's own account of the soul between death and resurrection is traced on the intermediate state; the pre-existence of souls on the origin of the soul.
