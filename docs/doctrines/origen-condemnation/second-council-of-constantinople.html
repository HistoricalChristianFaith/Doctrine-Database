The Second Council of Constantinople (553) on Origen

Dates: 5 May – 2 June 553, eight sessions, at Constantinople · Wikipedia
Summary: “Condemned by the Fifth Ecumenical Council” is the single most-cited fact about Origen, and it is the messiest. Three different things travel under that one sentence and have to be separated before any of them can be assessed: the fifteen anathemas against Origen and the Origenists, which the manuscripts attach to the council but which most modern scholarship assigns to a separate or preliminary proceeding; the council's own eleventh canon, which names Origen last in a roll of heresiarchs and whose text is disputed; and the reception, which from the seventh century onward treated the matter as settled and never distinguished the two. This page sets out what the documents say and where the evidence runs out. It is one block of the timeline of verdicts passed on Origen; the ten years'-earlier imperial edict that supplied the charge-list is on Justinian's page.

What the council was called for

The council was Justinian's, not the pope's. Its business was the Three Chapters — the writings of Theodore of Mopsuestia, Theodoret of Cyrrhus, and Ibas of Edessa — which the emperor had already condemned by edict in 543 and 551 and now wanted a council to endorse. Pope Vigilius had been brought from Rome to Constantinople by force in 546 and would not cooperate; he declined even to open the council, so Justinian did. Eutychius, patriarch of Constantinople, presided, with about 150 bishops in the hall attached to Hagia Sophia; the Italian bishops stayed away with Vigilius. On 14 May the pope issued his first Constitutum, condemning sixty passages from Theodore but refusing to anathematize a dead man, and forbidding the council to proceed without him. The council struck his name from the diptychs at its seventh session, and Justinian had him detained. At the eighth and last session, on 2 June, the bishops published fourteen anathemas against the Three Chapters. Vigilius gave way on 8 December, and in 554 issued a second Constitutum which does not so much as mention the council.1

Origen is not in that agenda. He had been dead three hundred years, he was not one of the Three Chapters, and — the point that has troubled everyone who has looked at the question — he is not mentioned in the summons or in the correspondence surrounding the council. The nineteenth-century English editors of the acts put the difficulty plainly:

To my mind the chief difficulty in supposing these anathematisms to have been adopted by the Fifth Ecumenical is that nothing whatever is said about Origen in the call of the council, nor in any of the letters written in connexion with it.2

The fifteen anathemas

What everyone quotes as “the condemnation of Origen” is a set of fifteen numbered anathemas, fuller and far more explicitly Christological than the nine Justinian had appended to his edict to Menas in 543. They open on the two doctrines that had been charged against Origen since Methodius, and they close on the same pair restated as a single thesis about time:

I. If anyone asserts the fabulous pre-existence of souls, and shall assert the monstrous restoration which follows from it: let him be anathema.

XV. If anyone shall say that the life of the spirits shall be like to the life which was in the beginning while as yet the spirits had not come down or fallen, so that the end and the beginning shall be alike, and that the end shall be the true measure of the beginning: let him be anathema.3

Between those two the list describes a complete system. The second anathematizes the doctrine that all reasonable creatures were originally naked intelligences, alike in substance and undifferentiated, who “no longer desiring the sight of God… gave themselves over to worse things, each one following his own inclinations, and that they have taken bodies more or less subtile, and have received names” — cherubim, seraphim, thrones, angels, the celestial ranks being so many grades of a single fall. The third condemns the view “that the sun, the moon and the stars are also reasonable beings, and that they have only become what they are because they turned towards evil.” The fourth reaches the bottom of the same ladder:

IV. If anyone shall say that the reasonable creatures in whom the divine love had grown cold have been hidden in gross bodies such as ours, and have been called men, while those who have attained the lowest degree of wickedness have shared cold and obscure bodies and are become and called demons and evil spirits: let him be anathema.4

Anathemas VI–IX turn Christological, condemning the notion that Christ is a single unfallen intelligence united to the Word rather than the Word himself, and that he passed through the ranks of beings assuming the form of each. The tenth rejects a risen body that is “ethereal” and “having the form of a sphere.” The twelfth and the fourteenth take the end of the story:

XII. If anyone shall say that the heavenly Powers and all men and the Devil and evil spirits are united with the Word of God in all respects, as the Nous which is by them called Christ and which is in the form of God, and which humbled itself as they say; and [if anyone shall say] that the Kingdom of Christ shall have an end: let him be anathema.

XIV. If anyone shall say that all reasonable beings will one day be united in one, when the hypostases as well as the numbers and the bodies shall have disappeared… moreover, that in this pretended apocatastasis, spirits only will continue to exist, as it was in the feigned pre-existence: let him be anathema.5

Two observations follow at once. The doctrine anathematized is a system, with a beginning, a fall by degrees, a graded cosmos of embodiment, and a return — and it is a system nowhere set out in that form in Peri Archōn. The spherical resurrection body, the ensouled stars, and the equality of all spirits with Christ at the end belong to the sixth-century Palestinian monks whose party name was the Isochristoi, “those equal to Christ”, and whose sources were Evagrian rather than Origenian. And — the fact most often missed — Origen's name occurs in none of the fifteen. They condemn propositions, not a man.

Whose anathemas are they?

The text as we have it was found by Peter Lambeck, the imperial librarian at Vienna, in the seventeenth century, and carries in his manuscript the heading “Canons, of the 165 holy Fathers of the holy fifth Synod, held in Constantinople” — 165 being the number of bishops who subscribed the acts of 553.6 That caption is the whole of the manuscript case for attributing them to the council, and editors have doubted it since Walch and Döllinger. The alternatives proposed have been three: that the fifteen were passed by the home synod of 543 alongside Justinian's nine (Hefele's preference, which he conceded rested on nothing decisive — “We certainly possess no strong and decisive proof that the fifteen anathematisms belong to the Constantinopolitan synod of the year 543”); that they were the work of the bishops already gathered at Constantinople in the weeks before the council opened on 5 May 553, which is Franz Diekamp's reconstruction of 1899 and has been the prevailing view since; or that the council itself passed them at a sitting whose record has not survived.7 Ilaria Ramelli states the modern position in its sharpest form: the fifteen “were already prepared by Justinian, or his counselors, before the opening of the council, and were appended to his letter to the conciliar fathers,” where “Origen is considered to be the inspirer of the so-called Isochristoi.”8 Norman Tanner's standard edition of the conciliar decrees prints the acts of 553 without them, on the ground that “recent studies have shown that these anathemas cannot be attributed to this council.”9

Against that stands one early and well-placed witness. Cyril of Scythopolis, a Judaean monk writing within five years of the council, closes his Life of Sabas by reporting that the assembled fathers issued a common and universal anathema against Origen, Theodore of Mopsuestia, and the teaching of Evagrius and Didymus on pre-existence and restoration, with the four patriarchs present and consenting. He is the earliest narrative source for what happened, and he is also a partisan of the party that had petitioned for the condemnation and took over the New Laura after the Origenists were expelled from it — a summary of what one side believed it had won, written by a man who wanted it to be true.10 The nineteenth-century editors, weighing the same materials, refused to decide: “It would seem that with the evidence now in our possession it would be the height of rashness to give a dogmatic answer to this question… After all is said the matter remains involved in the greatest obscurity.”11

The eleventh canon

The council's own fourteen anathemas against the Three Chapters do contain Origen's name, once, in the eleventh:

If anyone does not anathematize Arius, Eunomius, Macedonius, Apollinarius, Nestorius, Eutyches and Origen, as well as their heretical books, and also all other heretics who have already been condemned and anathematized by the holy, catholic and apostolic church and by the four holy synods which have already been mentioned, and also all those who have thought or now think in the same way as the aforesaid heretics and who persist in their error even to death: let him be anathema.12

The clause is not a trial and passes no charge: it is a loyalty formula requiring assent to condemnations already made, and Origen stands in it as a name on a list. Two features of that list have been pressed. He comes last, out of the chronological order the other six observe — Arius, Eunomius, Macedonius, Apollinaris, Nestorius, Eutyches run from the 320s to the 440s, and the man from the 230s is put after all of them. And the parallel roll in Justinian's Homonoia, the draft of the anathemas issued from the imperial chancery, does not have him. Ramelli takes both together, with the further fact that Vigilius's own documents never name Origen, as evidence that the name was inserted afterwards: “This suggests an interpolation. Indeed, several anathemas, including those which mention Origen, did not belong to the official acts of the council, but were interpolated later.”13 The Greek acts of 553 are lost and the council is known from a Latin version, which is what makes the charge possible to bring and hard to settle. The older editors thought it not worth entertaining — “That Origen was condemned by name in the Eleventh Canon of this council there seems no possible reason to doubt” — and even they added that this settles nothing about the fifteen.14 The question is weighed on its own terms on the summary timeline.

The reception

Whatever the acts contained, the church read them one way. Constantinople III (680–681) described “the fifth holy synod, the latest of them, which was gathered here against Theodore of Mopsuestia, Origen, Didymus and Evagrius”; Nicaea II (787) anathematized “the mythical speculations of Origen, Evagrius and Didymus, as did the fifth synod, that assembled at Constantinople.”15 Neither reopened anything; both ratify by reference, and both harden the Origen–Didymus–Evagrius triad into a formula that the medieval East and West inherited whole. It is worth noting that the fathers of 680 were themselves already worried about the integrity of the Greek acts of 553, having found reason to suspect that they had been tampered with.16 From the seventh century the distinction between the fifteen anathemas, the eleventh canon, and Origen's own books is simply not drawn by anyone; the slogan does the work.

Notes

  • The three questions are separable and have different answers. That the fifteen anathemas exist, and are of the 550s, is certain. That the council in session passed them is doubted by most modern editors and asserted by the earliest narrative witness. That the eleventh canon names Origen is what the Latin acts say; whether it said so in 553 is disputed on the strength of the Homonoia and Vigilius's silence. That the church from 680 onward believed Origen had been condemned personally by an ecumenical council is not in doubt at all.
  • Henri Crouzel, whose Origène remains the mature statement of the case, concluded that Origen was never officially condemned by the church — a judgement about the canonical form of the proceedings, not about whether the doctrines in the anathemas are objectionable.17
  • The same council, at its eighth session, gave the term aeiparthenos — ever-virgin — its conciliar standing, which is treated on the perpetual virginity timeline.18
