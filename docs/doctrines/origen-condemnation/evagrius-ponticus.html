Evagrius Ponticus on the condemnation of Origen

Dates: c. 345–399 · Wikipedia
Summary: Evagrius is the hinge of this whole story, though he is not one of Origen's judges. A deacon of the Cappadocian circle turned desert monk, he took the boldest speculations of Peri Archōn — the pre-cosmic unity of minds, their fall, the graded cosmos, and the final restoration — and forged them into a tight, numbered metaphysics in his Kephalaia Gnostica. It is that system, not Origen's third-century book, that the sixth-century Palestinian monks held and the fifteen anathemas of 553 describe almost clause for clause; so the modern case that the council condemned an Evagrian Origenism turns on the text set out here. This page covers his place on the timeline of verdicts passed on Origen: who he was, what the Kephalaia Gnostica actually says in its unexpurgated form, why later scholarship reads the anathemas back onto him rather than Origen — and the closing irony that the church went on reading this condemned author too, for centuries, under a saint's name.

The man

Cappadocian deacon, then desert monk

Evagrius was born about 345 in Pontus — at Ibora, the son of a country-bishop — and formed in the circle of the Cappadocian Fathers. The Philokalia's own note on him states the pedigree plainly:

Evagrios the Solitary, also known as Evagrios Pontikos, was born in 345 or 346, probably at Ibora in Pontus… A disciple of the Cappadocian Fathers, he was ordained reader by St Basil the Great and deacon by St Gregory the Theologian (Gregory of Nazianzos), and he accompanied the latter to the Council of Constantinople in 381 (the second Ecumenical Council).1

He stayed on in the capital under Nectarius, a celebrated preacher and controversialist; then, by his biographer Palladius's account, an entanglement with a married woman and a warning vision drove him to flee. He was received at Jerusalem by Melania the Elder and Rufinus — the same Rufinus whose Latin Peri Archōn would later light the Origenist controversy — and in 383 went down into Egypt, first to the monks of Nitria and then to the remoter settlement of the Cells (Kellia), where he spent the rest of his life under Macarius the Great and Macarius of Alexandria. He died there in 399, in his fifty-fourth year, the same year Theophilus of Alexandria turned on the Origenist monks of Nitria.2 He was never a bishop, never tried, never named in his lifetime as a heretic; like Origen, he died in communion, honoured as a master of the ascetic life.

The system: the Kephalaia Gnostica

Evagrius's ascetic and contemplative works — the Praktikos, the Gnostikos, the treatise On Prayer — were prized and copied for centuries. But he also wrote a work of pure speculative theology, the Kephalaia Gnostica (“Gnostic Chapters”), six “centuries” of terse, deliberately obscure sentences setting out a cosmology. It survives in two Syriac versions: a common recension (S1) that had been doctrinally cleaned up, and, in a single British Library manuscript, an unexpurgated recension (S2) recovered and published by Antoine Guillaumont in 1958. Read in the S2 form, the Kephalaia Gnostica states, in Evagrius's own numbered chapters, the scheme the sixth century would anathematize.3

An original unity of minds, and a fall from it

The beginning is a unity (henad) of created minds (logikoi) contemplating God; evil enters not by God's making but by a “movement” away from that unity:

The first movement of the logikoi is the separation of the nous from the Unity that is in it.4

The fallen mind cools into “soul,” and by degrees into the graded bodies of the present cosmos:

The soul is the nous which, through negligence, has fallen from the Unity; and through its carelessness, has descended to the rank of the praktike.5

This is Origen's pre-cosmic descent of the soul — the doctrine traced as a belief in its own right on the origin of the soul — tightened into a first principle of a system.

A restoration in which bodies and distinctions vanish

Because the end answers to the beginning, the whole graded order is finally dissolved and the minds return to their original equality:

Accompanying the knowledge that concerns the logikoi are the destruction of worlds, the dissolution of bodies, and the abolition of names; while [there] remains the equality of knowledge according to the equality of substances.6

This is the apokatastasis — the end “like to the life which was in the beginning” that the fifteenth anathema of 553 condemns as a stated principle, and the vanishing of “the hypostases as well as the numbers and the bodies” that its fourteenth condemns by name.

A Christ who is a mind, distinct from the Word

The most distinctive — and the most clearly un-Origenist — move is Christological. For Evagrius “Christ” is not simply the eternal Word but the one created mind that did not fall, the nous that stayed united to the knowledge of God and so is called anointed:

The intelligible anointing is the spiritual knowledge of the holy Unity, and the Christ is he who is united to this knowledge.7

That distinction — a nous-Christ beside the Word — is exactly what the twelfth anathema of 553 anathematizes, condemning anyone who says that the powers and all men and the devil “are united with the Word of God… as the Nous which is by them called Christ.” It is set out in the Kephalaia Gnostica; it is not in Peri Archōn.

Why he stands on this timeline

Evagrius passes no verdict on Origen; he belongs here because the verdict later passed on Origen was, in its fullest form, a verdict on Evagrius's book. When Guillaumont recovered the S2 text he argued, in a paper of 1961 and a book of 1962, that the fifteen anathemas of 553 track the propositions of the Kephalaia Gnostica almost point by point — the spherical resurrection body, the nous-Christ, the final equality of every spirit — and quote Peri Archōn nowhere. On that reading the sixth-century Palestinian Origenist monks, the Isochristoi (“equal to Christ”) and Protoktistoi (“first-created”), were Evagrians, and Origen was anathematized for a system assembled a century and a half after his death and three after his own.8

The thesis has organized the field, but it is not the last word, and two cautions belong on Evagrius's own page. First, the continuity runs the other way as much as the discontinuity: Evagrius was a self-conscious Origenist, and the Kephalaia Gnostica is a radicalization of Peri Archōn, not a rival import — so to trace the anathemas to Evagrius is to trace them to a development of Origen, whose premises (the pre-existent fall, the restoration of all) really are in the third-century book. Second, Ilaria Ramelli — who published the standard English translation of the unreformed S2 in 2015 — presses the qualification harder still, arguing that the propositions condemned under Justinian belong not to Evagrius or Origen themselves but to later Origenists who radicalized and distorted Evagrius, so that the S2 text is closer to Origen's genuine thought than to what 553 condemned. The net effect is to loosen the tie to any single author: the condemned system is harder to pin on Origen, on Evagrius, or on the third-century book than the flat verdict allows. Whether the anathemas therefore condemn an Evagrian Origenism rather than Origen is weighed on its own page.9

The condemned author the church kept reading — under a saint's name

The summary of this timeline closes on the paradox of Origen: a condemned man whom the tradition never stopped reading. Evagrius is the sharper case of the same thing, and it is worth setting beside Origen's. The Kephalaia Gnostica was suppressed, mutilated, and preserved in full only in a single Syriac manuscript that escaped the censors. But Evagrius's devotional work was never lost at all — because it travelled under another name. His treatise On Prayer (153 chapters) circulated for centuries, was printed in Migne's Patrologia Graeca among the works of St Nilus of Ancyra, and entered the eighteenth-century Greek Philokalia still ascribed to Nilus; his authorship was recovered only in the twentieth century, by Irénée Hausherr. The Philokalia's modern editors state the fact directly:

Several of Evagrios' works have come down under the name of other authors. This is the case with the writing On Prayer, which in the Greek Philokalia is ascribed to Neilos; but recent research has made it plain beyond any reasonable doubt that this is a writing of Evagrios.10

It is the exact mirror of Origen's fate on this page. The church anathematized the speculative theologian and quietly kept the spiritual master — reading, in this case, the condemned author's own prayers for a thousand years in the belief that they were a saint's.

Notes

  • Evagrius is the reason the later half of this timeline keeps saying that the sixth-century quarrel owed “more to Evagrius of Pontus than to Origen”: the Kephalaia Gnostica is the book the Palestinian monks were actually reading, and the edict of 543 and the council of 553 are answering it under Origen's name.
  • His pre-cosmic fall of minds is the same doctrine, systematized, that the origin of the soul traces; his universal restoration is the scheme weighed on the apokatastasis page.
  • The one point at which the anathemas plainly catch Evagrius and not Origen — the nous-Christ united to the Word — is also the point at which the “condemned for what was not his” defence of Origen is strongest; the countervailing point, that the pre-existence and the restoration framing the list are genuinely Origen's, is why that defence does not carry all the way. Both are set out on the Evagrian-Origenism argument page.
