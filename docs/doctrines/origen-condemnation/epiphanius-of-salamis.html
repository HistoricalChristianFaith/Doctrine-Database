Epiphanius of Salamis on the condemnation of Origen

Dates: c. 315 – 403; bishop of Salamis (Constantia) in Cyprus from 367 · Wikipedia
Summary: Epiphanius did what no one before him had done: he gave Origen a numbered entry in a catalogue of heresies. Panarion 64, “Against Origen, also called Adamantius” (c. 375–377), turns a disputed teacher into a heresiarch listed between the Melchizedekians and the Encratites, and supplies the five-item charge-sheet — the Son a creature, souls pre-existent, paradise allegorized, the flesh not raised, Christ's kingdom ending — that Theophilus, Jerome and eventually Justinian would all work from. This page covers his share of the timeline of verdicts passed on Origen: what he charged, what he quoted, what he passed on as biography, and what can be checked.

The books and their date

The Ancoratus and the Panarion

Epiphanius attacked Origen twice within a few years. The Ancoratus (“the anchored man”), written in 374 as a compact statement of the faith, already ran a long section against him; the Panarion (“medicine chest”), begun the next year and issued about 377, is the catalogue of eighty heresies from Adam to Epiphanius's own day in which Origen is number 64. The Panarion refers the reader back to the earlier book — “I have already mentioned things of this sort about him, and discussed them at length, in some of my other works”1 — and then repeats the case at far greater length, because the genre now required it: a heresy catalogue must have an entry.

Two Origens, and an admission about the first

There are two consecutive Origen entries, and Epiphanius is candid that he cannot connect them. Heresy 63 is a libertine sect he calls “the first type of Origenists, who are shameful as well”, and of these he writes:

There are people called Origenists, but this kind of Origenist is not to be found everywhere… They are named Origenists, but I am not sure after whom. I do not know whether they are derived from the Origen who is called Adamantius the Author, or from some other Origen. Still, I have learned of this name.2

Heresy 64 is Origen of Alexandria himself. Epiphanius keeps the two apart in principle — heresy 64 “does not train its disciples to perform the obscenity” — but he has placed them side by side under one name, and he opens 64 by declaring the second sect “a dreadful sect and worse than all the ancient ones,” casting on the Godhead “an evil suspicion… one worse than the obscenity.”3 The adjacency is the point of a catalogue: the reader who consults the reference work meets Origen in that company.

The charge-sheet in one sentence

Each book of the Panarion carries a summary index, and the entry there is the form in which the charges travelled. It is worth reading whole, because almost every later list is a variation on it:

64. Other Origenists, the disciples of the Origen who is called Adamantius the Author. They reject the resurrection of the dead, represent Christ and the Holy Spirit as creatures, allegorize Paradise, the heavens and all the rest, and foolishly say that Christ's kingdom will come to an end.4

Twenty-three years later the synod Theophilus of Alexandria convened in 400 issued its condemnation, and Jerome translated it into Latin; among the errors it enumerates are that “the Son compared with us is truth, but compared with the Father he is falsehood” and that “Christ's kingdom will one day come to an end.”5 The catalogue entry had become a conciliar finding.

What he actually charged

That the Son is a creature

The first charge is Trinitarian, and it is the one Epiphanius puts first because it is the one that made Origen dangerous in the 370s — the Arian controversy was the fight of his lifetime, and he wanted Origen assigned to it. “Arius took his cue from Origen,” he writes, “and so did the Anomoeans who succeeded him.” The specific accusation runs:

For Origen claims, and at once dares, if you please, to say first that the Only-begotten Son cannot see the Father, and neither can the Spirit behold the Son; and angels surely cannot behold the Spirit, nor men the angels. And this is his first downfall. For he does not believe that the Son is of the Father's essence, but represents him as entirely different from the Father, and created besides. But he holds that he is called “Son” by grace.6

The evidence he then produces for it is a single word. Quoting Origen's preface to the commentary on the first psalm, in which Origen asks Ambrose to pray to the Father “through our Savior and High Priest, the originated (genētos) God”, Epiphanius fastens on the adjective: “since I have found in many instances that Origen wrongly distinguishes between the Only-begotten God and the Father's Godhead and essence… it is plain that by saying ‘originated God’ he is pronouncing him a creature.” He then records, and rejects, the reading his contemporaries were pressing on him: “though some would like to outwit me and say that ‘originated’ is the same thing as ‘begotten,’ this is not admissible… ‘Originated’ is one thing, ‘begotten,’ another.”7 The whole Christological indictment thus rests on a terminological distinction that Epiphanius acknowledges was disputed when he made it, applied retrospectively to a writer of the 220s.

That souls pre-existed and fell into bodies

The second charge is the one with the longest future — it becomes the first of Justinian's anathemas in 543. Epiphanius states it, and then attacks the etymological arguments Origen had used to support it:

He says that the human soul is preexistent, and that souls are angels and celestial powers, but have sinned and so been shut up in this body as a punishment. They are sent down by God as a punishment, to undergo a first judgment here. And so the body is called a “frame” (dema), says Origen, because the soul has been “bound” (dedesthai) in the body, imagining the ancient Greek fabrication. And he spins other yarns about this as well. He says that we speak of a “soul” (psychē) because it has “cooled off” (psychesthai) in coming down.8

Both etymologies are in Peri Archōn, and the charge as Epiphanius frames it is close to the one Methodius had brought eighty years earlier — that the body is a fetter put on after a fall, which Methodius attacks on his own page. Epiphanius adds the exegetical evidence: Origen reads “Before I was humbled, I offended” and “Return unto thy rest, O my soul” as the soul's own speech about a heavenly offence and a heavenly homecoming, which Epiphanius characterizes as smearing “on texts from the sacred scriptures that suit him, though not as they are or with their real interpretation.”9

That Adam lost the image, and the coats of skins are bodies

Tied to the same doctrine is the reading of Genesis 3:21. “He says that Adam lost the image of God. And this is why the skin tunics are signalized in scripture, for ‘He made them tunics of skin and clothed them’ refers to the body.”10 Later in the entry Epiphanius quotes the argument he is refusing, in the second person: “Was God a tanner, to make skin tunics for Adam and Eve when no animals had yet been slaughtered?… What the scripture meant, then, was not skin tunics, but the body of earth which surrounds us.”11 Methodius had taken the same verse the other way, reading the coats as mortality rather than as flesh; the proof-text is a fixed feature of the quarrel from the third century to the sixth. Whether the flat equation “the skins are the body” is securely Origen’s own is, however, a point on which the charge outruns the surviving evidence: his own explicit remarks on Genesis 3:21 are guarded, and modern students divide over whether he held it — weighed, with Epiphanius’s wider claim that Origen’s allegory “dissolves the historical sense,” on its own page.

The first half of the charge — “Adam lost the image of God”, which in Jerome’s Latin becomes the eighth error, that the image “was lost, and was no longer in man after he was expelled from Paradise” — is on weaker ground still, for it inverts what Origen taught. Origen’s settled distinction is that man keeps the image, given at creation, and must acquire the likeness, reserved for the consummation (Peri Archōn III.6.1; Against Celsus IV.30); he goes so far as to call it “impious” to think the divine image corrupted out of man, and finds its “traces” still in the fallen. The irony is that Epiphanius refutes “Adam lost the image” with the very texts — Genesis 9:6, James 3:9 — that state Origen’s own position, having first fathered on him its contradiction; the misstatement is weighed on its own page.

That the resurrection is emptied out, and paradise allegorized

The largest single charge, and the one to which most of the entry is given, is the resurrection. Epiphanius's formulation is careful about the inconsistency he thinks he has found:

He makes the resurrection of the dead a defective thing, sometimes nominally supporting it, sometimes denying it altogether, but at other times saying that there is a partial resurrection. Finally, he gives an allegorical interpretation of whatever he can — Paradise, its waters, the waters above the heavens, the water under the earth. He never stops saying these ridiculous things and others like them.12

He rests it, again, on the commentary on the first psalm, and here he does something unusual for a heresiologist: he prints the passage he objects to, at length, before answering it. What he prints is Origen pressing the physical difficulties of a resurrection of this same flesh — whether “all the blood that has been lost in bleedings will rise with our bodies… and all the flesh that has wasted away in illness, and all the hair we have ever had, or only the hair we had at the last” — against “the simpler believers” who cannot say what they think a resurrection is a resurrection of.13 Epiphanius calls these “the would-be sage's trifling objections to the truth; I have been obliged to quote them as proof for those who wish to know the full sense of his disbelief in the resurrection.”14 His own reply, when it eventually comes, is that the ensouled body and the spiritual body are the same body in two states — Enoch and Elijah were taken up in the flesh, and Christ “arose from the dead, not by raising a different body, but his own body and not different from his own”, changed “to spiritual fineness.”15

What he had read

The question of Epiphanius's sources is a live one, and the entry itself is the best evidence about it, in both directions.

He quotes, at length, and says why

Two blocks of the chapter are quotation. The first is Origen's own commentary on the first psalm — its preface, addressed to Ambrose, and its argument about the risen body — introduced with a promise of exactness: “I shall quote his own words from the First Psalm along with his doctrinal speculations in it — word for word, so that no one may call my attack on him vexatious.”16 The commentary is otherwise lost; the prosecution file is now the chief witness to it. The second block, some fifty chapters, is Methodius's dialogue on the resurrection, copied out because “I may rest content with the blessed Methodius' remarks against Origen with reference to the matter of the resurrection. I shall present these here, word for word.” He knows he is anthologizing rather than arguing, and says so: “since I have promised to say a few things in its refutation about every sect — there are not few of them! — I content myself with quoting Methodius' work only this far.”17 The continuous Greek of Methodius survives because of this decision, and only because of it.

The six thousand books

Against the quotation stands a claim Epiphanius makes in his own voice, taunting Origen: “since you've worked so hard and carried the struggle of writing so many books out to such useless length. For if the story of your composing 6000 books is true, you energy-waster…” — a figure he there attributes to report rather than to inspection.18 It became a weapon in the Latin quarrel a generation later. Rufinus charged that Epiphanius had “declared in the audience of a vast multitude of the brethren that he had read six thousand of his books”; Jerome, defending him, disputed both halves — the reading (“ten or twenty books, or at most thirty, would have been sufficient”) and the arithmetic (“Count up the index contained in the third volume of Eusebius… you will not find six thousand, but not a third of that number of books”).19 The exchange establishes that within twenty years of Epiphanius's death the extent of his reading in Origen was already the thing his opponents attacked and his allies had to defend.

What he conceded to the Tall Brothers

The last scene of his life bears on the same question. In 403, aged and in Constantinople to help Theophilus of Alexandria break John Chrysostom, Epiphanius met the Egyptian monks he had come to condemn. Sozomen's account is a straight exchange:

Ammonius and his companions went to Epiphanius, at the permission of the empress. Epiphanius inquired who they were, and Ammonius replied, “We are, O father, the Long Brothers; we come respectfully to know whether you have read any of our works or those of our disciples?” On Epiphanius replying that he had not seen them, he continued, “How is it, then, that you consider us to be heretics, when you have no proof as to what sentiments we may hold?” Epiphanius said that he had formed his judgment by the reports he had heard on the subject; and Ammonius replied, “We have pursued a very different line of conduct from yours. We have conversed with your disciples, and read your works frequently, and among others, that entitled ‘The Anchored’… Ought you then to condemn the absent upon mere report, and of whom you know nothing with assured certitude?” Epiphanius was measurably convinced, and dismissed them.20

He sailed for Cyprus soon after and died at sea. The concession is about the living Origenists of 403, not about the Panarion's reading of Origen — a distinction worth keeping — but Sozomen is reporting it of the man who had catalogued Origen as heresy 64, and the monks' retort names the Ancoratus as the book they had read and he had not reciprocated. The Constantinople episode belongs to the Theophilus–Chrysostom timeline; the Palestinian fuse he lit in 393–394 belongs to the Origenist controversy.

The manner of it

Methodius had argued with Origen; Epiphanius prosecutes him. The entry calls him “this braggart with his illusory wisdom, this searcher out of the unsearchable and exhibitor of the heavenly realms, who… has filled the world with nonsense”, and rules that “in his position on doctrines, and about faith and higher speculation, he is the wickedest of all before and after him, except for the shameless behavior in the sects.”21 The closing image is the one the Panarion's title promises — every sect a venomous animal and every chapter its antidote:

…as a medicine, like an antidote, for each wild beast and poisonous snake — I mean these as symbols of the sects — and for this sect of Origenists, which looks like a toad noisy from too much moisture which keeps croaking louder and louder… Ah, how badly you have been hurt, and how many others you have hurt — as though you have been bitten by a baneful viper, I mean secular education, and become the cause of others' death.22

The diagnosis is characteristic and was widely repeated: the learning is the disease. Vincent of Lérins would make the same judgement the moral of the whole affair fifty-odd years later.

The biography he passes on

Epiphanius also transmits a life of Origen, and it is not Eusebius's. He begins generously — son of the martyr Leonidas, tortured, “often dragged around the city, insulted, and subjected to excruciating tortures” — and tells a story of defiance at the Serapeum, where the pagans shaved his head, set him on the temple steps, and made him hand palm branches to the worshippers: “Taking the branches he cried out without fear or hesitation, with loud voice and a bold mind, ‘Come get Christ's branch, not the idol's!’”23 Then the story turns:

But his deeds did not remain worthy of the prize till the end… With diabolical malice the workers of iniquity thought of mistreating him sexually and making that his punishment, and they secured a black to abuse his body. But Origen could not bear even the thought of this devil's work, and shouted that, given the choice of either, he would rather sacrifice. Certainly, as is widely reported, he did not do this willingly either. But since he had agreed to do it at all, he heaped incense on his hands and dumped it on the altar fire. Thus he was excluded from a martyr's status at that time by the confessors and martyrs who were his judges, and expelled from the church.24

On this account Origen leaves for Palestine in disgrace, and the sequel is the most memorable scene in the chapter: asked to preach at Jerusalem, he “stood up and simply recited the verse of the forty-ninth Psalm, omitting all the intervening verses, ‘But unto the ungodly saith God, Why dost thou preach my laws and takest my covenant in thy mouth?’ And he rolled the scroll up, gave it back, and sat down in floods of tears, and all wept with him.”25

Four things can be said against it without any appeal to Origen's merits. Eusebius, writing a century closer and from the Caesarean archive, gives the removal to Palestine an entirely different cause — the irregular ordination and Demetrius's anger — and reports the Decian tortures Origen endured without recanting, decades after the date this story would require; the tale of a forced choice between rape and sacrifice reads as a transfer of the story Eusebius tells of Origen's own pupil Potamiaena, who was threatened with rape by gladiators and put to death; Epiphanius elsewhere in the chapter has Origen resident twenty-eight years at Tyre and seems unaware that he ever headed the school at Alexandria; and he himself has already reported the ordination as prior to the sacrifice on hearsay (“They say…”).26 Epiphanius does enter a caveat of his own, though he applies it only to the reports of Origen's self-mutilation and of a memory drug: “though I have no faith in the exaggerated stories about him, I have not neglected to report what is being said.”27 That sentence is the Panarion's method in miniature, and it is why the chapter has to be read as two things at once: an unmatched deposit of otherwise lost text, and a case for the prosecution assembled from what was being said. Whether the apostasy story is credible as biography is weighed on its own page.

Notes

  • The five-item summary of heresy 64 is the joint from which the later charge-sheets swing. Its “Christ's kingdom will come to an end” reappears verbatim among the errors listed by Theophilus's Alexandrian synod of 400; its pre-existent souls “shut up in this body as a punishment” is the substance of the first anathema of Justinian's edict of 543, which condemns those who say souls “were made to descend into bodies as a punishment.”28 Between the heresy-catalogue and the imperial edict the wording barely moves.
  • Epiphanius is quoting a Greek Origen that later disputants no longer had. That makes him a witness on the question of whether the offending passages were interpolated by heretics, which Rufinus argued and which is weighed on its own page: what he reports from the commentary on the first psalm in the 370s agrees closely with what Methodius had reported in the 290s.
  • Epiphanius appears on this wiki also as a witness on several belief-timelines, among them the perpetual virginity of Mary, the assumption, the Old Testament canon and the Septuagint's origin — in several of which the Panarion is likewise the only surviving report of the position he is refuting.
