Augustine of Hippo on the condemnation of Origen

Dates: 354–430 · Wikipedia
Summary: Augustine is the West's most careful verdict on Origen, and the one that decided which charges the Latin Middle Ages would carry. He came to the case late, from outside the Jerome–Rufinus quarrel, at the prompting of a Spanish priest who had found Origenist books circulating as a cure for Priscillianism. He rejected two doctrines outright — the pre-existent fall of souls into bodies as punishment, and the eventual release of the damned and of the devil — and he declined to endorse the rest of the catalogue he had inherited, saying so in his own book of heresies. On the question the whole controversy turned on, the origin of the soul, he refused all his life to decide. This page covers his share of the timeline of verdicts passed on Origen; his permanent hesitation about the soul is traced on the origin of the soul, where he has a page of his own.

How the question reached him: Orosius, 414

Augustine did not go looking for Origen. In 414 the Spanish priest Paulus Orosius arrived at Hippo with a memorandum on the state of his home province, and the burden of it was that the cure had turned out to be a second disease: Priscillianism had been answered in Spain with books of Origen's, and the readers had taken the medicine along with the poison. Orosius sets out what he had heard taught, and the list is a fourth-century Latin reader's Origenism in six articles — that nothing God made began to be made (“Deus enim quaecumque fecit faciendo non coepit”); that angels, principalities, powers, souls and demons are of one origin and one substance, each placed according to the quality of its deserts (“Maiorem locum minor culpa promeruit” — a lesser fault earned a higher place); that the world was made last of all so that souls which had sinned beforehand might be purged in it; that the eternal fire is neither real fire nor eternal; that after the purging of conscience all the souls of sinners will return into the unity of the body of Christ; and — this one, Orosius says, they wanted to assert and could not carry — that since the good substance made in the devil cannot perish, his malice being wholly burnt away, that substance too must one day be saved.1

Augustine's reply, Ad Orosium contra Priscillianistas et Origenistas, is the first thing he wrote on the subject, and it opens by refusing the simple verdict. He grants the grief Orosius feels — error ought to have been driven out by truth, not by another falsehood, “for this is to change an evil, not to avoid it” — and then says flatly that the province got real good out of those books, precisely at the point where Priscillian was worst:

Verumtamen quia de ipso Deo creaturarum omnium conditore, hoc est de ipsa coaeterna et incommutabili trinitate ab eis, qui ad vos libros Origenis attulerunt, id traditum esse commemoras quod veritas habet… non parum boni ex libris illis provincia vestra percepit, in ea re maxime, in qua capitaliter erratur.2

— since what those who brought Origen's books taught your people about God the maker of all things, that is about the co-eternal and unchangeable Trinity itself, is what the truth holds, your province got no small good from those books, in the very matter where the error is mortal. (No published English version of this treatise is available online; the renderings here are from the Latin.) What he does forbid is the last article on Orosius's list, and he forbids it as a trespass rather than as a refuted opinion: “do not dare to think anything about the correction of the devil and his angels and their restoration to their former state—not because we begrudge the devil and the demons… but because we ought to add nothing of our own presumption to the final sentence of the highest and most truthful Judge.”3 The argument he then runs against a temporary hell is the one he would use for the rest of his life — the symmetry of the two eternities in Matthew 25: if mercy persuades us that the punishment of the wicked will not be endless, what shall we believe about the reward of the just, “when in both parts, in the same place, in the same sentence, in the same word, eternity is pronounced?” Shall we say the just too will one day slide back out of that sanctification into uncleanness and death? “Absit hoc a christianae fidei sanitate.”4

The City of God: two chapters that fixed the Latin charge-sheet

Book XI.23 — the fall of souls into bodies

Writing the eleventh book about 417, Augustine reached the goodness of creation and stopped to name the one Christian account of the world's origin he could not accept. It is the most exact Latin statement of the doctrine, and the first thing to notice is that he sources it: not to rumour or to a catalogue, but to the book itself.

But they say that souls, though not, indeed, parts of God, but created by Him, sinned by abandoning God; that, in proportion to their various sins, they merited different degrees of debasement from heaven to earth, and diverse bodies as prison-houses; and that this is the world, and this the cause of its creation, not the production of good things, but the restraining of evil. Origen is justly blamed for holding this opinion. For in the books which he entitles peri archōn, that is, Of Origins, this is his sentiment, this his utterance. And I cannot sufficiently express my astonishment, that a man so erudite and well versed in ecclesiastical literature, should not have observed, in the first place, how opposed this is to the meaning of this authoritative Scripture, which, in recounting all the works of God, regularly adds, “And God saw that it was good.”5

Two objections follow, and both are arguments rather than authorities. The first is exegetical: Genesis gives the world's creation one cause — that a good God should make good creatures — and a cosmos built as a house of correction is not what “behold, it was very good” describes. The second is a reduction: if bodies are graded penalties, then the devils, who are deepest in wickedness, ought to have the grossest bodies of all, whereas in fact “the wickedest devil possesses an ethereal body, while man, wicked, it is true, but with a wickedness small and venial in comparison with his, received even before his sin a body of clay.” And if the sun exists because exactly one soul sinned to exactly that degree, then a hundred souls sinning alike would have given us a hundred suns. He ends with a joke sharp enough to have been remembered: men who hold such opinions “should aim at confining, not souls of which they know not what they say, but themselves.”6

Book XXI.17 — the tender-hearted, and where Origen went past them

Ten books and about ten years later, arguing against the various parties who expected the damned to be released, Augustine gives Origen a paragraph that is worth reading slowly, because it does three separate things: it states the doctrine, it reports that the Church has condemned him for it, and it says which error was the worst — and the worst is not universal salvation.

I must now, I see, enter the lists of amicable controversy with those tender-hearted Christians who decline to believe that any, or that all of those whom the infallibly just Judge may pronounce worthy of the punishment of hell, shall suffer eternally… In respect of this matter, Origen was even more indulgent; for he believed that even the devil himself and his angels, after suffering those more severe and prolonged pains which their sins deserved, should be delivered from their torments, and associated with the holy angels. But the Church, not without reason, condemned him for this and other errors, especially for his theory of the ceaseless alternation of happiness and misery, and the interminable transitions from the one state to the other at fixed periods of ages; for in this theory he lost even the credit of being merciful, by allotting to the saints real miseries for the expiation of their sins, and false happiness, which brought them no true and secure joy, that is, no fearless assurance of eternal blessedness.7

“The Church, not without reason, condemned him” is, for this timeline, the sentence that matters. Written in the 420s, more than a century before any council touched the question, it shows that a Latin bishop with no stake in the Egyptian and Palestinian quarrels already treated Origen's condemnation as an accomplished ecclesiastical fact — presumably the synods and papal letters of 400–401, which are the only proceedings there were. It also shows what he thought had been condemned: not the hope of mercy as such (which he here calls an error of tenderness and argues with “amicably”), but the endless wheel. Augustine's specific complaint against the apokatastasis is that it is not merciful enough — a restoration that can be lost again gives the saints no security, and a heaven you may fall out of is not heaven. The full refutation of a temporary hell follows six chapters later, resting on the same reading of Matthew 25 he had given Orosius: “as the eternal life of the saints shall be endless, so too the eternal punishment of those who are doomed to it shall have no end.”8

De haeresibus 43 (c. 428): what he would and would not put on the list

At the end of his life Augustine compiled, for the deacon Quodvultdeus, a catalogue of eighty-eight heresies. Origen has an entry — number 43 — and it is the most instructive document on this page, because it shows a careful man handling a charge-sheet he did not compile and separating the parts he will sign from the parts he will not. (Number 42, confusingly, is a different and obscure Origen, a libertine sect Epiphanius names; the Alexandrian is 43.)

He opens by quoting Epiphanius verbatim — the four charges from the Panarion's index: that the Origenists reject the resurrection of the dead, make Christ and the Holy Spirit creatures, and allegorize paradise, the heavens and all the rest. Then he steps back from three of the four:

Haec quidem de Origene Epiphanius. Sed qui eum defendunt unius eiusdemque substantiae esse dicunt docuisse Patrem et Filium et Spiritum Sanctum, neque resurrectionem reppulisse mortuorum; quamvis et in istis eum convincere studeant qui eius plura legerunt.9

— This much about Origen from Epiphanius. But those who defend him say that he taught the Father, the Son and the Holy Spirit to be of one and the same substance, and did not reject the resurrection of the dead; though those who have read more of him try to convict him on these points too. That is a heresiologist declining to vouch for his own entry: the Trinitarian and resurrection charges are reported as contested, with the defence given last word and the prosecution reduced to the observation that better-read men press them. Augustine had not read much Origen and does not pretend otherwise.

What he does own, he owns without qualification, and it is one doctrine in two movements — the purging and release of all the damned including the devil and his angels, and then, after immense ages, the relapse of all the liberated back into the same evils, the alternation of blessednesses and miseries running on for ever in both directions:

Quis enim catholicus christianus vel doctus vel indoctus non vehementer exhorreat eam quam dicit purgationem malorum… ipsum etiam postremo diabolum atque angelos eius, quamvis post longissima tempora, purgatos atque liberatos regno Dei lucique restitui, et rursus post longissima tempora omnes qui liberati sunt ad haec mala denuo relabi et reverti, et has vices alternantes beatitudinum et miseriarum rationalis creaturae semper fuisse, semper fore?10

— For what catholic Christian, learned or unlearned, would not violently shudder at that purgation of the wicked which he speaks of… that even the devil himself at the last, and his angels, though after the longest ages, are purged and freed and restored to the kingdom of God and the light; and that again after the longest ages all who were freed slide back and return to these same evils; and that these alternating turns of blessedness and misery of the rational creation always have been and always will be? He signs off by referring the reader to the City of God for the refutation, and by naming the source he thinks Origen took it from: not Scripture badly read but “the philosophers from whom Origen learnt these things.” The Latin West's short entry on Origen thus came to rest on exactly one doctrine, and on a cyclical version of it that Peri Archōn raises as a difficulty rather than asserts — the point at which Augustine's verdict and the sixth century's begin to converge, and the point at which both begin to diverge from the book.

What he refused to decide

The doctrine that drew the heaviest fire in every other quarter — the pre-existence of souls — Augustine had rejected in the City of God in its Origenist form, as a fall and a punishment. But the underlying question of where any soul comes from he declined all his life to settle, and he declined it in writing, to Jerome, in the middle of the controversy. Asked to adjudicate in 415, he answered that he did not know, listed the opinions he could not choose between, and said what was at stake for him: “where can the soul, even of an infant snatched away by death, have contracted the guilt which, unless the grace of Christ has come to the rescue by that sacrament of baptism which is administered even to infants, involves it in condemnation?”11 Five years later he was still saying “I have no certain knowledge how it came into my body”, and in the Retractations at the end: “as to its origin… I did not know then, and I still do not know.” The full record of that refusal, and the reason Origen's answer was the one solution he could not take, is on his page on the origin of the soul.12

Notes

  • Augustine's is the narrowest of the Latin verdicts. Where Epiphanius listed four charges and Theophilus's synod eight, Augustine holds one — universal restoration and the cycle that follows it — and expressly leaves the Trinitarian and resurrection charges unresolved. Nothing in his writing corresponds to the propositions of 543 and 553 about the pre-existent equality of minds or the spherical risen body.
  • He never read the case as a Latin party quarrel. He was on cordial terms with neither faction: his correspondence with Jerome is its own timeline, and his one recorded comment on that quarrel's bitterness is a plea to end it. His route into the Origen question was Spanish and Priscillianist, not Palestinian.
  • “The Church, not without reason, condemned him” (City of God XXI.17) is the earliest plain statement by a major Latin writer that the condemnation of Origen is a settled fact of church discipline. It precedes any conciliar act on the subject by well over a century, and rests on the synods and papal letters of 400–401 — whose scope is weighed on its own page.
  • Augustine appears on this wiki also on the origin of the soul, the Jerome–Augustine correspondence, and a number of the belief timelines.
