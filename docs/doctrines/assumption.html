The Assumption / Dormition of Mary

The belief traced here is that Mary, at the end of her earthly life, was taken up body and soul into heaven — her flesh not left to the corruption of the grave but glorified in anticipation of the general resurrection. The East tells it as the Dormition (“falling asleep,” Koimēsis), a name that presupposes she truly died; the West came to call it the Assumption, defined as Catholic dogma only in 1950. The two names mark the same event from two angles, and the wiki treats them as one doctrine.

Of all the Marian doctrines this is the one built, almost by design, around a silence. The New Testament says nothing of how Mary’s life ended; no early Father records a death, a tomb, or a translation; and the first writer to raise the question at all, Epiphanius of Salamis (c. 377), flatly confesses that no one knows her end and that Scripture “kept silence.” The belief surfaces in the apocryphal Transitus Mariae (“Passing of Mary”) narratives — surviving in fifth- and sixth-century manuscripts but, on the dominant scholarly dating of the earliest of them (the Six Books), reaching back into the fourth century; some from heterodox circles, and branded apocryphal at Rome — before it enters mainstream preaching with Gregory of Tours in the West (c. 590) and is dogmatized in feast and homily in the East. Even its greatest medieval champions kept a striking reserve: John of Damascus (c. 730) grounds it frankly on tradition rather than Scripture, and Paschasius Radbertus (c. 860) warns against the very apocrypha the belief grew from and refuses to decide whether her body was raised. The reader’s live question, then, is not only whether the doctrine is true but on what it can rest when the record is this thin — and the timeline is laid out earliest first to let that development show.

One distinction runs through the page. The Dormition presupposes a real death and so a soul that survives it — the same middle state between death and resurrection traced on the intermediate-state timeline — while the Assumption is essentially the claim that Mary skips the ordinary wait, her body raised and glorified ahead of everyone else’s. The 1950 definition deliberately leaves open whether she died first, choosing the guarded phrase “having completed the course of her earthly life.”

Timeline

Epiphanius of Salamis (c. 377)

Epiphanius of Salamis, in the long treatise against the Antidicomarianites that became Panarion 78 — the same anti-heretical work in which he defends Mary’s perpetual virginity — is the earliest writer to raise the question of Mary’s end, and he answers it with open agnosticism. “Let them search the Scriptures,” he challenges his opponents; “they will not find Mary’s death; they will not find whether she died or did not die.”1 He lays out three possibilities — that she died and was buried, that she was martyred, or that she remained alive — and settles on none: “I do not dare to speak, but I keep my own thoughts and I practice silence.”2 His witness is the anchor of the doctrine’s long first chapter: whatever later tradition would affirm, in the late fourth century an exceptionally learned bishop could say only that no one knew.

The Six Books Dormition apocryphon (c. 400; date disputed)

The Six Books apocryphon — the Syriac “Departure of my Lady Mary from this World,” one of the two oldest Dormition narrative families — is the earliest datable text to tell the bodily assumption as a full story. Mary truly dies; then, at the Lord’s command, “the twelve apostles bore [her body] as it went to the Paradise of Eden,” where Christ calls “Mary, rise,” and “straightway she was restored to life” and is crowned in glory.3 Its surviving manuscripts are fifth- and sixth-century, but the dominant scholarly dating places composition in the fourth: Shoemaker locates “a likely origin by the second half of the fourth century if not even earlier,” and Bauckham dates its concluding apocalyptic tour “from the fourth century at the latest, but perhaps considerably earlier” — against a late-dating minority (Jugie, Mimouni) that reads it as post-Chalcedon.4 If the early datings hold, datable Dormition belief stands not two centuries after Epiphanius’s “no one knows” but in his own generation — the single most important check on reading the doctrine as a purely late-antique invention, though it establishes early circulation of the narrative, not an apostolic pedigree.

Pseudo-Melito, Transitus Mariae (the Latin Passing of Mary, c. 500)

The Transitus attributed to Melito of Sardis — in fact pseudonymous, a Latin narrative of the late fifth or sixth century — is the most widely copied Western telling of the bodily assumption and the likeliest conduit by which the story reached Latin preaching (Gregory of Tours epitomizes just this kind of account). It narrates a real death and then a bodily translation: at the Lord’s word “Mary rose from the tomb,” and the angels bore her “into the paradise of God.”5 What sets it apart is its prologue, which presents the work as an orthodox correction of an earlier Transitus it brands heretical — the writing of one “Leucius,” who “vitiated, by his impious writing, the assumption of the blessed ever-virgin Mary … to such a degree that it would be impious not only to read it in the church of God, but even to hear it.”6 The tradition is here caught conceding, in its own prologue, that the narrative of Mary’s end grew up in doctrinally compromised hands — a Latin counterpart to John of Thessalonica’s Greek disavowal, and a chief exhibit in the argument over the apocryphal foundation.

Gregory of Tours (c. 590)

Gregory of Tours gives the earliest plain narrative affirmation in the Latin West. Retelling, in his Glory of the Martyrs, the account that had reached him through the Transitus tradition, he writes that when Mary’s earthly course was finished and the apostles had gathered, “the Lord … commanded the body of Mary be taken in a cloud into paradise; where now, rejoined to the soul, Mary dwells with the chosen ones.”7 Two centuries after Epiphanius’s “no one knows,” the bodily assumption is now told as settled fact — but told out of the apocryphal narratives, not out of Scripture or any named apostolic witness.

The feast fixed for 15 August — Emperor Maurice (582–602)

Emperor Maurice marks the point at which the commemoration became an empire-wide institution rather than a local Palestinian one. Later tradition — the Liber Pontificalis and the fourteenth-century historian Nikephoros Kallistos — credits him with fixing the feast of the Dormition on 15 August throughout the Greek Empire, ending a patchwork in which “some kept this feast in January, with the monks of Egypt; others in August, with those of Palestine.”8 The feast itself was older than the decree: a “Memory of Mary” is already kept on 15 August in the fifth-century Armenian Lectionary of Jerusalem.9 Maurice standardized an existing observance; the act is liturgical history, not fresh evidence for the doctrine — and the attribution rests only on much later sources.

Theoteknos of Livias (c. 600)

Theoteknos of Livias, bishop of a see east of the Jordan, preached one of the two oldest surviving Greek homilies for the feast — and the earliest to call it an Assumption (Análēpsis, a “taking up”) rather than a Dormition. He affirms the bodily claim without hedging: “even though the God-bearing body of that holy one did taste death, it was not corrupted; for it was kept incorrupt and free of decay, and it was lifted up to heaven with her pure and spotless soul.”10 Two centuries after Epiphanius’s “no one knows,” a real death joined to an incorrupt, heaven-borne body is stated as settled fact — the formula the later homilists would inherit.

John of Thessalonica (c. 615)

John of Thessalonica, introducing the feast to a church that had not been keeping it, left the frankest early admission about its sources. The narratives of Mary’s end, he explains, had been so corrupted by heretics “adding words of their own” that “our ancestors distanced themselves from these accounts as not in accord with the catholic Church”; he preaches only after purging the apocrypha, “taking no account of fabricated stories.”11 The tradition here is caught policing its own documentary foundations at the very moment it enters the liturgy.

Modestus of Jerusalem (c. 630)

Modestus of Jerusalem, patriarch of the very see that hosted the Dormition tradition, is the name attached to what is often called the oldest surviving Greek homily for the feast. It affirms both a real death and an incorrupt, heaven-borne body: Mary “did not experience corruption in that life-sheltering body of hers, even in the tomb,” being “preserved [from it] by Christ the Savior,” who “called her to himself and invested her with an incorruptibility touching all her corporeal frame.”12 The attribution is contested, however — a reference to Christ’s two wills, defined only in 680–681, leads many scholars to date the surviving text later in the seventh century and to call its author “pseudo-Modestus”13 — so its exact place on the timeline is uncertain, even as it testifies to a confident, detailed Jerusalem preaching of the bodily glorification.

Germanus of Constantinople (c. 720)

Germanus of Constantinople, patriarch and Dormition homilist, preaches Mary’s genuine death together with her body’s incorruption as firmly as any Father: her “flesh did not undergo the corruption of the tomb,” and it was “impossible that you, the vessel which bore God, should be dissolved and decomposed into the dust of death.”14 His witness to her incorrupt, assumed body was firm enough that Pius XII would quote him by name in the 1950 definition.

Andrew of Crete (c. 725)

Andrew of Crete, in three homilies for the feast, gives the bodily assumption its most developed early statement in the East. He holds Mary’s death to be real — a Koimēsis, a sleep unto resurrection — yet her tomb “allow[ed] no corruption,” and “today, Jesus transports the Queen of the human race outside of her earthly dwelling.”15 A few years before John of Damascus, the assumption is confident Eastern preaching and the empty tomb its stock argument.

John of Damascus (c. 730)

John of Damascus, in three homilies preached at Gethsemane, gives the Dormition its classic Eastern statement, and he is candid that it does not rest on Scripture. He affirms the bodily glory outright — “Why do you seek in the tomb one who has been assumed to the heavenly courts?”16 — and rests the claim on a tradition he takes pains to relay: the Euthymiac History, appended to his second homily, which traced the belief to Juvenal of Jerusalem’s report, at the Council of Chalcedon (451), that Mary’s tomb had been opened at the Empress Pulcheria’s request and found empty, only the grave-clothes remaining.17 The doctrine is preached with full confidence, yet its warrant is explicitly tradition, not the sacred text.

Paschasius Radbertus (c. 860)

Paschasius Radbertus, abbot of Corbie, wrote the West’s most influential Assumption sermon — the letter Cogitis me, circulated for centuries under the name of Jerome — and its most striking note is caution. He warns his readers against “that apocryphal work on the passing of the virgin,” the same Transitus narratives Rome’s Gelasian Decree had listed among the books “not to be received,”18 and declines to settle the bodily question: “many of our people doubt whether she was assumed with the body or left the body behind,” and “it is more fitting to hold an opinion with a pious desire than to define unadvisedly what is not known without danger.”19 Nine centuries after Christ, the doctrine’s ablest Latin advocate still held its bodily form as pious opinion, not defined faith.

Pope Pius XII — Munificentissimus Deus (1950)

Pope Pius XII closed the long development by definition, declaring it “a divinely revealed dogma: that the Immaculate Mother of God, the ever Virgin Mary, having completed the course of her earthly life, was assumed body and soul into heavenly glory.”20 The wording was chosen with care: the guarded phrase “having completed the course of her earthly life” was framed so as not to decide whether Mary died before she was assumed — leaving open the very question Epiphanius had confessed no one could answer, fifteen centuries earlier.

Arguments

The doctrine’s live questions are weighed off the timeline, and they fall into two clusters with a third standing on its own. One cluster turns on the documents the belief actually grew from; the other on whether Scripture supplies any warrant for it; and a final argument weighs the silence that precedes even those documents.

Related arguments — the apocryphal foundation

Two arguments on one fault line. The belief’s earliest narratives are the fifth- and sixth-century apocryphal Transitus / Liber Requiei texts — and its ablest patristic champion rests the doctrine not on Scripture but on a tradition whose traceable chain runs back into those very narratives. Together they ask what the doctrine’s documentary foundation really is.

Are the apocryphal Transitus narratives a sound documentary foundation? (assessment: weak)

The Transitus foundation — whether the fifth-/sixth-century Transitus Mariae cycle can serve as a sound documentary basis for the doctrine. Weak as a foundation: these earliest narratives carry the marks of a heterodox origin at their oldest recoverable layer (Shoemaker locates the Palm-of-the-Tree-of-Life tradition behind the Liber Requiei in a gnostic, demiurgic milieu), and the tradition itself disowned them — condemned at Rome (the Gelasian Decree), purged by John of Thessalonica, warned against by Paschasius. A doctrine cannot rest its historical claim on sources its own defenders judged spurious. This rates the evidentiary basis, not whether the belief is false: the heterodox milieu of the earliest recorders does not make the recorded event false, and Shoemaker’s own dating puts the belief’s emergence behind the surviving apocrypha rather than in them.

Does John of Damascus’s “tradition” trace to the apostles, or into the apocrypha? (assessment: plausible)

Tradition or apocrypha? — John of Damascus (c. 730) preaches the doctrine with full confidence yet grounds it frankly on tradition, not Scripture; so the case hinges on where that tradition leads. Plausible, and on the documentary evidence the more probable reading: the traceable chain (Dormition homilies → Gregory of Tours → the Transitus narratives → the Liber Requiei) runs into the fifth-/sixth-century apocrypha, not up to an independent apostolic memory — and the one document that would supply an earlier pedigree, the Euthymiac History appended to John’s own homily, is a demonstrable later interpolation, no older than the apocrypha it was meant to antedate. It stops short of sound because an argument from a traceable written chain caps rather than strictly disproves an untraced oral tradition.

Related arguments — the Scriptural case

Two proof-texts adduced to give the doctrine a footing in Scripture. The belief is admittedly built on a silence and rests, by its own defenders’ account, on tradition rather than the sacred text — so the question is whether the passages they do cite carry any evidentiary weight, or only show a bodily assumption to be conceivable.

Does the woman clothed with the sun (Revelation 12) attest Mary’s bodily glorification? (assessment: weak)

The Revelation 12 argument — whether the woman clothed with the sun, shown in heavenly glory and delivered from the dragon, attests Mary’s bodily assumption, as Munificentissimus Deus invokes her among the doctrine’s scriptural intimations. Weak as evidence for bodily glorification: even where a Marian layer in the chapter is granted, that is the New-Eve identity question — weighed as contested on the Mary-as-New-Eve timeline — and identity is not glorification. Revelation 12 narrates no resurrection or incorruption of Mary’s body; its subject is the messianic community’s war with the serpent, its primary patristic referent the Church, and its magisterial user (Pius XII) adduces it, in Catholic exegetes’ own words, “with some freedom,” not as demonstration. A fitting emblem once the doctrine stands on other grounds, not a support for it.

Do the “saints raised” texts (1 Corinthians 15; Matthew 27:52–53) supply a Scriptural analogy? (assessment: weak)

The “saints raised” analogy — whether the resurrection texts (Christ the firstfruits and “death swallowed up in victory,” 1 Corinthians 15; the bodies of the saints raised at the crucifixion, Matthew 27:52–53) supply a Scriptural precedent for a bodily assumption. Weak as proof, though the modest point behind it — that a bodily glorification is scripturally conceivable — is plausible: analogy establishes possibility, not that Mary’s assumption occurred; Matthew 27:52–53, the strongest-sounding prop, is cited nowhere in the 1950 definition and is itself text-critically disputed; and 1 Corinthians 15’s own ordering — “every man in his own order… afterward they that are Christ’s at his coming” (15:23) — tells against a lone bodily assumption ahead of the general resurrection. At most these show the doctrine is not incoherent, which is all its defenders claim Scripture supplies.

A final argument weighs the silence that precedes even the apocrypha:

  • Does the silence of the first centuries refute the Assumption’s apostolic antiquity? — contested. The factual core (no attestation for some three centuries; Epiphanius’s “no one knows”; the belief is a demonstrable late-antique development, first surfacing in apocrypha its own church branded spurious) is near-consensus and conceded even by Catholic historians, so the doctrine cannot be shown to be of apostolic antiquity. What is contested is the further inference: an argument from silence caps a claim rather than disproving the event, the belief’s defenders rest it on tradition and fittingness rather than a documentary chain, the revisionist dating (Shoemaker, Bauckham) pushes its emergence back toward Epiphanius’s own century, and the “no relics / empty tomb” counter draws on the same silence to the opposite end — strong by the historian’s lights, answerable by the theologian’s.
