The Assumption / Dormition of Mary

The belief traced here is that Mary, at the end of her earthly life, was taken up body and soul into heaven — her flesh not left to the corruption of the grave but glorified in anticipation of the general resurrection. The East tells it as the Dormition (“falling asleep,” Koimēsis), a name that presupposes she truly died; the West came to call it the Assumption, defined as Catholic dogma only in 1950. The two names mark the same event from two angles, and the wiki treats them as one doctrine.

Of all the Marian doctrines this is the one built, almost by design, around a silence. The New Testament says nothing of how Mary’s life ended; no early Father records a death, a tomb, or a translation; and the first writer to raise the question at all, Epiphanius of Salamis (c. 377), flatly confesses that no one knows her end and that Scripture “kept silence.” The belief surfaces in the fifth- and sixth-century apocryphal Transitus Mariae (“Passing of Mary”) narratives — some from heterodox circles, and branded apocryphal at Rome — before it enters mainstream preaching with Gregory of Tours in the West (c. 590) and is dogmatized in feast and homily in the East. Even its greatest medieval champions kept a striking reserve: John of Damascus (c. 730) grounds it frankly on tradition rather than Scripture, and Paschasius Radbertus (c. 860) warns against the very apocrypha the belief grew from and refuses to decide whether her body was raised. The reader’s live question, then, is not only whether the doctrine is true but on what it can rest when the record is this thin — and the timeline is laid out earliest first to let that development show.

One distinction runs through the page. The Dormition presupposes a real death and so a soul that survives it — the same middle state between death and resurrection traced on the intermediate-state timeline — while the Assumption is essentially the claim that Mary skips the ordinary wait, her body raised and glorified ahead of everyone else’s. The 1950 definition deliberately leaves open whether she died first, choosing the guarded phrase “having completed the course of her earthly life.”

Timeline

Epiphanius of Salamis (c. 377)

Epiphanius of Salamis, in the long treatise against the Antidicomarianites that became Panarion 78 — the same anti-heretical work in which he defends Mary’s perpetual virginity — is the earliest writer to raise the question of Mary’s end, and he answers it with open agnosticism. “Let them search the Scriptures,” he challenges his opponents; “they will not find Mary’s death; they will not find whether she died or did not die.”1 He lays out three possibilities — that she died and was buried, that she was martyred, or that she remained alive — and settles on none: “I do not dare to speak, but I keep my own thoughts and I practice silence.”2 His witness is the anchor of the doctrine’s long first chapter: whatever later tradition would affirm, in the late fourth century an exceptionally learned bishop could say only that no one knew.

Gregory of Tours (c. 590)

Gregory of Tours gives the earliest plain narrative affirmation in the Latin West. Retelling, in his Glory of the Martyrs, the account that had reached him through the Transitus tradition, he writes that when Mary’s earthly course was finished and the apostles had gathered, “the Lord … commanded the body of Mary be taken in a cloud into paradise; where now, rejoined to the soul, Mary dwells with the chosen ones.”3 Two centuries after Epiphanius’s “no one knows,” the bodily assumption is now told as settled fact — but told out of the apocryphal narratives, not out of Scripture or any named apostolic witness.

The feast fixed for 15 August — Emperor Maurice (582–602)

Emperor Maurice marks the point at which the commemoration became an empire-wide institution rather than a local Palestinian one. Later tradition — the Liber Pontificalis and the fourteenth-century historian Nikephoros Kallistos — credits him with fixing the feast of the Dormition on 15 August throughout the Greek Empire, ending a patchwork in which “some kept this feast in January, with the monks of Egypt; others in August, with those of Palestine.”4 The feast itself was older than the decree: a “Memory of Mary” is already kept on 15 August in the fifth-century Armenian Lectionary of Jerusalem.5 Maurice standardized an existing observance; the act is liturgical history, not fresh evidence for the doctrine — and the attribution rests only on much later sources.

Theoteknos of Livias (c. 600)

Theoteknos of Livias, bishop of a see east of the Jordan, preached one of the two oldest surviving Greek homilies for the feast — and the earliest to call it an Assumption (Análēpsis, a “taking up”) rather than a Dormition. He affirms the bodily claim without hedging: “even though the God-bearing body of that holy one did taste death, it was not corrupted; for it was kept incorrupt and free of decay, and it was lifted up to heaven with her pure and spotless soul.”6 Two centuries after Epiphanius’s “no one knows,” a real death joined to an incorrupt, heaven-borne body is stated as settled fact — the formula the later homilists would inherit.

John of Thessalonica (c. 615)

John of Thessalonica, introducing the feast to a church that had not been keeping it, left the frankest early admission about its sources. The narratives of Mary’s end, he explains, had been so corrupted by heretics “adding words of their own” that “our ancestors distanced themselves from these accounts as not in accord with the catholic Church”; he preaches only after purging the apocrypha, “taking no account of fabricated stories.”7 The tradition here is caught policing its own documentary foundations at the very moment it enters the liturgy.

Germanus of Constantinople (c. 720)

Germanus of Constantinople, patriarch and Dormition homilist, preaches Mary’s genuine death together with her body’s incorruption as firmly as any Father: her “flesh did not undergo the corruption of the tomb,” and it was “impossible that you, the vessel which bore God, should be dissolved and decomposed into the dust of death.”8 His witness to her incorrupt, assumed body was firm enough that Pius XII would quote him by name in the 1950 definition.

Andrew of Crete (c. 725)

Andrew of Crete, in three homilies for the feast, gives the bodily assumption its most developed early statement in the East. He holds Mary’s death to be real — a Koimēsis, a sleep unto resurrection — yet her tomb “allow[ed] no corruption,” and “today, Jesus transports the Queen of the human race outside of her earthly dwelling.”9 A few years before John of Damascus, the assumption is confident Eastern preaching and the empty tomb its stock argument.

John of Damascus (c. 730)

John of Damascus, in three homilies preached at Gethsemane, gives the Dormition its classic Eastern statement, and he is candid that it does not rest on Scripture. He affirms the bodily glory outright — “Why do you seek in the tomb one who has been assumed to the heavenly courts?”10 — and rests the claim on a tradition he takes pains to relay: the Euthymiac History, appended to his second homily, which traced the belief to Juvenal of Jerusalem’s report, at the Council of Chalcedon (451), that Mary’s tomb had been opened at the Empress Pulcheria’s request and found empty, only the grave-clothes remaining.11 The doctrine is preached with full confidence, yet its warrant is explicitly tradition, not the sacred text.

Paschasius Radbertus (c. 860)

Paschasius Radbertus, abbot of Corbie, wrote the West’s most influential Assumption sermon — the letter Cogitis me, circulated for centuries under the name of Jerome — and its most striking note is caution. He warns his readers against “that apocryphal work on the passing of the virgin,” the same Transitus narratives Rome’s Gelasian Decree had listed among the books “not to be received,”12 and declines to settle the bodily question: “many of our people doubt whether she was assumed with the body or left the body behind,” and “it is more fitting to hold an opinion with a pious desire than to define unadvisedly what is not known without danger.”13 Nine centuries after Christ, the doctrine’s ablest Latin advocate still held its bodily form as pious opinion, not defined faith.

Pope Pius XII — Munificentissimus Deus (1950)

Pope Pius XII closed the long development by definition, declaring it “a divinely revealed dogma: that the Immaculate Mother of God, the ever Virgin Mary, having completed the course of her earthly life, was assumed body and soul into heavenly glory.”14 The wording was chosen with care: the guarded phrase “having completed the course of her earthly life” was framed so as not to decide whether Mary died before she was assumed — leaving open the very question Epiphanius had confessed no one could answer, fifteen centuries earlier.

Arguments

The doctrine’s central historical question is weighed off the timeline in an argument page.

  • Does the silence of the first centuries refute the Assumption’s apostolic antiquity? — contested. The factual core (no attestation for some three centuries; Epiphanius’s “no one knows”; the belief is a demonstrable late-antique development, first surfacing in apocrypha its own church branded spurious) is near-consensus and conceded even by Catholic historians, so the doctrine cannot be shown to be of apostolic antiquity. What is contested is the further inference: an argument from silence caps a claim rather than disproving the event, the belief’s defenders rest it on tradition and fittingness rather than a documentary chain, the revisionist dating (Shoemaker) pushes its emergence back toward Epiphanius’s own century, and the “no relics / empty tomb” counter draws on the same silence to the opposite end — strong by the historian’s lights, answerable by the theologian’s.
