Augustine of Hippo — the sign, not the words

Dates: c. 417 (the Tractates on John; the companion readings span the Harmony of the Gospels c. 400 and the sermons); Augustine 354–430 · Wikipedia
Summary: Augustine is the most influential Latin voice on the passage and the one who pointedly declines to decode the words. Where Jerome reads the ground-writing as a legible list of the accusers' sins, Augustine reads the act as sign: the same finger that graved the Law on stone now writes on soil, because the Law was cut in stone "for the hard-hearted" while grace is "sown" on ground that can bear fruit. He offers a small cluster of figural readings — the accusers "written on earth, not in heaven"; the stooping as the humility of the Incarnation; the earth as fruitful soil against barren stone — but never a set of words. His scene closes not with a decoded message but with an image that outlived every reconstruction: the two left alone, "the wretched woman and Mercy."

The refusal to decode — and the sign that replaces it (Tractates on John 33.5)

Augustine's fullest treatment is the thirty-third tractate on John, where the whole weight falls on Jesus as at once the Lawgiver and the Judge who will not be trapped between mercy and justice. When he reaches the finger on the ground, he does not ask what was written; he asks what the writing means — and answers with the law/grace contrast that becomes his signature reading of the scene:

What else does He signify to you when He writes with His finger on the ground? For the law was written with the finger of God; but written on stone because of the hard-hearted. The Lord now wrote on the ground, because He was seeking fruit… This is the voice of Justice: Let her, the sinner, be punished, but not by sinners: let the law be fulfilled, but not by the transgressors of the law… The two were left alone, the wretched woman and Mercy. But the Lord, having struck them through with that dart of justice, deigned not to heed their fall, but, turning away His look from them, "again He wrote with His finger on the ground."

— Tractates on John 33.5. Note what Augustine does not do: he never supplies the sentence, never names a proof-text for the content, and treats the second stooping (8:8) as the averted gaze of a Judge who "deigned not to heed their fall." The content is left blank on purpose; the meaning is carried by the gesture. This is the fountainhead of the whole "the act is the message" line that runs down to Calvin and the modern consensus.1

Three figural readings of the ground (Harmony of the Gospels 4.10)

In the Harmony of the Gospels Augustine gathers three ways of reading why the ground — still not what the words were, but what the choice of soil signifies:

He wrote with His finger upon the ground, as if He would indicate that people of the character of these men would be written on earth, and not in heaven, as He also admonished His disciples to rejoice that their names were written in heaven! Or, it may be that He meant to convey the idea that it was by humbling Himself (which He expressed by bending down His head) that He wrought signs upon the earth; or, that the time was now come when His law should be written, not, as formerly, on the sterile stone, but on a soil which would yield fruit.

— The Harmony of the Gospels 4.10. The three readings — the accusers "written on earth, not in heaven" (Jeremiah 17:13 against Luke 10:20); the bowed head as the humility of the Incarnation working "signs upon the earth"; and the ground as fruitful soil against "sterile stone" — are the exact three that Thomas Aquinas would take over and set in order six centuries later. Augustine offers them as figures of the whole event, not as candidates for the missing text.2

"I begin with you" — the turning-away as accusation (Sermon 16A)

In a sermon on the episode Augustine reads even the turning away as speech. The Redeemer, "not the condemner," turns from the accusers as if to say that if sins are to be judged, the judgment starts with the men who brought the charge:

This turning away from them is no trivial thing. Something is implied in this turning away. As if He would say: "You bring a sinner to Me, you sinners. If you think I should condemn sins, I begin with you."

— Sermon 16A. This is the same instinct as Jerome's "he wrote their sins," but Augustine keeps it in the gesture rather than putting words on the ground: the message is that the accusers stand accused, and it is delivered by the averted face, not by a legible list.3

Grace on the ground, the Law on stone (Sermon 272B)

A second sermon compresses the law/grace figure to its core — the finger of God is the Holy Spirit, and where that finger once wrote on stone it now writes on soil that can receive seed:

What did this indulgence signify? Grace. What did that hardness signify? The law given on stones. Therefore, the Lord was writing with His finger, but now on the ground, from which fruit could be received. However, whatever is sown on stone does not sprout, because it cannot send down roots. The finger of God, and the finger of God: the law was written by the finger of God, the finger of God, the Holy Spirit.

— Sermon 272B. The identification of the "finger of God" with the Holy Spirit (Exodus 31:18; cf. Luke 11:20) is the hinge Bede would repeat, and the stone/soil contrast is Augustine's most durable single contribution to the reading of the scene.4

Where Augustine sits on the timeline

Augustine writes a generation after Ambrose's first guesses and about contemporaneously with Jerome, and the contrast between the two Latin doctors sets the terms for everything after. Jerome hands down the durable content-reading ("he wrote their sins," fixed to Jeremiah 17:13); Augustine hands down the durable refusal to decode, reading the act as the passage from law to grace. Both readings are then catalogued together by the schoolmen — Bonaventure lists "Augustine responds that He wrote what He spoke" first among the options, and Aquinas makes Augustine's law-on-soft-earth figure the climax of his three reasons. That the single most authoritative Latin father on the passage is the one who would not say what was written is the strongest patristic anchor for the modern verdict that the Evangelist left the content blank by design.5

Notes

  • Augustine reads the gesture; Jerome reads the text. The two exhaust the ancient Latin options between them, and the later catalogues (Bonaventure, Aquinas) simply arrange the pair. See the summary timeline for how the split propagates.
  • Augustine's law/grace figure — God's finger writing on soft ground rather than stone — is doctrinally of a piece with his theology of grace elsewhere; here it functions specifically as a way of not answering the content question while still reading the scene as revelation.
