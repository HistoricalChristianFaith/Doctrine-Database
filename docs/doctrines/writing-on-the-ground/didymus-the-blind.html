Didymus the Blind — the story in Greek, but no writing on the ground

Dates: c. 380 (the Commentary on Ecclesiastes, recovered among the Tura papyri); Didymus c. 313–398 · Wikipedia
Summary: Didymus, head of the catechetical school at Alexandria, is the earliest Greek writer known to retell the story of the woman accused before Jesus — roughly a decade before Ambrose first guessed at the content in Latin. His testimony matters twice over. First, it qualifies the textbook claim (Metzger's) that "no Greek Church Father prior to Euthymius Zigabenus" so much as mentions the passage. Second, and decisively for this question: the form Didymus knows has no writing on the ground at all. He reports only the "let him who has not sinned cast the stone" saying and the accusers' withdrawal — the stooping and writing of John 8:6, 8 are simply absent. The earliest Greek shape of the scene is one in which Jesus writes nothing.

What Didymus reports (Commentary on Ecclesiastes 223.6b–13a)

Commenting on Ecclesiastes 7:21–22 — "do not take to heart everything people say… for you know in your own heart that many times you yourself have cursed others" — Didymus turns to the danger of judging others and adduces the story as an illustration:

We find, therefore, in certain gospels [the following story]. A woman, it says, was condemned by the Jews for a sin and was being sent to be stoned in the place where that was customary to happen. The Savior, it says, when he saw her and observed that they were ready to stone her, said to those who were about to cast stones, "He who has not sinned, let him take a stone and cast it. If anyone is conscious in himself not to have sinned, let him take up a stone and smite her." And no one dared. Since they knew in themselves and perceived that they themselves were guilty in some things, they did not dare to strike her.

— Didymus, Commentary on Ecclesiastes, on Eccl 7:21–22, trans. Bart Ehrman.1 Three features stand out. Didymus introduces it as something found "in certain gospels" (ἐν τισιν εὐαγγελίοις) — he does not attribute it to John. The account has the woman merely "condemned… for a sin," not specifically adultery. And it turns entirely on the spoken challenge and the accusers' guilty conscience; there is no finger, no dust, no inscription. This is an intervention-in-an-execution story, close in shape to the fragment Eusebius credits to Papias (a woman "accused of many sins") and to the Gospel according to the Hebrews, rather than the fuller Johannine pericope that frames the saying between two acts of writing.

Why a witness with no writing belongs on this timeline

This page catalogues what Jesus was thought to have written on the ground. Didymus earns his place precisely by supplying a counter-datum: the oldest Greek form of the episode carries the reproach of the accusers without the writing gesture. That is a live piece of evidence for the view — argued by Ehrman among others — that the dust-writing (and much else in John 7:53–8:11) belongs to a later, conflated stage of the tradition rather than to its earliest attested core. If so, the entire interpretive contest that follows — Ambrose's oracle, Jerome's "their sins," the schoolmen's menu, the modern reconstructions — is a set of answers to a question the story did not originally pose.2

Notes

  • Careful scope: Didymus attests the story circulating in Greek in fourth-century Alexandria; he does not attest the Johannine passage as such (he cites "certain gospels," not John). So he corrects the "no Greek father comments on it" claim only in its loose form — strictly, he is a witness to the pericope's tradition, not a commentator on John 8. See the summary timeline and, for the first Greek commentator on the Johannine text itself, Euthymius Zigabenus (c. 1118).
  • Didymus's copy of the Commentary on Ecclesiastes survived only by accident: it was among the sixth- and seventh-century codices hidden in a cave at Tura, south of Cairo, and rediscovered in 1941. The pericope sits at page 223 of that papyrus.
