Bonaventure — the guesses catalogued, and "no other pen"

Dates: c. 1257 (Commentary on John); Bonaventure c. 1221–1274 · Wikipedia
Summary: By the thirteenth century the answers had become a set to be surveyed, and Bonaventure lays them out as one: Augustine ("He wrote what He spoke"), Ambrose ("Earth, earth, write down the rejected men," from Jeremiah), the Interlinear Gloss ("their sins," the crystallized form of Jerome), and "others" who say he wrote letters in which each could read his own sins. He registers the Greek puzzle too — that some thought the story "was not found in the ancient Greek books," or was inserted "just as the story of Susanna into Daniel," and notes Chrysostom's silence — and, asking why Jesus wrote with his finger, sets the deflating literal answer beside the mystical one: "I believe He did not have another pen."

The four reasons for writing (on John 8:6)

Before cataloguing the content, Bonaventure asks why Jesus wrote at all, and gives four reasons — the first of them Bede's self-examination moral, quoted by name:

The moral reason why He wrote before the sentence and after on the ground is to teach us, as Bede says, that "before we rebuke a sinning neighbor, and after we have rendered the ministry of correction, we should examine ourselves with a worthy investigation of humility, lest perchance we be entangled either in the same sins that we rebuke, or in others." Another reason is to show that a sentence is to be delivered with deliberation and first written down, and that words are to be measured according to the order of law. The third reason is to show that He condemns unwillingly… The fourth reason is to give them opportunity to desist from their malicious case.

— Commentary on John 8. The four reasons — self-examination (Bede), deliberate written judgment, reluctant condemnation, and room to withdraw — are nearly the same set that Aquinas would give, minus the ranking; the second reason (write the sentence before pronouncing it) even anticipates the legal reconstruction that T. W. Manson would offer seven centuries later.1

The menu of contents (on John 8:6)

Then the catalogue proper — four answers to "what was He writing?", each tagged to its source:

As to what is asked: what was He writing? Augustine responds that He wrote what He spoke. But Ambrose says that He wrote: "Earth, earth, write down the rejected men," and this is drawn from Jeremiah chapter twenty-two. The Interlinear Gloss says that He was writing "their sins." Others say that He was writing letters in which each one could read his own sins.

— Commentary on John 8. This is the clearest single inventory of the ancient options: Augustine's "what He spoke," Ambrose's Jeremiah-oracle ("earth, earth"), Jerome's "their sins" (now settled as the Gloss's flat reading), and the folk-legend that each accuser saw his own sins spelled out. Bonaventure reports them without adjudicating — the weighing is left to Aquinas.2

"No other pen" — the finger, literal and mystical (on John 8:6)

Asked why Jesus wrote with his finger, Bonaventure gives a famously deflating literal answer alongside the moral and allegorical ones:

As to what is asked: why did He write with His finger? The reason is literal, because I believe He did not have another pen. There is also a moral and allegorical reason: moral, because by the finger is understood discernment; He wished each person to examine himself with discernment; allegorical, to signify that He was the one who had written the Law with His finger, Exodus chapter thirty-one.

— Commentary on John 8. The moral sense (finger = discernment) is Alcuin's; the allegorical (the finger that wrote the Law) is Augustine's and Bede's. What is Bonaventure's own is the dry literal reason — a rare note of common sense in a tradition otherwise given to decoding — that a man stooping in the dust simply had nothing else to write with.3

The Greek puzzle: Susanna, and Chrysostom's silence (on John 8:6)

Bonaventure also records the doubt about the passage's authenticity — the analogy to the deutero-canonical Susanna, the claim that it "was not found in the ancient Greek books," and Chrysostom's failure to comment on it:

Some respond that the whole thing seems to have been done by human contrivance; whence they said that this was inserted into the Gospel of John, just as the story of Susanna into Daniel. As testimony for this matter they adduced that Chrysostom makes no mention of this… As for the fact that Chrysostom says nothing about it, I do not know the reason. Some say that it was not found in the ancient Greek books, because John added it after the Gospel was completed.

— Commentary on John 8. Bonaventure himself sides with "Augustine and our expositors," who "explain it as part of the sequence of the Gospel," but he faithfully preserves the textual doubt — the medieval Latin awareness that the Greek tradition had passed the story over, which the summary traces back to Chrysostom's homilies and Euthymius Zigabenus. His guess that "John added it after the Gospel was completed" is an early attempt to square the pericope's canonical standing with its absence from the oldest Greek copies.4

The second writing: room to depart (on John 8:8)

On the second stooping Bonaventure quotes Bede directly and attaches the two Jeremiah verses that underlie the "written in the earth" readings:

"And again bending down, he wrote on the ground," so that he might give those who were confounded a place to depart without shame. Whence Bede: "He wished to bend down before his tempters and write on the ground, so that, turning his face to something else, he might give them free departure, since he foresaw that those struck by his response would depart sooner than ask further questions"; Jeremiah seventeen: "All who forsake you shall be confounded: those who depart from you shall be written in the earth"; Jeremiah twenty-two: "O earth, earth, hear the word of the Lord: Write this man as barren."

— Commentary on John 8. The pairing is exact: Jeremiah 17:13 grounds Jerome's "their sins… written in the earth," and Jeremiah 22:29–30 grounds Ambrose's "earth, earth" oracle. Bonaventure thus lays out not only the guesses but the two proof-texts the Latin tradition kept returning to.5

Where Bonaventure sits on the timeline

Bonaventure is the great cataloguer. Nearly everything the fathers had said about the scene — Augustine's "what He spoke," Ambrose's Jeremiah oracle, Jerome's "their sins," the folk-legend of the self-reading letters, Bede's self-examination and "room to depart," Alcuin's finger-of-discretion, and the Greek doubt about the passage — is gathered on his page and tagged to its owner. He does not rank them; that is the contribution of his younger contemporary Aquinas, who takes the same menu and, "unusually among the ancients and medievals," weighs it. Read together, the two Franciscan-and-Dominican masters mark the moment the tradition became self-conscious about its own guessing — the distant ancestor of the modern monographs that catalogue three dozen proposals.6

Notes

  • Bonaventure catalogues; Aquinas adjudicates. The two thirteenth-century pages are best read as a pair — same options, different verdict.
  • His "I believe He did not have another pen" is the tradition's one deflationary note before Calvin, and worth setting against the elaborate content-reconstructions on either side of it.
  • Bonaventure's report of the Susanna analogy and Chrysostom's silence is a medieval Latin witness to the textual question that the summary frames through the Greek tradition (Chrysostom, Euthymius Zigabenus, and Metzger).
