Jerome — "he wrote their sins"

Dates: c. 415 (the Dialogue against the Pelagians); Jerome c. 347–420 · Wikipedia
Summary: Jerome supplies the single most durable answer to the question the Evangelist leaves open: Jesus wrote the sins of the accusers. In one sentence of the Dialogue against the Pelagians he names the content ("the sins… of those who were making the accusation, as well as the sins of all mortal beings") and anchors it to a proof-text (Jeremiah 17:13, "Those who depart from you shall be written in the earth"). That reading crystallized over the following centuries into the Interlinear Gloss's flat "their sins," and then — three or four centuries on — into the very text of Scripture, when scribes added "the sins of every one of them" to John 8:8 itself.

The one sentence that fixed the tradition (Against the Pelagians 2.17)

Jerome is arguing, against the Pelagians, that all are sinners — and reaches for the adultery pericope as an illustration. In passing he gives the reading that would eclipse all others:

The Scribes and Pharisees kept accusing her and kept earnestly pressing the case, for they wished to stone her to death, according to the law. "But Jesus, stooping down, began to write with his finger on the ground," the sins, to be sure, of those who were making the accusation, as well as the sins of all mortal beings according to what is written in the prophet, "Those who depart from you shall be written in the earth."

— Dialogue against the Pelagians 2.17. Two things make this the fountainhead. First, it is content, not figure: unlike Augustine, who read the act as sign and refused to name any words, Jerome states plainly what was written — sins, the accusers' and everyone's. Second, it comes with a proof-text. Jerome's Jeremiah 17:13 ("Those who depart from you shall be written in the earth") supplies the mechanism: the ground-writing enrolls the departing accusers among those "written in the earth," the opposite of the just "written in heaven." The verse recurs in nearly every later treatment — in Bonaventure, in Aquinas, and in the scribal gloss itself.1

Where Jerome sits on the timeline

Jerome writes at almost the same moment as Augustine, and the two doctors divide the field between them for the next thousand years. Augustine reads the gesture and declines the content; Jerome reads the content and gives it a proof-text. The later catalogues keep both on the menu — Bonaventure lists "the Interlinear Gloss says that He was writing 'their sins'" alongside Augustine's and Ambrose's options, and Aquinas ranks the Jeremiah-based readings against the "better opinion" that Jesus wrote his own spoken words. But Jerome's answer had a second life the others did not: it hardened, via the Gloss, into popular certainty, and then into manuscripts. Around the ninth century a cluster of mostly Byzantine witnesses added to John 8:8 the words "the sins of every one of them," so that the verse now states what Jerome had inferred. Metzger judges the addition a later expansion made "in order to satisfy pious curiosity concerning what it was that Jesus wrote upon the ground" — which is to say, Jerome's reading, four centuries on, written into Scripture.2

Notes

  • Jerome reads the text; Augustine reads the gesture. Between them they exhaust the ancient Latin options, and the schoolmen (Bonaventure, Aquinas) simply catalogue the pair. See the summary timeline for the downstream scribal gloss that turns Jerome's inference into text.
  • The proof-text Jerome uses (Jeremiah 17:13) is the same verse Augustine and Aquinas invoke for the "written on earth, not in heaven" figure — the one biblical hook shared across the whole Latin reading of the scene.
  • One caveat to keep in view: Jerome states plainly what was written, but the Evangelist twice withholds the content (John 8:6, 8), and Jerome offers his reading in a single passing sentence to prove that all men are sinners — not as an exegesis of the words. His inference was later mistaken for the text's own testimony (the scribal gloss). Whether the reading holds is weighed on its own page: Did Jesus write the accusers' sins? (assessed contested).
