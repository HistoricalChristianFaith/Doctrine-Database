Papias of Hierapolis — the earliest witness to the story

Dates: c. 110 (the Exposition of the Sayings of the Lord, in five books; Papias's floruit is dated variously c. 100–130) · Wikipedia
Summary: Papias, bishop of Hierapolis in Phrygia and a hearer of the presbyter John, is the earliest writer known to have told the story of the woman accused before Jesus — older than Didymus by two and a half centuries, and older than any Latin guesser at the ground-writing by nearly three. But his form, as Eusebius reports it, carries no finger, no dust, and no inscription: it is simply "a story of a woman accused of many sins before the Lord," which Eusebius says stood "in the Gospel according to the Hebrews." His witness therefore anchors the timeline where it now opens, and does so as evidence for the same point Didymus makes: the oldest forms of this episode know nothing of writing on the ground.

Claims & evidence

The earliest report of the story — without the writing (Eusebius, Church History 3.39.17)

Papias's own five books survive only in fragments quoted by later writers. The decisive notice is Eusebius's, who, having just recorded what Papias transmitted about Mark and Matthew, closes his summary of Papias's contents with a single sentence about the woman:

And the same writer uses testimonies from the first Epistle of John and from that of Peter likewise. And he relates another story of a woman, who was accused of many sins before the Lord, which is contained in the Gospel according to the Hebrews. These things we have thought it necessary to observe in addition to what has been already stated.

— Eusebius of Caesarea, Church History 3.39.17 (reporting Papias, c. 110). Two features matter for this timeline. First, the charge is "many sins," not adultery, and the setting Eusebius names is the Gospel according to the Hebrews, not John — so the notice attests an early, floating tradition of an accused woman brought to Jesus, not yet the Johannine pericope in the shape we now read. Second, and decisively, there is no writing on the ground at all: Papias's is the oldest known form of the episode, and like Didymus's Greek form two and a half centuries later, it has Jesus write nothing.1

The identification with John 7:53–8:11 (Rufinus; the Syriac and Armenian tradition)

That Papias's "woman accused of many sins" is the woman taken in adultery of John 8 is an identification made by later readers, not by Papias or Eusebius. Rufinus, translating Eusebius into Latin around 400, was the first on record to suppose the two stories the same — a supposition the standard editions still flag at this line. The connection hardens in the Eastern tradition: the tenth-century Arabic chronicler Agapius of Menbij reports that the Hierapolitan master who "wrote five treatises on the Gospel" spoke, "in his treatise on the Gospel of John," of "a woman who was adulterous" brought to Christ, whom he answers "Whoever of you knows that he is innocent of what she has done, let him testify against her" — and the thirteenth-century Armenian Vardan Arewelts’i likewise says "the story of that adulterous woman … was written about by a certain Papias, a student of John … Eusebius wrote about this."

And he mentions in his treatise on the Gospel of John, that in the book of John the Evangelist, he speaks of a woman who was adulterous, so when they presented her to Christ our Lord … He told the Jews who brought her to Him, "Whoever of you knows that he is innocent of what she has done, let him testify against her with what he has." So when He told them that, none of them responded with anything and they left.

— Agapius of Menbij, Universal History (10th c.), on the events of the twelfth year of Trajan. These later witnesses show how firmly antiquity came to read Papias as the first attestation of the pericope; they do not, however, recover any ground-writing, and the medieval retellings supply the "first stone" saying rather than the finger in the dust.2

Where Papias sits on the timeline

Papias is the head of the whole tradition and, at the same time, a witness against the antiquity of the detail this page tracks. The scene existed — in some form, and very early — by c. 110; but the two oldest independent reports of it (Papias here, Didymus at Alexandria c. 380) both lack the writing on the ground, and Papias's version is not even securely attached to John. The finger, the dust, and the (unspecified) inscription of John 8:6, 8 are on the present evidence a feature of a later, fuller stage of the story — which is exactly why the reconstructions catalogued on the summary timeline are guessing at the content of a verse the earliest witnesses did not yet read.3

Notes

  • Whether Papias's "woman accused of many sins" is the pericope adulterae is genuinely open. The charge Eusebius names is broader than adultery, and the home he names is the Gospel according to the Hebrews; the identification with John 8 is Rufinus's and the later East's, not Papias's. Many modern critics accept a family relationship — the story Papias knew, the story Didymus knew, and the Johannine pericope belong to one growing tradition — without treating the three as verbally identical.
  • The five-book Exposition of the Sayings of the Lord is lost; everything above is preserved at second hand (Eusebius, and the Syriac and Armenian chroniclers who depend on him). Papias is placed at c. 110 by his own report that he inquired of those who had followed the apostolic elders; the anchor precedes Didymus (c. 380) by design, opening the timeline at its true head.
