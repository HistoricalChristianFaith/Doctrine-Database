Euthymius Zigabenus — the Greek commentator who obelizes the passage

Dates: c. 1118 (the Commentary on the Four Gospels); Euthymius c. 1050–1120 · Wikipedia
Summary: Where the Latin West spent a millennium guessing what Jesus wrote, the Greek East, so far as its surviving commentaries go, said almost nothing — and the one Byzantine commentator to reach the passage, Euthymius Zigabenus, reaches it only to bracket it. In his Commentary on the Four Gospels he notes that everything from John 7:53 to 8:12 is, "in the accurate copies, either not found or marked with an obelus, because these things seem spurious and added." He is the terminus Metzger has in mind in the much-quoted line that "no Greek Church Father prior to Euthymius Zigabenus" comments on the story. He does then expound the passage as edifying — but he never asks what Jesus wrote; the Greek tradition's characteristic response to the scene is text-critical doubt, not reconstruction.

The critical note (Commentary on the Four Gospels, on John 7:53–8:11)

Παρὰ τοῖς ἀκριβέσιν ἀντιγράφοις ἢ οὐχ εὕρηται ἢ ὐβέλισται.

— "In the accurate copies [these things] either are not found or have been obelized." Migne's parallel Latin gives the fuller sentence: one should know that everything from this place [7:53] as far as "Again Jesus spoke to them saying, I am the light of the world" [8:12] is, "in the more accurate copies, either not found, or marked through with an obelus, because they seem illegitimate and added" (quia videntur illegitima et adjecta).1 The obelus (–, the "spit" mark Origen and the Alexandrian critics used to flag a suspect line) is the point: Euthymius does not merely omit the pericope, he records that trained scribes had marked it as doubtful. Having said so, he goes on to comment on the story as spiritually useful — so his verdict is textual, not doctrinal.

Where he sits, and why the note is probably older than he is

Euthymius writes under Alexius Comnenus, around 1118, a compiler by trade: his Gospel commentary is largely a catena, stitched from earlier Greek exegetes (Chrysostom, Theophylact, and others). His verdict on the pericope carries real weight as the first Greek commentator to register it, but the text-critical note behind it is almost certainly inherited, not original. A closely parallel remark on the longer ending of Mark (16:9–20) stands in his commentary and matches, nearly word for word, Theophylact of Ohrid a generation earlier — so the "accurate copies do not contain it" judgment is a piece of received Byzantine scholarship that Euthymius passes on, the sediment of centuries in which the accurate Greek copies of John lacked these verses.2

Notes

  • Euthymius is the mirror image of the Latin guessers on the summary timeline. Ambrose, Jerome, and the schoolmen ask what Jesus wrote; Euthymius asks whether the verses reporting that he wrote anything are even part of the Gospel. For the earliest Greek witness to the story — also without any writing detail — see Didymus the Blind (c. 380).
  • His note is the load-bearing evidence for the framing of the whole page: content-guessing about the ground-writing is, in antiquity, an almost exclusively Latin habit, and the Greek East's one recorded reaction is to doubt the text.
