Thomas Aquinas — the same menu, weighed

Dates: c. 1270 (Commentary on John; the Catena Aurea c. 1264); Aquinas 1225–1274 · Wikipedia
Summary: Aquinas gives the same inventory as Bonaventure but, unusually among the ancients and medievals, weighs it. Of the two live options — that Jesus wrote Ambrose's Jeremiah oracle, or that he wrote the very words he spoke — he judges the second "the better opinion," but immediately adds the caution that later readers would echo: "neither of these opinions is certain." He then reads the choice of the ground itself three ways — the testers "written on the earth" (Jeremiah 17:13) against the just written in heaven; the signs wrought in the assumed flesh; and the soft earth, against the stone tablets, as "the sweetness and the softness of the New Law." In the Catena Aurea he had already gathered the fathers' comments; in the Commentary he sorts them.

Weighing the two options (Commentary on John 8, on 8:6)

Where Bonaventure listed four candidate contents without ranking, Aquinas narrows to two and picks — then withholds certainty:

Jesus wrote his sentence on the earth with his finger… Some say that he wrote the words Jeremiah: "O earth, earth, listen—write down this man as sterile" (Jer 22:29). According to others, and this is the better opinion, Jesus wrote down the very words he spoke, that is, Whoever among you is without sin, let him be the first to cast a stone at her. However, neither of these opinions is certain.

— Commentary on John 8. This is the tradition's first real adjudication. Aquinas prefers the reading that the writing simply doubled the spoken sentence (the option Bonaventure had labelled Augustine's, "He wrote what He spoke") over Ambrose's Jeremiah oracle — but the closing "neither… is certain" is the honest limit that Calvin would later turn into an outright refusal and the modern consensus into a verdict of unrecoverability.1

Three readings of the ground (Commentary on John 8, on 8:6)

Aquinas then takes over Augustine's three figural readings of why the ground and sets them in order — the accusers written on earth; the signs of the Incarnation; and the soft earth of the New Law:

Jesus wrote on the earth for three reasons. First, according to Augustine, to show that those who were testing him would be written on the earth: "O Lord, all who leave you will be written on the earth" (Jer 17:13). But those who are just and the disciples who follow him are written in heaven… Secondly, he wrote on earth to show that he would perform signs on earth… And so he says that Jesus was bending down, by the mystery of the Incarnation, by means of which he performed miracles in the flesh he had assumed. Thirdly, he wrote on the earth because the Old Law was written on tablets of stone (Ex 31; 2 Cor 3), which signify its harshness… But the earth is soft. And so Jesus wrote on the earth to show the sweetness and the softness of the New Law that he gave to us.

— Commentary on John 8. The three are Augustine's, credited to him by name, but Aquinas gives them their crispest form: Jeremiah 17:13 against Luke 10:20 for the first, the Incarnation "in the flesh he had assumed" for the second, and the soft-earth/stone-tablet contrast (Exodus 31; 2 Corinthians 3) for the third. This is the law/grace figure of Augustine and Bede brought to its most schematic point.2

Three things in giving sentence (Commentary on John 8, on 8:6)

Aquinas also reads the gesture as a model of judgment, matching each element to a judicial virtue — the bending as kindness, the finger as discretion, the writing as certitude:

First, there should be kindness in condescending to those to be punished; and so he says, Jesus was bending down… Secondly, there should be discretion in determining the judgment and so he says that Jesus wrote with his finger, which because of its flexibility signifies discretion… Thirdly, there should be certitude about the sentence given; and so he says, Jesus wrote.

— Commentary on John 8. The "finger… signifies discretion" is Alcuin's figure, here folded into a three-part anatomy of right judgment (kindness, discretion, certitude) that parallels Bonaventure's four reasons for the writing.3

The second writing: firmness, and freedom to leave (Commentary on John 8, on 8:8)

On the second stooping Aquinas gives three reasons — the firmness of the sentence, the accusers' unworthiness to be looked at, and (Bede's reading) consideration for their embarrassment:

Jesus continued to write, and again bending down, he wrote. He did this, first, to show the firmness of his sentence, "God is not like a man, who may lie…" (Num 23:19). Secondly, he did it to show that they were not worthy to look at him… Thirdly, he did this out of consideration for their embarrassment, to give them complete freedom to leave.

— Commentary on John 8. The third reason is Bede's "give them room to depart," which Aquinas had also preserved in the Catena Aurea; the first (the sentence is fixed and will not be revised) is his own addition to the reading of the doubled gesture.4

The Catena: gathering the fathers (Catena Aurea on John 8)

A few years before the Commentary, Aquinas had assembled the patristic comments on the pericope into the Catena Aurea (Golden Chain) — the anthology that is now our source for the words of Alcuin and for the catena form of Augustine's and Bede's comments on the scene. There Augustine's Tractate 33 is excerpted ("Let the sinner be punished, but not by sinners"), Bede's "writing with His finger on the ground perhaps showed, that it was He who had written the law on stone," and Alcuin's "the ground denotes the human heart… the finger… discretion." The Catena is thus the pipe through which the earlier readings reached the schools — and the reason several of them survive at all.5

Where Aquinas sits on the timeline

Aquinas and Bonaventure are the twin thirteenth-century peaks of the Latin reading, and they divide the labour: Bonaventure catalogues the guesses, Aquinas ranks them. His verdict — the "better opinion" is that Jesus wrote his own spoken words, but "neither… is certain" — is the closest the pre-modern tradition comes to the honest agnosticism that Calvin and the modern consensus would make the default. And his three readings of the ground itself gather up, in their tightest form, the whole Augustinian line that runs from the Tractates through Bede. After Aquinas the speculative tradition has essentially said what it has to say; what follows is refusal (Calvin) and, much later, reconstruction on new, legal and ritual grounds.6
