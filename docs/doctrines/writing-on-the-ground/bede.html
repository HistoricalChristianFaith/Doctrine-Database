Bede — the same finger, and a look turned away

Dates: c. 730 (Homilies on the Gospels); Bede c. 673–735 · Wikipedia
Summary: Bede folds two strands together. From Augustine he takes the figural reading — the finger on the ground shows that this is the very one who "once wrote the Ten Commandments of the law on stone with his finger, that is, by the action of the Holy Spirit." But to the second stooping (8:8) he adds a strikingly practical reading found nowhere before him: by looking away, Jesus gave the abashed accusers room to withdraw without being watched, since he foresaw they "would be more inclined to depart quickly than to ask him more questions." He then draws the moral that Alcuin would inherit: that Jesus wrote both before and after his verdict teaches us to examine ourselves before and after we correct a neighbour.

The same finger that wrote the Law (Homilies 1.25)

Bede opens with the Augustinian identification — the finger on the ground is the finger that graved the Decalogue — and reads the choice of stone for the old Law as fitted to a "hard-hearted and defiant people":

When the Lord was about to give pardon to the sinful woman, he desired to write with his finger on the ground, in order to point out that it was he himself who once wrote the Ten Commandments of the law on stone with his finger, that is, by the action of the Holy Spirit. And it is good that the law was written upon stone, since it was given to subdue the inmost hearts of a hard-hearted and defiant people.

— Homilies on the Gospels 1.25. The "finger… that is, by the action of the Holy Spirit" is Augustine's gloss (Exodus 31:18 with Luke 11:20); Bede passes it on intact and makes the stone/hard-heartedness link explicit.1

The look turned away, so they could go (Homilies 1.25)

Bede's distinctive contribution is to read the second writing not as a further sign but as a considerate act. Averting his gaze, Jesus spared the accusers the shame of being watched as they slipped out:

In line with our usual human way of doing things, we can understand that the reason why the Lord might wish to bend before his unprincipled tempters and to write on the ground was that by directing his look elsewhere he might give them the freedom to go away. He foresaw that as they had been astounded by his answer, they would be more inclined to depart quickly than to ask him more questions.

— Homilies on the Gospels 1.25. This "human way of doing things" reading — the writing as a tactful turning-away rather than a coded message — is quoted almost verbatim by both Bonaventure and Aquinas (who lists it among his three reasons for the second stooping), and it anticipates the modern instinct that the gesture may be doing something other than spelling out words.2

Write before and after: examine yourself (Homilies 1.25)

Bede then turns the doubled writing — before the verdict and after — into a rule for anyone who would correct another. That Jesus wrote twice teaches us to search ourselves both before and after we rebuke a neighbour:

Figuratively speaking, the fact that both before and after he gave his opinion he bent and wrote on the ground admonishes us that both before we rebuke a sinning neighbor and after we have rendered to him the ministry of due correction, we should subject ourselves to a suitably humble examination, lest perhaps we be entangled in the same things that we censure in [our neighbors] or in any other sort of misdeeds… what saving remedy is left for us except that, when we look at some other sinner, we immediately bend down—that is, we humbly observe how we would be cast down by our frail condition if divine benevolence did not keep us from falling? Let us write with a finger on the ground—that is, let us meticulously ponder with discrimination whether we can say with blessed Job, "For our heart does not censure us in all our life."

— Homilies on the Gospels 1.25. This self-examination moral — the ground as the human heart, the finger as discernment — is the seed Alcuin would tighten into a compact allegory a generation later, and which Bonaventure would quote under Bede's own name.3

Where Bede sits on the timeline

Bede stands between the fathers he transmits and the schoolmen who transmit him. Looking back, he consolidates Augustine's figural reading (the finger of God, the Holy Spirit, stone against ground); looking forward, his practical "give them room to go" reading and his "examine yourself before and after" moral both pass into the medieval catena tradition, quoted by name in Bonaventure and Aquinas. He does not add a new theory of what was written — he leaves the content where Augustine left it — but he enriches the reading of the gesture more than any single father between Augustine and the thirteenth century.4

Notes

  • Bede is the conduit: Augustine's figural reading and the self-examination moral reach the schoolmen through his Homilies. Alcuin compresses the moral; Bonaventure and Aquinas quote the "give them room to depart" reading almost word for word.
  • Like Augustine, Bede offers no candidate words. His interest is entirely in what the twofold gesture signifies and teaches — a sign-and-moral reading, not a content reconstruction.
