Did Jesus write Torah verses against the false witnesses?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — the theme it serves (the accusers, not the woman, are the ones the Law condemns) has real footing in the scene, but the specific claim that Jesus wrote out Exodus 23:1b then 23:7a is an over-precise conjecture, resting on a self-estimated writing limit, for a content the Evangelist twice withholds · advanced by J. D. M. Derrett (1963), followed by Leon Morris

Related arguments: the modern reconstructions — see also the Roman-verdict theory · the Numbers 5 ordeal

The claim

Derrett's reconstruction turns on a physical constraint of his own devising: a person stooped over the ground, scratching with a finger from a low, seated posture, could form no more than about sixteen Hebrew characters in a line.1 Using that self-imposed ceiling as a filter — it disqualifies any longer candidate text — he reconstructs the withheld content as two short clauses of the Torah's law on witnesses: the first writing (8:6) was Exodus 23:1b — "you shall not join hands with a wicked man to be a malicious witness" — and the second (8:8) was Exodus 23:7a — "keep far from a false charge" — so that Jesus, in the manner of a rabbinic legal opinion, silently indicts the accusers as the guilty party, the same verse (Exod 23:7) Daniel cites to acquit Susanna.2

Who advances it

Derrett proposed the reconstruction in a learned 1963 study of the pericope's legal background, and Leon Morris inclined to it in his 1971 commentary. Derrett's wider thesis — that the scene turns on the unworthiness of the accusers and their testimony, not on the woman's guilt — is shared far more widely than his specific guess at the inscription; Raymond Brown, who rejects the guess, grants the underlying point about the accusers and the law of witnesses.3

The evidence for

The theme is genuinely in the text. Jesus' spoken words — "he that is without sin among you, let him first cast a stone at her" (John 8:7) — invoke the Torah rule that the witnesses cast the first stone: "The hand of the witnesses shall be first upon him to put him to death" (Deuteronomy 17:7; cf. 13:9). The surrounding chapters press the question of admissible testimony (John 7:51, "doth our law judge any man before it hear him?"; 8:13, "thou bearest record of thyself"). So a reading on which the accusers are the ones the Law arraigns — as false or disqualified witnesses — is well anchored, and Exodus 23:1–7 (the false-witness law that stands behind Susanna's acquittal) is the natural Pentateuchal text for it. Derrett's contribution is to make the connection concrete: those are the very words, he argues, that Jesus traced in the dust.

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The scene really does put the accusers on trial, and the law of witnesses (Deut 17:7; Exod 23:1–7) really is the operative background — Jesus' spoken sentence turns the witnesses' own privilege of casting the first stone against them. As a reading of the scene's legal theme, "the Torah condemns these witnesses, not this woman" is sound.

What is overstated or imprecise. The step from that theme to Derrett's specific reconstruction — that Jesus wrote out Exodus 23:1b at 8:6 and 23:7a at 8:8 — is conjecture the text does not license. The Evangelist twice reports the writing and twice omits the content, and Derrett's method reverses that reticence, supplying precise verse-references the author declined to give. The load-bearing physical premise, the "sixteen Hebrew characters," is Derrett's own back-of-the-envelope estimate of how much a person can scratch in dust from a stooped position, not a datum — and it does double duty, first as a filter to select short clauses and then as if it were evidence they were written. Notably, Derrett himself rejected Deuteronomy 17:7 as the inscription (its wording would not compress within his character limit) while keeping it as the background to Jesus' spoken words — so even on his own account the strongest witness-law hook is a spoken echo, not something written. Chris Keith notes that "few have accepted this solution as it requires an incredibly creative reader … and is based upon Derrett's speculation as to how many Hebrew characters a human can write from a sitting position," and groups it with readings that "invent wholesale the verse references for each act of writing," sacrificing what the text gives for what it withholds.4

Assessment

Weak. Keep the two questions apart. The interpretation Derrett's argument serves — that the episode arraigns the accusers under the Torah's law of witnesses, so the real defendants are the men holding the stones — is well grounded, carried by Jesus' spoken invocation of the first-stone rule (Deut 17:7) and the surrounding testimony-motif, and it does not depend on Derrett at all. But the argument as Derrett states it is a claim about the writing: that its withheld content was Exodus 23:1b and 23:7a, fixed by a self-estimated sixteen-character limit. That specific reconstruction does not hold on its best case — it supplies exactly the content the Evangelist twice and pointedly declines to supply, and its physical linchpin is a guess. So the theme is sound and the inscription is not: as a decoding of what Jesus wrote, weak. (For the sounder, spoken-word version of the same theme, see the timeline's treatment of the witnesses and the scene as a whole.)
