Alcuin of York — the ground is the heart, the finger is discretion

Dates: c. 800; Alcuin c. 735–804 · Wikipedia
Summary: Alcuin drops the question of content altogether and turns the whole scene into a compact moral allegory. The ground is the human heart; the finger, "jointed and flexible," is discretion. The gesture instructs us, when we see faults in our neighbours, "not immediately and rashly to condemn them, but after searching our own hearts to begin with, to examine them attentively with the finger of discretion." He takes over Bede's self-examination moral and tightens it into a two-part figure. His words survive because Aquinas gathered them into the Catena Aurea.

The allegory of ground and finger (on John 8:6)

Where Jerome asked what the words were and Augustine read the act as the passage from law to grace, Alcuin reads the physical elements of the gesture as a moral cipher — and the content question simply falls away:

The ground denotes the human heart, which yieldeth the fruit either of good or of bad actions: the finger jointed and flexible, discretion. He instructs us then, when we see any faults in our neighbours, not immediately and rashly to condemn them, but after searching our own hearts to begin with, to examine them attentively with the finger of discretion.

— preserved in Thomas Aquinas, Catena Aurea on John 8. The move is Bede's self-examination moral compressed to its allegorical core: ground = heart, finger = discretion, lesson = judge yourself before your neighbour. Alcuin makes no claim about what Jesus wrote, because on his reading the writing is not a message to be decoded but an emblem of how we ought to weigh a neighbour's fault.1

The fixed eyes that let the accusers withdraw (on John 8:8)

On the second stooping Alcuin follows Bede's practical reading — the averted gaze as a courtesy that lets the abashed accusers slip away — and again reads it back into the moral of self-examination:

This is like our Lord; while His eyes are fixed, and He seems attending to something else, He gives the bystanders an opportunity of retiring: a tacit admonition to us to consider always both before we condemn a brother for a sin, and after we have punished him, whether we are not guilty ourselves of the same fault, or others as bad.

— preserved in Thomas Aquinas, Catena Aurea on John 8. This restates Bede's "give them room to depart" alongside the "before and after" self-scrutiny, keeping Alcuin's whole reading on the moral register rather than the evidential one.2

Where Alcuin sits on the timeline

Alcuin marks the point where the Latin tradition, having catalogued the guesses, turns the scene wholly moral. He inherits the self-examination reading from Bede and hands on a crisp allegory that the schoolmen keep in circulation: Aquinas preserves both of Alcuin's comments verbatim in the Catena Aurea, and the "finger = discretion" figure reappears in Aquinas's own Commentary on John and in Bonaventure's "moral and allegorical" reason for the finger. With Alcuin the content question is not answered but set aside — the writing is treated as instruction, not information.3

Notes

  • Alcuin's comments reach us only as excerpts in Aquinas's Catena Aurea; no independent commentary of his on the pericope is quoted here. The wording is that of John Henry Newman's English Catena.
  • The "ground = heart, finger = discretion" allegory is the tightest form of the self-examination line that begins with Bede and continues into Bonaventure; none of the three offers a reconstruction of the words.
