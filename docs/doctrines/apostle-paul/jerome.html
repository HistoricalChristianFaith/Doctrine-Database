Jerome on Paul

Dates: c. 393 (Jerome, Lives of Illustrious Men / De Viris Illustribus, ch. 5) · Lives of Illustrious Men · back to the Paul timeline
Summary: Jerome opens his catalogue of Christian authors with Peter and gives Paul the fifth entry. He supplies a compact biography drawn from Acts and the epistles, states the release-and-second-imprisonment openly, and closes with the fullest and most precise early statement of the martyrdom: beheaded at Rome in Nero’s fourteenth year, the same day as Peter, and buried on the Ostian way.

Claims & evidence

Origin and conversion

Jerome begins with a birthplace not found in Acts — Giscala in Judea, whence the family removed to Tarsus — and then follows the New Testament:

Paul, formerly called Saul, an apostle outside the number of the twelve apostles, was of the tribe of Benjamin and the town of Giscalis in Judea. When this was taken by the Romans he removed with his parents to Tarsus in Cilicia. Sent by them to Jerusalem to study law he was educated by Gamaliel a most learned man whom Luke mentions. But after he had been present at the death of the martyr Stephen… he proceeded to Damascus, where… he was transformed from a persecutor into an elect vessel.

— Jerome, Lives of Illustrious Men 5. Jerome explains the name-change from the proconsul Sergius Paulus, the first convert of the first journey, and has Paul “ordained apostle to the Gentiles by Peter, James and John.”

The release: “that the gospel of Christ might be preached also in the West”

Jerome states the acquittal at the first trial as fact and gives its purpose — the further, western mission:

It ought to be said that at the first defence, the power of Nero having not yet been confirmed, nor his wickedness broken forth to such a degree as the histories relate concerning him, Paul was dismissed by Nero, that the gospel of Christ might be preached also in the West.

— Lives of Illustrious Men 5. He reads 2 Timothy’s “I was delivered out of the mouth of the lion” as Paul’s own veiled word on Nero, “clearly indicating Nero as lion on account of his cruelty.” This “preached also in the West” is Jerome’s version of the Spanish mission (see the Spain argument).

The martyrdom: beheaded in Nero’s fourteenth year, buried on the Ostian way

Jerome then gives the death with a precision no earlier source matches:

He then, in the fourteenth year of Nero on the same day with Peter, was beheaded at Rome for Christ’s sake and was buried in the Ostian way, the twenty-seventh year after our Lord’s passion.

— Lives of Illustrious Men 5. The fourteenth year of Nero is A.D. 67/68; the burial “in the Ostian way” matches the site of Gaius’s “trophy” that Eusebius reports, where the basilica of San Paolo fuori le Mura stands over the traditional tomb.

Notes

  • The classic shape. With Jerome the tradition reaches the form the Western church would keep: the birthplace at Giscala, the release for a western mission, the beheading in Nero’s fourteenth year on the same day as Peter, and the tomb on the Ostian way. Later writers largely repeat him.
  • Dependent, not independent. Jerome is a compiler here; his entry draws on Acts, the epistles, and Eusebius (whom he plainly follows on the two imprisonments). His value is as the fullest and most precise statement of a tradition already fixed, not as a fresh witness.
  • “Preached also in the West.” Jerome does not name Spain in so many words, but his “the West” is the same tradition Clement (“the extreme limit of the west”) and the Muratorian canon (“the journey of Paul… to Spain”) transmit — the intended mission of Romans 15, now told as accomplished.
