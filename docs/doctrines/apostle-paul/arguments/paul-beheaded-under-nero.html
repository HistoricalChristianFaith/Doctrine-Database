Was Paul beheaded at Rome under Nero?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: sound — that Paul was executed at Rome under Nero (c. 64–67) rests on a chain of early, broad, and independent witnesses as strong as any in apostolic history — Clement of Rome within a generation, then Ignatius, Dionysius of Corinth, the Roman presbyter Gaius pointing to the tomb on the Ostian way, Tertullian, Eusebius, and Jerome — with no rival account of a different death anywhere. The fact of the Roman martyrdom under Nero is as secure as the death of any apostle; the specific manner (beheading by the sword, rather than the cross) is early (Tertullian) and fits Paul’s Roman citizenship, a half-step less certain than the fact itself; the picturesque details (milk from the neck, the three fountains) are late legend that does not touch the core.

The claim

The tradition, in its classic form, has two parts. First, the fact: Paul died a martyr at Rome under the emperor Nero, in the persecution of the mid-60s. Second, the manner: he was beheaded — put to the sword, the execution Roman law reserved for a citizen — whereas Peter, no citizen, was crucified. Jerome fixes the date in Nero’s fourteenth year (A.D. 67/68) and the burial on the Ostian way, where San Paolo fuori le Mura stands. This page weighs both parts, and distinguishes the firm core from the legendary embellishment that later gathered around it.1

The evidence for

Clement of Rome (c. 96) — a Roman bishop within a generation — names Paul among the “pillars” martyred “in our own generation,” who “suffered martyrdom under the prefects.” Ignatius of Antioch (c. 107), writing to Rome on his own way to martyrdom, appeals to Peter and Paul as those who had commanded there before him. Dionysius of Corinth (c. 170), quoted by Eusebius, says Peter and Paul “suffered martyrdom at the same time.” The Roman presbyter Gaius (c. 200) could point visitors to the apostles’ graves: “if you will go to the Vatican or to the Ostian way, you will find the trophies” — the Ostian way being Paul’s. Tertullian (c. 200) is the first to specify the sword: Paul “wins his crown in a death like John’s” (John the Baptist, beheaded), and, more explicitly, “that Paul is beheaded… Then does Paul obtain a birth suited to Roman citizenship… when in Rome he springs to life again ennobled by martyrdom.” Eusebius (c. 325): “Paul was beheaded in Rome itself… under Nero.” Jerome (c. 393): “in the fourteenth year of Nero… beheaded at Rome … buried in the Ostian way.” The witnesses span Rome, Antioch, Corinth, Carthage, and Caesarea within a century and a half; they converge; and nothing anywhere reports a different end.2

The evidence against (and the limits)

There is no counter-tradition to overturn — no source has Paul dying elsewhere, or otherwise, or surviving. The real questions are about precision, not the fact. The manner is one step softer than the fact. Clement, the earliest witness, says only “under the prefects,” not “beheaded”; the sword is first named a century later by Tertullian. It is highly credible — a citizen was beheaded, not crucified, and no source contradicts it — but it is an inference confirmed late rather than attested at the start. The exact date floats. Sources range from c. 64 (the immediate aftermath of the fire of Rome and Nero’s persecution, Tacitus) to Jerome’s 67/68; “under Nero” (who died June 68) is the firm bracket, the year within it uncertain. The embellishments are late. The Acts of Paul adds milk spurting from the severed neck; the medieval tradition of the Tre Fontane — the head bouncing thrice, each bounce raising a spring — is later still. These are hagiography and prove nothing about the core, which does not depend on them.3

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The martyrdom of Paul at Rome under Nero satisfies every test historians apply to an ancient claim: it is early (Clement, within thirty years), multiple (at least six independent witnesses across five regions), and unopposed (no rival account exists). Gaius’s physical “trophy” on the Ostian way, already shown c. 200, ties the claim to a specific venerated site. On the evidence, the fact is as certain as anything in first-century church history outside the New Testament — which is why surveys of the apostles’ fates place Paul’s death, with Peter’s and James son of Zebedee’s, at the highest grade of probability.

What to keep separate. The beheading and the exact year are slightly less secure than the bare fact of a Roman martyrdom under Nero — the manner attested a century on (though wholly consistent with his citizenship), the year varying among the sources. And the vivid legends — the milk, the three fountains — are devotional accretion, to be neither believed as history nor allowed to discredit the firm core they decorate. Rated on the fact, and on the well-grounded manner, the tradition holds.

Assessment

Sound. Paul’s execution at Rome under Nero is among the best-attested facts of apostolic history — early, multiple, independent, and unrefuted; the beheading by the sword is early enough and fits his Roman citizenship, and stands as the mainstream verdict even if a half-step below the fact in certainty. The late-legendary trimmings (the milk, the Tre Fontane springs) are set aside as embellishment. The core interpretation — martyred at Rome, under Nero, by beheading — is sound.
