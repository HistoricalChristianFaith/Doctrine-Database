Clement of Rome on Paul

Dates: c. 96 (Clement, bishop of Rome, First Epistle to the Corinthians) · 1 Clement · back to the Paul timeline
Summary: The earliest datable witness after the New Testament to Paul’s death — a Roman bishop, writing from the very city where it happened, perhaps thirty years after the event, to remind the Corinthians of the martyrs of “our own generation.” His two sentences on Paul are the seed of the whole later tradition: the endurance, the reach to “the extreme limit of the west,” and the martyrdom at Rome.

Claims & evidence

Peter and Paul, the “pillars” of the present generation

Clement is urging the Corinthians away from the jealousy that had split their church, and he reaches for recent examples of what envy costs. He turns to the two apostles the Roman church buried within living memory:

Let us take the noble examples furnished in our own generation. Through envy and jealousy, the greatest and most righteous pillars [of the Church] have been persecuted and put to death. Let us set before our eyes the illustrious apostles. Peter, through unrighteous envy, endured not one or two, but numerous labours; and when he had at length suffered martyrdom, departed to the place of glory due to him.

— Clement of Rome, First Epistle to the Corinthians 5. That Peter and Paul died as martyrs “in our own generation” is stated here by a Roman leader a single generation removed — the firmest anchor of the whole tradition.

Paul’s endurance, the “limit of the west,” and his death

Then the sentence on Paul, which every later source is in effect expanding:

Owing to envy, Paul also obtained the reward of patient endurance, after being seven times thrown into captivity, compelled to flee, and stoned. After preaching both in the east and west, he gained the illustrious reputation due to his faith, having taught righteousness to the whole world, and come to the extreme limit of the west, and suffered martyrdom under the prefects. Thus was he removed from the world, and went into the holy place, having proved himself a striking example of patience.

— First Epistle to the Corinthians 5. The catalogue of sufferings (“seven times… in captivity… stoned”) tracks Paul’s own list in 2 Corinthians 11, a sign Clement knew the Pauline letters. Two phrases carry the weight of the later argument: “the extreme limit of the west” (τερμα της δυσεως), read by many as Spain — the Pillars of Hercules being the western edge of the world for a writer at Rome — and “suffered martyrdom under the prefects,” placing his death at Rome under the imperial officials.

Notes

  • Why this witness matters most. Clement is early (c. 96), Roman (writing where Paul died), and independent of the apocryphal Acts that came later. His testimony that Paul was martyred is as close to the event as any we have — the reason surveys of the apostles’ fates rate Paul’s death at the very top of the scale. See the martyrdom argument.
  • “The extreme limit of the west.” From Rome, “the west” naturally means Spain, and this line is the oldest support for the Spanish-mission tradition. It is not decisive — the phrase can be read rhetorically — but it is why the question is a live one. Weighed on the Spain argument.
  • “Under the prefects.” Clement does not name Nero or specify beheading; he says only that Paul died at the hands of the authorities. The precise manner (the sword) and the emperor (Nero) are supplied by the later witnesses — Tertullian, Eusebius, Jerome — not here.
