Tertullian on Paul

Dates: c. 200 (Tertullian of Carthage, The Prescription Against Heretics) · Prescription Against Heretics · back to the Paul timeline
Summary: Tertullian is our earliest explicit witness to the manner of Paul’s death. Pointing readers to the churches the apostles founded, he singles out Rome — “happy” for the blood of Peter and Paul — and describes Paul’s end by a pointed comparison: a death “like John’s,” that is, by beheading, the sword that Roman law reserved for a citizen, over against Peter’s cross.

Claims & evidence

Rome, “where Paul wins his crown in a death like John’s”

Arguing that the apostolic churches are the touchstone of true doctrine, Tertullian tells his reader to visit them — Corinth, Philippi, Thessalonica, Ephesus — and above all Rome:

Since, moreover, you are close upon Italy, you have Rome, from which there comes even into our own hands the very authority (of apostles themselves). How happy is its church, on which apostles poured forth all their doctrine along with their blood! where Peter endures a passion like his Lord’s! where Paul wins his crown in a death like John’s! where the Apostle John was first plunged, unhurt, into boiling oil, and thence remitted to his island-exile.

— Tertullian, The Prescription Against Heretics 36. The three comparisons are each precise. Peter’s “passion like his Lord’s” is crucifixion; the Apostle John’s ordeal is the boiling oil and exile; and Paul’s “death like John’s” — here John the Baptist, the one scriptural figure famously beheaded — means execution by the sword. This is the first source to specify how Paul died, and it names the death that fits his citizenship.

“That Paul is beheaded” — the sword and the citizen

In the Scorpiace Tertullian is even more explicit, and ties the beheading directly to Paul’s Roman citizenship. Appealing to the public record of the Caesars against a heretic, he writes:

…that Paul is beheaded has been written in their own blood… We read the lives of the Caesars: At Rome Nero was the first who stained with blood the rising faith. Then is Peter girt by another, when he is made fast to the cross. Then does Paul obtain a birth suited to Roman citizenship, when in Rome he springs to life again ennobled by martyrdom.

— Tertullian, Scorpiace 15. Here the three points are named together: Paul is beheaded; Peter is fixed to the cross; and Paul’s beheading is “a birth suited to Roman citizenship” — the sword being the citizen’s execution. Tertullian names the emperor elsewhere as well: “Nero was the first who assailed with the imperial sword the Christian sect, making progress then especially at Rome” (Apology 5). With Tertullian, then, all the elements of the classic tradition are in place and joined to their author: Peter crucified, Paul beheaded, both at Rome, both under Nero.

Notes

  • “A death like John’s.” The comparison only works if John here is John the Baptist (beheaded), not John the apostle (whom the same sentence has just described surviving boiling oil and dying in exile). Read that way — the standard reading — Tertullian is the earliest source to state the beheading explicitly; Clement of Rome had said only “martyrdom under the prefects.”
  • Beheading fits the citizen. Roman law spared a citizen the cross; the sword was the citizen’s execution. That Paul — “free born” (Acts 22:28) — was beheaded while Peter was crucified is exactly what one would expect, which is part of why the tradition is credited. See the martyrdom argument.
  • Carthage, not Rome. Tertullian writes from North Africa, independent of the Roman local tradition — yet he takes Peter’s and Paul’s Roman deaths as common knowledge, a mark of how widely and early the tradition was held.
