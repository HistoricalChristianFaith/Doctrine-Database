The New Testament on Paul

Dates: c. 34–67 (Paul’s conversion to his death; the letters c. 48–67, Acts c. 62–90) · back to the Paul timeline
Summary: Paul is the best-documented figure in the New Testament after Jesus himself. Acts follows him from the stoning of Stephen to a Roman prison, and thirteen letters bear his name. What the New Testament does not supply is his death: Acts stops with him under house-arrest at Rome, still preaching — so the one great gap the later tradition fills is what happened after Acts 28.

Claims & evidence

Origin: a Pharisee of Tarsus, a Roman citizen

Paul gives his own pedigree more than once. He was “a Jew of Tarsus, a city in Cilicia,” brought up in Jerusalem “at the feet of Gamaliel,” taught “according to the perfect manner of the law of the fathers”; of “the stock of Israel, of the tribe of Benjamin, an Hebrew of the Hebrews; as touching the law, a Pharisee.” He was a Roman citizen — and, unusually, by birth:

And the chief captain answered, With a great sum obtained I this freedom. And Paul said, But I was free born.

— Acts 22:28. He worked a trade, tent-making, alongside Aquila and Priscilla (Acts 18:3). The double identity — strict Pharisee and Roman citizen of a Greek city — shapes the whole career: it is his citizenship that lets him appeal to Caesar, and (in the later tradition) that dictates the manner of his death, the sword rather than the cross.

The persecutor

Paul enters the narrative not as a convert but as an enemy. He is first named as the young man at whose feet the witnesses laid their coats while they stoned Stephen — “And Saul was consenting unto his death” (Acts 8:1) — and then as the church’s chief hunter:

As for Saul, he made havock of the church, entering into every house, and haling men and women committed them to prison.

— Acts 8:3. He himself never softened it: “I persecuted this way unto the death” (Acts 22:4); “beyond measure I persecuted the church of God, and wasted it” (Galatians 1:13).

The Damascus road

On the way to Damascus with letters from the high priest to arrest believers there, Paul was struck down by a light from heaven and a voice — “Saul, Saul, why persecutest thou me?” — blinded, and sent on to be baptized by Ananias, who was told:

Go thy way: for he is a chosen vessel unto me, to bear my name before the Gentiles, and kings, and the children of Israel.

— Acts 9:15. Acts tells the conversion three times (chs. 9, 22, 26); Paul tells it himself as the moment God “called me by his grace… to reveal his Son in me, that I might preach him among the heathen” (Galatians 1:15–16).

The missionary journeys and the Jerusalem council

From Antioch, set apart with Barnabas by the Holy Spirit (Acts 13:2), Paul made three long journeys across Cyprus, Asia Minor, and Greece, planting churches at Galatia, Philippi, Thessalonica, Corinth, and Ephesus. The question whether his Gentile converts must be circumcised and keep the law of Moses was settled at the Jerusalem council (Acts 15; Paul’s own account, Galatians 2:1–10) in their favour. He describes the sheer scope of the labour without false modesty: “I laboured more abundantly than they all: yet not I, but the grace of God which was with me” (1 Corinthians 15:10).

Arrest, appeal to Caesar, and Rome

A riot at the Temple over a rumoured defilement led to Paul’s arrest (Acts 21:27–36). Held two years at Caesarea, he used his citizen’s right to appeal past the provincial governor to the emperor — “I appeal unto Caesar” (Acts 25:11) — and was shipped to Rome, surviving a storm and shipwreck off Malta. Acts ends not with a trial or a verdict but with the apostle at liberty in his own hired house:

Preaching the kingdom of God, and teaching those things which concern the Lord Jesus Christ, with all confidence, no man forbidding him.

— Acts 28:30–31. That open ending is the seam the tradition works: everything about Paul’s release, his hoped-for journey west, and his death lies beyond it.

Not one of the Twelve — and the plan for Spain

Paul insists he is a true apostle, but by a distinct and later call: the risen Christ appeared to him “last of all… as of one born out of due time” (1 Corinthians 15:8), and his commission is specifically to the nations — “I am the apostle of the Gentiles” (Romans 11:13). Writing to Rome before his arrest, he lays out a plan Acts never shows completed:

Whensoever I take my journey into Spain, I will come to you… When therefore I have performed this, and have sealed to them this fruit, I will come by you into Spain.

— Romans 15:24, 28. Whether he ever carried it out is the substance of the Spain argument.

Notes

  • The one deliberate gap. Unlike the other apostles, whose obscurity is near-total, Paul is richly documented right up to Rome — and then Acts simply stops. The later sources are not inventing a life from nothing but answering one specific question the New Testament leaves open: what became of him after the two years of Acts 28.
  • Paul’s own voice. Much of what we know comes not from Acts but from Paul’s letters, where he reports his origin, conversion, journeys, and sufferings first-hand (2 Corinthians 11 catalogues the beatings, stonings, and shipwrecks that Clement of Rome would later echo). This makes his life uniquely checkable against primary self-testimony.
  • Citizenship and the sword. The detail that Paul was a Roman citizen “free born” is not incidental: Roman law spared a citizen crucifixion, so the tradition that Paul was beheaded — while Peter, no citizen, was crucified — fits the legal reality the New Testament itself records.
