The Acts of Paul and Acts of Paul and Thecla on Paul

Dates: c. 160 (the Acts of Paul, a mid-2nd-century apocryphon; its Thecla episode circulated separately) · Acts of Paul and Thecla (ANF vol. 8) · Acts of Paul · back to the Paul timeline
Summary: A pious second-century romance built around Paul, orthodox in aim but legendary in substance — and the source of two things that outlived it: the earliest description of what Paul looked like, and (in its closing Martyrdom) a miracle-laden retelling of the beheading under Nero that the sober sources already carried. Tertullian records that its author was deposed for writing it. It is devotion and legend, not evidence for the life — but its portrait shaped how the West pictured Paul for a millennium.

Claims & evidence

The earliest portrait of Paul

As Onesiphorus waits on the road from Iconium to meet a Paul he has never seen, the text gives the description that art and imagination would follow ever after:

And he saw Paul coming, a man small in size, bald-headed, bandy-legged, well-built, with eyebrows meeting, rather long-nosed, full of grace. For sometimes he seemed like a man, and sometimes he had the countenance of an angel.

— Acts of Paul and Thecla 3 (tr. Alexander Walker). Whether or not it preserves any real memory — a bald, short, heavy-browed man — it is the oldest surviving picture of Paul, and it is why he is drawn small, balding, and long-bearded in Christian art from the catacombs onward.

The Thecla romance

The tale itself follows Thecla, a young noblewoman of Iconium who hears Paul preach virginity, breaks her engagement to follow him, and is condemned to the fire and to the beasts for it — surviving both by miracle (a cloud quenches the flames; a lioness defends her in the arena), then baptizing herself in a tank of water and going out to teach. It is a story told to commend continence and the apostolic life, not a historical report; its Paul is a preacher of a rigorous asceticism, and its interest is in Thecla, who became a widely venerated saint in her own right.

The Martyrdom: beheading under Nero, dressed in miracle

The Acts of Paul closes with a Martyrdom that carries the same fact the older witnesses report — Paul beheaded at Rome under Nero — but wraps it in wonders: Paul promises to appear again after death; when he is beheaded, milk rather than blood spurts from the severed neck onto the soldiers; and he returns to Nero in a vision. The core (death by the sword, at Rome, under Nero) is the tradition the reliable sources establish; the milk and the apparition are hagiographic embellishment. The martyrdom-fact is not from this romance — it is older and better attested — but the romance shows how quickly the bare fact acquired legendary decoration.

A book its own church rejected

The Acts of Paul did not pass as authentic even in antiquity. Tertullian — who otherwise admired Paul — reports its exposure:

…in Asia, the presbyter who composed that writing, as if he were augmenting Paul’s fame from his own store, after being convicted, and confessing that he had done it from love of Paul, was removed from his office.

— Tertullian, On Baptism 17, objecting to those who cited Thecla’s self-baptism as warrant for women baptizing. So the work was known to be a pious fabrication within a generation or two of its writing.

Notes

  • Low biographical value, high cultural value. Like the other apocryphal Acts, this one tells us next to nothing reliable about Paul’s life — but it is priceless as the earliest physical portrait and as evidence of how second-century Christians venerated him. It is treated here as devotion and legend, never laundered into fact (see the apostles hub).
  • The same Thecla appears elsewhere on this wiki. The Acts of Paul and Thecla is also a witness on the purgatory timeline, for its prayer that the dead Falconilla be “transferred to the place of the just” — an early instance of prayer for the dead.
  • Deposed, not condemned as heresy. Tertullian’s presbyter was disciplined for forgery, not for teaching error — he wrote “from love of Paul.” The episode is a clean early example of the church distinguishing genuine apostolic memory from well-meaning invention.
