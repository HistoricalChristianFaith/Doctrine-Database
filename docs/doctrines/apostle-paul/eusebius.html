Eusebius on Paul

Dates: c. 325 (Eusebius of Caesarea, Ecclesiastical History) · Church History · back to the Paul timeline
Summary: Eusebius gathers the earlier reports and adds the physical monuments. He follows Acts to Paul’s first Roman custody, then records the tradition of a release, a second imprisonment, and martyrdom under Nero; for the death he cites the Roman presbyter Gaius, who could point to the apostles’ “trophies” on the Vatican and the Ostian way, and Dionysius of Corinth, who joins Peter and Paul in a single martyrdom.

Claims & evidence

The first Roman custody, the release, and the second imprisonment

Eusebius first tracks the story of Acts — Paul “sent bound to Rome” under Festus, two years in open custody — and then supplies what Acts does not: an acquittal, a return to preaching, and a second, fatal arrest:

Thus after he had made his defense it is said that the apostle was sent again upon the ministry of preaching, and that upon coming to the same city a second time he suffered martyrdom. In this imprisonment he wrote his second epistle to Timothy, in which he mentions his first defense and his impending death.

— Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History 2.22.2–3. This is the classic form of the release-and-second-imprisonment scheme, and Eusebius grounds it in 2 Timothy’s language of a “first defense” already past and a death near at hand — the framework behind the traditional dating of the Pastoral epistles and the Spanish mission (see the Spain argument).

“Paul was beheaded in Rome itself” — and the trophies

For the death, Eusebius states the manner plainly and then anchors it in physical evidence still visible in his day:

It is, therefore, recorded that Paul was beheaded in Rome itself, and that Peter likewise was crucified under Nero. This account of Peter and Paul is substantiated by the fact that their names are preserved in the cemeteries of that place even to the present day.

He then quotes Gaius, a Roman churchman of c. 200, who names the graves:

But I can show the trophies of the apostles. For if you will go to the Vatican or to the Ostian way, you will find the trophies of those who laid the foundations of this church.

— Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History 2.25.5–7. The “trophies” (tropaia) are the memorial shrines — the Vatican for Peter, the Ostian way for Paul, precisely where the two great basilicas would later rise. Eusebius adds the testimony of Dionysius, bishop of Corinth (c. 170), that Peter and Paul “suffered martyrdom at the same time” (2.25.8).

The mission, “from Jerusalem to Illyricum”

Distributing the mission-fields of the apostles, Eusebius quotes Origen on Paul’s range and end:

What do we need to say concerning Paul, who preached the Gospel of Christ from Jerusalem to Illyricum, and afterwards suffered martyrdom in Rome under Nero?

— Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History 3.1.3 (citing Origen’s Commentary on Genesis). “From Jerusalem to Illyricum” echoes Paul’s own words in Romans 15:19; the death “under Nero” is by now the settled tradition, reported from an independent third-century source.

Notes

  • The trophies fix the sites. Gaius’s tropaia on the Vatican and the Ostian way — already shown to visitors around A.D. 200 — are the earliest testimony to the two apostolic tombs, and they sit exactly where St Peter’s and San Paolo fuori le Mura now stand. This is the strongest strand in the case that the Roman memory is genuine, not invented.
  • Two imprisonments, one death. Eusebius reports the release as tradition (“it is said”), not as documented fact; it is nonetheless early and coherent, and it is the scaffolding on which the Pastoral epistles and the Spanish journey hang. The martyrdom itself he treats as certain.
  • Convergence of independent witnesses. By assembling Gaius (Rome), Dionysius (Corinth), and Origen (Alexandria/Caesarea) — three regions, none dependent on the others — Eusebius shows that the Roman martyrdom of Paul under Nero was the common property of the whole early church, not a local Roman claim.
