The Intermediate State (the soul between death and resurrection)

What becomes of a person between death and the general resurrection? Scripture speaks of the dead in Sheol / Hades, of "Abraham's bosom" and a place of "torments" divided by a "great gulf" (Luke 16), of the penitent thief "with me in paradise" that very day (Luke 23:43), of the believer "absent from the body … present with the Lord" (2 Cor 5:8), and of the sinning angels cast into Tartarus (2 Pet 2:4). Out of these the Church built a doctrine of the intermediate state. Two questions divide its interpreters:

  1. Is the soul conscious in the interval? Does it live, perceive, rest, or suffer at once — or does it "sleep" (and even, for some, die) until the resurrection?
  2. Is the place structured? Is Hades a single realm with compartments (Abraham's bosom / a place of torment / the angelic prison of Tartarus), or are there simply two final destinies, heaven and hell, entered immediately?

The earliest and long-dominant reading is a conscious, structured Hades: every soul descends to a subterranean holding-place where the righteous rest (in "Abraham's bosom") and the wicked suffer, all awaiting the resurrection, while Christ's own descent to the dead frees the captive patriarchs. The medieval West then elaborated the map — adding purgatory (traced on its own purgatory and prayer-for-the-dead timeline) and the limbus patrum, and (after the descent) sending the fully-cleansed straight to the vision of God. The Reformation collapsed the structure: keeping the soul's conscious survival but denying any third place — "besides these two places … the Scripture acknowledgeth none." A structured, two-compartment Hades has lately been revived by popular teachers such as Doug Wilson.

A note on the shape of this debate. Conscious survival is the tradition's overwhelming majority position — the real divide is over the structure of the intermediate place, and over when the righteous enter glory. But it is not quite unanimous: an early Syriac current (Aphrahat) teaches a genuine sleep of the dead until the resurrection, the minority soul-sleep reading the Reformation's left wing revived and Calvin's Psychopannychia rebutted. The early Fathers (and the Second Temple background) picture a structured Hades that detains even the righteous until Christ; the high-medieval West keeps compartments but lets the cleansed see God at once; the Reformed confessions deny compartments altogether. Whether a two-compartment Hades (Tartarus vs. Paradise) is taught by the texts, and whether the "sleep" of death means real unconsciousness, are weighed adversarially in the Arguments & cruxes section below (contested; weak).

Timeline

1 Enoch (c. 300 BC)

1 Enoch gives the earliest detailed map of the structured intermediate state: the spirits of the dead are gathered into "hollow places" in a great western mountain and separated into divisions — the righteous by "the bright spring of water," the sinners apart — "to receive them till the day of their judgement."1 It is the literary ancestor of the Abraham's-bosom / place-of-torment geography.

2 Maccabees (c. 124 BC)

2 Maccabees gives the earliest explicit witness that the living can aid the dead: Judas Maccabeus, finding idolatrous tokens on his fallen soldiers, sends a sin offering to Jerusalem for them and "made a reconciliation for the dead, that they might be delivered from sin" — reasoning that, but for the resurrection, it would be "superfluous and vain to pray for the dead." It is the seed of the prayers-for-the-dead / purgatory line.2

The Wisdom of Solomon (c. 50 BC)

The Wisdom of Solomon, an Alexandrian Jewish wisdom book roughly contemporary with Philo, gives the Second Temple background its clearest statement of conscious post-mortem rest for the righteous: "the souls of the righteous are in the hand of God, and there shall no torment touch them"; though "in the sight of the unwise they seemed to die … they are in peace," "their hope full of immortality."3

The New Testament (c. AD 60–90)

The New Testament supplies the data the later doctrine is built on. Jesus' account of the rich man and Lazarus sets "Abraham's bosom" against a place of "torments," with "a great gulf fixed" so that none may pass between;4 he promises the thief, "To day shalt thou be with me in paradise" (Luke 23:43); and Paul is "willing rather to be absent from the body, and to be present with the Lord" (2 Cor 5:8), "having a desire to depart, and to be with Christ" (Phil 1:23) — a conscious post-mortem state, two destinies, and the believer already "with Christ."5

Ignatius of Antioch (c. 107)

Ignatius, writing on his way to martyrdom, gives one of the earliest hints of the descent: the prophets who as Christ's disciples "did wait for Him as their Teacher" were, at his coming, "raised … from the dead" — implying that the righteous dead were held below until Christ came to them.6

Polycarp of Smyrna (c. 125)

Polycarp, a disciple of John, confesses that God raised Christ "having loosed the bands of the grave" — quoting Acts 2:24, whose "Western" text reads "loosed the pains of Hades," a descent-assuming echo of Christ's passage through and release from the realm of the dead.7

The Odes of Solomon (c. 125, date disputed)

The Odes of Solomon, an anonymous early Christian hymnbook, give in Ode 42 a vivid first-person descent: "Sheol saw me and was shattered, and Death ejected me and many with me"; the dead cry out to be brought "out from the bonds of darkness," and the descending Christ makes "a congregation of living among his dead" and leads them out — an early poetic witness to a holding-place of the dead broken open at the harrowing.8

The Shepherd of Hermas (c. 150)

The Shepherd of Hermas pictures the righteous who died before Christ awaiting the gospel in the realm below: the apostles and teachers, "after falling asleep," "preached … to those who were asleep" and gave them "the seal," so that those who "had previously fallen asleep descended dead, but rose up again alive" — an early witness to the evangelization of the dead (cf. 1 Pet 3:19; 4:6).9

Justin Martyr (c. 165)

Justin holds that "the souls of the pious remain in a better place, while those of the unjust and wicked are in a worse, waiting for the time of judgment" — and brands those who say the souls of the dead "are taken to heaven" at once, denying the resurrection, as people one should "not imagine … are Christians."10

Melito of Sardis (c. 170)

Melito of Sardis, bishop and paschal preacher, puts the harrowing of Hades on the lips of the risen Christ in his On Pascha — the oldest surviving Easter homily: "I am the one who destroyed death, and triumphed over the enemy, and trampled Hades under foot, and bound the strong one, and carried off man to the heights of heaven." An early liturgical proclamation of the descent as victory over the realm of the dead.11

Clement of Alexandria (c. 200)

Clement assumes a conscious, divided Hades — "who in his senses can suppose the souls of the righteous and those of sinners in the same condemnation?" — and makes Christ's descent an act of preaching: "the Lord preached the Gospel to those in Hades," and the apostles after him, so that even the righteous who died before the Incarnation might hear, repent, and be saved.12

Irenaeus (c. 202)

Irenaeus teaches that the souls of the departed "go away into the invisible place allotted to them by God, and there remain until the resurrection" — for Christ himself "dwelt in the place where the dead were" three days, so the disciples ought not to suppose they will fly straight above the heavens.13

Tertullian (c. 220)

Tertullian, in A Treatise on the Soul, makes Hades "a vast deep space in the interior of the earth" that holds every soul until the resurrection — the faithful refreshed in Abraham's bosom, the wicked in torment, all conscious in the interval — with the martyrs the sole exception: "The sole key to unlock Paradise is your own life's blood."14

Hippolytus of Rome (c. 235)

Hippolytus, in Against Plato, on the Cause of the Universe, gives the fullest patristic geography: one Hades detaining all souls until judgment, but divided — the righteous "conducted in the light toward the right … to a locality full of light," which "we call by the name Abraham's bosom," the unrighteous "dragged toward the left," with "a deep and vast abyss" set between that none can cross.15

Origen of Alexandria (c. 240)

Origen makes the descent structural: with the Incarnation it is one of the Word's two "shoes," and by it Christ "release[d] those who were held there" in the underworld and "led captivity captive" (Eph 4:8), reopening to the penitent thief — and "all the saints for whom he had descended" — the paradise Adam had closed.16

An Ancient Homily on Holy Saturday (c. 300, date disputed)

An anonymous ancient homily for Holy Saturday — transmitted under the name of Epiphanius of Salamis but spurious and of uncertain date — gives the descent its most celebrated dramatic voice: "there is a great silence on earth today … because the King is asleep … God has died in the flesh and hell trembles with fear." The descended Christ seeks Adam "as for a lost sheep," takes him by the hand — "Awake, O sleeper, and rise from the dead" — and leads the imprisoned dead out of Hades to enthronement in heaven.17

Eusebius of Caesarea (c. 324)

Eusebius, in the Church History, records the harrowing as a fixed article of gospel preaching: Christ "descended into Hades, and burst the bars which from eternity had not been broken, and raised the dead; for he descended alone, but rose with many, and thus ascended to his Father."18

Aphrahat the Persian Sage (c. 345)

Aphrahat, the earliest Syriac Father, gives the timeline its one positive witness to soul-sleep: "when men fall asleep, they sleep this slumber without knowing good from evil … until the Judge come," so that "as yet no one has received his reward" — neither the righteous their kingdom nor the wicked their torment. He qualifies it in the Syriac manner: the heavenly "spirit which the righteous receive … goes to our Lord until the time of the Resurrection," while the buried man sleeps.19

Cyril of Jerusalem (c. 350)

Cyril of Jerusalem, catechizing at the Holy Sepulchre, teaches that Christ "went down into the regions beneath the earth, that thence also He might redeem the righteous" — David, Samuel, the prophets, and John the Baptist, "imprisoned" since Adam — and "sent forth His prisoners out of the pit wherein is no water" (Zech 9:11), the classic Eastern statement of the descent emptying the holding-place of the OT saints.20

Athanasius of Alexandria (c. 371)

Athanasius, defending the integrity of Christ's body, takes the descent for granted: the body "was laid in a grave, when the Word had left it, yet was not parted from it, to preach, as Peter says, also to the spirits in prison" (1 Pet 3:19) — a witness later adduced by Aquinas. (His De Synodis 8 "gate-keepers of hell" line, by contrast, is Athanasius quoting the Arian-leaning Sirmium creed to refute it, not his own.)21

Ambrose of Milan (c. 380)

The early Latin Easter hymn Aurora Lucis Rutilat, traditionally ascribed to Ambrose, sets the harrowing in the Church's worship: "Hell is writhing in despair," and the risen "Earth's great king … leads His saints to life and light." (The attribution to Ambrose is traditional, not certain.)22

Gregory of Nyssa (c. 395)

Gregory of Nyssa, in the dialogue with Macrina On the Soul and the Resurrection, reads "the bosom of Abraham … as a symbol of the good state of the soul" — a condition of rest rather than a locality — and argues that the simple, uncompounded soul consciously survives the body's dissolution, keeping watch over its own elements until the resurrection.23

John Chrysostom (c. 407)

Chrysostom teaches that the soul is conscious the instant it leaves the body: the righteous are carried off by angels "as a crowned victor" to Abraham's bosom (so Lazarus), the gulf between the states "fixed, because it cannot be loosened, moved, or shaken," and the faithful "depart … to be with Christ."24

Augustine of Hippo (c. 430)

Augustine holds that "during the time … which intervenes between a man's death and the final resurrection, the soul dwells in a hidden retreat, where it enjoys rest or suffers affliction" according to its earthly desert,25 and that the dead "are benefited by the piety of their living friends" through prayer, alms, and the Eucharistic sacrifice — the first systematic warrant for helping the dead, and (held tentatively) for a purgatorial fire; the fallen angels meanwhile are kept in Tartarus for judgment.26

Cyril of Alexandria (c. 444)

Cyril of Alexandria, in his Commentary on Luke, counts the harrowing among the works that prove Christ to be God: he "utterly abolished death … and spoiled hell, and overthrew the tyranny of the enemy … and opened the gates above to the dwellers upon earth, and united earth to heaven" — the despoiling of Hades below paired with the reopening of heaven above.27

Leo the Great (c. 447)

Pope Leo I, refuting the Priscillianists, treats the descent as plain catholic doctrine: "when Christ descended to the realms below, his flesh rested in the tomb" — the soul's descent and the body's true burial and resurrection held together against docetic denial, grounded in "thou wilt not leave my soul in Hades" (Ps 16:10).28

The Gospel of Nicodemus (c. 450, date disputed)

The Gospel of Nicodemus — its second part, the Descent into Hell — gives the harrowing its earliest sustained narrative: the patriarchs and prophets waiting in "the shadow of death," John the Baptist heralding "the dayspring … coming from on high unto us that sit in darkness," and the descending Christ shattering the gates of brass — "Remove, O princes, your gates … and the King of glory shall come in" (Ps 24) — to take "the right hand of Adam" and lead "all the saints" up out of Hades into paradise. An apocryphon, not a Father — cited as a vivid early witness to the belief, not as history; through it the Harrowing of Hell entered the medieval liturgy, drama, and the Anastasis icon.29

Gregory the Dialogist (c. 604)

Gregory the Great reads Abraham's bosom as "the secret rest of the fathers" before the last judgment, holds that each soul is judged at death and enters rest or punishment at once, and gives the West its classic statement of a cleansing fire: "there must be a … fire before judgment, because of some minor faults that may remain to be purged away."30

Bede (c. 735)

Bede transmits the structured intermediate state to the medieval West: Abraham's bosom is "the rest of the blessed … to which they are received after this life," and his Vision of Drythelm (Ecclesiastical History V.12) maps distinct intermediate regions — a flowery resting-place "into which the souls of those are received who depart the body in good works, but … are not so perfect as to deserve to be immediately admitted into the kingdom of heaven."31

John of Damascus (c. 749)

John of Damascus gives the Eastern systematic basis for a conscious intermediate state — the soul is "a living essence, simple, incorporeal … immortal, reasoning and intelligent" — and teaches that Christ's deified soul "descended into Hades" and "freed those who had been bound for ages."32

Thomas Aquinas (c. 1274)

Aquinas systematizes the abodes of souls: heaven, hell, purgatory (for "those who die in grace with venial sins"), the limbus patrum — the OT "Abraham's bosom," emptied when "Christ descended into hell [and] delivered the holy Fathers from thence" — and the limbo of children. Since the descent the fully-cleansed pass straight to the vision of God, others to purgatory.33

Benedict XII (c. 1336)

Benedict XII, in the constitution Benedictus Deus, dogmatically resolved the controversy John XXII had stirred over when the blessed see God: the fully-purified souls, immediately after death and any needed purgation, "already before they take up their bodies again and before the general judgment," see "the divine essence with an intuitive vision and even face to face." The cleansed enter glory at once — the mature Latin answer Aquinas had anticipated.34

Martin Luther (c. 1533)

Luther, in his Easter sermon at the castle of Torgau (1533), gives the descent a third Reformation reading — neither the Catholic emptying of the limbus patrum nor Calvin's torments-on-the-cross, but a real, victorious descent: "hell has been torn apart for us through Christ, and … the devil's kingdom and power have been destroyed, which is why Christ died, was buried, and descended." He refuses to speculate on the manner — "let him only stick to the words … in a simple way."35

John Calvin (c. 1542)

Calvin, in Psychopannychia, defends conscious survival against the "soul-sleep" of some Anabaptists — "the soul, after the death of the body, still survives, endued with sense and intellect" — but rejects the scholastic apparatus: the limbus patrum, "though this fable has the countenance of great authors … is nothing but a fable," and purgatory likewise.36

The Formula of Concord (1577)

The Formula of Concord, the last Lutheran confession, makes Luther's Torgau reading binding (Solid Declaration, Art. IX): "we simply believe that the entire person, God and man, after the burial descended into hell, conquered the devil, destroyed the power of hell, and took from the devil all his might" — yet "how this occurred we should … reserve until the other world." A confessionalized victorious descent, refusing both Calvin's reading and Rome's emptied limbus.37

The Westminster Confession (1646)

The Westminster Confession (ch. XXXII) gives the mature Reformed position: the souls of men "neither die nor sleep" but "immediately return to God"; the righteous "are received into the highest heavens" and the wicked "cast into hell" — and, pointedly, "besides these two places for souls separated from their bodies, the Scripture acknowledgeth none."38 No Abraham's bosom as a third place, no limbo, no purgatory.

The Catechism of the Catholic Church (1992)

The Catechism of the Catholic Church gives the modern Catholic magisterial restatement of the descent (§§632–635): Christ "descended there as Saviour, proclaiming the Good News to the spirits imprisoned there" (1 Pet 3:19) and delivered "these holy souls, who awaited their Saviour in Abraham's bosom" — yet "did not descend into hell to deliver the damned … but to free the just who had gone before him." It consolidates Benedict XII and the patristic consensus into the Church's current formula, and draws the limit the harrowing does not cross: the freeing of the just, no universal deliverance.39

N. T. Wright (c. 2008)

N. T. Wright, in Surprised by Hope, keeps a conscious intermediate state — "paradise," a "blissful garden where God's people rest prior to the resurrection," a state of "restful happiness" consciously with Christ — but subordinates it to the real hope: bodily resurrection, "life after life after death," against the popular reduction of the gospel to "going to heaven when you die."40

Doug Wilson (c. 2021)

Doug Wilson, a Reformed/CREC pastor, revives the structured two-compartment Hades against his own confession's flatter map: "There was a chasm between Tartarus on the one side, and Paradise on the other … The lowest pit of Hades … was called Tartarus, and that is where the angels were locked up." He reads Christ's descent (1 Pet 3:18–20) as heralding victory — not the gospel — to those imprisoned angels.41

Arguments & cruxes

Sub-claims marshaled for (or against) a reading above, which cut across the timeline rather than sitting at one date — each weighed adversarially on its own page, with an assessment of the interpretation it serves. See project.md §4 for the page type.

Related cruxes — the descent

Seven cruxes on Christ's descent to the realm of the dead — what it was and what it accomplished: the bare descensus and the freeing of the OT righteous, the firstfruits raising of the saints at the cross, the relocation of Paradise, the content of the proclamation, the pedigree of the creed clause that summarizes it, the authenticity of the lost prophecy adduced as its scriptural proof, and the lone conceit that John the Baptist went as Christ's forerunner into Hades. They interlock and are best read together.

Did the descended Christ free the OT righteous and "lead captivity captive"? (assessment: plausible)

The descent to the dead — whether the crucified Christ went down to the realm of the human dead and freed the OT righteous from a holding-place ("Abraham's bosom" / the limbus patrum), "leading captivity captive" (Eph 4:8) and emptying it, so the righteous now go straight to be "with the Lord." The crux is distinct from the 1 Peter 3:19 crux, which concerns the imprisoned angels. Two questions divide: the bare descensus (Christ's soul went to the dead) is sound — creedal, affirmed East and West, grounded in Acts 2:27/31; but the specific liberation-of-a-holding-place mechanism, though the pre-Reformation mainstream (Irenaeus, Clement, Origen, Cyril of Jerusalem, Jerome, John of Damascus, Aquinas's emptied limbus), leans on the genuinely ambiguous Eph 4:9 ("the lower parts of the earth" — Hades, or the incarnation? — Aquinas allows both), is partly inferred from resurrection-texts, and is denied by the Reformed who reframe the descent and recognize no third place (Calvin; Westminster). Sound on the descent, probable-but-unsettled on the emptied limbus.42

Were the saints raised at the cross OT righteous freed from Sheol, a firstfruits with the risen Christ? (assessment: contested)

The raising of the saints — whether the "many bodies of the saints which slept" who "arose, and came out of the graves after his resurrection" (Matt 27:52–53) are Old-Testament righteous released from Sheol by the descent, a firstfruits raised with Christ, the gospel's own narrative evidence for the descent to the dead. The meaning is the patristic mainstream: a firstfruits and sign of the general resurrection (Chrysostom, Gregory the Great, Aquinas) and a witness to the harrowing's freeing of the dead (Ignatius, Hilary, Jerome, Theophylact). But the verse times the saints' emergence to Christ's own resurrection and makes him "the firstborn of the resurrection from the dead" (Jerome), so the Holy-Saturday release of OT saints from Sheol is an inference the text does not state, and Matthew's bare "saints which slept" is over-specified as the OT patriarchs. And the episode's historicity is genuinely disputed: uniquely Matthean, absent from the other gospels, and steeped in OT apocalyptic resurrection imagery (Ezek 37; Dan 12:2), it is read as a theological/apocalyptic sign by interpreters ancient (Origen) and modern (Brown, Licona) and as literal history by the patristic mainstream and conservative defenders. Well-grounded in meaning, unsettled in mechanism and historicity.43

"He descended into hell": is the creed clause original, and should it be retained? (assessment: contested)

The descent clause — not the doctrine of the descent (weighed just above) but the words "he descended into hell" in the Apostles' Creed: where they came from and whether they belong. The textual history is firm and favors the critics: the clause is absent from the oldest form of the creed (the Old Roman Symbol, Marcellus c. 340), first surfaces in a creed at the Arian-leaning "Dated Creed" of Sirmium (359), is reported by Rufinus (c. 404) as missing from the Roman and Eastern creeds and meaning only "buried," and enters the received Western text only in the eighth century (Pirminius). So the words are a late, non-original addition — Wayne Grudem (JETS 1991) presses exactly this to argue the clause be dropped, and John Piper reaches the same "omit" verdict on exegetical grounds. But whether to retain it is genuinely divided: David Scaer (JETS 1992) and the evangelical retrieval grant the history yet keep the article, because the descent doctrine it summarizes is older than the clause and itself sound (the descent-to-the-dead crux), and the Arian provenance of the first attestation does not taint a non-Arian line. Strong by philology's lights for "late addition," contested on retention — most of the tradition converging on keeping it as "he descended to the dead."44

Did Paradise relocate from Hades to heaven after the Ascension? (assessment: contested)

The relocation of Paradise — whether the abode of the righteous dead was transferred from a compartment of Hades up to the third heaven at the harrowing/ascension (so believers now go up, "present with the Lord," not down to Abraham's bosom). Distinct from the descent-to-the-dead crux (the freeing of the OT righteous): this is the relocation thesis. The conclusion — the believing dead now consciously with Christ above — is broadly shared (2 Cor 5:8; Phil 1:23; the Latin emptying of the limbus patrum into the beatific vision, Aquinas and Benedict XII; the Reformed "highest heavens," Westminster). But the specific relocation of a place is denied by the earliest Fathers, who — writing after the ascension — keep the ordinary faithful in an intermediate Hades and name this very thesis to reject it (Tertullian De Anima 55: "to no one is heaven opened … the patriarchs and prophets have removed from Hades, you tell me" — no, only the martyrs are in that Paradise; Irenaeus AH V.31; Hippolytus), and its pillar (Luke 23:43 paradise = 2 Cor 12:4 "third heaven") is undercut by John 20:17 / Acts 2:27, which put Christ's Good-Friday soul in Hades, not heaven. Strong by Latin-scholastic and much-of-the-NT lights, weak by early-patristic and Eastern ones.45

What did Christ proclaim to the spirits in prison — victory, or salvation? (assessment: contested)

The content of the proclamation — not who the "spirits in prison" are (the 1 Peter 3:19 identity crux) nor whether the descent freed the OT righteous (the descent-to-the-dead crux), but what Christ announced when he "went and preached" (1 Pet 3:19): a herald of condemnation and victory over the imprisoned spirits, or an offer of salvation — the postmortem "second chance" the claim denies (so Wilson: "not the word for preaching the gospel … The Lord was declaring that He was now the final victor"). The content tracks the identity: condemnation if the spirits are the Watchers (the modern-critical "proclamation of triumph" — Dalton, Bauckham, Schreiner), salvation if the human dead. Narrowly the reading is secure — no Father evangelizes the fallen angels, and 1 Enoch keeps them bound till judgment; Gregory the Great and Augustine refuse a universal second chance. But across the descent tradition it collides with the patristic mainstream, which made the proclamation a salvific preaching of the gospel to the human dead (Clement — "the Lord preached the Gospel to those in Hades," even floating their repentance; Irenaeus; Cyril of Alexandria; Hippolytus; Bede on 1 Pet 4:6's euangelizō), and the lexical prop that kēryssō excludes the gospel is imprecise (Mark 16:15). The answer divides by identity and by era.46

The "excised" descent-prophecy: authentic Scripture cut by the Jews, or an apocryphal logion? (assessment: weak)

The excised descent-prophecy — whether a one-line prophecy of the descent that Justin and Irenaeus quote as Scripture ("The Lord God remembered His dead people of Israel who lay in the graves; and He descended to preach to them His own salvation") is a genuine canonical passage the Jews deliberately excised from Jeremiah (so Justin, Dial. 72) — or Isaiah (so Irenaeus, AH III.20.4) — or an apocryphal logion the Fathers trusted by mistake. It would be the Old Testament's plainest descent oracle were it authentic; but it survives in no Hebrew or Greek text of either prophet, Irenaeus fathers it on Isaiah, Jeremiah, and no one across his five citations (the signature of a free-floating testimonium), and Justin's "the Jews cut it out" is a formula he also applies to Jeremiah 11:19, which never left the canon. An early and sincere witness to how scriptural the descent already felt, but not the excised prophecy it was taken for — and the descent doctrine it served never rested on it.47

Did John the Baptist go as Christ's forerunner into Hades? (assessment: weak)

John the Baptist as forerunner in Hades — whether the Baptist, beheaded by Herod before the crucifixion, went down to the realm of the dead ahead of Christ and there preached as his forerunner, announcing to the souls in Hades that the Saviour was about to descend and ransom them. Hippolytus alone asserts it (On Christ and Antichrist §45): "He also first preached to those in Hades, becoming a forerunner there … that the Saviour would descend to ransom the souls of the saints." The pieces are individually sound — John's earthly forerunner office is firm Scripture (Mal 3:1; Luke 1:76; John 1:23), his death before Christ is history (Matt 14:10), and the descent he heralds is broadly held — but their combination into a postmortem heralding in Hades is a single Father's conceit, unrepeated in the tradition, with no scriptural warrant (Scripture says nothing of John after death), and resting on the already-contested peopled Hades and postmortem-preaching readings. A coherent homiletical flourish on the descent, dispensable even by the descent's own logic.48

Is a two-compartment Hades (Tartarus vs. Paradise) taught by Scripture? (assessment: contested)

The two-compartment Hades — whether a single underworld divided into Tartarus (the prison of the rebel angels, 2 Pet 2:4) and Paradise / Abraham's Bosom (the righteous dead, Luke 16:22–26) is genuinely taught by the texts, or read into them from Second Temple and Greek cosmology. The structured intermediate Hades is the early-patristic and Eastern mainstream (1 Enoch 22; Hippolytus; Tertullian) and fits a plain reading of Luke 16's gulf; but reading that parable as cosmography is disputed, the present-tense two-compartment claim is undercut even within the structured tradition (the limbus patrum is emptied at Christ's descent, so the righteous no longer wait there — Aquinas), and the Reformed confessions deny any third place outright (Calvin; Westminster). A genuine, long-standing divide with strong support on one side and principled denial on the other.49

Is soul-sleep (psychopannychism) taught? (assessment: weak)

Soul-sleep — whether Scripture's pervasive "sleep" language for death (Dan 12:2; Eccl 9:5,10; John 11:11–14; 1 Thess 4:13–15; 1 Cor 15:51) teaches that the dead are genuinely unconscious until the resurrection, or is a euphemism for the body's death. The idiom is real and the doctrine rightly centres the bodily resurrection (so Aphrahat, and the same concern recurs in modern conditionalism); but on its best case unconsciousness does not hold: "sleep" most naturally describes the body laid in the dust, the Ecclesiastes texts speak "under the sun," and the conscious-state texts positively require awareness — the martyrs "under the altar" cry out and are answered (Rev 6:9–11), and Paul counts it "far better" "to depart, and to be with Christ" (Phil 1:23). Even Aphrahat keeps a conscious "spirit … with our Lord," and the tradition closes ranks (Calvin's Psychopannychia; Westminster: souls "neither die nor sleep").50

Are Gehenna and the lake of fire one final state, distinct from intermediate Hades? (assessment: sound)

Gehenna and the lake of fire — whether Gehenna = the lake of fire = the second death names the post-judgment, eternal state ("hell" proper), distinct from the intermediate Hades/Sheol that presently holds the dead. The distinction is the patristic and scholarly mainstream and follows directly from Rev 20:14, where Hades is emptied and then itself "cast into the lake of fire" — so the two cannot be the same place. Hippolytus keeps the "lake of unquenchable fire … prepared against the day determined by God" unoccupied while Hades already detains souls, and Irenaeus equates the lake of fire, the second death, and Gehenna outright. The one inferential step — that the Synoptics' Gehenna and the Apocalypse's lake of fire name the same thing — is well grounded though not verbatim in any single verse; and English "hell" (rendering both Hades and Gehenna) hides the very distinction. The separable question of the final state's nature — eternal torment vs. annihilation — is contested in its own right and does not touch this crux.51
