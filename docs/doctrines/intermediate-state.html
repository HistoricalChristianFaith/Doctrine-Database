The Intermediate State (the soul between death and resurrection)

What becomes of a person between death and the general resurrection? Scripture speaks of the dead in Sheol / Hades, of "Abraham's bosom" and a place of "torments" divided by a "great gulf" (Luke 16), of the penitent thief "with me in paradise" that very day (Luke 23:43), of the believer "absent from the body … present with the Lord" (2 Cor 5:8), and of the sinning angels cast into Tartarus (2 Pet 2:4). Out of these the Church built a doctrine of the intermediate state. Two questions divide its interpreters:

  1. Is the soul conscious in the interval? Does it live, perceive, rest, or suffer at once — or does it "sleep" (and even, for some, die) until the resurrection?
  2. Is the place structured? Is Hades a single realm with compartments (Abraham's bosom / a place of torment / the angelic prison of Tartarus), or are there simply two final destinies, heaven and hell, entered immediately?

The earliest and long-dominant reading is a conscious, structured Hades: every soul descends to a subterranean holding-place where the righteous rest (in "Abraham's bosom") and the wicked suffer, all awaiting the resurrection, while Christ's own descent to the dead frees the captive patriarchs. The medieval West then elaborated the map — adding purgatory and the limbus patrum, and (after the descent) sending the fully-cleansed straight to the vision of God. The Reformation collapsed the structure: keeping the soul's conscious survival but denying any third place — "besides these two places … the Scripture acknowledgeth none." A structured, two-compartment Hades has lately been revived by popular teachers such as Doug Wilson.

A note on the shape of this debate. Conscious survival is the tradition's overwhelming majority position — the real divide is over the structure of the intermediate place, and over when the righteous enter glory. But it is not quite unanimous: an early Syriac current (Aphrahat) teaches a genuine sleep of the dead until the resurrection, the minority soul-sleep reading the Reformation's left wing revived and Calvin's Psychopannychia rebutted. The early Fathers (and the Second Temple background) picture a structured Hades that detains even the righteous until Christ; the high-medieval West keeps compartments but lets the cleansed see God at once; the Reformed confessions deny compartments altogether. Whether a two-compartment Hades (Tartarus vs. Paradise) is taught by the texts, and whether the "sleep" of death means real unconsciousness, are weighed adversarially in the Arguments & cruxes section below (contested; weak).

Timeline

1 Enoch (c. 300 BC)

1 Enoch gives the earliest detailed map of the structured intermediate state: the spirits of the dead are gathered into "hollow places" in a great western mountain and separated into divisions — the righteous by "the bright spring of water," the sinners apart — "to receive them till the day of their judgement."1 It is the literary ancestor of the Abraham's-bosom / place-of-torment geography.

2 Maccabees (c. 124 BC)

2 Maccabees gives the earliest explicit witness that the living can aid the dead: Judas Maccabeus, finding idolatrous tokens on his fallen soldiers, sends a sin offering to Jerusalem for them and "made a reconciliation for the dead, that they might be delivered from sin" — reasoning that, but for the resurrection, it would be "superfluous and vain to pray for the dead." It is the seed of the prayers-for-the-dead / purgatory line.2

The Wisdom of Solomon (c. 50 BC)

The Wisdom of Solomon, an Alexandrian Jewish wisdom book roughly contemporary with Philo, gives the Second Temple background its clearest statement of conscious post-mortem rest for the righteous: "the souls of the righteous are in the hand of God, and there shall no torment touch them"; though "in the sight of the unwise they seemed to die … they are in peace," "their hope full of immortality."3

The New Testament (c. AD 60–90)

The New Testament supplies the data the later doctrine is built on. Jesus' account of the rich man and Lazarus sets "Abraham's bosom" against a place of "torments," with "a great gulf fixed" so that none may pass between;4 he promises the thief, "To day shalt thou be with me in paradise" (Luke 23:43); and Paul is "willing rather to be absent from the body, and to be present with the Lord" (2 Cor 5:8), "having a desire to depart, and to be with Christ" (Phil 1:23) — a conscious post-mortem state, two destinies, and the believer already "with Christ."5

Justin Martyr (c. 165)

Justin holds that "the souls of the pious remain in a better place, while those of the unjust and wicked are in a worse, waiting for the time of judgment" — and brands those who say the souls of the dead "are taken to heaven" at once, denying the resurrection, as people one should "not imagine … are Christians."6

Clement of Alexandria (c. 200)

Clement assumes a conscious, divided Hades — "who in his senses can suppose the souls of the righteous and those of sinners in the same condemnation?" — and makes Christ's descent an act of preaching: "the Lord preached the Gospel to those in Hades," and the apostles after him, so that even the righteous who died before the Incarnation might hear, repent, and be saved.7

Irenaeus (c. 202)

Irenaeus teaches that the souls of the departed "go away into the invisible place allotted to them by God, and there remain until the resurrection" — for Christ himself "dwelt in the place where the dead were" three days, so the disciples ought not to suppose they will fly straight above the heavens.8

Tertullian (c. 220)

Tertullian, in A Treatise on the Soul, makes Hades "a vast deep space in the interior of the earth" that holds every soul until the resurrection — the faithful refreshed in Abraham's bosom, the wicked in torment, all conscious in the interval — with the martyrs the sole exception: "The sole key to unlock Paradise is your own life's blood."9

Hippolytus of Rome (c. 235)

Hippolytus, in Against Plato, on the Cause of the Universe, gives the fullest patristic geography: one Hades detaining all souls until judgment, but divided — the righteous "conducted in the light toward the right … to a locality full of light," which "we call by the name Abraham's bosom," the unrighteous "dragged toward the left," with "a deep and vast abyss" set between that none can cross.10

Origen of Alexandria (c. 240)

Origen makes the descent structural: with the Incarnation it is one of the Word's two "shoes," and by it Christ "release[d] those who were held there" in the underworld and "led captivity captive" (Eph 4:8), reopening to the penitent thief — and "all the saints for whom he had descended" — the paradise Adam had closed.11

Aphrahat the Persian Sage (c. 345)

Aphrahat, the earliest Syriac Father, gives the timeline its one positive witness to soul-sleep: "when men fall asleep, they sleep this slumber without knowing good from evil … until the Judge come," so that "as yet no one has received his reward" — neither the righteous their kingdom nor the wicked their torment. He qualifies it in the Syriac manner: the heavenly "spirit which the righteous receive … goes to our Lord until the time of the Resurrection," while the buried man sleeps.12

Cyril of Jerusalem (c. 350)

Cyril of Jerusalem, catechizing at the Holy Sepulchre, teaches that Christ "went down into the regions beneath the earth, that thence also He might redeem the righteous" — David, Samuel, the prophets, and John the Baptist, "imprisoned" since Adam — and "sent forth His prisoners out of the pit wherein is no water" (Zech 9:11), the classic Eastern statement of the descent emptying the holding-place of the OT saints.13

Gregory of Nyssa (c. 395)

Gregory of Nyssa, in the dialogue with Macrina On the Soul and the Resurrection, reads "the bosom of Abraham … as a symbol of the good state of the soul" — a condition of rest rather than a locality — and argues that the simple, uncompounded soul consciously survives the body's dissolution, keeping watch over its own elements until the resurrection.14

John Chrysostom (c. 407)

Chrysostom teaches that the soul is conscious the instant it leaves the body: the righteous are carried off by angels "as a crowned victor" to Abraham's bosom (so Lazarus), the gulf between the states "fixed, because it cannot be loosened, moved, or shaken," and the faithful "depart … to be with Christ."15

Augustine of Hippo (c. 430)

Augustine holds that "during the time … which intervenes between a man's death and the final resurrection, the soul dwells in a hidden retreat, where it enjoys rest or suffers affliction" according to its earthly desert,16 and that the dead "are benefited by the piety of their living friends" through prayer, alms, and the Eucharistic sacrifice — the first systematic warrant for helping the dead, and (held tentatively) for a purgatorial fire; the fallen angels meanwhile are kept in Tartarus for judgment.17

Gregory the Dialogist (c. 604)

Gregory the Great reads Abraham's bosom as "the secret rest of the fathers" before the last judgment, holds that each soul is judged at death and enters rest or punishment at once, and gives the West its classic statement of a cleansing fire: "there must be a … fire before judgment, because of some minor faults that may remain to be purged away."18

Bede (c. 735)

Bede transmits the structured intermediate state to the medieval West: Abraham's bosom is "the rest of the blessed … to which they are received after this life," and his Vision of Drythelm (Ecclesiastical History V.12) maps distinct intermediate regions — a flowery resting-place "into which the souls of those are received who depart the body in good works, but … are not so perfect as to deserve to be immediately admitted into the kingdom of heaven."19

John of Damascus (c. 749)

John of Damascus gives the Eastern systematic basis for a conscious intermediate state — the soul is "a living essence, simple, incorporeal … immortal, reasoning and intelligent" — and teaches that Christ's deified soul "descended into Hades" and "freed those who had been bound for ages."20

Thomas Aquinas (c. 1274)

Aquinas systematizes the abodes of souls: heaven, hell, purgatory (for "those who die in grace with venial sins"), the limbus patrum — the OT "Abraham's bosom," emptied when "Christ descended into hell [and] delivered the holy Fathers from thence" — and the limbo of children. Since the descent the fully-cleansed pass straight to the vision of God, others to purgatory.21

Benedict XII (c. 1336)

Benedict XII, in the constitution Benedictus Deus, dogmatically resolved the controversy John XXII had stirred over when the blessed see God: the fully-purified souls, immediately after death and any needed purgation, "already before they take up their bodies again and before the general judgment," see "the divine essence with an intuitive vision and even face to face." The cleansed enter glory at once — the mature Latin answer Aquinas had anticipated.22

John Calvin (c. 1542)

Calvin, in Psychopannychia, defends conscious survival against the "soul-sleep" of some Anabaptists — "the soul, after the death of the body, still survives, endued with sense and intellect" — but rejects the scholastic apparatus: the limbus patrum, "though this fable has the countenance of great authors … is nothing but a fable," and purgatory likewise.23

The Westminster Confession (1646)

The Westminster Confession (ch. XXXII) gives the mature Reformed position: the souls of men "neither die nor sleep" but "immediately return to God"; the righteous "are received into the highest heavens" and the wicked "cast into hell" — and, pointedly, "besides these two places for souls separated from their bodies, the Scripture acknowledgeth none."24 No Abraham's bosom as a third place, no limbo, no purgatory.

N. T. Wright (c. 2008)

N. T. Wright, in Surprised by Hope, keeps a conscious intermediate state — "paradise," a "blissful garden where God's people rest prior to the resurrection," a state of "restful happiness" consciously with Christ — but subordinates it to the real hope: bodily resurrection, "life after life after death," against the popular reduction of the gospel to "going to heaven when you die."25

Doug Wilson (c. 2021)

Doug Wilson, a Reformed/CREC pastor, revives the structured two-compartment Hades against his own confession's flatter map: "There was a chasm between Tartarus on the one side, and Paradise on the other … The lowest pit of Hades … was called Tartarus, and that is where the angels were locked up." He reads Christ's descent (1 Pet 3:18–20) as heralding victory — not the gospel — to those imprisoned angels.26

Arguments & cruxes

Sub-claims marshaled for (or against) a reading above, which cut across the timeline rather than sitting at one date — each weighed adversarially on its own page, with an assessment of the interpretation it serves. See project.md §4 for the page type.

Is a two-compartment Hades (Tartarus vs. Paradise) taught by Scripture? (assessment: contested)

The two-compartment Hades — whether a single underworld divided into Tartarus (the prison of the rebel angels, 2 Pet 2:4) and Paradise / Abraham's Bosom (the righteous dead, Luke 16:22–26) is genuinely taught by the texts, or read into them from Second Temple and Greek cosmology. The structured intermediate Hades is the early-patristic and Eastern mainstream (1 Enoch 22; Hippolytus; Tertullian) and fits a plain reading of Luke 16's gulf; but reading that parable as cosmography is disputed, the present-tense two-compartment claim is undercut even within the structured tradition (the limbus patrum is emptied at Christ's descent, so the righteous no longer wait there — Aquinas), and the Reformed confessions deny any third place outright (Calvin; Westminster). A genuine, long-standing divide with strong support on one side and principled denial on the other.27

Is soul-sleep (psychopannychism) taught? (assessment: weak)

Soul-sleep — whether Scripture's pervasive "sleep" language for death (Dan 12:2; Eccl 9:5,10; John 11:11–14; 1 Thess 4:13–15; 1 Cor 15:51) teaches that the dead are genuinely unconscious until the resurrection, or is a euphemism for the body's death. The idiom is real and the doctrine rightly centres the bodily resurrection (so Aphrahat, and the same concern recurs in modern conditionalism); but on its best case unconsciousness does not hold: "sleep" most naturally describes the body laid in the dust, the Ecclesiastes texts speak "under the sun," and the conscious-state texts positively require awareness — the martyrs "under the altar" cry out and are answered (Rev 6:9–11), and Paul counts it "far better" "to depart, and to be with Christ" (Phil 1:23). Even Aphrahat keeps a conscious "spirit … with our Lord," and the tradition closes ranks (Calvin's Psychopannychia; Westminster: souls "neither die nor sleep").28

Is a purgatorial fire before judgment (and prayers for the dead) taught? (assessment: contested)

A purgatorial fire — whether some of the saved undergo a post-mortem cleansing fire before the last judgment (Augustine's tentative purgatorius ignis; Gregory the Great's "cleansing fire before judgment," Dialogues IV.39; Aquinas's purgatory), and whether the dead are aided by the prayers, alms, and Eucharistic sacrifice of the living. The two claims are of unequal strength. Prayer for the dead is a near-consensus of the ancient Church (Tertullian, Cyprian, Cyril of Jerusalem, Augustine — and the East to this day), with a Second Temple seed in 2 Macc 12. But the cleansing fire rests on a doubly-read proof-text — "saved; yet so as by fire" (1 Cor 3:13–15), which the Greek exegetes (Chrysostom, Theophylact, Theodoret) take as the fire of judgment, not an intermediate purgation — on an inference from Matt 12:32, and on the canonicity-dependent 2 Macc 12; the East prays for its dead yet never received the Latin satisfactory fire, and the Reformed deny both (Calvin "a fable"; Westminster). Strong by Latin-patristic and liturgical lights, weak by Greek-exegetical and Reformed ones.29

Did the descended Christ free the OT righteous and "lead captivity captive"? (assessment: plausible)

The descent to the dead — whether the crucified Christ went down to the realm of the human dead and freed the OT righteous from a holding-place ("Abraham's bosom" / the limbus patrum), "leading captivity captive" (Eph 4:8) and emptying it, so the righteous now go straight to be "with the Lord." The crux is distinct from the 1 Peter 3:19 crux, which concerns the imprisoned angels. Two questions divide: the bare descensus (Christ's soul went to the dead) is sound — creedal, affirmed East and West, grounded in Acts 2:27/31; but the specific liberation-of-a-holding-place mechanism, though the pre-Reformation mainstream (Irenaeus, Clement, Origen, Cyril of Jerusalem, Jerome, John of Damascus, Aquinas's emptied limbus), leans on the genuinely ambiguous Eph 4:9 ("the lower parts of the earth" — Hades, or the incarnation? — Aquinas allows both), is partly inferred from resurrection-texts, and is denied by the Reformed who reframe the descent and recognize no third place (Calvin; Westminster). Sound on the descent, probable-but-unsettled on the emptied limbus.30
