Astrology and the Stars

From the second century to the seventh, the Christian writers are remarkably united against astrology — not star-watching, but genethlialogy: the casting of a birth-horoscope to read a person's character and destiny from the position of the heavens. Their objection is doctrinal, not merely scientific. Astral determinism, they hold, destroys free will, empties reward and punishment of meaning, makes the Creator the author of every sin, and usurps the providence of God — and, several add, the art was not discovered but taught, by the fallen angels, to enslave mankind. Around this consensus run the recurring flashpoints: the empirical disproof from twins; the embarrassment (and the answer) of the Magi's star; and the careful line, drawn again and again, between the superstition of the nativity-casters and the legitimate astronomy that studies the courses of the heavens and lifts the mind to their Maker. Yet the consensus, near-total, was not quite monolithic: a scattered minority — the Christian-era astrologer Firmicus Maternus, Zeno of Verona's baptismal reuse of the zodiac, the Valentinians who escaped Fate at the font, and the astral determinism the councils had to condemn in the Priscillianists — accommodated or appropriated the art rather than repudiating it. What follows are the witnesses, earliest first.

Timeline

Listed earliest first. The near-unanimous verdict is rejection of astral fatalism; the interest lies in the different grounds — demonological, logical, empirical, moral, and scientific — on which it is refused, in the handful (Clement, Origen, Isidore) who carefully save what is true in the study of the stars, and in the one dissenter, Firmicus Maternus, who defends the art itself.

Ignatius of Antioch (c. 107)

The earliest Christian comment on the Nativity already reads the Magi's star as the ruin of the stars' power. On his way to martyrdom Ignatius tells the Ephesians that when Christ was manifested "a star shone forth in heaven above all the other stars," so novel that "every kind of magic was destroyed, and every bond of wickedness disappeared; ignorance was removed, and the old kingdom abolished." The one star that led astrologers to Christ is, for Ignatius, the star that broke astrology.1

Tatian (c. 165)

Tatian traces Fate itself to the fallen angels. The demons, he says, "having shown [men] a plan of the position of the stars, like dice-players… introduced Fate, a flagrant injustice"; the zodiac is their fabrication, "the delineation of the zodiacal circle is the work of gods." But the Christian is lifted out of that slavery: "we are superior to Fate, and instead of wandering demons, we have learned to know one Lord who wanders not; and, as we do not follow the guidance of Fate, we reject its lawgivers." The tradition that the same rebel angels taught the forbidden arts links this doctrine to the sons of God and the Nephilim, and is weighed on the fallen-angels argument.2

Bardaisan of Edessa (c. 200)

The earliest Syriac voice mounts a different attack — from the diversity of human law. In The Book of the Laws of Countries, Bardaisan (through his pupil Philip) argues that whole nations living under the same stars keep utterly incompatible customs, and that Christians everywhere keep one law regardless of the heavens: "wherever we are, we are all called after the one name of Christ… nor does the Fate of the celestial Governors compel them." The rule holds generally: "the laws of men prevail over the decree of the stars." (Bardaisan still allowed the stars a hand in outward fortune; his firm denial is that they compel the will.)3

Clement of Alexandria (c. 200)

Alexandria's more accommodating voice draws the crucial distinction. Astronomy, for Clement, is a genuine good that raises the mind toward God: by it a man "is elevated along with heaven… studying ever divine things… from which Abraham… ascended to the knowledge of Him who created them." It is astral fatalism, not the study of the heavens, that the Church rejects — a line the later tradition keeps sharp.4

Tertullian (c. 203)

Tertullian classes astrology as a species of condemned magic, and its origin with the rebel angels: "those angels, the deserters from God, the lovers of women, were likewise the discoverers of this curious art." He grants that the Magi's art was real, but time-bound: "that science has been allowed until the Gospel, in order that after Christ's birth no one should thenceforward interpret any one's nativity by the heaven" — and the Magi, warned to return "another way," figure the astrologer forsaking his craft. His verdict: "You know nothing, astrologer, if you know not that you should be a Christian." (On the deserter-angels as the teachers of the art, see the fallen-angels argument and cf. the Nephilim; on the "until the Gospel" claim, see the star-of-Bethlehem argument.)5

Hippolytus of Rome (c. 225)

Hippolytus devotes the opening of his Refutation of All Heresies to laying bare the Chaldeans' technique — the casting of the horoscope, the arithmetic of the zodiac — precisely in order to discredit it, and to show that the heretics had simply borrowed the astrologers' machinery. The art, he says, is "calculated both to deceive and blind the soul indulging in vain expectations"; and it has a single point of failure: "if the horoscope be removed… the entire system of the Chaldeans vanishes along with (them)."6

Origen (c. 231)

Origen frames the most sophisticated position, turning on the pro-astrology proof-text itself — "let them be for signs" (Genesis 1:14). The stars, he argues, signify but do not cause: they are a book whose signs the angelic "Powers" (and, exceptionally, inspired men like the Magi) can read, while ordinary men, "deceived by certain observations… supposed that they from whom they thought they received the signs were the original causes of the events which the Word says the signs indicate." Foreknowledge is thus preserved without astral necessity, and free will stands untouched.7

Methodius of Olympus (c. 290)

In the eighth discourse of the Symposium, Thecla presses a historical argument: the constellations are recent human inventions, so no one was ever really "born under a sign," and "our ancestors lived without destiny." The moral upshot is the standard one, driven home so plainly "that it is now possible even for a child to… perceive their error: that to do good or evil is in our own power, and not decided by the stars."8

Firmicus Maternus (c. 336)

The lone dissenting voice on the timeline — and a revealing one. Firmicus, a Sicilian senator, wrote the fullest surviving Latin handbook of astrology, the Mathesis, defending genethlialogy as a sacred science; then, a decade or so later and now a Christian, he wrote De errore profanarum religionum urging the emperors to abolish paganism. His Mathesis does not refute the Fathers' objections so much as accommodate them: astral fate is kept, but folded under "the Highest God," the emperor alone is exempted from the stars' decree ("the Emperor alone is not subject to the course of the stars… his destiny is governed by the judgment of the Highest God"), and the astrologer is charged to be pure, honest, and above bribery. Whether he was already a Christian when he wrote it is debated; that a man of the Christian empire wrote and circulated it is not — which makes him the concrete face of the accommodationist strand the polemicists were answering. (See the accommodation argument.)9

Basil of Caesarea (c. 378)

The sixth homily of Basil's Hexaemeron is the fullest attack in the Greek Fathers. He presses three lines at once: the practical — the birth-instant can never actually be fixed, so both the astrologers and those who gape at them "are supremely ridiculous"; the ridiculous — deriving a man's character from the beast a constellation is merely named after ("He who is born under the Balance will be just… Is not this the height of folly?"); and the moral — that astral fate makes law, judgment, "reward for justice… punishment for sin" all pointless, and, since God made even the "malignant" stars, "the Creator [becomes] the author of evil." Basil's dossier became the pattern for Ambrose and the Latin West.10

Gregory of Nyssa (c. 380)

Gregory's dialogue Against Fate refutes a pagan astrologer on the same free-will ground, adding a fresh empirical argument: whole cities of people, born at every different hour, perish together "in a single instant" in an earthquake or a flood — a common fate that no shared horoscope can explain. The animal-qualities of the signs he finds simply incoherent, and concludes that human action "seems determined by each person's free will."11

Ambrose of Milan (c. 387)

Ambrose carries Basil's dossier into the Latin West, in the sixth sermon of his own Hexameron (on the fourth day). He first turns the very order of creation against the stars: the luminaries were made only "on the fourth day," after the earth had already greened, so "the beetle is older than the sun, the grass is older than the moon" — the life of the world cannot hang on lights kindled after it. Then he presses Basil's three lines. The practical — the birth-instant can never be caught, for while the midwife works "the Chaldean listens, sets the horoscope," "points pass, time flies irreparably," and so "the whole task is full of vanity." The ridiculous — to call a man born under Aries generous "because the ram willingly sheds its fleece" is to read a human character off "the qualities of cheap food." And the reductio — were birth star-fixed, "kings would be born daily" and no throne could pass to a son. The Chaldeans' art is a spider's web in which "the weak get entangled… while those with stronger senses" break through.12

John Chrysostom (c. 390)

Preaching on the Magi, Chrysostom meets the great objection head-on: the Bethlehem star cannot vindicate astrology, because it was no ordinary star at all. It travelled "from north to south," shone while the sun was up, and "when they were to move, it moved; when to stand, it stood" — conduct "not… of a star, but of some power highly endued with reason." Far from submitting to the heavens, Christ came to "put down astrology, and take away fate, and… overthrow all such sorcery." (See the star-of-Bethlehem argument.)13

Augustine of Hippo (c. 400–417)

Augustine, who had consulted the mathematici in his youth, becomes their most thorough critic. Across the Confessions, On Christian Doctrine, and City of God he assembles the whole case: the horoscope-casters "sell inexperienced men into a miserable bondage" and traffic in "a baleful fellowship between men and devils"; twins, a free man's son and a slave born "at the same moment," and Esau and Jacob all prove that identical charts yield opposite lives; the astrologers' own potter's-wheel escape he turns against them; and astral fatalism, he insists, shifts the blame for every sin onto "the Creator and Ordainer of heaven and stars." (See the twins argument.)14

Gregory the Great (c. 591)

Against the Priscillianist heretics, who cited the Bethlehem star to prove "that every man is born under the influence of a star," Gregory answers from the Gospel's own word order: the star "went before them till it came and stood over where the Child was" — so it was "not the Child who followed the star, but the star that followed the Child." Man was made before and above the luminaries: "the stars [were made] for man," not man for the stars. And Jacob, born gripping Esau's heel yet utterly unlike him in life, disproves any birth-fixed fate.15

Isidore of Seville (c. 620)

The encyclopedist fixes the distinction that endures. In the Etymologies he separates astronomy (the courses and motions of the heavens) and even the "natural" part of astrology (the seasons, the reckoning of sun and moon) from its "superstitious" part — "that which the mathematici follow, who prophesy by the stars… and endeavor to predict the nativities and characters of men from the course of the stars." Legitimate star-science is kept; only the nativity-casting is condemned.16

Arguments

Five cross-cutting sub-claims are weighed on their own merits: the empirical disproof the Fathers pressed hardest, the standing puzzle of the Magi's star, the historical question of whether some Christians accommodated the art rather than refusing it, the demonological aetiology — that astrology was not discovered but taught, by the fallen angels — and the modern charge that the church's war on astrology was a war on astronomy too.

Do twins refute astrology? (assessment: sound)

The twins argument — that people born under one horoscope (twins; a slave and a free son born at the same instant; Esau and Jacob) meet opposite fates, so the birth-chart determines nothing. Pressed by Basil and Augustine after Carneades and Cicero, and unbroken by the astrologers' one reply (Nigidius's unobservable interval, which defeats the astrologer too), it is a sound refutation of the deterministic natal astrology the Fathers targeted — though it leaves a merely "inclining," probabilistic astrology to be met on other grounds.17

Did the star of Bethlehem abolish astrology, or vindicate it? (assessment: contested)

The star-of-Bethlehem argument — the Fathers' reading (Ignatius, Tertullian, Chrysostom, Gregory the Great) that the Magi's star ended astrology rather than endorsing it. As theology it is mainstream, primitive, and coherent with Scripture's wider anti-stargazer polemic; but as exegesis of Matthew 2 it reads in a verdict the text withholds — which is why the same narrative could be, and was (by the Priscillianists), cited to the opposite end.18

Did some early Christians accommodate astrology? (assessment: sound)

The accommodation argument — Tim Hegedus's historical thesis that the patristic reaction, though overwhelmingly hostile, was not monolithic. A real minority accommodated or appropriated the art: Firmicus Maternus wrote a Christian-era handbook that keeps astral fate under a supreme God; Zeno of Verona rebaptized the twelve zodiac signs for his neophytes; the Valentinian Excerpta ex Theodoto taught that the faithful pass out from under Fate at the font; and the councils' own anathemas against the Priscillianists prove astral determinism had adherents inside the Church. The bounded claim is sound — but the accommodators are a scattered minority, half of them heterodox or condemned, and even the orthodox among them keep the stars under God and refuse the deterministic natal astrology that was the Fathers' real quarry.19

Did the fallen angels teach astrology? (assessment: contested)

The fallen-angels argument — the demonological aetiology, gestured at by Tatian ("the demons… introduced Fate") and Tertullian ("those angels, the deserters from God… were… the discoverers of this curious art") and rooted in 1 Enoch 8, that astrology was not discovered by men but taught to them by the rebel angels of Genesis 6. As a reading of the tradition it is sound — 1 Enoch 8 explicitly lists astrology among the Watchers' teachings, and the Fathers consciously invoke that pedigree to cast astral Fate as a bondage the Christian renounces — but as an account of astrology's actual origin it is a theological aetiology resting on a single legendary stream, laid over the documented Babylonian human institution and standing apart from the logical, empirical, and moral grounds on which the same Fathers principally rest their case. (The shared Watcher-instruction tradition ties this doctrine to the sons of God and the Nephilim.)20

Did the church condemn astronomy along with astrology? (assessment: weak)

The astronomy argument — the modern "church versus science" charge that the Fathers, in rejecting astrology, were hostile to the study of the heavens as such. The witnesses tell against it: from Clement and Origen through Basil and Augustine to Isidore's textbook heading "the difference between astronomy and astrology," the tradition consistently keeps — and often praises — the observation of the heavenly courses while condemning only the casting of nativities. The strongest contrary evidence (Tatian's sweeping talk of the zodiac as "the work of gods," Augustine's counsel to neglect star-lore as an exegetical distraction) falls short of a condemnation of astronomy.21
