Irenaeus of Lyons on the Rabbinic Corruption of Scripture

Dates: c. AD 189 (Against Heresies Book 3, composed at Lyons) · Wikipedia
Summary: The earliest post-Justin statement of the corruption charge, and the first to fasten it on named later revisers and a single verse. In Against Heresies III.21.1 Irenaeus attacks Theodotion and Aquila, "both Jewish proselytes," for rendering Isaiah 7:14 "a young woman shall conceive" against the Septuagint's "virgin," and the Ebionites who lean on them to deny the virgin birth. His defence of the older Greek is the earliest form of the dating filter the later Fathers would systematize: because the Seventy translated "much before the period of our Lord's advent," their version stands clear of any suspicion of anti-Christian bias, whereas the post-Christian revisions do not. His charge falls on the later Greek versions, though, not (yet) on a rabbinic mutilation of the Hebrew.

Claims & evidence

The later Jewish revisers mistranslated Isaiah 7:14 — "young woman" for the Septuagint's "virgin"

"God, then, was made man, and the Lord did Himself save us, giving us the token of the Virgin. But not as some allege, among those now presuming to expound the Scripture, [thus:] `Behold, a young woman shall conceive, and bring forth a son,' as Theodotion the Ephesian has interpreted, and Aquila of Pontus, both Jewish proselytes. The Ebionites, following these, assert that He was begotten by Joseph; thus destroying, as far as in them lies, such a marvellous dispensation of God, and setting aside the testimony of the prophets which proceeded from God."

— Against Heresies III.21.1, source (also New Advent), trans. ANF 1. This is the earliest Christian naming of the two second-century revisers whose Greek the synagogue preferred to the Septuagint — Aquila above all — and the earliest attachment of the corruption charge to the christological showpiece, Isaiah 7:14. Where Justin had accused anonymous "teachers" of cutting whole logia, Irenaeus narrows the quarrel to a named translator and a single word (Hebrew 'almah, Septuagint parthenos "virgin," Aquila/Theodotion neanis "young woman").

The Septuagint is trustworthy because it predates Christ — the dating filter

"But it was interpreted into Greek by the Jews themselves, much before the period of our Lord's advent, that there might remain no suspicion that perchance the Jews, complying with our humour, did put this interpretation upon these words. They indeed, had they been cognizant of our future existence, and that we should use these proofs from the Scriptures, would themselves never have hesitated to burn their own Scriptures."

— Against Heresies III.21.1, source. Here Irenaeus states, a generation before Eusebius and long before Chrysostom and Philoxenus, the reasoning that would become the standing patristic rule: a version made before Christ cannot have been bent against him, so the pre-Christian Septuagint is innocent by construction and the suspicion falls on the later revisions. It is the seed of the whole malice thesis — the inspired-and-early Greek set against the synagogue's rival text — even though Irenaeus locates the divergence in the later Greek versions rather than in any Jewish tampering with the Hebrew.

Notes

  • The charge falls on the revisers, not the Hebrew. Irenaeus does not accuse the synagogue of mutilating its Hebrew text; his target is the post-Christian Greek versions of Aquila and Theodotion that displaced the Septuagint. That is the narrowest and best-documented form of the corruption charge, weighed on the Aquila-revision argument (weak): Aquila's neanis is in fact the more accurate rendering of the unchanged Hebrew 'almah that the Septuagint had glossed parthenos, so the flagship "distortion" folds a translation choice into a charge of textual malice — a slip to keep in view whenever Irenaeus's example is cited.
  • The same chapter, read for the other doctrine. III.21.1 is the polemical setup for the famous inspiration-miracle of III.21.2–3 (the seventy elders sealed in separate cells, their identical drafts proving the Greek divinely inspired) — Irenaeus's fuller role in building the Septuagint legend belongs to the companion doctrine, the Origin and Inspiration of the Septuagint. The two cohere: the inspired-and-early Greek is exactly the premise that makes the synagogue's divergent version look like corruption.
  • Dating. Book 3 is anchored here at c. 189 rather than the earlier c. 175 sometimes given for Against Heresies, because III.21.1 already knows Theodotion's revision — which the ANF editor dates to 181 — so the book must postdate 181; c. 189 sits within the standard c. 180–189 window for the work's composition at Lyons.
