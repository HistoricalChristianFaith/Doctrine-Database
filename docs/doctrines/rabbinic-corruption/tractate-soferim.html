Tractate Soferim on the Rabbinic Corruption of Scripture

Dates: c. 750 (minor post-Talmudic tractate, redacted about the middle of the eighth century in the Land of Israel) · Wikipedia
Summary: The Jewish tradition's hostile face toward the Greek Torah — the mirror of the sanguine account in Tractate Megillah. Where the older Talmudic layer praises the Greek as a providential, even divinely-guided rendering, this late minor tractate (c. 750) records the memory that the day the Torah was put into Greek for Ptolemy "was as ominous for Israel as the day on which the golden calf was made." It is a genuine Jewish counter-voice — but a late one, and it belongs on this timeline for what it does not say as much as for what it does: it charges no rabbinic mutilation of the Hebrew, does not answer the Christian corruption charge, and laments the Greek rendering itself, not any tampering with the source. The rabbinic tradition on the Septuagint is thus two-faced, and both faces are older than any medieval "eighth of Tevet" fast that dramatized this one.

Claims & evidence

The day the Torah was written in Greek "was as ominous for Israel as the day… the golden calf was made"

"It once happened that five elders wrote the Torah for King Ptolemy in Greek, and that day was as ominous for Israel as the day on which the golden calf was made, since the Torah could not be accurately translated."

— Tractate Soferim 1:7, Sefaria. The grievance is precise, and worth reading exactly: the tractate does not say the Jews altered the Hebrew, nor that the Greek was falsified against Christ. It laments that any translation at all was a loss — "the Torah could not be accurately translated" — so the calamity is the very act of rendering the sacred Hebrew into a gentile tongue, not a plot against a rival's proof-texts. The same passage goes on to give the familiar sanguine legend as a second, adjacent account (Ptolemy sealing elders apart, who agree on the same deliberate changes) — the two moods sitting side by side in one paragraph.

The medieval dramatization: the eighth of Tevet, "darkness… in the world for three days"

"On the eighth of Tevet the Torah was written in Greek during the days of King Ptolemy and darkness was in the world for three days."

— Shulchan Arukh, Orach Chayim 580:2, Sefaria, codifying the fast-day list of the medieval Megillat Ta'anit Batra scholion. The "three days of darkness" clause is not in Soferim itself; it is the later liturgical embellishment that turned the Soferim lament into a commemorated fast (8 Tevet), and it is quoted here only to show how the hostile strand grew in transmission — from a tractate's aside, to a scholion, to a code's fast-day. The hostility is real but the drama is medieval, and neither layer accuses the rabbis of corrupting the Hebrew.

Notes

  • It is the counter-voice, but a late and limited one. Seated on this timeline as a Jewish-side witness, Soferim is easy to over-read. It is not a second-century rabbinic rejection of the Septuagint; it is a minor tractate redacted about the middle of the eighth century — later than most of the Christian witnesses to the corruption charge, and centuries after Megillah. And its complaint is orthogonal to the charge this doctrine weighs: it mourns that the Torah was Hellenized at all, and nowhere claims that either side mutilated the text. It is the Jewish tradition's ambivalence about the Greek, not a Jewish reply to the Christian accusation of Hebrew tampering.
  • The older layer runs the other way — and must be shown alongside it. The Babylonian tradition praises the Greek as divinely superintended (b. Megillah 9a: "the Holy One… sent into the heart of each of them a counsel"), and the Palestinian tradition holds the Torah "may be correctly translated only into Greek," reading Bar Qappara's Genesis 9:27 — "that one should speak Japhet's language in Shem's tents" — as praise of Greek in the synagogue.1 So the rabbinic verdict on the Septuagint is genuinely two-faced, and to cite only Soferim's "golden calf" line would misrepresent the tradition as uniformly hostile. The hinge phrase kol tzorkah ("in its entirety / fittingly") is used negatively in Soferim's setting and positively in the Palestinian Talmud's — the same idiom bent to opposite moods.
  • Five elders, not seventy-two. Soferim's hostile opening names five elders who wrote the Torah for Ptolemy, where Megillah (and the Letter of Aristeas tradition) has the famous seventy-two. The discrepancy is a mark of the fluidity of the legend across the rabbinic parallels — the count, like the list of "changes," drifts between retellings — and a caution against treating any single version as fixed history.
  • Dating. Masekhet Soferim is one of the "minor tractates" appended to the Talmud, generally thought to have originated in the eighth-century Land of Israel and finally redacted about the middle of that century — hence its slot here at c. 750, deliberately placed after the great patristic accusers rather than among them.2
