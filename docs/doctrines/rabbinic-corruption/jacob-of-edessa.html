Jacob of Edessa on the Rabbinic Corruption of Scripture

Dates: c. 692 (life c. 640–708; his Chronicon, a continuation of Eusebius, is dated c. 692, but the chronology charge below belongs to his Old-Testament scholia / Commentary on the Octateuch, the work of his last years) · Wikipedia · back to timeline
Summary: The foremost Syriac scholar of his age — bishop of Edessa, reviser of the Syriac Old Testament, grammarian and chronicler — Jacob of Edessa carries the chronology strand of the corruption charge into the Syriac East, and states its motive more nakedly than any witness between Eusebius and Agapius. Where Eusebius merely "suspected" the Jews had shortened the antediluvian begetting-ages, Jacob names their purpose: the Hebrews, "wanting to pervert the computation of years, in order to show that Christ had not yet arrived," cut a hundred years from the patriarchs before their sons were born. He adds that even some Hebrew sources he had seen preserved the longer (Septuagint) figure — an early form of the modern deflation camp's appeal to external witnesses. The charge reaches us, however, only through a Genesis scholion transmitted by later editors, and under a documented attribution tangle (it was long filed under Ephraem the Syrian before modern scholarship reassigned it to Jacob).

Claims & evidence

The Hebrews "pervert the computation of years, in order to show that Christ had not yet arrived"

"The Hebrews, wanting to pervert the computation of years, in order to show that Christ had not yet arrived, subtract 100 years from Adam before he fathered Seth."

— Jacob of Edessa, scholion on Genesis (from his Commentary on the Octateuch), preserved in G. S. Assemani, Bibliotheca Orientalis I (Rome, 1719), pp. 65–66; English in William Adler, Time Immemorial: Archaic History and Its Sources in Christian Chronography from Julius Africanus to George Syncellus (Washington, DC: Dumbarton Oaks, 1989), p. 48, as reproduced and discussed in Henry B. Smith Jr., "Setting the Record Straight… Part 2".1 This is the chronology strand at its most explicit on the question of intent. The bare textual observation is the Eusebian one — a hundred years stand between the Hebrew and the Greek at each antediluvian generation (Adam begets Seth at 130 in the Hebrew, 230 in the Greek) — but where Eusebius offered a guess for the motive ("to encourage early marriages") and flagged it as a guess, Jacob supplies the motive the later charge would settle on: the deflation was done so that Christ should seem not yet to have come. The Hebrew totals make the world too young for the Messiah's appointed age, and on Jacob's reading that is precisely why they were shortened — the anti-messianic-chronology motive that Julian of Toledo and Agapius would press, here stated a century before Julian's treatise. ⚠ The exact wording reaches us only through Assemani's transmission and Adler's translation, quoted online via Smith; the Genesis scholion has not been consulted in a direct edition (see the attribution note below).

Even some Hebrew sources gave the longer figure — Adam begot Seth at 230

"Jacobus Edessenus, who flourished about A.D. 700, states, that he found in some sufficiently accurate Hebrew histories, that Adam begat Seth when he was 230 years old."

— William Hales, A New Analysis of Chronology, vol. 1 (London, 1830), p. 280 (reporting Jacob), as cited in Smith, "Setting the Record Straight… Part 2".2 Jacob does not rest the charge on the Septuagint alone: he reports that he had found the longer figure (230, the Greek reading) even in Hebrew sources he judged reliable — an early version of the argument the modern deflation camp makes from external witnesses, that a long primeval chronology once circulated in Hebrew too, before the proto-Masoretic figures were reduced. Whatever the worth of Jacob's "Hebrew histories," the move is the same one the chronology crux weighs: that the short Masoretic numbers are secondary to a longer original.

Notes

  • What Jacob adds to the charge. The chronology strand begins with Eusebius, who suspected the Jews "made bold to shorten" the begetting-ages but named only a weak motive ("to encourage early marriages") and marked it a surmise. Jacob's contribution is the explicit anti-Christian motive — the deflation was done "to show that Christ had not yet arrived" — which he states flatly, a full century before Julian of Toledo built a treatise on it and two and a half before Agapius dramatized it as a post-Resurrection plot. He is the earliest of the chronographers to fuse the textual claim and the messianic motive into one sentence.
  • The Ephraem–Jacob attribution tangle. The scholion comes down through Assemani's Bibliotheca Orientalis, where it (and the Syriac material around it) was filed under Ephraem the Syrian; following Assemani, William Hales likewise cited it now under Ephraem, now under Jacob. Modern scholarship (Ben-Zion Wacholder; Bas ter Haar Romeny on Jacob's Genesis work; William Adler) reassigns the chronology material to Jacob of Edessa. The attribution is defensible and now standard, but it is a modern correction of an early misfiling, not a clean reading of a self-evidently-Jacob manuscript — worth keeping in view when the line is pressed.3
  • Not the Chronicon. Jacob's Chronicon — a continuation of Eusebius's canon-tables, c. 692, surviving only in fragments — is not where the charge sits; the accusation belongs to his Genesis scholia / Commentary on the Octateuch, the fruit of his late labour revising the Syriac Old Testament against the Greek. The two works share a date and an author but not the polemic; the timeline anchor here (c. 692) is conventional for this whole phase of his career.
  • On the evidence. The textual question Jacob presses — were the Genesis 5/11 begetting-ages deliberately shortened in the Hebrew? — is genuinely open and is weighed on the Genesis 5/11 chronology crux (assessment: contested). But the motive Jacob makes explicit (anti-messianic chronology) is the half of the thesis that even its modern defenders (Henry B. Smith Jr.; Jeremy Sexton) decline to lean on, arguing the direction of change on text-critical grounds rather than the anti-Christian purpose Jacob assumes. He shares the antediluvian genealogy with the Flood doctrine's timeline.
  • Sourcing. The page cites the scholion as preserved in Assemani's Bibliotheca Orientalis (1719) with Adler's English (Time Immemorial, 1989, p. 48) and Hales's notice (New Analysis, 1830, 1:280) — reproduced and discussed in Smith's Associates for Biblical Research article "Setting the Record Straight on the Primeval Chronology of the Septuagint (Part 2)," which is the accessible online locus. Jacob is named in the same chain on p. 122 of Smith's ICC 2018 paper. Neither Assemani's Latin nor Adler's volume has been read here in a direct edition, so the exact wording above carries a caveat until verified against them.
