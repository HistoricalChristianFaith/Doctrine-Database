Justin Martyr on the Rabbinic Corruption of Scripture

Dates: c. 165 · Wikipedia · back to timeline
Summary: The earliest and fullest patristic statement of the charge. In the Dialogue with Trypho Justin tells his Jewish interlocutor that "your teachers" both disparage the Septuagint of the seventy elders and have physically cut passages out of the Scriptures — specifically the texts that prove Jesus to be the crucified Christ — and that the excision was recent ("only a short time since they were cut out"). He names particular deletions (a Jeremiah logion, a descent oracle, "from the wood" in Psalm 96) and the most famous reading-dispute (Isaiah 7:14, "virgin" vs. "young woman"). It is an accusation of deliberate, anti-Christian tampering, the seed of the whole later tradition — though his own examples are weaker than his rhetoric, several being free-floating testimonia rather than texts the Jews ever held.

Claims & evidence

The Jewish teachers impugn the Septuagint of the seventy elders

"If therefore, I shall show that this prophecy of Isaiah refers to our Christ, and not to Hezekiah, as you say, shall I not in this matter, too, compel you not to believe your teachers, who venture to assert that the explanation which your seventy elders that were with Ptolemy the king of the Egyptians gave, is untrue in certain respects? For some statements in the Scriptures, which appear explicitly to convict them of a foolish and vain opinion, these they venture to assert have not been so written."

— Dialogue with Trypho, ch. 68, historicalchristian.faith. The opening move: the Jewish teachers no longer trust the very Greek translation their own seventy elders made for Ptolemy, and "frame another" reading wherever the Seventy's wording tells against them. Given the tradition that those seventy translators were divinely guided, this is already a charge of turning against an inspired text.

They "have altogether taken away many Scriptures" from the Seventy's translation — including reading Isaiah's "virgin" as "young woman"

"But I am far from putting reliance in your teachers, who refuse to admit that the interpretation made by the seventy elders who were with Ptolemy [king] of the Egyptians is a correct one; and they attempt to frame another. And I wish you to observe, that they have altogether taken away many Scriptures from the translations effected by those seventy elders who were with Ptolemy, and by which this very man who was crucified is proved to have been set forth expressly as God, and man, and as being crucified, and as dying."

— Dialogue with Trypho, ch. 71, historicalchristian.faith. The motive is stated plainly: the removed passages are precisely the ones that "expressly" proved the crucified Jesus to be God and man. In the same chapter Justin names the textual dispute that would become the standard proof-text of the whole controversy — Isaiah 7:14: "you contradict the statement, `Behold, the virgin shall conceive,' and say it ought to be read, `Behold, the young woman shall conceive.'" When Trypho asks for examples — "tell us some of the Scriptures which you allege have been completely cancelled" — Justin obliges in the chapters that follow.

Named excisions, "only a short time since they were cut out": Esdras, Jeremiah, and a descent oracle

"And from the sayings of Jeremiah they have cut out the following: `I [was] like a lamb that is brought to the slaughter: they devised a device against me, saying, Come, let us lay on wood on His bread, and let us blot Him out from the land of the living; and His name shall no more be remembered.' And since this passage from the sayings of Jeremiah is still written in some copies [of the Scriptures] in the synagogues of the Jews (for it is only a short time since they were cut out)… And again, from the sayings of the same Jeremiah these have been cut out: `The Lord God remembered His dead people of Israel who lay in the graves; and He descended to preach to them His own salvation.'"

— Dialogue with Trypho, ch. 72, historicalchristian.faith. Three specific deletions are alleged: a passover saying he ascribes to Esdras ("This passover is our Saviour and our refuge…"), the Jeremiah "lamb led to the slaughter / lay wood on His bread" logion, and a one-line oracle of Christ's descent to the dead. Justin insists the tampering is recent and incomplete — the Jeremiah passage is "still written in some copies… in the synagogues of the Jews," the cutting being only lately done.

"From the wood" cut out of Psalm 96:10 — and Trypho's incredulity

"And from the ninety-fifth (ninety-sixth) Psalm they have taken away this short saying of the words of David: `From the wood.' For when the passage said, `Tell ye among the nations, the Lord hath reigned from the wood,' they have left, `Tell ye among the nations, the Lord hath reigned.'"

— Dialogue with Trypho, ch. 73, historicalchristian.faith. Justin's most-cited single example: the alleged deletion of the words "from the wood" (read as a pointer to the cross) from Psalm 96:10. His Jewish partner does not concede the point. Trypho answers, "Whether [or not] the rulers of the people have erased any portion of the Scriptures, as you affirm, God knows; but it seems incredible" — and Justin grants it "does seem incredible," more horrible than the golden calf, yet presses the charge anyway.

The teachers would have deleted still more had they understood it; and they alter live readings

"…but from those which are even now admitted by you, which had your teachers comprehended, be well assured they would have deleted them, as they did those about the death of Isaiah, whom you sawed asunder with a wooden saw."

— Dialogue with Trypho, ch. 120, historicalchristian.faith. The charge is one of will, not only of deed: the teachers spare a Christological text only where they fail to see its force. And alongside outright deletion Justin alleges altered readings — in Dialogue ch. 137 he sets the Seventy's "Let us take away the righteous, for he is distasteful to us" against "what your version has: `Let us bind the righteous, for he is distasteful to us'" (historicalchristian.faith), quietly correcting the words he had first quoted in their form.

Notes

  • The charge vs. the evidence. Justin frames the divergences as deliberate, recent, anti-Christian excision ("only a short time since they were cut out"). But his concrete examples are uneven: Isaiah 7:14 "virgin/young woman" is a genuine translation dispute (LXX parthenos against the Hebrew almah), while several "excised" texts — the Jeremiah descent oracle especially — appear in no Hebrew or Greek manuscript and have the look of free-floating testimonia rather than passages any synagogue ever held. The modern account (divergent Hebrew Vorlage, not malicious cutting) is weighed at the doctrine's master crux.
  • "From the wood" runs the other way. Justin's flagship example — that the Jews cut "from the wood" from Psalm 96:10 — is, on the text-critical evidence, the reverse of what he charges: the clause is absent from the Hebrew and from the mainstream Greek tradition and surfaces only in Christian-transmitted copies, so it is a Christian addition to some Septuagint manuscripts (read as a prophecy of the cross), not a Jewish deletion from the Hebrew. Trypho's "it seems incredible" registers the implausibility. The point is set out on the master crux.
  • Cross-link. The "descended to preach… in the graves" oracle Justin says the Jews cut from Jeremiah is weighed in its own right on the Intermediate State doctrine — The "excised" descent-prophecy — authentic Scripture cut by the Jews, or an apocryphal logion? (assessment: weak) — which finds it an early, sincere witness to how scriptural the descent already felt, but not in fact an excised text.
  • Justin is a verse-less witness here: the Dialogue argues over whole passages and the integrity of the Septuagint, not a single verse, so he anchors the timeline by date (c. 165) rather than by a commentary verse.
