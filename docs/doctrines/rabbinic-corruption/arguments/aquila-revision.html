Aquila's revision: anti-Christian distortion, or literalizing toward the Hebrew?

Type: supporting argument / crux (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — the charge that Aquila deliberately distorted the words of the Septuagint to defeat the christological proof-texts does not hold on its best available case. Aquila's divergences from the Greek are the predictable output of a thoroughgoing literalizing method that aligns the Greek with the (now-standardized) proto-Masoretic Hebrew — a translation philosophy, prosecuted everywhere in the version (the accusative particle, the etymologies), not a verse-by-verse campaign against Christ; and his single most-cited "anti-Christian" reading, neanis ("young woman") at Isaiah 7:14, is the accurate literal rendering of the standing Hebrew 'almah, which the Septuagint had interpretively glossed parthenos. The genuine kernel — that these literal readings served, and were welcomed in, the rabbinic anti-Christian polemic of Aquila's milieu — is a use and a coloring, not the origin of the version · back to timeline · advanced by Epiphanius of Salamis

Related cruxes: the corruption charge — see also the master corruption charge · the Septuagint legend · the reverse charge · the Simeon-the-Just legend (its hagiographic mirror)

An argument page weighs a single sub-claim adduced in support of a main interpretation — a proof-text, a parallel, a historical thesis — and renders an adversarial assessment of the interpretation it serves. Unlike a person-detail page it sits across the timeline rather than at one date. This crux takes the corruption charge in its narrowest, best-documented form: not anonymous "teachers" mutilating the Hebrew, but a single named translator, Aquila (c. 130), producing a Greek version the synagogue preferred to the Septuagint — and asks whether his divergences are distortion or method. Every factual claim is sourced; the proponent's own words are quoted verbatim.

The claim

"He was moved not by the right motive, but (by the desire) so to distort certain of the words occurring in the translation of the seventy-two that he might proclaim the things testified to about Christ in the divine Scriptures to be fulfilled in some other way, on account of a certain shame that he felt (to proffer) a senseless excuse for himself."

— Epiphanius of Salamis, Treatise on Weights and Measures §15 (c. 392), historicalchristian.faith.1 Epiphanius pronounces the whole version "incorrect and perversely translated," and frames it biographically: Aquila, a baptized Christian expelled from the church for clinging to astrology, turned Jew "embittered in mind… puffed up with vain jealousy," learned Hebrew, and made his revision out of spite. The interpretation the charge serves is that the divergence between Aquila's Greek and the Septuagint is deliberate, anti-Christian textual distortion, aimed precisely at the messianic proof-texts — the reading the rest of the patristic chorus (Irenaeus already, then Philastrius, Chrysostom, Jerome in his sharper moods) takes for granted. The rival reading, which this crux weighs it against, is the modern scholarly one: that Aquila produced a hyper-literal revision toward the proto-Masoretic Hebrew — a translation philosophy, not malice.

Who advances it

The malice reading is the patristic default, not Epiphanius's invention. Irenaeus already singled out the "young woman" of Isaiah 7:14 as the work of "Theodotion the Ephesian… and Aquila of Pontus, both Jewish proselytes" presuming to expound the Scripture against the Septuagint's "virgin" (Against Heresies III.21.1, c. 180); Epiphanius (c. 392) gives it its fullest biographical form; Philastrius of Brescia brands as heretics those who "spit out" the Seventy and prefer Aquila; and Chrysostom charges the post-Christ Jewish translators with "darkening the prophecies on purpose." The dissenting ancient voice is Jerome, who mocks Aquila's literalism yet uses him constantly and concedes his fidelity to the Hebrew. The modern reconstruction that reframes the whole question is anchored in Dominique Barthélemy's Les Devanciers d'Aquila (1963), which set Aquila within a Hebraizing revisional movement (the kaige group) older than and broader than the proof-texts, and in the rabbinic and text-critical scholarship that reads his method as a school discipline of fidelity to the standardized Hebrew.2

The evidence for

The version really did move the Greek away from the readings the Church used, in the era and milieu the charge names. The charge is not a fantasy. Aquila (a Gentile from Pontus, a proselyte, conventionally dated to the twelfth year of Hadrian, c. 128–130) was remembered by both Jerome and the Talmud as a disciple of Rabbi Akiba — the same Akiba who was the soul of the Bar Kokhba revolt and a fierce opponent of the Christians — and he worked in the very generation when the synagogue was settling on a single authoritative Hebrew text and disputing the proof-texts with the Church. His revision did, demonstrably, replace Septuagint readings the Christians leaned on with renderings closer to the rabbinic Hebrew, and at Isaiah 7:14 it put "young woman" where the Church read "virgin." A polemicist who saw a fresh Greek version, made by a convert in Akiba's circle, track the Hebrew away from the christological readings and gain the synagogue's preference was reacting to a real pattern, not an imagined one.3

Adversarial assessment

What holds. Aquila's Greek does diverge from the Septuagint toward the proto-Masoretic Hebrew; he did belong to the rabbinic, anti-Christian milieu of Akiba; and the divergences do fall, among many other places, on the disputed verses. Those literal readings were genuinely useful to the synagogue's side of the proof-text dispute, and there is no reason to doubt they were welcomed for it — the polemical edge the Fathers felt was real.

What is overstated, and what is simply wrong.

  1. The divergences are the output of a method applied to the whole text, not a verse-by-verse assault on Christ. Aquila's governing principle is extreme literalism: he renders the Hebrew word for word, particle for particle, even where it breaks Greek. His signature is the translation of the untranslatable accusative marker 'et by syn ("with") — Jerome ridicules his "syn ton ouranon kai syn tēn gēn" for Genesis 1:1, "which neither Greek nor Latin admits of" — and his habit of construing "not words only but their etymologies as well," so that grain, wine, and oil come out as "pouring," "fruit-gathering," and "shining." A man who mangles Genesis 1:1 and the words for oil and wine in pursuit of the Hebrew letter is not selectively doctoring the messianic verses; he is doing to every verse what the charge imagines him doing to a chosen few. The divergences at the proof-texts are the same fidelity-to-the-Hebrew that produces his unreadable particles — a translation philosophy, visible on every page.4

  2. The literalizing revision predates Aquila and is broader than the polemic. Aquila did not invent the method; he is the culmination of an existing Hebraizing movement. The Greek Minor Prophets scroll from Naḥal Ḥever (8ḤevXIIgr), published by Dominique Barthélemy in Les Devanciers d'Aquila (1963), shows a systematic revision of the Old Greek toward the proto-Masoretic Hebrew already underway in the first century AD — the kaige recension, the "forerunners of Aquila" of his title, worked under the Palestinian rabbinate. Aquila stands at the end of that school tradition, not at the start of a campaign; the very features taken for his malice (the slavish particles included) belong to a revisional program that swept the whole Greek Bible toward the Hebrew, books without a single contested christological verse included. A movement that revises Habakkuk and Jonah as zealously as Isaiah is not explained by anti-Christian intent.5

  3. The flagship example is an accurate translation, not a distortion. At Isaiah 7:14 the standing Hebrew word is 'almah — a young woman of marriageable age; virginity is connoted, not denoted. The Septuagint chose the interpretive parthenos ("virgin"); Aquila rendered the same unchanged Hebrew word by its ordinary literal equivalent, neanis ("young woman") — which is, lexically, the more exact rendering. Nothing in the Hebrew was altered; the dispute is over how to translate a word, and on the narrow philological question Aquila is closer to the Hebrew than the version he revised. To call the more literal of two renderings a "distortion of the words" inverts the case: it is the Septuagint's parthenos that reads doctrine into the Hebrew, and Aquila's neanis that follows it. (That this is a translation question, not a textual corruption of the Hebrew, is why the master charge mis-files it; here it is the test case of method versus malice.)6

  4. The Church's own scholars used Aquila for the very fidelity the charge calls malice. Origen gave Aquila a column in the Hexapla and prized him precisely because his slavish literalism let a reader recover the Hebrew behind the Greek; Jerome, who jeers at the particles, reads and cites Aquila throughout and grants his closeness to the Hebrew; and Epiphanius himself — in the same treatise that calls Aquila "perversely translated" — appeals to him (with Symmachus and Theodotion) to recover Hebrew words the Seventy had omitted, treating his version as a corrective to the Septuagint. A version that the Fathers reach for whenever they want the Hebrew is doing something other than hiding it. The accuser who uses Aquila as a check on the Greek has half-conceded that the divergence is one of method.7

Assessment

Weak. The interpretation Epiphanius's charge serves — that Aquila deliberately distorted the words of the Seventy out of anti-Christian malice, to reroute the messianic proof-texts — does not hold on its best available case. What should be trusted is the kernel: Aquila's revision really did move the Greek toward the proto-Masoretic Hebrew and away from the readings the Church used; he really did belong to the rabbinic, anti-Christian milieu of Rabbi Akiba; and his literal readings really were serviceable to the synagogue's side of the proof-text dispute, and welcomed there. What must be flagged is the leap from that kernel to "distortion." Aquila's divergences are the uniform product of a thoroughgoing literalizing method — particle for particle, etymology for etymology — applied to every book of the Greek Bible, contested verses and uncontested alike; the method is older than Aquila and broader than the polemic (Barthélemy's kaige revision, already at work in the first century under the Palestinian rabbinate); his single most-cited "anti-Christian" reading, neanis at Isaiah 7:14, is the more accurate rendering of the unchanged Hebrew word, not a tampering with it; and the Church's own scholars (Origen, Jerome, even Epiphanius) used Aquila precisely because of his fidelity to the Hebrew. The charge mistakes the predictable byproduct of a translation philosophy for a targeted campaign — and frames it, in Epiphanius, with a hostile biography (the embittered apostate) that the version's systematic literalism does not need and does not support. So the distortion reading is weak; the legitimate-literalizing reading is sound; and the honest residue is only that a defensible literal version, made in a hostile milieu, was doubtless preferred for readings that happened to cut against the Christian proof — a use of the version, not its genesis.
