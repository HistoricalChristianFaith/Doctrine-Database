The reverse charge: did the Jews accuse the Christians of corrupting Scripture?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — as a symmetric mirror of the Christian charge it is weak: the accusation of deliberate textual tampering ran overwhelmingly in one direction (Christian against Jew), and the Jewish counter took a different shape — the Septuagint legend (the divergences are the translators' own doing, not corruption) and, above all, a charge of Christian misinterpretation and mistranslation, not mutilation of the shared Hebrew. Where a Jewish counter was textual it was rare, late (medieval), and — at its one clearest point (Psalm 96:10's "from the wood") — actually right · counter to the charge advanced by Justin Martyr, Epiphanius, and Agapius

Related arguments: the corruption charge — see also the master charge (the Christian thesis this one mirrors) · the Septuagint legend (the Jewish tradition's own, non-malicious account of the divergences) · the Aquila revision · the tikkune soferim (the charge's strongest in-house evidence).

This argument weighs the mirror image of the doctrine's master charge: not "did the Jews corrupt the text?" but "did the Jews bring the same accusation back — that the Christians corrupted it?"

The claim

The doctrine's master charge — that post-Second-Temple rabbinic Judaism deliberately altered the Hebrew text to deny Christ — invites an obvious question of symmetry. If Christians said the Jews mutilated Scripture, did the Jews return the charge: that it was the Christians who tampered with the text? The question is whether, and how, that counter-accusation actually ran in the sources. The interpretation being weighed is the affirmative: that there was a genuine, symmetric Jewish charge of Christian textual corruption — that the polemic over the diverging texts was a two-way exchange of "you falsified Scripture," each side accusing the other of the same crime.

Who advances it

No single proponent states the symmetric thesis as baldly as the master charge is stated on the Christian side; it is rather the natural framing a modern reader supplies — and the way the dispute is sometimes flattened in popular retellings — when the diverging texts are described as a mutual accusation. The serious scholarship that maps the actual shape of the exchange is Irven M. Resnick, "The Falsification of Scripture and Medieval Christian and Jewish Polemics" (Medieval Encounters 2.3, 1996), which traces both the Christian charge and the Jewish counter across the Middle Ages, and the standard survey of the medieval Jewish polemical literature, David Berger's edition of the Nizzahon Vetus (The Jewish-Christian Debate in the High Middle Ages, 1979). The broad asymmetry is also the explicit finding of the master secondary source for this whole doctrine, the Wassersteins' Legend of the Septuagint.1

The evidence for

A real Jewish counter did exist, and it grew. The Jews were not silent under the corruption charge. From the very first recorded debate they answered it, and by the high Middle Ages a whole polemical literature (Sefer Yosef ha-Meqanne; Milḥamot ha-Shem; the Nizzaḥon Vetus) systematically rebutted the Christian reading of the Hebrew Bible verse by verse. Resnick documents that part of why the Christian falsification charge eventually lost its polemical usefulness was precisely that "Jews… turned the charge of falsification against Christians themselves" — so a reverse accusation is genuinely attested, not invented. Resnick adds two further ironies that cut the same way: the Christian conspiracy-claim presupposed a uniformity among Jewish copies of Scripture that was conspicuously wanting in the Christians' own variant Greek and Latin copies; and as the Church's polemical front turned eastward, Christians found themselves defending the textual integrity of the Hebrew Bible against the Muslim charge of falsification (taḥrīf) — the same charge they had leveled at the Jews now turned on the whole biblical tradition they shared.2

And at one point the reverse charge is simply correct. The Christian side's own flagship example backfires: Justin's "the Jews erased `from the wood' from Psalm 96:10" is, by the modern text-critical judgment, the reverse of what happened — the clause is a Christian addition to some Greek copies, read as a prophecy of the cross, absent from the Hebrew and the mainstream Greek alike. On that verse a Jew who said "it is you who added to the text" would have been right. The general structural point — that the Christians' Greek and Latin had drifted from the Hebrew — was even conceded, and acted on, from within the Church by Jerome, whose whole program of the Hebraica veritas ranks the synagogue's Hebrew above the Christians' Greek and goes behind the Greek to correct it.3

Adversarial assessment

What holds. There was a Jewish counter, it is well attested, and on at least one textual point it lands. The exchange was not one party talking. What fails is the symmetry: that the counter was a mirror-image charge of deliberate textual mutilation, running as loud and in the same key as the Christian charge it answered. On three counts the picture is asymmetric, and the asymmetry is the finding.

  1. The tampering charge ran overwhelmingly one way. The accusation that the other side maliciously altered the text is, in the sources, a Christian instrument — frequent, formulaic, and old, with its own technical vocabulary of excision (Justin alone deploys it across the Dialogue; the Wassersteins note it as "a frequent accusation brought by early Christian writers… that they were… maliciously tampering with its text"). There is no early-Jewish counterpart of comparable volume or fixity. The Jewish side did not, in antiquity, mount a developed thesis that the Christians had cut and rewritten the Hebrew Scriptures — not least because the Hebrew text was in Jewish hands, so the charge would have had to be that Christians corrupted their own Greek, a narrower and rarer claim.4

  2. The Jewish explanation of the divergences was different in kind — and non-malicious. Asked why the Greek differs from the Hebrew, the Jewish tradition's own answer was not "the Christians changed it" but the Septuagint legend: the seventy-two translators themselves, shut in their cells, deliberately (and, in the telling, providentially) altered a set list of passages for Ptolemy. That account locates the change in the translators, sanctions it from God, and predates the Christian dispute entirely — the opposite of an accusation that Christians corrupted the text. Where antiquity's Jews did brand Christian writings as falsehood, the target was the gospels, not the shared Old Testament: the Tannaitic ruling that the gilyonim and "the books of the minim" be let burn even with the divine Names in them (and the rabbinic pun turning euangelion into 'awon gillayon, "sin of the margin"). That is a charge of heresy against new books, not of mutilation of the old text both shared.5

  3. What the Jews actually charged was misreading, not mutilation. From the first recorded debate the Jewish answer to the proof-texts is a charge of mistranslation and misinterpretation, not textual corruption. Justin reports his opponents' objection to Isaiah 7:14 in exactly these terms: "you and your teachers venture to affirm that in the prophecy of Isaiah it is not said, `Behold, the virgin shall conceive,' but, `Behold, the young woman shall conceive…' and you explain the prophecy as if it referred to Hezekiah" — a quarrel over what the Greek word should be and whom the oracle means, with the consonantal Hebrew unchanged and undisputed. Tellingly, when Justin presses the textual form of the mirror charge — that the rulers erased "from the wood" — Trypho will not take it up: "Whether the rulers of the people have erased any portion of the Scriptures, as you affirm, God knows; but it seems incredible." The Jewish disputant declines the very symmetry this argument tests. The medieval polemical anthologies keep that shape: the Nizzaḥon Vetus and its kin attack christological exegesis as forced and the Christians' Latin and Greek as mistranslations of the Hebrew — a charge that the Christians read and rendered the text wrongly, not that they physically falsified it.6

The one place the reverse charge turns textual, it is sound — which deepens, not relieves, the asymmetry. The honest residue is striking: where a Jewish counter was a charge of Christian textual interference — Psalm 96:10's "from the wood," and the cluster of free-floating "testimonies" the Fathers ascribed to the prophets and the Jews to no canonical book — modern scholarship sides with the Jewish side. So the rarer, quieter reverse charge is, at its one clearest textual point, better grounded than the loud charge it answers. The asymmetry, then, is double: the Christians accused more often and more sweepingly, and on the flagship cases they were the ones who had added to the text.7

Assessment

Weak — as the symmetric thesis it states. The accusation of deliberate textual tampering did not run both ways in equal measure; it was overwhelmingly a Christian charge against the Jews, with its own vocabulary and a centuries-long pedigree, and no early-Jewish mirror of comparable force. The Jewish side answered the diverging texts in two other registers instead: by the Septuagint legend, which credits the divergences to the translators' own (God-sanctioned) work rather than to anyone's malice, and — far more substantially — by a sustained charge of Christian misinterpretation and mistranslation, voiced already by Trypho over Isaiah 7:14 and systematized in the medieval polemical anthologies. A genuine counter-charge of Christian textual falsification does surface, but late (the high-medieval reversal Resnick documents) and as one factor among several that blunted the Christian charge, not as an ancient symmetric accusation. What should be trusted is the qualified version: the Jews did push back, mostly on the ground of reading and rendering, and at the one point where the reverse charge becomes textual (Psalm 96:10's "from the wood" as a Christian addition) it is actually correct. What should be flagged is the tidy picture of two sides trading the identical accusation: that symmetry is a modern projection. The most useful way to fold this into the doctrine is as the present member of the "Related arguments — the corruption charge" group — the mirror held up to the master charge, showing the polemic's real shape: not a balanced duel over a mutilated text, but one loud charge of mutilation answered, for the most part, by a charge of misreading — and rebutted, at its flagship example, by the discovery that the addition was Christian.
