Were the Genesis 5/11 patriarchal lifespans shortened (MT) or inflated (LXX) — and was it tampering?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — the direction of the divergence (did the Hebrew deflate, or the Greek inflate?) is a genuinely open text-critical problem, and a serious modern camp revives the very deliberate-shortening reading the Fathers intuited; but the patristic motive (Eusebius's guess, "to encourage early marriages") is conjecture — and even the modern camp's argued anti-Christian messianic-chronology motive stays short of proof — the text-critical mainstream leans the other way (Septuagint inflation), and the divergence is demonstrably older than the Christian–Jewish polemic · advanced across the timeline by Eusebius, Julian of Toledo, and Agapius · cross-links the Flood doctrine's antediluvian genealogy

Related arguments: the Genesis chronology — see also The "second Cainan" — original reading, or a secondary plus?

An argument page weighs a single sub-claim adduced in support of a main interpretation — a proof-text, a parallel, a historical thesis — and renders an adversarial assessment of the interpretation it serves. Unlike a person-detail page it sits across the timeline rather than at one date. This argument isolates the one strand of the master corruption charge that rests on a real and still-unsettled text-critical problem: the divergent numbers of the Genesis 5 and 11 genealogies. Every factual claim is sourced; the proponents' own words are quoted verbatim.

The claim

"It is only the number of years before their sons were born which is shorter in the Jewish copies. Therefore we suspect that this was something which the Jews did. They made bold to shorten the time before the fathering of children to encourage early marriages."

— Eusebius of Caesarea, Chronicon Book 1 (c. 325), historicalchristian.faith (trans. from the Armenian) — the patristic root of the thesis.1 Eusebius set the Septuagint, Hebrew, and Samaritan reckonings of Genesis 5–11 side by side, observed that the Greek and Hebrew agree in each patriarch's total lifespan but differ — by a patterned hundred years apiece — in the begetting-ages before the flood (so that Adam-to-flood comes to 2,242 years in the Greek against 1,656 in the Hebrew, a 586-year gap), and inferred that the shorter Hebrew figures were a deliberate Jewish abridgement. Three and a half centuries later Julian of Toledo built a whole anti-Jewish treatise on the same gap (the Hebrew totals make the world too young for Messiah's sixth age to have arrived), and Agapius of Hierapolis (c. 942) gave it its fullest narrative form: Annas and Caiaphas, he says, "cut off from the history from the world, from Adam to the time of Abraham, 1389 years," so the Jews could argue the Messiah "would come only in the last days… we are still in the middle of the days of the world."2 The thesis has two separable parts: a textual claim (the Hebrew numbers were shortened, the Greek preserves the older reading) and a motive claim (the shortening was deliberate and aimed against the Christian reckoning of the Messiah's time).

Who advances it

On the timeline the chronology strand runs from Eusebius (its inventor) through Julian of Toledo to Agapius, and is the half of the master corruption charge that touches a live text-critical question rather than a settled one. In the modern debate the deliberate-shortening reading has — strikingly — been revived, not by polemicists but by a conservative chronology camp: Henry B. Smith Jr. (Associates for Biblical Research) and Jeremy Sexton argue that the Septuagint preserves most of the original numbers and that the proto-Masoretic figures are the result of a "deliberate and systematic post–AD 70" deflation by the rabbis — the patristic thesis, restated in text-critical terms.3 Against them stand two opposed camps. The text-critical mainstream (Ronald Hendel, Emanuel Tov, John Wevers) traces the hundred-year differences to the Septuagint's Hebrew Vorlage rather than the translators, and generally judges the longer Greek (and the Samaritan) figures to be the secondary, schematically inflated ones — the reverse of the patristic charge. And a traditional Masoretic-priority camp (the Answers Research Journal line) likewise holds the Septuagint and Samaritan numbers inflated and the Masoretic Text superior. The result is a three-way standoff with no consensus on which witness preserves the original.4

The evidence for

The divergence is real, patterned, and almost certainly intentional on someone's part. This is not a case of random scribal drift. Across Genesis 5 and 11 the begetting-ages of the patriarchs differ between the traditions by a clean, recurring hundred years, and the totals are wildly far apart — from Adam to Abraham the Masoretic Text yields about 2,008 years, the Samaritan Pentateuch 2,249, and the primary Septuagint manuscripts about 3,394, a gap of nearly fourteen centuries. Numbers that diverge by so exact and so systematic an interval did not arise by accident; some deliberate, schematic editing of the chronology happened in at least one of the traditions. That much is common ground across the otherwise-warring camps.5

The "shortening" reading has a sharp internal-consistency argument in its favour. Smith's case for Masoretic deflation (rather than Septuagint inflation) turns on the shape of the discrepancies: the begetting-ages that were not reduced are precisely the ones whose reduction would have produced a visible contradiction with other figures in the text (Methuselah, Lamech, Noah, Shem, Terah), as though an editor deflated the chronology while carefully avoiding changes that would betray the tampering — whereas a would-be inflator of the Greek had no reason to leave Jared's, Methuselah's, and Lamech's ages un-inflated. He further notes external witnesses to a long Hebrew chronology already circulating in and before the first century, and presses that no inflation hypothesis has produced a coherent motive for adding the years. The argument is serious, has not been simply refuted, and is the reason the directional question remains genuinely open after seventeen centuries.6

A long chronology is independently attested — in Hebrew as well as Greek sources — from before the Christian era, so the divergence is not a post–AD-70 invention. The strongest part of the case is external rather than internal. The long (Septuagint-type) primeval reckoning is already on record well before any Christian–Jewish dispute could have shaped it: Demetrius the Chronographer (c. 220 BC), the earliest Hellenistic-Jewish chronographer, computes from the long figures (preserved in Clement and Eusebius), and Eupolemus (c. 160 BC) — a Palestinian Jew, on the usual identification the priestly envoy of 1 Maccabees 8 — works from a similarly long chronology in his On the Kings in Judea (Fragment 5, quoted in Clement's Stromata). More pointedly for the Hebrew side, the first-century Liber Antiquitatum Biblicarum (Pseudo-Philo), a Hebrew-composed retelling of Genesis, carries the longer Genesis 5 begetting-ages — a witness Smith calls "devastating" to the shorter Masoretic figures — and Josephus (Antiquities 1), working from a Hebrew Vorlage, likewise reports the longer ante-diluvian ages on the analysis of Thackeray and Nodet. The reckoning the early Christian chronographers inherited (Theophilus of Antioch, d. c. 183; Julius Africanus, c. 170–240) was this same long one. The force of the point is limited but real: it does not by itself prove the long numbers original — the mainstream can still hold that a longer Vorlage had already been inflated by the second century BC — but it does show the two chronologies were diverging, in both languages and in Hebrew-based texts, long before the rabbis the charge accuses could have touched anything.7

Adversarial assessment

What holds. Unlike the rest of the master charge, this strand is not closed against its proponents. The divergence is real and patterned; deliberate, systematic editing of the chronology by someone is more probable than innocent accident; which of the three witnesses preserves the original numbers is a debate seventeen centuries old and still unsettled; and a serious modern scholarly camp (Smith–Sexton) actively defends the very reading the Fathers reached — that the Hebrew was the text that was shortened. To that extent Eusebius was reacting to a real phenomenon and reaching a conclusion that remains, on the bare direction-of-change, live.

What is overstated or runs the other way.

  1. The text-critical mainstream leans the opposite direction. Hendel, Tov, and Wevers attribute the hundred-year differences to the Septuagint's Hebrew Vorlage and generally treat the longer Greek (and Samaritan) numbers as the secondary, schematically inflated ones — often a +100 added to push begetting-ages past the round century. On the majority text-critical reading it is the Greek that grew, not the Hebrew that was cut. The deliberate-deflation thesis is thus a minority position, not the established finding; the most one can claim is a genuinely contested question, not a vindicated charge.8

  2. The motive divides into a patristic guess and a modern argued case — and only the former is pure conjecture. Eusebius offers his "to encourage early marriages" as an explicit guess ("we suspect"), and Agapius's "so the Messiah would seem not yet due" is a narrative motive supplied centuries after the fact; both are conjecture. But it is not true that no motive can be grounded. The modern deflation camp supplies a developed, sourced one — rabbinic messianic chronology. The Seder Olam Rabbah (c. AD 140–160, attributed to R. Yose b. Halafta) fixes Creation at 3761 BC, which on the contemporary "anno mundi 4000 = messianic age" scheme (Talmud, Sanhedrin 97a) placed the expected advent around AD 240; after the Bar Kochba collapse (c. 135) the rabbis had a live incentive to push the messianic clock later and to re-read Daniel 9:24–27 toward the Temple's destruction rather than toward Christ — and deflating the primeval begetting-ages by a patterned hundred years apiece does exactly that, lowering the world's reckoned age and so deferring the 4000-year mark. So Smith's camp argues an anti-Christian messianic-chronology purpose from evidence, not the bare direction of change alone. This is a real, argued motive — but it stays a case, not a proof: it presupposes the deflation reading it is meant to motivate (itself contested), and it must reckon with the Samaritan Pentateuch's pre-Christian short figures, which no messianic plot explains. The honest finding is two-part — Eusebius's "early marriages" is conjecture; the modern camp's messianic-chronology motive is argued and serious but unproven.9

  3. The Samaritan Pentateuch breaks the "rabbinic plot" framing. The shorter antediluvian begetting-ages are not unique to the rabbinic Hebrew: the Samaritan Pentateuch also carries low figures in Genesis 5, paralleling the Book of Jubilees (c. 160–150 BC). A short chronology therefore existed in a pre-Christian, non-rabbinic text-tradition — so "short = a post-AD-70 rabbinic deflation against Christ" cannot be the whole story. Any account of the divergence has to reckon with a third line that no one accuses of anti-Christian malice.10

  4. On the one name the charge specifies, the direction reverses. Agapius's most concrete example is the "second Cainan" — a Kainan inserted between Arpachshad and Shelah in Genesis 11, which he says the Jews cut out of the Hebrew. But this Cainan is absent from the Masoretic Text, the Samaritan Pentateuch, 1 Chronicles, Josephus, Philo, the Vulgate, and — tellingly — from Eusebius's own chronicle; it stands only in the Septuagint, Jubilees, Demetrius, and most copies of Luke 3:36, and is widely judged a secondary plus (a harmonizing insertion, rejected as an interpolation by Africanus, Eusebius, and Jerome). The flagship "deletion," in other words, is more likely a Greek-side addition — the same reversal that undoes Justin's Psalm 96:10 example on the master argument. (The Kainan question is weighed in full — including the modern originality case of Smith and Sarfati, and why even granting it yields no deliberate deletion — on the dedicated "second Cainan" argument.)11

  5. The divergence predates the polemic, and the Dead Sea Scrolls do not settle it. A long Greek chronology of LXX type is attested already in Demetrius the Chronographer (late third century BC), before any Christian–Jewish dispute — so whatever the direction of change, the two reckonings were diverging centuries before Annas and Caiaphas could have touched anything, which refutes Agapius's "after the Resurrection" dating outright. At the same time, the Qumran biblical scrolls — decisive for the other divergences the master charge rests on (4QSamuela; the two Hebrew editions of Jeremiah) — happen not to preserve the Genesis 5/11 begetting-ages in a way that adjudicates between the traditions. That gap in the physical evidence is exactly why this strand, alone among the corruption charge's planks, stays open.12

Assessment

Contested. The interpretation this strand serves — that the Genesis 5/11 chronology was deliberately altered, and the Hebrew is the text that was shortened — does not resolve cleanly either way, and that is the honest finding. What should be trusted: the divergence is real and too patterned to be accidental, so deliberate schematic editing by someone is the likely explanation; the direction of that editing is a genuinely unsettled text-critical problem, and the Masoretic-deflation reading the Fathers intuited has a serious modern defense (Smith, Sexton) that the field has not refuted. What must be flagged: the text-critical mainstream (Hendel, Tov, Wevers) leans the other way — Septuagint inflation of a longer Greek Vorlage, not Hebrew deletion — so the deflation thesis is a strong minority position, not the established result; the Samaritan Pentateuch's pre-Christian short figures show a short chronology that owes nothing to anti-Christian malice; the "second Cainan" the charge names as a deletion is more likely a Greek-side addition; and the divergence is demonstrably older than the Christian–Jewish polemic, which kills the post-Resurrection-plot framing whatever the direction. On the motive, distinguish two things: Eusebius's "to encourage early marriages" and Agapius's "so the Messiah would seem not yet due" are bare conjecture, but the modern deflation camp does ground an argued purpose — an anti-Christian messianic chronology (the post–Bar Kochba rabbis deferring the anno mundi 4000 messianic mark and re-reading Daniel 9 away from Christ) — that is serious yet still unproven, because it presupposes the very deflation reading it would motivate. So the textual question is contested and live; the deliberate-anti-messianic-tampering reading specifically has an argued motive but remains short of proof. This argument is the one plank of the master charge (overall weak) that rests on a real and open problem, and it shares its subject matter — the antediluvian genealogy of Genesis 5 — with the Flood doctrine's timeline.
