Did the Jews remove genuine Scripture — not merely reject it — including books testifying to Christ (Wisdom 18:15)?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — for the interpretation as stated, that post-Second-Temple Judaism excised whole books of genuine Scripture, among them deuterocanonical books bearing clear testimony to Christ (the flagship being Wisdom 18:15's "almighty word leaped down from heaven"). Two true things underlie it — the Fathers really do bring a removal charge (Justin, Origen), and Wisdom 18:15 really does carry a venerable christological reading-tradition in the Latin church — but the interpretation overshoots both. The patristic charge is of removed passages, not removed books; the "books" version founders on the modern finding that there was no closed Hebrew canon in the apostolic age for anyone to remove the deuterocanon from (the rabbinic stream settled narrower, it did not excise an established wider canon), and Wisdom — composed in Greek and never in the Hebrew Bible — was never the Jews' to drop; its christological reading is a typological application laid over a plain sense (the destroying word at the Passover/Exodus) that the same expositors keep

(weak), which weighs the removal of passages (Justin's excised logia) and the altered readings; the mirror canon question on the companion timeline — Did the Jews reject the deuterocanon — was the first-century Jewish canon closed and Hebrew-only? Here the sub-claim turns the more common "the Jews rejected the deuterocanon" on its head: the patristic counter-charge that the Jews removed genuine Scripture, books with such plain testimony to Christ that they had to go — Wisdom 18:15 the showpiece.

The claim

The popular case for the shorter Protestant Old Testament leans on the formula "the Jews themselves rejected the Apocrypha" — weighed, and found weak as stated, on the canon timeline. The argument here is the answering move from the other side: that "rejected" is too mild, that the Fathers charge something stronger — the Jews removed what had been Scripture. The point is pressed two ways. First, a roster of Fathers is said to charge active excision rather than mere non-reception: "more like the Jews removed — Justin, Origen, Isidore." Second, the removed material is said to include books with unmistakable witness to Christ, the showpiece being Wisdom 18:14–15:

"For while all things were in quiet silence, and the night was in the midst of her swift course, thine Almighty word leaped down from heaven out of thy royal throne, as a fierce man of war into the midst of a land of destruction."

Read by Christians from antiquity as a prophecy of the Word's coming down from heaven at the Incarnation — and embedded as such in the Roman Christmas liturgy — the verse is offered as the kind of plain christological testimony a hostile Judaism had motive to suppress: a book that says this could not be allowed to stand, so it was cut.1

Who advances it

The proponents. The "removed, not merely rejected" framing is a staple of contemporary Catholic and Orthodox apologetics answering the Protestant canon case, and it does rest on genuine patristic texts. Justin Martyr (c. 165) charges that the Jewish teachers "have altogether taken away many Scriptures" from the Greek of the Seventy — naming particular excisions and insisting "it is only a short time since they were cut out." Origen (c. 240), defending the Greek Susanna and Bel against Julius Africanus, says the Jewish leaders "hid from the knowledge of the people" passages that told against their elders, some surviving only in the apocrypha. To these a third name, Isidore of Seville (c. 636), is often added. The Wisdom-18:15 prong is urged in popular apologetics — e.g. a widely-circulated video arguing that "several [early Christian Fathers] clearly quote from / interpret that passage as prophecy" of Christ, so that its removal looks motivated.2

The adversaries. Protestant and a broad swath of mainstream canon scholarship answer that "removed" imports an event that did not happen: there was no fixed, closed Hebrew canon in the apostolic age from which the deuterocanon could be removed, so the divergence is non-reception of books a Hebrew-measured stream never counted, not the excision of an established wider canon. On the Wisdom prong they answer that the verse's plain sense is the destroying "word" of the tenth Egyptian plague, and that a book composed in Greek and never in the Hebrew Bible cannot have been the Jews' to drop.

The evidence for the affirmative reading

The Fathers really do bring a removal charge — and it is not nothing. Justin's accusation is verbatim that of excision ("taken away," "cut out"), and he names the missing items: a Jeremiah logion ("Come, let us lay on wood on His bread"), a one-line descent oracle, the Esdras passover saying, the "from the wood" of Psalm 96:10. Origen, pressed by Africanus that the Susanna story stands in no Hebrew Daniel, answers not by surrendering it but by charging suppression: the leaders "hid from the knowledge of the people as many of the passages which contained any scandal against the elders, rulers, and judges, as they could," preserved now only "in uncanonical writings (Apocrypha)" — and he refuses, on the same principle, to "reject as spurious the copies in use in our Churches" and beg the synagogue for texts "untampered with."3 And there is a real Jewish act in the background that the charge can point to: the rabbis ruled against a book in actual use by name — the Tosefta decrees that "the books of Ben Sira and all books written from then on do not defile the hands" — a ruling that presupposes some Jews were treating Sirach as Scripture, which the Talmud itself goes on quoting as one. So a stream of Judaism did, at some point, actively exclude books others had received.4

The christological reading of Wisdom 18:15 is venerable, not invented. The application of the "almighty word" leaping down from the royal throne to the Word's coming at the Incarnation is no modern apologetic flourish: it is the settled reading of the Latin church. Bonaventure takes the passage to describe "the coming of Christ in the flesh… called the almighty word of God through eternal generation… said to have come from heaven, from the royal thrones, through the temporal assumption of flesh," glossing "in the silence of midnight" by the fullness of time of Galatians 4. Rabanus Maurus reads the "word of the Lord" here as "the Son of God, of whom John says, ‘In the beginning was the Word.’" Above all, the church prayed the verse at Christmas: the Roman Introit Dum medium silentium for the Sunday within the Octave of the Nativity is Wisdom 18:14–15 nearly verbatim, and Allen Cabaniss could title a 1956 study of it "an early Christmas text." A book read this devotedly for Christ does look, to the proponent, exactly like one a hostile Judaism would want gone.5

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The Fathers do level a removal charge, in their own words; Origen and Justin are real witnesses to it, not later constructions. A Jewish stream did rule books out of use that other Jews had treated as Scripture (Sirach by name). And Wisdom 18:15 carries a genuine, ancient, liturgically-anchored christological reading-tradition — which this wiki does not discount merely because it is a fulfilled-prophecy claim. To that extent the proponent is standing on something.

Where the interpretation, as stated, gives way. The claim needs the Jews to have removed whole books of genuine Scripture, among them Wisdom, because of their testimony to Christ. On that it fails — on four counts.

  1. The patristic charge is of removed passages, not removed books. Justin's list is a set of verses — a Jeremiah line, a descent oracle, "from the wood" — not a single deuterocanonical book; that charge is weighed (and found weak) on the deliberate-corruption page, where the Dead Sea Scrolls show the divergent readings already in pre-Christian Hebrew copies and the flagship "from the wood" turns out to be a Christian addition, not a Jewish deletion. Origen's charge, likewise, is of suppressed narratives embarrassing to Jewish elders (Susanna; the sawing of Isaiah) — and his stated motive is the elders' shame, not the prophecies of Christ. To carry either over to "the Jews cut whole books of the deuterocanon out of the canon" is a category slide: the witnesses named for the strong claim made the narrow one.6

  2. There was no closed Hebrew canon to remove the deuterocanon from. "Removed" presupposes an established list these books once stood in and were cut out of. The leading canon scholarship denies that list existed in the apostolic age: no Jewish canon, Palestinian or Alexandrian, was yet closed in the first century (Sundberg, Lim, McDonald); the Hebrew-only table is a post-70 rabbinic settlement that hardened narrower rather than excised wider. What the rabbinic ruling against Sirach shows is exclusion-in-process — a stream declining to count a book some Jews used — which is precisely non-reception, the thing the strong "removal" framing was meant to escape. The honest description is the one the mirror canon page reaches from the other side: not "rejected from a closed canon," not "removed from a closed canon," but "never jointly canonized while the canon was still open."7

  3. Wisdom was never the Jews' Hebrew book to drop — and its christological sense is typological, not the plain one. The Wisdom of Solomon was composed in Greek, in Alexandria, in the last century or so before Christ; it has no Hebrew original and never stood in the rabbinic Hebrew canon, so there was nothing to "cut out." And 18:15's plain sense, in its own chapter, is not the Incarnation but the tenth plague: Wisdom 18:5–19 retells the slaying of Egypt's firstborn at the Passover, and the "almighty word" that "leaped down… as a fierce man of war… and filled all things with death" is the destroying agent of that night. The same expositors who read it of Christ say so plainly: Bonaventure's literal gloss on the verse is "into the land of Egypt to be destroyed by the death of the firstborn," and his christological reading he marks expressly "allegorically"; the liturgy's own homiletic tradition grants the "original context… is not about the Nativity." The christological application is a real and edifying typology — but a typology read onto the text is no evidence that the text was removed for it, and the nearest the New Testament itself comes to Wisdom 18 is Hebrews 4:12's echo of the "sharp sword" word of judgment, not a messianic citation.8

  4. The witness-list overreaches at Isidore. Of the three names marshaled, Isidore of Seville does not in fact charge the Jews with removing Scripture. He relays the Septuagint inspiration-legend at full strength, lists the later Greek versions without a word of malice, assigns the one textual "corruption" he names to the gentiles (healed by Ezra), and ranks Jerome's Hebrew-based Vulgate "truer" than the Greek — the inverse of the corruption thesis. He is, as the timeline notes, the witness who held every ingredient of the charge and inferred none of it. Enlisting him for "the Jews removed Scripture" does not survive reading him.9

Proponent overreach to flag. The slide is from a defensible narrow claim — the Fathers charged the Jews with cutting certain passages, and a rabbinic stream excluded certain books from use — to an indefensible broad one: a deliberate, christologically-motivated excision of whole genuine books of Scripture, Wisdom among them. The narrow claim is real; the broad one imports a closed canon that did not yet exist and a Hebrew Wisdom that never existed. And the Wisdom-18:15 prong proves the reading-tradition, not the removal: that Christians cherished the verse for Christ tells us how the church read it, not that the synagogue cut it. Origen on this page, and Isidore on his, carry the flag for what the sources actually say.

Note: Origen's removal charge in the Letter to Africanus

Because Origen is the proponent's strongest single witness, his words repay reading in full. Julius Africanus had written to object that the story of Susanna (in the Greek Daniel) could not be genuine Scripture, being absent from the Hebrew. Origen's reply concedes the absence but reverses the inference: the Greek tradition preserves what the Hebrew custodians dropped. "They hid from the knowledge of the people," he writes, "as many of the passages which contained any scandal against the elders, rulers, and judges, as they could, some of which have been preserved in uncanonical writings (Apocrypha)" — instancing the sawing-asunder of Isaiah, "found in none of their public books," which "probably the Jews have purposely tampered with." Yet two cautions hold his testimony short of the strong claim built on it. His charged motive is the elders' shame, not the suppression of christological prophecy. And the books he is defending — Susanna, Bel — he is defending precisely as the Church's, against a Christian (Africanus) who doubted them: the dispute he answers is internal, over what the Church should hold, and his great principle is conservative, not accusatory — that the Church must keep "the copies in use in our Churches" rather than re-edit its Bible to the synagogue's. That principle cuts as much against "go correct our text by the Hebrew" as against "the Jews mutilated the Hebrew." Origen is a genuine witness to a removal charge; he is not a witness to the wholesale-deuterocanon-excision version of it.10

Assessment

Weak — for the interpretation as stated. That post-Second-Temple Judaism removed whole books of genuine Scripture, the deuterocanon among them, and did so because books like Wisdom testified too plainly to Christ, does not hold on its best case. The patristic "removal" charge the argument recruits is real but narrow — of passages (Justin's logia, Origen's suppressed scandal-narratives), not of whole books, and Origen's stated motive is the elders' shame, not christology. The "whole books" version needs a closed Hebrew canon the deuterocanon was cut out of, and the leading scholarship finds no such closed canon in the apostolic age — the rabbinic stream settled narrower, it did not excise an established wider canon, so this is non-reception, not removal. Wisdom in particular was composed in Greek and never stood in the Hebrew Bible, so it was never the Jews' to drop; its christological reading, though ancient and liturgically dear (Bonaventure; Rabanus Maurus; the Dum medium silentium Introit), is a typology laid over a plain sense — the destroying word of the tenth plague — that the same expositors preserve. And the third witness usually named, Isidore, levels no Jewish-removal charge at all. What survives is a modest residue: the Fathers did accuse the Jews of cutting particular passages (weighed weak on the deliberate-corruption page), and a Jewish stream did rule particular books out of use (weighed on the canon page) — neither of which delivers the sweeping, motivated excision of inspired books the headline asserts.
