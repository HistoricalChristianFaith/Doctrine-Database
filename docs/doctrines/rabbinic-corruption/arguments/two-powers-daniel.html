Did rabbinic Judaism suppress a divine-Messiah ("two powers in heaven") reading of Daniel 7?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — that early rabbinic Judaism suppressed a binitarian, divine-Messiah reading of Daniel 7 ("two powers in heaven") is genuinely disputed among specialists, strong in one respect and weak in another. What is well-grounded is that the rabbis did police the plural throne-vision: they branded belief in "two powers in heaven" a heresy of the minim, and Rabbi Akiba's reading of Daniel 7:9's two thrones — "one for Him and one for David" (the Messiah) — was publicly rebuked and made to yield. What is contested is the larger claim built on that fact: whether the rebuke suppressed a pre-existing divine-Messiah exegesis of Daniel (Alan Segal; Daniel Boyarin) or reacted more diffusely to a second divine figure whose "two thrones = two gods" reading over-reads Daniel (Peter Schäfer), with a minimalist pole holding the rabbis resisted binitarianism from the outset (Menahem Kister). Either way the episode belongs on this doctrine only by contrast: whatever was policed, it was the interpretation — the consonantal plural karsavan ("thrones") stands in the Masoretic Text unaltered to this day, so the case cannot support the master text-corruption charge; it is its mirror — meaning curbed, text untouched · advanced by Alan Segal and Daniel Boyarin

The claim

"One throne for Him and one for David; this is the statement of Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Yosei HaGelili said to him: Akiva, how long shall you make the Divine Presence profane, by presenting it as though one could sit next to Him?"

— b. Ḥagigah 14a, on the plural "thrones" of Daniel 7:9, Sefaria.1 The modern argument reads this rebuke as the visible edge of a rabbinic campaign against a divine, enthroned second figure — the "one like a son of man" of Daniel 7:13 who receives dominion beside the Ancient of Days — a reading the synagogue is said to have suppressed because the Church had made it the charter of Christ's divinity. As Daniel Boyarin puts it, the Danielic vision preserves "a very ancient enthronement of a second divine figure," so that "the theology of the Gospels, far from being a radical break with Judaism, is a highly conservative return to the very most ancient moments" of it — a Jewish reading the rabbis then policed out.2

Who advances it

The scholarly lineage runs from Alan Segal, whose Two Powers in Heaven (1977) established "two powers in heaven" (Hebrew shtei rashuyot ba-shamayim) as a very early rabbinic heresy-category — the rabbis' name for the belief that a second divine power sat enthroned beside God — attested already in the Tannaitic period and aimed at "heretics" (minim) who read plural or doubled scriptural theophanies as two deities. Daniel Boyarin (The Jewish Gospels, 2012, and earlier essays) pressed the case onto Daniel 7 in particular: he made Akiba's two-thrones baraita the rabbinic survival of an old apocalyptic reading in which the Son of Man is a younger divine figure enthroned beside the Ancient of Days, and traced a continuous line — Second-Temple apocalyptic (Daniel, 1 Enoch, 4 Ezra) → the Palestinian Mekhilta → the Babylonian Talmud — of a binitarian idea the rabbis progressively rejected. Downstream, popular Christian and Messianic apologetics take the package whole, presenting Daniel 7 as proof that a divine-Messiah reading was native Judaism until the rabbis cut it off. The chief critic is Peter Schäfer (Two Gods in Heaven, 2020; and his 2012 review of Boyarin, "The Jew Who Would Be God"), who accepts the antiquity and Jewishness of a "second god" idea but rejects Boyarin's specific Daniel exegesis and his continuous "bridge"; a further pole is Menahem Kister, on whose minimalist reading rabbinic Judaism "resisted binitarian temptations of any kind."3

The evidence for

The rabbis really did police the plural throne-vision, and Daniel 7 sat at the center of it. The foundations are firm. The two-powers heresy is a genuine, early rabbinic category, not a modern construct: the Tannaitic midrash and both Talmuds repeatedly stage a min pressing a doubled or plural theophany — "let us make man" (Genesis 1:26), the two divine names, the plural "thrones" of Daniel — and supply the standing rebuttal that "wherever the heretics found support for their heresy, its refutation is beside it."4 And Daniel 7:9 is expressly one of those flashpoints. In the fullest version (b. Ḥagigah 14a), the very plural that troubles the tradition — "till thrones were placed" beside "His throne was fiery flames" — draws Akiba's messianic gloss, "One for Him and one for David," and Rabbi Yosei the Galilean's sharp reproof that this "profanes the Divine Presence… as though one could sit next to Him." The parallel at b. Sanhedrin 38b sets the same exchange squarely in the two-powers frame — the heretics' proof-texts and their refutation — and has Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya correct Akiba to a reading that keeps both thrones for God alone: "one for a throne and one for a stool."5 That a commanding sage floated a two-thrones reading, was rebuked, and the reading was re-glossed to erase the second seat is a real, documented act of interpretive containment — exactly the phenomenon the argument names, and on the exact verse the Church leaned on. Segal's category and Boyarin's Daniel focus both rest on this genuine textual bedrock.

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The policing is real and the sources are firsthand: two-powers was a live rabbinic heresy; Daniel 7's plural thrones were one of its battlegrounds; Akiba's messianic reading of the second throne was publicly rebuked and re-glossed. On the narrow question "did the rabbis curb a messianic/second-figure reading of Daniel 7?" the answer is yes, and Segal's establishment of the heresy-category is not seriously contested.

What is contested or overstated.

  1. Akiba's recorded reading is Davidic, not overtly divine. What the baraita actually preserves is a throne "for David" — the Davidic Messiah, a human king enthroned beside God — and the rebuke treats even that as compromising God's uniqueness ("as though one could sit next to Him"). The leap from "a second throne for the human Messiah" to "a second divine figure, the younger God of Daniel 7:13" is Boyarin's reconstruction, read back through later apocalyptic and Christian lenses, not a datum of the text. So the strongest form of the claim — that a fully divine-Messiah reading was being suppressed — is an inference, and a disputed one.6

  2. The "two thrones = two gods" exegesis over-reads Daniel. Schäfer's objection is exegetical: Daniel 7:9 names an unspecified plurality of thrones ("thrones were placed"), not two, and the likeliest occupants — beside the Ancient of Days — are the seated heavenly court the vision itself convenes for judgment (7:10, "the court sat"), not a second deity. Reading the plural as precisely two, one for an older and one for a younger God, is a rabbinic-era gloss (Akiba's), later still on Boyarin's own timeline, imported back into the sixth-century-BC vision. Whether Daniel 7 itself teaches a second divine power — as against being read that way in later, contested exegesis — is exactly where the specialists divide.7

  3. The "continuous suppression" bridge is disputed on every span. Boyarin's maximalist line — an unbroken binitarian tradition from Second-Temple apocalyptic through the Palestinian Mekhilta to the Babylonian Talmud — is precisely what Schäfer contests: he reads the Mekhilta not as a preserved Palestinian bridge but through the later Bavli, and locates the sharp rejection of a second power mainly in Babylonian Judaism (where, he argues, it was also partly revived), not in a single early Palestinian purge. Kister's pole denies the bridge outright. The scholars agree that a "second god" idea is genuinely ancient and Jewish; they disagree, without a settled winner, on whether the rabbis suppressed a continuous Danielic reading or reacted piecemeal to a diffuse inheritance — which is the difference between the argument's strong form and its weak one.8

  4. Whatever was curbed, it was meaning, not text — so this cannot feed the corruption charge. This is the decisive point for the doctrine that hosts the exhibit. Even granting the strongest suppression thesis, nothing here touches the consonants: the plural כָּרְסָוָן (karsavan, "thrones") stands in the Masoretic Text of Daniel 7:9 unaltered to the present, the very reading that let Akiba and the minim press their case. The rabbis argued the plural down (Elazar's "throne and footstool"); they did not cut it out. So the episode is the exact opposite of the master deliberate-corruption charge — it shows the tradition policing interpretation while leaving the received text in place — and it is enlisted here as that contrast, not as evidence of tampering. To reclassify interpretive suppression as textual corruption would be the same category error the master argument flags at Isaiah 7:14.9

Assessment

Contested. That early rabbinic Judaism suppressed a divine-Messiah, "two powers in heaven" reading of Daniel 7 is a serious, well-sourced thesis with named proponents on both sides and no clear winner — the wiki's standard for contested. Trust the bedrock: two-powers was a real rabbinic heresy-category (Segal), Daniel 7's plural thrones were one of its battlegrounds, and Akiba's second-throne reading was publicly rebuked and re-glossed — a genuine act of interpretive containment on the very verse the Church prized. Flag the reach: Akiba's recorded reading is of the Davidic (human) Messiah, so the fully divine-Messiah version is Boyarin's contested reconstruction; reading Daniel 7's "thrones" as exactly two gods over-reads a vision that convenes a whole heavenly court (Schäfer); and the continuous Second-Temple-to-Bavli suppression "bridge" is disputed at every span (Schäfer; Kister denies it outright). Above all, for this doctrine the case earns its place by contrast, not support: whatever was curbed was the interpretation, while the consonantal text — the plural karsavan — stands untouched in the Masoretic Text to this day. It is therefore no evidence of textual corruption but its mirror, and the strongest illustration on the wiki of the master charge's own crucial distinction: the rabbis policed what a verse was allowed to mean, and left what it said exactly where they found it.
