Origen of Alexandria on the Rabbinic Corruption of Scripture

Dates: c. AD 240 (Letter to Africanus) · Wikipedia
Summary: The timeline's most two-sided witness. Replying to Julius Africanus — who had objected that the Greek story of Susanna could be no true Scripture, standing in no Hebrew Daniel — Origen concedes the Hebrew omission but reverses the inference: the Jewish leaders "hid from the knowledge of the people" passages that told against their elders, so the Greek tradition preserves what the Hebrew custodians dropped. Yet the same letter cuts against the malice thesis: his Hexaplaric labor had mapped the Greek–Hebrew divergences even-handedly, and his governing principle is to keep the copies the Churches use rather than re-edit the Bible to the synagogue's Hebrew. His charge is narrow — of suppressed passages, charged to the elders' shame, not of whole books and not of the christological proof-texts.

Claims & evidence

The Jewish leaders suppressed passages that shamed their elders

"But probably to this you will say, Why then is the `History' not in their Daniel, if, as you say, their wise men hand down by tradition such stories? The answer is, that they hid from the knowledge of the people as many of the passages which contained any scandal against the elders, rulers, and judges, as they could, some of which have been preserved in uncanonical writings (Apocrypha)… Now we know very well that tradition says that Esaias the prophet was sawn asunder; and this is found in some apocryphal work, which probably the Jews have purposely tampered with, introducing some phrases manifestly incorrect, that discredit might be thrown on the whole."

— A Letter to Africanus §9, source, trans. ANF 4 (the same text is in the local corpus as A Letter from Origen to Africanus, Ante-Nicene Fathers, vol. 4). This is the strongest single patristic witness for the "removal" form of the charge. But note its register: the motive Origen assigns is the elders' shame (the scandal-narratives about the two elders of Susanna, the sawing of Isaiah), not the suppression of prophecies of Christ; and what is hidden are passages and narratives, some surviving in the apocrypha, not whole books of the canon. His testimony is weighed in full — and found short of the sweeping claim built on it — on the "removed, not merely rejected" argument.

He had mapped the Greek–Hebrew divergences even-handedly, by obelisk and asterisk

"But why should I enumerate all the instances I collected with so much labour, to prove that the difference between our copies and those of the Jews did not escape me?… other instances are to be found in Genesis, which I marked, for the sake of distinction, with the sign the Greeks call an obelisk, as on the other hand I marked with an asterisk those passages in our copies which are not found in the Hebrew."

— A Letter to Africanus §4, source. Origen — whose Hexapla set the Hebrew and the Greek versions in parallel columns — knew the divergences between the Septuagint and the Hebrew more precisely than any Father before him, and describes them here as textual variety: passages the Greek has and the Hebrew lacks (marked with an asterisk) and passages the Hebrew has and the Greek lacks (marked with an obelisk). That symmetric survey sits uneasily beside a one-sided "the Jews cut it out" thesis; Origen registers the differences as differences, not uniformly as mutilation.

Keep the copies the Churches use — do not re-edit the Bible to the Hebrew

"And, forsooth, when we notice such things, we are forthwith to reject as spurious the copies in use in our Churches, and enjoin the brotherhood to put away the sacred books current among them, and to coax the Jews, and persuade them to give us copies which shall be untampered with, and free from forgery!"

— A Letter to Africanus §5, source. The rhetorical question is a refusal: Origen will not discard the Church's received Greek text merely because it diverges from the synagogue's Hebrew. The principle is conservative and cuts both ways — it tells as much against "go correct our Greek by the Hebrew" (the program Jerome would later adopt) as against "the Jews mutilated the Hebrew." His settled instinct is to preserve what the Churches hold, not to arbitrate the two texts by accusing either side.

Notes

  • A witness who complicates the charge as much as he feeds it. Origen is regularly enlisted for the corruption thesis, and the §9 suppression-charge is genuinely his. But read whole, the Letter to Africanus is the least conspiratorial of the accusing texts: it charges hidden passages (not books), for the elders' shame (not to deny Christ), while mapping the divergences even-handedly and refusing to "correct" the Church's Bible to the Hebrew. The overreach to flag on the proponent's behalf is the slide from Origen's narrow, shame-motivated charge to a wholesale, christologically-motivated excision — a move his own words do not license.
  • The dispute is internal. The books Origen defends (Susanna, Bel) he defends as the Church's, against a fellow Christian (Africanus) who doubted them — so his great principle is not accusation of the Jews but conservation of the received text. That principle is the through-line linking him to the nuanced dissenters later on the timeline, Jerome and Augustine, who likewise decline to turn the Greek–Hebrew divergence into a simple story of malice.
  • Dating. Anchored at c. 240, the usual date of the Letter to Africanus (Origen's Caesarean period), consistent with its citation on the "removed, not merely rejected" argument; his Contra Celsum (c. 248) is the anchor used where his broader corpus is in view.
