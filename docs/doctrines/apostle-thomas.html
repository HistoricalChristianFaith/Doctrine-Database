Thomas

Thomas is the apostle whom the Gospels let us hear speak. Where the others are only names in a list, John gives him three lines — the loyalty that says “Let us also go, that we may die with him,” the bafflement that asks “how can we know the way?” and above all the doubt that will not believe the resurrection until it touches the wounds, breaking at last into the highest confession in the Gospel, “My Lord and my God.” His name is not a name either: Thomas (Aramaic te’oma) and its Greek gloss Didymus both mean “the Twin,” and the Syriac church would call him Judas Thomas and wonder whose twin he was. Beyond the New Testament, Thomas carries the best-developed missionary tradition of any apostle after Peter and Paul: an early notice sends him to Parthia, the fullest of all the apocryphal Acts sends him to India to a king whom coins have since proved real, and the ancient Christians of Kerala trace their founding to him to this day. This page is a history of what was remembered and claimed about Thomas, set out earliest source first: the Gospel notices, Origen’s bare allotment of Parthia, the romance of the Acts of Thomas, the Edessene cult that Ephrem and the pilgrim Egeria attest, the medieval legend, and the modern scholarship that grades them all. This is a biographical tradition-timeline: its “witnesses” are the datable sources that report on Thomas, and the contested questions — did he really reach India? was he the twin of Jesus? — are weighed on their own argument pages below rather than settled by assertion. For the shared ladder of sources and how far to trust each rung, see the apostles hub.

Timeline

The New Testament (c. 30–90)

The New Testament names Thomas in all four lists of the Twelve, but only John’s Gospel lets him act and speak. When Jesus resolves to return to Judea though the Jews there had sought to stone him, it is Thomas who rallies the rest: “Let us also go, that we may die with him.” At the Last Supper he interrupts with the honest question “Lord, we know not whither thou goest; and how can we know the way?” — drawing out the answer “I am the way, the truth, and the life.” And after the resurrection he is the disciple who was not there, who swears he will not believe “except I shall see in his hands the print of the nails… and thrust my hand into his side,” and who, when Jesus offers him exactly that, answers “My Lord and my God” — the episode that gave the language its “doubting Thomas.” Three times John calls him Didymus, “the Twin.” Of his life after Pentecost the New Testament says nothing.1

Origen, preserved by Eusebius: Parthia (c. 230, reported c. 325)

The earliest tradition of where Thomas went is the soberest. Origen, in a lost commentary on Genesis quoted by Eusebius, gives the apostles’ “division of the world”: “Parthia… was allotted to Thomas as his field of labor, Scythia to Andrew, and Asia to John.” This is a bare geographical notice, without marvels — the Parthian empire of Persia, not yet India. Eusebius elsewhere preserves a second early strand: the Edessene legend that “after the ascension of Jesus, Judas, who was also called Thomas, sent” the apostle Thaddaeus to King Abgar of Edessa — the first link, however legendary, between Thomas and the Syriac East that would become the heartland of his cult.2

The Acts of Thomas (c. 220–240, Syriac, Edessa)

The Acts of Thomas is the fullest and most famous of the apocryphal Acts, and the wellspring of the India tradition. Written in Syriac at Edessa in the early third century, it opens with the apostles dividing the world by lot: India falls to Thomas, who refuses until the risen Christ sells him as a slave-carpenter to a merchant buying for the Indian king Gundaphar. Commissioned to build the king a palace, Thomas gives the money to the poor and tells him he has raised a palace in heaven instead. The tale runs on through miracles and conversions to a martyrdom: under King Misdaeus, whose wife and son Thomas had baptized, the apostle is led to a hill and run through with spears by four soldiers. It is romance-hagiography of low biographical value — but its king Gundaphar turns out to be a real Indo-Parthian monarch, unknown from any other literary source and recovered only in the nineteenth century from coins and an inscription, which is why this Acts, alone among its kind, has to be taken seriously.3

Ephrem the Syrian and the shrine at Edessa (4th c.)

By the fourth century Thomas had a tomb-cult in the Syriac world. Ephrem the Syrian (c. 306–373), who lived out his last decade at Edessa, sings in his hymns of the apostle martyred in India whose bones a merchant carried back to Edessa — picturing even the demons dismayed that the apostle they thought slain in India had come to trouble them at Edessa. An early Syriac martyrology fixes the memory: “3 July, St. Thomas who was pierced with a lance in India. His body is at Urhai [Edessa], having been brought there by the merchant Khabin. A great festival.” And about 384 the pilgrim Egeria, on her way through Mesopotamia, “straightway repaired to the church and memorial of Saint Thomas” at Edessa. Whatever the truth of the India mission, the relic and its shrine at Edessa are firmly attested a century before Egeria and were a major pilgrimage site.4

The Golden Legend (c. 1260)

Jacobus de Voragine’s Golden Legend gathers the medieval Thomas: the palace built in heaven for King Gundaphorus, the raising of the king’s dead brother who had seen it, the mission to a second Indian king Mesdeus, the idol shattered, and the martyrdom by the spears (or swords) of the pagan priests. It is late hagiography, a thousand years and more after the events, drawing on the old Acts and the apostle-lists; but it is the form in which the Latin West received Thomas, and the source of his emblem, the carpenter’s square.5

Modern scholarship

Critical study grades Thomas’s tradition as the strongest of the apostolic missions outside Peter and Paul — which is not to say secure. The living witness is the Mar Thoma or Saint Thomas Christians of Kerala, an ancient community that traces its founding to Thomas’s landing at Muziris (Cranganore) in AD 52 and his martyrdom at Mylapore, near modern Chennai, about AD 72, with his tomb at San Thome. Sean McDowell, weighing each martyrdom account on a probability scale, rates Thomas’s death in India “more probable than not” — below the near-certain deaths of Peter, Paul, and James, but well above the apostles for whom nothing early survives. The anchor is Gundaphar’s recovered historicity; the doubts are the elasticity of ancient “India,” the romance character of the Acts, and the possibility that the Kerala link was read back onto the community later. What can be said is more than for most: a Thomas was numbered among the Twelve, an early and independent tradition sent him east, the Syriac church kept his relic and his feast by the fourth century, and a continuous Indian church has borne his name for as far back as its records reach.6

Arguments

Did Thomas evangelize India? (assessment: contested)

The India mission is the best-evidenced of all the apocryphal apostolic journeys, and still genuinely disputed. In its favour: the Acts of Thomas names a king, Gundaphar, whom no one could have invented — a real Indo-Parthian ruler of the right era, lost to literary memory and known now only from coins; the Saint Thomas Christians of Kerala are a demonstrably ancient community that has always claimed him as founder; his relic and cult at Edessa are firmly fixed by the fourth century; and Sean McDowell rates the martyrdom “more probable than not.” Against it: the Acts is a romance, “India” in antiquity stretched from Arabia to the subcontinent, and the Kerala tradition, however old, cannot be traced in writing to the first century. Strong for an apocryphal mission, short of proof. Contested.7

Was Thomas the twin brother of Jesus? (assessment: weak)

The “twin” question begins in solid ground and ends in speculation. That “Thomas” and “Didymus” both mean “twin,” and that the Syriac church called him Judas Thomas, is plain fact; but the further claim — made by the Acts of Thomas, the Gospel of Thomas, and the Book of Thomas the Contender — that he was the literal twin brother of Jesus is a distinctively gnostic and Syriac embellishment, at odds with the Gospels’ own account of Jesus’ brothers and unknown to the wider church, which read the epithet as marking Thomas a twin of someone unnamed, or took it symbolically. The name is real; the twin-of-Jesus is legend. Weak.8
