Baptist Successionism

Baptist successionism (also "Baptist perpetuity," and in its hardest form the "Trail of Blood" thesis) is the claim that Baptists — or the believers'-baptism, free-church Christians they identify with — form an unbroken succession of true churches from the apostles, independent of Rome, and so did not originate at the Reformation. On this reading a chain of dissenting movements (Montanists, Novatianists, Donatists, Paulicians, Bogomils, Albigenses, Waldenses, Anabaptists) carries the apostolic faith underground through the centuries, surfacing as the modern Baptists. The view is closely tied to nineteenth-century Landmarkism (J. R. Graves) and was popularized by J. M. Carroll's 1931 pamphlet The Trail of Blood. The mainstream historical account holds, instead, that modern Baptists arose in the early seventeenth century out of English Separatism (the General Baptists of John Smyth and Thomas Helwys, c. 1609; the Particular Baptists, c. 1638), and that the "succession" gathers doctrinally diverse and often heretical groups into a lineage the documents do not support. Interpreters divide along two axes: whether the claim is one of literal church genealogy (an institutional chain of baptisms) or only of perpetual Baptist principles (believers' baptism and a gathered church recurring in every age), and whether the dissenting groups invoked were in fact Baptist in any meaningful sense.

Charles Spurgeon (c. 1861)

Charles Spurgeon, welcoming the Baptist churches of London to the newly opened Metropolitan Tabernacle in 1861, gave the era's most-quoted pulpit statement of the thesis: "We believe that the Baptists are the original Christians," who "did not commence our existence at the Reformation" and "never came from the Church of Rome, for we were never in it, but we have an unbroken line up to the apostles themselves" — their principles "sometimes veiled and forgotten, like a river which may travel underground for a little season," yet always attested.1 The claim is stated more strongly than his wider corpus bears out — Spurgeon prized the largely paedobaptist Puritans and never defended the documentary chain strict successionism requires — so it reads as celebratory pulpit rhetoric, and his readers debate whether he meant a literal genealogy of churches or a perpetuity of Baptist principles.2

James Milton Carroll (1931)

James Milton Carroll, a Texas Southern Baptist minister, gave the thesis its most concrete and widely circulated form in The Trail of Blood — five lectures with a fold-out chart, completed before his death in January 1931 and published posthumously that year. Distinguishing his claim from Roman apostolic succession — "Baptists do not believe in Apostolic Succession… It is to His churches that He promised a continual existence… until He comes again" (Matt. 16:18) — he charted an unbroken line of "true" churches from Christ through a roll of persecuted dissenting groups (Montanists, Novationists, Donatists, Paulicians, Albigenses, Waldenses, and others) down to the modern Baptists, who on this reading are neither Catholic nor Protestant, never having come from the Church of Rome.3 The chain is the thesis's weakest link: it gathers groups whose theology Baptists repudiate — the dualist Paulicians and Albigenses, the paedobaptist Donatists, the ecstatic Montanists — and when Carroll's own cited sources were tracked down they proved largely unverifiable, leaving the mainstream account (modern Baptists arising from early-seventeenth-century English Separatism) in place.4
