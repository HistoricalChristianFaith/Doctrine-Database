The Acts of Andrew

Dates: c. 150–210 (the apocryphal romance; the surviving Greek Acts and Martyrdom is a later reworking) · Wikipedia
Summary: The apocryphal romance that supplies almost everything the tradition “knows” about Andrew's end: his mission in Achaia, his clash with the proconsul Aegeates at Patras, and his death bound — not nailed — to a cross so as to prolong his suffering. It is romance-hagiography of low biographical value; its worth here is as the wellspring of the Patras–crucifixion tradition, not as reliable history.

Claims & evidence

Achaia, Patras, and the proconsul Aegeates

Accordingly the proconsul Ægeates, having come into the city of Patras, began to compel those believing in Christ to worship the idols…

— Acts and Martyrdom of the Holy Apostle Andrew (ANF vol. 8). The scene is set in Achaia, in the Peloponnesian city of Patras; the whole central section is a long dialogue between Andrew and Aegeates on the “mystery of the cross,” ending in the sentence of crucifixion.

Bound, not nailed, to prolong the death

And they having come up, lifted him on the cross; and having stretched his body across with ropes, they only bound his feet, but did not sever his joints, having received this order from the proconsul: for he wished him to be in distress while hanging…

— ibid. Andrew is tied to the cross rather than nailed, so that he will die slowly; the text then has him hang and preach for days before he dies. Note what the earliest form of the death-story does not say: it does not describe an X-shaped cross. The apostle is simply “lifted on the cross” — the familiar saltire shape is a much later addition, weighed on the argument page The X-shaped “St Andrew's cross.”

Notes

  • Low biographical value, early witness to veneration. The Acts of Andrew belongs to the second-century “Leucian” corpus of apostolic romances (with the Acts of Peter, John, and Thomas); it carries encratite tendencies and is of little use as biography. It is valuable in a different way — as evidence of how early and how vividly Andrew was venerated, and as the source of the Patras–crucifixion tradition later writers inherited. On the house treatment of the apocryphal Acts, see the apostles hub.
  • Two texts, one tradition. The original Acts of Andrew survives only in fragments and epitomes (notably Gregory of Tours's sixth-century Latin Book of the Miracles of the blessed Andrew); the connected Greek Acts and Martyrdom quoted here, and the related Epistle of the presbyters and deacons of Achaia, are later reworkings of the martyrdom that carried the Patras story into the medieval church.
  • What it does and does not establish. That Andrew was martyred by crucifixion at Patras is possible and, on the best modern grading, more plausible than not — but it rests on this late and legendary source, not on any early sober witness; the historicity question is weighed at Was Andrew martyred by crucifixion at Patras?
