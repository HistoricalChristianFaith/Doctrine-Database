The X-shaped “St Andrew's cross”

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — that Andrew died on a diagonal, X-shaped cross (the crux decussata or saltire) is a late-medieval embellishment, unknown to any ancient account of his death; the earliest sources have him simply bound to “the cross,” with no word of its shape. (This concerns the shape only; that Andrew was crucified at Patras at all is a separate, better-grounded question — plausible.)

The claim

The claim: Andrew was crucified not on an upright Latin cross but on a diagonal one shaped like the letter X — the “St Andrew's cross” or saltire now borne on the flags of Scotland and of many other places, and fixed in art and heraldry as his emblem.

Who advances it

The saltire is today the near-universal artistic and heraldic attribute of Andrew, but it has no ancient advocate at all. The oldest death-narratives — the apocryphal Acts of Andrew and the derived Greek Acts and Martyrdom — say only that he was “lifted on the cross” and bound by ropes; they never describe its form. No Father, no apostle-list, and no early legendary source specifies an X. There is thus no rival ancient account of the shape to weigh against this one — the alternatives are simply an ordinary cross, or (most honestly) an unknown shape — so this page weighs the saltire claim and folds those possibilities in as limits on it.1

The evidence for

The positive case is entirely late and iconographic. The diagonal cross begins to attach to Andrew in Western art and devotion only around the tenth to fourteenth centuries, becoming standard in the later Middle Ages; the Golden Legend (c. 1260) and subsequent hagiography then hardened the image, and heraldry (the Scottish saltire, and much else) carried it everywhere. As the historical summary in the Catholic Encyclopedia frankly concedes, the cross “is commonly held to have been the decussate cross, now known as St. Andrew's, though the evidence for this view seems to be no older than the fourteenth century.”2

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The saltire is a genuine and ancient later tradition in the sense that matters least here — it is a real, continuous artistic and devotional convention, and there is nothing impossible in a diagonal crucifixion as such (Roman crucifixion took varied forms). As Andrew's emblem, the X-cross is entirely legitimate; the question is only whether it reports how he actually died.

What is overstated or imprecise. As a historical claim about the manner of Andrew's death it has no early footing whatever. Every ancient source that describes the death describes a plain binding to “the cross” and is silent on its shape; the X first appears more than a millennium after the event, in medieval art, not in any narrative claiming to report the crucifixion. A detail that surfaces only in the fourteenth century, with an unbroken earlier silence behind it, cannot be treated as a memory of the first — it is an emblem read back onto the death, not evidence of it.3

Assessment

Weak. Keep the emblem and the event apart. That Andrew was crucified is part of a plausible tradition; that the cross was X-shaped is a late-medieval iconographic development with no ancient attestation — the earliest death-narratives describe only a binding to the cross and never its form. The saltire is a fine symbol and a poor historical report.
