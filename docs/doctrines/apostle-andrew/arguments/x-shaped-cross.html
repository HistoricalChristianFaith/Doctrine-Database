The X-shaped “St Andrew's cross”

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — that Andrew died on a diagonal, X-shaped cross (the crux decussata or saltire) has no ancient footing. Taken at its strongest — not the bare emblem but the tradition that Andrew, like Peter, chose a cross unlike Christ's out of unworthiness — the reading is still late and derivative: the earliest death-narratives have him simply bound to “the cross” with no word of its shape, the sixth-century epitome that first describes the cross calls it an ordinary upright one, and the X appears only later, in medieval art. The shape-tradition is a genuine devotional emblem read back onto the death, not a memory of it. (This concerns the shape only; that Andrew was crucified at Patras at all is a separate, better-grounded question — plausible.)

The claim

The claim: Andrew was crucified not on an upright Latin cross but on a diagonal one shaped like the letter X — the “St Andrew's cross” or saltire now borne on the flags of Scotland and of many other places, and fixed in art and heraldry as his emblem.

The claim in its strongest form

Two versions must be told apart. The bare claim — “the cross was X-shaped” — has no ancient advocate at all; it lives only in later art and heraldry. But there is a stronger form worth steelmanning, because it supplies the one thing the bare emblem lacks: a motive. In the developed tradition Andrew, deeming himself unworthy to die on a cross like his Lord's, asked to be crucified on a differently-shaped one — exactly the reasoning attached to Peter's head-downward cross. On this reading the saltire is not an artist's flourish but a remembered choice, and the parallel to Peter's inversion (Andrew was Peter's brother) gives it a certain narrative plausibility. This is the version the argument must weigh; the possibilities of an ordinary cross or an unknown shape are folded in below as limits on it.1

The evidence for

The positive case is entirely late and iconographic. The oldest death-narratives — the apocryphal Acts of Andrew and the derived Greek Acts and Martyrdom — say only that he was “lifted on the cross” and bound by ropes; they never describe its form, and they know nothing of any request to change its shape. The diagonal cross begins to attach to Andrew in Western art only around the tenth century, does not become the iconographic standard until the seventeenth, and hardens into his fixed emblem through the Golden Legend (c. 1260) and heraldry — above all the Scottish saltire, whose first firm documentary appearance is the 1286 seal of the Guardians of Scotland, showing Andrew on an X-cross. As the historical summary in the Catholic Encyclopedia frankly concedes, the cross “is commonly held to have been the decussate cross, now known as St. Andrew's, though the evidence for this view seems to be no older than the fourteenth century.”2

Adversarial assessment

What holds. There is nothing impossible in the claim. Roman crucifixion took varied forms, a diagonal or forked cross is physically unremarkable, and the unworthiness-motive is not absurd on its face — it is the very reasoning the tradition gives for Peter, whose head-downward death is itself graded plausible. As Andrew's devotional and heraldic emblem the X-cross is wholly legitimate; the only question is whether it reports how he actually died.

Why the strong form still fails. The steelman does not survive its dating. First, the silence is not neutral: the earliest source that actually describes the cross — the sixth-century Latin epitome of the Acts by Gregory of Tours — has Andrew bound to an ordinary upright cross of the kind Christ died on, not a saltire. So the tradition begins with a positive description of a different shape, and the X displaces it only centuries later.3 Second, the very motive that gives the strong form its force — the humble request for an unlike cross — is precisely what marks it as a doublet: it is a migrating hagiographical topos, told of Peter first and attached to Andrew late, to explain an iconography that had already arisen, not a datum that generated it. And even in Peter's better-attested case that requested-inversion detail is judged the weak, secondary overlay, not the historical core. A shape unknown to every narrative for a thousand years, contradicted by the one early source that does describe it, and rationalized by a borrowed motive, cannot be a memory of the crucifixion — it is an emblem read back onto the death.

Assessment

Weak. Keep the emblem and the event apart. That Andrew was crucified is part of a plausible tradition; that the cross was X-shaped — even in the stronger, motive-supplying form where he is said to have chosen it — is a late iconographic development with no ancient attestation, positively contradicted by the earliest source that describes the cross at all, and resting on a motive borrowed from Peter's legend. The saltire is a fine symbol and a poor historical report.
