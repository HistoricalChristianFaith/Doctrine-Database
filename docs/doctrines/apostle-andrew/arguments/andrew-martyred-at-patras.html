Was Andrew martyred by crucifixion at Patras?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: plausible — a coherent and unrefuted tradition with a fixed location and manner, but one whose whole weight rests on a late and legendary source; on the best modern grading it is more plausible than not, short of the near-certainty that attaches to Peter, Paul, or James son of Zebedee. (This weighs the fact and place of the martyrdom; the celebrated X-shaped cross is a separate and weaker question, treated here.)

The claim

Accordingly the proconsul Ægeates, having come into the city of Patras, began to compel those believing in Christ to worship the idols… And they having come up, lifted him on the cross…

— Acts and Martyrdom of the Holy Apostle Andrew. The claim: Andrew ended his mission in Achaia and was crucified at Patras, under the Roman proconsul Aegeates, bound to the cross to prolong his death.1

The evidence for

The tradition is fixed and unrivalled: from the second-century Acts of Andrew onward the sources agree on a single city (Patras), a single province (Achaia), a single magistrate (Aegeates), and a single manner (crucifixion). No competing city ever claimed to hold Andrew's death or his grave, and Achaia had a real early Christian presence for such a mission to fit. Jerome independently locates Andrew's ministry “in Achaia,” corroborating at least the region, and the Achaian church kept his memory and, later, his relics. Sean McDowell's critical survey, weighing exactly this evidence, grades Andrew's martyrdom as “more plausible than not” — the tradition is early enough, consistent, and uncontested, even if thin.2

Adversarial assessment

What holds. A martyrdom is not made doubtful by being a martyrdom, and this one has the marks of a settled memory rather than a free invention: a named place and official, a plausible geography, no rival account, and corroboration of the region from a sober source (Jerome). Crucifixion of a provincial troublemaker under a Roman magistrate is entirely in character for the period.

What is overstated or imprecise. The load-bearing source is the apocryphal Acts of Andrew — a romance of low biographical value — and everything vivid in it (the two-day speech, the dialogue on the cross, the crowd's revolt) is hagiographical elaboration, not report. Jerome and the earlier notices give a mission in Achaia, not a martyrdom there; the proconsul “Aegeates” is unattested outside the legend; and the older, soberer tradition (Origen, Eusebius) sent Andrew not to Achaia at all but to Scythia, on the Black Sea. So the death-at-Patras story is coherent and uncontradicted, but it is carried by a single late and legendary stream, with only regional corroboration behind it — enough for “more plausible than not,” not for “sound.”3

Assessment

Plausible. That Andrew was crucified at Patras in Achaia under the proconsul Aegeates is a fixed, unrivalled, and coherent tradition, corroborated in its geography by Jerome and graded “more plausible than not” by the best modern survey — but its whole detailed weight rests on the legendary Acts of Andrew, and an older tradition placed his labours in Scythia instead. It is more likely than not, and well short of the near-certainty that attaches to the best-attested apostolic deaths.
