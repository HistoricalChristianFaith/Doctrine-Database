Did Andrew found the church of Byzantium?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — the tradition that Andrew founded the church of Byzantium and ordained Stachys as its first bishop rests on a late, mutually-dependent apostle-list and served an evident purpose — to give the see of Constantinople an apostolic founder to set against Rome's Peter; it has no early attestation. (That Andrew laboured somewhere on the Black Sea coast, and perhaps in Achaia, is a separate and better tradition; this weighs only the specific Byzantium-foundation claim.)

The claim

The claim: the apostle Andrew came to Byzantium — the town Constantine would later refound as Constantinople — preached there, and consecrated Stachys (the “Stachys my beloved” greeted in Romans 16:9) as its first bishop, making the church of the imperial capital an apostolic foundation in its own right.

Who advances it

The claim is a Byzantine one, and its purpose is transparent. As Constantinople rose to rival Rome, its patriarchate needed an apostle of its own; Andrew — Peter's elder brother and the “first-called” — was the ideal candidate, and the tradition of his Black Sea mission gave a foothold. The specific Byzantium–Stachys story appears in the late apostle-list literature (the so-called Pseudo-Dorotheus of Tyre and kindred catalogues) and is elaborated in Byzantine chroniclers such as Nicephorus Callistus; it becomes the standard charter of the see of Constantinople. It is independently advocated — a positive foundation-claim with real proponents and a proof-text (Romans 16:9) — which is why it earns its own page rather than a fold-in.1

The evidence for

Two things lend the tradition a little colour. First, the older “division of the world” genuinely placed Andrew on the Black Sea (Origen's Scythia), and Byzantium sits at that sea's threshold, so a foundation there is not geographically absurd. Second, Andrew's relics were translated to Constantinople — carried to the Church of the Holy Apostles in 357 under Constantius II — so the city had an early and real Andrean cult onto which a foundation-legend could fasten. The identification of the first bishop with a man Paul actually greets in Romans gives the story a scriptural anchor.2

Adversarial assessment

What holds. Andrew's association with the Black Sea region is old, and the translation of his relics to Constantinople in 357 is a genuine early datum; the city's devotion to him is real, not invented. Nothing forbids an apostle having passed through the region.

What is overstated or imprecise. The foundation-claim itself has every mark of a tendentious later construction. Its earliest sources are the apostle-lists — mutually dependent, of uncertain date and authorship, and critically judged worthless as history — and it surfaces just when Constantinople most needed an apostolic pedigree against Rome. The Stachys identification is a pious harmonisation with Romans 16, not evidence of a Byzantine episcopate; the earliest and soberest tradition sent Andrew to Scythia, not to Byzantium; and no source within centuries of the apostle knows of a church he planted there. The transparent ecclesiastical-political motive, joined to the lateness and dependence of the witnesses, tells strongly against it as history — while leaving its real value, as a monument of how Constantinople remembered and claimed its apostle, untouched.3

Assessment

Weak. The Byzantium-foundation tradition rests on the late, mutually-dependent apostle-lists, harmonises its first bishop with a name in Romans, and answers an obvious need of the see of Constantinople for an apostolic founder to match Rome's Peter. Andrew's Black Sea mission and the 357 translation of his relics are real; a church he founded at Byzantium is not established by any early witness. Its worth is as church memory and ecclesiastical charter, not as history.
