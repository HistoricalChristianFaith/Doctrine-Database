The New Testament on Andrew

Dates: c. 30–90 (the events, and the writing of the Gospels and Acts) · Wikipedia
Summary: The bedrock. Andrew is a fisherman of Bethsaida, Simon Peter's brother, and — in John's Gospel — a disciple of John the Baptist who hears the Baptist point to Jesus, follows him, and at once fetches his brother Peter. He is named in all four lists of the Twelve. The New Testament says nothing of where he went or how he died.

Claims & evidence

A fisherman of Bethsaida, Simon Peter's brother

Andrew is introduced, wherever he appears, by his relationship to his more prominent brother. He and Simon are fishermen of the Sea of Galilee, called together from their nets; John's Gospel names their town as Bethsaida, “the city of Andrew and Peter.”

Now as he walked by the sea of Galilee, he saw Simon and Andrew his brother casting a net into the sea: for they were fishers. And Jesus said unto them, Come ye after me, and I will make you to become fishers of men. And straightway they forsook their nets, and followed him.

— Mark 1:16–18 (cf. Matthew 4:18–20; John 1:44, Bethsaida).

A disciple of John the Baptist, the “first-called,” who brings Peter to Jesus

John's Gospel gives Andrew his one distinct scene. He is first a follower of John the Baptist; when the Baptist points to Jesus as “the Lamb of God,” Andrew follows Jesus, spends the day with him, and then goes to find Simon. Because he is called before his brother, later Greek tradition surnamed him Prōtoklētos, “the first-called.”

One of the two which heard John speak, and followed him, was Andrew, Simon Peter's brother. He first findeth his own brother Simon, and saith unto him, We have found the Messias, which is, being interpreted, the Christ. And he brought him to Jesus.

— John 1:40–42 (the whole call, John 1:35–42).

Twice more in John: the loaves, and the Greeks

Andrew appears twice again in the Fourth Gospel, each time bringing someone to Jesus. He points out the boy with the loaves before the feeding of the five thousand, and, with Philip, brings word of the Greeks who “would see Jesus.”

One of his disciples, Andrew, Simon Peter's brother, saith unto him, There is a lad here, which hath five barley loaves, and two small fishes: but what are they among so many?

— John 6:8–9; and the Greeks, “Philip cometh and telleth Andrew: and again Andrew and Philip tell Jesus” (John 12:20–22). He is also among the four — Peter, James, John, and Andrew — who ask Jesus privately about the end (Mark 13:3).

Named in all four lists of the Twelve

Andrew stands in the first group of four in every apostle-list, always alongside his brother, Peter, and the sons of Zebedee.

Now the names of the twelve apostles are these; The first, Simon, who is called Peter, and Andrew his brother…

— Matthew 10:2 (cf. Mark 3:18; Luke 6:14; Acts 1:13).

Notes

  • The silence about his end. The New Testament records the death of only one apostle — James son of Zebedee (Acts 12:2). Of Andrew it tells us his origin and call, but nothing of a mission field or a martyrdom; everything on those subjects — Scythia, Achaia, the cross at Patras — belongs to the later tradition traced on the summary timeline.
  • Always the brother. Andrew's role in the Gospels is consistently that of the one who brings others to Jesus — Peter, the boy, the Greeks — a pattern later writers made much of, and the seed of his epithet Prōtoklētos.
