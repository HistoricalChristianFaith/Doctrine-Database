Origen (in Eusebius) on Andrew

Dates: c. 230 (quoted by Eusebius, c. 325) · Wikipedia
Summary: The earliest datable notice of an Andrew mission field. In a fragment preserved by Eusebius, Origen assigns Andrew to Scythia — the region north of the Black Sea — as part of the “division of the world” among the apostles. It says nothing of Achaia or of a martyrdom.

Claims & evidence

Andrew's field: Scythia

Such was the condition of the Jews. Meanwhile the holy apostles and disciples of our Saviour were dispersed throughout the world. Parthia, according to tradition, was allotted to Thomas as his field of labor, Scythia to Andrew, and Asia to John, who, after he had lived some time there, died at Ephesus.

— Origen's account of the apostles' fields, quoted in Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History 3.1. “Scythia” in this older, sober tradition means the coast north of the Black Sea; the same passage sends Thomas to Parthia, John to Asia, and Peter to Rome. This is the seed of Andrew's later patronage of the Black Sea lands and, eventually, of Russia.

Notes

  • The oldest layer, and a bare one. Origen (preserved by Eusebius a century later) is the first named writer to give Andrew a mission field, and he gives only one word — Scythia. The fuller story of Achaia, Patras, and a crucifixion is nowhere here; it enters with the apocryphal Acts of Andrew and is echoed, for the Achaian mission, by Jerome.
  • A stereotyped scheme. The “division of the world” that Eusebius took from Origen distributes the apostles neatly across the map (Thomas–Parthia, Andrew–Scythia, John–Asia, Peter–Rome); its schematic tidiness is itself a caution against pressing any single assignment too hard. On how far to trust each rung of the tradition, see the apostles hub.
