The Perpetual Virginity of Mary

Did Mary remain a virgin her whole life — ever-virgin, the mother of Jesus and of no one else — or did she and Joseph go on to have other children after his birth? The whole question turns on a single phrase. The Gospels and Epistles repeatedly name “the brethren of the Lord” (Matthew 13:55; Mark 6:3; John 7:5; Acts 1:14; 1 Corinthians 9:5; Galatians 1:19), and even list four of them by name — James, Joseph (Joses), Simon, and Jude — alongside unnamed sisters. Who were they? If they were Mary’s own later-born children, she was not ever-virgin; if they were kin in some looser sense, the perpetual virginity stands. Everything hangs on which reading of “brother” the evidence will bear.

Three historic answers dominate the tradition, and it is convenient to use the names J. B. Lightfoot gave them in his 1865 study of the question, after each view’s most zealous fourth-century advocate:1

  • The Helvidian view — the brethren are sons of Joseph and Mary, born after Jesus. This is the plain-sense reading of “brother,” and it denies the perpetual virginity. Named for Helvidius, who pressed it at Rome (c. 383) against the era’s exaltation of virginity; Tertullian is usually reckoned its earliest patristic representative.
  • The Epiphanian view — the brethren are sons of Joseph by a former wife, older step-brothers of Jesus and no blood-kin of Mary at all. This preserves Mary’s perpetual virginity while giving “brother” a natural family sense. Named for Epiphanius of Salamis, it claims the widest early attestation — the second-century Protevangelium of James, Clement, Origen, Eusebius, Gregory of Nyssa, and the Eastern churches — and Lightfoot judged it the reading best supported by tradition and by the Gospel evidence.
  • The Hieronymian view — the brethren are maternal cousins, sons of “Mary of Clopas,” the Virgin’s sister, so that “brother” means kinsman. This preserves the virginity of Mary and of Joseph alike. Named for Jerome, who first advanced it (c. 383) in his reply to Helvidius; it became the standard reading of the Latin West through Augustine and the Western service-books. Lightfoot argued it was the latest-born of the three and unknown before Jerome devised it.

The witnesses below are set out earliest first — each father slotted by date, with his own words where they survive — so that the drift of opinion, and the point at which Jerome’s new hypothesis reshaped the Western tradition, can be read off the timeline itself.

Timeline

The Gospel of Peter — c. 150

Named alongside the Protevangelium as a source of the very same idea is the Gospel of Peter — an apocryphal gospel whose own words on the brethren do not survive, so that the attestation is owed entirely to Origen, who reports that “some… basing it on a tradition in the Gospel according to Peter… or the Book of James, say that the brethren of Jesus were sons of Joseph by a former wife, whom he married before Mary,” those who say so wishing to “preserve the honour of Mary in virginity to the end.”2 The text Origen names was esteemed by the second-century Docetae and already long in circulation when Serapion of Antioch found it at Rhossus, which sets it “about the middle of the second century at the latest, and probably somewhat earlier” — a named text as old as the Protevangelium carrying the Epiphanian solution; but the attestation is only second-hand, so the Gospel of Peter’s own testimony remains ⚠ unverified.3

The Protevangelium of James — c. 150

The earliest witness whose own text survives entire is not a father but an anonymous infancy romance, the Protevangelium of James — falsely ascribed to James the Lord’s brother and usually placed near the middle of the second century, its testimony (unlike the Gospel of Peter’s) preserved in full. It is the fountainhead of the Epiphanian reading: it makes Joseph an aged widower who already “had children” before he was ever betrothed to Mary, so that the “brethren of the Lord” are his sons by a former marriage and no blood-kin of Mary at all.4 The same detail is carried through the narrative — Joseph enrols “my sons” at Bethlehem and leaves “his two sons” with Mary at the cave — while no daughter is ever named,5 and the closing colophon presents “James” the supposed author as already a grown man at Herod’s death.6 The book is legendary, not historical — J. B. Lightfoot judged it “a purely fictitious… narrative” — so its worth is as evidence that belief in Mary’s perpetual virginity, and the specific device of explaining the brethren as Joseph’s earlier children, were already in circulation by c. 150, seeding a whole family of later infancy apocrypha that copy it.7

Hegesippus — c. 175

The earliest historical witness — not a legend but a Palestinian Hebrew Christian who gathered the traditions of the Lord’s kindred — is Hegesippus, whose five books of Memoirs survive only where Eusebius quotes them. He is decisive because he keeps two relationships distinct: James and Jude are always styled the Lord’s brother, while Symeon, who succeeded James as bishop of Jerusalem, is the Lord’s cousin — “All proposed him as second bishop because he was a cousin of the Lord,” the son of Joseph’s brother Clopas.8 Eusebius twice repeats the point from him — Symeon “was a cousin, as they say, of the Saviour, for Hegesippus records that Clopas was a brother of Joseph.”9 Yet where Hegesippus names James he calls him “the brother of the Lord,” and Jude he calls one “who is said to have been the Lord’s brother according to the flesh.”10 Because the brethren (James, Jude) are thus never confused with the cousin (Symeon son of Clopas), the earliest Palestinian tradition tells against the Hieronymian reading, which makes the brethren themselves cousins; and by the qualifier “said to be,” Lightfoot judged Hegesippus to lean Epiphanian rather than Helvidian — “the testimony of Hegesippus seems distinctly opposed to the Hieronymian view, while of the other two it favours the Epiphanian rather than the Helvidian.”11

Clement of Alexandria — c. 200

The earliest father to state the Epiphanian reading outright and in his own voice is Clement of Alexandria. Commenting on the Epistle of Jude — in notes from his lost Hypotyposeis that survive in a sixth-century Latin abridgement by Cassiodorus — he explains why Jude, though kin to the Lord, styles himself only “brother of James”: because he was one of Joseph’s sons and reverence forbade the nearer claim. “Jude, who wrote the Catholic Epistle, the brother of the sons of Joseph… did not say that he himself was His brother… he was His brother, (the son) of Joseph.”12 The statement must be Clement’s own and not the translator’s, for Cassiodorus, a Latin churchman, held the rival Hieronymian view and would have purged such a line rather than insert it. A separate Greek extract in Eusebius, where Clement notes that “there were two Jameses,” has been claimed for the Hieronymian side, but it “neither states nor implies that there were two only” and does not contradict the plain note on Jude; J. B. Lightfoot judged Clement’s Epiphanian statement “explicit” and set him second, after the Gospel of Peter and Protevangelium, among the “sons of Joseph by a former wife” witnesses.13

Tertullian — c. 210

The earliest father usually reckoned a Helvidian — the first to read the “brethren” as Mary’s own later children — is Tertullian, though he states it nowhere as a thesis: it falls out of his anti-Marcionite polemic. Insisting against Marcion and Apelles that Christ was truly born, he argues that the mother and brethren who “stood without” must have really been His — “if… He made them ‘His mother and His brethren’ who were not so, how could He deny them these relationships who really had them?”14 His argument, as J. B. Lightfoot put it, “seems to imply that the Lord’s brethren were His brothers in the same sense in which Mary was His mother.” And where he speaks of Mary’s virginity directly, he confines it to the conception and denies it survived the birth: “although she was a virgin when she conceived, she was a wife when she brought forth her son… under the very law of ‘opening the womb,’” a virgin only “about to marry once for all after her delivery.”15 Helvidius later claimed his authority, and Jerome, unable to deny it, could only retort that Tertullian was “not a man of the Church” — so that Lightfoot judged it “highly probable” (though, he cautioned, not absolutely certain) that this “strenuous… advocate of asceticism” held the Helvidian view.16

The Clementine Literature — c. 220

Between Tertullian and Origen stands a witness of a wholly different kind: the Pseudo-Clementine literature, the romance of a Jewish-Christian (Ebionite) Christianity whose extant Greek Homilies and Latin Recognitions descend from a third-century source drawing on second-century Ebionite writings. It is no friend of the doctrine — these circles denied the virgin birth — yet it bears on the brethren question at one narrow point, for it exalts James the Lord’s brother and keeps him rigorously apart from the Twelve. The prefixed Epistle of Clement hails James as “the bishop of bishops, who rules Jerusalem,” and the Homilies make him, “called the brother of my Lord,” the standard by which every “apostle or teacher or prophet” must be tested.17 The Recognitions — in a Temple-disputation scene founded on the early Ebionite Ascents of James — states the distinction outright: the twelve apostles, “James the son of Alphaeus” among them, dispute in turn and report to “James, the chief of the bishops,” the Lord’s brother who presides over them.18 J. B. Lightfoot set the work in his catena for just this feature: James is “distinguished from and in some respects exalted above the Twelve,” while “James the son of Alphaeus is distinctly mentioned among the Twelve as one of the disputants” — so that, like Hegesippus, the Clementines tell against the Hieronymian reading that folds the Lord’s brother into the Twelve, while leaving the Epiphanian and Helvidian readings, which both keep him distinct, untouched.19

Origen — c. 248

The fullest early exponent of the Epiphanian reading — and the first to give it a documentary pedigree — is Origen, Clement’s successor at Alexandria. Commenting on Matthew 13:55 he reports and endorses the view that “the brethren of Jesus were sons of Joseph by a former wife, whom he married before Mary,” a tradition he traces to two named second-century texts, the Gospel according to Peter and the Book of James (the Protevangelium); those who so read it “wish to preserve the honour of Mary in virginity to the end,” and Origen adds in his own voice that he thinks it “in harmony with reason that Jesus was the first-fruit among men of the purity which consists in chastity, and Mary among women.”20 Elsewhere he glosses “the Lord’s brother” as owing something to James’s character and not only to kinship — not inconsistent with the reading, since it too denies a blood-tie to Mary. J. B. Lightfoot drew two conclusions: that Origen “declares himself very distinctly in favour of the Epiphanian view,” and that he was “wholly unaware of the Hieronymian, the only alternative which presented itself being the denial of the perpetual virginity” — strong evidence that Jerome’s cousins-theory did not yet exist.21

The Apostolic Constitutions — c. 380

By the later fourth century the “James not one of the Twelve” tradition had hardened into a fixed reckoning, and no text states it more plainly than the Syrian church order issued in the apostles’ own name, the Apostolic Constitutions — a large compilation put together around 380 out of older, partly third-century material. Its closing apostolic roster runs through the Twelve, “James the son of Alphaeus” among them, and then adds two more names past Matthias: “James the brother of the Lord and bishop of Jerusalem, and Paul the teacher of the Gentiles, the chosen vessel,” so that the Lord’s brother stands as a fourteenth apostle, a supernumerary like Paul.22 The same threefold reckoning — the Twelve, then Paul, then James — runs through the body of the work (ii.55, vii.46, viii.4), and the standard English editor noted that “the clear distinction it makes between James the son of Alphaeus and James the brother of our Lord” is itself “an incidental proof of the early origin of this compilation,” since “the theory of Jerome, which identifies them, was later.”22 J. B. Lightfoot set the work in his catena for exactly this feature — it distinguishes the two Jameses, “making him, like St. Paul, a supernumerary apostle, and thus counting fourteen in all” — which tells squarely against the Hieronymian reading that folds the Lord’s brother into the Twelve, while leaving the Epiphanian and Helvidian readings, which both keep him distinct, untouched.23
