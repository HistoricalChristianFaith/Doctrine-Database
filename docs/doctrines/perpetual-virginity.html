The Perpetual Virginity of Mary

Did Mary remain a virgin her whole life — ever-virgin, the mother of Jesus and of no one else — or did she and Joseph go on to have other children after his birth? The whole question turns on a single phrase. The Gospels and Epistles repeatedly name “the brethren of the Lord” (Matthew 13:55; Mark 6:3; John 7:5; Acts 1:14; 1 Corinthians 9:5; Galatians 1:19), and even list four of them by name — James, Joseph (Joses), Simon, and Jude — alongside unnamed sisters. Who were they? If they were Mary’s own later-born children, she was not ever-virgin; if they were kin in some looser sense, the perpetual virginity stands. Everything hangs on which reading of “brother” the evidence will bear.

Three historic answers dominate the tradition, and it is convenient to use the names J. B. Lightfoot gave them in his 1865 study of the question, after each view’s most zealous fourth-century advocate:1

  • The Helvidian view — the brethren are sons of Joseph and Mary, born after Jesus. This is the plain-sense reading of “brother,” and it denies the perpetual virginity. Named for Helvidius, who pressed it at Rome (c. 383) against the era’s exaltation of virginity; Tertullian is usually reckoned its earliest patristic representative.
  • The Epiphanian view — the brethren are sons of Joseph by a former wife, older step-brothers of Jesus and no blood-kin of Mary at all. This preserves Mary’s perpetual virginity while giving “brother” a natural family sense. Named for Epiphanius of Salamis, it claims the widest early attestation — the second-century Protevangelium of James, Clement, Origen, Eusebius, Gregory of Nyssa, and the Eastern churches — and Lightfoot judged it the reading best supported by tradition and by the Gospel evidence.
  • The Hieronymian view — the brethren are maternal cousins, sons of “Mary of Clopas,” the Virgin’s sister, so that “brother” means kinsman. This preserves the virginity of Mary and of Joseph alike. Named for Jerome, who first advanced it (c. 383) in his reply to Helvidius; it became the standard reading of the Latin West through Augustine and the Western service-books. Lightfoot argued it was the latest-born of the three and unknown before Jerome devised it.
