The Perpetual Virginity of Mary

Did Mary remain a virgin her whole life — ever-virgin, the mother of Jesus and of no one else — or did she and Joseph go on to have other children after his birth? The whole question turns on a single phrase. The Gospels and Epistles repeatedly name “the brethren of the Lord” (Matthew 13:55; Mark 6:3; John 7:5; Acts 1:14; 1 Corinthians 9:5; Galatians 1:19), and even list four of them by name — James, Joseph (Joses), Simon, and Jude — alongside unnamed sisters. Who were they? If they were Mary’s own later-born children, she was not ever-virgin; if they were kin in some looser sense, the perpetual virginity stands. Everything hangs on which reading of “brother” the evidence will bear.

Three historic answers dominate the tradition, and it is convenient to use the names J. B. Lightfoot gave them in his 1865 study of the question, after each view’s most zealous fourth-century advocate:1

  • The Helvidian view — the brethren are sons of Joseph and Mary, born after Jesus. This is the plain-sense reading of “brother,” and it denies the perpetual virginity. Named for Helvidius, who pressed it at Rome (c. 383) against the era’s exaltation of virginity; Tertullian is usually reckoned its earliest patristic representative.
  • The Epiphanian view — the brethren are sons of Joseph by a former wife, older step-brothers of Jesus and no blood-kin of Mary at all. This preserves Mary’s perpetual virginity while giving “brother” a natural family sense. Named for Epiphanius of Salamis, it claims the widest early attestation — the second-century Protevangelium of James, Clement, Origen, Eusebius, Gregory of Nyssa, and the Eastern churches — and Lightfoot judged it the reading best supported by tradition and by the Gospel evidence.
  • The Hieronymian view — the brethren are maternal cousins, sons of “Mary of Clopas,” the Virgin’s sister, so that “brother” means kinsman. This preserves the virginity of Mary and of Joseph alike. Named for Jerome, who first advanced it (c. 383) in his reply to Helvidius; it became the standard reading of the Latin West through Augustine and the Western service-books. Lightfoot argued it was the latest-born of the three and unknown before Jerome devised it.

The witnesses below are set out earliest first — each father slotted by date, with his own words where they survive — so that the drift of opinion, and the point at which Jerome’s new hypothesis reshaped the Western tradition, can be read off the timeline itself.

Timeline

The Gospel of Peter — c. 150

Named alongside the Protevangelium as a source of the very same idea is the Gospel of Peter — an apocryphal gospel whose own words on the brethren do not survive, so that the attestation is owed entirely to Origen, who reports that “some… basing it on a tradition in the Gospel according to Peter… or the Book of James, say that the brethren of Jesus were sons of Joseph by a former wife, whom he married before Mary,” those who say so wishing to “preserve the honour of Mary in virginity to the end.”2 The text Origen names was esteemed by the second-century Docetae and already long in circulation when Serapion of Antioch found it at Rhossus, which sets it “about the middle of the second century at the latest, and probably somewhat earlier” — a named text as old as the Protevangelium carrying the Epiphanian solution; but the attestation is only second-hand, so the Gospel of Peter’s own testimony remains ⚠ unverified.3

The Protevangelium of James — c. 150

The earliest witness whose own text survives entire is not a father but an anonymous infancy romance, the Protevangelium of James — falsely ascribed to James the Lord’s brother and usually placed near the middle of the second century, its testimony (unlike the Gospel of Peter’s) preserved in full. It is the fountainhead of the Epiphanian reading: it makes Joseph an aged widower who already “had children” before he was ever betrothed to Mary, so that the “brethren of the Lord” are his sons by a former marriage and no blood-kin of Mary at all.4 The same detail is carried through the narrative — Joseph enrols “my sons” at Bethlehem and leaves “his two sons” with Mary at the cave — while no daughter is ever named,5 and the closing colophon presents “James” the supposed author as already a grown man at Herod’s death.6 The book is legendary, not historical — J. B. Lightfoot judged it “a purely fictitious… narrative” — so its worth is as evidence that belief in Mary’s perpetual virginity, and the specific device of explaining the brethren as Joseph’s earlier children, were already in circulation by c. 150, seeding a whole family of later infancy apocrypha that copy it.7

Hegesippus — c. 175

The earliest historical witness — not a legend but a Palestinian Hebrew Christian who gathered the traditions of the Lord’s kindred — is Hegesippus, whose five books of Memoirs survive only where Eusebius quotes them. He is decisive because he keeps two relationships distinct: James and Jude are always styled the Lord’s brother, while Symeon, who succeeded James as bishop of Jerusalem, is the Lord’s cousin — “All proposed him as second bishop because he was a cousin of the Lord,” the son of Joseph’s brother Clopas.8 Eusebius twice repeats the point from him — Symeon “was a cousin, as they say, of the Saviour, for Hegesippus records that Clopas was a brother of Joseph.”9 Yet where Hegesippus names James he calls him “the brother of the Lord,” and Jude he calls one “who is said to have been the Lord’s brother according to the flesh.”10 Because the brethren (James, Jude) are thus never confused with the cousin (Symeon son of Clopas), the earliest Palestinian tradition tells against the Hieronymian reading, which makes the brethren themselves cousins; and by the qualifier “said to be,” Lightfoot judged Hegesippus to lean Epiphanian rather than Helvidian — “the testimony of Hegesippus seems distinctly opposed to the Hieronymian view, while of the other two it favours the Epiphanian rather than the Helvidian.”11

Clement of Alexandria — c. 200

The earliest father to state the Epiphanian reading outright and in his own voice is Clement of Alexandria. Commenting on the Epistle of Jude — in notes from his lost Hypotyposeis that survive in a sixth-century Latin abridgement by Cassiodorus — he explains why Jude, though kin to the Lord, styles himself only “brother of James”: because he was one of Joseph’s sons and reverence forbade the nearer claim. “Jude, who wrote the Catholic Epistle, the brother of the sons of Joseph… did not say that he himself was His brother… he was His brother, (the son) of Joseph.”12 The statement must be Clement’s own and not the translator’s, for Cassiodorus, a Latin churchman, held the rival Hieronymian view and would have purged such a line rather than insert it. A separate Greek extract in Eusebius, where Clement notes that “there were two Jameses,” has been claimed for the Hieronymian side, but it “neither states nor implies that there were two only” and does not contradict the plain note on Jude; J. B. Lightfoot judged Clement’s Epiphanian statement “explicit” and set him second, after the Gospel of Peter and Protevangelium, among the “sons of Joseph by a former wife” witnesses.13

Tertullian — c. 210

The earliest father usually reckoned a Helvidian — the first to read the “brethren” as Mary’s own later children — is Tertullian, though he states it nowhere as a thesis: it falls out of his anti-Marcionite polemic. Insisting against Marcion and Apelles that Christ was truly born, he argues that the mother and brethren who “stood without” must have really been His — “if… He made them ‘His mother and His brethren’ who were not so, how could He deny them these relationships who really had them?”14 His argument, as J. B. Lightfoot put it, “seems to imply that the Lord’s brethren were His brothers in the same sense in which Mary was His mother.” And where he speaks of Mary’s virginity directly, he confines it to the conception and denies it survived the birth: “although she was a virgin when she conceived, she was a wife when she brought forth her son… under the very law of ‘opening the womb,’” a virgin only “about to marry once for all after her delivery.”15 Helvidius later claimed his authority, and Jerome, unable to deny it, could only retort that Tertullian was “not a man of the Church” — so that Lightfoot judged it “highly probable” (though, he cautioned, not absolutely certain) that this “strenuous… advocate of asceticism” held the Helvidian view.16 That he denied a virginity outlasting the birth is uncontested; whether he positively taught the Helvidian brethren-reading is disputed — weighed at “Is Tertullian the earliest Helvidian?” (assessment: contested).

The Clementine Literature — c. 220

Between Tertullian and Origen stands a witness of a wholly different kind: the Pseudo-Clementine literature, the romance of a Jewish-Christian (Ebionite) Christianity whose extant Greek Homilies and Latin Recognitions descend from a third-century source drawing on second-century Ebionite writings. It is no friend of the doctrine — these circles denied the virgin birth — yet it bears on the brethren question at one narrow point, for it exalts James the Lord’s brother and keeps him rigorously apart from the Twelve. The prefixed Epistle of Clement hails James as “the bishop of bishops, who rules Jerusalem,” and the Homilies make him, “called the brother of my Lord,” the standard by which every “apostle or teacher or prophet” must be tested.17 The Recognitions — in a Temple-disputation scene founded on the early Ebionite Ascents of James — states the distinction outright: the twelve apostles, “James the son of Alphaeus” among them, dispute in turn and report to “James, the chief of the bishops,” the Lord’s brother who presides over them.18 J. B. Lightfoot set the work in his catena for just this feature: James is “distinguished from and in some respects exalted above the Twelve,” while “James the son of Alphaeus is distinctly mentioned among the Twelve as one of the disputants” — so that, like Hegesippus, the Clementines tell against the Hieronymian reading that folds the Lord’s brother into the Twelve, while leaving the Epiphanian and Helvidian readings, which both keep him distinct, untouched.19

Origen — c. 248

The fullest early exponent of the Epiphanian reading — and the first to give it a documentary pedigree — is Origen, Clement’s successor at Alexandria. Commenting on Matthew 13:55 he reports and endorses the view that “the brethren of Jesus were sons of Joseph by a former wife, whom he married before Mary,” a tradition he traces to two named second-century texts, the Gospel according to Peter and the Book of James (the Protevangelium); those who so read it “wish to preserve the honour of Mary in virginity to the end,” and Origen adds in his own voice that he thinks it “in harmony with reason that Jesus was the first-fruit among men of the purity which consists in chastity, and Mary among women.”20 Elsewhere he glosses “the Lord’s brother” as owing something to James’s character and not only to kinship — not inconsistent with the reading, since it too denies a blood-tie to Mary. J. B. Lightfoot drew two conclusions: that Origen “declares himself very distinctly in favour of the Epiphanian view,” and that he was “wholly unaware of the Hieronymian, the only alternative which presented itself being the denial of the perpetual virginity” — strong evidence that Jerome’s cousins-theory did not yet exist.21

Victorinus of Pettau — c. 300

One name in Lightfoot’s catena resists every camp: Victorinus of Pettau (Poetovio, martyred c. 304), the earliest Latin commentator on Scripture, whose word on the brethren both sides of the later Roman controversy tried to claim. Helvidius quoted him for the reading that the brethren were Mary’s own sons; Jerome, unwilling to leave the appeal unanswered, denied that sense and counterclaimed him — “as regards Victorinus, I assert… that he spoke of the brethren of the Lord not as being sons of Mary, but brethren in… kinship, not by nature.”22 But his own comment does not survive, and his two extant works — the Commentary on the Apocalypse and On the Creation of the World — honour Mary’s virginity in the conception (the Son “has at this time taken a beginning from the Virgin”) while saying nothing whatever of who the brethren were. That a Helvidian and Jerome could each honestly enlist the same man is itself the clue: J. B. Lightfoot inferred that Victorinus “did little more than repeat the statements of the evangelists respecting the Lord’s brethren,” naming them without deciding whose sons they were, and in his conspectus files Victorinus of Pettau under the lone heading Uncertain — so that his own reading of the disputed point remains ⚠ unverified. (He is not the later Marius Victorinus the Philosopher, c. 360, a separate and definite witness who read Galatians 1:19 as denying that James was an apostle.)23

Eusebius of Caesarea — c. 324

The fullest witness of the early fourth century — and the man through whose quotation the older Palestinian tradition survives at all — is Eusebius of Caesarea, and he reads that tradition as the Epiphanian tradition does. He explains that James “was called the brother of the Lord because he was known as a son of Joseph, and Joseph was supposed to be the father of Christ” — a reputed brotherhood built on Joseph’s reputed fatherhood, so that James is Joseph’s son and no blood-kin of Mary at all.24 And he everywhere reckons James the Lord’s brother a supernumerary apostle like Paul — “one of the so-called brethren of the Saviour,” numbered among “many others who were called apostles, in imitation of the Twelve, as was Paul himself” (i.12), and holder of an episcopal chair received “from the Saviour himself and the apostles” (vii.19) — which, like Hegesippus, tells against the Hieronymian reading that folds him into the Twelve. J. B. Lightfoot accordingly set Eusebius in his conspectus of the “sons of Joseph by a former wife” authorities, next after Origen, and judged that his and Epiphanius’ dependence on Hegesippus “materially strengthens” the case that the earliest Palestinian evidence leans Epiphanian.25

Cyril of Jerusalem — c. 350

A quieter Eastern witness of the same generation is Cyril of Jerusalem, whose Catechetical Lectures (c. 350) never pose the brethren-question but twice handle James the Lord’s brother as a figure apart from the Twelve. Rehearsing the resurrection appearances of 1 Corinthians 15 as a mounting ladder of witnesses — Cephas, then the Twelve, then the five hundred, then James, then Paul — he names “James, His own brother, and first Bishop of this diocese” as a step beyond the Twelve, and elsewhere couples “the Apostles” and “James the bishop of this Church” as distinct parties.26 That places him, like Hegesippus, the Clementine writings, and Marius Victorinus, among the witnesses for “James the Just not one of the Twelve,” and so against the Hieronymian reading that folds the Lord’s brother into the Twelve — while leaving the Epiphanian and Helvidian readings, which both keep him distinct, untouched. But the point is drawn by inference from Cyril’s language, not stated by him; J. B. Lightfoot, who filed him under that heading with a query — “Cyril of Jerusalem (?)” — cautioned that “as this… is only an inference from his language, and not a direct statement of his own, too much stress must not be laid on it,” so his witness on the disputed point remains ⚠ a guarded inference.27

Hilary of Poitiers — c. 353

The Epiphanian reading reaches the Latin West a full generation before Jerome in Hilary of Poitiers, whose Commentary on Matthew — the earliest Latin exposition of a Gospel to survive almost intact, written about 353 — is the first work by a Latin father to answer the brethren question in his own voice. He makes them Joseph’s sons by an earlier marriage and argues it from the cross: “if there had been sons of Mary who were not rather produced from a previous marriage of Joseph’s, Mary never would have been transferred to the apostle John as his mother at the time of the Passion” — the care of a son being needless had Mary’s own sons survived to give it.28 And the negative testimony weighs as much: a learned bishop refuting the deniers of the virginity about 353 knows only two options, the Epiphanian solution and the Helvidian denial, and reaches for the former; of the Hieronymian cousins-theory he betrays no knowledge whatever. J. B. Lightfoot concluded that Hilary “not only adopts the Epiphanian solution, but shows himself entirely ignorant of the Hieronymian” — one of the “two Hilaries” (with the Ambrosiaster) whose silence tells against Jerome’s reading having been heard of before Jerome devised it — and set him in his conspectus of the “sons of Joseph by a former wife” authorities, next after Eusebius.29

Marius Victorinus the Philosopher — c. 360

The first Latin commentator on St Paul — the aged rhetorician Marius Victorinus, whose late conversion Augustine would celebrate — reads the “James not one of the Twelve” question straight off the grammar of Galatians 1:19. Paul writes that at Jerusalem he saw “other of the apostles… none, save James the Lord’s brother,” and Victorinus takes the “save” (Greek ei mē) as opposition, not exception: it shuts James out of the apostolic number rather than counting him in. He says so flatly — “Therefore Paul here denied that James was an apostle… when he called him brother, he denied him to be an apostle” — and sets the reading against the Symmachiani, the Jewish-Christian sect that “make James an apostle, as it were a twelfth.”30 James is the Lord’s brother “according to the flesh” and no apostle, visited by Paul only “out of honour.” J. B. Lightfoot set him in the catena for just this — he “takes ei mē… as expressing not exception but opposition, and distinctly states that James was not an Apostle” — classing him with Hegesippus, the Clementine writings, and the Apostolic Constitutions under “James the Just not one of the Twelve,” a witness against the Hieronymian reading that folds the Lord’s brother into the Twelve, while leaving the Epiphanian and Helvidian readings, which both keep him distinct, untouched.31

Athanasius of Alexandria — c. 360

The most authoritative Greek theologian of the age gives the timeline something the brethren-witnesses do not: not an argument for the doctrine but an assumption of it. Athanasius of Alexandria, defending the reality of Christ’s flesh against the Arians in his Four Discourses Against the Arians (c. 356–360), calls the Lord’s mother “Mary Ever-Virgin” (Greek aēiparthenos) as a settled, uncontroversial premise: “let those who deny that the Son is from the Father by nature… deny also that He took true human flesh of Mary Ever-Virgin.”32 The point under dispute is the reality of the flesh — against the Valentinian docetists on one side (“though Valentinus rave”) and the Arians on the other — not Mary’s virginity, which is simply presupposed as part of her standard title. A witness who argues for a doctrine shows it was contested; one who merely assumes it shows it was established — so this incidental use, from mid-fourth-century Alexandria and a full generation before Helvidius raised the challenge at Rome (c. 383), attests how fixed the perpetual-virginity title already was in the Greek East. It bears on the perpetual virginity directly but leaves the brethren-genealogy untouched: Athanasius takes no side between the Epiphanian and Hieronymian accounts.33

Eunomius of Cyzicus — c. 360

The one named head the “against” camp can set in the mid-fourth-century Greek East is Eunomius of Cyzicus, the sharp-tongued leader of the Anomoeans — the “Neo-Arian” extreme that held the Son unlike (anomoios) the Father in essence. A generation after his brief episcopate an anonymous, Arian-leaning homilist — the author of the Incomplete Commentary on Matthew (Opus imperfectum, c. 400) — expounding “he knew her not until she had given birth to a son,” reports that “some people, following Eunomius, think that until Mary gave birth Joseph did not have carnal relations with her, but afterwards he knew her and she gave birth to children.”34 That is the plain Helvidian denial, and it sets Eunomius up as a fourth-century Eastern head for it, beside the Roman Helvidius and the Arabian Antidicomarianites. But the attribution comes only from this later, unsympathetic notice — the homilist himself rejects the reading — and nothing on Mary survives from Eunomius’s own hand; so his position is, on the strict evidence, ⚠ unverified. It rests, characteristically, on Matthew 1:25’s “until,” the very proof-text weighed among the arguments below.35

Ephraem the Syrian — c. 363

The Epiphanian reading’s Syriac witness is Ephraem the Syrian (Ephrem), the deacon-poet of Nisibis and Edessa and the greatest of the Syriac fathers, who in his Commentary on the Acts of the Apostles — the Syriac lost, surviving in an Armenian version — treats the brethren as sons of Joseph and no children of Mary: expounding the apostle-roll of Acts 1:13 he calls Jude “son of Joseph, who also was brother of the Lord,” and Simon likewise “son of Joseph father of the Lord, and brother of the Lord,” so that his perpetual-virginity stance is the stepbrother account of Joseph’s prior family.36 But he differs sharply from the timeline’s other Epiphanians on a second point: where Hegesippus, the Clementines, Marius Victorinus, Cyril of Jerusalem, and the Apostolic Constitutions keep the Lord’s brethren apart from the Twelve, Ephraem folds two of them into it. He reads the apostle “Judas of James” (Luke 6:16; Acts 1:13) as Jude the brother of James — the Lord’s brother and author of the Epistle of Jude, and the same man Matthew and Mark call Lebbaeus and Thaddaeus — and the apostle “Simon the Zealot” (the Cananaean) as Simon the Lord’s brother, while keeping both distinct from the apostle Judas Thomas. So a respected fourth-century Syrian father does place two of the brethren (Jude and Simon) among the Twelve — a genuine early witness that cuts against the “brethren distinct from the Twelve” corollary, though on the Epiphanian basis (they are Joseph’s sons, not cousins), not the Hieronymian. It complicates that corollary without overturning it: Ephraem does not fold in James, and the reading “Judas of James” = Jude the brother is the less-natural filling of the ellipsis (the earliest versions read “son”).36

The Antidicomarianites — c. 370

The timeline’s one named party for the Helvidian denial — as against the individual writers who lean that way — is the Antidicomarianites, an obscure sect of the Arabian churches in the later fourth century who “maintained that the Lord’s mother bore children to her husband Joseph.”37 They wrote nothing that survives: the sect is known only from Epiphanius of Salamis, who came forward against them — “not very long before Jerome wrote… against the Helvidians at Rome” — in a pastoral letter he later folded into his catalogue of heresies as Panarion 78. There he reports the very claim he means to refute, that “certain persons are venturing to say that she had marital relations after the Savior’s birth,” and answers it with the Epiphanian solution the sect took its name from opposing: the Virgin was “entrusted” to Joseph, an aged widower with children, “not… for marriage… He was called her husband because of the Law.”38 Because the party’s teaching survives only in this hostile summary, its own voice remains ⚠ unverified; but the position is unmistakably Helvidian, and J. B. Lightfoot files it accordingly in his conspectus under “sons of Joseph and Mary,” beside Tertullian and Helvidius — while cautioning that, though Augustine equated the sect with Helvidius’ Roman party, “considerations of time and place alike resist this identification.” A contrary Arabian reaction, in the same years, produced the Collyridians, who swung to the other extreme and “paid divine honours to the Virgin.”39

Ambrosiaster — c. 375

A second Latin voice for the Epiphanian reading, close on the heels of Hilary of Poitiers and still before Jerome, is Ambrosiaster — the modern name for the anonymous Roman commentator on Paul whose work long passed under Ambrose’s name, and whom J. B. Lightfoot dubbed “the Ambrosian Hilary.” Expounding “James the Lord’s brother” (Galatians 1:19), he makes James a son of Joseph and reads the title exactly as he reads “son of Joseph” applied to Jesus — a reputed relationship, not a blood-tie to Mary: “This James was the son of Joseph, who was therefore called the brother of the Lord, because Joseph was also called the father of Him, the Lord.”40 He clinches it with a reductio against the deniers of the virginity: the two titles stand or fall together, so “if these were His true brothers, then Joseph will be His true father” — which no Christian will grant. Like the other Latins before Jerome he knows only two options, the Epiphanian solution and the Helvidian denial, and betrays no knowledge of the Hieronymian cousins-theory; J. B. Lightfoot judged that “his testimony entirely coincides with that of his greater namesake” Hilary of Poitiers — the second of the “two Hilaries” whose silence tells against Jerome’s reading — and set him in his conspectus of the “sons of Joseph by a former wife” authorities, next after Hilary and before Gregory of Nyssa.41

Basil of Caesarea (Basil the Great) — c. 377

The timeline’s frankest witness — a father who holds Mary’s perpetual virginity firmly yet concedes it is not a required article of faith — is Basil the Great, bishop of Caesarea in Cappadocia. Preaching on the Nativity, he faces the hard word of Matthew 1:25 (Joseph “knew her not till she had brought forth”), grants that it might be read as the Helvidian reads it, and says outright that such a conclusion “would not have affected the teaching of our religion at all,” since Mary’s virginity was necessary only up to the service of the Incarnation. He nonetheless affirms the ever-virginity, resting it on the mind of the Church rather than on exegetical necessity: “since the lovers of Christ do not allow themselves to hear that the Mother of God (Theotokos) ceased at a given moment to be a virgin, we consider their testimony to be sufficient.”42 As to who the brethren were Basil does not expressly say, so J. B. Lightfoot files him in his conspectus simply among the witnesses for the “perpetual virginity of Mary” who leave the brethren-question open; but from Basil’s supporting appeal to an apocryphal story — that Zacharias was slain for testifying to Mary’s virginity, a tale close to the Protevangelium (§§ 23–24) — Lightfoot infers that he took the Epiphanian account which ran through those infancy gospels. That last step is Lightfoot’s inference from a detail he reports rather than one quoted here from Basil, so Basil’s brethren-view remains, on the strict evidence, ⚠ unverified.43

Epiphanius of Salamis — c. 377

The father whose name the reading itself carries is Epiphanius of Salamis, bishop in Cyprus, whose refutation of the Antidicomarianites in Panarion 78 (c. 377) is the fullest fourth-century statement of the Epiphanian view — the reading J. B. Lightfoot named after him. He did not invent it — it runs back through the infancy gospels, Clement, Origen, and Eusebius — but he set it out in the greatest detail: Joseph was a widower of “over eighty” when the Virgin was entrusted to him, already father of six children by a former wife — “Joseph begot James when he was somewhere around forty years old. After him he had a son named Joses—then Simeon after him, then Judah, and two daughters, one named Mary and one, Salome; and his wife died.”44 The brethren are so called, he argues, because they were the elder sons of that household and Joseph its reputed father, not because Mary bore them; and the Virgin remained ever-virgin — a point he pressed even to the curious argument that the mother of the Lion of Judah, like a lioness, could bear but a single cub. Yet, discussing the brethren-question again and again and at length, Epiphanius betrays no knowledge whatever of the Hieronymian cousins-theory; J. B. Lightfoot — who set him in the “sons of Joseph by a former wife” column of his conspectus, and judged that the shared dependence of Eusebius and Epiphanius on Hegesippus “materially strengthens” the Epiphanian case — counted his silence, with Origen’s and the “two Hilaries’,” as evidence that Jerome’s reading did not yet exist.45

The Apostolic Constitutions — c. 380

By the later fourth century the “James not one of the Twelve” tradition had hardened into a fixed reckoning, and no text states it more plainly than the Syrian church order issued in the apostles’ own name, the Apostolic Constitutions — a large compilation put together around 380 out of older, partly third-century material. Its closing apostolic roster runs through the Twelve, “James the son of Alphaeus” among them, and then adds two more names past Matthias: “James the brother of the Lord and bishop of Jerusalem, and Paul the teacher of the Gentiles, the chosen vessel,” so that the Lord’s brother stands as a fourteenth apostle, a supernumerary like Paul.46 The same threefold reckoning — the Twelve, then Paul, then James — runs through the body of the work (ii.55, vii.46, viii.4), and the standard English editor noted that “the clear distinction it makes between James the son of Alphaeus and James the brother of our Lord” is itself “an incidental proof of the early origin of this compilation,” since “the theory of Jerome, which identifies them, was later.”46 J. B. Lightfoot set the work in his catena for exactly this feature — it distinguishes the two Jameses, “making him, like St. Paul, a supernumerary apostle, and thus counting fourteen in all” — which tells squarely against the Hieronymian reading that folds the Lord’s brother into the Twelve, while leaving the Epiphanian and Helvidian readings, which both keep him distinct, untouched.47

Gregory of Nyssa — c. 382

A third Cappadocian voice — Basil’s younger brother — takes the same Epiphanian reading but layers a peculiar twist on it. Gregory of Nyssa, in a festal homily On the Resurrection of Christ, “certainly adopted the Epiphanian account” — the brethren are Joseph’s sons, not Mary’s — and he keeps the Lord’s brother clear of the Twelve, reading Mark’s “James the little” (Mark 15:40) as marked off from “James the son of Alphaeus” the “great,” who was an apostle, “which the Lord’s brother was not.”48 But he joins to it a much-criticized identification: the “Mary the mother of James and Joses” of the Passion narratives (Matthew 27:56; Mark 15:40) is, he says, “the mother of God and none else” — the Virgin styled so not because she bore them but because she “undertook the education of these her stepsons,” Joseph’s sons by his former wife. J. B. Lightfoot, who filed Gregory in his catena of the “sons of Joseph by a former wife” authorities next after Ambrosiaster, judged that Marian identification “very untenable” — a conjecture beyond the evidence, paralleled in Chrysostom and in an infancy apocryphon but sitting awkwardly on the Epiphanian scheme — while leaving Gregory’s Epiphanian core intact. The resurrection homily survives in Migne’s Patrologia Graeca (In Christi resurrectionem oratio ii, PG 46, col. 647–648), where Gregory’s own Greek bears out the report; the one lingering caveat is that this oration is of disputed authenticity, excluded from the modern critical edition as a dubium, so the doubt is whether the sermon is Gregory’s, not whether it says what is reported.49 A second and firsthand strand of his Marian teaching survives in his Homily on the Nativity of Christ, where he reads Mary’s “How shall this be, seeing I know not a man?” (Luke 1:34) as proof that she had already consecrated her virginity to God — the oldest witness to the “vow” reading of that verse, weighed among the arguments below.115

Helvidius — c. 383

The man who gave the Helvidian reading its name — and whose treatise provoked the reply that created Jerome’s rival theory — is Helvidius, a layman of Rome who about 383 published a tract maintaining that the “brethren of the Lord” were the true sons of Joseph and Mary, born after Jesus, so that Mary was not ever-virgin. His aim was polemical: to check the age’s exaltation of virgin over married life by the example of the Lord’s own mother, who (he held) had gone on to bear children to her husband. His treatise is lost, surviving only where Jerome quotes it to refute it in The Perpetual Virginity of Blessed Mary, Against Helvidius — so his own words come to us at second hand and remain ⚠ unverified.50 Jerome preserves his arguments: from Matthew’s “knew her not till she had brought forth” and her “first-born son” Helvidius pressed that both phrases imply later children, and he cried that “the same persons are there brethren of the Lord and sons of Mary”; for older warrant he appealed to Tertullian and Victorinus of Pettau. He convinced enough people that contemporaries spoke of the Helvidians as a party — like-minded voices followed in Bonosus of Sardica and the monk Jovinian — and J. B. Lightfoot files him in his conspectus under “sons of Joseph and Mary,” at the head of the Helvidian column beside Tertullian and the Antidicomarianites. It was Jerome’s answer to him that, on Lightfoot’s reckoning, first launched the rival Hieronymian cousins-theory into the world.51

Jerome — c. 383

The reading that would govern the Latin West for a thousand years was minted in reply to Helvidius by Jerome, whose The Perpetual Virginity of Blessed Mary, Against Helvidius (c. 383) first advanced the Hieronymian theory: the brethren are the Lord’s maternal cousins, sons of “Mary of Clopas” the Virgin’s sister, so that “brother” means kinsman and the virginity of Mary and Joseph alike is preserved. He grounded it on the range of the word in Scripture — “In Holy Scripture there are four kinds of brethren—by nature, race, kindred, love” (of which the third, kinship, fits the brethren, as when Abraham calls his nephew Lot “brother”) — and on identifying “the Mary who is described as the mother of James the less” with “the wife of Alphaeus and sister of Mary the Lord’s mother… called by John the Evangelist ‘Mary of Clopas,’” making the brethren her sons and the Lord’s cousins.52 He pressed the reading to its limit — “I claim still more, that Joseph himself on account of Mary was a virgin, so that from a virgin wedlock a virgin son was born” — and restated it firmly a decade later in Lives of Illustrious Men (392): James is “the son of Joseph by another wife, as some think, but, as appears to me, the son of Mary sister of the mother of our Lord.” Yet Jerome could name no father who had taught the cousins-reading before him: challenged for authorities he could only gesture at “the whole series of ancient writers—Ignatius, Polycarp, Irenaeus, Justin Martyr, and many other apostolic and eloquent men” without quoting one of them to the point, and dismissed Helvidius’ witness Tertullian only as one who “did not belong to the Church.” J. B. Lightfoot — who gave the reading Jerome’s name — judged it the latest-born of the three theories and struck out for the occasion, on the strength that “no decisive instance can be produced of a writer holding Jerome’s view, before it was propounded by Jerome himself”; and he traced Jerome’s own grip loosening over time — vague in the Commentary on Galatians (387), firm again in 392 and 398, but by the Letter to Hedibia (c. 406) listing Mary of Clopas and the mother of James and Joses as distinct women, so that the very identification “which was the keystone of his own theory” he now named “with such indifference.”53

Didymus the Blind — c. 386

From Alexandria, and in the same years Jerome was minting his cousins-theory at Rome, comes an Eastern voice reading the deniers’ own proof-text the other way: Didymus the Blind, the blind exegete who headed the Alexandrian catechetical school for half a century. In the treatise On the Trinity (c. 386) traditionally ascribed to him, he turns to Matthew 1:25 to illustrate the terms “first-born” and “only-begotten” and reads “until she brought forth her first-born son” as no bar to a virginity outlasting the birth: Mary “neither… marry anyone else, nor did she ever become the Mother of anyone else, but even after childbirth she remained always and forever an immaculate virgin.”54 That places him beside Chrysostom and Chromatius in reading the Matthean “till” and “firstborn” as no bar to the perpetual virginity — the legs weighed at the infancy-language argument (assessment: weak) and, for the narrower modern refinement, the “heōs hou” argument (assessment: weak) — and squarely against the Helvidian denial. Like Athanasius before him he settles the virginity itself, not the identity of the brethren, and so cannot be filed by camp. (The attribution of On the Trinity to Didymus is disputed on stylistic grounds; whoever wrote it, the passage is a genuine fourth-century Alexandrian reading of Matthew 1:25.)55

Bonosus of Sardica and Jovinian — c. 390

Two further Western voices rose against the perpetual virginity in the same decade as Helvidius, and J. B. Lightfoot files both in the Helvidian column of his conspectus — though he marks the second with a query, for they did not hold one position. Bonosus, bishop of Sardica in Illyria, is reported to have held the plain Helvidian reading, that Mary bore children to Joseph after Jesus; condemned by the synod of Capua (392), he left no text, so his teaching survives only in Lightfoot’s notice and the conciliar record and remains ⚠ unverified.56 Jovinian — an anti-ascetic monk who taught at Rome, condemned by synods at Rome and Milan about 390 — is the better attested but the stranger witness: his campaign was against the exaltation of virginity over marriage (“a virgin is no better as such than a wife”), and his error about Mary was not the Helvidian claim of later children but a denial of her virginity in the birth itself (in partu): “She was a virgin when she conceived, but not a virgin when she brought forth.” That formula, which the Council of Milan pointedly noted “differs from that of Helvidius,” is preserved firsthand in the synod’s reply to Pope Siricius drawn up by Ambrose, who answered it with the shut “gate” of Ezekiel 44; Jovinian’s wider case Jerome refuted at length in the two books Against Jovinianus.57

Ambrose of Milan — c. 392

The last of the great Latins to take up the brethren before Jerome’s reading swept the West is Ambrose of Milan, who devoted a treatise — On the Instruction of the Virgin and the Perpetual Virginity of Mary (De Institutione Virginis, c. 392) — to refuting the deniers of the perpetual virginity, the party that had gathered around Bonosus of Sardica. Reaching the “brethren of the Lord,” Ambrose takes the Epiphanian line — “brother” is a wide term (Psalm 22:22; Romans 9:3), so “the brothers could be from Joseph, not from Mary” — and he even allows that one who looks into it “will find” this to be so; but he pointedly refuses to press the point: “We did not consider it necessary to pursue this, since it is clear that the name of brotherhood is common to many.”58 He asserts the virginity without reserve — Mary is the “closed gate” of Ezekiel 44 that “shall not be opened by Joseph” — while leaving the exact standing of the brethren an open door. J. B. Lightfoot read that reticence as deliberate: Ambrose “had heard of, though possibly not read, Jerome’s tract,” would not “abandon the view with which he was familiar… in favour of the novel hypothesis put forward by Jerome,” and, prizing “coöperation against a common enemy,” declined to divide the champions of the virginity “by the discussion of details” — so Lightfoot set him last in the “sons of Joseph by a former wife” column of his conspectus, after Epiphanius.59

Augustine of Hippo — c. 394 → later

The witness whose weight settled the question for the Latin West is Augustine, and with him Jerome’s new cousins-theory passes from a controversialist’s hypothesis into the standard reading. In his early Commentary on Galatians (on Galatians 1:19, c. 394, while still a presbyter) he left the alternative open — James “is to be understood either as [one] of the sons of Joseph by another wife” (the Epiphanian account) “or as of the kindred of Mary his mother” (the kinsman reading Jerome had pressed into the cousins-theory), committing to neither; but in his mature works he holds Jerome’s reading without wavering.60 Expounding John 2:12 he lays it down as a rule of scriptural usage: “The kinsmen of Mary, of whatever degree, are the brethren of the Lord… when thou hast known this rule, thou wilt find that all the blood relations of Mary are the brethren of Christ” — guarding the virginity in the same breath (“surely Mary did not give birth a second time? Far from it! With her begins the dignity of virgins”).61 Against the Manichaean Faustus he is blunter still: those “called in the Gospel the Lord’s brothers are certainly not children of the Virgin Mary, but all the blood relations of the Lord.”62 He does not add the later refinement that identifies Clopas with Alphaeus — that step, Lightfoot notes, “does not anywhere appear” in Augustine — but his adoption of the core theory was decisive: “thus supported, it won its way to general acceptance in the Latin Church,” and Lightfoot files him in his conspectus under the Hieronymian column, “Sons of the Virgin’s Sister,” between Jerome and Theodoret.60

John Chrysostom — c. 398

The East’s point of transition is written across the works of a single father, John Chrysostom, who — as J. B. Lightfoot observed — “wrote both before and after Jerome’s treatise had become generally known, and his expositions of the New Testament mark a period of transition.” In the Antiochene homilies he is plainly Epiphanian: expounding Matthew 1:25 he defends the perpetual virginity (Joseph “knew her not till…” fixes only what was true before the birth, and “not even after this… could that righteous man ever have endured to know her”) and, asked in what sense the “brethren” are brethren, answers that they are so called only as Joseph was reputed husband — “How then… are James and the others called His brethren? In the same kind of way as Joseph himself was supposed to be husband of Mary” — no blood-kin of Mary at all; the same reading recurs in his homilies on John 7:5 and 1 Corinthians 9:5, which keep the Lord’s brother distinct from the Twelve, and betray no knowledge of the Hieronymian account.63 But in the Commentary on Galatians (on 1:19) he turns: Paul, he says, might have named James “the son of Cleophas, as the Evangelist does” — grafting Jerome’s cousins-identification onto the older scheme, and, by taking Clopas for the apostolic Alphaeus, appearing to complete it. So Chrysostom witnesses on both sides of the divide — Epiphanian early, Hieronymian late — and Lightfoot alone among his authorities enters “[Chrysostom]” in both columns of his conspectus, though he judged the Galatians passage “somewhat confused” and perhaps interpolated. Throughout, on either side of the shift, Mary is held to have borne no child but Jesus.64

Pelagius — c. 406

The first commentator to fall in behind Jerome’s new reading is Pelagius, who expounded Galatians 1:19 at Rome only a few years after Jerome — though which Pelagius did so is a textual question. His genuine note, recovered by Alexander Souter from beneath the later accretions, is terse and aimed at the deniers of the virginity: “Against those who say that blessed Mary had other sons — for we read that there were two apostles named James, one the son of Alphaeus and the other of Zebedee, neither a son of Mary or of Joseph; rather, they are called the Lord’s brothers from kinship (de propinquitate).”65 That is the Hieronymian substance — Mary bears no later children, the brethren reduced to kinsmen — but stripped of Jerome’s apparatus: Pelagius neither names “Mary of Clopas” nor rehearses the fourfold sense of “brother.” The fuller gloss that does parrot Jerome’s “four kinds of brethren” and identifies James as a son of “Mary of Clopas, the sister of the Lord’s mother” survives only in the interpolated Pseudo-Jerome recension and the St Gall manuscript — the very tampering J. B. Lightfoot suspected when he wrote that Pelagius “adopts his theory and even his language… unless his text has been tampered with here.” Souter’s disentangling of the genuine Pelagius from the Pseudo-Jerome interpolations bears the suspicion out; and Lightfoot files Pelagius in his conspectus under column C, “Sons of the Virgin’s Sister,” the first name to follow Jerome’s into it.66

Chromatius of Aquileia — c. 407

A Latin contemporary of Pelagius — Chromatius, bishop of Aquileia and friend of Jerome, Rufinus, Ambrose, and Chrysostom — meets the perpetual-virginity question not at the brethren but at the other great proof-text, the “until” of Matthew 1:25. Preaching on the verse in his Tractates on Matthew, he rebuts head-on the inference that “he knew her not till she had borne her firstborn son” implies Joseph knew her afterward: “not a few careless people insist on asking whether after the Lord’s birth the holy mother Mary had relations with Joseph. But this is not admissible on the grounds of either faith or truth… Far be it indeed that after the sacrament of so great a mystery and after the birth of the sublime Lord, one should believe that the Virgin Mary was intimate with a man.” He backs it not with grammar but with Old-Testament type — Miriam who stayed a virgin unsullied after beholding the heavenly signs, Noah and Moses who abstained after they were made worthy to speak with God, so much the more the Virgin who was “worthy of carrying him in her virginal womb.”67 That sets him beside his Eastern contemporary Chrysostom as a patristic voice reading the Matthean “till” as no bar to a virginity outlasting the birth — the reading weighed at the heōs hou argument (assessment: weak) — and squarely against the Helvidian denial. But his witness stops there: he settles that Mary bore no other children, not who the “brethren” were, and his surviving tractates never reach a brethren-solution — so, unlike Pelagius, Chromatius cannot be filed Epiphanian or Hieronymian; he attests the perpetual virginity itself while leaving the three-camp question untouched.68

Leporius — c. 426

How settled the perpetual virginity had become in the Latin West by the 420s is shown not by a treatise defending it but by a retraction that simply confesses it. Leporius, a Gallic monk and priest who had taught Pelagian and proto-Nestorian errors about the person of Christ, was set right in Africa by Aurelius of Carthage and Augustine, and drew up under their eye a Document of Amendment (Libellus emendationis, c. 426). Rehearsing the two births of the one Son, his confession names the Lord’s mother with the perpetual-virginity title as a fixed clause of the creed: Christ is “born of the Father before the ages, and in times most recent, made man of the Holy Spirit and the ever-virgin Mary” (semper virgo).69 That a disciplinary text, weighed word by word and offered as proof of a corrected faith, reaches for semper virgo as the natural style for Mary marks the phrase as settled confession, not party slogan. Like Athanasius, Didymus, and Chromatius, Leporius attests the virginity itself and leaves the brethren-question — Epiphanian or Hieronymian — untouched.

Cyril of Alexandria — c. 428

That the Eastern flirtation with Jerome’s cousins-theory was a passing episode, not a conversion, is shown by Cyril of Alexandria, patriarch of the East’s chief see and roughly the contemporary of Theodoret, who reads the “brethren of the Lord” the old Epiphanian way. Expounding John 7:3–5, he explains the brethren’s unbelief by the low estimate they still had of Jesus, whom they reckoned a mere kinsman “in truth begotten of their father Joseph”; and pressing Jeremiah’s type “the house of thy father,” he fixes whose sons they were — “Very watchfully did the Prophet, having named His brethren, profitably add, The house of Thy father, lest they too should be supposed to have been of the blessed Virgin, rather than of His father Joseph alone.”70 The brethren belong to “His father Joseph alone” and are no children of the Virgin — a brotherhood only reputed, resting on Joseph’s reputed fatherhood, exactly as Eusebius and Epiphanius had framed it. J. B. Lightfoot cites Cyril (from the seventh book of the Glaphyra on Genesis, a passage reported here only at his hand and so on its own ⚠ unverified, though the firsthand Commentary on John settles the point) as evidence that, the late Chrysostom and Theodoret aside, “the Epiphanian view maintained its ground in the East… and seems to have been held by later Greek writers almost, if not quite, universally,” and files him in the “sons of Joseph by a former wife” column of his conspectus.71

Theodoret of Cyrus — c. 440

The clearest Eastern statement of the Hieronymian reading — and the last witness to the original fourth-and-fifth-century controversy — is Theodoret of Cyrus, who, expounding Galatians 1:19 in his commentary on the fourteen Pauline epistles (c. 440), does what no earlier Greek father had done so plainly: he names the rival Epiphanian solution in order to reject it. James “the brother of the Lord” was not so by nature, and was “not, as some suppose, the son of Joseph by a previous marriage but the son of Clopas and cousin of the Lord. For his mother was the sister of the Lord’s mother” — the apparent brotherhood a double accident of the two Marys’ shared name and the families’ shared house.72 He then turns the Epiphanians’ own cross-argument to Hieronymian ends: the Gospels show “the Blessed Virgin had no other son,” for the dying Lord “gave her to the most divine John, but he would not have committed her to another if the blessed James… had been her son” — the very proof Hilary had used for Joseph’s first family, now made to lodge the brethren in Mary’s sister’s instead. So a generation after Chrysostom first (and haltingly) grafted Jerome’s cousins onto the Antiochene scheme, Theodoret holds the Hieronymian view outright and by choice; J. B. Lightfoot files him last of all in his conspectus of column C, “Sons of the Virgin’s Sister.”73

Peter Chrysologus — c. 440

In the Latin West the same confession is preached from the pulpit by Peter Chrysologus (“golden-worded”), bishop of Ravenna and the most celebrated Italian preacher of his generation. In a sermon on the two Adams (Sermon 117, “The First Adam, and the Last Adam, Born of a Virgin”), setting the Virgin’s childbearing apart from that of ordinary women as a sign of God’s power rather than a work of nature, he presses Mary’s virginity through all three moments — conception, birth, and after: “Where are they who think that the Virgin’s conception and giving birth to her child are to be likened to those of other women?… The Virgin conceives, the Virgin brings forth her child, and she remains a virgin.”74 The threefold formula — virgin in conceiving, in bearing, and remaining so (virginitas ante partum, in partu, post partum) — is the same clause Augustine had fixed in the Latin memory (virgo concepit, virgo peperit, virgo permansit, Sermon 186) and that Leo the Great would carry into the Christological definitions of mid-century. Like Leporius, Athanasius, and Chromatius, Chrysologus attests the perpetual virginity itself — here with weight on the in partu and post partum virginity — and leaves the identity of the brethren untouched.

Leo the Great — c. 450

The same Latin confession is spoken with the fullest doctrinal authority of the age by Leo the Great, bishop of Rome (440–461), whose dogmatic Tome would be acclaimed at the Council of Chalcedon in 451. Preaching on the Feast of the Nativity, he grounds Mary’s virginity in the “new character” of Christ’s birth — “not by intercourse with man but by the power of God was it brought about” — and states it through all three moments: “a Virgin conceived, a Virgin bare, and a Virgin she remained,” the reader to consider “not the condition of her that bare but the will of Him that was born.”75 The virginity is, on Leo’s account, the very pattern of the Incarnation — “the uncorrupt nature of Him that was born had to guard the primal virginity of the Mother” — the same reading his Tome presses when it makes the Virgin Mother “bring Him forth without the loss of her virginity, even as she conceived Him without its loss.” This is the threefold formula (virginitas ante partum, in partu, post partum) Augustine had fixed in the Latin memory, carried in the years around Chalcedon into the Christological mainstream of the West. Like Peter Chrysologus, his contemporary in the other chief Latin pulpit, and like Leporius, Athanasius, and Chromatius, Leo attests the perpetual virginity itself — with weight on the in partu and post partum virginity — and leaves the identity of the brethren untouched.

Second Council of Constantinople — 553

What the Fathers had confessed the church now writes into the definition of the faith itself. The Second Council of Constantinople (553), the fifth ecumenical council, convened by Justinian to settle the Christological aftermath of Chalcedon, sets the perpetual virginity into a canon of the creed. Anathematizing those who deny the Word’s two nativities, its second capitulum names the mother of the second nativity as “the holy and glorious Mary, Mother of God and always a virgin” (Greek aeiparthenos): “coming down from heaven and being made flesh of the holy and glorious Mary, Mother of God and always a virgin, and born of her: let him be anathema.”76 The title is not the council’s subject — its business is the two natures and the “Three Chapters” — but it is spoken in passing as settled and shared truth, the sign of a belief that by the mid-sixth century needs no defence in the East but is simply part of how the church names the Virgin. As with John of Damascus two centuries later, the concern is the virginity itself, not the identity of the brethren, so it cannot be filed by camp; but it marks the point at which the perpetual virginity passes into conciliar dogma — a fact weighed on this timeline’s “late invention” argument (assessment: weak), since the doctrine was defined in an ecumenical creed thirteen centuries before Trent.

The Lateran Council of 649 — 649

A century on, the Latin West states the doctrine with equal formality and greater fullness. The Lateran Council of 649, a Roman synod of 105 bishops convened under Pope Martin I to condemn Monothelitism, devotes its third canon to Mary and confesses her ever-virgin through all three moments — before, in, and after the birth. The “holy Mother of God and ever Virgin and immaculate Mary… conceived of the Holy Spirit without seed… and… incorruptibly bore [Him], her virginity remaining indestructible even after His birth” — and denial is put under anathema: “let him be condemned.”77 Where Constantinople II had named Mary aeiparthenos in a single clause, the Lateran canon spells the triple virginity out — the same threefold formula (virginitas ante partum, in partu, post partum) Leo the Great and Augustine had fixed in the Latin memory — and makes it a term of communion with Rome. Though a local synod rather than an ecumenical council, it was presided over and sanctioned by the pope and its faith was afterward received in the East at the Third Council of Constantinople (680–681). Like the councils and Fathers around it, it settles the virginity itself and leaves the identity of the brethren untouched; and it is the Western counterpart to Constantinople II in dating the doctrine’s conciliar definition to the seventh century — again bearing on the “late invention” argument (assessment: weak).

The Venerable Bede — c. 735

The same settledness appears in the Latin West with Bede, the Northumbrian monk of Wearmouth–Jarrow, whose Commentary on Luke reads Mary’s reply to Gabriel — “How shall this be, seeing I know not a man?” (Luke 1:34) — as her reverent disclosure of a settled purpose of virginity. She “reverently expressed the purpose of her mind, that is, that she had decided to lead a virginal life” (Latin: propositum suae mentis reverenter exposuit, vitam videlicet virginalem se ducere decrevisse) — “the first among women to devote herself to such great virtue.” Bede sets her question apart from Zachariah’s unbelief: she did not ask “How will I know this?” — a demand for a sign — but “How will this be?”, inquiring after “the order of obedience to which she should submit, not asking for a sign to believe”; having read Isaiah’s “a virgin shall conceive” but not how, she asks the angel “what she did not find in the prophet.”78 His gloss makes Bede a firsthand Latin proponent of the vow-of-virginity reading of Luke 1:34 — weighed on this timeline as the vow-of-virginity argument (assessment: contested) — carrying into the medieval West the reading Augustine had made the Latin locus classicus and which the schools would make their common opinion.

John of Damascus — c. 749

Three centuries on, the perpetual virginity is no longer argued but codified: John of Damascus — the last of the Greek Fathers, whose Exact Exposition of the Orthodox Faith (c. 743) gathered the settled dogma of the East into a system — devotes a chapter of it to the “ever-virgin” (aeiparthenos) Mother of God. He states the doctrine at its fullest reach: she “thus remains even after the birth still virgin, having never at any time up till death consorted with a man,” and answers the two stock objections from Matthew 1:25 exactly as the fathers before him. To “her first-born son” he replies that “he who is first-begotten is first-born even if he is only-begotten… the word ‘first-born’ means that he was born first but does not at all suggest the birth of others”; and to “knew her not till she had brought forth,” that “the word ‘till’ signifies the limit of the appointed time but does not exclude the time thereafter” — the same reading of the phrase weighed on this timeline’s “heōs hou” argument (assessment: weak) — illustrating it, as Jerome had, with the risen Christ’s “lo, I am with you always, even unto the end of the world” (Matthew 28:20), which does not mean he departs after it.79 His concern is the virginity itself, not the identity of the brethren, which the chapter never takes up; so he cannot be filed by camp — but as a doctrinal summary of the whole Eastern tradition his is the weightiest Byzantine witness that the perpetual virginity was, by the eighth century, fixed and unquestioned in the Greek Church.

Theophylact of Ohrid — c. 1075

The timeline’s medieval coda is Theophylact of Ohrid, a Byzantine archbishop and exegete who, six centuries after the fourth-century controversy, tries to fuse the two virginity-preserving readings. Commenting on both Matthew 13:55 and Galatians 1:19 he harmonizes the Epiphanian and Hieronymian accounts by the Mosaic levirate: “Cleopas and Joseph were brothers; when Cleopas died childless, Joseph raised up seed for him and begot him and his other brothers” — so James is the Lord’s reputed brother as Joseph’s natural son and his cousin as Clopas’s legal son, Joseph the natural and Clopas the legal father.80 The Matthew comment spells the mechanism out: Clopas “died childless,” Joseph “took his wife in accordance with the law and had six children by her,” the daughter Mary “called the daughter of Cleopas, in accordance with the law.”81 The device keeps the brethren out of the Virgin’s womb either way — the perpetual virginity untouched — but the harmonization itself gained no lasting foothold: J. B. Lightfoot notes this “novel suggestion… found but little favour, and the Eastern Churches continued to distinguish between James the Lord’s brother and James the son of Alphaeus,” the Greek, Syrian, and Coptic calendars keeping a separate day for each.82

Bernard of Clairvaux — c. 1153

The vow-reading of Luke 1:34 gets its most dramatic medieval statement from Bernard of Clairvaux, the Cistercian abbot and “mellifluous doctor,” in the fourth of his early homilies In Praise of the Virgin Mother (the “Missus est” sermons). Where earlier witnesses simply assert that Mary’s “How shall this be done, because I know not man?” presupposes a vow, Bernard alone stages the objection the vow raises — how could a woman betrothed to Joseph have vowed never to know a man? — and answers it from inside Mary’s own supposed reasoning. She “doubts not the fact, but only inquires about the manner”; then, imagined as one already under vow, she reckons openly with the cost: “Since my Lord knows… that His handmaid has made a vow to know no man, by what law shall it please Him to work this wonder? If I must break my vow that I may bring forth such a Son, I rejoice on account of the Son, but I grieve because of my vow. Nevertheless, His will be done.”83 The tension is then dissolved not by denying the vow but by the virgin birth itself — God works the conception virginally, so she bears the Son “as a Virgin” and her resolve is honoured, not annulled. His meditation is a firsthand instance of the vow-of-virginity argument (assessment: contested), in the strong juridical form (a formal vow already sworn) rather than the modest propositum that Bede gives — homiletic drama, not a claim that such a vow was legally possible in first-century Galilee.

Thomas Aquinas — c. 1270

Two centuries later the doctrine is no longer defended but demonstrated: Thomas Aquinas, the great Dominican of the Latin schools, gives Mary’s virginity a whole question of his Summa Theologiae (III, q. 28), and in its third article condemns the Helvidian denial with scholastic finality — “without any hesitation we must abhor the error of Helvidius, who dared to assert that Christ’s Mother, after His Birth, was carnally known by Joseph, and bore other children.” He arrays four reasons: the denial derogates from Christ’s perfection as the Only-begotten; it insults the Holy Ghost, “whose ‘shrine’ was the virginal womb”; it dishonours the dignity of God’s Mother; and it imputes “extreme presumption” to Joseph — concluding that Mary, “as she was a virgin in conceiving Him and a virgin in giving Him birth, so she remained a virgin ever afterwards.”84 On the identity of the brethren he is squarely Hieronymian: following Jerome he makes them “not sons of Joseph, but cousins of the Saviour,” holds that Joseph too “is rather to be believed to have remained a virgin,” and in his Commentary on Matthew flatly rejects the alternative — “Helvidius understood these to be sons of Mary. But this is false; rather they were his cousins” — so his witness preserves the virginity of Mary and of Joseph alike, the settled reading of the medieval West.85

Huldrych Zwingli — c. 1522

The first voice of the Reformation to speak for the doctrine is not Wittenberg’s but Zürich’s: Huldrych (Ulrich) Zwingli, in a sermon on the Virgin Mary preached in Zürich on 17 September 1522 (Eine Predigt von der ewig reinen Magd Maria, printed 1524). He professes the perpetual virginity in the fullest terms — disclaiming that he has ever “thought, still less taught, or declared publicly, anything concerning the subject of the ever Virgin Mary, Mother of our salvation, which could be considered dishonorable, impious, unworthy or evil,” and confessing “with all my heart according to the word of holy gospel that this pure virgin bore for us the Son of God and that she remained, in the birth and after it, a pure and unsullied virgin, for eternity.”86 Like Luther a year later and Calvin a generation on, his concern is the virginity itself, not the identity of the “brethren,” which this sermon never takes up — so he cannot be filed by camp; but that the reformer of Zürich held the doctrine as firmly as any Father makes him, with Luther and Calvin, one of the magisterial Reformers who retained it rather than swept it away.

Martin Luther — c. 1523

The doctrine’s most striking late witness is the Reformer who broke with Rome: Martin Luther, defending Mary’s perpetual virginity in That Jesus Christ Was Born a Jew (1523). He meets the Helvidian denial at the Matthean proof-texts and rejects it outright — the reading that “before they came together” and “knew her not till she had brought forth her first-born son” (Matthew 1:18, 25) imply Joseph knew Mary afterward is a “carnal” misreading, so that “one cannot from these words conclude that Mary, after the birth of Christ, became a wife in the usual sense; it is therefore neither to be asserted nor believed.” He answers it with the Fathers’ idiom-argument: the Scriptural “until” is “the common idiom” that fixes what held before a terminus without implying any change after — “Pharaoh believed not Moses, until he was drowned in the Red Sea” (yet never believed at all), and likewise “Sit at my right hand, till I make your enemies your footstool” (Psalm 110:1), Genesis 28:15, and Isaiah 42:4 — “so that this babble of Helvidius is without justification.”87 That sets him beside Chrysostom, Chromatius, John of Damascus, and Aquinas in reading the Matthean “till” as no bar to a virginity outlasting the birth — the reading weighed at the “heōs hou” argument (assessment: weak). His concern here is the virginity itself, not the identity of the brethren, which the text never takes up, so he cannot be filed by camp — but that a leading magisterial Reformer held the doctrine (as did Zwingli and Calvin) makes him one of the timeline’s more arresting entries.

John Calvin — c. 1562

A second magisterial Reformer holds the doctrine, and more cautiously than Luther: John Calvin, expounding Matthew 1:25 both in his Harmony of the Evangelists and in a Geneva sermon on Matthew 1:22–25 (pub. 1562). His hinge is not a positive demonstration of the perpetual virginity but a denial that the text decides against it — “no just and well-grounded inference can be drawn from these words of the Evangelist, as to what took place after the birth of Christ,” for “first-born” is used “for the sole purpose of informing us that he was born of a virgin,” and Joseph’s knowing her not “till” the birth “is limited to that very time… What took place afterwards, the historian does not inform us.” He treats a dogmatic verdict wrung from the verse as mere “curiosity” and “an extreme fondness for disputation,” yet from the pulpit he is sharper, dismissing the suggestion “that the Virgin Mary had other children… but what folly this is!”88 The two grounds — the neutral “till” and the non-implicative “first-born” — are those weighed at the “heōs hou” argument (assessment: weak), where Calvin’s further parallels (Matthew 18:34; 2 Peter 1:19) are cited among the non-terminative readings of the two-word phrase. Like Luther his concern is the virginity, not the identity of the brethren, which these texts never take up, so he too cannot be filed by camp — but the nuance is worth marking: Calvin rejects the Helvidian denial and personally held Mary ever-virgin without resting that belief on the verse, siding against the reading that finds later children in the text while declining to read the perpetual virginity out of it.89

J. B. Lightfoot — c. 1865

The timeline’s last witness is the scholar whose vocabulary frames the whole of it: J. B. Lightfoot, whose 1865 dissertation “The Brethren of the Lord” gave the three views their names and assembled the patristic catena this page still leans on. It is easy to read him only as the neutral adjudicator, but he was not neutral: weighing the solutions on the Scriptural notices, he abandons the Hieronymian reading — “there is not the slightest indication that the Hieronymian solution ever occurred to any individual or sect or church, until it was put forward by Jerome himself” — and comes down for the Epiphanian: “taking the scriptural notices alone, the Hieronymian account must be abandoned; while of the remaining two the balance of the argument is against the Helvidian and in favour of the Epiphanian,” a reading that “has the highest claims to the sanction of tradition… seems especially to represent the Palestinian view.” So, unlike the Reformers just above him, Lightfoot both holds the perpetual virginity and files by camp — the brethren are step-brothers, “sons of Joseph by a former wife, before he espoused the Virgin.” On the two stock Helvidian proof-texts he reads with the tradition: Matthew’s “till she brought forth” “neither asserts nor implies any subsequent intercourse,” and Luke’s “firstborn” marks “not the birth of other children, but the special consecration of this one” — the finding weighed at the infancy-language argument (assessment: weak).90

Related arguments — the cousin theory

The Hieronymian reading — that the brethren were the Lord’s cousins — is built on several kinds of philological support, weighed here as a set: that the word “brother” (adelphos) can mean “kinsman,” that the “James and Joses” named among the brethren are the “James the little and Joses” whose mother, a second Mary, stands at the cross (Mark 15:40), that a further chain of identifications makes the brethren the apostles James and Jude, and that John 19:25 makes “Mary of Clopas” the Virgin’s sister — together with the source-critical claim that a second-century witness (a fragment bearing the name “Papias”) already attested the reading before Jerome. All are found wanting. Two further arguments run the other way, against the cousin-reading: the historical objection that the theory is a fourth-century novelty with no witness before Jerome — one he himself came to hold loosely — and the testimony of the earliest historical witness of all, Hegesippus, best read as telling against the cousins rather than for them.

Does “brother” (adelphos) mean “cousin” in “the brethren of the Lord”? (assessment: weak)

Does “brother” (adelphos) mean “cousin”? — the whole Hieronymian reading rests on giving the Greek adelphos the sense “kinsman,” as Jerome licensed it with his “four kinds of brethren” and the Old-Testament parallels of Abraham calling his nephew Lot “brother” (Genesis 13:8) and Laban calling Jacob “brother” (Genesis 29:15). The lexical premise is real — adelphos does carry an extended kinship range in biblical Greek, and even a critic of the cousin-theory (Bauckham, himself an Epiphanian) grants the word “could sometimes be used more loosely of other close kinship relationships” — so the reading cannot be disproved from the dictionary. But as positive support it is weak: every extended instance is a single, already-known relationship named loosely in direct speech, whereas the cousin-reading needs a whole group habitually and exclusively styled “brothers” by indifferent parties, one singled out as “James the Lord’s brother” — which, as J. B. Lightfoot objected, is “scarcely conceivable.” The precise word for cousin (anepsios) was available and used elsewhere (Colossians 4:10; Numbers 36:11 LXX), and Hegesippus uses it for the family’s one real cousin, Symeon son of Clopas, while calling James and Jude adelphos — so the semantics leave cousin barely possible but lend the identification no positive weight.91

Does the separate word for “kinsman” (suggenēs) tell against the cousin-reading? (Luke 14:12; 21:16) (assessment: weak)

Does the separate word for “kinsman” tell against the cousins? — a lexical companion to the point above, but a distinct move: Greek had a plain word for “relative/kinsman” (suggenēs, used about a dozen times in the New Testament), and twice sets it beside adelphos in a single breath — “thy brethren (adelphous), neither thy kinsmen (suggeneis)” (Luke 14:12), “brethren, and kinsfolks” (Luke 21:16) — so, the argument runs, a cousin would have been called suggenēs, and the brethren, always adelphoi, were siblings. The observation is true, but as proof it is an argument from silence, and the silence breaks three ways: a category-list (friends/neighbours, parents/kinsfolk) enumerates overlapping terms, it does not decree them mutually exclusive; the Septuagint the Evangelists wrote out of freely calls collateral kin adelphos where suggenēs would fit — Lot the nephew (Genesis 13:8; 14:14) and Jacob addressed by his uncle Laban (Genesis 29:15), the paradigm kinsman-tie — so “they would have said suggenēs” is falsified on Jerome’s own parallels; and the modern “restrict the test to New-Testament usage” escape (Svendsen, White) is ad hoc and, even granted, reaches only probability while failing to distinguish the Helvidian from the Epiphanian (a stepbrother is adelphos too). Tellingly, the ablest critics of the cousins — Lightfoot, Bauckham — grant the loose range and rest their case on usage, not this lexical silence: weak.92

Do the identifications that complete Jerome’s cousin-theory hold? (Alphaeus = Clopas; “Jude of James”; “James the little”) (assessment: weak)

Do the completing identifications hold? — Jerome himself claimed only that the brethren were sons of “Mary of Clopas” the Virgin’s sister; the fuller reading in which two of them are also apostles among the Twelve needs three further identifications grafted on: (a) Alphaeus = Clopas (making James the Lord’s brother = James son of Alphaeus of the Twelve), (b) “Jude of James” (Luke 6:16) read as Jude the brother of James, not his son, and (c) “James the little” (Mark 15:40) taken as “James the less,” implying only two Jameses. Each is a later graft — J. B. Lightfoot found Alphaeus = Clopas “unknown to Jerome himself,” first in Chrysostom and Theodoret — and each is the less-probable reading: Clopas (QLWPY) is a distinct name from Alphaeus (HLPY), “Jude of James” reads more naturally “son” (so the Peshito and Thebaic), and ho mikros means “the little” (stature), not the comparative “the less” that Jerome’s Latin minor supposed. The brethren are, besides, set apart from the Twelve (John 7:5; Acts 1:14; Jude 17). The chain gives the cousin-reading no positive weight, and the theory’s strongest modern form (Blinzler’s) discards all three props.93

Does John 19:25 make “Mary of Clopas” the Virgin’s sister — two sisters both named Mary? (assessment: weak)

Does John 19:25 make “Mary of Clopas” the Virgin’s sister? — the maternal cousinhood at the heart of Jerome’s reading needs the received punctuation of John 19:25 (“his mother’s sister, Mary the wife of Clopas”) to be read as one apposition, so that the verse names only three women and “Mary of Clopas” is the Virgin’s sister — which yields two sisters both named Mary. J. B. Lightfoot isolated this as the third of three assumptions on which the maternal cousinhood rests (“if any one of these be rejected, this cousinhood falls to the ground”) and judged it “most improbable… a difficulty far surpassing that of supposing two or even three cousins bearing the same name”; the Herodian parallel offered in its defense fails, since “Herod was a family name” and a “humble Jewish household” would not court such confusion. The early versions read four women instead — the Peshito inserts a conjunction (“His mother and His mother’s sister, and Mary of Cleophas and Mary Magdalene”), the Jerusalem Syriac lectionary punctuates the same way, the Thebaic reads “Mary daughter of Clopas” — and the coupled arrangement the Greek admits (paralleled in the apostle-lists, Matthew 10:2–4) leaves the Virgin’s sister unnamed, plausibly the Salome of Mark 15:40. As positive support for the cousin-reading the punctuation-argument therefore fails; the theory’s strongest modern form (Blinzler’s) discards it, deriving the cousinhood, if at all, on the fathers’ side via Hegesippus — weak.94

Are “James the little” and Joses (Mark 15:40) the Lord’s brothers James and Joses? (assessment: weak)

Are “James the little” and Joses the Lord’s brothers? — beneath the props above lies the one identification no form of the cousin-reading can shed, not even the pared-down modern defence (Blinzler’s) that discards all the rest: that the “James and Joses” among the brethren (Mark 6:3) are the “James the little and Joses” whose mother, a Mary distinct from the Virgin, watches the cross (Mark 15:40). The coincidence of names is tempting, but Richard Bauckham — himself holding the perpetual virginity on the Epiphanian reading — raises two impediments. First, James the Lord’s brother, leader of the Jerusalem church, “could certainly not have been called ‘the little’ in any sense other than a reference to his physical height,” and never was so styled — his titles were “the Lord’s brother” and “the Just” — so Mark’s epithet serves “to distinguish this James from the Lord’s brother… and this James’ mother from the mother of Jesus.” Second, the Lord’s Joses was insignificant beside James, yet Mark calls the cross-Mary “the mother of Joses” (Mark 15:47) as readily as “the mother of James” (Mark 16:1), which shows her two sons were equally well known — unlike the brothers of Jesus. So “no relatives of Jesus appear among the women disciples named by the Synoptic evangelists.” This removes the foundation the theory cannot do without, where the sibling arguments removed only superstructure — weak.95

Was the cousin theory a novelty without earlier witness — one Jerome himself let go? (assessment: contested)

Was the cousin theory a novelty Jerome himself let go? — the standing objection to the whole Hieronymian reading is one of timing: it appears all at once in Jerome’s Against Helvidius (c. 383) and nowhere before. J. B. Lightfoot pressed the case in five moves — Jerome argues from Scripture and can “not… name a single writer” for his account of the relationship (though for the virginity Ignatius, Polycarp, Irenaeus, and Justin “occur to him at once”); “no decisive instance can be produced of a writer holding Jerome’s view, before it was propounded by Jerome himself”; Jerome holds even the pivot loosely from the first (“contentiosum funem non traho,” §15); and by the Letter to Hedibia (c. 406–7) he lists the keystone Marys as distinct persons, prompting Lightfoot to ask whether he had “relinquished his own hypothesis.” The historical core is strong and near-consensus — the pre-Jerome timeline (Origen “wholly unaware,” Hilary “entirely ignorant,” Epiphanius who “would have hailed” it) knows only the Epiphanian and Helvidian options, the one alleged early prop, the “Papias” fragment, is spurious and post-Jerome, and the whole apocryphal infancy tradition is uniformly Epiphanian in shape, which “would scarcely have taken this form, if the Hieronymian view… had been received or even known when they were written.” But its force against the reading is disputed: novelty is not disproof, an argument from silence is not decisive, and the theory’s modern defenders (Blinzler, McHugh) rebuild it independently of Jerome’s tradition-claim — while the “he abandoned it” strand is Lightfoot’s plausible but unproven conjecture, which the Hedibia passage permits without requiring. Weighty by the historian’s lights, short of decisive as a disproof, and not to be overstated into “Jerome recanted” — contested.96

Does the “Papias” fragment give early (pre-Jerome) support for the cousin theory? (assessment: unsupported)

Does the “Papias” fragment give early support? — the standing weakness of the Hieronymian reading is that it has no witness before Jerome (c. 383). A short Latin catena headed with the name Papias — printed by Grabe and Routh among the remains of Papias of Hierapolis (fl. c. 110), listing the several Marys and making the brethren sons of “Mary the wife of Clopas or Alphaeus” — was long cited at the head of the patristic authorities to fill exactly that gap. J. B. Lightfoot showed it spurious: it survives only in a Bodleian manuscript “of the date 1302 or 1303,” its load-bearing sentence (“Maria uxor Alphei”) is taken from Jerome’s Against Helvidius “almost word for word” (so the text is later than the theory it is offered to attest), and had the real Papias — whose writings “were in Jerome’s hands” — written it, Jerome, “eager as he was to claim the support of authority, could not have failed to refer to” it, nor would so early a passage have left “no traces” in the tradition. Lightfoot afterwards fixed the author: not the bishop of Hierapolis but a medieval namesake, Papias the eleventh-century lexicographer of the Elementarium. As pre-Jerome evidence the fragment supplies nothing — it is Jerome, at second hand — and the objection it was meant to answer stands: unsupported.97

Does Hegesippus tell against the cousin-theory — or can “dēuteron” (HE 4.22.4) mean “a second cousin”? (assessment: sound)

Does Hegesippus tell against the cousin-theory? — unlike the four supports above, this argument runs against the Hieronymian reading. Hegesippus (c. 175), the earliest historical witness on the Lord’s kindred, keeps two relationships verbally distinct: James and Jude are the Lord’s brother, while Symeon son of Clopas — who succeeded James at Jerusalem — is his cousin. A reading that makes the brethren themselves cousins must explain that distinction away, and its defenders (Blinzler, McHugh) do so at one word: they read Jerome’s Eusebian source (H.E. iv.22.4) as calling Symeon “a second (i.e. another) cousin of the Lord” (onta anepsion tou kyriou dēuteron), implying James too was a cousin — and the Loeb translation renders it just so. But the “second [bishop]” reading is more probable: the office (episkopos) is named in the same sentence, the whole passage is about episcopal succession, and the parallel at H.E. iii.22 numbers that same succession (“second” bishop) while still calling James “the brother of our Saviour.” So Richard Bauckham renders “they all put forward [as] the second [bishop]” and concludes Hegesippus “must be held to count against the Hieronymian view.” The finding is modest — it tells against the cousin-reading without proving the virginity or deciding between the Epiphanian and Helvidian — but on the better grammar it holds: sound.98

Related arguments — the later-children question

Four arguments turn on the direct question — were the brethren Mary’s own children, born after Jesus? — rather than on the cousin-word crux weighed above, and are read here as a set: the affirmative Helvidian reading itself (that the plain sense of the recurring unit “his mother and his brethren” is Mary’s own sons), the Helvidian proof-texts drawn from the nativity, the modern linguistic refinement that presses the two-word Greek “until” (heōs hou) of Matthew 1:25, and the pro-virginity argument drawn from the cross. None, on its own, settles the question.

Were the brethren Mary’s own children — the natural (Helvidian) reading? (assessment: contested)

Were the brethren Mary’s own children? — the affirmative core of the Helvidian reading, weighed here as an interpretation in its own right rather than through the rebuttals of the rival theories. Its positive case is the plain sense of the text: the Gospels name “his mother and his brethren” as a fixed unit again and again (Matthew 12:46; Mark 3:31; Luke 8:19; John 2:12; Acts 1:14), which most naturally designates Mary’s own nearest kin — and the four brethren are named (James, Joses, Simon, Judas) as real, traceable figures. Richard Bauckham — himself holding the perpetual virginity on the Epiphanian reading — grants that the Helvidian view “probably most modern exegetes, even including some Roman Catholic scholars, hold,” and that “the Gospel traditions regularly refer to Jesus’ brothers in company with his mother.” But two honest limits keep it short of proof. First, that fixed phrasing establishes only that the brethren belonged to the household of Joseph and Mary — it tells decisively against the cousin- and figurative readings, but a stepbrother belonged to that household too, so it does not by itself distinguish the Helvidian from the Epiphanian; the full-sibling sense is the natural default, not a demonstrated one. Second, the only early witnesses unambiguous on the relationship take the Epiphanian view (weighed at the stepbrother tradition), and Bauckham judges “the historical evidence is not sufficient for a firm decision between the Helvidian and Epiphanian views.” So the reading is strong by the lights of modern grammatical-historical exegesis — the natural sense and the majority view — yet rests on presumption rather than positive evidence and faces a serious, early, unrefuted rival: contested.99

Do “till she brought forth” (Matthew 1:25) and “firstborn son” (Luke 2:7) imply Mary bore other children? (assessment: weak)

Do the infancy phrases imply siblings? — the Helvidian reading has always leaned on two phrases from the infancy narratives: Joseph “knew her not till she had brought forth” her son (Matthew 1:25) and Mary’s “firstborn son” (Luke 2:7). Neither implies later children. “Till” (heōs hou) regularly denies a prior state without asserting its reversal — Michal “had no child unto the day of her death” (2 Samuel 6:23), the raven did not return though it flew “until the waters were dried up” (Genesis 8:7), and the risen Christ is “with you… unto the end of the world” without ceasing to be (Matthew 28:20); the phrase secures the virginal conception and is silent on what followed. “Firstborn” (prōtotokos) is a consecration term for the child that “openeth the womb” (Luke 2:23; Exodus 13:2), fixed at birth and used of an only child no less than the eldest of many — a Jewish epitaph even names a woman who died bearing her firstborn. Jerome made both replies the classics; Richard Bauckham, no Helvidian, grants the words “cannot be pressed” and “need not” imply siblings. The phrases are consistent with the Helvidian reading but lend it no positive weight, which must come, if at all, from the plain “brethren” themselves — so as a proof-text the argument is weak.100

Does the two-word Greek “until” of Matthew 1:25 (heōs hou) — not the one-word heōs — imply Mary bore other children? (assessment: weak)

Does the two-word “until” (heōs hou) prove relations after the birth? — a modern, linguistically-armoured refinement of the “till” leg of the infancy-language case. Eric Svendsen (popularized by James White) concedes that the one-word heōs is neutral, but argues that Matthew 1:25 uses the narrower two-word heōs hou, which across its fifteen relevant New-Testament occurrences always marks a terminus after which the state changes — so Joseph knew Mary after the birth. It scores one fair point: Jerome’s stock parallels (Michal, the raven, “with you… till the end of the world”) really are the one-word heōs. But the conclusion does not follow. The same two-word phrase runs non-terminatively in contemporary Koine — Septuagint Psalm 72:7 (“peace till the moon be removed”), which Chrysostom, whose own text of Matthew 1:25 is heōs hou, cites on this very verse while denying it implies any later change — and inside the New-Testament sample itself (Matthew 18:34, the unpayable debt, which Calvin and Wesley read as never discharged). A fifteen-instance sample cannot fix an exceptionless rule against a ~5–14% base rate of non-terminating use, so Matthew 1:25 sits comfortably as the expected exception and cannot be counted for itself; and Svendsen’s a-priori dismissal of the Septuagint and the fathers as “irrelevant” misapplies the “semantic obsolescence” caution, which bars reading an obsolete sense across a demonstrated diachronic shift — none is shown. The two-word “until” is neutral, exactly as the one-word is — weak.101

Does Jesus entrusting Mary to John (John 19:26–27) show she had no other sons? (assessment: contested)

Does the entrustment to John disprove siblings? — on the cross Jesus commends his mother not to a brother but to the beloved disciple John, who “from that hour… took her unto his own home” (John 19:26–27); had Mary borne the four sons the Helvidian reading requires, a dying son would not have consigned her to a man outside the family. The fathers pressed it hard — Hilary: Mary “never would have been transferred to the apostle John… at the time of the Passion” had she sons of her own; Theodoret bent the same proof to Hieronymian ends — and J. B. Lightfoot judged it “powerless against the Epiphanian” but urged “with fatal effect” against the Helvidian. Yet it does not decide the question: the brethren were unbelievers and apparently absent at the cross (John 7:5), so the choice of the believing disciple has a ready reason; a defender of the virginity like Richard Bauckham reads the scene as a theological act — Mary received into the community of disciples (John 20:17) — and calls the argument “beside the point”; and even at full strength it cannot distinguish the Epiphanian from the Hieronymian reading, nor does the step-son version escape the same filial-duty objection it presses on the Helvidian. It raises the cost of the Helvidian reading without closing it — contested.102

Related arguments — the reality of the Lord’s brethren

Three arguments reach past the disputed word “brother” to the historical reality behind it — that the Lord’s brethren were real, flesh-and-blood kin — drawn not from the contested term but from the brethren’s traceable descendants, from Paul’s notice of them as a known married-missionary class, and from the letter of Jude’s own self-designation. All three anchor “brother of the Lord” as literal kinship, and all three are neutral between the Helvidian and Epiphanian readings, so none by itself settles the perpetual virginity — each leaves that to be decided on other grounds. (A further set of arguments reaches from the same letter of Jude to its authenticity — grouped just below.)

Do the brethren’s traceable family line — Jude’s named grandsons — support reading “brother of the Lord” as literal kinship? (assessment: sound)

Do Jude’s grandsons support a literal-kinship reading? — a step from the timeline to the descendants of the brethren, asking what the earliest historical tradition shows about the reality of the Lord’s family. Hegesippus (c. 175, preserved by Eusebius) reports that two grandsons of Jude “the Lord’s brother according to the flesh” — named Zoker and James — were poor Galilean farmers with a 39-plethra smallholding, examined before Domitian as Davidides, released, and afterwards leaders (“they ruled the churches”) as relatives of the Lord, surviving until Trajan. Richard Bauckham — himself holding the perpetual virginity on the Epiphanian reading — sets aside the Domitianic-persecution frame and the credal little speech as apologetic and hagiographical, but accepts the core as reliable memory: one of the four named brethren demonstrably married, had children, and left a family line remembered by name two generations on, coherent with the married-missionary picture of 1 Corinthians 9:5 and the wider desposynoi tradition. That anchors “brother of the Lord” as a designation of literal kinship — real flesh-and-blood family, not a figurative or honorific title. But it is neutral between the Helvidian and Epiphanian readings (a married Jude with descendants fits a full brother and a stepbrother alike), tells only mildly against the Hieronymian (cousins marry too), and does not by itself touch Mary’s virginity — so on the literal-kinship interpretation it is adduced for, sound, while leaving the choice among the readings to be settled elsewhere.103

Does 1 Corinthians 9:5 — the “brothers of the Lord” as married travelling apostles, a known class — support reading “brother of the Lord” as literal kinship? (assessment: sound)

Do the married-missionary brothers of 1 Corinthians 9:5 support a literal-kinship reading? — a companion to Jude’s grandsons, reaching the same anchor from Paul rather than Hegesippus. Paul’s one incidental notice of the brothers besides James — “have we not power to lead about… a wife, as well as other apostles, and as the brethren of the Lord, and Cephas?” — ranks “the brothers of the Lord” beside “the rest of the apostles” and Cephas as a recognized class of missionaries with the accepted right to travel with a believing wife. Richard Bauckham — himself holding the perpetual virginity on the Epiphanian reading — takes this “at first sight merely incidental” verse as strong evidence that “brother(s) of the Lord” was a settled designation for a specific, well-known group: married men engaged in travelling missionary work (with James, Jerusalem-based, not primarily in view), ranked with the apostles (which “resolves the rather more ambiguous” Galatians 1:19), and corroborated from an independent quarter by Julius Africanus’ desposynoi preaching throughout Palestine from Nazareth and Kokhaba (ap. Eusebius, H.E. i.7.14). That the brethren were a real, known, married class of literal kin — not a figure of speech — holds on the verse’s face, and it presses J. B. Lightfoot’s objection that cousins would not be “commonly and exclusively styled his ‘brothers’” against the Hieronymian reading; the further step that “brother of the Lord” was a quasi-technical apostolic title is a probable reconstruction the sound core does not need. But like the grandsons datum it is neutral between the Helvidian and Epiphanian readings and does not by itself touch Mary’s virginity — on the literal-kinship interpretation it is adduced for, sound.104

Does Jude 1’s “brother of James” denote literal kinship, or a figurative brotherhood (gloss, spiritual fraternity, or “co-worker”)? (assessment: sound)

Does “brother of James” mean literal kinship? — the third of the literal-kinship arguments, alongside Jude’s grandsons and the married missionaries, reaching the same anchor from the self-designation of the letter of Jude: “Jude… and brother of James” (Jude 1). Richard Bauckham — himself holding the perpetual virginity on the Epiphanian reading — argues the phrase itself fixes the identification of the author as the Lord’s brother, since James is “the only man in the primitive church who could be called simply ‘James’ without risk of ambiguity” — James the Lord’s brother, the Jerusalem leader — so Jude names himself by kinship to that James. Against this stand the minority readings that empty “brother” of literal kinship — a scribal gloss (Barns), spiritual fraternity (du Plessis), or “co-worker” (Ellis), most tied to identifying the author with Judas Barsabbas or the apostle Judas Thomas instead. But adelphos governing a personal name is the ordinary New-Testament idiom of kinship, and (Bauckham’s rebuttal) had the writer been a Barsabbas or a Thomas the obvious tag was the surname, not “brother of James.” A corroborating datum runs alongside: the author conspicuously declines the higher title “brother of the Lord” — which outsiders (Paul, Galatians 1:19; 1 Corinthians 9:5) apply to the family but which he will not assume for himself — a reticence Bauckham reads as favouring the letter’s authenticity and as fitting a fixed, externally-applied kinship designation rather than a self-assumed honorific. So the phrase is most naturally a real kinship note — pressing against any purely-figurative “brother” — yet, like its companions, it fixes only that Jude was kin and brother of James, not whose son, so it is neutral between the Helvidian and Epiphanian readings and does not by itself touch Mary’s virginity: on the literal-kinship interpretation it is adduced for, sound.105

Related arguments — the authenticity of Jude

Four further arguments — all Richard Bauckham’s — reach the same historical family not through what “brother” means but through the letter of Jude itself, asking whether it is a genuine artifact of Jesus’ kin: from its learned Palestinian pesharim-style exegesis, its knowledge of an obscure Palestinian source (the Testament of Moses behind Jude 9), its distinctive christological vocabulary (the title despotēs, tied to the desposynoi), and the Enochic Lukan genealogy that seems to spring from Jude’s own circle. The first three read features internal to the letter; the fourth argues from an external document. Each fixes the letter’s Palestinian, Enoch-steeped milieu securely, but the step from milieu to author remains disputed — and, even granting authenticity, all four are neutral between the Helvidian and Epiphanian readings, touching Mary’s virginity only at second hand.

Across the aisle: the two Enoch data these arguments rest on — Jude 14–15 quoting 1 Enoch 1:9 (“Enoch… the seventh from Adam, prophesied”) and Jude 6 echoing 1 Enoch 10:12 — are the same primary base that two other doctrines weigh from their own angles: on the Nephilim, whether the early Church received the book of Enoch as Scripture; and on the Old Testament canon, whether Jude’s quoting it as prophecy shows the canon was still open in the apostolic age. Those ask what standing 1 Enoch had; these ask whether the letter that quotes it is an authentic work of Jude the Lord’s brother — the same brother whose reality the perpetual-virginity question turns on.

Does the Epistle of Jude’s learned, Palestinian pesharim-style exegesis support the letter’s authenticity? (assessment: contested)

Does Jude’s learned Palestinian exegesis support the letter’s authenticity? — the first of the authenticity arguments, reaching the letter’s genuineness not from what “brother” means but from what kind of letter Jude is. Richard Bauckham — himself holding the perpetual virginity on the Epiphanian reading — shows that Jude 4–19 is an elaborate, formal work of Jewish exegesis in the manner of the Qumran thematic pesharim: a four-“text”-plus-interpretation structure, houtoi (“these are…”) demonstrative-explanation formulae, catchword (gezerah shawah) technique, a christological modification of the quoted 1 Enoch 1:9 (supplying kyrios as its subject), and a probable use of Enoch in Aramaic — all marks of a genuine first-century Palestinian Jewish Christianity, in a letter written while the apostles still lived. He takes this as evidence for the letter’s authenticity, and so for a real Jude within Jesus’ family. The foundation is strong — the letter is demonstrably early and Palestinian, which rules out a late Hellenistic forgery — but the payoff (“Evidently Jude the Lord’s brother was one such skilled exegete”) presupposes the authenticity it is offered to prove: learned Palestinian exegesis fixes the milieu, not the man, and a skilled pseudepigrapher of the same circle could imitate it, so authorship remains genuinely disputed. And even granting authenticity, it is neutral between the Helvidian and Epiphanian readings and does not by itself touch Mary’s virginity — contested.106

Does the Epistle of Jude’s knowledge of the obscure Palestinian Testament of Moses (Jude 9) support the letter’s authenticity? (assessment: contested)

Does Jude’s knowledge of the obscure Palestinian Testament of Moses support the letter’s authenticity? — the second of the authenticity arguments, a companion to the exegesis case, reaching authenticity not from Jude’s method but from his knowledge of a specific, obscure source. In Jude 9 the letter alludes — without explanation, assuming its readers know it — to the dispute of Michael and the devil over the body of Moses, a story Richard Bauckham (himself Epiphanian) reconstructs from the lost ending of the Testament of Moses, a Palestinian Jewish work so little known it “is never referred to by name in any document of the early church outside the lists of apocryphal books.” The early writers who tried to explain Jude 9 (Clement, Origen, the catenists) did not have the story, reaching instead for a later, probably Egyptian “Assumption of Moses”; and 2 Peter 2:11, rewriting Jude 9, simply eliminates the story it did not know — marking a different milieu, and Jude’s priority. So the letter belongs to “the Palestinian Jewish Christian circles from which an authentic letter of Jude the Lord’s brother would come.” The core is strong — the unexplained allusion is a genuine mark of an early, Palestinian, insider document, ruling out a late Hellenistic forgery — but, like its exegetical companion, the payoff presupposes the authenticity it is offered for: obscure source-knowledge fixes the milieu, not the man, and a pseudepigrapher of the same circle could allude to the same source; the case rests on Jude 9 alone (Bauckham rejects Charles’s further alleged allusions as “not proven”). And even granting authenticity, it is neutral between the Helvidian and Epiphanian readings and does not by itself touch Mary’s virginity — contested.107

Does the Epistle of Jude’s distinctive christological vocabulary — the title “Master” (despotēs), tied to the desposynoi — support the letter’s authenticity? (assessment: contested)

Does Jude’s christological vocabulary (the title despotēs) support the letter’s authenticity? — the third of the authenticity arguments, a further companion to the exegesis and Testament of Moses cases, reaching authenticity not from Jude’s method or his source but from his christological vocabulary. Richard Bauckham — himself holding the perpetual virginity on the Epiphanian reading — shows that the four christological titles recoverable for the relatives’ circle (Messiah; “the Lord”; “our Lord”; and “Master,” despotēs) are the four found in Jude, and that the rarest is load-bearing: christological despotēs (Jude 4, “our only Master and Lord Jesus Christ”) is otherwise unattested in the first century — the sole other instance, 2 Peter 2:1, depending on Jude — yet is “only explicable” because the relatives were known as hoi desposynoi, “the Master’s people” (Julius Africanus ap. Eusebius, H.E. i.7.14). So the letter speaks the kin-circle’s own idiom — evidence for its authenticity. The core is strong, and (unlike the method- and source-arguments) the despotēs datum is hard to fake, since a distant forger had no literary model for it. But, like its companions, the payoff presupposes the authenticity it is offered for: the vocabulary fixes the letter’s milieu — the desposynoi’ circle — more tightly than its author, and a writer within that circle, for whom the idiom was native speech, fits as well as the historical Jude; the desposynoi/Davidic facts it leans on are weighed already at Jude’s grandsons and the married missionaries. And even granting authenticity, it is neutral between the Helvidian and Epiphanian readings and does not by itself touch Mary’s virginity — contested.108

Does the Lukan genealogy’s origin in Jude’s circle — its Enochic structure resting on the two Enoch data Jude deploys — support the letter’s authenticity? (assessment: plausible)

Does the Lukan genealogy’s origin in Jude’s circle support the letter’s authenticity? — the fourth of the authenticity arguments, distinct in kind: where its three companions read features internal to the letter (method, source, vocabulary), this one argues from an external document. Richard Bauckham — himself Epiphanian — reconstructs the genealogy of Jesus in Luke 3:23–38 as a deliberate Enochic scheme (seventy-seven generations Adam→Jesus, Enoch seventh and Jesus seventy-seventh), built on 1 Enoch 10:12 — whose two load-bearing data are exactly the two Enoch tags the short letter of Jude independently deploys (Jude 6 echoes 1 Enoch 10:12, “the great day of their judgment”; Jude 14 calls Enoch “the seventh from Adam”). So he locates the pre-Lukan genealogy’s composition in Jude’s own learned circle: “if the genealogy is not the work of Jude himself, it must certainly come from his circle.” The convergence is real and striking, and it fixes a shared early-Palestinian, Enoch-steeped milieu securely — and, as an external corroboration, is different in kind from the other three. But the tighter claim — that the genealogy is specifically a Jude-circle product corroborating the letter’s authenticity — is an ingenious minority reconstruction resting on a deliberately-77 reading (found in most, not all, manuscripts) and on inferring authorship from a shared milieu; the genealogy could belong to that milieu without belonging to Jude, and Bauckham hedges at just that seam. So this is the softest of the four strands: coherent and not ruled out, short of consensus — and, like its companions, neutral between the Helvidian and Epiphanian readings, touching Mary’s virginity only at second hand — plausible.109

Related arguments — the onomastic argument

Two arguments turn on the same mechanism — the statistics of the tiny first-century Palestinian Jewish name-pool, tabulated by Tal Ilan and applied by Richard Bauckham — and are read here as a set. One asks whether the male names shared across the households (James, Joses, Simon) make a kinship reading of the brethren an improbable coincidence; the other, whether the female names that tradition gives Jesus’ sisters (Mary and Salome) preserve genuine early memory. In each the onomastic data does defusing or corroborating work — dissolving an objection, or crediting a tradition — without, on its own, establishing any one reading of the “brethren.”

Do the shared names (James, Joses, Simon) across two families make a kinship reading of the brethren incredible — or is the coincidence unremarkable given the tiny Jewish name-pool? (assessment: sound)

Are the shared names too much of a coincidence? — a defensive rebuttal, not an argument for any one reading. The kinship theories — the Hieronymian cousins and the Epiphanian stepbrothers alike — posit a second household beside Joseph and Mary’s, and the objection is that two related families both bearing James, Joses, and Simon is an improbable coincidence, better dissolved by supposing only one family (Mary’s own), which is the Helvidian reading. J. B. Lightfoot answered it in four moves — the Jewish names in use “were very few,” these three “among the most common” (twelve Simons and nearly as many Joseses in the New Testament, “nineteen Josephs, and twenty-five Simons” in the index to Josephus); cousinhood “diminishe[s] rather than increase[s]” the difficulty, since a common relative’s name “naturally repeats itself in households connected with each other”; and the names sit in the Lord’s own genealogies. Modern onomastics confirms it: on Tal Ilan’s corpus, tabulated by Richard Bauckham, Simon and Joseph/Joses are the two commonest male Jewish names of the era (borne by 15.6% of men on record) and Judas the fourth — so the recurrence is exactly what the name-pool predicts. The rebuttal is defusing only: it removes a naming objection to the kinship readings without establishing any of them, and the cousin configuration it partly shields still fails on other grounds — but as a rebuttal it holds: sound.110

Do the traditional names of Jesus’ sisters — Mary and Salome — preserve genuine early memory? (assessment: plausible)

Do the sister-names preserve early memory? — a step to the side of the brethren-crux, asking what the earliest family tradition is worth from the sisters (Mark 6:3) rather than the brothers. Post-canonical tradition names the two sisters Mary and Salome — in Epiphanius (Panarion 78, Joseph’s daughters by his first wife) and, before him, the Gospel of Philip (“his sister… a Mary”) and probably the Protevangelium of James (the unexplained “Salome” at 19:3). Richard Bauckham — himself Epiphanian — grounds their authenticity in onomastics: of 247 named Palestinian Jewish women of the era, Mary and Salome together account for ~47.7%, yet Salome was “a peculiarly Palestinian name” (only two of the diaspora women bore it), so a second-century Gentile inventor “is unlikely to have” chosen it — whence the names most probably trace to Palestinian Jewish-Christian memory, and the sisters “played some part in the early Christian movement.” The case is strong on its own terms, but its bearing here is limited: authentic names are not the sisters’ parentage, and sisters exist on all three readings of the “brethren,” so the datum is neutral between Helvidian, Epiphanian, and Hieronymian — it raises the credit of the early extra-canonical family tradition (in which the stepbrother reading also travels) without establishing that the sisters were Joseph’s daughters by a former wife — plausible.111

Related arguments — the Epiphanian (stepbrother) reading

Three arguments bear specifically on the Epiphanian (stepbrother) reading — that the brethren were Joseph’s sons by a former wife — rather than on the meaning of “brother” or the later-children question. The first weighs the reading itself, as an interpretation, on its own positive merits; the second asks whether its early, unrivalled second-century pedigree is genuine historical memory; the third whether its making the brethren only Joseph’s (and so only reputed) kin is the disadvantage the Helvidian reading claims. Together they defend and weigh the reading — strong on tradition, exegetically unstrained — without, on the evidence, establishing it over the Helvidian.

Were the brethren Joseph’s sons by a former marriage? — the Epiphanian reading on its merits (assessment: contested)

The Epiphanian reading on its merits — the symmetric counterpart to the Helvidian reading’s own page, asking the positive question of the stepbrother interpretation: taken on its own evidence, how strong is the reading that the brethren were Joseph’s sons by a marriage before Mary, no blood-kin of hers? Its strengths are real: it gives “brother” its natural sense (no lexical special-pleading, no brethren-apostles), so of the three historic solutions it beats the Hieronymian outright and shares the field only with the Helvidian; it carries the earliest and only pre-Nicene attestation, uniform in the East and plausibly Palestinian in root; and it loses nothing to the reputed-kinship objection. J. B. Lightfoot judged that on the Scriptural notices “the balance of the argument is against the Helvidian and in favour of the Epiphanian.” But the reading has no positive New Testament proof-text — it rests on a former marriage nowhere in Scripture, its two scriptural props (the entrustment to John and the antiquity of the tradition) are each themselves only contested, and the reputed-kinship rebuttal merely neutralises an objection without discriminating; most modern exegetes prefer the Helvidian, and the reading’s own best defender, Richard Bauckham, judges “the historical evidence is not sufficient for a firm decision between the Helvidian and Epiphanian views.” Strong by tradition’s lights, weaker by grammatical-historical exegesis’s, with no decider in the text — the mirror image of the Helvidian verdict: contested.112

Is the second-century stepbrother tradition genuine historical memory or a construct protecting Mary’s virginity? (assessment: contested)

Memory or Marian construct? — a step back from the timeline to ask what its earliest stretch is worth. The Epiphanian reading (the brethren are Joseph’s sons by a former wife) is the only solution positively attested before the late fourth century, surfacing c. 150 in the Protevangelium of James, the Gospel of Peter, and the Infancy Gospel of Thomas — early enough that good tradition still survived, and assumed rather than argued by its first carriers, as if older than the books that hold it. But Richard Bauckham, himself an Epiphanian, notes it travels in early-second-century Syria in a tight cluster with two other extra-biblical beliefs — Mary’s Davidic descent and the miraculous (painless) birth — whose shared tendency is to magnify her virginity, so it may be one more product of that devotion rather than a datum behind it; and the test that would break the tie, a plausible Palestinian Jewish-Christian pedigree, is met only in part (the three witnesses that might trace the tradition directly to Palestine — Sedulius Scotus’ “Gospel of the Hebrews,” the Second Apocalypse of James, and Hegesippus’ extant fragments — all fail, while Epiphanius’s “James was Joseph’s firstborn son” is more plausibly drawn from Hegesippus than from the infancy gospels, an indirect pedigree that is a plausibility about a source, not a proof). The two readings remain, on this evidence, empirically indistinguishable — the tradition “could preserve an accurate historical memory… [or] could be a legendary construct… We cannot be sure” — so the antiquity gives the Epiphanian view serious weight without settling it: contested.113

Is the Epiphanian reading weakened by making the brethren only reputed kin — or does Joseph’s reputed fatherhood license calling his sons the Lord’s “brethren”? (assessment: sound)

Does Joseph’s reputed fatherhood license the brethren-title? — the Epiphanian reading’s one apparent disadvantage is that it leaves the brethren with “really no relationship” to Jesus, where the Helvidian keeps a blood tie — the Helvidian’s strongest intuitive card. J. B. Lightfoot answered that the advantage is “rather seeming than real”: “it is very natural that those who called Joseph His father should call Joseph’s sons His brethren,” for the register of reputed / legal kinship is one Scripture itself uses of Joseph — by Mary’s own lips (“thy father and I have sought thee,” Luke 2:48) and by the Evangelist (“his parents,” Luke 2:41, 43), neither in any doubt of the virginal conception. And the move is not a modern apologetic device but the ancient rationale itself: Eusebius says James “was called the brother of the Lord because he was known as a son of Joseph, and Joseph was supposed to be the father of Christ” (H.E. ii.1), and Chrysostom that the brethren were so called “in the same kind of way as Joseph himself was supposed to be husband of Mary.” As a rebuttal it holds — the “no real relationship” objection dissolves, and the Helvidian’s intuitive edge with it — though, like the other defensive arguments, it defuses an objection without positively establishing the stepbrothers over Mary’s own sons: sound.114

Related arguments — the vow of virginity

Two arguments read Luke 1:34 — Mary’s “How shall this be, seeing I know not a man?” — as evidence that she had already resolved, or vowed, to remain a virgin. They share the one proof-text and are best read together. The exegetical case argues that a betrothed woman told she will bear a son would have had no cause for the question unless virginity was for her a settled purpose; the grammatical case argues that the present tense of “I know not” renders a Semitic participle of permanent condition. Both hang on how much weight four words will bear — and the philological one, on its own strongest witness’s reckoning, reaches only Mary’s virginity at the conception, not a lifelong vow.

Does Mary’s reply to Gabriel — “How shall this be, seeing I know not a man?” (Luke 1:34) — imply she had taken a vow of perpetual virginity? (assessment: contested)

Does Luke 1:34 imply a vow of virginity? — a positive exegetical argument distinct from the whole brethren-crux: that Mary’s question, put by a betrothed woman told she will bear a son, treats her virginity as an obstacle and so makes sense only if she had already resolved never to “know” a man. It is old and weighty — stated firsthand by Gregory of Nyssa (a Nativity homily: she “cleaves to virginity… nor will she relinquish her resolve”) and, as the Latin locus classicus, by Augustine (“which assuredly she would not say, unless she had before vowed herself unto God as a virgin”), quoted by Aquinas as the sed contra of the very article he gives it (Summa III q. 28 a. 4), and the common opinion of the medieval schools. Aquinas answers the betrothal puzzle by a two-stage vow — conditional before the espousals, absolute with Joseph after — and the Josephite-marriage argument clears the theological obstacle. But the case overreaches as proof: it hangs on four words; Calvin calls the inference “unfounded and altogether absurd” (no institution of vowed celibacy among the Jews; a vowed betrothal would be strange), which bites the strong juridical vow hardest; a live rival reading exists (Cajetan’s astonishment at conceiving virginally then and there, or the messianic almah of Isaiah 7:14), so the popularizers’ “makes no sense unless…” is an overstatement; and the magisterium itself has retreated from “vow” to “the will to remain a virgin” (John Paul II), with modern critical exegesis giving the vow little support. The strong form (a formal, already-sworn vow) is weak; the modest form (a settled resolve of virginity that her question presupposes) is the defensible steelman — ancient, coherent, but resting on one contested clause against a coherent rival, strong by the tradition’s lights and weak by modern philology’s: contested.115

Does the present tense of “I know not a man” (Luke 1:34) — a Semitic participle of permanent condition — prove Mary vowed lifelong virginity? (assessment: weak)

Does the grammar of “I know not a man” prove a vow? — the philological companion to the vow argument, and a clean case of an apologetic overreaching its own source. The copy-pasted claim is that the Greek present ándra ou ginóskō is a continuous, permanent disposition, rendering a Hebrew/Aramaic active participle (yodaat, reconstructed ki enneni yodaat ish) that names a “permanent condition of virginity” — so the verb itself proves a vow. The grammatical datum is genuinely real: the present is striking against the Old Testament’s aorist/perfect virginity idiom (Genesis 19:8; Numbers 31:18; Judges 11:39; 21:12), and the participle behind it does carry a durative, forward-leaning sense. But the strongest witness the apologists cite — Manuel Miguéns, whose analysis Warren Carroll calls “indispensable” — expressly declines the vow inference: he is “not concerned… whether the expression implies a vow,” concluding only that Mary “was a virgin at the time of the annunciation and… was going to be a virgin for the near future, i.e. at the time when she was to conceive Jesus.” The chain Miguéns → Carroll → tract silently upgrades “virgin for the near future” into “a permanent condition,” and the durative reach (which the rival readings already accommodate) into a lifelong vow; Basil shows a father can hold the doctrine firmly while refusing to rest it on the grammar of “know.” The datum is sound; the proof of a vow is not: weak.116

Does the resurrection appearance to James (1 Corinthians 15:7) explain the brethren’s turn from unbelief to faith? (assessment: sound)

Does the appearance to James bridge John 7:5 and Acts 1:14? — the one positive argument here, and the one that does not turn on the meaning of “brother.” The Gospels leave a gap: the brethren are unbelievers shortly before the crucifixion (John 7:5) yet gathered with the faithful just after the ascension (Acts 1:14). J. B. Lightfoot bridges it with a line from the oldest resurrection creed — “then he was seen of James” (1 Corinthians 15:7) — the “James” without epithet being, on the strong majority reading, the Lord’s brother and bishop of Jerusalem (James of Zebedee being already dead, Acts 12:2), so that a personal appearance of the risen Christ is the turning-point that won the Lord’s kin. The datum is bedrock-early (the pre-Pauline formula of 1 Corinthians 15:3–7, vouched by a named witness) and, as a corollary, tells against the Hieronymian reading, since brethren who disbelieved and stood apart from the Twelve (Acts 1:14) cannot have been two of the Twelve. Its softest prop is the narrative Lightfoot dresses it in, from the Gospel according to the Hebrews (preserved by Jerome, Lives of Illustrious Men 2): early and Judaic, but tendentious — it makes James swear his fast at the Lord’s cup, casting the appearance as a reward for prior devotion rather than a convert’s turning-point — and the argument neither needs it nor is decided by it between the Epiphanian and Helvidian readings, which both keep the brethren clear of the Twelve. On its load-bearing claims, sound.117

Was Jude the Lord’s brother the apostle Thomas, Jesus’ physical twin? (assessment: weak)

Was Jude the apostle Thomas, Jesus’ twin? — a different way to identify one of the brethren, which, if it held, would make Jude Mary’s own child (a twin shares his mother’s womb). In the East Syrian church of Edessa the apostle Thomas was known as “Judas Thomas,” and, reading the name Thomas (Aramaic for “twin”) as a description, two sectarian works — the Acts of Thomas and the Book of Thomas — make him the twin of Jesus; some moderns (Koester, Layton) take that as a physical twinship and so identify him with Jude the Lord’s brother. Richard Bauckham — himself holding the perpetual virginity on the Epiphanian reading — shows the reading will not bear the weight: the twin-of-Jesus motif is “confined to the Acts of Thomas and the Book of Thomas,” known early “only in the east Syrian area,” and serves “a highly theological purpose” (Thomas as the disciple’s spiritual “double”), coloured by the Dioscuri myth — so “the main impetus… came from the theory of spiritual twinship,” and a physical twin of Jesus is “hard to imagine” being “taken seriously in circles where the tradition of the virgin birth was current.” “Judas” was most likely Thomas’s own personal name (a surname “Twin” marking a real but unknown twinship, as “Peter” marked Simon), he “is never called ‘Judas, the brother of James’” as Jude 1 styles its author, and Ephraem, who cherished “Judas Thomas,” distinguishes him from Jude the Lord’s brother outright. A real tradition, but on its best case the identification does not hold, and it lends the Helvidian reading no positive weight — weak.118

Is Tertullian the earliest Helvidian — did he teach that Mary bore children after Jesus? (assessment: contested)

Is Tertullian really Helvidian? — a question about how to read the timeline’s earliest supposed Helvidian witness, on which more turns than first appears. Two claims must be kept apart. That Tertullian (c. 210) denied Mary a virginity outlasting the birth — the virginitas in partu — is secure and uncontested: he says she “was a wife when she brought forth her son,” her womb “opened” as in any childbirth. But that he positively held the Helvidian reading — the brethren were Mary’s own later children — is an inference from his anti-Marcionite polemic, not a statement he makes, and it is disputed: J. B. Lightfoot judged it “highly probable” (the plain sense of “brother,” joined to Tertullian’s pointed refusal of the Epiphanian device though an ascetic had every motive to use it), while Catholic scholars (Blinzler, McHugh) read his explicit statements as touching only the manner of the birth, and note that his brethren-argument needs the brethren merely real — which the Epiphanian reading equally supplies. Richard Bauckham makes the antiquity of the whole debate hinge on the verdict — the Epiphanian view is “the only view positively attested… before Tertullian, if Tertullian is correctly interpreted as holding the Helvidian view, or before the late fourth century, if he is not” — and treats the question as genuinely open. No clear winner: contested.119

Could two or three of the Lord’s brethren have been among the Twelve Apostles? (assessment: sound)

Could the brethren have been apostles? — the completed Hieronymian reading, in the form that came to be taught, needs two (perhaps three) of the brethren to be members of the Twelve — James the Lord’s brother = the apostle James son of Alphaeus, Jude the Lord’s brother = the apostle “Jude of James.” This is a distinct objection from whether the completing identifications hold: it would bite even if they did, for the Gospel and Acts data range the brethren outside the apostolic college at every turn. J. B. Lightfoot presses five converging points — the brethren disbelieve shortly before the crucifixion (John 7:5), are still distinguished from the Twelve after the ascension (Acts 1:14), while James’s resident office at Jerusalem is “hardly compatible with the world-wide duties” of the itinerant Twelve, Jude’s epistle “seems to disclaim the title of an Apostle” (Jude 17), and the apostle Jude is never once designated “the Lord’s brother” in any list (Lebbaeus, Thaddaeus, “Jude of James,” “Judas not Iscariot”), though that tag “would have fixed the person meant at once.” No plank is decisive alone — a cousin-theorist can reply to each, and the James-as-bishop and argument-from-silence points are soft — but the convergence is heavy, and modern criticism has “generally abandoned” the identification of the brethren with apostles. Its reach is bounded: it sinks the completed Hieronymian theory, not the cousin-reading as such (Blinzler’s pared-down form keeps the brethren clear of the Twelve too), and it is neutral between the Epiphanian and Helvidian readings — sound.120

Is a never-consummated (“Josephite”) marriage unbiblical — does 1 Corinthians 7 forbid a permanently celibate Joseph and Mary? (assessment: weak)

Is a celibate marriage unbiblical? — a theological objection distinct from the grammatical “until” dispute: that a real marriage never consummated is itself contrary to Scripture, so a permanently celibate Joseph–Mary marriage is implausible. Its modern popularizers (Mike Winger, GotQuestions.org) rest it on 1 Corinthians 7:3–5 (“defraud ye not one the other”) and on Matthew’s “took unto him his wife” (1:24) read as a full marital union. As a disproof it does not hold. The very verse cited carries its own exception — “except it be with consent” (7:5), “by permission, and not of commandment” (7:6) — so Paul forbids only unilateral deprivation, not a mutually vowed continence, and the chapter positively exalts celibacy (7:7–8, 7:32–35). And the objection’s premise (that a “wife” never known carnally is no true wife) is denied by the classical theology of marriage-by-consent, stated on this very case by Augustine: the marriage was fully real — “there was offspring… faithfulness… the bond… Only there was no nuptial cohabitation” — because “the designation of wife was neither destroyed nor made untrue, where there never had been… any carnal connection,” with Epiphanius (“called her husband because of the Law”) and Chromatius to the same effect. The argument shows at most that a Josephite marriage is unusual and not the scriptural norm — not that it is unbiblical or impossible — and the “obviously unbiblical” framing sits ill with the doctrine’s retention by Luther, Calvin, and Zwingli: weak.121

Is Mary’s perpetual virginity positively grounded in the “consecrated womb / new tomb” typology and the shut gate of Ezekiel 44:2 — not merely in ascetic devaluation of marriage? (assessment: plausible)

A positive rationale, not merely ascetic? — the one argument here that adjudicates a positive ground for the doctrine rather than the meaning of “brother.” J. B. Lightfoot observed that the belief was “not… suggested solely by the false asceticism of the early Church” but rested “quite as much” on a typology of fitness: as the Lord’s body lay in a sepulchre “wherein never man before was laid” (John 19:41; Luke 23:53), “so it seemed fitting that the womb consecrated by His presence should not thenceforth have borne any offspring of man.” The parallel is stated firsthand by Origen (Against Celsus ii.69: the virgin-born Lord fittingly laid in a new tomb, “purity symbolically indicated”), and its scriptural spine is the “shut gate” of Ezekiel 44:2, read of Mary almost with one voice — Jerome, Ambrose, Augustine, Cyril of Alexandria, Theodoret, John of Damascus, Aquinas. The historical core is sound — the ground really is positive and not merely ascetic, and Lightfoot’s further caveats (the reading throve first in Palestine, “where there was less disposition… to depreciate married life,” and before ascetic views mastered the Church) hold. But as a ground it is short of demonstrative: it argues from fittingness (“it seemed fitting”; “symbolically indicated”), not necessity; Ezekiel 44:2 is a temple-gate prophecy applied to Mary by accommodation (Jerome: what “some… understand”), not its plain sense; and the fathers press it chiefly for the virginity in the birth (the intact delivery), a distinct claim from the after-the-birth question the brethren debate contests. It adorns and corroborates the doctrine without proving it: plausible.122

Is the perpetual virginity a late invention — a dogma “defined nearly 2,000 years after Christ,” unheard of by early Christians? (assessment: weak)

Is it a late Roman invention? — a historical objection to the doctrine as a whole, distinct from every “brother”-crux above: the Reformed apologist James White’s charge that the Marian dogmas are “defined nearly 2,000 years after the birth of Christ” and demanded de fide though “generations of Christians lived and died without ever even having heard of such things.” Two levers — the timing of the definition and the antiquity of the belief — from which he infers the doctrine cannot be apostolic. As applied to the perpetual virginity it does not hold. The belief is second-century (the Protevangelium and Gospel of Peter, c. 150; Origen, c. 248), was affirmed as Aeiparthenos at Constantinople II (553) and defined at the Lateran Synod of 649, and was retained by Luther, Zwingli, and Calvin — so “nearly 2,000 years” and “never even heard of” are both false of it. The figure fits the two dogmas White bundles with it, the Immaculate Conception (1854) and the Assumption (1950), not this one; and the load-bearing inference — late definition, therefore not apostolic — proves too much, since Nicaea (325) and Chalcedon (451) defined doctrines confessed far earlier: definition follows challenge, and is not invention. Its one true kernel (the formal dogma does postdate the apostles, and the two later dogmas are nineteenth- and twentieth-century) keeps it off unsupported, but as a charge against the perpetual virginity it is weak.123

Does the perpetual virginity “have its origin in gnosticism, not Christianity”? (assessment: weak)

Is it a Gnostic import? — the origins companion to the “late invention” charge above, again from James White’s Top Ten List: that the perpetual virginity “has its origin in gnosticism, not Christianity, and [has] no foundation in apostolic doctrine or practice.” It misfires on both terms. The label is wrong: the serious scholarship in this neighbourhood assigns the earliest Marian legend (the Protevangelium) not to Gnosticism but to a Syrian encratite/ascetic milieu — and encratism, rigorist Christian continence, is not the dualist-mythological Gnosticism White names. The polarity is backwards: Gnostic Christology denied Mary a real motherhood — the Valentinians held Christ “passed through Mary just as water through a tube,” taking nothing of her flesh — whereas the perpetual virginity presupposes exactly the bodily childbearing they dissolved; the Ebionites, oppositely, denied the virgin birth outright, so neither heretical wing generated the doctrine. And its champions are the anti-Gnostic fathers who refuted that very error — Tertullian (real flesh “of the substance of Mary”), Cyril of Jerusalem (“nor yet by passing through the Virgin as through a channel; but was of her made truly flesh”), John of Damascus (not “as through a pipe”) — and Bauckham locates the whole second-century cluster in Syrian Jewish-Christian, not Valentinian, tradition. The one honest kernel — the doctrine’s early elaboration kept ascetic company — is a claim about milieu, not truth or Gnostic descent, which keeps the charge off unsupported without saving it: weak.124

Is Clement of Alexandria’s witness undermined by textual unreliability — that it survives only in an expurgated sixth-century Latin translation? (assessment: weak)

Is Clement’s witness a bad text? — a source-critical objection aimed at one witness rather than the doctrine: granting that Clement “appears to support” Mary’s virginity, Thorncrown Ministries discounts him because “the Greek original is lost” and only a sixth-century Latin adaptation by Cassiodorus survives, “written specifically to correct offending sentiments” — quoting the Catholic Mariologist Juniper Carol, “we cannot rely absolutely on this text.” It misfires by a category error: it hangs that caveat on the Stromateis VII.16 passage, which survives in Greek and was never a Cassiodorus adaptation — Carol’s caution is about Clement’s Latin-surviving Adumbrationes (the notes on the Catholic Epistles, source of the remark on Jude), a different work. Where the caveat does apply (the Jude note) it cuts the wrong way: a Latin editor purging error and holding the rival Hieronymian view would have struck Clement’s Epiphanian line, not inserted it (Lightfoot), so its survival marks fidelity, not tampering. And the Greek passage the objection names is itself firsthand evidence that Mary’s virginity in the birth was believed “down to our own time,” c. 200 — corroborating the early currency the charge would deny. The lost Hypotyposeis and the admitted editor keep it off unsupported, but as an argument against Clement it is weak.125
