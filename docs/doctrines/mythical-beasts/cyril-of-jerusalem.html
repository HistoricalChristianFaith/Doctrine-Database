Cyril of Jerusalem on the dragon in the Jordan, bound at Christ's baptism

Dates: c. 350 (Catechetical Lectures, Lecture III, On Baptism; delivered to baptismal candidates at Jerusalem during Lent, commonly dated c. 348–350) · Wikipedia
Summary: Instructing candidates for baptism, Cyril weaves Job 40–41 and Psalm 74 into a baptismal typology: a great "dragon" lay in the waters — "the dragon that draweth up Jordan into his mouth" (Job 40:23 LXX) — and because "it was necessary to break the heads of the dragon in pieces" (Ps 74:14), Christ "went down and bound the strong one in the waters" at His own baptism, so that the baptized "might receive power to tread upon serpents and scorpions." Cyril takes Job's picture of the beast at face value — "great and terrible," its tail too heavy for a fishing-boat — but bends the whole image toward salvation-history: the dragon is the adversary, death, whose "sting" is "drowned by Baptism." He is the earliest witness on this page, and his dragon belongs to the allegorical strand — the enemy Christ overcomes — though pressed into service as a figure of what happens in the baptismal water rather than as a portrait of the devil in the abstract.

Claims & evidence

Baptismal typology — the dragon in the waters bound at Christ's descent into the Jordan (Job 40:23; Ps 74:14)

According to Job, there was in the waters the dragon that draweth up Jordan into his mouth. Since, therefore, it was necessary to break the heads of the dragon in pieces, He went down and bound the strong one in the waters, that we might receive power to tread upon serpents and scorpions. The beast was great and terrible. No fishing-vessel was able to carry one scale of his tail: destruction ran before him, ravaging all that met him. The Life encountered him, that the mouth of Death might henceforth be stopped, and all we that are saved might say, O death, where is thy sting? O grave, where is thy victory? The sting of death is drawn by Baptism.

— Catechetical Lectures III.11 (On Baptism), trans. NPNF2 vol. VII. Cyril is explaining why Christ, though sinless, was baptized: "that thereby we again by our participation might receive both salvation and honour." He then reads the baptism as a combat in the deep. The "dragon that draweth up Jordan into his mouth" is Job 40:23 (the Septuagint of what the Hebrew calls Behemoth, who "trusteth that he can draw up Jordan into his mouth"); the command to "break the heads of the dragon in pieces" is Psalm 74:14; the promised "power to tread upon serpents and scorpions" is Luke 10:19. The beast's terrible bulk — its tail too heavy for a fishing-vessel — is Job's leviathan-language (Job 40:26 LXX = 41:7; "destruction ran before him," Job 41:13 LXX). Christ's going down into the Jordan is the going-down of "the Life" to bind "the strong one," so that when the believer descends into the baptismal water the sealed soul is not "swallowed up by the terrible dragon."1

For thou goest down into the water, bearing thy sins, but the invocation of grace, having sealed thy soul, suffereth thee not afterwards to be swallowed up by the terrible dragon. Having gone down dead in sins, thou comest up quickened in righteousness.

— Catechetical Lectures III.12. The typology completes itself in the candidate: as Christ went down to bind the dragon, so the baptized goes down into the same water — guarded now by "the invocation of grace" — and comes up "quickened in righteousness."2

Notes

  • An allegorical dragon, but with a baptismal turn. Cyril belongs with the dragon-as-adversary strand — the beast is "the strong one," death, whom Christ binds — not with the naturalists who ask whether leviathan is a real animal. But his interest is narrower and more concrete than the moral or cosmic allegories of Basil and Athanasius: the dragon is what lurks in the baptismal water, bound there once by Christ's descent so that every later descent into the font is safe. The whole apparatus of Job's monster is enlisted to dramatize what the candidate is about to undergo.
  • "Break the heads of the dragon" (Ps 74:14) — the shared Psalm-74 motif. Cyril's proof that the dragon "must" be broken is Psalm 74:14, "Thou brakest the heads of leviathan in pieces." The same verse anchors Augustine's allegory of "the heads of the dragons in the waters" broken "upon the water" of Baptism — so Cyril (c. 350) and Augustine (c. 415) independently tie Psalm 74's broken dragon-heads to the baptismal water, a full generation and a half apart. Basil reaches for the kindred image of "the sword of God… drawn against the dragon" (from Isa 27:1). The three form a cluster of the "broken/slain dragon" reading, of which Cyril is the earliest here.
  • Job 40:23 read of "the dragon." Cyril's "dragon that draweth up Jordan into his mouth" is, in the Hebrew, said of Behemoth (Job 40:23); the Septuagint and Cyril's usage let the two great beasts of Job blur into one "dragon" of the waters — the same fluidity Ephrem works with when he calls Behemoth "a dragon… a land animal" alongside the aquatic Leviathan. Cyril, however, is not classifying creatures at all; he reads the Jordan geographically (the actual river of Christ's baptism) and the dragon typologically (the adversary drowned there).
  • The lectures' date. Cyril's Catechetical Lectures were delivered to baptismal candidates at Jerusalem, most likely early in his career and commonly dated c. 348–350; c. 350 is the round figure used here. Cyril himself lived c. 313–386.
  • Cross-links. Compare Athanasius (the dragon "drawn with a hook by the Saviour" — the same victory over Job's beast), Augustine (Ps 74's dragon-heads broken "upon the water" of Baptism — the closest parallel), Basil (the sword drawn against the dragon), Ephrem the Syrian (Behemoth and Leviathan as the devil to be conquered by Christ), and John of Damascus (the literal-animal reading pressed hardest, against which Cyril's fully figurative dragon stands at the other pole).
