Gregory the Great on Behemoth & Leviathan (the ancient enemy)

Dates: c. 540–604; the Morals on the Book of Job (Moralia in Iob) composed c. 579–595, with the exposition of Behemoth and Leviathan in Books XXXII–XXXIV · Wikipedia
Summary: Pope Gregory I gives the ancient tradition's fullest sustained allegorical reading of Job's two great beasts. Across the closing books of his vast Morals on the Book of Job, both Behemoth (Job 40:15) and Leviathan (Job 41) are one figure: the devil, "the ancient enemy," created good by God yet fallen into wickedness, and in the end drawn out and destroyed by Christ. Where Ephrem had glossed "Behemoth is a dragon, that is, a land animal" as bare zoology, Gregory reads the very same verse as pointing past the animal to Satan; and his account of Leviathan "caught with a hook" — the bait of Christ's flesh concealing the sting of His Godhead — is the developed form of the hook-and-Saviour motif already sounded by Athanasius and Cyril.

Claims & evidence

Behemoth is the ancient enemy, the devil (Job 40:15)

Whom does He suggest, under the name 'Behemoth,' except the ancient enemy? which being interpreted from the Hebrew word, means 'Animal' in the Latin tongue. For when his malice is added below, his person is plainly pointed out.

— Morals on the Book of Job XXXII.xii.16 (on Job 40:15, "Behold Behemoth, which I made with thee"), trans. Library of the Fathers (Oxford, 1844–50); Morals on the Book of Job, Book XXXII, Job 40:15. Gregory takes the whole of God's closing speech to Job as unmasking the devil, and the beast's very name confirms it: "Behemoth" is simply the Latin for "animal," a placeholder that "his malice… added below" fills in with a person. The creature of the text is not denied; it is read straight through to the enemy it figures.

The devil was created good by God, then fell — against the Manichees (Job 40:15)

…how is that most wicked race said to have not been made, when the Lord declares that He created that Behemoth, the author, namely, of wickedness, who was rightly fashioned by nature?

— Morals on the Book of Job XXXII.xii.17; Book XXXII. Reading God's "which I made with thee" as spoken of the fallen angel — "made together" with man not in one substance but in the shared "knowledge of reason" — Gregory presses the point against Manichaeism: the "race of darkness" was not an uncreated evil principle. Behemoth, "the author… of wickedness," was nonetheless "rightly fashioned by nature"; his evil is a corruption of a good creature, not a rival first cause. The devil is a made thing that ruined itself.

Behemoth "eats hay as an ox" — the enemy devouring the carnal life (Job 40:15; Isa 11:7)

For when Isaiah observed the life of sinners devoured by the ancient and insatiable enemy, he said, the lion shall eat straw like the ox. [Is. 11, 7] But what is signified by the words hay, and straw, except the life of the carnal? Of which it is said by the Prophet, All flesh is hay. [Is. 40, 6]

— Morals on the Book of Job XXXII.xiii.18; Book XXXII, Isaiah 11:7. Gregory reads the ox's diet allegorically and by cross-reference: the "hay" Behemoth eats is "the life of the carnal" ("All flesh is hay," Isa 40:6), and the same beast is Isaiah's "lion" that "shall eat straw like the ox." One Spirit speaks in both prophets, so the pictures interpret each other — the monster of Job and the lion of Isaiah are the single "ancient and insatiable enemy" feeding on the souls of sinners.

Leviathan drawn out with a hook — Christ's flesh the bait, His Godhead the sting (Job 41:1)

But this Leviathan was caught with a hook, because when in the case of our Redeemer he seized through his satellites the bait of His Body, the sharp sting of His Godhead pierced him through. For a hook held as it were the throat of its swallower, when both the bait of the flesh appeared for the devourer to seize, and at the time of His passion His Godhead was concealed, in order to kill him.

— Morals on the Book of Job XXXIII.ix.17 (on Job 41:1, "Wilt thou be able to draw out Leviathan with a hook?"), trans. Library of the Fathers; Morals on the Book of Job, Book XXXIII, Job 41:1. Gregory names Behemoth and Leviathan two names for one enemy, and on God's question "Canst thou draw out leviathan with an hook?" he supplies the celebrated answer: God can, and did, in the Incarnation. Christ's mortal flesh is the bait the devil "swallows" at the Passion; concealed within it, "the sharp sting of His Godhead" is the barb that kills the swallower. The "line" of the hook, Gregory adds, is the genealogy of Christ in Matthew 1, let down "in this abyss of waters, that is, in this boundlessness of the human race." It is the fullest patristic development of the hook-and-Saviour reading of Job 41 that Athanasius and Cyril had already voiced.

Notes

  • The pure-allegory strand at full length. Gregory is to the dragon-as-devil reading what John of Damascus is to the literal-animal reading: its most developed ancient exponent. Where Athanasius compressed the leviathan-as-devil figure into a sentence and Basil into a moral gloss, Gregory unfolds it over three books, and through the Moralia's enormous medieval influence this became the West's default reading of Behemoth and Leviathan.
  • A direct foil to Ephrem on the very same verse. On Job 40:15 — "Behold now behemoth, which I made with thee" — Ephrem reads "Behemoth is a dragon, that is, a land animal," bare zoology; Gregory reads the same words as pointing past the animal to "the ancient enemy." The two make the clearest literal-versus-allegorical pair on this page, precisely because they gloss the same lemma in opposite directions. (Gregory holds only the allegory here; he shows none of the Augustinian both/and that keeps a literal creature alongside the figure.)
  • The hook motif matured. Gregory's Leviathan "caught with a hook" — flesh as bait, Godhead as the concealed sting — is the same reading of Job 41:1–2 that Athanasius put as "like a dragon he was drawn with a hook by the Saviour" and Cyril as Christ binding "the strong one in the waters." Gregory supplies the mechanism the earlier witnesses only gestured at: why the hook works — the Incarnation hides the deity that destroys the devourer.
  • Against Manichaean dualism. Gregory's insistence that Behemoth "was rightly fashioned by nature" turns the allegory to doctrinal use: the devil is a created good gone wrong, not an eternal evil principle. The beast of Job thus witnesses, for Gregory, against the "two principles" of Mani.
  • Transmission. The quotations are from the Morals on the Book of Job, Gregory's verse-by-verse allegorical commentary in thirty-five books, in the nineteenth-century Library of the Fathers translation (Oxford). Behemoth falls in Book XXXII, Leviathan in Books XXXIII–XXXIV, following the Vulgate's numbering (where Job 40:15 is "verse 10").
