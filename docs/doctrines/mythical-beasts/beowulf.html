Beowulf and the fire-dragon

Dates: narrative set c. 6th century (Scandinavia, the "Vendel Era") · surviving in a single manuscript (the Nowell Codex, British Library Cotton MS Vitellius A.xv) written c. 1000 · Wikipedia
Summary: The Old English epic Beowulf closes with the aged hero's death fighting a fire-breathing, hoard-guarding, night-flying dragon — roused when a fugitive steals a jewelled cup from its barrow-treasure. Aided by the young warrior Wiglaf, Beowulf slays the worm but dies of its venomous bite. This is legend, not a report offered as fact or a reading of Scripture: it belongs to this page not as a datable witness on the belief-timeline but as a cornerstone of the Northern/Germanic dragon tradition — the stock fire-drake (winged, flaming, brooding on gold in an ancient mound) that would shape the medieval and modern dragon in the Western imagination.

The episode

A thief steals a cup from the dragon's hoard and rouses it

The dragon — a "hoard-guard" that has brooded three hundred winters over the buried treasure of a vanished people — is woken when a fugitive slave, fleeing a beating, blunders into the barrow and carries off a single gilded cup to appease his lord:

yet the wretched fugitive
rallied anon from fright and fear
ere he fled away, and took the cup
from that treasure-hoard.

— Beowulf (roughly lines 2214–2231), trans. Francis B. Gummere, The Oldest English Epic (Macmillan, 1910), Part V (elfinspell.com); full text also at the Poetry Foundation. The theft is the hinge of the whole final movement: a small, almost accidental act of desperation looses a catastrophe, and the poet dwells on the melancholy of the treasure itself — heirlooms of a dead race, "the last survivor" having laid them in the earth long before the dragon ever found and claimed them.

The dragon, a night-flying fire-drake, burns the land in revenge — Beowulf's own hall among the ruins

Then the baleful fiend its fire belched out,
and bright homes burned. The blaze stood high
all landsfolk frighting. No living thing
would that loathly one leave as aloft it flew.

— Beowulf (around lines 2302–2315), trans. Gummere, Part V (elfinspell.com). Finding a cup gone from its count of gold, the worm rages through the night sky wrapped in flame and lays the countryside waste; the destruction reaches the king himself, for word comes to Beowulf that "the king's own home, of buildings the best, in brand-waves melted, that gift-throne of Geats." The creature is the fully-formed Germanic fire-drake: winged, nocturnal, venomous, and — crucially — a hoard-brooder, its malice bound to the gold it guards. This is the pattern the later Western dragon inherits, and it stands apart from the classical "flying serpent" of Herodotus and Ammianus, which was offered as sober natural history rather than heroic legend.

Beowulf and Wiglaf kill the worm; the hero dies of its bite

The old king's sword fails him and the dragon's fangs close on his neck; only the young kinsman Wiglaf, alone of the troop, stands with him, striking the beast lower down so that its fire slackens, and then Beowulf drives home a war-knife:

Heedless of harm, though his hand was burned,
hardy-hearted, he helped his kinsman.
A little lower the loathsome beast
he smote with sword … the Weders'-helm
smote that worm asunder, felled the foe,
flung forth its life. So had they killed it,
kinsmen both, athelings twain.

— Beowulf (around lines 2697–2709), trans. Gummere, Part VI (elfinspell.com). The victory is shared — "kinsmen both" — but it is Beowulf's last: the dragon's venom works in the wound the fangs have torn, and the hero, having asked to see the won hoard and named Wiglaf his heir, dies (lines ~2817–2820). The poem ends with his barrow-burial. The dragon-fight is thus of a piece with the saint-versus-dragon tales — the beast slain, but here at the cost of the slayer — rather than with the this-worldly beast-reports of Sozomen or Marco Polo.

Notes

  • Why it sits off the timeline. Every other entry on this page is offered by its author as fact (natural history, an eyewitness report) or as a reading of Scripture (literal, allegorical). The Beowulf-poet offers neither: the fire-drake is a figure of heroic legend, and no one — then or now — took it as a claim that such a creature had lived. It therefore anchors the page's third axis (the "pagan legend to be sifted from fact") rather than its chronological belief-line, and is placed in a distinct "dragon in legend" section on the summary.
  • A Christian poem about a heathen past. The surviving poem is the work of a Christian author looking back on pre-conversion Scandinavia; God is invoked and the treasure lies under a curse, yet the dragon itself carries no explicit devil-allegory of the kind Augustine, Gregory, and Bede read into Scripture's dragon. It is a beast of story, not a cipher for Satan — which is exactly why it illuminates the legendary strand the exegetes were trying to sift out.
  • The stock fire-drake. Winged, flaming, venomous, nocturnal, and coiled on buried gold, Beowulf's dragon fixes the template later borne by the Norse Fáfnir, the dragon of the Völsunga saga, and ultimately the dragons of modern fantasy. It marks the point where the biblical/classical serpent and the Northern hoard-worm converge into the familiar Western dragon.
  • Dating. The narrative is set among sixth-century Geats and Danes (Hygelac's raid, dateable to c. 520, is a fixed point); the single surviving copy was written c. 1000, and scholars still dispute how much earlier the poem was composed. The dragon episode runs from about line 2200 to the poem's end at line 3182.
