The Tombstone winged monster (1890)

Dates: the alleged sighting Sunday, April 20, 1890; reported six days later in the Tombstone Epitaph (Tombstone, Arizona Territory), Saturday, April 26, 1890, p. 3 · Library of Congress scan
Summary: A frontier newspaper item — famous ever since — in which two ranchers on horseback with Winchesters supposedly chase and shoot a winged, alligator-like "monster" on the desert between the Whetstone and Huachuca mountains, giving it dimensions (a 160-foot wingspan, an eight-foot head, eyes like a dinner plate) no living creature could bear. There was never a follow-up, a specimen, a photograph, or a second witness; the account is widely regarded as a tall tale of the kind Western papers ran to fill columns and draw readers. It belongs to this page not as a report anyone can credit but as legend — the modern, "living-pterosaur"/thunderbird end of the dragon-story arc, and a caution: exactly the sort of vivid, unverifiable beast against which the older "sift legend from fact" readers were working.

The account

The article as it ran in the Epitaph

Under the headline "Found on the Desert" and the subhead "A Strange Winged Monster Discovered and Killed on the Huachuca Desert," the paper opens with the discovery:

A winged monster, resembling a huge alligator with an extremely elongated tail and an immense pair of wings, was found on the desert between the Whetstone and Huachuca mountains last Sunday by two ranchers who were returning home from the Huachucas. The creature was evidently greatly exhausted by a long flight and when discovered was able to fly but a short distance at a time. After the first shock of wild amazement had passed the two men, who were on horseback and armed with Winchester rifles, regained sufficient courage to pursue the monster and after an exciting chase of several miles succeeded in getting near enough to open fire with their rifles and wounding it. The creature then turned on the men, but owing to its exhausted condition they were able to keep out of its way and after a few well directed shots the monster partly rolled over and remained motionless.

— Tombstone Epitaph, April 26, 1890, p. 3, "Found on the Desert." The dead beast is then measured into plain impossibility: it "measured about ninety-two feet in length and the greatest diameter was about fifty inches," had "only two feet" set just ahead of the wings, a head "about eight feet long, the jaws being thickly set with strong, sharp teeth," and eyes "as large as a dinner plate" that "protruded about half way from the head." One partly-folded wing, straightened out, gave "a measurement of seventy-eight feet, making the total length from tip to tip about 160 feet"; the wings were "composed of a thick and nearly transparent membrane and were devoid of feathers or hair, as was the entire body."1

The promised proof that never came

The report ends by staging its own verification — a wing-tip souvenir, a trip to town for supplies, and a plan to skin the carcass and ship the hide to the experts:

The men cut off a small portion of the tip of one wing and took it home with them. Late last night one of them arrived in this city for supplies and to make the necessary preparations to skin the creature, when the hide will be sent east for examination by the eminent scientists of the day.

— Tombstone Epitaph, April 26, 1890, p. 3.2 None of it materialised. There was no follow-up article in the Epitaph or any other paper, no hide was ever sent east, no "eminent scientist" recorded receiving anything, and no second witness came forward. The story simply stopped — the tell-tale shape of a frontier tall tale, in a town whose silver mines had flooded and whose fortunes (and newspaper circulation) had fallen on hard days by 1890.

Notes

  • Why it sits off the timeline. Every witness on the belief-timeline offered his beast either as fact (natural history, an eyewitness report he stood behind) or as a reading of Scripture. The Epitaph item is neither creditable natural history nor exegesis: it is an unverifiable, physically impossible newspaper yarn that its own paper never followed up. It is placed in the "dragon in legend" section of the summary alongside Beowulf's fire-drake — a legend, here the modern cryptid kind — not among the datable witnesses.
  • Why it cannot be a real animal. The dimensions rule it out on their face: a 160-foot wingspan is about the height of a sixteen-storey building, many times any flying creature that ever lived, and the "extremely elongated tail," membranous wings, and dinner-plate eyes are the furniture of imagination, not of any known pterosaur (whose largest kinds spanned some thirty to forty feet and had short tails). The verdict here is evidential, not metaphysical: this page grants real dragon-reports elsewhere (Sozomen's Bishop Donatus, Marco Polo's Yunnan "serpents," later read as crocodiles) — but those were sober, plausibly-sized reports by named authorities, where this is an anonymous single-source marvel that collapses under its own measurements.
  • The apocryphal "thunderbird photograph." The 1890 article contains no photograph — the Epitaph could not print photographs in 1890 — yet a durable legend holds that a picture once ran of the dead creature "nailed to a barn" with a row of men before it. That claim first surfaces only in 1963, in pulp magazine articles by Jack Pearl (Saga) and H. M. Cranmer (Fate); the Epitaph's own staff searched their archives in the 1960s and found nothing, and cryptozoologist Karl Shuker, who has tracked the case for decades, reports that every "thunderbird photo" circulated since has been exposed as a modern hoax (one a Revell pterosaur model kit, another a manipulation that left the original deer's hooves in the frame). The "photo" is best explained as a false memory, not a lost document.3
  • What it illustrates. The Tombstone monster is the recent end of the very stream the Fathers were trying to sift: a striking creature asserted as fact, propagated because it is vivid, and impossible to check — the "pagan legend to be sifted from fact" reborn as tabloid cryptozoology. It stands as the page's cautionary bookend to the credulous-yet-earnest natural history of Herodotus and Athanasius Kircher, showing the same appetite for a monstrous "specimen" running on, now unmoored from any witness who would stake his name on it.
