John of Damascus on dragons and mythical beasts

Dates: c. 675–749 · Wikipedia
Summary: In a short treatise On Dragons and Ghosts John draws a sharp line between real creatures and folkloric fiction: dragons exist, but they are ordinary animals — large serpents bred from other serpents — and the pagan tales that make them intelligent shapeshifters who take human form and carry off women are, he argues, logically impossible. He is the tradition's clearest early voice for reading the "dragon" (drakōn / tannin) as a natural beast rather than an apparition.

Claims & evidence

Dragons are real, but they are ordinary animals — serpents bred from other serpents

I am not telling you, after all, that there are no dragons; dragons exist but they are serpents borne of other serpents. Being just born and young, they are small; but when they grow up and get mature, they become big and fat so that exceed the other serpents in length and size. It is said they grow up more than thirty cubits; as for their thickness, they get as thick as a big log.

— On Dragons and Ghosts (Peri drakontōn), trans. Timothy Alferov from Maxim Kozlov's Russian rendering, creationism.org. John places the dragon squarely among the created kinds of animal — a beast Adam would have named (Gen 2:19), not a marvel of a different order. It is a natural-history judgment: the dragon is simply the largest of the serpents.

Dragons do not take human form, turn into serpents at will, or possess reason — only two rational natures exist

Some people contrive that dragons can both take the human form and turn into serpents, sometimes small, sometimes huge… so it follows with all possible clarity that dragons are intelligent beings exceeding men greatly, which has not been true, and never will be.

— On Dragons and Ghosts, creationism.org. John's refutation runs on a theological premise: God created only two rational (intelligent) natures — angels and men — so a shapeshifting, scheming dragon would be a third, which does not exist. The folk-belief that dragons change shape, steal women, or guard treasure with cunning is therefore ruled out not by naturalism but by the doctrine of creation. He caps the point with a swipe at the credulous: of the tale that a dragon can be driven off or killed by thunder, "When I heard this I laughed!"

The dragon's natural description — a bearded, horned kind, and (unlike other serpents) non-venomous

This dragon is a type of beast, like the rest of the animals, for it has a goat-like beard, and a horn at the back of its head. Its eyes are large and gold-coloured.

— On Dragons and Ghosts, as reproduced in Answers in Genesis (same Alferov/Kozlov translation). This "bearded" kind is elsewhere in the treatise called an agathodemon. John treats even the strangest reported features (beard, horn, golden eyes) as the marks of a species of animal, not of a supernatural being, and notes a further natural peculiarity:

All serpent kinds are poisonous, except dragons, for they do not emit poison.

— On Dragons and Ghosts, creationism.org.

He treats dragon reports as natural history — the Regulus / Bagradas giant serpent

One day, when Regulus, a Roman consul, was fighting against Carthage, a dragon suddenly crept up and settled behind the wall of the Roman army… When the dragon's hide, as Dio says, was measured by order of the senate, it happened to be, amazingly, one hundred and twenty feet long, and the thickness was fitting to the length.

— On Dragons and Ghosts, creationism.org. The episode — the giant serpent slain by Regulus at the river Bagradas during the First Punic War, its skin sent to the Roman Senate — comes to John from Cassius Dio. He cites it not as a legend but as a datum: a report of a real, if extraordinary, animal, of exactly the kind his natural-history account of dragons leads one to expect.

Notes

  • Provenance. The Greek Peri drakontōn (with the companion Peri stryggōn, "On Ghosts/Witches") is printed among John's works in Migne, Patrologia Graeca 94, cols. 1599–1604. The readily available English is Rev. Timothy Alferov's rendering of Fr. Maxim Kozlov's modern Russian translation (Moscow, 1997); it circulates most widely in young-earth-creationist venues, which quote it to argue that pre-modern Christians reported dragons as real animals. The text is John's; the reading it is put to is a modern one.
  • Method over content. Kozlov's own framing stresses the treatise's value as an exercise in "soberness of mind" — John distinguishing reported animal from folk-fiction — rather than as a catalogue of zoology. That sober-realist temper is the point of interest here: it is a worked example of an eighth-century theologian reading a "monster" of legend as a creature of God's ordinary world.
  • Cross-links. The same instinct — read Scripture's great beasts as real animals — bears on the Flood and Nephilim witnesses, where leviathan, behemoth, and the giants of Genesis 6 raise the parallel question of literal creature vs. allegory vs. legend.
