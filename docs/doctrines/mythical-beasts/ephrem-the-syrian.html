Ephrem the Syrian on Behemoth, Leviathan & the dragon

Dates: c. 373 (Commentary on Job, surviving in the Syriac catena tradition) · Wikipedia
Summary: Ephrem the Syrian reads the two great beasts of Job 40–41 along both of the axes this doctrine turns on, and he is the earliest witness on this page to hold them together. On Job 40:15 he gives a flat, one-line zoological identification: Behemoth is a land dragon, Leviathan a sea animal — the pair distinguished simply by habitat, as real creatures of earth and water. Yet on Job 41 the same monsters are allegory: their "prodigious nature" describes the devil, "destined to be conquered by the power of Christ when the fullness of time will come." He thus anticipates Augustine's both/and by a generation, though tersely and without Augustine's explicit statement of the genre principle that governs it.

Claims & evidence

Literal reading — Behemoth is a land dragon, Leviathan a sea animal (Job 40:15)

The Behemoth is a dragon, that is, a land animal, just as the Leviathan is an aquatic sea animal.

— Commentary on Job 40.15, trans. in the Ancient Christian Commentary on Scripture (Old Testament VI: Job, ed. Manlio Simonetti & Marco Conti, 2006); Job 40:15. On the verse where God begins the great creation-speech — "Behold now behemoth, which I made with thee; he eateth grass as an ox" (Job 40:15) — Ephrem offers no symbol at all, only a natural-history gloss: the two names denote two real animals told apart by their element, the one a beast of the land and the other of the sea. "Dragon" here is a plain kind of creature, not a figure, and Leviathan is simply its aquatic counterpart. This is the literal-animal reading in its barest form — the beasts of Job placed among the ordinary orders of God's creation.

Allegorical reading — the two beasts describe the devil, to be conquered by Christ (Job 41)

"All that is under heaven belongs to me. And I will not be silent." These words indicate the devil, whose prodigious nature is described in these two beasts, who is destined to be conquered by the power of Christ when the fullness of time will come.

— Commentary on Job (on Job 41), trans. in the Ancient Christian Commentary on Scripture (OT VI: Job, 2006); Job 41:10–11. On the boast God puts in Leviathan's mouth, Ephrem turns from zoology to figure: the "two beasts," Behemoth and Leviathan together, describe the devil, whose overweening nature they picture and whose defeat is fixed for the fullness of time, when Christ will overthrow him. The monster of Job is here a mask for the enemy — the same reading Athanasius had already pressed on this page, that Leviathan and the dragon stand for Satan hooked and tamed by the Saviour.

Notes

  • Both readings, one interpreter — a generation before Augustine. Ephrem holds the same both/and that Augustine would later make explicit: the beast is a real animal where the text catalogues creation (Job 40:15), and a figure of the devil where the text celebrates the coming victory (Job 41). The difference is that Ephrem simply does both, in two terse glosses, without articulating the principle — literal where the verse describes a creature, allegorical where it describes the enemy — that Augustine states outright.
  • Behemoth and Leviathan as a literal pair. Ephrem's identification is unusually clean for the literal-creature strand: he does not merely allow that the beasts are real, he sorts them — land dragon and sea animal — on the single criterion of habitat. Whether Behemoth and Leviathan of Job 40–41 are best read as two real creatures so identified is a live question across the tradition; Ephrem is an early and forthright voice for the affirmative.
  • Transmission. Ephrem's Commentary on Job does not survive as a continuous work; these glosses come down through the Syriac catena tradition and are gathered in the Ancient Christian Commentary on Scripture, which prints some of the Syriac Job material in English for the first time.
  • Cross-links. Compare Athanasius (Leviathan as pure allegory of the devil), Augustine (the same both/and, stated as a principle), and John of Damascus (the literal-animal reading pressed hardest). Ephrem's naturalistic handling of Scripture's monsters also echoes his reading of the giants of Genesis 6 on the Nephilim page, where he removes any need for superhuman parentage.
