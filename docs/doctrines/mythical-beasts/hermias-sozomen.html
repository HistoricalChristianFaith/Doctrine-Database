Sozomen on Bishop Donatus and the dragon of Chamaegephyrae (Ecclesiastical History 7.26)

Dates: Sozomen, c. 400–450; his Ecclesiastical History was composed at Constantinople c. 440–443, under Theodosius II. The event he reports is credited to Donatus, bishop of Euroea in Epirus, who flourished in the early fourth century · Wikipedia
Summary: The church historian Sozomen, cataloguing holy bishops of the recent past, relays as sober local history that Donatus of Euroea destroyed "a dragon of enormous size" that had stationed itself on the highway at Chamaegephyrae and was devouring "sheep, goats, oxen, horses, and men." The bishop killed it unarmed — making the sign of the cross in the air and spitting into its mouth — and the carcass took "eight pair of oxen" to haul away for burning. Sozomen offers no allegory: for him the beast is a plain, dead animal, measured against "the noted serpents of India," its corpse a public-health hazard and its slaying one more attested miracle of a saintly bishop. He is this page's witness for the dragon as a literal creature encountered in living memory — and its earliest representative of the saint-versus-dragon hagiographic motif that would later cluster around figures such as St George.

Claims & evidence

Donatus of Euroea slays a real dragon that was devouring men and cattle (Ecclesiastical History 7.26)

The inhabitants of the country relate many extraordinary miracles which he performed, of which the most celebrated seems to have been the destruction of a dragon of enormous size. It had stationed itself on the high road, at a place called Chamaegephyrae and devoured sheep, goats, oxen, horses, and men. Donatus came upon this beast, attacked it unarmed, without sword, lance, or javelin; it raised its head, and was about to dash upon him, when Donatus made the sign of the cross with his finger in the air, and spat upon the dragon. The saliva entered its mouth, and it immediately expired. As it lay extended on the earth it did not appear inferior in size to the noted serpents of India. I have been informed that the people of the country yoked eight pair of oxen to transport the body to a neighboring field, where they burnt it, that it might not during the process of decomposition corrupt the air and generate disease.

— Sozomen, Ecclesiastical History VII.26 ("St. Donatus, Bishop of Euroea, and Theotimus, High-Priest of Scythia"), trans. from the Greek (Bohn/Bagster edition); Sozomen, Ecclesiastical History, Book VII.1 What is striking is the register: this is not exegesis of a biblical tannin or a moralized leviathan but a contemporary report, set down by a mainstream fifth-century historian and sourced to eyewitness testimony ("the inhabitants of the country relate," "I have been informed," and closing, "the inhabitants of Isoria… bear testimony to the truth of this narration"). Sozomen treats the dragon as an ordinary, if terrible, animal — comparable in bulk to the great serpents of India (the pythons of classical natural history), heavy enough to need sixteen oxen to move, and dangerous as a rotting carcass. The miracle he means to record is the bishop's unarmed, cruciform conquest of it, not the mere existence of the beast, which he reports without a flicker of doubt.

Notes

  • A literal beast, not a figure. Where Athanasius, Basil, Gregory the Great, and Bede read Scripture's dragon straight through to the devil, Sozomen never allegorizes. His dragon is a carcass hauled off by oxen and burned to keep the air clean. He belongs with the literal-animal readers — Augustine on the "huge kind of living creatures" of Psalm 148, Julian of Eclanum on Job's real and terrifying behemoth, and above all John of Damascus, who three centuries later would insist dragons are simply large serpents and cite exactly this kind of measured-carcass report (the Regulus serpent of the Bagradas) as natural history. Sozomen supplies the same genre a domestic, Christian, and contemporary instance.
  • Reporter vs. protagonist — who anchors the slot. The deed is credited to Donatus, bishop of Euroea in Epirus, who flourished in the early fourth century (traditionally under Constantine). But the witness for how the beast was understood is Sozomen, who records the story c. 440–443 as received local history a century or so after the fact. The timeline slot is therefore anchored to Sozomen the reporter (c. 450), with Donatus named as the protagonist; it is Sozomen's flat, credulous, natural-historical framing — not any surviving word of Donatus — that makes this a datum for the doctrine.
  • The saint-versus-dragon motif. This is, on this page, the earliest full specimen of the hagiographic dragon-slaying pattern — a holy man overcoming a man-eating dragon by faith and the sign of the cross rather than by arms — the same motif that would later gather around Sylvester, Marcellus, Theodore, and most famously St George. Sozomen's version is notable precisely for being told as history within a mainstream church history, not as frank legend: the beast is weighed, measured, and disposed of like any large dead animal, and the narrative is guaranteed by named local witnesses.
  • Chapter numbering and translation. The account stands at Book VII, chapter 26 in the Greek text and the older Bohn/Bagster English translation carried at historicalchristian.faith (also in the NPNF2 vol. II translation of Chester D. Hartranft). Some editions and secondary summaries cite the same episode as 7.16; the passage is identical — a chapter-numbering divergence between editions, not a different text. The NPNF editor notes that the chapter derives from an independent Greek Life of Donatus that Sozomen (or a later translator) folded into the History.
