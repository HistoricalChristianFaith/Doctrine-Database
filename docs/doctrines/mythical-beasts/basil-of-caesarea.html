Basil of Caesarea on the dragon as the crooked serpent

Dates: c. 368 (Homilies on the Psalms, on Psalm 32 LXX = Psalm 33; assigned to Basil's presbyterate, c. 364–370) · Wikipedia
Summary: Preaching on the "upright" who are called to praise God, Basil the Great reads Scripture's dragon as pure allegory — the "crooked serpent" of Isaiah 27:1, "the author of sin," against whom "the sword of God is drawn." He turns the image into a moral contrast: the serpent is crooked, "which makes many twists and turns in its progress," and to follow it is to make one's own life "crooked, uneven and filled with contrarieties"; to follow the Lord is to "make his paths straight." Like Athanasius, Basil has no interest here in whether the dragon is a real animal; the beast is a figure of the devil and of the sin he authors.

Claims & evidence

Allegorical reading — the dragon is the crooked serpent, the author of sin (Isa 27:1)

Let us earnestly endeavor, therefore, to flee every crooked and tortuous act, and let us keep our mind and the judgment of our soul as straight as a rule, in order that the praise of the Lord may be permitted to us since we are upright. In the same way the serpent, which is the author of sin, is called crooked, and the sword of God is drawn against the dragon, the crooked serpent, which makes many twists and turns in its progress.… Therefore one who follows the serpent shows that his life is crooked, uneven and filled with contrarieties; but one who follows after the Lord makes his paths straight and his footprints right.

— Homilies on the Psalms, on Psalm 32 (LXX; = Psalm 33), trans. Agnes Clare Way, Saint Basil: Exegetic Homilies (Fathers of the Church, vol. 46, 1963); Isaiah 27:1. Basil is expounding the psalm's opening call — "Rejoice in the Lord, O ye righteous; for praise is comely for the upright" (Ps 33:1) — and reaches for Isaiah's oracle to explain who the upright are by their opposite. Isaiah had promised that "in that day the Lord… shall punish leviathan the piercing serpent, even leviathan that crooked serpent; and he shall slay the dragon that is in the sea" (Isa 27:1); Basil reads "crooked serpent" morally — the serpent is the author of sin, and its crookedness is the twisting, doubling path of the sinful life. The "sword of God" is "drawn against" this dragon, so that the choice before the hearer is a choice of paths: crooked after the serpent, or straight after the Lord.

Notes

  • A pure dragon-as-devil witness — with a moral turn. Basil sits squarely in the allegorical strand alongside Athanasius: the dragon is not a creature to be classified but the enemy to be named. Where Athanasius fixes on the devil vanquished — "drawn with a hook by the Saviour" — Basil fixes on the devil as tempter, the "author of sin" whose crookedness the sinner imitates. The beast becomes a mirror for the moral life: to walk after the serpent is to grow crooked, to walk after the Lord is to grow straight.
  • Isaiah 27:1 as the proof-text. Basil's dragon is specifically the "crooked serpent… the dragon that is in the sea" of Isaiah 27:1 — the same verse this page's intro lists among Scripture's monster passages. The Hebrew there names leviathan; Basil, reading the Greek, keeps the moral force of "crooked" (skolios) and lets the etymology do the exegetical work, a play on straight (orthos) versus crooked that governs the whole passage.
  • The homily's date. Basil's exegetical Homilies on the Psalms are commonly assigned to his presbyterate (he was ordained presbyter c. 364 and became bishop of Caesarea in 370), and are generally reckoned to predate his creation homilies (the Hexaemeron); precise dates for the individual homilies cannot be fixed, so c. 368 is an approximation within that window.
  • Cross-links. Compare Athanasius (the dragon hooked and tamed by the Saviour), Ephrem the Syrian and Augustine (both/and — real animal and figure of the devil), and John of Damascus (the literal-animal reading pressed hardest). Basil belongs with the first of these: for the purposes of this homily the dragon is wholly a figure, and the question of the animal never arises.
