Ambrose of Milan on the unicorn — the single horn as the unique Word of God

Dates: c. 390 (Ambrose, bishop of Milan 374–397; the exegetical works cited here belong to his episcopate, the latest left unfinished at his death) · Wikipedia
Summary: Ambrose, the great Latin bishop of Milan, reads Scripture's "unicorn" (unicornis, the Latin for the Septuagint's one-horned re'em) as a christological figure — but along a different strand from the Physiologus's virgin-capture. For Ambrose the meaning lies in the singleness of the horn: the one horn signifies the one, only-begotten Word of God, "not many words." Expounding Joseph's blessing (Deut 33:17), Psalm 118, and the "son of unicorns" of Psalm 28:6 (LXX; = Ps 29:6), he asks outright, "who is the unicorn, if not the only begotten Son of God, and the only Word of God, which was in the beginning with the Father?" — the Word whose single horn gores the nations to the ends of the earth. He is a strong Latin witness parallel to Tertullian and the Physiologus, on the one-God/one-Word axis later systematized by Aquinas — and he gives no virgin-capture.

Claims & evidence

"Who is the unicorn, if not the only begotten Son of God?"

Receive still, that Moses himself signified. He added: Horns of a unicorn, the horns of it: in them the nations shall be scattered (Deut. XXXIII, 17)… This is the Ancient of Days. For who is the unicorn, if not the only begotten Son of God, and the only Word of God, which was in the beginning with the Father? This Word has mortified and vivified the people of the Gentiles with its horn.

— Ambrose, Commentaries on the Twelve Davidic Psalms, on Psalm 44 (LXX; = Ps 45), expounding the blessing of Joseph.1 This is Ambrose's sharpest statement of the reading: the unicornis of Deuteronomy is the only-begotten Son, the one Word "in the beginning with the Father" (John 1:1–2). Where Satan too "has his horns" (Dan 7:21–22), the one horn of the unicorn is set against them — "good horns, with which he scatters our adversary the lion" — so that the Word gores the nations not to destroy but to convert them ("I will strike and I will heal," Deut 32:39). The single horn is thus a sign of Christ's unique generation and rule, not of any zoology.

The one horn signifies the one Word, "not many words"

…for he is the firstborn from the dead, having horns like a unicorn. But when he says horns, why did he use unicorn? For the unicorn itself, as experts say, is not found among the generations of wild beasts. And therefore we ought to value the unique Word more, because the substantive Word of God is one, not many words.

— Ambrose, On the Blessings of the Patriarchs (De benedictionibus patriarcharum), on Joseph's blessing.2 Here Ambrose makes the interpretive move explicit. He notices the very seam Tertullian had noticed — Deuteronomy gives the beast plural "horns" yet names it a "unicorn" — and, granting that the animal "is not found among the generations of wild beasts," turns the singleness of the horn into doctrine: because the beast is one-horned, it figures the one Word of God, "one, not many words" (unum, non multa verba). The reading is deliberately un-zoological: the point is not what a unicorn is, but that its oneness images the Word's oneness.

The unique Word spread through all the nations

…as Moses said: The firstborn of his bull carries the glory, his horns are like the horns of a unicorn; with them he shall gore the nations together to the ends of the earth (Ibid., 17). For this unique Word of God is foretold by the oracle that it is to be spread throughout the whole world among the nations.

— Ambrose, Exposition on Psalm 118 (Expositio in Psalmum CXVIII), again on Deut 33:17.3 The same verse yields the same figure a third time: the "unicorn" is "this unique Word of God," and the goring of the nations "to the ends of the earth" is read as the Word's spread through the whole world. Across three separate works Ambrose returns to Deut 33:17 and reads its one-horned beast the same way — the constancy showing this is a settled element of his christology, not a passing conceit.

Notes

  • A distinct strand from the virgin-capture. Ambrose's unicorn is christological, like the Physiologus's, but the mechanism is different. The Physiologus reads the whole scene — the fierce beast tamed only in a virgin's lap — as the Incarnation, and the single horn as "I and the Father are one." Ambrose never tells the hunt; he allegorizes the horn's singleness alone (the one Word, the one God, Christ's unique generation). His is a Western parallel to the Greek/Eastern reading, not a borrowing from it — and it is the strand later systematized by Thomas Aquinas on Ps 29:6, weighed on the argument page The KJV "unicorn" (Hebrew re'em), whose "allegorical tradition" side he reinforces on the Latin/monotheism axis.
  • The saints, too, called "unicorns." Expounding "the beloved is like the son of unicorns" (Ps 28:6 LXX; = Ps 29:6), Ambrose extends the figure to the faithful: as the beast's horns grow to "signify the progress of a fuller age," so "when horns begin to sprout… from our soul, they seem to signify the progress of a more perfect virtue," until the saints are "fortified with horns like the helmets of their own heads" against the world — a moral application riding atop the christological one.4
  • Job 39:9 turned a different way. On the other classic unicorn proof-text — "And the unicorn will serve you" (Job 39:9) — Ambrose reads not Christ but the enemy compelled to serve: the devil is permitted to tempt "as an adversary, but defends as a servant," God turning "against the devil the price of His own malice" so that the tempter unwillingly works the saint's good. It is a separate application of the same word, closer to the dragon-as-defeated-devil strand than to the unique-Word reading, and shows Ambrose engaging the unicorn texts across the board.5
  • Works and dating. The three expositions belong to Ambrose's episcopate (374–397): the Exposition on Psalm 118 to the later 380s, On the Blessings of the Patriarchs to the 390s, and the Commentaries on the Twelve Davidic Psalms to his last years, left unfinished at his death in 397. All three are Latin, and all three read Deut 33:17's unicornis the same way — the reading was fixed in Ambrose's mind whatever the occasion.
