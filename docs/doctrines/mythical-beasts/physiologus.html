The Physiologus on the unicorn — captured by a virgin, read as Christ

Dates: c. 2nd–4th century AD (Greek, Alexandria; the christianized recensions, incl. the Syriac, by the early 4th c.) · Wikipedia
Summary: The Physiologus ("the Naturalist") — an anonymous Greek book of animal lore compiled at Alexandria in late antiquity, the fountainhead of every medieval bestiary — gives the unicorn its enduring Christian shape. It describes a small but ferociously strong one-horned beast that no hunter can take by force, yet that grows tame and leaps into the lap of a pure virgin, and so is caught and led to the king's palace; and it reads the whole scene as a figure of Christ, the "horn of salvation" (Luke 1:69) raised up in Israel, made flesh of the Virgin, whose single horn signifies "I and the Father are one." Where Tertullian had already turned the versional "unicorn" into a figure of the Cross, the Physiologus fixes the one-horned image itself in Christian imagination — independent of any settled zoology of the Hebrew re'em — and hands the virgin-and-unicorn allegory to Ephrem, Gregory the Great, Isidore, and the bestiary and tapestry tradition after them.

Claims & evidence

A small, fierce, one-horned beast taken only by a virgin

The monoceras, that is, the unicorn, has this nature: he is a small animal like the kid, is exceedingly shrewd, and has one horn in the middle of his head. The hunter cannot approach him because he is extremely strong. How then do we hunt the beast? Hunters place a chaste virgin before him. He bounds forth into her lap and she warms and nourishes the animal and takes him into the palace of kings.

— Physiologus, ch. on the unicorn (monoceros), trans. Michael J. Curley.1 This is the whole of the natural-history notice on which the later legend is built: not the graceful horse of modern fancy but a small, kid-sized, unmasterable beast — the fierceness and single central horn inherited from the one-horned marvels of Greek lore (Ctesias's Indian ass, Aristotle's oryx, Pliny's monoceros), the virgin-capture the Physiologus's own contribution. The point of the notice is never zoological description for its own sake; each trait is set down to be allegorized.

The unicorn is Christ, "the horn of salvation," made flesh of the Virgin

Likewise the Lord Christ has raised up for us a horn of salvation in the midst of Jerusalem, in the house of God, by the intercession of the Mother of God, a virgin pure, chaste, full of mercy, immaculate, inviolate.

— the christological application, here in the Syriac recension of the Physiologus (early 4th century), which makes the reading explicit and Marian.2 The allegory rests on two proof-texts already read of the "unicorn": the "horn of salvation… raised up… in the house of… David" of Zechariah's prophecy (Luke 1:69), and the psalmist's "my horn shalt thou exalt like the horn of a unicorn" (Ps 92:10; 91:11 LXX). The fierce beast that no power can subdue, tamed only in the lap of a spotless virgin, becomes the Son whom "neither leadership, nor power, nor throne… could understand," incarnate of Mary; and his single horn is read as his oneness with the Father.

The developed allegory of the medieval bestiary (its direct descendant)

Thus, our Lord Jesus Christ spiritually symbolises the unicorn… His having one horn on his head signifies that he himself asserts: "I and the Father are one."

— the Rochester Bestiary (c. 1230), whose unicorn chapter is a faithful expansion of the Physiologus's.3 The bestiary keeps every element of the parent text — "a small animal similar to a small goat… very fierce… one horn in the middle of its head," which "leaps onto her lap and embraces her" when it sees the virgin — and spells out the doctrine the Physiologus had folded into the image: the smallness figures the humility of the Incarnation, the single horn the unity of the Godhead (John 10:30), the virgin the mother of the Lord. Through this line the Physiologus's unicorn passed into Isidore's Etymologiae, the great illuminated bestiaries, and the virgin-and-unicorn tapestries of the later Middle Ages.

Notes

  • The fountainhead of the christological-unicorn strand. The Physiologus does not begin the Christian reading of the "unicorn" — Tertullian, about 207, already took the "unicorn" of Deut 33:17 for the Cross, its horns the ends of the ship's-yard — but it fixes the reading that won: the beast is Christ, and its one horn a christological sign. From here the motif runs straight through the Fathers on this page. Ephrem, glossing Job 39:9, already knows "the virgin hunter can win it" and that the single horn means "one truth for the righteous"; Gregory the Great, on the same verse, tells the virgin-capture in full — "a virgin is placed before it, who opens to it her bosom… in which… it lays down its head." The Physiologus is the common source standing behind both.
  • Independent of the re'em zoology. The one-horned animal the Physiologus allegorizes is a creature of the Greek versions, not of the Hebrew Bible: the Septuagint had rendered the wild ox (re'em) by monokeros, "one-horn," and the whole tradition builds on that word. So the Physiologus is at once the richest fruit of the christological reading and the clearest case of how a translation artifact generated a lasting theology — the point weighed on the argument page The KJV "unicorn" (Hebrew re'em), whose "allegorical tradition" this page documents at its source.
  • A Latin echo in the same generation. The christological "unicorn" is not only Greek and Eastern. Ambrose of Milan (d. 397), expounding Joseph's blessing, reads the same one-horned beast as the unique Word: Christ is "the firstborn from the dead, having horns like a unicorn… the unicorn itself, as experts say, is not found among the generations of wild beasts. And therefore we ought to value the unique Word more, because the substantive Word of God is one, not many words."4 Ambrose does not give the virgin-capture; his is the allegory of the horn's singleness (the one Word, the one God), the strand later systematized by Aquinas — a Western parallel to the Physiologus's reading rather than a borrowing from it.
  • Text, versions, and dating. The Physiologus survives in many recensions and languages (Greek, Latin, Syriac, Armenian, Ethiopic…) and was "rewritten and translated many times, so it existed in many versions"; the Greek core is placed at Alexandria in the 2nd–4th century, the christianized and Marian development (the Syriac) by the early 4th. For the Greco-Roman prehistory of the one-horn and the transmission into the bestiaries, see Odell Shepard, The Lore of the Unicorn (1930).5
