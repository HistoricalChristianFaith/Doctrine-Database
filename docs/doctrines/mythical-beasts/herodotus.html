Herodotus on the winged serpents of Arabia

Dates: c. 484 – c. 425 BC (the Histories composed c. 440 BC) · Wikipedia
Summary: The earliest witness on this page, and a pre-Christian pagan one: in the Histories Herodotus reports the winged serpents of Arabia as a matter of natural history — real creatures whose bones, he claims, he went to see for himself near Buto, kept from overrunning Egypt by the ibis, and hanging in swarms over the frankincense trees of Arabia. He interprets no Scripture; he supplies the classical natural-history tradition of the "flying serpent" that later readers of the Bible's saraph — the "fiery flying serpent" (Isa 14:29; 30:6) — and of the great beasts inherited as background fact.

Claims & evidence

He claims to have gone to see the serpents' boneyard near Buto; the ibis destroys them

I went once to a certain place in Arabia, almost exactly opposite the city of Buto, to make inquiries concerning the winged serpents. On my arrival I saw the back-bones and ribs of serpents in such numbers as it is impossible to describe… With the spring the winged snakes come flying from Arabia towards Egypt, but are met in this gorge by the birds called ibises, who forbid their entrance and destroy them all. The Arabians assert, and the Egyptians also admit, that it is on account of the service thus rendered that the Egyptians hold the ibis in so much reverence.

— Herodotus, Histories 2.75, trans. George Rawlinson, Wikisource. Herodotus offers the boneyard not as legend but as autopsy — historiē, first-hand inquiry: he says he travelled to the spot and counted the heaps of vertebrae himself. The winged serpent is, for him, a real animal whose seasonal migration and predator (the ibis) are established facts of Egyptian and Arabian report.

He describes the serpent's form — a water-snake with featherless, bat-like wings

The serpent is shaped like the water-snake. Its wings are not feathered, but resemble very closely those of the bat.

— Herodotus, Histories 2.76, trans. Rawlinson, Wikisource. The description is soberly zoological — the creature is placed by comparison to two familiar animals (the water-snake and the bat), exactly the register a naturalist uses for a strange but real kind. This is the enduring image the phrase "flying serpent" carried into later natural history and, through it, onto the biblical saraph.

The winged serpents guard the frankincense trees of Arabia — the same that invade Egypt

For the trees which bear the frankincense are guarded by winged serpents, small in size, and of varied colours, whereof vast numbers hang about every tree. They are of the same kind as the serpents that invade Egypt; and there is nothing but the smoke of the styrax which will drive them from the trees.

— Herodotus, Histories 3.107, trans. Rawlinson, Wikisource. The frankincense-guarding serpents of Book 3 are expressly identified with the Egypt-invading serpents of Book 2 — one creature, reported twice. Herodotus treats their swarming numbers as a real economic obstacle to the spice trade, overcome only by the smoke of styrax gum.

Divine providence keeps them few — the reproduction that limits the winged serpents

Herodotus rounds off the account (3.108–109) with a reflection that reads the balance of nature as designed: a divine foresight, he says, has made the timid, edible creatures prolific, lest they be eaten off the earth, while holding down the fierce and harmful ones. Were the vipers and the winged serpents of Arabia to breed at the ordinary rate, he reasons, human life would be impossible — but the female is killed by the male in mating, and the young in turn gnaw their way out of the mother, so the deadly kinds stay few. The winged serpent is thus folded into a rational, providential order of nature — extraordinary, but no less a real animal for that.

— Herodotus, Histories 3.108–109, trans. Rawlinson, Wikisource.

Notes

  • Reported as fact, not myth. Herodotus does not present the winged serpents as a fable to be sifted: he claims autopsy of their remains, names their predator and their range, and reasons about their population as he would about hares or lions. Whatever the creatures actually were — modern readers have proposed migrating snakes, locust swarms, or a garbled memory of fossil beds — the point for this page is the mode: a "flying serpent" entered the ancient record as natural history, centuries before any Christian read the Bible's saraph.
  • The classical chain. The same winged/flying serpents recur in Strabo, Diodorus Siculus, Cicero, and Pliny, and — four centuries after Herodotus and now contemporary with the Church Fathers — in Ammianus Marcellinus, who repeats the Arabia-to-Egypt migration and the ibis that destroys them. Herodotus is the head of that stream.
  • A trade-route folklore. The frankincense-guarding serpents sit on a real map of commerce. Egyptian and Arabian mariners worked the incense coasts of the Red Sea and the Indian Ocean, and the marvel travelled with the cargo: as the folklorist Ernest Ingersoll observed, these seafarers "sailed up and down the Red Sea, exploring Sinai and Yemen; visited Socotra, where grew the dragon-blood tree… searched the Arabian coast, gathering frankincense (said to be guarded in its growth by small winged serpents)." The winged serpent of Book 3 is thus fastened to the very Arabian spice trade whose costly, hard-won cargo the tale conveniently hedged about with terror — a guardian-monster of exactly the kind that tends to gather around a scarce and valuable commodity. On the same sea-lane lay the island of Socotra and its dragon's-blood tree (Dracaena cinnabari), whose red resin lent the dragon its name at the other end of the route. — Ernest Ingersoll, Dragons and Dragon Lore (New York: Payson & Clarke, 1928), ch. 2, "Wanderings of the Young Dragon," sacred-texts.com.
  • The scriptural tie. Scripture's "fiery flying serpent" (Hebrew saraph meʻophep, Isa 14:29; 30:6; cf. the seraphim of Num 21:6–8) has often been read alongside these classical reports — whether the Bible preserves an independent memory of the same Arabian creature, or the two traditions simply share a region and an image, is a further question weighed on its own terms on the argument page Is the "fiery flying serpent" of Isaiah 30:6 a real winged reptile? (assessment: weak as a literal flyer; the real-snake-and-emblem core plausible), which finds these classical reports one much-copied folklore rather than independent corroboration.
  • Cross-links. Herodotus stands with the literal-animal readers on the timeline — Augustine on the dragon as "a huge kind of living creatures," Sozomen on Bishop Donatus's measured dragon, and John of Damascus on the dragon as an ordinary large serpent — though, as a pagan naturalist, he arrives at the real beast from outside the Christian frame rather than from within it.
