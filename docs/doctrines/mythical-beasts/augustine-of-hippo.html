Augustine of Hippo on dragons and mythical beasts

Dates: c. 415 (Expositions on the Psalms) · Wikipedia
Summary: Augustine reads Scripture's "dragons" along both of the axes the doctrine turns on, and the two readings sit side by side in his Expositions on the Psalms. On Psalm 148:7 ("Dragons and all abysses") the dragon is a literal animal — "a huge kind of living creatures, greater there are not upon the earth" — a real beast that praises its Maker by moving the beholder to praise Him. On Psalm 74:13–14 ("Thou hast broken the heads of the dragons in the waters") the dragon is allegory: the demons and, chief of them, "the devil himself," whose broken head is broken pride. He is thus the tradition's clearest early witness that the two readings need not compete — the same interpreter takes the beast as real where the text describes nature and as a figure where the text describes God's victory.

Claims & evidence

Literal reading — the dragon is a real animal, the largest of living creatures (Ps 148:7)

"Dragons" live about the water, come out from caverns, fly through the air; the air is set in motion by them: "dragons" are a huge kind of living creatures, greater there are not upon the earth. Therefore with them he begins, "Dragons and all abysses."

— Exposition on Psalm 148, New Advent (NPNF1 vol. 8). Expounding "Praise ye the Lord from the earth. Dragons and all abysses" (Ps 148:7), Augustine treats the dragon as an ordinary, if extraordinary-sized, creature of the natural world — it lives about the water, emerges from caverns, moves the air in flight. He then presses the obvious objection to a psalm that bids the beasts "praise" God, and answers it without allegorizing the dragon away:

What? Think we that the dragons form choirs, and praise God? Far from it. But do ye, when you consider the dragons, regard the Maker of the dragon, the Creator of the dragon: then, when you admire the dragons, and say, "Great is the Lord who made these," then the dragons praise God by your voices.

— Exposition on Psalm 148, New Advent. The dragon here is a datum of creation, not a symbol: it "praises" God only in the sense any creature does, by prompting its beholder to glorify the Creator. This is the literal-animal reading in full — the beast is real, and its place is among "all cattle; creeping things, and flying fowl" of the psalm's roll-call of creatures.

Allegorical reading — the dragons are the demons, and the one great dragon is the devil (Ps 74:13–14)

"You have confirmed in Your virtue the sea, You have broken in pieces the heads of dragons in the water." Dragons' heads, that is, demons' pride, wherewith the Gentiles were possessed, You have broken in pieces upon the water: for those persons whom they were possessing, You by Baptism have delivered.

— Exposition on Psalm 74, New Advent (NPNF1 vol. 8). Here the same word carries no zoology at all. The "heads of dragons" broken "in the water" are the demons' pride broken in the water of Baptism, by which the Gentiles are freed from possession. Augustine then narrows from the many dragons to their one chief:

For those dragons have their chief, and he is himself the first great dragon… We understand by dragons all the demons that war under the devil: what single dragon then, whose head was broken, but the devil himself ought we to understand?… That is, the beginning of sin… The beginning of all sin is pride. There hath been broken therefore the head of the dragon, hath been broken pride diabolical.

— Exposition on Psalm 74, New Advent. The broken head of the dragon is read through Genesis 3:15 — the seed of the woman marking the serpent's head — so that "the head of the dragon" becomes "the beginning of sin," which is pride. The dragon is here wholly a figure: the devil and the demonic host under him, defeated at the root by Christ.

Notes

  • Two readings, one interpreter. The interest of Augustine for this doctrine is precisely that he does not choose. Where the text catalogues creatures (Ps 148), the dragon is a real animal; where the text celebrates God's victory over evil (Ps 74), the dragon is the devil. The literal and the allegorical readings are governed by genre and context, not by a fixed verdict on what a "dragon" is — a model of the both/and that later witnesses often collapse into an either/or.
  • Shared proof-text. The "breaking of the heads of the dragon(s)" of Psalm 74:13–14 (with its companion Leviathan-crushing of Ps 74:14 and Isa 27:1) is a stock patristic type of Christ's defeat of Satan, read the same way by other fathers on the same verse. Augustine's baptismal application — the dragons' heads broken "in the water" — ties the figure specifically to the exorcistic and delivering force of Baptism.
  • Cross-links. Augustine's willingness to read Scripture's great beasts as real creatures where the text warrants also bears on the Flood and Nephilim witnesses, where leviathan, behemoth, and the giants of Genesis 6 raise the same literal-vs-allegory-vs-legend question. Compare John of Damascus, who presses the literal-animal reading much harder and leaves the allegory aside.
