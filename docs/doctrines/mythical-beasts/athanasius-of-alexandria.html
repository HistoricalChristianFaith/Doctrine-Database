Athanasius of Alexandria on the dragon and the devil

Dates: c. 360 (Life of Antony; Athanasius c. 296–373) · Wikipedia
Summary: In the Life of Antony — the enormously influential monastic biography Athanasius wrote soon after the hermit's death — the great beasts of Job stand wholly for the devil. Putting the discernment of spirits into Antony's mouth, Athanasius has the demons appear "as the Lord revealed the devil to Job," clothed in leviathan's fire and smoke, only for the whole boast to collapse: "like a dragon he was drawn with a hook by the Saviour." The Job 41 image of leviathan hauled out on a hook (Job 41:1–2, "Canst thou draw out leviathan with an hook?… Canst thou put an hook into his nose?") is read entirely as allegory — the devil defeated and leashed by Christ. Athanasius is the tradition's earliest witness on this page, and a pure representative of the dragon-as-devil strand later shared by Augustine (on Ps 74).

Claims & evidence

The demons appear in leviathan's likeness — "as the Lord revealed the devil to Job" (Job 41)

And he said they often appeared as the Lord revealed the devil to Job, saying, "His eyes are as the morning star. From his mouth proceed burning lamps and hearths of fire are cast forth. The smoke of a furnace blazing with the fire of coals proceeds from his nostrils. His breath is coals and from his mouth issues flame." When the prince of the demons appears in this wise, the crafty one, as I said before, strikes terror by speaking great things…

— Life of Antony §24, NPNF2 vol. IV. The description Athanasius quotes is the Lord's speech out of the whirlwind over leviathan (Job 41:18–21 LXX). He takes the passage not as natural history of a sea-beast but as a portrait of "the prince of the demons" — the devil, who "strikes terror by speaking great things." The fearsome zoology of Job's leviathan has become a mask the devil wears to frighten monks.

The dragon "drawn with a hook by the Saviour" — leviathan leashed by Christ (Job 41:1–2)

And though speaking words so many and so great in his boldness, without doubt, like a dragon he was drawn with a hook by the Saviour, and as a beast of burden he received the halter round his nostrils, and as a runaway his nostrils were bound with a ring, and his lips bored with an armlet. And he was bound by the Lord as a sparrow, that we should mock him. And with him are placed the demons his fellows, like serpents and scorpions to be trodden underfoot by us Christians.

— Life of Antony §24, NPNF2 vol. IV. This is the heart of the passage. The very questions God puts to Job to humble him — "Canst thou draw out leviathan with an hook?… Canst thou put an hook into his nose? or bore his jaw through with a thorn?" (Job 41:1–2), rhetorical because no man can — Athanasius answers in Christ: the Saviour has drawn the dragon out with a hook, ringed his nostrils, bored his lips, and leashed him "as a sparrow, that we should mock him." The dragon is the devil throughout; the fishing-and-taming imagery of Job 41 is the mechanism of the devil's defeat, so that his demonic "fellows" are now "serpents and scorpions to be trodden underfoot" (Luke 10:19) by Christians. The reading is purely allegorical — no interest at all in whether leviathan is a real animal.

Notes

  • A pure dragon-as-devil witness. Where Augustine holds the literal and the figurative readings side by side (real dragons on Ps 148, the devil on Ps 74), Athanasius here works only the figurative axis: the beasts of Job are the devil and his demons, full stop. The Life of Antony's concern is spiritual warfare, not zoology, so the leviathan/dragon appears purely as a type of the defeated enemy.
  • The Job 41 "hook." The image rests on the NPNF footnotes' own citations — Job 41:1 for "drawn with a hook," Job 41:2 for the ring and bored lips — the same leviathan-with-a-hook verses that anchor the wider patristic type of Christ hauling up and mastering the devil. It sits alongside Augustine's "breaking of the heads of the dragons" (Ps 74:13–14) as the stock scriptural furniture of the harrowing/defeat-of-Satan tradition.
  • Influence. The Life of Antony was among the most widely read texts of late antiquity (Augustine records in the Confessions that hearing it told drove his own conversion), so its treatment of the Job beasts as the devil helped fix the dragon-as-devil reading across the later tradition.
