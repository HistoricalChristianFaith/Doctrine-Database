The Bagradas serpent: does its many-author attestation prove a real giant animal?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: plausible — a real, large snake (most likely an African rock python) encountered by Regulus's army at the Bagradas in 256 BC is a plausible, arguably probable, historical kernel; but the argument's two load-bearing props overstate it — the "many independent authors" collapse to essentially one Livian source-tradition copied author-to-author, and the constant "120 feet" is a grossly inflated, copied figure (roughly six times any real python), so the breadth of attestation is not independent corroboration and the giant is legend accreting on a real kernel · advanced by John of Damascus

Related arguments: dragons as real creatures — see also did the early Christians read dragons, leviathan, and behemoth as real animals? (contested), the same literal-animal reading weighed across the patristic tradition as a whole.

Here the claim is not about how to read a verse but about a piece of reported natural history — that a whole shelf of sober Roman authors independently record a colossal serpent, so it must have been a real animal of extraordinary size. The interpretation it serves is the literal-animal reading of Scripture's and legend's great serpents: if Rome preserved the hide of a 120-foot snake, dragons are creatures, not myths.

The claim

One day, when Regulus, a Roman consul, was fighting against Carthage, a dragon suddenly crept up and settled behind the wall of the Roman army… When the dragon's hide, as Dio says, was measured by order of the senate, it happened to be, amazingly, one hundred and twenty feet long, and the thickness was fitting to the length.

— John of Damascus, On Dragons and Ghosts, creationism.org. John relays the episode not as a fable but as a datum — a report of a real, if extraordinary, animal. The modern form of the argument presses the point further: the same serpent is recorded by Pliny, Aulus Gellius (from Tubero), Valerius Maximus, Cassius Dio, Silius Italicus, Florus, and Orosius, and that breadth of independent testimony is said to lift the story out of legend and establish a genuine giant.

Who advances it

On this page's timeline the datum is carried by John of Damascus (c. 730), who cites Cassius Dio's measured hide to show that dragons are ordinary — if outsized — animals. The modern multiple-attestation argument is a staple of young-earth-creationist "dragon-legend" literature (Answers in Genesis, which reproduces John's treatise, marshals such reports to argue that humans and large reptiles coexisted), and it rests on a real and impressive body of ancient testimony: the story enters serious Roman annalistic history, not just poetry, and it enters it early.

The evidence for

The core is genuinely well-attested and old. The earliest namable source is Q. Aelius Tubero (late first century BC), whose lost history is quoted by Aulus Gellius:

Tubero in his Histories has recorded that in the first Punic war the consul Atilius Regulus, when encamped at the Bagradas river in Africa, fought a stubborn and fierce battle with a single serpent of extraordinary size, which had its lair in that region; that in a mighty struggle with the entire army the reptile was attacked for a long time with hurling engines and catapults; and that when it was finally killed, its skin, a hundred and twenty feet long, was sent to Rome.

— Aulus Gellius, Attic Nights 7.3.1 Pliny the Elder, writing his Natural History a generation earlier, files the same beast among "serpents of remarkable size" and adds the detail that its remains were kept as a public trophy: the snake was "besieged, like a fortress, by means of ballistae and other engines of war," and "its skin and jaws were preserved in a temple at Rome, down to the time of the Numantine war" (i.e. for over a century).2 Valerius Maximus gives a vivid battle-piece — the serpent barring the army from the river, seizing men in its mouth and crushing others in its coils, impervious to missiles until brought down by a barrage of ballista-stones — and Cassius Dio, the one Greek witness, records that the hide was carried to Rome and measured before the Senate at 120 feet.3 That a real, very large snake underlies all this is entirely credible on its own terms: African rock pythons (Python sebae) are native to the region and are among the largest snakes alive, and a python barring a watering-place and resisting light weapons is exactly the kind of encounter a campaigning army could have had and remembered.4

Adversarial assessment

What holds. A real event with a real animal at its core is the most economical reading. The report is early (Tubero and, behind him, Livy, both within about two centuries of 256 BC), it entered mainstream Roman history rather than surviving only as folklore, and the creature it describes — a very large constrictor in North Africa — is zoologically unremarkable. Richard Stothers, surveying the ancient "great serpent" reports, takes just this view: they rest on real large snakes (and, for the sea-serpents, whales), the Bagradas snake among them.5 So the modest interpretation — there was a real large snake — is well-grounded.

What is overstated or imprecise. The argument's force, however, is made to rest on two props that do not bear the weight the "real giant" conclusion needs.

  1. The "many independent authors" are not independent. This is the crux. The witnesses collapse to a single source-tradition. Valerius Maximus names his source outright — the serpent "related by Titus Livius, carefully and eloquently" — so he is not corroborating Livy but copying him; Orosius's dramatic version descends from Livy too; Florus, the Periocha (summary) of Livy's lost Book 18, Vibius Sequester, and the epitomators of Valerius are all downstream notices of the same strand; and Silius Italicus's long set-piece is poetic elaboration, not a separate report. Gellius preserves the one earlier name, Tubero, at second hand. Only Cassius Dio's Greek account is arguably an independent line, and it is the latest of all (second–third century AD) and the one that most openly tips the animal toward drakōn, "dragon." So the roll-call of authorities is a roll-call of transmitters of one tradition (Livy, and behind him Tubero), not a convergence of independent eyewitness. Multiplicity of copies is not multiplicity of testimony.6

  2. The "120 feet" is a copied, grossly inflated figure. The largest African rock pythons reliably run to about six metres — roughly twenty feet — so 120 feet is about six times any real snake, and physically impossible for a serpent's anatomy. Tellingly, the number does not vary as one would expect from independent measurement: it is the same round figure in Tubero, Pliny, Valerius, and Dio, precisely the signature of a single datum handed down, not of four men measuring a hide. The measurement "by order of the Senate" lends the number an air of officialdom, but the number itself is the tradition's, carried forward unchanged.4

  3. The tradition visibly grew a monster. The literary strand shows embellishment at work. Florus casts the serpent as "born, as it were, to avenge Africa" — an aetiological flourish, not a field note — and Silius Italicus frames his snake as a member of an "implicit canon of great dragons" (the Giants, the Lernaean Hydra), assimilating the episode to the Greek dragon-slaying topos, with the narrator a thin double of Vergil. Daniel Ogden, the leading authority on classical dragon-lore, reads the Bagradas serpent as exactly this kind of literary drakōn: whatever its factual kernel, it was worked up into the shape of a mythic dragon-fight.7

None of this requires denying a real animal — and it should not be denied by a naturalistic reflex either; a very large snake is a perfectly ordinary thing. What fails is the specific inference from breadth of attestation to a real giant. The breadth is illusory (one tradition, many copies), and the giant is the part the tradition added. The sober kernel and the 120-foot marvel are two different claims, and only the first survives the weighing.

Assessment

Plausible. That a real, large snake — most plausibly an African rock python — lay behind the Bagradas story is coherent, unrefuted, and arguably probable: the report is early, it entered serious Roman history, and the creature it describes is zoologically ordinary. What the argument cannot deliver is its headline. The "many independent authors" reduce, on inspection, to a single Livian (and, behind Livy, Tuberonian) source-tradition — Valerius Maximus cites Livy by name, and Florus, Orosius, Silius, the Periocha, and the epitomators all descend from it — so the attestation is broad in copies but narrow in origin, not the independent corroboration the argument needs. And the invariant "120 feet" is a copied, physically impossible figure (about six times any real python), a mark of legend accreting on the kernel rather than of measurement. Trust the report as evidence that a real large serpent was encountered and remembered; distrust it the moment it is used to prove a giant, or offered as many witnesses rather than one much-copied one. (John of Damascus is not faulted for relaying Cassius Dio accurately — Dio does report the senatorial measurement; the overreach is the modern use of the roll-call as independent proof.)
