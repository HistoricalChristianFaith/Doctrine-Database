What are Behemoth and Leviathan (Job 40–41) — literal beast, chaos-monster, or the devil?

Type: interpretive argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: the rival identifications are best read as layered, not rivals — a real, formidable creature at the descriptive base (the hippopotamus and crocodile of the scholarly default, poetically amplified with the ancient Near East's chaos-monster imagery), over which Scripture itself and the Fathers build a figure of the devil. On the bare literal identity the reading is contested (river-animal vs. mythic amplification, no clean winner); the reception's move to devil-allegory is sound as a second sense the text invites; the young-earth "dinosaur" reading alone is weak.

See also: Related arguments — dragons as real creatures, especially did the early Christians read dragons, leviathan, and behemoth as real animals? (contested), which weighs the patristic literal strand and the "dragons = dinosaurs" leap in detail. This page is the doctrinal core those arguments branch from.

Job's God-speeches close with two set-piece beasts: Behemoth, a grass-eating land brute whose "bones are like bars of iron," who "moveth his tail like a cedar" and "drinketh up a river" (Job 40:15–24), and Leviathan, the un-hookable, scaled, fire-breathing sea-terror who "maketh the deep to boil like a pot" and is "a king over all the children of pride" (Job 41). The same two names recur elsewhere pointed very differently: Leviathan is God's plaything in the sea (Ps 104:26), the many-headed dragon whose heads God crushes and feeds to the desert-dwellers (Ps 74:13–14), and the "piercing serpent… the dragon that is in the sea" the LORD will slay "in that day" (Isa 27:1).1 Out of this the tradition split four ways over what the beasts are: (a) literal known animals — the hippopotamus and crocodile of standard modern commentary; (b) literal but extraordinary or extinct creatures — the young-earth "dinosaur" reading; (c) mythopoetic chaos-monsters — the primeval dragon of Near-Eastern combat myth, poetically deployed; and (d) allegory for the devil — the reading of Athanasius, Cyril, Gregory, and Augustine. The question here is not how to read one verse but which identification best fits the text and its reception — and whether these four are rivals or layers.

The four readings

(a) Literal known animals — hippopotamus and crocodile. The standard critical identification, first argued at length by the Huguenot scholar Samuel Bochart (Hierozoicon, 1663) and carried by the major modern commentaries (Marvin Pope, John Hartley), reads Behemoth as the hippopotamus and Leviathan as the crocodile — both Nile animals an Israelite could know by report. The fit is close: Behemoth "eateth grass as an ox" (Job 40:15), "lieth under the shady trees, in the covert of the reed, and fens," among "the willows of the brook" (40:21–22), and lets a river run into his mouth (40:23) — a riverine grazer, exactly the hippo's habits; Leviathan's impenetrable scaly hide, terrible teeth, and churning of the water (41:15–32) suit the crocodile.2 A cognate patristic instinct read them simply as the largest creatures of land and sea: Ephrem the Syrian (c. 373) — "the Behemoth is a dragon, that is, a land animal, just as the Leviathan is an aquatic sea animal" — and Thomas Aquinas, who literally identified them as the elephant and the whale, "which among other land animals… has a certain preeminence because of the size of his body" and "the large whales which have an increase in size over every other genus of animal."3

(b) Literal but extraordinary / extinct — the "dinosaur" reading. Young-earth interpreters grant the beasts are literal animals but deny they are the hippo or crocodile, fixing on two phrases the identification strains over: Behemoth "moveth his tail like a cedar" (40:17) — a huge tail unlike the hippo's stub — and "his bones are like bars of iron" (40:18). On this reading Behemoth is a sauropod dinosaur, contemporary with man, Job's testimony to a young earth.4

(c) Mythopoetic chaos-monster. A reading with both ancient and modern warrant takes Leviathan (and behind him Behemoth) not as any animal of the field but as the primeval chaos-dragon of Near-Eastern combat myth, poetically enlisted. Leviathan is the exact name of the Ugaritic Lôtān, the twisting seven-headed sea-serpent slain by Baal and Anat in the Baal Cycle; Isaiah's "leviathan the piercing serpent, even leviathan that crooked serpent" (Isa 27:1) reproduces the Ugaritic epithets almost word for word, and Ps 74:13–14 has God crush the heads (plural) of the sea-dragon at creation — a cosmogonic victory, not a zoological note.5 Strikingly, an ancient exegete reached the same conclusion: Ishodad of Merv (9th c.) reports that "the Interpreter" — Theodore of Mopsuestia — "calls it an imaginary dragon that the author [of Job] has poetically invented by himself," reasoning that no such single, mateless creature exists in a world where "all animals have been created in pairs."6

(d) Allegory for the devil. The dominant patristic reading makes the two beasts one figure — the ancient enemy. Gregory the Great devotes the close of his Moralia to it: "Whom does He suggest, under the name 'Behemoth,' except the ancient enemy?" and Leviathan is the devil "caught with a hook" by the concealed Godhead of the incarnate Christ.7 Athanasius (leviathan = the devil "drawn with a hook by the Saviour"), Cyril of Jerusalem (the dragon in the Jordan bound at Christ's baptism), and Aquinas (the whole passage "a metaphor" for "the evil of the devil") stand with him; and on Ps 104:26's Leviathan "whom thou hast made to play therein," Augustine and Caesarius of Arles read the sporting sea-dragon as the devil whom God, foreseeing his malice, "created… good" and now uses.8 (A fifth, Jewish, reception runs alongside: Ishodad notes "the Jews assert that it is an ox, and… some day they will eat it and the Leviathan as well" — the Talmudic eschatological banquet on Behemoth and Leviathan.)9

Adversarial assessment

The literal base is strong but genuinely contested. On the plain descriptive detail the hippopotamus and crocodile fit Behemoth and Leviathan better than any rival: the grass-eating, reed-lying, river-drinking land-brute is a hippo to the life, and the scaly, un-hookable water-terror a crocodile. This is why it is the scholarly default and why a cognate literal instinct is patristic (Ephrem, Aquinas). But the identification does not fully domesticate the text. Leviathan in Job 41 breathes fire and smoke, makes the deep boil, and has "not his like… upon earth" (41:33) — language that overshoots any Nile crocodile; and the same name elsewhere is unmistakably the mythic chaos-dragon: the multi-headed sea-monster God crushes at creation (Ps 74) and the "crooked serpent" of Isaiah 27:1 that answers, epithet for epithet, the Ugaritic Lôtān. So the honest verdict on the bare identity is contested: the poet is plainly describing a real, formidable animal and amplifying it with the color of the primeval dragon — the reason readings (a) and (c) have each drawn serious defenders, ancient (Aquinas vs. Theodore of Mopsuestia) and modern alike.

The devil-allegory is a layer, not a rival. The patristic move from beast to devil is not a displacement of the literal creature but a second sense built atop it, and the text invites it: Behemoth is "the chief of the ways of God" and Leviathan "a king over all the children of pride" (40:19; 41:34) — superlatives that strain past any animal — and the very name "Leviathan / dragon" is, in Ps 74, Isa 27, and Revelation 12, a cosmic enemy God defeats. The best literal readers hold both senses at once under a genre rule: Ephrem calls Behemoth "a land animal" on Job 40:15 yet the same beasts "the devil… destined to be conquered by… Christ" on Job 41; Julian of Eclanum affirms "the true description of the beast according to the … story" first and adds "the strength of spiritual iniquity" atop it; Aquinas grants the Lord "in the literal sense intended to express the characteristics of elephant and whale" and then reads them as a "metaphor" of Satan. So allegory and literal are not competing answers to one question but answers to two — what is the creature? and what does the text, by its excess, signify? As reception the devil-reading is sound; as the literal sense it was never meant to be, and the Fathers who state their method say so.

The "dinosaur" reading is weak. It is the one identification the text actively resists. Behemoth "eateth grass as an ox" and lies "in the covert of the reed, and fens" among "the willows of the brook" — a marsh-grazing riverine herbivore, precisely the hippopotamus and precisely not a sauropod (grass as a widespread plant postdates the Mesozoic). The two props are thin: "moveth his tail like a cedar" describes the stiffening or swaying of the tail (many read the following clause, "the sinews of his stones are wrapped together," as continuing a description of the beast's loins and virility, 40:16–17), and "bones like bars of iron" is plain hyperbole for strength, matched by "strong pieces of brass" in the same verse. And "dinosaur" is a category coined in 1842 — to read it back into Job supplies a meaning the word never carried, the same anachronism weighed and found weak next door.10 The verdict is evidential, not metaphysical: one need not deny that God could make a behemoth of any kind to see that Job 40 describes a grass-eating river-beast, not a Mesozoic reptile.

Assessment

Layered, not rivals. The four identifications are not four answers to one question but a real creature seen through three further lenses. At the descriptive base stands a formidable literal animal — the hippopotamus and crocodile of the scholarly default, and of a cognate patristic instinct (Ephrem, Aquinas); on the bare identity the reading is contested, because the poet plainly amplifies that animal with the chaos-monster color of the primeval dragon (Ugaritic Lôtān; the many-headed Leviathan of Ps 74; Isaiah's crooked serpent), a mythopoetic layer with both ancient (Theodore of Mopsuestia) and modern warrant. Onto this the tradition built its devil-allegory, licensed by the text's own excess ("king over all the children of pride") and by the dragon's role elsewhere as God's cosmic enemy — a second sense that is sound as reception and that the best literal readers hold alongside the creature, not instead of it, under a genre rule Augustine and Julian state outright. Only the young-earth "dinosaur" identification is weak, resisted by the grass- and-reed habitat the text insists on and by an anachronism the sources will not bear. Trust the beasts as real, awe-provoking animals amplified by ancient dragon-lore and read by the Church as figures of the devil; distrust any single reading pressed as the whole — the hippo/croc as if the poetry were a field guide, the chaos-dragon as if no animal were in view, the allegory as if it erased the creature, or the "dinosaur" as if Job dated the earth.
