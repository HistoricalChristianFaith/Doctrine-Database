The KJV "unicorn" (Hebrew re'em) — a real one-horned animal?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — three things must be kept apart. That the referent is a real animal, not the mythical horse, is settled — both sides grant it, so the popular jab that Scripture "believes in unicorns" misses. That the English word "unicorn" is an artifact of transmission (Hebrew re'em → Greek μονóκερως "one-horn" → Latin unicornis/rhinoceros → "unicorn") is also documented. But whether the animal was one-horned turns on its identity, and that is genuinely unsettled: the two-horned wild ox (aurochs) is the leading modern candidate but rests on the northern cognate (Akkadian rīmu) and a forest range that never reached the biblical deserts; the one-horned reading of the oldest versions is a serious, defensible ancient identification — the Arabian oryx, which did range Sinai and Canaan, whose nearest cognate (Arabic rīm) means "oryx," and whose two straight horns read as one in profile, reconciling the Hebrew's plural "horns" (Deut 33:17) with the ancients' real (not fabulous) one-horned zoology, in which μονóκερως was simply the Greek word for the rhinoceros. The Septuagint's reading is not to be presumed inferior to the Masoretic-anchored modern one · advanced on one side by modern lexicography and the later versions ("wild ox"); on the other by the oldest translators, by Answers in Genesis, and by the geographic and philological case for the oryx

Related arguments: the re'em / unicorn — see also Is the Bible's "unicorn" the fabulous one-horned horse of legend? (weak), which weighs whether the word names the fabulous beast; this page weighs which real animal it names.

The King James Version calls a "unicorn" nine times — God brings Israel out of Egypt "as it were the strength of an unicorn" (Num 23:22; 24:8); Joseph's "horns are like the horns of unicorns" (Deut 33:17); the psalmist is heard "from the horns of the unicorns" and his own "horn" is exalted "like the horn of an unicorn" (Ps 22:21; 92:10); Lebanon skips "like a young unicorn" (Ps 29:6); the untameable "unicorn" will not "abide by thy crib" (Job 39:9–10); and the "unicorns" fall with "the bullocks with the bulls" in the slaughter of Edom (Isa 34:7). All nine render one Hebrew word, re'em. The question is zoological, not doctrinal: does that word name a real one-horned (or one-horned-looking) animal, or a two-horned wild ox whose single horn belongs only to the Greek and Latin that translated it?1

Two readings

The mainstream reference verdict is that re'em is a wild ox and the horn a translation artifact:

There can be no doubt that some species of wild ox is intended… The rendering "unicorn" arose from the μονóκερως of the Septuagint and the unicornis and rhinoceros of the Vulgate.

— here in the words of McClintock and Strong's Cyclopedia ("Unicorn"), with the versional history spelled out by the International Standard Bible Encyclopedia and the Jewish Encyclopedia.1 On that reading re'em denotes a real, formidable, two-horned bovine, and the single horn belongs not to the Hebrew but to the Greek and Latin words that translated it.

But the confidence of that verdict outruns the evidence at one joint: the animal's own identity was lost early and has never been settled. The aurochs is only the leading candidate — a nineteenth-century proposal that "we will, of course, never know with certainty" — and the oryx and the rhinoceros remain live rivals, "wild ox," "wild bull," "buffalo," "oryx," and "rhinoceros" all standing behind the modern versions and their footnotes.2 Two of those candidates — the oryx and the rhinoceros — are real animals that the ancients themselves counted one-horned. So the second reading weighed here is that the oldest translators, closer to a living identification than we are, saw and named a real one-horned (or one-horned-looking) beast, and that their μονóκερως records a fact about the animal, not merely a slip about a word.

Who advances each

The wild-ox / translation-artifact reading is the settled position of modern lexicography and Bible reference: Brown–Driver–Briggs and HALOT gloss re'em as "wild ox"; the Jewish Encyclopedia, the ISBE, McClintock–Strong, and Britannica identify it with the aurochs; and every major revision since the KJV — the Revised Version, ASV, RSV, NIV, ESV — retires "unicorn" for "wild ox."1 The one-horned reading has two very different champions. The oldest is the tradition itself — the Septuagint, the Vulgate, and the whole patristic and medieval line that read a one-horned beast (below). The most vocal modern one is young-earth apologetics (Answers in Genesis): the biblical unicorn was a real animal — an aurochs, or an extinct one-horned beast such as Elasmotherium — not a fairy-tale horse, "for to think of the biblical unicorn as a fantasy animal is to demean God's Word."3 The soberest form of the one-horned case, though, is neither ancient piety nor modern apologetic but a straightforward zoological and philological argument for the oryx, set out below.

The case for the two-horned wild ox

The best-attested cognate is bovine. Re'em (רְאֵם) is cognate with Akkadian rīmu, the "wild bull" or aurochs (Bos primigenius) that Mesopotamian kings hunted as the very emblem of strength and that strides, in glazed brick, across Babylon's Ishtar Gate; the Ugaritic rʾm, the earliest attestation of the word, is likewise a wild bull. The picture several verses paint fits that animal — a beast of overwhelming strength (Num 23:22), untameable, refusing the plough and the crib (Job 39:9–10), and above all paired in the herd with "the bullocks" and "the bulls" (Isa 34:7). The aurochs was no legend: it survived in Europe until 1627, when the last cow died in the Jaktorów Forest of Poland.4

The English "unicorn" is a fact about the versions. Whatever the animal, the word travelled. The Septuagint rendered re'em by μονóκερως (monokeros, "one-horned") — which was itself the ordinary Greek word for the rhinoceros. Jerome's Vulgate carried the Greek horn into Latin, splitting it two ways — rhinoceros at Numbers 23:22, 24:8, Deuteronomy 33:17, and Job 39:9–10, and unicornis at Psalm 22:21, 29:6, 92:10, and Isaiah 34:7 — and the English of Coverdale, Geneva, and the King James simply calqued the Latin unicornis as "unicorn." So the English "unicorn," with its later freight of the fairy-tale horse, is a fossil of transmission; the live question is only what the underlying animal was.5

The Hebrew most likely gives the beast two horns. The decisive verse is one the KJV keeps in plain view. Deuteronomy 33:17 blesses Joseph: "his horns are like the horns of unicorns" — one re'em's two horns imaged as Ephraim and Manasseh, the pair with which "he shall push the people together." A literally single-horned animal has no plural "horns" to give; the Hebrew has one re'em bearing "horns," which points to a two-horned beast and rules out a genuine one-horn — a rhinoceros, or the fabulous horse — as the re'em itself. The bovine setting of Isaiah 34:7 points the same way. This is the strongest single card in the wild-ox hand — and, as the next section shows, it is satisfied not only by the aurochs but by a two-horned animal that reads as one-horned in profile.6

The case for a real one-horned animal

Set against that is a case the "mere artifact" verdict passes over, and it does not depend on keeping a literal single horn. It has three prongs.

The identity is unsettled, and the aurochs is not native to the biblical lands. The aurochs was a beast of high-rainfall forest — central and south-eastern Europe, Anatolia, and Mesopotamia. There is little sign it lived in the deserts of Canaan, Sinai, and Arabia in the biblical period. The great wild horned animal that did range those lands is the Arabian oryx (Oryx leucoryx): into the early 1800s it "could still be found in the Sinai, Palestine, the Transjordan, much of Iraq, and most of the Arabian Peninsula." An animal named without explanation, its identity assumed known to every ancient reader, is far likelier to have been the oryx of the writers' own wilderness than a forest ox they would have known only by report.7

The cognates split — and the nearest one means "oryx." The Semitic evidence is not the one-way street the wild-ox case implies. Akkadian rīmu and Ugaritic rʾm, the northern cognates, do mean "wild bull"; but Old Arabic rīm — the cognate of the very desert where Sinai and Edom lie — means "oryx," and Modern Hebrew took re'em back for the oryx, not the ox. The word's meaning shifted with its animals: "wild ox" where the aurochs ranged, "oryx" where it did not. To read the Hebrew re'em off the Akkadian alone, against the Arabic of its own homeland, is precisely to let one cognate overrule the geography.7

The oryx reconciles the plural "horns" with the one horn — and the ancients' one-horn was real zoology. The oryx carries two long, straight horns; yet "from certain angles, the oryx may seem to have one horn rather than two," and because the horn cannot regrow, an oryx that loses one is one-horned for life. So the oryx answers both halves of the puzzle at once: it has the two "horns" Deuteronomy 33:17 requires and the single horn the versions saw. And the one-horned category the Septuagint reached for was not fable but the natural history of its day: Aristotle lists the oryx and the "Indian ass" among real solid-hoofed one-horned animals, Pliny adds the Indian ox (the rhinoceros) and the fierce monoceros, and μονóκερως was the plain Greek word for the rhinoceros — a real, genuinely one-horned beast. Whether the translators identified re'em with the oryx seen in profile, with the rhinoceros of hearsay, or with a one-horned iconographic convention for the wild ox (the aurochs is drawn side-on, one horn showing, on Assyrian reliefs), their μονóκερως is a deliberate placement of the animal in a real zoological class, not a careless error — and, as the oldest surviving identification, it has a claim the modern reconstruction cannot simply wave aside.7

The versional "one horn" in the Fathers

Whatever the animal, the entire ancient interpretive tradition had a one-horned beast before it — the monokeros/unicornis/rhinoceros of its versions — and built richly on the single horn. Cosmas Indicopleustes, writing about 550, took the "unicorn" for a real terrible beast "quite invincible," whose "strength lies in his horn"; he could not claim to have seen one, but drew it from "four brazen figures… set up in the four-towered palace of the King of Ethiopia," and marshalled Psalm 22:21, Psalm 29:6 ("the son of unicorns"), and Numbers 23:22 as "complete testimony to the strength… and glory of the animal."8 The Greco-Roman one-horn tradition — Ctesias's one-horned Indian ass (c. 400 BC), Aristotle's one-horned oryx, the fierce monoceros of Pliny (Nat. Hist. 8.31) and Aelian — had by then fused, in the Alexandrian Physiologus, with the biblical monokeros into the great medieval emblem: the unicorn so fierce no hunter can take it, tamed only when it lays its head in a virgin's lap, read as Christ made flesh in the Virgin. That lore surfaces in the exegesis: Ephrem on Job 39:9 — "similar to an ox… armed with a single horn" — already knows that "the virgin hunter can win it," and Gregory the Great tells it in full ("a virgin is placed before it, who opens to it her bosom… in which… it lays down its head"), naming the beast "monoceros… that is, the unicorn."9 And the single horn became doctrinally load-bearing: Ephrem made it "one truth for the righteous"; Thomas Aquinas, glossing "the son of unicorns" (Ps 29:6), explained that "the Jews are called unicorns inasmuch as they glory in the worship of one God," and on Job 39:9 described "the rhinoceros or unicorn… with one horn in the middle of his forehead"; and Ambrose made the one horn the one, only-begotten Word of God.10 The christological use was there from the first: already about 207, Tertullian — the earliest Christian to read the beast — took "his horns are the horns of a unicorn" (Deut 33:17) not for any animal ("no… one-horned rhinoceros… nor any two-horned minotaur") but for the Cross, its two horns the arms of the ship's-yard and the "unicorn" the upright mast, joining to it the "horns of the unicorns" of Psalm 22:21.11 None of this settles the zoology — the Fathers were reading their versions, not the Negev — but it shows the one-horned reading was not a stray medieval fancy: it was the universal ancient text, and its horn carried the weight of monotheism and Incarnation.

Adversarial assessment

What holds on each side. The transmission of the English word is documented at every link, and no one need defend the fairy-tale horse: the referent is a real animal, and on that the young-earth apologists and the lexicographers agree. Deuteronomy 33:17 does most likely give the beast two horns, which excludes a literally single-horned re'em — no rhinoceros, no magic horse, as the animal itself. So far the wild-ox reading is on firm ground.

Where the wild-ox verdict overreaches. It hardens a leading guess into a fact and treats the oldest witnesses as simply mistaken. Three things resist that. (1) The identity is not settled — "we will never know with certainty" — and the aurochs, a forest animal, is a poor fit for the deserts the biblical writers actually knew, where the oryx was the great wild horned beast. (2) The cognates split: the northern rīmu/rʾm mean "wild ox," but the Arabic rīm of the biblical wilderness means "oryx," and to fix the sense from the Akkadian alone is to overrule the geography. (3) The oryx reconciles the data the wild-ox reading treats as contradiction: it has the two horns Deuteronomy needs and the single horn the versions saw (two straight horns reading as one in profile), and it places the Septuagint's μονóκερως — the Greek for the rhinoceros — in the ancients' real one-horned zoology rather than in fable. On this the received framing has it backwards: the one-horned reading may preserve a real feature of a real, local animal that the later "wild ox" gloss loses.

Where the one-horned case in turn overreaches. It cannot be pressed into a literal single horn. The young-earth reach for an Elasmotherium or a lost one-horned "kind" runs straight into Deuteronomy 33:17's plural "horns," and even sympathetic creationist zoologists concede the point — Jonathan Sarfati, defending re'em as a real animal, faults the "unicorn" rendering on exactly that grammar. Nor does the oryx win outright: Isaiah 34:7's "bullocks with the bulls" and the strongest cognate (Akkadian rīmu) tilt toward a bovine, i.e. the aurochs. The honest picture is a real standoff — the aurochs fitting the bovine context and the chief cognate but not the geography, the oryx fitting the geography, the nearest cognate, and the one-horn tradition but not the bovine pairings.312

Assessment

Contested. Sort the claims. Is the referent a real animal rather than a myth? Yes — settled on both sides; the popular sneer that the Bible "believes in unicorns" fails. Is the English word "unicorn" an artifact of transmission (re'em → μονóκερως → unicornis/rhinoceros → "unicorn")? Yes — the word's history is documented. Was the animal literally one-horned? Probably not — Deuteronomy 33:17 gives one re'em a pair of "horns," so neither the rhinoceros nor the fabulous horse can be the re'em as such. But the live and genuinely unsettled question — what animal was it, and was the versions' single horn an error? — the wild-ox verdict closes too soon. The aurochs is only the leading modern candidate, and a forest ox foreign to the deserts the writers knew; the Arabian oryx, which ranged those deserts, whose nearest cognate (Arabic rīm) means "oryx," which Modern Hebrew took back for the oryx, and whose two horns read as one in profile, matches both the Hebrew's plural "horns" and the ancients' one-horned zoology — in which μονóκερως was simply the rhinoceros. Trust that the beast is real, and that it most likely bore two horns; distrust the further claim that the Septuagint's μονóκερως is a mere blunder to be corrected by the later Hebrew — the oldest translators may have named, in the oryx of their own wilderness, a real animal that reads as one-horned, and the Greek is not to be presumed inferior to the Masoretic reading here. The one place the one-horned case does fail is the literal single horn — the Elasmotherium and lost one-horned "kinds" of young-earth apologetics, which Deuteronomy 33:17 denies.
