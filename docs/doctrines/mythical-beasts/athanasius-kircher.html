Athanasius Kircher on dragons and mythical beasts

Dates: 1601–1680 · Mundus Subterraneus published 1665 · Wikipedia
Summary: The Jesuit polymath Athanasius Kircher, in the "De Draconibus" chapter of his vast survey of the underground world Mundus Subterraneus (Amsterdam, 1665), treats dragons as real, cataloguable creatures of natural history. He holds the existence of enormous serpents to be "entirely certain," defends even the disputed winged dragons against the scholars who call them imaginary, and adduces museum specimens and named naturalists' reports as evidence. He is the latest witness on this page and shows how far into the age of learned science the literal-dragon view survived.

Claims & evidence

The existence of enormous serpents is "entirely certain" — they are found everywhere

The existence of dragons, or rather serpents, of enormous size is entirely certain, for they are found everywhere, and most especially in Ethiopia, India, and in other places in the tropical zones under the present rule of various European maritime powers.

— Athanasius Kircher, Mundus Subterraneus (Amsterdam, 1665), "De Draconibus," trans. Darius Matthias Klein, "A Natural History of Dragons" (christianlatin.blogspot.com); original Latin edition scanned at archive.org (Tomus II). Kircher writes in the middle of the scientific revolution — a contemporary of Boyle and Newton, and himself an experimenter, geologist, and one of the first to look at plague-matter under a microscope. Yet on dragons he stands with the page's literal / natural-history strand: the great serpents are ordinary, if extraordinary-sized, fauna of the hot zones, their existence a settled fact rather than a figure of Scripture or a piece of legend.

Winged dragons are real — the doubters are contradicted by histories and eyewitnesses alike

With regards to the winged dragons there is no agreement among the Authors, who for the most part deemed them to be imaginary. But they are contradicted both histories from all eras and eyewitness accounts as well. Winged dragons - small, large, and enormous - have occurred in every era in all parts of the globe; see Cardinal Barbarini, Book VII, Chapter 23, concerning winged dragons, which he himself claimed to have seen in the museum of Gulselm Asulica the Parisian. Here also we must make mention of the authoritative account of Bellon, who tells that he saw the complete bodies of winged dragons which he found in Egypt after a zealous search.

— Kircher, Mundus Subterraneus, "De Draconibus," trans. Klein, christianlatin.blogspot.com. Here the natural-history posture is at its boldest: where most learned authors of his day judged the winged dragon fabulous, Kircher takes their skepticism itself to be refuted by "histories from all eras and eyewitness accounts." His star witness is the French naturalist Pierre Belon (Bellon, 1517–1564), who reported finding in Egypt the complete bodies of winged dragons — Kircher relays that one had "a distended belly, two feet, two wings anatomically similar to those of bats, and the tail of a serpent," a description that closely tracks Herodotus's bat-winged Arabian flying serpent nearly two thousand years before. It is the same appeal to remains and eyewitnesses that Herodotus made ("I saw the back-bones and ribs of serpents") and that Sozomen and Marco Polo made for their this-worldly beasts — now marshaled by a seventeenth-century scholar as a case against his own colleagues' doubt.

A stuffed dragon may still be seen in the Barberini museum at Rome

A similar dragon can still be seen at Rome in the Museum of Cardinal Francis Barbarini…

— Kircher, Mundus Subterraneus, "De Draconibus," trans. Klein, christianlatin.blogspot.com. Kircher points to a preserved specimen — a dragon stuffed and mounted in the collection of Cardinal Francesco Barberini, some fifteen palms in length, with two rows of teeth — as physical proof of the kind. Such cabinet-of-curiosities "dragons" were a staple of the age; many were fabricated composites (dried rays, or lizard-and-bat assemblages sold to credulous collectors), and their presence in the great museums helped keep the literal dragon alive in learned natural history long after. That Kircher, a serious naturalist, cites the specimen straight is a measure of how the empirical turn could reinforce rather than dissolve the dragon: a mounted body in a cardinal's museum looked like evidence.

Notes

  • Where he sits on the timeline. Kircher (1665) is the latest witness on this page, later even than Marco Polo (c. 1298), and its one early-modern voice. He belongs to the literal / natural-history strand — Herodotus, Ammianus, Augustine, Julian, Sozomen, John of Damascus, Marco Polo — reading the dragon as a real animal rather than an allegory of the devil or a passage of Scripture. His special interest is chronological: he shows the literal-dragon view surviving into the 1660s, held by a leading scholar of the scientific age, and even fortified by that age's appetite for specimens and eyewitness "data."
  • The "De Draconibus" digression. Most of Mundus Subterraneus is careful early science — geology, hydrology, volcanoes, one of the first works of speleology. The dragon chapter is a credulous excursion by comparison: Kircher retails second- and third-hand narratives (the dragon of Rhodes slain by the knight Deodato of Gozon; a Swiss dragon episode) as readily as measured specimens. The value of the page is precisely that tension — a first-rate natural philosopher applying the era's evidentiary language (museums, eyewitnesses, "certain") to what later science would file as folklore.
  • Modern afterlife. Kircher's dragon passages, and this winged-dragon defense in particular, are frequently quoted in young-earth and "living-pterosaur" literature as testimony to real dragons within historical memory. The wording most often circulated there ("Of winged dragons, dispute has only arisen between authors… Winged dragons — small, great, and greatest — have been produced in all times in every land") is a looser rendering of the same passage; the more careful translation by Darius Matthias Klein is quoted above. Read in context, Kircher's "winged dragons" gather together Belon's Egyptian specimens, museum mounts, and classical reports — the very materials a modern zoologist assigns to composite fakes, gliding Draco lizards, and the Herodotean flying-serpent tradition.
