Tertullian on the unicorn's horns as a type of the Cross

Dates: c. 155 – c. 220 (the two passages here from Against Marcion Book III and An Answer to the Jews, both c. 198–212) · Wikipedia
Summary: Tertullian, the earliest Christian witness on this page, reads the "unicorn" of Scripture not as a natural creature to be identified but as a christological figure of the Cross. Twice — in Against Marcion III.18 and, in nearly identical words, in An Answer to the Jews 10 — he takes the blessing "his horns are the horns of a unicorn" (Deut 33:17) and maps the beast onto the shape of the cross: its two "horns" are the arms of the yard, its single upright body the mast, so that Christ "in this manner horned" pushes the nations. He rejects the wooden zoological readings outright ("no one-horned rhinoceros… nor any two-horned minotaur") in favour of the type, and joins the same figure to the "horns of the unicorns" of Psalm 22:21. He heads the christological-allegory strand of unicorn interpretation later carried by the Physiologus and the medieval bestiary.

Claims & evidence

The unicorn's horns are the extremities of the Cross (Deut 33:17)

Joseph likewise was a type of Christ… when he is blessed by his father in these words: "His glory is that of a bullock; his horns are the horns of a unicorn; with them shall he push the nations to the very ends of the earth," —he was not, of course, designated as a mere unicorn with its one horn, or a minotaur with two; but Christ was indicated in him—a bullock in respect of both His characteristics: to some as severe as a Judge, to others gentle as a Saviour, whose horns were the extremities of His cross. For of the antenna, which is a part of a cross, the ends are called horns; while the midway stake of the whole frame is the unicorn. By this virtue, then, of His cross, and in this manner "horned," He is both now pushing all nations through faith, bearing them away from earth to heaven; and will then push them through judgment, casting them down from heaven to earth.

— Against Marcion III.18 ("Types of the Death of Christ"), trans. Peter Holmes, ANF vol. III; Tertullian, Against Marcion, Book III; the blessing quoted is Deut 33:17. Tertullian does not read the "unicorn" as an animal at all: pressing his case against Marcion that the Old Testament everywhere prefigures the Cross, he treats the one-horned beast of the versions as a picture of the crucified Christ — the antenna (the transverse yard-arm of a ship's mast, the standard ancient analogy for the cross-beam) supplying the two "horns," the upright mast the single "unicorn." He is emphatic that neither a literal rhinoceros nor a mythical minotaur is meant; the point is the type, the beast's horns become the arms by which Christ "pushes" the nations, first in faith and then in judgment.

The same figure, restated against the Jews (Deut 33:17; Ps 22:21)

For Joseph is withal blest by his father after this form: "His glory (is that) of a bull; his horns, the horns of an unicorn; on them shall he toss nations alike unto the very extremity of the earth." Of course no one-horned rhinoceros was there pointed to, nor any two-horned minotaur. But Christ was therein signified: "bull," by reason of each of His two characters,—to some fierce, as Judge; to others gentle, as Saviour; whose "horns" were to be the extremities of the cross. For even in a ship's yard—which is part of a cross—this is the name by which the extremities are called; while the central pole of the mast is a "unicorn."

— An Answer to the Jews 10 ("Concerning the Passion of Christ"), trans. S. Thelwall, ANF vol. III; Tertullian, An Answer to the Jews. The two treatises share this material almost verbatim (chapters 9–14 of An Answer to the Jews run parallel to Against Marcion III). Here as there, Tertullian folds a second unicorn text into the same cross-typology: expounding the twenty-first Psalm (Ps 22 in modern numbering) as "the whole passion of Christ," he glosses "Save me… from the horn of the unicorns my humility" as meaning "from the ends… of the cross, as we have above shown," and observes that "neither David himself… nor any of the kings of the Jews" suffered such a cross — only Christ.
— An Answer to the Jews 10, on Ps 22:21 (cf. the parallel at Against Marcion III.18).

Notes

  • Earliest Christian unicorn-reading — and a christological, not zoological, one. Where the later Fathers on this page dispute whether Scripture's great beasts are real animals or figures, Tertullian, a century before them, simply bypasses the question for the unicorn: the beast is a type, its horns the arms of the Cross. He thus heads the christological-allegory strand that the re'em / "unicorn" argument tracks — the reading later developed in the Physiologus and the medieval bestiary, where the one-horned beast becomes Christ, captured in a virgin's lap.
  • Both horns and one horn at once. Tertullian is notably unbothered by the tension a strict single-horn reading creates. Deuteronomy 33:17 gives the beast plural "horns," yet calls it a "unicorn" (monokeros in the Greek his Latin followed); Tertullian turns the seam to advantage — the two horns are the cross-beam's ends, the "unicorn" the single upright — rather than resolving it. This makes him an oblique witness to the point the unicorn argument presses: the "unicorn" is a word of the versions (Deut 33:17's own grammar requires more than one horn), and given that word, interpreters built allegory on it.
  • Whose blessing? Tertullian ascribes the words to Joseph being "blest by his father." The wording is in fact from Deuteronomy 33:17 — Moses' blessing on the tribe of Joseph — not from Jacob's blessing in Genesis 49 (which lacks the horns-of-a-unicorn phrase); he reads the Mosaic blessing as a patriarchal one on Joseph himself.
