Marco Polo on dragons and mythical beasts

Dates: 1254–1324 · Travels dictated c. 1298 · Wikipedia
Summary: The Venetian merchant, in the account of his eastern journeys dictated c. 1298, reports as plain natural history that the province of Carajan (Yunnan) is home to enormous clawed "serpents" that can swallow a man whole — a first-person-style traveler's "dragon report" of the kind that filled the medieval imagination. He offers no allegory and reads no Scripture; he describes a beast. Later editors, from Henry Yule onward, identify the animal as the crocodile, and Polo's account stands as a worked example of how a real, alarming reptile hardened into a "great serpent" in a pre-modern report.

Claims & evidence

Carajan is home to enormous clawed serpents that can swallow a man whole

In this province are found snakes and great serpents of such vast size as to strike fear into those who see them, and so hideous that the very account of them must excite the wonder of those to hear it. I will tell you how long and big they are. You may be assured that some of them are ten paces in length; some are more and some less. And in bulk they are equal to a great cask, for the bigger ones are about ten palms in girth. They have two forelegs near the head, but for foot nothing but a claw like the claw of a hawk or that of a lion. The head is very big, and the eyes are bigger than a great loaf of bread. The mouth is large enough to swallow a man whole, and is garnished with great [pointed] teeth.

— The Book of Ser Marco Polo II.xlix ("Concerning a further part of the Province of Carajan"), trans. Henry Yule, rev. Henri Cordier (3rd ed., 1903), vol. II (Project Gutenberg); also the complete Yule–Cordier edition, vol. II (archive.org). Carajan is Polo's name for Yunnan, in the far south-west of the Mongol empire; he passed through it on the overland journeys he served Kublai Khan. The description is offered flatly, as a marvel of the region's fauna — no gloss, no moral, no Scripture — the report of a merchant-observer cataloguing what a distant province holds.

They lair in caverns by day and are hunted by night, their gall prized as medicine

Polo continues that the beasts spend the day in caverns to escape the heat and come out only at night to feed, devouring any animal they can seize; the hunters study the tracks the serpents habitually take down to the water and bury sharpened iron-spiked stakes in the sand of those paths, so that the beast, dragging its great weight along, is gashed and killed. The gall is then carefully secured, "for it is used as a medicine," given in wine against the bite of a mad dog, administered to women in hard labour, and applied to boils and eruptions; and the flesh is sold dear as a delicacy (Travels II.xlix, Yule–Cordier, vol. II). The circumstantial, practical detail — the caverns, the buried spikes, the pharmacy of the gall — is what marks the passage as a natural-history report rather than a fable: Polo is relaying (whether from sight or from local informants) how a real and dangerous animal was actually taken and used.

Yule's identification: the "great serpents" are crocodiles

It cannot be doubted that Marco's serpents here are crocodiles, in spite of his strange mistakes about their having only two feet and one claw on each, and his imperfect knowledge of their aquatic habits. He may have seen only a mutilated specimen.

— Henry Yule, editorial note to II.xlix, Yule–Cordier, vol. II (Project Gutenberg). This is the standard reading, and a compelling one: the wide man-swallowing jaws, the great teeth, the prominent eye-orbits ("bigger than a great loaf"), the day-lairing and night-feeding, and the taking of the beast along its water-tracks all fit a large crocodilian. Polo's errors — two legs rather than four, a single hawk-like claw, "ten paces" (about thirty feet) of length — are the ordinary distortions of a second-hand or half-glimpsed report, the very process by which an ordinary reptile becomes a "great serpent." The account thus belongs to this page not as evidence of an unknown monster but as a specimen of the mechanism behind the medieval dragon-report: a real animal, imperfectly seen and vividly retold.

Notes

  • Where he sits on the timeline. Polo is the latest medieval witness on this page and its one high-medieval traveler (only the early-modern Jesuit Athanasius Kircher, in 1665, comes later). Like Sozomen's dragon of Chamaegephyrae, his is a this-worldly sighting reported as fact rather than a passage of Scripture to be interpreted; he stands with the literal-animal / legend strand — Augustine, Julian, John of Damascus — but supplies the merchant's-eye, secular register that Herodotus and Ammianus Marcellinus supply among the ancients. His "great serpents" are the medieval bestiary's raw material: a real beast at the core of a fabulous description.
  • Translations differ. Yule–Cordier's "two forelegs… but for foot nothing but a claw like the claw of a hawk or that of a lion" reads, in William Marsden's earlier rendering, "two short legs, having three claws like those of a tiger" — a divergence that itself shows the instability of the report as it passed through manuscripts and translators. The substance is constant: a huge, clawed, wide-jawed reptile of the southern rivers.
  • Modern afterlife. The passage is sometimes enlisted in young-earth or "living-dinosaur" literature as a genuine sighting of a non-crocodilian monster; that use runs against the plain fit with a crocodile that Yule and later editors record, and against Polo's own framing of the beast as one animal among a province's fauna.
