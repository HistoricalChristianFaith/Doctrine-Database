Ammianus Marcellinus on the winged snakes of Arabia

Dates: c. 330 – c. 400 (the Res Gestae completed c. 391; the Egyptian excursus of Book 22 written in the 380s) · Wikipedia
Summary: A pagan Roman soldier-historian, and the last great writer of classical Latin history — a contemporary of the Church Fathers who read Job's beasts, but working entirely outside their frame. In the Egyptian digression of his Res Gestae he repeats, as sober natural history, the ancient report that the sacred ibis destroys flocks of winged snakes that come laden with poison from the marshes of Arabia. He carries Herodotus's flying serpent down into the fourth century as an established fact of the Egyptian countryside.

Claims & evidence

The ibis is honoured as a serpent-killer

Among the birds of Egypt, the variety of which is countless, is the ibis, a sacred and amiable bird, also valuable, because by heaping up the eggs of serpents in its nest for food it causes these fatal pests to diminish.

— Ammianus Marcellinus, Res Gestae 22.15.25, trans. C. D. Yonge, tertullian.org. Ammianus sets the ibis among the ordinary fauna of Egypt and explains its sanctity functionally: the bird is revered because it keeps down the serpents, gathering their eggs for food. This is the same rationale Herodotus gave for the Egyptians' reverence — the ibis as the country's living defence against snakes.

The ibis destroys flocks of winged snakes flying poison-laden from Arabia

They also sometimes encounter flocks of winged snakes, which come laden with poison from the marshes of Arabia. These, before they can quit their own region, they overcome in the air, and then devour them.

— Ammianus Marcellinus, Res Gestae 22.15.26, trans. C. D. Yonge, tertullian.org. This is Herodotus's account (Histories 2.75; 3.107–109) carried into late antiquity: the winged snakes that fly from Arabia toward Egypt, and the ibis that meets and destroys them before they can enter. Ammianus reports it flatly, as the kind of regional natural-history detail his Egyptian excursus is full of — not as a wonder to be doubted, and not as a scriptural figure to be interpreted.

Notes

  • A pagan witness among the Fathers. Ammianus (c. 330–400) wrote within the same decades as Ephrem, Augustine, and Julian of Eclanum, all of whom were weighing Job's behemoth and leviathan as literal creature or allegory. He shares none of that project: an old-religion Roman historian, he is simply recording, in an ethnographic digression, what was said of Egypt's wildlife. His value here is precisely that independence — the flying serpent turns up as plain fact in a secular history, not as anyone's exegesis.
  • The classical chain. The winged/flying serpents run from Herodotus (c. 440 BC) through Strabo, Diodorus, Cicero, and Pliny, and reach Ammianus here in the late fourth century AD — an eight-hundred-year continuity of the same report. Where John of Damascus would later relay the Regulus/Bagradas serpent from Cassius Dio as natural history, Ammianus does the same for the Arabian winged snakes, and does it as a pagan.
  • The scriptural tie. As with Herodotus, these winged serpents of Arabia sit near Scripture's "fiery flying serpent" (saraph meʻophep, Isa 14:29; 30:6) — a possible convergence of two independent memories, weighed on its own terms and not settled on this page.
