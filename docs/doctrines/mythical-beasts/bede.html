Bede on the dragon among the devil's names (Revelation 20:2)

Dates: c. 673–735; the Commentary on Revelation (Explanatio Apocalypsis) is one of Bede's earliest works, composed c. 703–709 · Wikipedia
Summary: The Venerable Bede, expounding Revelation 20:2 — "And he laid hold on the dragon, that old serpent, which is the Devil, and Satan" — reads the four titles the verse heaps on the enemy as a set of etymologies, each name naming a distinct vice. "Dragon" is not a separate creature but one of the devil's names, given "on account of the malice of harming." Bede's is a purely allegorical reading in the line of Gregory the Great, whose Moralia he knew intimately; where Gregory unfolds Behemoth-and-Leviathan-as-devil over three books, Bede compresses the dragon-as-devil equation into a single glossarial sentence — a small witness to how thoroughly the figurative reading had become the West's default by the eighth century.

Claims & evidence

"Dragon" is one of the devil's names, given for his malice in harming (Revelation 20:2)

And he seized the dragon, that ancient serpent, etc. The devil is interpreted as flowing downward. In Greek, he is called the accuser. Satan means adversary or transgressor. He is called a dragon because of the malice of harming; a serpent because of the cunning of deceiving; the devil because of his fall from his status; and Satan because of his obstinacy in opposing the Lord.

— Commentary on Revelation (Explanatio Apocalypsis) on Revelation 20:2, "And he laid hold on the dragon, that old serpent, which is the Devil, and Satan"; Bede, Commentary on Revelation, Revelation 20:2. Bede takes the four titles John piles on the enemy at the binding — dragon, serpent, devil, Satan — and reads each as an etymology exposing a facet of his character: the dragon harms (malice), the serpent deceives (cunning), the devil (diabolus, "flowing downward," and in Greek "the accuser") names his fall from estate, and Satan ("the adversary" or "transgressor") his obstinate opposition to God. On this reading the "dragon" of Revelation is not a beast at all but a name for the one enemy, glossed alongside his other names; the whole picture is pressed straight through to the person of the devil, with no interest in whether a dragon is a real animal.

Notes

  • In Gregory's line, in miniature. Bede's dragon-as-devil equation is the same reading Gregory the Great had given at full length in the Morals on the Book of Job — a work Bede knew and drew on throughout his exegesis. Where Gregory unfolds Behemoth and Leviathan as "the ancient enemy" over Books XXXII–XXXIV, Bede reduces the equation to a one-line gloss on a list of names. He belongs to the pure-allegory strand (with Athanasius, Basil, and Gregory), not to the literal-animal reading that his near-contemporary John of Damascus was defending in the East at almost the same moment.
  • Names as etymologies. The device — distributing a chain of scriptural titles across a matching chain of vices — is a patristic commonplace (the diabolus/"accuser" and Satan/"adversary" etymologies are as old as Origen and Jerome). Bede's contribution here is simply to fold "dragon" into that list, so that the beast-name is read as one more title of the devil, defined by "the malice of harming."
  • Transmission. The quotation is from Bede's Explanatio Apocalypsis, his verse-by-verse commentary on Revelation and among the earliest of his works (c. 703–709), in the English translation carried at historicalchristian.faith. The comment falls at Revelation 20:2, in Bede's exposition of the binding of the dragon "for a thousand years."
