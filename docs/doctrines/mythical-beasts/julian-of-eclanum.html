Julian of Eclanum on Behemoth & Leviathan

Dates: c. 386 – c. 455 (Exposition on the Book of Job, written in exile, c. 420s) · Wikipedia
Summary: Julian of Eclanum reads the great beasts of Job 40–41 first and foremost as real, terrifying creatures whose very existence edifies — a compact theodicy: God's power in fashioning even beasts that frighten us, Providence's goodness in banishing the deadly ones from human haunts into the wilderness, and His severity against vice. He is emphatic that this literal picture is "the true description of the beast according to the faithful understanding of the story," yet he lets a second, allegorical layer stand alongside it for "a plane of higher intellect" — the monsters as an image of "the strength of spiritual iniquity," carrying the sin of pride. He thus belongs with the both/and readers, but tilts the balance toward the literal creature more decidedly than Ephrem or Augustine.

Claims & evidence

Literal reading — the terrifying beast edifies: God's power, Providence's goodness, His severity against vice (Job 40:15)

Through the creation of such a hateful and tremendous beast people are given three opportunities of edification. They can recognize that the power of the Creator did not only make those beasts that would have served human beings but also fashioned those who frighten them; they can understand the goodness of Providence, because it removed those beasts that would have been deadly from the midst [of humans] and placed them in the wilderness. There they can learn how severe he is against vices. These [beasts] that are troublesome to mortals according to their size and strength are also subject to his regulation.

— Exposition on the Book of Job, on Job 40:15 (Behold now behemoth, which I made with thee), trans. in the Ancient Christian Commentary on Scripture (Old Testament VI: Job, ed. Manlio Simonetti & Marco Conti, 2006); Job 40:15. Julian takes Behemoth as an actual creature and asks what a good God gains by making so fearsome a thing. His answer is a small theodicy in three movements: the Creator's power is shown in fashioning beasts that terrify and not only beasts that serve; His Providence is shown in mercy, driving the deadly kinds out of human haunts and into the wilderness; and His severity against vice is shown in beasts kept under His regulation despite their size and strength. The dread the animal inspires is not a flaw in creation but a lesson — the literal creature made to instruct.

Literal reading affirmed as primary — and an allegorical layer allowed for "higher intellect" (Job 41:34)

This is the true description of the beast according to the faithful [understanding] of the story. There must be no doubt about their great size because of the power of the Creator. However, [these words] are also addressed to a plane of higher intellect, so that we may believe through them that something else is signified as well, and therefore the strength of spiritual iniquity may be perceived more easily in the image of the bodies. As these [beasts] are deadly for the body, so they are for human morality, and as the sin of pride must be imputed to them, so it must be punished.

— Exposition on the Book of Job, on Job 41:34 (he is a king over all the children of pride), trans. in the Ancient Christian Commentary on Scripture (OT VI: Job, 2006); Job 41:34. Here Julian states his order of readings outright: the literal one is "the true description of the beast according to the faithful understanding of the story," and the huge size is to be believed, not explained away, "because of the power of the Creator." Only then, and without displacing that literal sense, does he grant a second reading "to a plane of higher intellect" — the beast as an image in which "the strength of spiritual iniquity" is more easily perceived, deadly to human morality as the animal is deadly to the body, and rightly charged with "the sin of pride." The figure rides on the fact; the creature is not dissolved into the symbol.

Notes

  • Both readings, literal first. Julian holds the same both/and as Ephrem and Augustine — a real animal and a figure of evil — but weights it differently. Where Augustine sorts the two senses by which verse he is on (literal on Ps 148:7, allegorical on Ps 74:14), Julian layers them on the same Job text: the creature is affirmed as the "true description" and the moral figure is added atop it for readers of "higher intellect." His centre of gravity is the literal, edifying beast, closer on this axis to John of Damascus than to the pure allegorists.
  • A theodicy of the fearsome creature. Julian's distinctive contribution is to treat the beast's very dreadfulness as a problem worth solving and to answer it from the creature's literal existence: even the animals that frighten us testify to the Creator's power, His providential mercy in confining them, and His governance of all strength. This is a different use of the literal reading from Ephrem's bare habitat-gloss — not classification but a lesson drawn from the terror the animal inspires.
  • The author. Julian, bishop of Eclanum, was Augustine's most formidable opponent in the Pelagian controversy and was deposed and exiled; his exegetical works, including this Exposition on Job, belong to that period of exile. The commentary is cited here for its reading of Job's beasts, with no bearing on the doctrinal quarrel that otherwise sets him against Augustine, whose treatment of the same beasts stands a few years earlier on this page.
  • Transmission. Julian's Exposition on the Book of Job is thinly preserved (Latin text in CCSL 88); the passages here are gathered and translated in the Ancient Christian Commentary on Scripture's Job volume, the same collection that prints the Ephrem Job material.
  • Cross-links. Compare Augustine (the both/and stated as a principle), Ephrem (the literal reading as bare zoology), and Gregory the Great and Bede (the beasts read as pure allegory of the devil), against whom Julian's insistence on "the true description of the beast" is a pointed foil.
