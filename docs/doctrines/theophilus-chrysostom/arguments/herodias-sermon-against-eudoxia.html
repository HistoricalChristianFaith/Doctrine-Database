Did Chrysostom preach the “Again Herodias raves” sermon against the empress Eudoxia?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — the sermon that carries the line is not his, the two historians who report it are not independent, and the contemporary source knows only a differently-worded allegation · reported by Socrates Scholasticus and Sozomen

Related arguments: the case against Chrysostom — see also whether Chrysostom was deposed for Origenism

The claim

When John became aware of this, he delivered in the church that celebrated oration commencing with these words: ‘Again Herodias raves; again she is troubled; she dances again; and again desires to receive John's head in a charger.’ This, of course, exasperated the empress still more.

— Socrates, Church History VI.18, link. The assertion is that John Chrysostom, provoked by the empress Eudoxia's move to convene a second council against him, preached a sermon casting her as Herodias and himself as the Baptist — the single most-quoted moment of the whole affair, and the source of the popular picture of a fearless preacher denouncing a vindictive empress to her face.1

Who advances it

Socrates Scholasticus, writing at Constantinople about 439–443, is the source. Sozomen repeats it a few years later in almost the same words — “Herodias is again enraged; again she dances; again she seeks to have the head of John in a basin” — and from those two it passes into every later telling, ancient and modern. A homily beginning with exactly this sentence circulates among Chrysostom's works, which has generally been taken to confirm it.2

The evidence for

Two historians, close in time and place. Socrates wrote in the capital within about thirty-five years of the events, among people who had lived through them and in a city where the “Johannite” party was still a living memory; Sozomen, writing there shortly after, gives the same episode. Neither is hostile to Chrysostom, so the report is not obviously an enemy's slander.3

The setting is not in doubt. That Chrysostom denounced the games held at the dedication of Eudoxia's silver statue, that the empress took it as aimed at her, and that she moved for a second council in consequence, is common ground between Socrates, Sozomen, and the reference tradition; the earlier sermon “against the vices to which females are peculiarly prone” is likewise attested, and was likewise read as aimed at her. A man who did those two things is a man who could plausibly have said the third.4

An extant homily carries the line. The sermon In decollationem sancti Iohannis opens with precisely these words, and is printed among Chrysostom's works.5

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The provocation itself is secure, and no part of this page disputes it. Chrysostom did attack the statue's games from the pulpit; Eudoxia did take the attacks personally; the second council followed. Even the most cautious reading leaves a bishop whose preaching the palace read as a public insult — and Sozomen, who reports the earlier sermon, records his own doubt only about whether the allusion was intended, not about whether it was made.

The homily is not his. In decollationem sancti Iohannis is classed among the pseudo-Chrysostomic works — catalogued in the Clavis Patrum Graecorum in the block reserved for the dubia et spuria, and rejected as spurious in the older critical tradition by Tillemont, Savile, and Montfaucon alike, the last two being the very editors who printed it. So no securely authentic sermon containing the line survives; what survives is a later composition that opens with it.6 The homily therefore cannot corroborate the historians — and the direction of dependence may well run the other way, a preacher supplying the sermon the histories said had been preached.

The two witnesses are one witness. Sozomen is not independent of Socrates here. That he worked from Socrates as his guide is the standard judgment on his sources, and at this very chapter the editors identify his authorities as Socrates VI.18 together with Palladius. Two attestations that reduce to one leave the line resting on a single writer some thirty-five to forty years after.7

The contemporary source has a different word, and no sermon. Palladius wrote his defence about 408, within a year of Chrysostom's death, as an insider who had been deposed at the Oak himself — and he is the one writer with an overwhelming motive to record so vivid a scene, since the treason charge turned on what Chrysostom had called the empress. He reports no Herodias sermon at all. What he reports is an allegation, and a different one: “The treason consisted in offensive language against the queen, whom, as they alleged, he had called Jezebel.” Note the shape of it — not a sermon Palladius quotes, but a charge he attributes to the prosecution and does not concede.8

The improbability of survival cuts both ways. A bishop who publicly compared a reigning Augusta to Herodias in the great church of the capital was inviting a capital charge, not a synodical one — and the synod that wanted him convicted of treason for insulting her did not, on the record we have, produce the sermon. It rested the charge on an allegation about a word.

Where the modern reading lands. Because the homily is spurious, recent scholarship treats the episode as very possibly a later dramatization rather than a report — the argument being that the whole Chrysostom-versus-Eudoxia confrontation was sharpened in Johannite circles after 407, when the empress, dead within months of the banishment, made an obvious and satisfying villain. That is a hypothesis about the story's growth, not a demonstration; but it does not need to be proved for this argument to fail. The argument fails on its own supports: the corroborating sermon is not Chrysostom's, and the second historian is the first historian.9

Assessment

weak. That Chrysostom provoked Eudoxia from the pulpit, and was destroyed partly for it, is secure and is not what is rated here. That he preached this sermon, with these words, against her, does not hold on its best available case: the homily that carries the line is not reckoned his, the two historians who report it reduce to one, and the contemporary best placed to know it — who needed exactly such a scene for his defence — knows only that his opponents alleged a different insult altogether. The honest statement is that Chrysostom said something from the pulpit that the palace took as an attack on the empress, and that the celebrated form of the words is attested a generation later and cannot be traced to him. The line should not be quoted as his without that qualification.
