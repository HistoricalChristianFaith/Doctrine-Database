Was John Chrysostom deposed for Origenism?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — Origen was the lever, not the grievance; the charge was raised by a man who held the doctrine himself, dropped once the synod convened, and abandoned once the objective was met · advanced by Theophilus of Alexandria

Related arguments: the case against Chrysostom — see also the “Herodias” sermon against Eudoxia

The claim

But, not to speak of his other misdeeds, he has taken the Origenists into his confidence, has advanced many of them to the priesthood, and by committing this crime has saddened with no slight grief that man of God, Epiphanius of blessed memory.

— Theophilus of Alexandria to Jerome, in Jerome, Letter 113 (a.d. 405), link. Theophilus is here sending Jerome, for translation into Latin, his invective against the deposed bishop of Constantinople; the sentence states the ground of the whole case. The interpretation it serves is that the fall of John Chrysostom belongs to the history of doctrine — that it was the Eastern climax of the Origenist controversy, in which a bishop who protected heretics was deposed for protecting them.1

Who advances it

Theophilus is the primary and interested proponent, and Jerome carried the reading into Latin for the West; that is why the affair enters the Western record at all, and why it is filed to this day under the “first Origenist crisis.” The framing survives in every account that treats Chrysostom's fall as a chapter of the Origen quarrel rather than of Constantinopolitan politics — and it is not a purely partisan framing: two of the forty-seven articles laid before the synod of the Oak do concern Origenism, and Bishop Isaac's memorial names the Origenists by name — “Ammonius, Euthymius, Eusebius, Heraclides, and Palladius” — as the men Chrysostom is blamed for consorting with.2

The evidence for

The strongest version of the case does not rest on Theophilus's honesty. It runs: whatever his motives, the events really were doctrinal in their content and their consequences.

The chain of causation is doctrinal at every link. Origen's books were condemned by synods at Cyprus and Alexandria; monks were expelled from Nitria as Origenists; those monks fled to Constantinople; Chrysostom sheltered them; and it was that shelter that brought Theophilus to the capital. Remove Origen from the story and there is no story: nothing else brought an Alexandrian patriarch across the sea with twenty-nine suffragans.3

The charge was not empty noise. Association with named Origenists was a real liability in the capital in 403, and it did material damage — it is what Epiphanius came to press, what he demanded Chrysostom disavow, and what he denounced publicly when Chrysostom would not. A bishop who declined to subscribe a condemnation and continued to house the condemned had put himself, by the standards of the moment, in an indefensible position, whoever exploited it.4

The doctrinal content was real. The Tall Brothers and their circle really did hold the Evagrian-Origenist positions that the Egyptian monastic majority rejected; the anthropomorphite dispute was a genuine theological division, not a fabrication. The wiki does not treat a doctrinal quarrel as unreal merely because ambitious men fought it.5

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The occasion was doctrinal, and the doctrinal dispute underneath it was genuine. The Origenism charge was a live and damaging accusation in 403, not a transparent fiction that nobody could have believed; that is why it worked. Any account that reduces the affair to naked ambition has to explain why this accusation was the one available, and the answer is that the Origenist question was really being fought at the same moment across the whole Greek and Latin church.

What the argument cannot survive. Four things, each independently damaging and all attested in sources that are not Chrysostom's partisans.

The accuser held the doctrine he prosecuted. Theophilus preached the incorporeality of God in church and in his paschal letter, was nearly killed for it by the anthropomorphite monks, and bought his escape by promising to anathematize Origen. Socrates says his own view never changed and that he knowingly misrepresented it: “entertaining the very same opinion respecting the Divine nature, yet to gratify his vindictive feelings, [he] did not hesitate to pervert what he and they had rightly taught.”6

The charge disappeared at the moment of trial. This is the decisive fact. Sozomen records that once the synod convened, Theophilus “judged it expedient to make no further allusion to the works of Origen”; Socrates is blunter: “for no mention was now made of Origen, but all were intent on urging a variety of criminations, many of which were ridiculous.” Of forty-seven articles, two touch Origenism, and the sentence rested on none of them — the synod deposed him for not appearing, and referred to the emperor the one charge it said it could not try, which was treason for words about the empress.7 A prosecution that drops its own indictment on the first day of the hearing was not brought for the indictment.

The heresy stopped mattering the moment the objective was met. Immediately after the deposition Theophilus communicated with Dioscorus and the Tall Brothers — the very men he had driven from Nitria as Origenists — which is how, Socrates says, the city worked out what had been going on. He then made his peace with the monks of Scetis, wept at the news of Ammonius's death, and went back to reading Origen, answering objections with the line about a meadow of flowers from which one culls the beautiful and steps over the thorny.8

A prior grievance is attested, and it is not doctrinal. Socrates dates the plot to the day of Chrysostom's consecration and names the cause — the lost election and the passed-over Isidore — which is two years before Origen enters the story at all. And the quarrel with the Tall Brothers, on the hostile telling and the friendly alike, began over Isidore and money: when Theophilus seized Ammonius by the neck shouting “Anathematize Origen, you heretic!”, “the only point at issue was the petition on behalf of Isidore.”9

Where the modern reading lands. The standard study does not treat “Origenism” in these years as either a straightforward doctrinal charge or an empty one: Elizabeth Clark argues that the word functioned as a code in which non-theological complaints — patronage, rivalry, alliance, the standing of monastic networks — could be pressed as heresy, and that the content of the charge was reconstructed at each stage to suit the target. On that reading Chrysostom was drawn into the Origenist orbit only because the routed monks fled to his city, and his mere toleration of them supplied the opening. That is neither “it was really about doctrine” nor “it was pure ambition”; it is the observation that in 403 a doctrinal accusation was the usable form for a grievance.10

Assessment

weak. That Chrysostom was deposed for Origenism does not hold on its best available case. Origenism supplied the occasion, the vocabulary, and the pretext for summoning a synod; it did not supply the grievance, the indictment, or the sentence. The accuser held the accused doctrine, dropped it at the hearing, restored the accused Origenists to communion within days of winning, and returned openly to reading Origen. The sentence rested on non-appearance and a treason referral. What should be kept from the argument is that the doctrinal dispute underneath was real and the accusation was damaging — a lever has to be made of something solid to bear weight. What should not be kept is the conclusion that the affair belongs to the history of doctrine rather than to the history of an episcopal quarrel. The rating is of the interpretation, not of the sincerity of any later writer who repeats it: many have taken Theophilus's framing at face value because it is the framing the surviving Latin documents were written to carry.
