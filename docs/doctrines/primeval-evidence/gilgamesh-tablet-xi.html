Gilgamesh Tablet XI on the Flood and the Nephilim in the material record

Dates: c. 1200–1000 BC (the Standard Babylonian text); surviving Nineveh copies 7th c. BC · dating method: two different ones, and the gap between them matters. The copies are dated archaeologically and epigraphically — tablets from Ashurbanipal's libraries at Nineveh, sealed by the sack of 612 BC, in dated Neo-Assyrian script. The recension is dated by literary-historical attribution: the Babylonian catalogue tradition ascribes it to Sîn-lēqi-unninni, and philologists place him late in the second millennium. That second figure is an inference about authorship, not a measurement, and it is the loosest date on this shelf's Mesopotamian texts · Wikipedia
Summary: Tablet XI of the Standard Babylonian Gilgamesh is the closest extra-biblical parallel to Genesis 6–9 that exists. Uta-napišti tells Gilgamesh how the gods resolved on a Deluge, how he was warned to demolish his house and build a boat, how he loaded aboard "the seed of all living creatures," how the storm ran six days and seven nights, how the boat grounded on Mount Nimuš, how he sent out a dove, a swallow, and a raven, and how he sacrificed on the mountain and the gods "gathered like flies" round the offering. Its evidential weight on this page is the sharpness of the parallel — and the fact that this, the closest version to Genesis, is also the latest of the three Mesopotamian flood texts, and demonstrably a literary borrowing from Atrahasis.

A note on where this sits. The timeline is ordered by the age of the evidence. Tablet XI comes last of the three not because its flood is younger but because its text is: it is the end of a chain of copying, not the head of one.

Claims & evidence

A late text in a long chain: the Standard Babylonian version, c. 1200–1000 BC

Eleven pieces of the Old Babylonian versions survive and eighteen more from later in the second millennium; the flood tablet itself is known from 184 first-millennium fragments, chiefly from the Assyrian libraries at Nineveh and later Babylonian collections. The Standard Babylonian text these attest is a deliberate editorial recension, attributed in Babylonian tradition to the scholar Sîn-lēqi-unninni of Uruk, who is placed towards the end of the second millennium BC.

— A. R. George, The Babylonian Gilgamesh Epic: Introduction, Critical Edition and Cuneiform Texts, 2 vols. (Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2003), vol. 1, open-access PDF. George also sets out the case that the flood story was inserted into the epic by that editor, taken over from Atrahasis; the seam is visible in the text itself, which at line 49 has the crowd assembling "at Atar-hasis's gate" — the other poem's hero-name left standing in a narrative whose survivor is otherwise called Uta-napišti.

The warning, the boat, and "the seed of all living creatures"

"Reed fence, brick wall! Reed fence, brick wall! Listen, O reed fence! Pay heed, O brick wall! O man of Shuruppak, son of Ubara-tutu, demolish the house, build a boat! Abandon riches and seek survival! Spurn property and save life! Put on board the boat the seed of all living creatures! The boat that you are going to build, her dimensions should all be matching: her breadth and length should be the same, cover her with a roof, like the Apsû."

— Gilgamesh XI.21–31, in A. R. George's translation, hosted with his permission at SOAS (BAPLAR), Tablet XI lines 1–163 (Akkadian transcription alongside). Every clause of the warning is Atrahasis III.i.20–31 recast — the reed wall, the demolished house, the spurned property, the Apsû-roof. What Gilgamesh adds is the phrase Genesis readers notice most: zēr napšāti kalāma, "the seed of all living creatures," loaded aboard.

The vessel is a cube, not the oblong ark of Genesis

"On the fifth day I set in place her (outer) surface: one 'acre' was her area, ten rods each her sides stood high, at ten rods each, the edges of her top were equal. I set in place her body, I drew up her design: I gave her six decks, I divided her into seven parts, I divided her interior into nine."

"I loaded aboard it whatever seed I had of living things, each and every one. All my kith and kin I sent aboard the boat, I sent aboard animals of the wild, creatures of the wild, persons of every skill and craft."

— Gilgamesh XI.57–63 and 84–86, George's translation. The difference is as instructive as the resemblance. Uta-napišti's boat is an equal-sided cube of six decks; Noah's is a 300×50×30-cubit oblong of three (Gen 6:15–16). And Uta-napišti's passenger list is a whole craftsman class as well as his family — against Genesis' insistently exact eight souls. Anyone arguing that the two texts report one event has to carry both the agreements and these disagreements.

Six days of deluge, the grounding on Mount Nimuš, the birds, the sacrifice

"For six days and seven nights, there blew the wind and the Deluge, the gale [and flattened the land]. When the seventh day arrived, the gale relented… the tempest grew still, the Deluge ended."

"On Mount Nimuš the boat ran aground, Mount Nimuš held the boat fast and did not let it move… When the seventh day arrived I brought out a dove, setting it free. Off went the dove… No perch was available for it, so it came back to [me]. I brought out a swallow, setting it free… No perch was available for [it], so it came back to me. I brought out a raven, setting it free. Off went the raven and it saw the waters receding. It was eating, bobbing up and down, it did not come back to me."

"I brought out an offering and sacrificed to the four corners of the earth, I strewed incense on the peak of the mountain… The gods smelled the savour, the gods smelled the sweet savour, the gods gathered like flies round the sacrificer."

— Gilgamesh XI.128–133, 142–156, and 157–163, George's translation. Genesis 8:4–12 has the same three beats in the same order — grounding on a mountain, birds released to test the waters, sacrifice on landing — with the birds reordered (raven first, then dove three times) and the mountain named Ararat rather than Nimuš. The closing line is the sharpest theological divergence in the whole comparison: Genesis 8:21 keeps the "pleasing aroma" and drops the swarm of hungry gods, because in the Mesopotamian version the flood has starved the gods of their offerings, and in Genesis there is nothing to starve.

The tablet's modern rediscovery: George Smith, 3 December 1872

On looking down the third column, my eye caught the statement that the ship rested on the mountains of Nizir, followed by the account of the sending forth of the dove, and its finding no resting-place and returning. I saw at once that I had here discovered a portion at least of the Chaldean account of the Deluge.

— George Smith, The Chaldean Account of Genesis (London, 1876), pp. 4–5. Smith read the tablet in the British Museum's Nineveh collection and announced it in a lecture to the Society of Biblical Archaeology on 3 December 1872 — in his own words, "my lecture on the Chaldean Account of the Deluge, which I delivered on Dec. 3, 1872." The date matters for this timeline in a different way from the others: it is the moment the flood-parallel question became a live one for Christian readers, and every argument on this page about corroboration versus common descent begins there.

Notes

  • "Mount Nimuš" is the reading now preferred for the name Smith transcribed as Nizir; it is generally identified with Pir Omar Gudrun in Iraqi Kurdistan — far from the Ararat range of Genesis 8:4.
  • The flood episode's status as an insertion is the single most important fact about this tablet for evidential purposes: it means the three Mesopotamian witnesses are not three independent reports but one tradition copied forward, which is exactly what the corroboration arguments must test.
  • Because the recension's date rests on an attribution rather than a colophon, this tablet is placed on the shelf at its softest end. Nothing important on this page turns on it: the Nineveh copies are firmly dated by their find-context, and the content is in any case older still, taken over from Atrahasis, whose copy is dated.
