Berossus on the Flood and the Nephilim in the material record

Dates: c. 281 BC (the Babyloniaca; no manuscript survives, and the text reaches us only through later epitomes) · dating method: historical, from the dedication — the work was written for Antiochus I, whose reign supplies the date; there is no manuscript to date by any physical or palaeographic means, and what we possess is a chain of quotation running Alexander Polyhistor → Josephus / Eusebius → Syncellus · Wikipedia
Summary: A Babylonian priest of Bel-Marduk who wrote his country's history in Greek for a Seleucid king, and in doing so carried the Mesopotamian antediluvian chronology out of cuneiform and into the literature of the West. His second book gives ten kings before the flood reigning 432,000 years in total, and a flood narrative — the hero Xisuthrus, a divine warning, a vessel, birds sent out three times, a landing in Armenia — that is recognisably the story of Gilgamesh XI in Greek dress. This is the channel by which the Babylonian primeval scheme reached Josephus, Eusebius, and the Byzantine chronographers, and thence Christian reckoning of the age of the world. Its evidential value here is as a transmission witness, and as a test case: the numbers get tidier as the tradition ages, which is not what preserved memory usually does.

Claims & evidence

Ten kings before the flood, 120 sari, 432,000 years

the first king was Alorus of Babylon, a Chaldæan: he reigned ten sari: and afterwards Alaparus, and Amelon who came from Pantibiblon: then Ammenon the Chaldæan, in whose time appeared the Musarus Oannes the Annedotus from the Erythræan sea… Then succeeded Megalarus from the city of Pantibiblon; and he reigned eighteen sari: and after him Daonus the shepherd from Pantibiblon reigned ten sari… Then reigned Euedorachus from Pantibiblon, for the term of eighteen sari… Then reigned Amempsinus, a Chaldæan from Laranchæ… Then reigned Otiartes, a Chaldæan, from Laranchæ; and he reigned eight sari. And upon the death of Otiartes, his son Xisuthrus reigned eighteen sari: in his time happened the great deluge. So that the sum of all the kings is ten; and the term which they collectively reigned an hundred and twenty sari.

— Berossus, Babyloniaca II, from Alexander Polyhistor, in I. P. Cory, Ancient Fragments, rev. ed. E. R. Hodges (London, 1876), full text at archive.org. A saros is 3,600 years, so 120 sari = 432,000 years. The names are Greek transcriptions of the Sumerian ones on the King List — Alorus is Alulim, Daonus the shepherd is Dumuzid the shepherd, Euedorachus is En-men-dur-ana of Sippar (Pantibiblon), Otiartes is Ubara-Tutu, and Xisuthrus is Ziusudra. So this is unmistakably the same tradition, at a remove of some fifteen centuries from the prism.

But it is not the same list. Berossus has ten kings where the Weld-Blundell prism has eight, and 432,000 years where the prism has 241,200. And 432,000 is a rounder number than 241,200, not a rougher one: it is exactly 120 sari, where the prism's figure is an untidy 67. The tradition has been regularised in transmission — the ten-figure scheme aligned with the ten patriarchs of Genesis 5 and the total pushed to a schematic round. That matters for any argument that the reigns encode a memory: a memory decays, but this datum improves with age, which is the signature of a scheme being tidied rather than a record being preserved.

The flood of Xisuthrus

The Deity, Cronus, appeared to him in a vision, and warned him that upon the fifteenth day of the month Dæsius there would be a flood, by which mankind would be destroyed. He therefore enjoined him to write a history of the beginning, procedure, and conclusion of all things; and to bury it in the city of the Sun at Sippara; and to build a vessel, and take with him into it his friends and relations; and to convey on board every thing necessary to sustain life, together with all the different animals; both birds and quadrupeds, and trust himself fearlessly to the deep.

After the flood had been upon the earth, and was in time abated, Xisuthrus sent out birds from the vessel; which, not finding any food, nor any place whereupon they might rest their feet, returned to him again. After an interval of some days, he sent them forth a second time; and they now returned with their feet tinged with mud. He made a trial a third time with these birds; but they returned to him no more: from whence he judged that the surface of the earth had appeared above the waters.

He therefore made an opening in the vessel, and upon looking out found that it was stranded upon the side of some mountain; upon which he immediately quitted it with his wife, his daughter, and the pilot. Xisuthrus then paid his adoration to the earth: and having constructed an altar, offered sacrifices to the gods, and, with those who had come out of the vessel with him, disappeared… they could distinguish his voice in the air, and could hear him admonish them to pay due regard to religion; and likewise informed them that it was upon account of his piety that he was translated to live with the gods… moreover that the place, wherein they then were, was the land of Armenia.

The vessel being thus stranded in Armenia, some part of it yet remains in the Corcyræan mountains of Armenia; and the people scrape off the bitumen, with which it had been outwardly coated, and make use of it by way of an alexipharmic and amulet.

— Berossus, Babyloniaca II, from Alexander Polyhistor, in Cory, Ancient Fragments. Almost every element has a cuneiform ancestor: the window opened in the vessel and the sacrifice are in the Eridu Genesis, the three bird-soundings are in Gilgamesh XI, the survivor's translation to the gods is in both. Two things are new. The burial of the writings at Sippara is an antediluvian-knowledge motif with no Genesis counterpart but a long afterlife — compare the pillars of Seth, on which the pre-Flood sciences are inscribed to survive the deluge. And the landing has migrated to Armenia, where Genesis 8:4 puts Ararat and Gilgamesh puts Mount Nimuš — a convergence with the biblical geography that a Hellenistic-era writer with Jewish neighbours may well be reflecting rather than independently attesting.

Nothing of Berossus survives directly: this is an epitome of an epitome

No manuscript of the Babyloniaca exists, and there is no evidence that late-antique readers had one. What circulated was Alexander Polyhistor's first-century-BC abridgement. From that abridgement the material passes to Josephus, who cites Berossus for the flood and for the ark's remains in Armenia; to Eusebius, whose Chronicle preserving it survives complete only in Armenian; and to the Byzantine monk George Syncellus, who excerpts Eusebius. Abydenus and Apollodorus supply parallel and partly divergent versions of the same king-list. So every sentence quoted above reaches us at third or fourth hand, and, as Paul-Alain Beaulieu observes of the corpus, material not originally present may have been drawn in and attributed to him along the way.

— On the transmission chain and its hazards, and for the modern critical collections — S. M. Burstein, The Babyloniaca of Berossus (1978); G. P. Verbrugghe and J. M. Wickersham, Berossos and Manetho, Introduced and Translated (Ann Arbor: University of Michigan Press, 2001); G. De Breucker in Brill's New Jacoby — see Babyloniaca (Berossus). For Josephus' use of him, Against Apion I.19: Berossus "makes mention of the deluge of waters… and of that ark wherein Noah, the origin of our race, was preserved; when it was brought to the highest part of the Armenian mountains."

Why this witness matters to the reconstruction

Berossus is the hinge between the cuneiform record and the Christian one. Both Eusebius and George Syncellus — already witnesses on the Nephilim timeline — are here in their other capacity, as chronographers trying to fit 432,000 Babylonian years against the far shorter span of Genesis 5. When a father or a Byzantine chronicler reaches for Chaldean antediluvian data, it is nearly always Berossus he is reaching through, at several removes. That makes him indispensable for the history of the idea and, for the same reason, weak as independent corroboration of the event: a Hellenistic priest writing in Greek for a Greek court, transmitted by a Jewish historian and two Christian chronographers, is not a channel in which biblical and Babylonian data can be assumed to have stayed uncontaminated.

Notes

  • Berossus' first book introduces Oannes, the amphibious sage from the Erythraean sea who "gave them an insight into letters and sciences, and arts of every kind" and taught men "to construct cities, to found temples, to compile laws" — the Greek face of the Mesopotamian apkallu sages, whose seventh member is the ascending figure compared with Enoch on the King List ≅ Enoch argument.
  • Julius Oppert observed in 1877 that Berossus' 432,000 years and the Masoretic 1,656 years from creation to the flood are both 86,400 units — lustra in the one case, weeks in the other — and inferred a common numerical basis. The observation is real; the inference is fragile, since the Masoretic figure is not the only one Scripture's textual tradition offers (the Samaritan and Greek chronologies differ), and picking the unit that makes the numbers match is the sort of move that will match almost anything.
  • A note on the date, since this witness's is of a different kind from the tablets above it. "c. 281 BC" is not a measurement of anything: it is the reign of the king the book was dedicated to. Nothing physical is being dated here, because nothing physical survives — and the interval between what Berossus wrote and what we can read is filled by three or four copyists with agendas of their own.
