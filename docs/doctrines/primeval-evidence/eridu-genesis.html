The Sumerian flood story (Eridu Genesis) on the Flood and the Nephilim in the material record

Dates: c. 1600 BC (the tablet; "not earlier than Late Old Babylonian") · dating method: palaeographic — the tablet bears no colophon, so it is dated by handwriting alone, the softest of the methods on this shelf · Wikipedia
Summary: A single broken tablet from Nippur — CBS 10673, about a third of the original — carries the only Sumerian-language flood narrative we have. Its hero is Ziusudra ("life of long days"), a king and priest who survives a seven-day deluge in a "huge boat," sacrifices when the sun returns, and is granted eternal life in Dilmun. Its evidential weight on this page is twofold: it shows the flood story running in Sumerian as well as Akkadian, and it fuses that story to the antediluvian king-list tradition — creation, the founding of the first cities, the descent of kingship, and then a flood dividing the ages. That fusion is what the King List argument has to reckon with.

A note on where this sits. This timeline is ordered by the age of the evidence. The tablet is placed after Atrahasis because Atrahasis is dated by a colophon and this one is dated only by palaeography — not because the Sumerian story is later in origin. On the contrary, a Sumerian flood tradition is very plausibly the older one; the tablet carrying it is simply the softer datum.

Claims & evidence

One tablet, one third preserved, dated only by its handwriting

The text survives in a single manuscript with no duplicates and no colophon. Miguel Civil, editing it, is explicit about what that costs:

The Sumerian flood story is preserved in CBS 10673, the bottom third, approximately, of the complete tablet, apparently from Nippur… In the absence of a colophon, a tablet can be dated only by palaeographic, orthographic, linguistic, and other internal criteria… In any case CBS 10673 is not earlier than Late Old Babylonian.

— M. Civil, "The Sumerian Flood Story," in Atra-ḫasīs: The Babylonian Story of the Flood, ed. W. G. Lambert and A. R. Millard (Oxford: Clarendon, 1969), edition online, p. 138. The tablet was first published by Arno Poebel in 1914. Roughly two-thirds of the composition — including the whole account of the flood's onset and most of its aftermath — is simply gone; every summary of "the Sumerian flood story," including this one, is a reconstruction around large holes.

Ziusudra is warned through a wall, and the flood runs seven days and seven nights

At that time, the king Ziusudra, the anointed [. . .]… Ziusudra hea[rd], standing by its side, he stood at the left of the side-wall[. . .] "Side-wall, I want to talk to you, [hold on] to my word, [pay atten]tion to my instructions: on all dwellings (?), over the Capitals the storm will [sweep]. The destruction of the descent of mankind [. . .]"

— Sumerian flood story, lines 145–157, in Civil's translation (Lambert and Millard, edition, p. 142); the bracketed gaps are the tablet's, not the editor's paraphrase. The warning-through-a-wall device is the same one used in Atrahasis III.i, where Enki, sworn to silence, addresses a reed wall so the hero may overhear. The Oxford ETCSL edition of the same composition supplies the duration where the Lambert–Millard text is broken: "after the flood had swept over the land, and waves and windstorms had rocked the huge boat for seven days and seven nights, Utu the sun god came out, illuminating heaven and earth."

The sun returns, the survivor sacrifices, and is given "life like a god"

And the destructive wind had rocked the huge boat in the high water, the Sun came out, illuminating the earth and the sky. Ziusudra made an opening in the huge boat, and the Sun with its rays entered the huge boat. The king Ziusudra prostrated himself before the Sun-god, the king slaughtered a large number of bulls and sheep.

The king Ziusudra prostrated himself before An (and) Enlil… (who) gave him life, like a god, elevated him to eternal life, like a god. At that time, the king Ziusudra, who protected the seed of mankind at the time (?) of destruction, they settled in an overseas country, in the orient, in Dilmun.

— Sumerian flood story, lines 205–211 and 254–260, in Civil's translation (Lambert and Millard, edition, p. 145). Two elements travel intact into Gilgamesh XI: the window opened in the vessel and the immortality granted afterwards at a remote place. Genesis shares the window (Gen 8:6) and the sacrifice (Gen 8:20) but pointedly withholds the deification — Noah leaves the ark a man, and the next thing recorded of him is that he gets drunk.

The story is welded to the antediluvian king-list

What survives of the tablet's opening columns is not a flood at all but a creation, a founding of the first five cities with their patron gods, and the descent of kingship from heaven — with the flood then dividing that sequence in two. Ziusudra belongs to the same king-list tradition, appearing as the flood-hero king at Shuruppak — in some versions a generation after Ubara-Tutu, the last antediluvian ruler of the standard list. Thorkild Jacobsen named the composition the "Eridu Genesis" precisely because of that shape: it is a primeval history running creation → cities → kings → flood → a new age, the same architecture as Genesis 1–9.

— Thorkild Jacobsen, "The Eridu Genesis," Journal of Biblical Literature 100 (1981): 513–529, JSTOR 3266116. The parallel of architecture is a stronger and more interesting datum than any single shared motif — and it cuts both ways, since a shared narrative architecture is exactly what one would expect either from a shared memory or from a shared literary inheritance.

Notes

  • Jacobsen's title is a modern coinage, not an ancient one; the composition has no preserved title of its own, and is also cited simply as "the Sumerian flood story."
  • Ziusudra's name is the Sumerian original behind the Greek Xisouthros of Berossus, and behind the epithet Uta-napišti rūqu, "Uta-napišti the Far-Away," in Gilgamesh XI — one hero under three languages' worth of names.
  • Dating by handwriting alone carries no internal error bar and is calibrated against other undated hands — the softest date on this shelf, and conceded as such in are these witnesses securely dated?, which weighs each method in turn.
