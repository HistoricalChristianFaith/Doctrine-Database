The Mesopotamian alluvial flood deposits on the Flood and the Nephilim in the material record

Dates: the deposits themselves — Ur, c. 3500 BC; Shuruppak (Fara), c. 2950–2850 BC; Kish, three separate layers from c. 3000–2900 BC down to c. 2600 BC (excavated 1928–1931) · M. E. L. Mallowan, Iraq 26 (1964)
Summary: Bands of water-laid, artefact-free silt sealed between occupation layers at several southern Mesopotamian tells. Leonard Woolley found the thickest of them at Ur in 1929 and announced it to the world's press as the Flood; layers were found in the same years at Kish and, most tantalizingly, at Shuruppak itself, the city of the flood hero. These are the only strata anyone has ever produced as direct physical evidence of the Genesis deluge, which is exactly why they belong on this shelf — and why what they turn out to show matters.

Claims & evidence

Woolley's pit at Ur (1929)

Woolley, digging below the Royal Cemetery, cut a shaft that eventually ran sixty-four feet down and struck, at about twelve metres, a band of clean water-laid silt roughly three metres thick with no potsherds, no bones, and no building debris in it — and below the silt, occupation again. He was candid that one narrow sounding proved nothing ("one could scarcely argue for the Deluge on the strength of a pit a yard square") and widened the excavation before publishing. His claim was for a catastrophic local inundation of the Tigris–Euphrates valley that had wiped out one civilization and been remembered as the end of a world. He did not claim a planet-covering flood; the press supplied that.

— C. Leonard Woolley, Ur of the Chaldees: A Record of Seven Years of Excavation (London: Ernest Benn, 1929), and for the Penn Museum's retrospective on the episode, "Reflections on the Mesopotamian Flood," Expedition.

Kish and Shuruppak

Woolley was not first. Stephen Langdon and Louis Watelin, working at Kish, had reported a flood layer directly above Jemdet Nasr material the year before. And in 1931 Erich Schmidt found a flood level immediately above Jemdet Nasr remains at Shuruppak — the city that the Sumerian King List makes the last seat of kingship before the deluge and that Atrahasis and Gilgamesh XI make the flood hero's home. A flood stratum at Shuruppak, dated to the end of the Jemdet Nasr period, is the closest thing in the ground to a match with the texts.

The layers are not contemporary, and not global

This is the decisive datum, and it emerged from the excavators' own work rather than from any critic. The deposits differ in date and in scale, and they do not line up:

  • Ur — up to about eleven feet of silt, c. 3500 BC.
  • Shuruppak — about fifteen inches, c. 2950–2850 BC.
  • Kish — three distinct flood layers, the earliest c. 3000–2900 BC and a later one c. 2600 BC, averaging under ten inches.

Max Mallowan, who had worked under Woolley at Ur as a young man, reopened the whole question in 1964. Reasoning from the King List's post-flood Kish dynasty and plausible reign-lengths, he placed the Mesopotamian flood of the tradition around 2900–2800 BC — which fits Shuruppak and the earliest Kish layer and is six hundred years too late for Woolley's Ur deposit. Woolley's silt, in other words, cannot be the flood the Sumerians remembered, whatever else it was. And the Ur deposit was not even regional: Eridu, seven miles away, was searched for it deliberately and showed nothing. What the tells record is a floodplain doing what floodplains do — repeated severe local inundations at different cities in different centuries.

— M. E. L. Mallowan, "Noah's Flood Reconsidered," Iraq 26/2 (1964): 62–82, with the reply of R. L. Raikes, "The Physical Evidence for Noah's Flood," Iraq 28 (1966): 52–63; the thicknesses, dates and the negative result at Eridu are collected by David MacDonald, "The Flood: Mesopotamian Archaeological Evidence," Creation/Evolution 8/2 (1988).

What the witness supplies, stated plainly

It supplies a real, severe, and repeated flood hazard in the exact landscape where the flood texts were written, and one deposit (Shuruppak, c. 2900 BC) that sits in the right place and about the right date to be an event the tradition remembers. It does not supply a synchronous horizon, and no reconstruction can take these strata as evidence of one event without contradicting their dates. That is the whole substance of the Ur-deposit argument, and it is also the reason the predictive test for a worldwide horizon is worth running separately — see the modern scientific record and the predictive arguments.

Note the scoping. The failure here is a failure of the identification "Woolley's silt = the Genesis Flood," and of any model requiring one simultaneous deposit. It is not a failure of the milder claim that a flood tradition has a historical kernel: an alluvial plain that drowns its cities every few centuries is a perfectly good soil for such a tradition to grow in.

Notes

  • Woolley's announcement is a case study in how a find travels.
