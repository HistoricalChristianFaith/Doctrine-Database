Does the peer-reviewed SAR-Doppler scan really reveal undiscovered structures inside the Great Pyramid?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — for the interpretation that Biondi and Malanga's 2022 micro-movement SAR-Doppler method can bear the internal reconstruction it claims: unlike the viral Khafre "underground city," this claim did clear peer review, targets the Great Pyramid's own interior rather than kilometres of bedrock, and rests on a real prior use of the same technique on Vesuvius — so it is not baseless. But the reconstruction has no independent ground-truth from the best-surveyed pyramid interior on Earth, the method has never been reproduced by anyone but its author, and its sister Vesuvius result under-reaches the depth conventional geophysics assigns the target it claims to have imaged. Advanced by Filippo Biondi and Corrado Malanga (the "Khafre Project")

The companion page on this shelf weighs the claim that went viral — a lost city read out of "SAR tomography" hundreds of metres beneath the second pyramid, Khafre's — and rates it unsupported, because the imaging method cannot physically reach those depths and the 2025 material was never reviewed. That page uses the earlier 2022 paper only as its honest foil: the "modest, peer-reviewed root" that names no city and no depth. This page takes up what that framing left out. The 2022 paper makes a large positive claim of its own — not about deep bedrock but about the Great Pyramid's own interior — and it cleared peer review in an indexed journal. So the question here is narrower, and genuinely open in a different way from the Khafre city: set the deep-penetration physics aside entirely, and ask whether this peer-reviewed method can bear even the internal-Khufu reconstruction it advertises. The answer turns on method and corroboration, not on personalities.

The claim

The 2022 paper is careful about what radar cannot do — and then claims to have done it anyway by an indirect route. Its abstract concedes the penetration problem, proposes reading background-seismic micro-movements instead, and states the result in the strongest possible terms:

… due to the poor penetrating action of electromagnetic waves inside solid bodies, the capability to observe inside distributed targets is precluded. … The present work describes an imaging method based on the analysis of micro-movements on the Khnum-Khufu Pyramid, which are usually generated by background seismic waves. The results obtained prove to be very promising, as high-resolution full 3D tomographic imaging of the pyramid's interior and subsurface was achieved. Khnum-Khufu becomes transparent like a crystal when observed in the micro-movement domain. Based on this novelty, we have completely reconstructed internal objects, observing and measuring structures that have never been discovered before.

— Filippo Biondi and Corrado Malanga, "Synthetic Aperture Radar Doppler Tomography Reveals Details of Undiscovered High-Resolution Internal Structure of the Great Pyramid of Giza," in the peer-reviewed journal Remote Sensing (2022). The body enumerates specific new features: two inclined, diverging internal ramps sloping at about 42 degrees inside the west and east faces; a horizontal "southern corridor" some 90 metres up near the south side; and a granite structure they name "Zed" with a two-slab oblique roof, alongside the long-known King's and Queen's chambers, Grand Gallery, and subterranean room. The paper's conclusion is not hedged at all: it claims the experimental results "definitively solve one of the oldest mysteries of human existence, the complete solution of the internal structure of Khnum-Khufu."1

Three things about the claim should be held in view. It is peer-reviewed — unlike the 2025 Khafre announcement. It is about the Great Pyramid's own interior and immediate subsurface, not a two-kilometre bedrock complex, so the "radar can't reach 648 metres" objection that sinks the Khafre claim does not directly touch it. And it turns entirely on whether the micro-movement inversion is valid — whether tiny vibrations read off satellite images can be turned into a true three-dimensional picture of what is inside a stone mountain. That is the question to test.

Who advances it

The same two-man core as the Khafre claim: Filippo Biondi, a radar engineer at the University of Strathclyde, who authored the method, and Corrado Malanga, a retired organic chemist at Pisa known separately as a writer on UFOs and alien abduction. As with the sibling page, it is the method and not the biography that is rated here — but the biography bears on the mythic overlay the same team later attached to the Khafre reading, and on how much independent scrutiny the technique has drawn. On that last point the honest fact cuts more than one way: this paper passed peer review and was published in an indexed journal, which the 2025 Khafre material never was.2

The evidence for

Steelmanned, this is a materially stronger case than the Khafre city, and it should be granted its best form. Synthetic-aperture radar is a genuine, powerful instrument, and Biondi's idea is not the naive "shine radar through rock" that critics rightly mock. It reads the faint micro-vibrations a structure returns under ambient background-seismic energy, recorded in a single-look-complex COSMO-SkyMed image, and inverts the Doppler distortions of those vibrations into a three-dimensional reconstruction. As a research proposal that is ingenious rather than absurd, and it cleared peer review.

The Vesuvius precedent is the strongest prop, and it must be weighed seriously. Biondi published the same SAR-echography Doppler-imaging technique earlier in 2022, in the same journal, on Mount Vesuvius — claiming to map the volcano's magma chamber and its main and secondary conduits to a depth of about three kilometres, again from a single COSMO-SkyMed image. If a peer-reviewed application of this method to a volcano drew no published objection, the argument runs, why should its application to a pyramid be dismissed out of hand? A technique is not discredited by the topic it is pointed at. This is the case's real backbone: a prior, independent, peer-reviewed use of the identical method on a different target.3

And the Great Pyramid's interior is not fully closed to the possibility of unmapped features. Legitimate subsurface work has repeatedly found real voids there: ScanPyramids' muon imaging located a corridor-shaped "Big Void" of at least thirty metres above the Grand Gallery in 2017, and a smaller "North Face Corridor" behind the entrance chevrons, later confirmed by endoscope in 2023. So the bare proposition that structures remain to be found inside Khufu is not itself unreasonable.4

Adversarial assessment

The best-surveyed pyramid interior on Earth supplies no corroboration — and that is the decisive test. The strength that makes this claim testable is exactly what defeats it. The inside of the Great Pyramid is arguably the most intensively probed structure in archaeology: muon radiography from three independent teams, endoscopy, ground-penetrating radar, resistivity, and thermal survey. If Biondi's 42-degree ramps, his 90-metre "southern corridor," and his granite "Zed" were really there, these methods — several of which did find genuine new voids in the same years — had every opportunity to register them. None did. Not one of the reconstruction's "structures that have never been discovered before" has been independently confirmed by muon imaging, endoscopy, or any geophysical survey; and conversely the reconstruction does not cleanly recover the features that are known to be there. A reading of the interior that the better-validated methods neither corroborate nor match is, on the ordinary standard of evidence, a set of processing artifacts rather than structures — possibility, not proof.4

The method lives only in its author's own papers. No independent group has reproduced Biondi's micro-movement SAR-Doppler inversion on any target — not a pyramid, not a volcano, not a building. The technique appears in his Vesuvius paper, this Khufu paper, and the later unreviewed Khafre material, and nowhere else; the Vesuvius article itself closes by inviting other groups to reproduce the method, which is a solicitation, not a demonstration. Even the proponents concede the point from the other direction: their own methodological defence states plainly that "no Egyptologist, debunker, nor skeptic has directly challenged the documented methodology of SAR Doppler Tomography." That is offered as vindication, but it cuts the other way too — a method neither independently replicated nor independently vetted has not been validated; it has only been left alone. An unrefuted method is not thereby a confirmed one.5

The Vesuvius precedent has limits — and under scrutiny it weakens the case rather than strengthening it. The analogy is not clean. A magma chamber is a large, hot, seismically active, low-contrast target for which independent seismic tomography exists as a check; discrete cold engineered voids in limestone are a different imaging problem. More to the point, the check does not favour Biondi: conventional seismic tomography and receiver-function studies place the top of Vesuvius's main magma chamber at roughly eight to ten kilometres depth, with shallow bodies near two to three kilometres read as solidified dike networks, not the chamber. Biondi's claimed three-kilometre imaging does not even reach the depth at which the target he says he imaged actually sits. The one place the method can be checked against an established geophysical picture, it does not match it. That is not a precedent that carries the pyramid; it is a second uncorroborated result by the same author using the same technique.3

"Peer-reviewed" is a real data point, not a verdict. The paper appeared in Remote Sensing, a legitimate, Scopus- and SCIE-indexed, Q1 journal with a real impact factor — that should be neither waved away nor leaned on as authority. Its publisher, MDPI, carries documented reputational questions (a brief appearance on Beall's list of predatory publishers in 2014–15 before a successful appeal, a 2024 downgrade of many of its journals by Finland's evaluation system, and a fast, special-issue-heavy model), and Remote Sensing itself once had a paper pass review that prompted its founding editor-in-chief to resign. None of this shows the Khufu paper is wrong; it shows only that clearing this particular review is a limited signal. Peer review here means the claim was not obviously unpublishable — not that the reconstruction is sound.6

The overstatement is its own tell. A method presented in its abstract as making a stone mountain "transparent like a crystal," and in its conclusion as having "definitively solved one of the oldest mysteries of human existence," is claiming a certainty that no single-image inversion of ambient vibrations — unreplicated and unconfirmed — can carry. This is best read not as falsehood but as a less-probable reading dressed as settled fact: the strong form of the claim ("the complete solution of the internal structure") runs far past what the evidence in hand supports, and the gap between the language and the corroboration is itself a reason for caution.1

Assessment

Weak. The interpretation this page rates — that the 2022 micro-movement SAR-Doppler method can bear the internal reconstruction of the Great Pyramid it advertises — is genuinely stronger than its viral Khafre sibling, and the difference is worth stating precisely so the rating is not read as a wave-off. It cleared peer review; it does not depend on the impossible deep-penetration physics that sinks the underground-city claim; and it rests on a prior, independent, peer-reviewed use of the same technique. Those are real supports, and they are why this rates weak rather than the Khafre claim's unsupported: the method is an ingenious, not-refuted research proposal, not a physical impossibility. But a proposal is not a validated instrument, and the reconstruction it produced has nothing under it. The most intensively surveyed pyramid interior in the world corroborates none of its new features and is not cleanly matched by them; the technique has never been reproduced by anyone but its author; the one sister result that can be checked against independent geophysics under-reaches its own target's known depth; and the paper's own language ("transparent like a crystal," "definitively solve") outruns the evidence by a wide margin. What would move this off weak is entirely ordinary and entirely absent: an independent group reproducing the inversion on a ground-truthed target, or a muon, endoscopy, or GPR survey confirming even one of the reconstructed structures. Until then the peer-reviewed root is a better-behaved claim than the city it grew into, but on its own interior reconstruction it remains possibility mistaken for proof.
