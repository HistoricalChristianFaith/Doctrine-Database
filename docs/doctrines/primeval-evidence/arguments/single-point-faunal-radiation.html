A global Flood predicts single-point post-Flood faunal radiation — does biogeography show it?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — as a prediction of the global-ark model. The prediction is unusually clean: if every surviving land animal walked off one vessel at one place, the living distribution of land animals should be a radiation from that place, and the fossil record should preserve the routes. What biogeography shows instead is a world partitioned into faunal provinces whose boundaries track ancient geography rather than distance from any single point, and — the sharper failure — the intermediate fossils that a radiation would have to leave along its routes are absent from those routes and present only where a deep-time distribution predicts them. The strongest defences of the model are real dispersal mechanisms that genuinely operate (rafting on vegetation mats, land bridges exposed at low sea level), and the honest report is that the model's own literature has largely conceded the point: its most careful advocates now appeal to divine direction of the dispersal, which converts the prediction into an explanation of why it cannot be checked. The failure is scoped to the single-landing-point global-ark model.

Related arguments: testing the premise forward — see also a synchronous worldwide sediment horizon · a recent eight-person bottleneck · convergent independent flood narratives · authenticated Nephilim material remains

The prediction

Genesis grounds the ark "upon the mountains of Ararat," and the animals go out of it. On the global-ark model that is a single release point for the entire terrestrial fauna of the earth, a few thousand years ago. The prediction follows directly and admits of no vagueness: the present distribution of land animals should be a radiation from one point in eastern Anatolia. Diversity should broadly decrease with distance and difficulty from that point; the animals of remote regions should be a subset of those nearer; and, because dispersal takes generations and generations leave dead animals, the routes should be marked by fossils — a trail of the dispersing kinds strung out between Ararat and wherever they ended up.

That last clause is what makes this a real test rather than a story. Living distributions alone can be explained by enough conjectured migrations. A migration that actually happened leaves remains along the way.

What would falsify it — committed in advance

The prediction fails if the world partitions into faunal provinces whose boundaries correspond to ancient continental configurations rather than to distance from Anatolia; if whole major groups are confined to regions they could only have reached by crossing terrain where they are entirely unrecorded, living or fossil; and if sharp faunal breaks occur across trivial distances where no barrier to a recent walking migration existed.

It would be confirmed by concentric structure — a radiation signature — and above all by the intermediate fossils. Finding Australian marsupials, or their ancestral kinds, in the Neolithic and later deposits of Iran, India and Southeast Asia would confirm it decisively. The absence is not an argument from silence in the weak sense: these are among the most intensively excavated regions on earth, and the deposits of the relevant few thousand years are precisely the ones archaeology digs through routinely.

The strongest case for

The dispersal mechanisms are real, and this must be granted without hedging. Over-water rafting on floating vegetation is not a creationist invention; it is the accepted explanation for how monkeys and rodents reached South America and how lemurs reached Madagascar, and it is observed in the modern world. Kurt Wise and Matthew Croxton set it out as a post-Flood mechanism in 2003, and Michael Oard developed the case that log mats — vast rafts of timber left floating after a global inundation — would have been available in unprecedented quantity precisely in the period required, carrying passengers across water in a window that later closed. Land bridges are equally real: lowered sea level during glaciation exposed Beringia, Sundaland and the Sahul shelf, and the model's ice-age reconstruction places that low stand immediately after the Flood, which is exactly when it is needed.1

And the model has thought about the fossil evidence rather than ignoring it. The Answers Research Journal treatment of the marsupial fossil record engages the problem directly, reading Cretaceous metatherians as animals that died in the Flood and the post-Flood boundary as the horizon above which dispersal is recorded — a genuine attempt to say what the model predicts stratigraphically rather than only where the animals ended up. Complicating data are cited by its own advocates, including the Tertiary Thylacinus material from Papua New Guinea and the report of Mesozoic monotremes in South America, both of which sit awkwardly for anyone assuming marsupials were always where they now are.2

Adversarial assessment

What holds. Rafting, land bridges and long-distance dispersal are genuine and well-attested; biogeography is not a discipline that denies animals cross oceans. The model is right that some distributions require over-water transport. And it is right that the fossil record of marsupials outside Australia is more complicated than a simple story allows.

The provinces track ancient geography, not distance from a point. Alfred Russel Wallace established in 1876 that the world's land fauna divides into regions with sharp boundaries, and the boundaries are not concentric about anywhere. Marsupials, living and fossil, are confined to Australasia and the Americas — two regions on opposite sides of the planet, joined in deep time through Antarctica and by nothing at all in the Holocene. Lemurs are confined to Madagascar. Most tellingly, the Wallace Line runs between Bali and Lombok, islands some twenty miles apart, and the fauna changes across it completely: a barrier that means everything to an animal that must cross deep water in a strait that has never been dry, and nothing whatever to an animal walking from Anatolia, which by that point has already crossed continents.3

The route fossils are missing, and this is the prediction's own test. If marsupials walked or rafted from Ararat to Australia within the last few thousand years, they were in Iran, in India, and in Southeast Asia on the way, in numbers, for generations. Nothing of the kind is recorded — no Holocene marsupial anywhere along the route, in regions whose Holocene deposits are exhaustively excavated. The same holds for the reverse trip the model also requires, since Australia's marsupials must first have reached the ark. A migration that leaves no trace over half the world in either direction is not a migration for which evidence has yet to be found; it is the specific prediction that was made, checked, and not met.

The intra-creationist critique is sharper than anything external, and it should be reported as such. The response published in the same journal argues that a single dispersal route would have offered every marsupial the same ecological conditions, that positing many different routes to get around this is untenable, and that no Asian corridor can be identified that was passable to marsupials and impassable to placentals — wherever marsupials could go, placentals could go, and placentals did not go. The koala case is put bluntly: it eats eucalyptus leaves, and there is no pathway of eucalypts from the Middle East to Australia. Parallel difficulties attach to the carnivores, who would have to linger near the ark while slower prey dispersed — which has fleet marsupial predators starting behind slow placental herbivores.4

And the model's own advocates have largely conceded. Oard's 2022 treatment allows that "God was possibly directing their dispersal"; a subsequent contributor argues there are no natural explanations available and attributes post-Flood biogeography to direct divine intervention. This is honest of them and it is decisive here. The concession does not make the position false — this wiki does not discount a claim merely because it requires a miracle, and a God who parts a sea can move a koala. What it does is withdraw the prediction. An argument that begins "the model predicts a radiation pattern, and here it is" cannot end "the pattern is absent because the dispersal was miraculously arranged" and still count as evidence. A creation.com contributor made the corresponding methodological point from inside the camp: by claiming their natural mechanisms explain biogeography better, creationists invite the counter that deep time supplies more and better mechanisms of the same kind.4

A note on method. Nothing here rests on holding a miracle impossible. The objection is that the distribution of animals was offered as physical evidence for the model, and physical evidence has to be able to come out the other way. Possibility is not proof. If the dispersal was supernaturally directed, then biogeography is silent about it in both directions and cannot be entered in the model's favour either.

What the failure does and does not touch. It falsifies the single-landing-point global-ark model. It does not touch a regional flood, on which the surviving fauna of unaffected regions never left home and no radiation is predicted at all — indeed the observed pattern is what a regional flood expects. And it does not touch the antiquity of the flood tradition, which stands on the cuneiform record.

Assessment

Weak — as a prediction of the global-ark model. The prediction was clean and checkable: one release point, therefore a radiation, therefore route fossils. Biogeography instead partitions the world into provinces whose boundaries follow ancient continental configurations — marsupials in Australasia and the Americas, lemurs in Madagascar, a total faunal change across twenty miles of deep water at the Wallace Line — and the intermediate fossils that any recent overland radiation would have strewn between Anatolia and Australia are absent from the most heavily excavated ground on earth.

The mechanisms the model invokes are genuine and are granted: rafting on vegetation mats and land bridges at low sea level really do move animals across water. What they do not produce is the observed pattern, and the most careful advocates of the model have said so in their own journals — that no corridor selective for marsupials over placentals can be identified, that no route supplies a koala with eucalypts, and finally that the dispersal must be attributed to direct divine direction. That last move is available and is not refuted here; but it retires the prediction rather than satisfying it, and a prediction retired cannot be counted as one confirmed. The failure belongs to the single-landing-point model and to nothing wider.
