Are the Eastern US mounds the "pyramids" of a lost 2,000-year civilization revealed by LiDAR?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — as the claim that publicly-available LiDAR has revealed, across the Eastern United States, the "pyramids, cities, palaces and geoglyphs" of "great & sophisticated civilizations" that mainstream archaeology has missed or under-rated. Two things have to be kept apart, and the argument runs them together. That unmapped earthworks still lie under the eastern woodland canopy, waiting to be found from the air, is a priori plausible and unremarkable — LiDAR has genuinely rewritten the map of pre-Columbian complexity in the Maya lowlands and the Amazon, and it would be surprising if the wooded East held no more to find. But the freight the argument loads onto that plausible point does not follow. The eastern mounds are not the work of an unknown or "lost" people: they are the securely-dated monuments of the Adena, Hopewell and Mississippian traditions, built across roughly 2,500 years (c. 1000 BC – AD 1500), whose builders, dates and material culture are known in detail — three of the complexes are a UNESCO World Heritage Site inscribed in 2023, and the largest earthwork north of Mexico, Cahokia's Monks Mound, has been excavated and radiocarbon-dated for over a century. Calling their flat-topped platform mounds "pyramids" is a terminological reframing, not a discovery; the "lost great civilization" is one already named, dated and well described. And the "well over 100" new sites are, on the proponent's own statement, an unverified remote-sensing count with field-checking still to come — which is to say, not yet evidence of anything. Advanced by Luke Caverns (Maya Exploration Center), with a companion "North American super city" framing associated with the mound-builder complexes.

The claim

Over roughly two years the explorer and Maya Exploration Center research associate Luke Caverns says he has used publicly-available airborne-LiDAR data to identify and map "well over 100" previously-uncharted sites across the Eastern United States, and he insists on describing them as cities, villages, pyramids, palaces and geoglyphs. In a 2026 post he wrote that "calling these anything short of ‘Pyramids’ is a disservice to the civilizations that ruled over the American East for over 2000 years," and set as one of his goals for the year to "shed light on the great & sophisticated civilizations that built enormous Pyramids, Palaces, & Geoglyphs." On-the-ground verification of the remotely-identified sites is promised for a field season still to come. A companion strand reframes the known mound-builder heartland — Cahokia, Poverty Point and their kin — as a North American "super city" to be set beside the great centres of Mesoamerica.1

The advocated interpretation is that the earthworks of the American East are the monuments of a sophisticated lost civilization that mainstream archaeology has missed, under-rated, or failed to dignify with the right word — and that LiDAR is now beginning to reveal its true extent.

Steelman: what is genuinely plausible here

The starting point is sound and should be granted without grudging. Airborne laser scanning really has transformed the map of pre-Columbian complexity where forest canopy had hidden it. The PACUNAM survey of northern Guatemala mapped some 60,000 structures under the Petén jungle in a single campaign, revising upward every estimate of Maya population and land use; LiDAR turned a scatter of temples on the Upano Valley floor of Amazonian Ecuador into a two-thousand-year-old network of platform towns, plazas and roads; and it exposed low-density urbanism with causeways and reservoirs across the Bolivian Llanos de Mojos. "Public LiDAR reveals uncharted earthworks in the wooded East" is therefore not a fringe hope but a reasonable expectation, and the mound cultures it would be finding were genuinely sophisticated: Cahokia was the largest urban settlement north of Mexico, with a population on the order of 10,000–20,000 at its peak and some 120 mounds, and Monks Mound — a four-tiered earthen platform about thirty metres high, covering roughly fourteen acres at its base, a footprint comparable to the Great Pyramid of Giza — is the largest prehistoric earthwork in the Americas.2

None of that is in dispute here, and this reconstruction has already conceded it in full. That early and large-scale monumental building recurs across the record inside the ordinary chronology — Watson Brake in Louisiana raised by mobile hunter-gatherers around 5,400–5,000 years ago, Poverty Point some 3,700–3,100 years ago, the Amazonian and Andean lidar cities — is exactly the calibration point made on this wiki's Göbekli Tepe page: a society building at monumental scale earlier or more elaborately than a textbook expected is normal, not an out-of-place anomaly that calls for a lost civilization.3

The two sub-claims

Once the conceded ground is set aside, the argument reduces to two claims that must be rated separately.

(i) Unmapped earthworks remain to be found in the East. Plausible and mundane. It commits one to nothing about who built them, when, or how sophisticated they were — only that not every feature under the canopy is yet on a map. Nobody contests it.

(ii) They are the "pyramids" of an unknown, "lost," sophisticated civilization that mainstream archaeology has missed. This is the reframing that carries the argument's weight, and it is the one to test. It has three moving parts: that the builders are unknown or lost; that "pyramid" is the right and revelatory word for their mounds; and that the "100+ sites" are established discoveries. All three fail.

Adversarial assessment: do the conditions hold?

The builders are named, dated, and anything but lost. The earthworks of the American East belong to a continuous, well-studied tradition: the Adena of the Ohio Valley (roughly the first millennium BC), the Hopewell interaction sphere (c. 1–400 AD), and the Mississippian mound-builders (c. AD 800–1600), with an Archaic prelude at Watson Brake and Poverty Point older still. These cultures are defined by decades of excavation, radiocarbon dating, and stratigraphy; their subsistence, exchange networks, ceramics, astronomy and mortuary practice are described in a large professional literature. Eight of the Ohio Hopewell complexes were inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in September 2023 — monumental geometric enclosures built between about 1 and 400 AD, laid out with a standard unit of measure and aligned to the cycles of the sun and moon. This is a known 2,500-year phenomenon with identified makers, not an unknown one; "lost great civilizations that ruled over the American East" describes cultures that fill museum galleries and a World Heritage citation.4

"Pyramid" is a relabelling, not a find. The flat-topped Mississippian platform mound is one of the best-described monument types in North American archaeology. These are earthen substructures — built up in basket-loaded stages to raise a temple, a council house, or a chief's residence above the plaza — and Monks Mound is the type specimen. That they are broadly "pyramidal" in silhouette has never been a secret; popular writing has called Cahokia a "pyramid" for generations. But swapping "platform mound" for "pyramid" adds no information about the structure; what it adds is an Old-World-monument connotation — the intimation of a stone-cut, engineered, quarried edifice on the Egyptian model — that the earthen fabric does not warrant. The reframing does rhetorical work precisely by importing a resonance the evidence withholds. An accurate description already existed; renaming it is not a discovery.5

LiDAR maps; it does not date, and it does not confirm. This is the recurring point of the out-of-place-artifact arguments on this reconstruction, and it applies with full force here. Airborne laser scanning is an altimeter: it records the shape of the ground under vegetation. It assigns no date, and — more to the present claim — it cannot by itself tell a genuine archaeological feature from a natural landform, a colonial-era field boundary, a modern earthwork, or a data artifact. A shape mapped from the air becomes a site only when someone walks it, digs it, or matches its material. That is why LiDAR-led projects publish their finds after pedestrian survey and excavation ground-truth the signatures, and why a raw count of anomalies is a list of hypotheses, not a list of sites. On the proponent's own account the "well over 100" have not yet been field-checked — verification is placed in a future season. His own hedge is the correct verdict on the remote claim: until the ground-truthing is done, the count is not evidence that these features are anything, still less "pyramids" of a lost civilization.6

The Maya–North America "super city" bridge has no material basis. Reframing Cahokia and the mound-builder heartland as a Maya-linked "super city" trades on a real family resemblance — both traditions built platform mounds around plazas — but there is no archaeological evidence of a Mesoamerican civilization ruling, colonizing, or directly building in the Mississippi Valley. Long-distance contact and the northward spread of maize agriculture are real and documented; a shared "super city" polity is not. The Mississippian world developed its own trajectory, and the resemblance is the ordinary convergence of societies solving the monument problem with earth and labour, not the trace of a single lost empire spanning the continent.7

Assessment

Weak — as the claim that the eastern earthworks are the "pyramids" of an unknown, lost, sophisticated civilization revealed by LiDAR.

The concessions are real and are entered before the verdict. LiDAR has genuinely rewritten the map of pre-Columbian complexity elsewhere, so the expectation that uncharted earthworks remain under the eastern canopy is reasonable, not fringe; and the mound cultures the argument gestures at were authentically sophisticated, Cahokia among the great pre-industrial cities of the hemisphere. Sub-claim (i) — more to be found — is plausible and costs nothing.

But sub-claim (ii), which is where the argument lives, does not stand. Its three loads all fail. The builders are not lost: the Adena, Hopewell and Mississippian traditions are among the most intensively studied in North American archaeology, dated by radiocarbon and stratigraphy across some 2,500 years, three of their complexes a 2023 UNESCO World Heritage Site. "Pyramid" is a relabelling of the well-described platform mound that adds an Old-World connotation the earthen fabric does not earn. And the "100+ sites" are, by the proponent's own statement, an unverified remote-sensing count awaiting field survey — and LiDAR, which dates nothing and cannot on its own distinguish a feature from a landform, cannot supply the confirmation the framing already assumes. The Maya "super city" link is a rhetorical bridge with no material footing.

What the argument points at is real, and it is exactly what this reconstruction has already granted and honoured: monumental building recurs across the human record inside the ordinary chronology, and the American East holds a magnificent instance of it. That is a reason to name the Adena, Hopewell and Mississippian builders and date their work, not to dissolve them into an anonymous "lost civilization" and rename their mounds. Possibility is not proof: that LiDAR could reveal an unmapped earthwork does not establish that these particular hundred-plus are anything, and the securely-dated complexity the claim rests on is genuine but already accounted for — a datum for the calibration point on the Göbekli Tepe page and the peopling-of-the-Americas example on the dating-security argument, not evidence of a civilization the record has missed.
