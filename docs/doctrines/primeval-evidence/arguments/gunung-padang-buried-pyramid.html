Is Gunung Padang a 25,000-year-old buried pyramid?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — as the claim that the terraced hill of Gunung Padang in West Java is not a natural volcanic promontory dressed by a late-prehistoric megalithic culture but a buried, man-made stepped pyramid whose deepest construction phase reaches ~25,000 BP (up to 27,000 BP), which would make it the oldest monument on earth by a wide margin and a direct material footprint of a lost Ice-Age civilization. The steelman rests on a 2023 geophysical survey (ground-penetrating radar, electrical-resistivity and seismic tomography, core drilling, and radiocarbon) that reported buried "construction" units under the visible terraces, the deepest carbon-dated to the late Pleistocene — a paper retracted in March 2024 over a specific, decisive error: the old radiocarbon dates were run on soil not associated with any artifact or feature that could be reliably read as man-made, so they date natural soil, not human building. Two sub-claims are separated and rated apart. The anthropogenic-architecture claim — that the deep units are built rather than natural — is weak: real subsurface anomalies exist, but nothing in them is demonstrably constructed, and the hill's stone is natural columnar andesite. The deep-dating claim is weaker still and fails on the association problem the retraction names. What survives is small and true: the upper terraces are genuine human work — but late-prehistoric, on the order of two millennia, not twenty-five. The retraction is weighed on its stated evidential ground, not on authority; a consensus that retracted the paper is not by itself the argument, so the ground it gives is what carries the weight here. Advanced by the survey's lead author Danny Hilman Natawidjaja and popularized by Graham Hancock, who featured the site in the opening episode of Ancient Apocalypse and is thanked in the retracted paper's acknowledgements.

The claim

Gunung Padang is a terraced hilltop site above the village of Karyamukti in the Cianjur regency of West Java: five stepped terraces climbing a promontory, buttressed with dry-stone retaining walls, reached from the valley by a long stone stair, and paved and walled with thousands of prismatic stone columns. The conventional reading is that it is a punden berundak — an Indonesian stepped ancestral sanctuary — one of the largest of its kind, raised in the late prehistoric period. The argument here is far larger. A team led by the geologist Danny Hilman Natawidjaja surveyed the hill with ground-penetrating radar (GPR), electrical-resistivity tomography (ERT), seismic tomography, trenching and deep core drilling, and concluded that beneath the visible terraces lie further built layers — a multi-phase construction whose deepest units, they reported, carbon-date to roughly 14,000–25,000 BP, with a possible initial phase toward 27,000 BP. On that reading Gunung Padang is not a hill that people terraced but a colossal, mostly buried stepped pyramid, the oldest monumental structure on earth, built during the last Ice Age — and so a material trace of exactly the kind of vanished high antiquity this reconstruction is testing for. The results were published in October 2023 in the peer-reviewed journal Archaeological Prospection, and the site had already anchored the opening episode of Graham Hancock's Netflix series Ancient Apocalypse the year before.1

What the hill is made of, conceded first

The concession owed at the outset is the same one owed at Göbekli Tepe and in the Andean cases: the raw material is real and the human hand on the upper site is real. Gunung Padang sits on the eroded neck of an extinct volcano; its building blocks are columnar joints — prismatic andesite/basalt columns formed naturally as thick lava cooled and cracked, the same geology that makes the Giant's Causeway. The bedrock intrusion is dated by potassium–argon to about 32 million years. Nobody carved these columns; they are a natural product of the volcano. What is genuinely human is the arrangement: on the terraces the columns are gathered, broken to length, and stacked — often laid horizontally or piled, rather than standing in the vertical palisade an undisturbed lava flow leaves — into retaining walls and platforms. That is real megalithic labour, and it is not in dispute. The whole question is whether anything below the terraces is also built, and how old the building is.2

The steelman: real subsurface anomalies

The survey's strongest fact is that the hill is not solid to its core. The converging geophysical methods — GPR, ERT and seismic tomography — agree that Gunung Padang has internal structure: zones the resistivity survey reads as very high-resistance anomalies (which the team interprets as voids or chambers), a matching low-velocity seismic anomaly, and layered horizons that the cores appear to pass through. During drilling at one hole the team recorded a large loss of circulation water — some tens of thousands of litres — which they read as evidence of an open cavity below. And the deep cores did recover organic material that returned late-Pleistocene radiocarbon ages. So the steelman is not nothing: there is layering under the hill, there are resistivity and seismic anomalies, and there is genuinely old carbon down there. The argument stands or falls on what those facts license.3

The dating method, and the flaw it hides

The official young age and the claimed deep age rest on the same method — radiocarbon — so the whole case turns on a single question the OOPArt method makes central: dated on what? Radiocarbon dates the death of an organism, not the act of building; a date means something for a structure only when the dated carbon is stratigraphically tied to a feature that is itself demonstrably man-made. This is precisely where the survey breaks. The old ages come from organic matter in soil recovered from the cores — and soil accumulates naturally around and within a natural hill over tens of thousands of years whether or not anyone ever built on it. Dating that soil dates the hill's natural history, not a pyramid. The decisive missing link is association: the survey never establishes that the deep, old-dated soil sits in, or against, an in-situ constructed feature rather than a natural volcanic-and-weathering deposit.4

This is not a reconstruction imposed after the fact; it is the stated ground on which the paper was retracted. In March 2024 Archaeological Prospection, by agreement between its co-editors-in-chief (Eileen Ernenwein and Gregory Tsokas) and the publisher, retracted the article. The retraction notice names one major error, not caught in peer review: "the radiocarbon dating was applied to soil samples that were not associated with any artifacts or features that could be reliably interpreted as anthropogenic or 'man-made,'" and therefore "the interpretation that the site is an ancient pyramid built 9,000 or more years ago is incorrect." Reviewers had vetted the geophysics; none had the archaeological expertise to catch that the dates hung on unassociated dirt. The critic Flint Dibble put the same point plainly: the paper's headline conclusion does not "really match any of the actual evidence presented in the body of the paper," because there is no evidence of human occupation in the deep, Ice-Age-dated layers at all.5

What the conventional date actually is

Set against the deep claim is a body of ordinary, associated archaeology. Lutfi Yondri, who has excavated at Gunung Padang for some three decades, dates the earliest terrace-building to roughly two thousand years ago: charcoal recovered beneath Terrace 1 returns a date near 117 BC, and samples from other terraces cluster around 50 BC, with construction spread across generations into the first millennium AD. That places Gunung Padang among the oldest and largest of Java's stepped sanctuaries — genuine, impressive, and firmly late-prehistoric, alongside the many other punden berundak built across the island in the first and early second millennia AD. This is the crucial contrast: these dates come from carbon in the terraces, tied to the human building, while the twenty-five-thousand-year figure comes from soil that is not.6

The authors' rebuttal, steelmanned

The authors contest the retraction and their case deserves to be stated at its strongest. They insist the deep units are not natural: they describe columnar rocks laid parallel to the layering (not in the perpendicular, interlocking palisade a lava flow produces), separated by what they read as mortar or fill; heterogeneous block shapes unlike a single cooling event; and they note that no natural columnar-jointing outcrop exists within some kilometres of the hill, so the columns on the terraces were brought, not formed in place. They point to surface finds — a bladed "kujang" stone, marked columns, cup-like sockets — as artifacts, and they argue their radiocarbon samples were taken from within these layered contexts and that a Bayesian (OxCal) model made the sequence internally consistent (deeper units older, fills intermediate). Their core grievance is procedural: that a retraction driven by third-party disagreement over interpretation, rather than by a demonstration that their data are false, is a form of censorship.7

Weighed fairly, the rebuttal relocates the dispute without winning it. Some of its facts are conceded — the terrace columns genuinely were gathered and stacked by human hands, and subsurface anomalies genuinely exist. But every load-bearing step toward a buried pyramid is an interpretation the evidence underdetermines. "Parallel columns with fill" is exactly what a mass of collapsed, slope-washed, and re-deposited volcanic column-debris produces on an eroding andesite hill; "mortar" between natural rocks is a reading, not a measured mineralogy; the surface artifacts are late and sit with the young terraces, not with the Ice-Age soil far below; and an internally consistent Bayesian model of soil ages is still a model of soil, however tidy — consistency among unassociated dates does not manufacture the association they lack. The procedural complaint has a real edge, and it is granted its due: a paper should not be retracted merely because its conclusions are unpopular. But that is not what the notice says. It names a specific, checkable evidential defect — dates with no anthropogenic anchor — and a defect of that kind is a factual error, not a difference of opinion.8

The proponent's overreach

The popular version, carried to a mass audience by Graham Hancock in Ancient Apocalypse, presses the case well past what even the survey claimed — treating the buried-layer geophysics as settled monumental architecture and the retraction as persecution of a suppressed truth. Two things are worth keeping straight. First, the closeness of the collaboration: Hancock's involvement with Gunung Padang predates both the Netflix series and the paper, and the retracted article thanks him in its acknowledgements for reading the manuscript — so this is not an independent scientific finding that a popularizer later seized, but a claim developed inside a shared program. Second, "the establishment retracted it, therefore it is being censored" is not an argument; it is the mirror image of "the establishment accepts it, therefore it is true," and this wiki rejects both. The honest handle is the one the notice itself supplies: the dates have no anthropogenic association. Where a redating rests on a single assumption-laden reading, that opening is taken seriously here — but at Gunung Padang the assumption (that deep old soil dates a deep old building) is not merely unproven; it is the exact thing the survey needed to show and did not.9

Assessment

Weak — as the claim that Gunung Padang is a buried man-made pyramid, and weaker still, and on the ground the retraction names, as the claim that its construction reaches ~25,000 BP.

The concessions are made in full, because a fair hearing requires them: the hill has real internal structure that the geophysics detected; the terrace columns were genuinely gathered and stacked by people; and a paper should not be retracted for reaching an unwelcome conclusion. But the deep-antiquity reading fails at the one joint everything hangs on. Radiocarbon dates the sample, not the act, and here the old dates were taken from soil that the survey never tied to any demonstrably built feature — so they date the natural aging of a volcanic hill, not the raising of a monument. That is the specific, checkable error the editors identified, and it is why the case does not survive: not because a lost Ice-Age civilization is impossible (this reconstruction does not rule such things out a priori), but because the evidence offered for one here dates the wrong thing.

What is left standing is worth stating plainly, so the verdict is not mistaken for a blanket dismissal of the site. Gunung Padang is a genuine and remarkable feat of late-prehistoric labour — one of the great stepped sanctuaries of the ancient Indonesian world, built from a natural hill and its own natural stone within roughly the last two millennia. That is a smaller and truer thing than the oldest pyramid on earth, and it is what the associated evidence actually supports. The buried-pyramid claim is the reconstruction's premise applied where the material record will not carry it — a place where the honest result of the exercise is to say so.
