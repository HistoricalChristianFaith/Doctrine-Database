Does Andean "megalithic-below, crude-above" masonry prove the fine work predates the Inca?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — as the claim that a recurring superposition across the great Peruvian sites — superb mortarless polygonal megalithic work on the bottom, cruder small-stone-and-clay-mortar masonry on top of it — is a relative-dating signature proving the fine phase predates the historical Inca, who merely inherited and patched it. Unlike the single-site cases, this is a purely dating thesis: not "this one wall is too fine to make," but "this pattern, wherever it appears, sorts two builders in time." The observation is granted in full — the two masonry styles are real, and where they meet the fine work generally is older than the rough work laid on it. What fails is the inference. The "crude" style is a documented Inca masonry register (pirca, fieldstone and mud mortar), deployed by the same builders for lower-status and utilitarian work; the contrast is functional and status grading within one culture (and often one interrupted building campaign), not a stratigraphy of two civilizations. The chroniclers who saw Cusco within a generation of the conquest credit named Inca kings with the fine walls, and radiocarbon anchors these sites to the fifteenth century. The one oral-tradition datum the thesis leans on — natives telling the Spanish the stonework "was already there" — is real but belongs to Tiwanaku in Bolivia, which genuinely is pre-Inca; it is misapplied to the Cusco-region sites, where the record runs the other way. A real kernel survives — pre-Inca cultures existed and some fabric was genuinely reused — but as a universal signature the thesis overreaches · advanced chiefly by Derek Olson (Megalithic Marvels)

Related arguments: out-of-place artifacts (dates under skepticism) — see also Sacsayhuamán's polygonal walls · Ollantaytambo's monoliths · Puma Punku's andesite H-blocks · whether this reconstruction's witnesses are securely dated

The claim

Derek Olson, who writes and leads tours as Megalithic Marvels, has made one observation his signature, and it is a better one than most in this literature because it is about a pattern rather than a single wonder. Walk the great Cusco-region sites — Sacsayhuamán, Coricancha, Ollantaytambo, Machu Picchu, the Inti Punku sun gate above it, Tipón — and again and again, he says, one sees the same three-deck sandwich: at the base, "the megalithic megaton mortarless stonework"; above it, "the small rough stone & clay mortar style"; and above that, where present, Spanish colonial work. From the superposition he draws a relative-dating inference in the form of two leading questions: "Wouldn't this mean that the inferior style was added at a later date?" and "If the Inca constructed both styles as the mainstream narrative states, why wouldn't they have just created everything out of the stronger & superior mortarless style?" On his second essay the megalithic phase is "something much more prehistoric and superior" than "the 550+ year old Inca walls and dwellings made of rough stone and clay mortar," and at Ollantaytambo the trapezoidal megalithic work "not only dwarfs, but stands out in stark contrast to the inferior rough stone and clay mortar Inca construction that surrounds it."1

The conclusion is that the two styles are two builders. The fine mortarless megalithic phase is pre-Inca; the historical Inca, whose fingerprint Olson takes to be the crude mortared style, inherited the great walls and are responsible only for the rough patching around and above them — a reading that, on this reconstruction's premise, opens a road back toward a lost, possibly prediluvian builder. The argument is buttressed by two further supports: an oral-tradition datum — the Inca reportedly told the Spanish the stonework "was already there" when they arrived — and the tool-hardness premise, that bronze-and-stone-age tools cannot dress granite and andesite to this finish.2

The observation, conceded

The starting point is real, and it is worth saying so plainly before it is taken apart. Two distinct masonry styles do occur at these sites; where they physically meet, the fine mortarless work generally is the earlier of the two, with the rough mortared work laid on and around it; and a viewer with a good eye will see the contrast at once. Olson has not invented a pattern. The Spanish themselves dismantled Inca ashlar to build colonial Cusco — the cathedral was raised from Sacsayhuamán's stone — so that later, cruder fabric sitting on and among the fine walls is exactly what four centuries of reoccupation, spoliation, and repair would leave. The question is not whether a temporal succession exists in the masonry. It is whether that succession sorts two cultures, one of them lost, or one culture's own registers and the centuries that came after it.3

The official dates, and the methods behind them

All the sites in Olson's list are attributed to the imperial Inca of the fifteenth century, and the attribution rests on two independent kinds of evidence rather than on style alone. The first is documentary: Spanish and Inca-informed chroniclers who saw Cusco within a generation of the conquest describe the Inca building these very walls. Pedro Sancho, Pizarro's secretary, saw Sacsayhuamán in 1534, before the great siege; Cieza de León recorded the fortress raised under the emperors by tens of thousands of drafted workers; and the Inca-descended Garcilaso de la Vega gives the succession of kings — Pachacuti begun, Túpac Yupanqui continued, Huayna Cápac unfinished at the last. The second is radiometric where it can reach: the site's own archaeology, and at Machu Picchu a run of AMS radiocarbon dates on human remains that place the estate in occupation from about AD 1420 to 1532 — if anything a couple of decades earlier than the textual date of 1438, and still squarely within known Inca history. The one recent redating of a site on Olson's list moved it by decades, and toward the Inca, not away.4

What would have to hold true for the claim

The thesis is a single inference — superposition entails culture-succession — and it stands only if three things hold.

(a) The rough mortared style is diagnostic of the Inca and foreign to the megalith-builders. The whole sorting depends on reading "small rough stone & clay mortar" as the Inca signature, so that its presence marks Inca work and its absence marks the older hand. If instead that style is one register among several used by a single culture, the sandwich sorts nothing.

(b) "Finer below, cruder above" means "earlier culture, then later culture," not "graded work within one campaign." A single builder who puts massive dressed blocks in a load-bearing foundation or a prestige facade and cheaper rubble in fill, terraces, and upper walls would leave the same vertical contrast. The reading requires that this ordinary functional grading be excluded.

(c) The megalith-builders left no other trace, and the surviving testimony favors their priority. A lost pre-Inca civilization capable of the finest stonework in the Andes should register somewhere beyond the walls themselves — in a material culture, in the chroniclers, in the natives' own memory — and that testimony should credit it rather than the Inca.

Adversarial assessment: do the conditions hold here?

The "crude" style is a documented Inca register. Condition (a) is where the thesis breaks, and it breaks on the mainstream description of Inca masonry itself. Inca building was never one style but a graded set of them, classified since John Rowe's 1946 survey and subdivided by Santiago Agurto into registers deployed by function and status: fine cellular and ashlar polygonal work "used on important buildings or perimeter walls," coursed and rougher work "employed mostly on terrace walls and river canalization," and at the base of the hierarchy pirca — fieldstone set in mud mortar — for terraces, storehouses, and the homes of commoners. Most Inca construction, in fact, was fieldstone and adobe; the mortarless ashlar Olson treats as a lost art was the minority style, reserved for the most sacred and elite structures, the Coricancha and the Sun Temple at Machu Picchu among them. So the "small rough stone & clay mortar style" is not a rival builder's fingerprint at all. It is pirca, a recognized Inca register, made by the same people who cut the ashlar, for the parts of a site where prestige masonry was not worth the labour. The contrast Olson reads vertically as two ages is the Inca's own horizontal division of labour by status and use.5

The superposition is one graded — and often interrupted — campaign, plus the centuries after. Condition (b) fails on the ground, and the cleanest proof is a site caught in the act. At Ollantaytambo, whose Temple Hill was abandoned mid-construction, the "two styles" are visibly one unfinished Inca project: fine prestige masonry with rough fieldstone-and-mortar work laid on and around it, tired stones stranded on the haul ramp, lifting bosses still on the blocks, and blocks prised from finished walls for reuse in a remodelling still under way. No lost civilization trails a kilometres-long litter of half-delivered dressed blocks. And where the rough overlay is genuinely later rather than merely cheaper, it is later by the mundane route the reconstruction can watch happening: post-conquest reoccupation and repair, and the Spanish quarrying of Inca ashlar for colonial Cusco. Olson's eye correctly perceives a temporal succession in the fabric; his error is to read the bottom of it as a vanished culture rather than as the prestige tier of the same one.6

The oral-tradition datum is real — but it is Tiwanaku's, and there it is correct. The "it was already there" testimony has a genuine source, and tracing it is decisive. It is Cieza de León's, and it is about Tiahuanaco in highland Bolivia, not the Cusco megaliths: "I asked the natives whether these edifices were built in the time of the Inca. They laughed at the question, affirming that they were made long before the Inca reign… that they had heard from their forebears that everything to be seen there appeared suddenly in the course of a single night." The sentence is a staple of the alternative-history canon by way of Graham Hancock's Fingerprints of the Gods, and the inference the thesis draws from it is exactly backwards as a general rule: Tiwanaku genuinely is pre-Inca, dated archaeologically to roughly 200 BC–AD 1000, so here the natives correctly disclaimed authorship of a site their ancestors had not built. That is precisely why the datum does not transfer to Cusco. For Sacsayhuamán, Coricancha, and Ollantaytambo the same chroniclers record the opposite — the Inca themselves as builders, by name and by drafted labour. A correct pre-Inca disclaimer about one genuinely older site has been generalized into a false universal signature covering sites the record assigns to the Inca.7

The tool-hardness prop is already spent. The buttress that the finish needs a lost tool is answered on the manufacturing pages and only summarized here, because it is a separate sub-claim: Jean-Pierre Protzen reproduced the fitted faces and joints by pounding andesite and porphyry with river-cobble hammerstones and grinding with sand. The premise that the hammer must be harder than the rock is itself a mistake — the operative property is toughness, resistance to cracking, and a quartzite cobble of roughly equal hardness but greater toughness flakes the andesite while surviving the blow. The residual honest caveat — that Protzen did not reproduce the very hardest stone (Ollantaytambo's rhyolite) or the heaviest blocks at full scale — is a limit on the demonstration's completeness, not a lost machine, and it bears on making, not dating. For the making question in full, see the Sacsayhuamán, Ollantaytambo, and Puma Punku pages.8

The kernel that survives

Condition (c) is where the thesis keeps a foothold, and the honesty guard requires that it be conceded exactly as far as the evidence carries it and no further. Pre-Inca cultures are real and are sometimes genuinely underneath. At Cusco and Sacsayhuamán the Killke culture (c. AD 900–1200) preceded the Inca, left large pottery deposits beneath the imperial structures, and did the earliest terracing of the hill; one former director of Peru's culture institute, Luis Lumbreras, has read radiocarbon as showing Killke construction of the fortress itself, though this is a minority reading — the mainstream view keeps the monumental polygonal walls Inca and the Killke layer an occupation. Tiwanaku in Bolivia really is centuries pre-Inca; Tipón's locale was occupied by Ayarmaca and Wari predecessors; and the Inca themselves acknowledged a debt to Tiwanaku's older cut stone, by tradition resettling its stoneworkers. Reuse of earlier fabric therefore does happen. So the thesis is true in specific, localized cases — the Inca sometimes did inherit older fabric — and false as the sweeping stratigraphic law Olson makes of it. That is the honest shape of the result: a real observation, a real kernel, and an overgeneralization laid over both.9

Assessment

Weak — as the claim that the "megalithic-below, crude-above" succession is a relative-dating signature proving the fine Andean megaliths predate the Inca.

The concessions are owed and are made in full. The pattern is real; where the two styles meet, the fine work generally is the earlier; pre-Inca cultures existed and were sometimes physically beneath; and the oral-tradition disclaimer the thesis leans on is, at its true address, correct. Given a page that took only the strongest facts and ignored their distribution, the argument would look formidable.

What defeats it is that every load-bearing step reads a single-culture, well-documented situation as a two-culture one. The "crude" style is pirca, a recognized Inca register used by function and status, not a foreign fingerprint; the vertical contrast is the Inca's own grading of labour, plus post-conquest reoccupation and spoliation, seen frozen mid-process at unfinished Ollantaytambo; the chroniclers who saw Cusco within a generation credit named Inca kings; radiocarbon anchors the sites to the fifteenth century, and the one recent redating moved toward the Inca by decades; and the "it was already there" testimony belongs to Tiwanaku, where it is true, not to the Cusco sites, where it is contradicted. The tool-hardness buttress is already spent on the manufacturing pages, and in any case concerns making, not dating.

The residue is genuine and small, and it is the reason the verdict is weak rather than unsupported: pre-Inca fabric is really reused in specific places, so the thesis captures something true whenever it points at a site that is in fact older. As a universal law of Andean masonry it fails; as a local observation about particular walls it occasionally lands, by accident of pointing at the right stone. The colonial pedigree of the larger frame is its own tell — the notion that a vanished race raised Peru's cyclopean walls is not an Andean memory but a European graft, argued as early as 1656 by Antonio de León Pinelo, who held such works must be the Nephilim's, built before Noah's Flood.10
