Did the giant lineage survive the Flood aboard the ark?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — the reading answers a real question and it has an ancient pedigree of a sort, since the rabbis did imagine a giant coming through the Flood by way of the ark. But the modern form, on which one of the sons' wives carried a dormant Nephilim inheritance, rests on a carrier the passenger list does not contain, on a reading of "perfect in his generations" as genetic purity that the term will not bear, and on a rabbinic pedigree that does not check out at the places it is usually cited. Its genetic component is the one part that could have been tested, and the record does not show it.

Related arguments: the post-Flood Nephilim problem — see also a second angelic incursion after the Flood · the giants as a warrior category rather than a lineage

The claim

Genesis 7:13 names the ark's passengers exhaustively: "In the selfsame day entered Noah, and Shem, and Ham, and Japheth, the sons of Noah, and Noah's wife, and the three wives of his sons with them, into the ark."1 Eight people, and no Nephilim among them. The interpretation assessed here accepts that count and locates the giant inheritance inside it. Noah is called "a just man and perfect in his generations" (Gen 6:9); his sons' wives are given no such description. So — the argument runs — one of the daughters-in-law, most often Ham's, was of Nephilim stock, and carried the line through the Flood in her own body, which is why the giant clans reappear precisely in the lands of Ham's son Canaan. The modern version adds a genetic mechanism: the inheritance was recessive or dormant, unexpressed in the immediate generation and reactivated later.

Who advances it

Its most developed statement is by Richard B. Sorensen, whose paper "Was Ham's Wife of the Nephilim?" was later reissued as "How did the Nephilim Reappear After the Flood?" (2020): Ham's wife was born of Nephilim stock and passed her inheritance to her children, above all to Canaan, and thence to the Canaanite giant clans; the framing is that the adversary, having failed to corrupt the messianic line, succeeded in this collateral one. The paper has drawn a published rebuttal from Ken Ammi, who argues that Sorensen imports into the Nephilim a set of physical and behavioural characteristics — great size, great strength, belligerence, sexual aggression — that the biblical text does not actually assign them.2 A genetic variant circulates in the popular prophecy literature, where Laura Sanger traces a Nephilim inheritance forward through the line of Ham and Ryan Pitterson runs the same descent, the mechanism being an inheritance carried dormant through the ark and expressed later.3 Michael Heiser lists the option in The Unseen Realm — "the 'bad seed' was carried through the flood in Noah's bloodline (either him or one of his sons)" — and declines it, preferring a second incursion.4

The evidence for

The mechanism is ancient, even if this particular carrier is not. The strongest thing the reading has going for it is that the idea of the giant line coming through the Flood by way of the ark is not a modern invention. Rabbinic tradition tells it about Og of Bashan: Pirqei de-Rabbi Eliezer 23 has Og sitting on a piece of wood under the gutter of the ark, swearing to Noah and his sons that he would be their servant forever, with Noah boring an aperture to pass him food each day, and reads Deuteronomy 3:11 — "For only Og, king of Bashan, remained of the remnant of the giants" — as the proof. Targum Pseudo-Jonathan on Genesis 14:13 says the same: Og "had been spared from the giants that died in the deluge, and had ridden protected upon the top of the ark, and sustained with food by Noah," preserved not for his righteousness but so that the world might see the power of God and remember that giants once rebelled.5 So the intuition that the ark is where the continuity has to run is genuinely old, and it is drawn from the same texts.

And it takes the Flood's totality seriously. Unlike the local-flood option, this reading does not soften the judgement to make room for survivors, and unlike the second-incursion reading it does not require an event the narrative never reports. Everything it needs happens inside a boat the text has already told us about.

The silence about the wives is real. Genesis says nothing whatever about the three daughters-in-law beyond their presence. The argument's observation that the text certifies Noah and not them is, as an observation about the text, correct.

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The problem is real, the ark is the obvious place to look for continuity, and the tradition did look there. That the wives go undescribed is a genuine gap. None of this is fabricated.

"Perfect in his generations" is a moral term, and this is where the reading breaks. The whole weight of the carrier hypothesis rests on reading tamim be-dorotav in Genesis 6:9 as a certificate of unmixed descent, so that its absence for the wives leaves a genetic opening. That is not what the phrase does. Tamim is the standard word for moral and cultic blamelessness — it is what God requires of Abraham in Genesis 17:1 — and the ancient commentators read Genesis 6:9 accordingly, with the qualifier "in his generations" understood as measuring Noah against his contemporaries, not as certifying his chromosomes. Jerome: "Specifically, it says 'in his generation' to show that his righteousness was not according to the perfected righteousness, but according to the righteousness of his generation." Augustine: Noah "was perfect as far as citizens of the city of God can be perfect during the pilgrimage of this present life, not, of course, as perfect as they are to be in that immortal life." Bede says the same at length. Three independent readers of the verse, working in different languages and centuries, all take the phrase as a comparative moral judgement; none takes it as a pedigree.6 Once the phrase means what they say it means, the "opening" left by the wives' silence is not an opening at all — the text simply does not certify or decertify anyone's ancestry, including Noah's.

The rabbinic pedigree usually offered for it does not check out. Surveys of the question routinely attribute the theory to Genesis Rabbah 26:7 ("the corruption came into the ark through Ham's wife") and to Rashi on Genesis 6. Both attributions fail on inspection. Genesis Rabbah 26:7 is a comment on Genesis 6:4 that gives the seven names of the giants — Eimim, Refa'im, Giborim, Zamzumim, Anakim, Avim, Nefilim — with etymologies, and says nothing about the ark or about Ham's wife. Rashi on Genesis 6:4 derives nephilim from their falling and their felling of the world, and glosses "and also after that" as the flood-generation's failure to learn from an earlier partial inundation in the days of Enosh — a comment that keeps the whole notice before the Flood and never mentions the wives.7 The corruption-in-the-ark motif that does exist in Genesis Rabbah is at 36:7, and it is a different claim in a different direction: Ham and the dog broke the ark's continence, which is offered as the reason Ham "emerged darkened" — a story about sexual misconduct aboard, not about a giant bloodline carried aboard.8 The genuine ancient parallel is the Og legend, and it is a different mechanism: a giant clinging to the outside of the hull, not a giant's descendant inside the family, and it is told frankly as legend rather than as exegesis of the passenger list.

It defeats the stated purpose of the judgement, and its answer to that is a concession. On the supernaturalist reading the reading assumes, the corrupted line is among the reasons for the Flood. If the corruption walked up the gangplank, the judgement did not accomplish what it was for. Defenders answer that the Flood was a reset preserving one clean line rather than an eradication — but that answer concedes the point at issue, since the line preserved is precisely the one alleged to be unclean.

The Canaan link is a coincidence of geography doing work it cannot do. That the giant clans turn up in the land of Ham's son Canaan is the argument's most rhetorically effective move and its least evidential one. The Table of Nations assigns Canaan to Ham for reasons internal to its own political geography, and the conquest narratives are set in Canaan because that is the land promised — so the "match" is guaranteed by the story's structure whatever anyone's ancestry. Deuteronomy 2 undercuts it further: the same catalogue of giant peoples places the Emim in Moab and the Zamzummim in Ammon, which are Lot's line, not Ham's, and the Horim in Seir, which is Esau's.9 The distribution does not track the alleged carrier.

And the genetic version makes the one testable claim in the set. "Dormant genes reactivated" is a mechanism with consequences. A recessive inheritance seeded in one of three founding couples and re-expressed later predicts sporadic outsized individuals scattered through all the descendant populations, not tidy giant clans localised in the Levant; and it predicts a measurable stature signal in the skeletal record where those clans lived. The record does not show it. A modelled corpus of some 6,098 measured prehistoric skeletons from the Near East and Europe yields an ordinary human stature surface with no outlier population, and the Ashkelon cemetery — the roster's most locatable claim, in the very territory to which Joshua 11:22 confines the surviving Anakim — gives a mean male stature of 154.6 cm. Authenticated gigantism exists but is invariably an endocrine pathology in a single individual, never a heritable population trait.10 The argument's own chosen mechanism is the place it is checkable, and it fails there.

A note on method. None of this turns on holding that God could not have permitted such a thing. The reading fails on ordinary evidential grounds: it needs a carrier the text does not supply, a reading of tamim the word does not support, a rabbinic warrant that is not in the sources cited, and a genetic signature that is not in the ground.

Assessment

Weak — not unsupported. Something real is being reached for: the post-Flood giants need an explanation, the ark is the only channel between the two worlds, and the tradition itself reached for that channel when it told the Og legend. A reading with an ancient analogue is not nothing.

But the modern form fails at every joint it needs. Genesis 7:13 names the eight and gives no carrier; "perfect in his generations" is a moral comparative, as Jerome, Augustine and Bede all read it, and cannot be made to certify a pedigree whose absence creates the alleged opening; the rabbinic warrant most often cited for the theory is not at Genesis Rabbah 26:7 or in Rashi, and the genuine parallel in the sources is a different mechanism told as legend; the Canaan geography is supplied by the narrative's own structure and is contradicted by Deuteronomy 2's placement of the same clans in Moab, Ammon and Seir; and the dormant-gene version predicts a stature signal that 6,098 measured skeletons and the Ashkelon cemetery do not contain. The theory is genuinely advocated, which is why it is rated here rather than ignored. It does not hold on its best available case.
