A recent single global Flood predicts a synchronous worldwide sediment horizon — is there one?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — as a prediction of the young single-date global model. The prediction is a good one: it is risky, it is stated in advance, and it is checkable by a method that demonstrably works, since the one genuinely global event geologists have looked for in this way — the end-Cretaceous impact — was found exactly as predicted, as a thin synchronous layer traceable on every continent. The Flood horizon has been looked for with the same tools and is not there. What is there instead is the strongest form the argument takes: continent-scale sediment packages, bounded by unconformities, that really do record water advancing across cratons — and that the flood model reads as the Deluge's surges. Those packages are real; their synchrony across continents, and above all their compression into a single year, is what fails. The failure is scoped: it falsifies a young single-date global deluge, not the claim that a flood tradition has a historical kernel.

The prediction

Grant the premise this timeline grants: a Flood happened. Now add the specific model that makes the sharpest prediction — a single deluge, global in extent, within the last several thousand years, which destroyed the land life of the earth and then drained. Such an event moves an enormous quantity of sediment and drops it. It should therefore have left one contemporaneous deposit, recognisable on every continent, occupying the same position in every sequence: a worldwide horizon with the same event on both sides of it — a world before, a world after.

This is a genuinely risky prediction, and it is the reason this page exists. It is not a vague expectation that "there should be lots of sedimentary rock." There are enormous quantities of sedimentary rock, and no model is distinguished by pointing at it. The prediction has teeth because it specifies synchrony: not many flood deposits at many dates, which is what a planet with rivers produces anyway, but one deposit at one date everywhere.

What would falsify it — committed in advance

The prediction fails if the sedimentary record turns out to be a set of local sequences deposited at many different times, correlated to each other by fossils, radiometric dates and magnetic reversals rather than by a shared event surface, and containing positive features that require long quiet intervals interleaved with deposition. It fails specifically if there are formations that cannot be produced quickly, sitting in the middle of the interval the Flood is supposed to occupy.

It would be confirmed — and this is the part that makes the test fair — if a single thin, chemically or mineralogically distinctive layer could be traced worldwide at the same stratigraphic position. That is not an impossible standard invented to be failed. It is precisely what Luis and Walter Alvarez and their colleagues found in 1980 when they measured iridium across the Cretaceous–Tertiary boundary in Italy, Denmark and New Zealand and got anomalies of roughly 30, 160 and 20 times background at exactly the same horizon, subsequently traced worldwide and matched to the Chicxulub crater. A single global catastrophe leaves a single global layer, the layer is findable, and the method that finds it is in routine use.1

The strongest case for

The megasequences are real, and they are the best evidence the model has. In 1963 Laurence Sloss divided the North American sedimentary cover into six great packages — Sauk, Tippecanoe, Kaskaskia, Absaroka, Zuni, Tejas — each hundreds of feet thick, each bounded above and below by an unconformity, and each recording seawater advancing across the continental interior and then withdrawing. This is mainstream stratigraphy, not a creationist construct, and it does describe water covering continental interiors on a scale no modern flood approaches. Since 2013 Timothy Clarey's Column Project at the Institute for Creation Research has extended the mapping using oil-well logs, outcrops and seismic data on several continents, and reads all six megasequences as successive surges of the Flood — the Sauk its beginning, the top of the Tejas its end.2

And the model has done real work against a specific counter-argument. The Coconino Sandstone of the Grand Canyon was for decades the textbook demonstration that at least one thick unit in the canyon's sequence is a fossil desert — wind-blown dunes, impossible under water, sitting in the middle of what the Flood must have deposited in weeks. John Whitmore and Paul Garner have contested that reading in detail: cross-bed dips averaging near 20° rather than the 30-plus of desert dunes, poor to moderate sorting with subangular grains, surviving mica flakes that wind transport tends to destroy, and sand injectites at the base. Whether or not the reinterpretation carries — and mainstream responses have been sharp, Lorence Collins arguing that an average dip taken across two hundred sites is not a meaningful statistic — the exchange shows the position engaging its hardest counter-evidence rather than avoiding it.3

Adversarial assessment

What holds. Continental flooding on a scale unlike anything modern is a genuine feature of the record; the megasequence packages exist and are bounded by unconformities; the late Cretaceous really was a maximum of continental inundation. Anyone answering the argument by denying that seas have repeatedly covered continental interiors is answering something else.

The packages are not synchronous, and that is the whole prediction. The megasequences are separated by unconformities that are the model's evidence and its problem at once. Higher-order sequences nested inside them show sea level rising and falling many times over, with the predictable internal development — transgressive lags, maximum flooding surfaces, prograding wedges — that sequence stratigraphy was built to describe and that petroleum geology depends on to find hydrocarbons. A model that reads the whole stack as one year's surges has to treat every one of those internal cycles as noise. And the boundaries themselves do not line up between continents: they are correlated by biostratigraphy and radiometric dating, which is to say by the very framework the model rejects. Reading a Sauk in Africa as the same event as a Sauk in North America requires the correlation tools whose results are being denied.4

Formations exist that require long quiet intervals in the middle of the supposed Flood year. This is the decisive class of evidence, because it is positive rather than merely absent. At Joggins in Nova Scotia, upright fossil tree trunks stand rooted in dozens of stacked horizons: each is a forest floor that grew, was buried, and was grown over again, and stacking them takes as many growth intervals as there are horizons. Reef complexes are built organism by organism at measurable rates. Thick evaporite sequences require repeated evaporation of standing water, not its arrival. Varved lake sediments preserve annual couplets in counts far exceeding a few thousand. Each of these sits inside the interval the Flood is asked to have deposited, and each requires the water to have been absent, or still, for a long time at that exact point in the sequence.5

The Coconino dispute, even resolved generously, does not reach the prediction. Suppose Whitmore and Garner are right that the unit is subaqueous. That removes one obstacle; it supplies no synchronous global horizon. And the intra-creationist literature has itself questioned whether the dip-angle criterion carries the weight placed on it. Winning a local depositional argument is not the same as passing the test the model set for itself.3

The strongest single datum is the one that runs continuously through the interval. The EPICA Dome C ice core preserves unbroken layered accumulation through eight glacial cycles — some 740,000 years of annual and seasonal layering, undisturbed. It is not merely that no flood layer appears in it; it is that a global deluge within the last several thousand years would have had to leave the top of that column intact while covering the ground it stands on.6

A note on method, since it matters here more than anywhere on this timeline. None of the above rests on ruling miracles out. The objection is narrower and harder than that: the model itself predicts a particular physical trace, the search for that trace is the model's own proposed test, and the trace is absent while a different and incompatible pattern is present. An appeal to miracle at this point does not rescue the prediction — it withdraws it. A Flood that leaves no signature because God arranged that it leave none is not making the claim this argument was advanced to make, and cannot be credited with the evidence it then declines to produce. Possibility is not proof, and a prediction that cannot fail is not a prediction.

What the failure does and does not touch. It falsifies the young single-date global model — a worldwide deluge within the last several thousand years leaving one deposit. It does not touch a local or regional catastrophic flood in Mesopotamia; the floodplain evidence for those is real and is weighed elsewhere on this timeline. It does not touch the antiquity of the flood tradition, which is secured by the cuneiform record independently of any geology. And it does not touch the theological claim of a divine judgement in history, which this page has no instruments to weigh.

Assessment

Weak — as a prediction of the young single-date global model. The prediction was well framed and the test is a fair one, run with tools that have found exactly this kind of horizon when it existed: the end-Cretaceous iridium anomaly is a synchronous worldwide layer, it was predicted, looked for, and found. The Flood horizon is not there. What the record holds instead is many local sequences correlated by fossils, isotopes and magnetic reversals, carrying positive markers of long quiet intervals — rooted forest floors stacked dozens deep at Joggins, reefs, evaporites, varves, and 740,000 years of undisturbed ice layering at Dome C — distributed through the very interval a single Flood year would have to occupy.

The honest credit to the argument is the megasequences: continent-scale packages bounded by unconformities, recording water advancing over cratons, are a real large-scale pattern and the model is not inventing them. What the model cannot get from them is synchrony between continents or compression into one year, and those are the two things the prediction required. The failure is confined to the model that made it. It says nothing against a historical flood remembered in Mesopotamia, and this timeline continues to weigh that smaller claim on its own evidence.
