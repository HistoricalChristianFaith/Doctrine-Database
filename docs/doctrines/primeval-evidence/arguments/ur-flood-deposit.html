Is Woolley's Ur "flood deposit" the biblical Flood?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — the silt band at Ur is a real, thick, water-laid stratum, but the Mesopotamian flood layers are not one event: Ur's dates to c. 3500 BC, Shuruppak's to c. 2950–2850 BC, Kish has three at different centuries, and Eridu seven miles from Ur has none at all, which is the signature of a floodplain flooding repeatedly rather than of a single deluge.

Related arguments: corroborating the Flood — see also the flood corpus as corroboration · the Black Sea deluge as a kernel · the King List's antediluvian reigns · Göbekli Tepe and a Flood horizon

The claim

In 1929, digging below the Royal Cemetery at Ur, Leonard Woolley's workmen cut through some three metres of clean, artefact-free water-laid silt at a depth of about twelve — and then struck occupation debris again beneath it. A sterile band that thick, sealed between two levels of human life, meant an inundation deep enough to end a world and long enough to resettle a river's whole load. Woolley said so, and the wires carried it around the planet as the discovery of Noah's Flood.1 The claim assessed here is the one the public took away and that still circulates: the Ur silt is the physical signature of the Flood of Genesis 6–9.

Who advances it

Woolley himself advanced a carefully hedged version — a catastrophic inundation of the Tigris–Euphrates valley, remembered as the end of an age; he wrote of it as a local flood, and elsewhere conceded that "one could scarcely argue for the Deluge on the strength of a pit a yard square."1 The unhedged version — Ur's silt is the biblical Flood — was supplied by the newspapers of 1929 and has been carried since in popular apologetic literature and in a long tail of documentaries. It also had a serious defender in the field: R. L. Raikes argued in 1966 for the physical reality and severity of the flooding against Mallowan's deflationary reading.2

The evidence for

The stratum is real and it is not small. Roughly eleven feet of clean silt with no cultural material in it, between occupation above and occupation below, is not an ordinary river deposit. Something drowned inhabited Ur deeply enough to bury it and long enough to bury it in still water.1

Ur is not alone. Langdon and Watelin had already found flood layers at Kish, and in 1931 Erich Schmidt found one at Shuruppak — which is not just another tell. Shuruppak is the flood hero's own city in Atra-ḫasīs, the home of Ubara-Tutu in Gilgamesh XI, and the last pre-flood seat of kingship in the Sumerian King List. A flood deposit at the one site the tradition names is precisely the kind of convergence an evidential argument is entitled to press.3

And the tradition's own date lands on it. Max Mallowan, once Woolley's assistant at Ur, reasoned from the King List's post-flood reign-lengths to a flood of Sumerian memory around 2900–2800 BC — and Shuruppak's layer, and the earliest at Kish, sit inside that window. So the deposits and the texts do meet: the tradition remembers a datable flood, and the ground at the named city shows one.4

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The Ur deposit is genuine; so are the Kish and Shuruppak layers; and severe, occasionally city-drowning flooding of the lower Tigris–Euphrates plain is an established fact of Mesopotamian archaeology. Anyone who treats the whole business as a nineteenth-century enthusiasm since discredited has the story wrong. The deposits are the best physical soil there is for a flood tradition.

But the layers do not line up, and the identification stands or falls on whether they do. This is the decisive datum. Ur's eleven feet dates to c. 3500 BC. Shuruppak's fifteen inches to c. 2950–2850 BC. Kish has three separate flood layers, the earliest c. 3000–2900 BC and another c. 2600 BC, each under ten inches. Six hundred years and more separate the two largest. A single deluge does not deposit silt at one city in the thirty-sixth century and at another in the thirtieth.5

The negative test was run, and it failed. Eridu lies seven miles from Ur. It was searched specifically for the Ur deposit and does not have it.5 A flood that covered the mountains did not spare a tell seven miles away. This is the cleanest kind of adverse evidence — a prediction the claim makes, checked at the obvious place, and absent.

Mallowan's date cuts against Woolley, not for him. The convergence noted above is real but it is a convergence on Shuruppak, not on Ur. If the flood the Sumerians remembered is c. 2900–2800 BC, Woolley's silt is some six centuries too early to be it — which is to say the argument's strongest chronological support is also the thing that retires its most famous exhibit.4

Thickness is not extent. Eleven feet of silt is a striking number and it does most of the rhetorical work here, but depth of deposit measures how much sediment a river dropped at one spot, not how much of the world was under water. A breached levee on an aggrading alluvial plain can bury a quarter of a city in a season and leave the next tell dry. On the scale the claim needs — a worldwide inundation — the deposits give nothing at all: no correlated horizon anywhere outside the southern floodplain, and none in the intervening ground.

Scope the failure honestly. What breaks here is the identification "Woolley's silt = the Flood," and with it any use of the Mesopotamian deposits as evidence of a global deluge. What is untouched is the milder claim that a flood tradition has a historical kernel — for which a floodplain that drowned its cities repeatedly, including the one city the tradition names, is exactly the right soil. The deposits are evidence of the right kind; they are evidence for a smaller proposition than the one advanced.

Assessment

Weak. The Ur stratum cannot be the Flood of Genesis, and the reason is arithmetic rather than interpretation: the Mesopotamian flood layers are non-synchronous — Ur c. 3500 BC, Shuruppak c. 2950–2850 BC, three at Kish spread across four centuries — and Eridu, seven miles from Ur, was searched for Woolley's deposit and has none. Those are the fingerprints of a floodplain flooding repeatedly at different places in different centuries, which is what the lower Tigris–Euphrates demonstrably did.

Two qualifications keep the verdict from doing more damage than it should. Woolley's own published claim was for a local catastrophe and was hedged; the global identification is largely a press artifact and should not be laid at his door. And the best chronological work on the question — Mallowan's, reasoning from the King List's own reign-lengths to c. 2900–2800 BC — does put a real flood at Shuruppak, the flood hero's city, at about the date the tradition itself implies. That is a genuine result and it belongs to the smaller claim: a severe, remembered, local flood behind a durable tradition. It is not Ur, and it is not the Flood as the argument means it.
