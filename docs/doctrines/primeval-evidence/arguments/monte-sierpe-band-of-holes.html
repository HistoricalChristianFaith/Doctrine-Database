Is Monte Sierpe (the "Band of Holes") a purpose-unknown feature of a lost antiquity?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — the feature is real, large, and deliberate, and its function genuinely was uncertain for the better part of a century, which is the true part of the claim. But the leap from "purpose unknown" to "therefore not Inca / a lost antiquity" fails on the one thing that never was in doubt: the band's date. It sits within a known Andean cultural horizon — Late Intermediate Period into the Inca Late Horizon (roughly AD 1000–1532) — fixed by surface ceramics, by a radiocarbon date of AD 1320–1405 from inside a hole, and by its position on the pre-Hispanic road between two Inca-era sites; and a 2025 drone-and-microbotanical study offers a datable, human, non-anomalous reading of what it was for (a local Indigenous system of accounting and exchange). The function question is not fully closed — marketplace versus tribute-accounting is debated and the khipu analogy has critics — but the attribution question, which is the one the lost-antiquity framing needs, is not open. Advanced by Brien Foerster (Hidden Inca Tours) and the wider ancient-astronaut / "unexplained" circuit.

The claim

On a dry ridge in the Pisco Valley of southern Peru, near the village of Humay and some thirty kilometres inland from the coast, thousands of pits are dug into the hillside in a continuous band that climbs the slope for about a kilometre and a half. The site is called Monte Sierpe, "serpent mountain." The recent drone survey counts roughly 5,200 holes — older popular accounts run the figure as high as six or seven thousand — each on the order of one to two metres across and half a metre to a metre deep, many stone-lined, laid out in a strip averaging some nineteen metres wide and broken into around sixty distinct blocks or segments separated by gaps. It first reached a wide audience as an aerial curiosity: the geologist Robert Shippee and the U.S. Navy pilot George Johnson photographed it from the air on their 1931 expedition, and National Geographic printed the image in 1933.1

The claim built on it is a "what is it?" argument rather than a manufacturing or precision claim — nobody supposes that digging holes required a lost technology. What is asserted is that the feature is anomalous: a purpose that no one can explain, on a scale and of a regularity that (on the strong reading) points past the historical Inca to a lost or pre-Inca antiquity, the same "vanished civilization" horizon this reconstruction is built to test. Brien Foerster's page puts the framing in one sentence:

the "Band Of Holes" is an ancient mysterious construction that academics can not explain.

— Hidden Inca Tours, "The Mysterious Band of Holes near Paracas, Peru". The ancient-astronaut literature goes further and supplies the maker outright — "the 'Band of Holes' was created by aliens" — and the general-interest "unexplained phenomena" outlets carry the puzzle as an open mystery.2

Who advances it

Monte Sierpe is a fixture of the Peruvian lost-history tour circuit rather than of a single scholarly thesis. Brien Foerster's Hidden Inca Tours features it among the Paracas-region "mysteries," and it circulates through the same ancient-aliens and "forbidden history" media that carry the Andean megalith claims weighed elsewhere in this group. The argument earns a page because the strong version is not silly at its root: the feature is genuinely large, genuinely deliberate, and its function genuinely resisted explanation for decades — a real puzzle, not a manufactured one. The overreach is in the second step, where "we did not know what it was for" is treated as "it cannot be the work of the people whose land, road, and pottery surround it."3

The steelman: the function really was long uncertain

The honest concession comes first, because it is substantial. Monte Sierpe is unique — there is no second site quite like it — and for the better part of a century no one could say with confidence what the holes were for. The proposals piled up without converging. Victor von Hagen's 1953 expedition took them for unused graves; John Hyslop, in his 1984 study of the Inka road system, floated storage — circular semi-subterranean silos of the kind found elsewhere on the south coast — explicitly "as a hypothesis for investigation"; others proposed defensive fighting-pits, water or fog collection, or agricultural use. None was demonstrated, and the site's very singularity kept the question open. A skeptic who pretends the function was always obvious is not being straight with the ground: it was not, and that genuine uncertainty is what gave the "unexplained mystery" framing its foothold.4

But the date and the maker were never the open question

The lost-antiquity reading needs the band to predate or stand apart from the historical Andean cultures — and this is exactly where the record is firm, and firm independently of what the holes were for. Monte Sierpe lies on the pre-Hispanic road network, between the Inca provincial centre of Tambo Colorado and the site of Lima La Vieja; the pottery on its surface is Late Intermediate Period and Late Horizon; and charcoal recovered from inside one of the holes returned a radiocarbon date of 635 ± 15 BP, calibrating to AD 1320–1405. The construction is placed at least to the Late Intermediate Period (AD 1000–1400), with continued use by the Inca (AD 1400–1532). That is a well-populated, well-dated stretch of Andean history — the coastal Chincha Kingdom and then the Inca state — not a horizon before recorded culture. The mechanisms that can genuinely relocate a monument's date have no purchase here: there is no earlier structure to appropriate, no re-cut inscription, no reused footings — just holes dug into a hillside, dated by their own contents and their own surroundings.5

What the holes were for: a datable, human answer

The uncertainty that fed the mystery has, moreover, begun to resolve toward an ordinary reading rather than an exotic one. A 2025 study in Antiquity by Jacob Bongers, Charles Stanish and colleagues brought drone photogrammetry and microbotanical analysis to the band and argued that it "functioned initially as a barter marketplace under the Chincha Kingdom and ultimately became a place to receive and distribute tribute and other goods within the Inca state" — in their summary, "a local, Indigenous system of accounting and exchange." Two lines of new evidence carry the reading. First, sediment from the holes preserved pollen of domesticated crops — maize, cotton, chili pepper, sweet potato — and a near-ubiquitous signal of Typha reed, consistent with goods and the woven baskets that carried them having been placed in the pits, not with graves or defensive works. Second, the roughly sixty-segment layout, with its regular internal runs of rows, structurally echoes an Inca knotted-string record (a khipu) excavated near Pisco, whose cords fall into similarly sized groups — the lead author's memorable gloss was an "Excel spreadsheet" for the Inca state, a pre-Inca "flea market" before it. The point for this page is not that the accounting interpretation is certain; it is that the best current evidence points to a datable, human, administrative function inside a known culture — the opposite of a purpose no one can explain.6

What is genuinely open — and what is not

Two honest limits belong on the mainstream side, and neither rescues the lost-antiquity claim. The specific function is not settled: the archaeologist Dennis Ogburn cautions that a marketplace and an accounting installation are "two very different" things and that the hole-to-khipu correspondence is still "tenuous," and the study itself argues a sequence (marketplace, then tribute) rather than proving a single use. And precisely who first dug the band — the Chincha attribution is inferred from context, not fixed by an inscription — remains less than certain. But both of these are disputes within the Andean historical frame, about which known culture and which mundane purpose; neither reopens the attribution the argument actually needs. Function-uncertainty is not maker-uncertainty: a feature can be a real puzzle as to its use and still be securely the work of the people whose road it lies on and whose pottery lies on top of it. The proponents' own field acknowledges the pull of the mystery — Stanish notes the site "has long been prominent in the pseudo-archaeology world, with rampant speculation and mischaracterization of the data on the ground" — which is why the answer here is found on that ground, not waved away.7

Assessment

Weak. The true kernel is conceded in full: Monte Sierpe is a real, large, deliberate, and genuinely singular earthwork whose function was uncertain for the better part of a century, and pretending otherwise would be dishonest. But the argument the mystery is made to serve — that an unexplained purpose points past the Inca to a lost or pre-Inca antiquity — conflates two separate questions and fails on the one that matters. The band's date and cultural attribution were never the open question: surface ceramics, a radiocarbon date of AD 1320–1405 from inside a hole, and its place on the Inca-era road network seat it firmly in the Late Intermediate–Late Horizon coastal world (Chincha, then Inca), a horizon with no earlier structure to appropriate and nothing for the date-moving mechanisms to grip. And the function question, far from being unanswerable, has begun to resolve toward an ordinary human reading — a local system of accounting and exchange, supported by crop and reed pollen in the pits and by a layout echoing Inca knotted-string records — even as the precise use stays debated. Possibility is not proof on either side, and the open function does not license the closed conclusion the framing draws from it. Kept in this set because it is a clean case of the pattern: a genuine puzzle asked to carry a claim about who and when that its own dating flatly refuses.
