Is the Yonaguni Monument a man-made structure or a natural formation?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — as the strong claim that the submerged sandstone mass off Yonaguni is a deliberately quarried monument (a stepped pyramid, roads, temples, a stadium), the reading fails: the rectilinear terraces, sharp angles and "steps" are what bedded, orthogonally jointed sandstone produces on its own, the identical fracture pattern is visible in the open air on Yonaguni's coast a few hundred metres away, the "structures" are still attached to the living bedrock rather than cut into free blocks, and the region's heavy seismicity supplies the fracturing — while the alleged carvings read as natural scratches and the one real toehold (a row of quarry-wedge-like holes) is ambiguous and isolated. The modest reading — that this is a natural formation that ancient people used or lightly modified — is by contrast plausible, and is held by the lead skeptic himself (Robert Schoch calls it "over 95% natural," lightly touched-up). Note that the ~10,000-BP submergence age is not in dispute here; unlike the other dating-skepticism cases in this group, what is contested is not the age but what the rock is · advanced by the site's chief investigator, marine geologist Masaaki Kimura, and popularized by Graham Hancock

The claim

The object is real and genuinely striking, and the argument should be met at its strongest. In 1986 Kihachiro Aratake, a local diving operator scouting for hammerhead sharks, found off the southern tip of Yonaguni — the westernmost inhabited island of Japan, some 100 km east of Taiwan — a great terraced mass of rock rising from about 25 metres of water to within a few metres of the surface, roughly 100 metres long and some 25 metres tall. Its most-photographed face is a stack of broad flat terraces and sheer walls meeting in clean right angles, so that the whole reads at a glance as a stepped pyramid or a ruined amphitheatre. Marine geologist Masaaki Kimura, of the University of the Ryukyus, dived it repeatedly from the early 1990s and concluded it was human work.1

Kimura's is the case to steelman, and it should be stated at full strength, because he is a credentialed field geologist who changed his mind toward the artificial reading after diving the site — not a writer working from photographs. He describes "a complicated, monolithic, stepped pyramid that rises from a depth of 25 meters," and reads across the Ryukyu shelf a whole drowned complex: ten features off Yonaguni and five more near Okinawa, spanning some 300 by 150 metres, among them castle ruins, a triumphal arch, five temples, a stadium, roads, water channels and retaining walls. He points to specific worked details — a stone-paved "loop road" bounded by a straight wall, a "drainage" channel, holes he reads as pillar-sockets, two round holes and a straight row of smaller holes on the "Triangle Pool" that look like the abandoned wedge-line of an ancient quarry, and quarry marks he attributes to stone tools (a wedge, kusabi) found around the islands. He further reports "rudimentary characters etched onto carved faces" — including a sign he likens to the local Kaida script — and animal reliefs, an "underwater sphinx" resembling an Okinawan king, a formation he reads as a crude moai-like face, and a flat rock cut like a turtle. His summary defence is that the design "is too regular" to be the work of a fracture zone.2

On this reconstruction's premise the stakes are plain. The shelf that carries the monument stood above water at the end of the last Ice Age and drowned as the sea rose in the early Holocene; if the terraces were cut by human hands, they were cut before submergence, which places monumental architecture on the Ryukyu shelf around ten thousand years ago — before any recognized civilization anywhere, a lost-civilization data point of exactly the kind the premise is testing for. This is why Graham Hancock dives it and presents the artificial reading as all but established.3

Not a dating-skepticism case: the age is not what is in dispute

It matters to say at the outset what this argument is not. The other out-of-place-artifact cases in this group turn on date — the claim that an object is much older than its official age, reaching back toward a pre-Flood horizon. Yonaguni is different. The submergence date is not seriously contested: the last glacial low-stand put sea level more than a hundred metres below today's, so a shelf now under 25 metres of water was dry land until the postglacial rise flooded it in the early Holocene, on the order of ten thousand years ago. Everyone in the dispute — Kimura included — agrees the rock was above water then and is under water now. The contested question is not when it was drowned but what it is: a quarried monument that the sea later covered, or a natural rock formation that the sea later covered. So this page rates a natural-versus-artificial claim, not a redating, and the honest guards of the group apply in that key: the burden is to show the rock was worked, and possibility is not proof.4

What would have to hold true for the claim

The strong claim — a deliberately quarried monument — needs two things, and both are conceded here at full strength.

(a) The rectilinear form exceeds what the rock does on its own. The load-bearing premise is that flat terraces, sheer walls, and repeated right angles at this scale are not the ordinary behaviour of this sandstone, so a maker is required to explain them. If jointed sandstone can and does produce these forms unaided, the premise is gone.

(b) The worked details are worked. Even granting the gross form to nature, the holes, the quarry-wedge line, the "loop road," and the carvings could still betray a human hand at the site. If any of these is a genuine tool-mark that nature does not make, the modest reading (natural feature, human use) becomes a foothold for more — so each deserves a fair look on its own.5

Adversarial assessment: do the conditions hold here?

The rock is bedded and orthogonally jointed, and that alone makes the terraces. Condition (a) fails on the geology. The monument is cut in the Early Miocene Yaeyama Group — fine sandstones and mudstones laid down roughly twenty million years ago and since tilted — and this rock carries two natural weaknesses at right angles to each other: near-horizontal bedding planes between the deposited layers, and sets of near-vertical joints. Rock of this kind breaks out in flat-topped, sheer-sided blocks along those two directions as a matter of course; the "steps" are the exposed edges of successive beds, the "walls" are joint faces. Schoch, who dived the site, put it plainly: "The rocks of the region break out neatly, both along horizontal and vertical planes, and naturally form the step-like appearance." Right angles here are not a signature of design; they are what two perpendicular fracture systems produce for free.6

The decisive fact: the same forms stand in the open air, a few hundred metres away. The single strongest argument against the monument reading is not theoretical. Identical stepped, terraced, right-angled formations occur on the subaerial coast of Yonaguni — above the waterline, in daylight, where no one proposes a drowned civilization — and along the shore both above and below the tide line. A natural process that demonstrably makes these forms where nobody built is the most economical explanation of the same forms where they are submerged. A 2019 field study by Takayuki Ogata and colleagues, examining the monument and the comparable coastal outcrops together, concluded that it is "a natural feature formed by weathering and erosional processes" on the bedding and joints of the sandstone. Yonaguni also sits in one of the most seismically active zones on earth, and repeated earthquakes fracture jointed rock along its existing planes — supplying, rather than requiring a mason, the crispness Kimura reads as tooling.7

The "structures" are attached to the bedrock, not assembled from cut blocks. This is the point Kimura's own word — monolithic — concedes and its force is easy to miss. A quarried monument is made of blocks that were cut free and set in place; Yonaguni's terraces and walls are not free blocks but faces of the living rock, still joined to the mass beneath them. There is no masonry here in the ordinary sense: nothing was detached, dressed, and stacked. That is exactly the state a jointed outcrop weathering along its planes would be in, and exactly what a built structure is not. The "pyramid," seen whole, reads (in one geologist-reviewer's phrase) "more like an oval cake" than a designed step-pyramid, and the celebrated "steps" are irregular and spaced feet apart — following the stratigraphy, not a human stride.8

The carvings are the weakest link, and they carry the "writing." Kimura's engraved animals, the Kaida-like sign, the "sphinx" and the moai-face are the part of the case that most needs to hold and least does. On inspection these are natural scratches and irregularities in the rock, their apparent regularity exaggerated by the angle of the photograph and by pareidolia — the same faculty that finds a face in the moon. Schoch, examining the alleged markings, found nothing that required a carver; John Anthony West, who dived the site with him, concluded that Kimura "had not looked carefully enough at natural processes at work." An "underwater sphinx" and a "cow relief" identified by resemblance, with no tool-marks that nature does not make, are not evidence of writing or sculpture — they are the resemblances the human eye supplies to any sufficiently broken rock face.9

The holes are the one real toehold — and they do not reach. The most serious single item is not the gross form but the small worked-looking details: the two round holes and the straight row of smaller holes on the "Triangle Pool," read as an abandoned quarry-wedge line, and the sockets read as pillar-holes. These deserve the fair look condition (b) asks for, because a genuine wedge-line would be hard for nature to fake. But they do not carry the monument. Round holes in submerged sandstone are routinely cut by trapped cobbles swirled in eddies (potholes) and by boring marine organisms; a short row of them is suggestive but isolated, unaccompanied by the detached, dressed blocks a real quarry leaves, and un-mapped, un-photographed to the standard that would settle it — the tool-mark evidence, as one archaeological reviewer noted, is "seriously lacking," resting on plaster casts without published grids or clear images. A single ambiguous feature against a whole formation that is otherwise plainly natural is exactly the residue the modest reading already covers.10

The live middle ground, and why the modest claim is plausible

It would be a symmetry failure to answer Kimura's overreach with an equal and opposite one — to insist the site is sterile of any human trace. The lead skeptic does not. Schoch's actual conclusion is that Yonaguni is "primarily a natural structure … over 95% natural," but that ancient inhabitants very likely "touched up" parts of it and "admired and utilized" it — his analogy is the painted cave, a natural cavity that is nonetheless a genuine site of human activity. On that reading the odd hole, a smoothed ledge, a step improved for footing, are entirely credible: people lived on Yonaguni, the formation stood above water within human memory of the region, and coastal peoples use the rock they are given. So the modest claim — a natural formation that humans used or lightly modified — is plausible, even likely. What is weak is only the strong claim on top of it: that the terraces themselves were quarried, that this is a designed pyramid-and-temple complex, that it is monumental architecture of a lost Ice-Age civilization.11

A useful cross-reference: Schoch is not a blanket maximalist

The instructive thing about this case is who argues which side. Robert Schoch is best known on this shelf for the opposite verdict: at Giza he reads the weathering of the Sphinx enclosure as rainfall erosion and argues the Sphinx is millennia older than dynastic Egypt — a maximal antiquity claim. At Yonaguni the same geologist, hoping to find corroboration for lost antiquity, dived the site and concluded against it: the rock is natural, the "monument" a formation. That a redating's most prominent proponent will say "natural" when the geology says natural is itself evidence about method — the reading follows the rock, not the wish. It also isolates what is really at issue at Yonaguni: not whether great antiquity is possible in principle, but whether this particular rock was worked, which is a question the rock answers.12

Assessment

Weak — as the strong claim that the Yonaguni Monument is a deliberately quarried, human-built monument (a stepped pyramid with roads, temples and a stadium); plausible — as the modest claim that it is a natural formation ancient people used or lightly modified.

The concessions are real and are made in full. The object is genuinely arresting — the terraces and right angles do read as architecture to the eye, and Kimura is a serious field geologist, not a fantasist, who came to the artificial reading through diving rather than despite it. His single best point — that the layout is "too regular" for chance — would carry weight if the rock in question were the wrong kind for it.

What defeats the strong claim is that the rock is exactly the right kind. Bedded, orthogonally jointed sandstone in a seismically battered zone makes flat terraces and sheer right-angled faces as its ordinary behaviour; the proof is that it does so in the open air a few hundred metres from the monument, above the tide, where nobody claims a builder. The "structures" are still fused to the bedrock rather than cut into free blocks, so there is no masonry to explain; the carvings and "script" are natural scratches read through pareidolia; and the one genuinely worked-looking detail, a short line of holes, is ambiguous, isolated, and un-documented to the standard it would need — and is in any case already covered by the modest reading. Neither Japan's cultural authorities nor Okinawa's have recognized the site as an artifact or worked it as one, and the 2019 field study reads it as weathering and erosion on joints and beds. Graham Hancock's presentation of the artificial reading as established outruns even Kimura's evidence, and well outruns the geology.

The honest residue is the middle ground the lead skeptic already occupies: a spectacular natural formation, standing on dry land within the human past of an inhabited island, that people surely saw and may well have touched — which is a real and interesting thing, and not a drowned Ice-Age city.
