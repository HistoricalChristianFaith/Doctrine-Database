Does Homo juluensis, the "large-head" hominin, attest the Nephilim?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — the datum is genuine, which already sets it apart from most of what gets offered on this question: Homo juluensis was proposed in a peer-reviewed paper in November 2024, and the measurement behind the name is real, an endocranial volume of about 1800 ml at Xuchang and some 1700 ml at Xujiayao, the largest known for any hominin of their time. The identification with the Nephilim fails on three separate joints. The name means large-headed, and the type material preserves no postcranial bone at all, so nothing in it speaks to stature — while the closest comparanda at the same brain volume, the Neanderthals, averaged about 164–168 cm. The fossils sit between roughly 230,000 and 105,000 years ago on four physical clocks resting on independent physics, two orders of magnitude outside any frame this timeline entertains, with no live path to re-dating them. And the taxon itself is contested by the specialists best placed to judge it. Whether large hominins could have existed is a separate and open question; this taxon does not answer it.

The claim

In December 2024 a new archaic human made the news under a name that translates as "large head people," and the identification followed within days: here at last is the material basis for the Nephilim of Genesis 6:4 and for the Anakim and Rephaim who descend from them — not a folk memory and not a hoaxed skeleton, but a distinct, unusually endowed human-like population known from excavated bone and published in a first-rank journal. The claim assessed here is that Homo juluensis supplies that basis.

Who advances it

Not the fossils' describers. Christopher Bae and Xiujie Wu propose the taxon to tidy an eastern Asian fossil record that has tended to absorb anything not readily assignable to Homo erectus, H. neanderthalensis or H. sapiens; nothing in their paper touches Genesis.1 The identification is carried by popular giant-literature and by online discussion downstream of the press coverage, and its weakest form is a pun in English — big skull, big man.

That form is not what this page weighs. The strongest version drops the skull size as the load-bearing element and runs instead on a structural correspondence: palaeoanthropology has independently established that archaic human populations distinct from H. sapiens existed within the last few hundred thousand years, and that at least one of them interbred with modern humans, leaving descendants — which is the shape of what Genesis 6:1–4 describes, two kinds and their offspring. Bae and Wu fold the Denisovans into juluensis; Denisovan introgression is measured, not inferred. On that version, the large braincase is a bonus rather than the argument, and the claim is that the record has produced a real population of the right general kind.

The evidence for

The taxon is ordinary science, published and peer-reviewed. Bae and Wu named Homo juluensis in Nature Communications in November 2024, from Mandarin 巨颅, "large-headed," with the Xujiayao material in northern China as holotype and the Xuchang crania from Lingjing in Henan as paratype, and proposed extending the species to the Xiahe mandible from Tibet, Penghu 1 from Taiwan, a molar from Laos, and the Denisovans.1 Whatever becomes of the name, the fossils are real, excavated, reconstructed and described. That is worth stating plainly at the outset, because on this particular question it is unusual: the rest of the "giant remains" file consists of objects that trace to their manufacture, and this one does not.2

The measurements are genuinely exceptional, and the argument is entitled to them. Xuchang 1, excavated at Lingjing between 2007 and 2014 and reconstructed from twenty-six fragments, has an endocranial volume of roughly 1800 ml — the size of the largest known Neanderthal crania and larger than most living people, with an extremely broad neurocranium relative to every other Late Pleistocene hominin. Xujiayao 6 is estimated at about 1700 ml, the largest known for any hominin of its period. For scale, Maba 1 comes in near 1300 ml and the modern average is about 1350.3 These are not marginal outliers dressed up; they are the reason the taxon was named.

And interbreeding between distinct human populations is documented rather than hypothesised. The Denisova Cave genome published in 2010 showed a population sharing common origin with Neanderthals that contributed about 4–6 per cent of the genomes of present-day Melanesians — a point estimate of 4.8 ± 0.5 per cent — meaning as much as 7.4 per cent of a Melanesian genome may derive from recent archaic admixture.4 Two kinds of human meeting and leaving offspring is not a thing this reconstruction has to argue for. It happened.

Adversarial assessment

A large braincase is not a large body, and the type material cannot speak to stature at all. This is the decisive anatomical point and it is not a quibble about wording. Between them the Xujiayao and Xuchang sites yield crania, partial faces, a mandibular ramus and teeth — and, as John Hawks notes of the assemblage, "none from the postcranial skeleton."3 Stature is estimated from long bones, chiefly femur and tibia; there are none. Whatever these people's height was, no one knows it, and the species name records the one dimension that was measured. The slide from "large head" to "giant" is a category error, and it belongs to the popular version of the argument, not to the paper.

The internal control settles what brain volume implies about height, and it points the wrong way. The comparanda at this cranial capacity are the Neanderthals, whose largest skulls reach the same 1800 ml — and Neanderthal stature, estimated by Hermann Helmuth from forty-five long bones, averages about 164–168 cm for males and 152–156 cm for females, shorter than post-war Europeans and roughly level with Europeans of 1900.5 The Pleistocene hominins with the biggest brains on record were short, heavy-set, cold-adapted people. Encephalization and stature are separate variables, and the tradition under test makes its claim about the second one — tribes before whom observers were "as grasshoppers," a measured object at nine cubits. Even granting the identification everything it asks, the taxon delivers a population of about Neanderthal height.

The chronology is two orders of magnitude off, and the re-dating path is not live. Because a dating claim deserves the method and not an appeal to authority, here is the state of it. Xuchang: optically stimulated luminescence on Layer 11 at Lingjing puts the crania between 125,000 and 105,000 years ago, with the overlying Layers 10 and 9 returning 100,000–90,000 — the stratigraphic order and the dates agree.3 Xujiayao: a 2025 study dated the hominin remains themselves by uranium-series and the associated mammalian teeth by coupled ESR/U-series, then Bayesian-modelled the result against the earlier published ages, constraining the upper cultural layer to between 199 ± 8 and 138 ± 10 ka and the lower to between 228 ± 17 and 205 ± 8 ka.6

The instability in those dates is real and is conceded — and it is of the wrong order to help. Xujiayao has a genuinely messy dating history: an early uranium-series determination of 125–104 ka, cosmogenic 26Al/10Be burial dating giving older than 140 ka, post-infrared infrared-stimulated luminescence at 160–220 ka, and ESR on quartz with magnetostratigraphy pooling at 260–370 ka.6 That is a threefold disagreement among specialists, and no page should paper over it by citing whichever number suits. But the question here is what would have to hold for these remains to fall inside a frame the rest of this timeline entertains — the Black Sea infill at c. 5600 BC, Woolley's Ur silt at c. 2900 BC, the Jishi Gorge outburst at c. 1920 BC. Reaching that window means moving the fossils by a factor of twenty to fifty; the published disagreement moves them by a factor of three, and every competing estimate is Pleistocene. Four clocks resting on independent physics — uranium-series decay, electron-spin-resonance trap accumulation, luminescence, cosmogenic-nuclide burial — would all have to fail in the same direction by the same enormous margin, alongside the stratigraphic superposition at Lingjing and an extinct late-Middle-Pleistocene macromammalian fauna in the same deposits.

And the mechanisms that do sometimes unseat a date have no purchase here. The precedents are real and this wiki takes them seriously where they apply: a monument usurped by a later ruler who claims his predecessor's work, a renovation recorded as a construction, older foundations reused in a later building, a style dated by the assumption it was meant to prove. Every one of those is a mechanism about things people made and re-made. These are not built objects. They are bones and teeth in a stratified deposit, dated by measuring physical processes in the specimens and in the sediment around them, with nothing for a later hand to appropriate. The differentiation the honest method requires is available and it comes out in the same place: this is not the loose end of the dating record, it is one of the well-anchored parts of it.

The taxon is a 2024 proposal that the leading specialists dispute. Chris Stringer holds that most of the fossils assigned to juluensis are a better morphological match for the Harbin cranium, already named Homo longi — which under the rules of nomenclature would take priority and make the new name redundant. Xijun Ni argues the material as published does not carry enough diagnostic information to erect a species. Yousuke Kaifu and Sheela Athreya note that the Denisovan assignment cannot even be tested against the holotype, because Xujiayao preserves the mandibular ramus while the Xiahe mandible preserves the body — the parts do not overlap.7 Then in April 2025 palaeoproteomic analysis of Penghu 1 — one of the specimens Bae and Wu had folded in — recovered 4,241 amino-acid residues from 51 proteins with two Denisovan-specific variants and a male Y-chromosome signature, identifying it as a Denisovan rather than resolving it into the new species.8 Bae expected the fight and said so. That is how taxonomy works, and it is no criticism of the paper. It is a straightforward objection to hanging an identification on the name eighteen months after it was coined.

The steelman, pressed, consumes the argument. If the real force of the claim is archaic admixture — two kinds of human, interbreeding, offspring — then juluensis is doing no work in it. Denisovan introgression stands on its own genetics and would stand if the name were withdrawn tomorrow; the large braincase contributes nothing to it; and the admixture that is actually documented has a consequence the Nephilim reading may not want, since its descendants are alive and walking around in Melanesia at 4–6 per cent, rather than being wiped out in a Flood. That broader argument — does archaic admixture answer to Genesis 6:1–4? — is a different claim, and it would have to be weighed on its own evidence, its own chronology, and its own textual footing. It is not the claim that a big skull from Henan is a Nephilim.

What is not being said. Nothing here rules out large hominins a priori, and nothing here rests on the premise that the extraordinary cannot happen. Two questions are being kept apart on purpose. Could a population of exceptional size have existed? Open — and unanswered by anything on this page. Does this taxon attest one? That is the narrow question, and it has an answer.

Assessment

Weak — as an identification, on its best available case. The rating is not "unsupported," and the difference matters: unlike the giant-skeleton corpus, there is a real object here, measured and published, and the measurement is as impressive as advertised. But the inference from it fails at each joint independently. The type material contains no postcranial bone, so it makes no claim about height; the closest comparanda at 1800 ml stood about 165 cm; four independent physical clocks put the fossils between roughly 230,000 and 105,000 years ago, with the real scholarly disagreement spanning a factor of three inside the Pleistocene and no mechanism on offer that could move them by the factor of twenty or more the identification needs; and the species itself is contested by Stringer, Ni, and Kaifu and Athreya, with the 2025 Penghu proteomics cutting across the grouping.

What survives in the argument's favour is worth keeping, because it is the one place on this shelf where the fossil record hands the reconstruction something rather than nothing: distinct archaic human populations really did exist alongside H. sapiens, really did interbreed with it, and really did include individuals with brains larger than almost anyone alive. That is a genuine finding, and it is not the Nephilim. The word behind the name means large-headed, the fossils are Pleistocene, and the taxon is eighteen months old and under argument — three ordinary facts, none of which needs a metaphysical premise to state, and which together are enough.
