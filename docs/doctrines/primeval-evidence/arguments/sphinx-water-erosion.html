Does the Great Sphinx's weathering push it far older than dynastic Egypt?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — a credentialed, peer-reviewed geological redating that is strong by weathering geology's lights and met by strong rivals and a hard archaeological gap, and so genuinely unresolved; but the scale the reconstruction needs is weak, since even the geologists who accept the water erosion reach only the Early Dynastic, not a pre-Flood horizon · advanced by Robert Schoch with John Anthony West

This is the flagship of the dating-skepticism cases, and it earns the billing: a geologist with a Yale doctorate, publishing in a peer-reviewed journal and debating his critics at scientific meetings, argues that the most famous monument in Egypt is thousands of years older than the dynasty credited with it. It is the one place on this shelf where the antiquity claim is not a fringe wave-off but a real, unresolved scientific disagreement, and it is weighed here as one — not dismissed for being unorthodox, and not adopted for being bold. The method is the one set out on the dating-security page, of which this is the headline worked example: steelman the redating in its strongest form, name the actual method behind the official date, state what would have to hold true for the older reading, and test whether it holds. The result is instructive precisely because the geology genuinely is open while the conclusion the reconstruction wants does not follow from it.

The claim

The most prominent weathering pattern seen on the body of the Sphinx and on the walls of the Sphinx enclosure is, in my opinion, primarily due to precipitation (rainfall) … Such weathering, I believe, takes thousands of years to develop, and its presence on the Great Sphinx indicates that the core body of this structure predates the time of Khafre.

— Robert M. Schoch, "Redating the Great Sphinx of Giza". The deep, undulating, vertical-fissured profile of the enclosure walls is read as rainfall erosion, which requires a much wetter climate than dynastic Egypt's — and therefore a date millennia before Khafre's conventional c. 2500 BC, reaching, in Schoch's later statements, toward the end of the last Ice Age.1

Who advances it

The redating is Robert M. Schoch's — a geologist and geophysicist with a Yale PhD (1983), an associate professor at Boston University — and this matters to how the argument is weighed: it is not folk speculation but a credentialed geomorphologist's reading of a rock face. He was brought to the monument in 1989 by John Anthony West, an independent Egyptologist working from R. A. Schwaller de Lubicz's mid-century remark that the Sphinx showed water weathering, and West's own frame around the observation was frankly speculative — a "lost, advanced civilization" behind the carving. Schoch kept his geology separate from that frame and presented it as science: a poster session at the Geological Society of America in October 1991, where some seventy geologists asked to be kept informed; a paper, "Redating the Great Sphinx of Giza," in the peer-reviewed journal Geoarchaeology in 1992; and a public debate with the Egyptologist Mark Lehner and the stone-conservation geologist K. Lal Gauri at the American Association for the Advancement of Science meeting in Chicago in February 1992.2 The most aggressive versions of the redating — West's, and later Graham Hancock's and Robert Bauval's 10,500 BC — run further than Schoch's own minimum, and it is Schoch's geological claim, not their lost-civilization overlay, that is rated here.

The evidence for

The core observation is real and specific. The walls of the pit from which the Sphinx was quarried — the enclosure — carry a deeply cut, rolling, vertically fissured profile: rounded undulating hollows and sharp vertical clefts that follow the rock's joints and faults, concentrated where running water would collect and run off. Schoch's contrast is that wind-and-sand erosion, which marks the rest of the Giza plateau and the Old Kingdom tombs cut into the same limestone, produces flat horizontal bands, whereas the enclosure's profile is the vertical, fretted signature of water working down a rock face over a long time. Egypt has not had that kind of sustained rainfall since well before the Old Kingdom; on this reading the weathering cannot have formed in the two thousand years the enclosure was open before it silted up, so the carving must be older — and older enough to reach a wetter climate regime.3

What raises the argument above one geologist's eye is that it did not stay one geologist's. Two others examined the enclosure independently and agreed that water, not wind, cut it — while landing on dates far short of Schoch's. Colin Reader, a British geologist, argued that the erosion is from rainfall run-off rather than rainfall as such, traced its uneven distribution inside the enclosure (heavier at the western end, stemmed where "Khufu's quarries" later cut off the catchment), and concluded that the excavation predates Fourth-Dynasty activity at Giza — but placed it in the Early Dynastic Period, the First or Second Dynasty, c. 3000–2800 BC, "to stay within the earliest known period of stone building in Egypt," and insisted the Sphinx is a product of Pharaonic culture, not a lost one. David Coxill, working separately again, concluded the Sphinx "is at least 5,000 years old and pre-dates dynastic times." That a water-erosion reading has been reached by three geologists on three independent looks is the strongest thing the argument has going for it.4

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The geomorphology is not settled, and it would be dishonest to say it is. The enclosure profile is a genuine anomaly against the plateau's wind-cut tombs; a credentialed geologist put a coherent rainfall reading of it into a peer-reviewed journal; and two more geologists independently read water in the same rock. This is the paragon case the dating-skepticism method exists for: strong by one discipline's lights and not knocked out on its own ground.

What the rival geology says. But the weathering has live rivals, and they are not consensus-by-authority; they are mechanisms. James Harrell attributed the profile to haloclasty — salt crystallising and re-crystallising from moisture in the sand that buried the enclosure for most of its history, a process that fissures soft limestone without any rainfall at all, and one visible in comparable Old Kingdom quarries. Gauri favoured salt weathering too, driven by atmospheric moisture and dew. And the Sphinx is not carved from uniform stone: it is cut through the alternating soft and hard beds of the Mokattam limestone (a hard basal member, a soft and unevenly bedded middle member forming most of the body and the enclosure walls, a harder member in the head), so that the softer bands recede and the harder ones stand proud — a rolling, layered profile that differential erosion produces regardless of whether the agent was rain, salt, or wind. When the same feature has three candidate causes and only one of them requires an early date, the early date is not established by the feature.5

Where the redating's added props fail. Schoch buttressed the weathering with a seismic survey (with Thomas Dobecki) reading a subsurface weathered zone as evidence of great age — but the anomaly admits reading as a natural bedding plane, and the velocities Dobecki measured run deeper behind the Sphinx than in front, which on Schoch's own logic would make the rump older than the front paws, an incoherent result for a single carving. And Mark Lehner's stratigraphy sets the Sphinx Temple's core blocks on the pre-existing northern enclosure wall of Khafre's Valley Temple, fixing the monument's position within Khafre's construction sequence rather than before it; Lehner also argued the limestone was not deeply weathered but cut back irregularly to seat the granite facing, a technique paralleled at Menkaure's temple. The stratigraphic and seismic supports, in short, lose on the evidence.6

The Khafre context. Against the redating stands a dense Fourth-Dynasty context: the Sphinx and its temple sit integrated with Khafre's valley temple and the causeway to his pyramid; a diorite statue of Khafre was found buried in a pit inside that valley temple; and the face has long been read as his. The Dream Stele of Thutmose IV, set between the paws about 1400 BC, preserves the syllable "Khaf" in a damaged royal cartouche — but it is honest to concede that this is weak evidence for authorship: it is a recycled temple lintel, its text is broken, the "Khaf" was completed to "Khafre" by a modern reader, and even at face value it records only what a king believed a thousand years after the fact. The context that carries weight is the architecture and the statue, not the stele.7

The archaeological gap. This is the hard objection, and it does not touch the geology — it touches the conclusion. To carve a two-hundred-foot monument out of bedrock takes an organised society with quarrying, tools, provisioning, and a hierarchy to direct it, and such a society leaves a great deal else behind. For the millennia Schoch's minimum needs — and far more for the Ice-Age versions — Egypt shows no trace of one: no monumental architecture, no complex settlements, no such infrastructure until the Predynastic ramp toward the First Dynasty. Kenneth Feder puts it that "there is no evidence at all of such a culture" before c. 2500 BC; even Lehner, granting bare possibility, calls an older civilization there of "a very, very low level of probability." A redating that a discipline can defend on the rock must still answer to the discipline that would have to supply the builders.8

And the scale the reconstruction actually needs. Here the argument turns on itself. Grant the water erosion entirely; it still does not reach a pre-Flood or Nephilim horizon. The disciplined form of the reading — Reader's and Coxill's — lands at the Early Dynastic, c. 3000 BC, a few centuries before Khafre and squarely inside known Egyptian culture. And the climate premise cuts the same way: Egypt's last sustained wet phase, the early-to-mid Holocene African Humid Period (the "Neolithic Subpluvial"), ran roughly to the fourth millennium BC, so even "rainfall erosion" points at most to a window a few centuries older, not to the deep antiquity the reconstruction is testing for. The pre-Flood-scale version of this argument is thus weak on two counts at once — the archaeological gap, and the fact that the geologists who accept the erosion do not endorse it.9

Assessment

Contested — and the split is the finding. Rate the water-erosion redating as a geological question and it is genuinely open: Schoch's rainfall reading of the enclosure is a real, peer-reviewed observation, reached independently by Reader and Coxill, and answered not by consensus but by rival mechanisms (haloclasty, atmospheric-moisture salt weathering, differential erosion of soft and hard limestone beds) that are themselves not decisive. Strong by weathering geology's lights, met by strong rivals and a hard archaeological gap, unresolved on its own ground — that is what contested names. But keep the two questions apart, as this shelf's method requires. The redating's added props — the seismic anomaly, read against a bedding plane and giving the incoherent result that the Sphinx's back is older than its front; the stratigraphy, which seats the Sphinx Temple on Khafre's valley-temple wall — lose on the evidence. And the scale the reconstruction wants is weak on its own terms: the geologists who accept the water erosion reach the Early Dynastic, not a lost world, and Egypt's early-Holocene wet phase would push the date at most a few centuries, while the millennia the pre-Flood frame needs run into a culture that has left no other trace. The honest verdict is therefore layered: a genuinely contested geomorphology, a modest redating (a few centuries) as its most defensible outcome, and no road from either to a pre-Flood horizon.
