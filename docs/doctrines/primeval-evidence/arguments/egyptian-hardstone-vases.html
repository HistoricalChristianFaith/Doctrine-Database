Did predynastic Egypt's hard-stone vases require lost machining technology to make?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — as the claim that the vessels require a lathe, CNC-level machining, or an inherited advanced technology to make. The craftsmanship is real and the best pieces are astonishing; the material is intractable and some of the measured symmetry is genuinely fine. But the manufacturing claim is a claim about a population, and it is tested on the wrong tail of it. The vessels that carry the "aerospace precision" figures are a handful of unprovenanced pieces in private hands; the vessels that come out of the ground — thousands of them, in museum collections with excavation histories — cluster statistically with modern hand-made replicas and not with lathe-made ones, on the one peer-reviewed metrology study that has compared them. The attested toolkit (copper tubular drills, stone borers, quartz-sand abrasive) is documented in the tool-marks, in the drill cores Petrie kept, and in excavated workshops, and it has been replicated. A second and separable sub-claim — that the vessels or the technique are much older than their Naqada–Early-Dynastic date, reaching a pre-Flood horizon — is also weak: these are portable grave goods from stratified, radiocarbon-anchored contexts, not a monument a later ruler could appropriate.

The claim

Two things are true and are the whole basis of the argument. The first is the material. From the Badarian and Naqada periods (roughly 4000–3000 BC) and into the Early Dynastic, Egyptian craftsmen worked vessels not only in soft travertine ("alabaster") but in granite, diorite, basalt, and the tough greywacke and siltstone of the Wadi Hammamat — stones at Mohs 6–7, harder than a copper tool and unworkable by any chisel. The second is the output. These were made by the tens of thousands: nearly forty thousand stone vessels were found stacked in the galleries beneath Djoser's Step Pyramid alone, many of them predating him by centuries, and thousands more come from Predynastic and Early Dynastic tombs. The best of them have thin walls, near-perfect bores, and closed forms with narrow necks opening into wide hollow interiors, so that the inside volume mirrors the outside shape.1

From this the argument infers what it takes to be the only sufficient cause. The popular case is built on 3D metrology. Ben van Kerkwyk (UnchartedX), working with the machinists and metrologists Alex Dunn, Nick Sierra, and Christopher Dunn, has published structured-light and CT scans of several granite and diorite vases and reports "concrete, undeniable evidence for utterly astounding geometric precision" — concentricity and wall-thickness figures said to run to thousandths of an inch, and, across separate objects, "the same radial traversal function," "the same fixed mathematical ratio between curvatures," and the recurring use of π and φ. The inference drawn is "design intent": the vessels were engineered in a coherent system and fabricated on a machine — a lathe or something CNC-like — and therefore descend from a technology the Neolithic could not have possessed on its own. On this timeline that technology is the arts 1 Enoch 7–8 says the Watchers taught, surviving out of the antediluvian world.2

An older and separate prop is Christopher Dunn's reading of one of Petrie's granite drill cores, "Core No. 7," on which Petrie described a spiral groove implying a feed of 0.100 inch per revolution — a rate Dunn argues is hundreds of times faster than a modern diamond drill and impossible for copper and sand.3

Who advances it

The claim belongs to the general out-of-place-artifact literature and travels in the popular form "no Stone Age hand could do this." Its modern engine is the UnchartedX scan project and the metrology community around it; its intellectual ancestor is Christopher Dunn's The Giza Power Plant (1998) and the machinist's-eye tradition that reads tool-marks on Egyptian hard stone as the signature of powered equipment. It earns a page because it is genuinely advocated, because it is one of the few places on this timeline where the reconstruction makes contact with a measurable material question, and because the strong version is not silly: it asks what the artisans actually had, and it forces the defender to answer with evidence rather than assertion.

The evidence for

The difficulty is not exaggerated. Nephrite's Chinese cousin aside, hard stone is not carved but abraded — ground away by loose sand harder than the tool, on a bit of copper or wood that leaves no mark of its own — and the process is slow. A closed vessel is harder still than an open bowl, because the interior has to be hollowed through a neck narrower than the belly, blind, by feel. That a Predynastic culture with no iron and only arsenical copper produced narrow-necked hard-stone jars in the thousands is a real achievement and the honest starting point of the argument.

And some of the measured precision is real. This is the argument's strongest card and it should be conceded in full. On the museum vessels that have been laser-scanned, the interior circularity of the best pieces is startling — cross-sections round to within two or three thousandths of an inch, a figure one instinctively associates with a turned surface rather than a hand-ground one. The bores are true, the profiles are smooth, and the eye that dismisses the whole business as "they only had sand" is not looking at the objects. The question the argument raises — how was that done without a machine? — is a good question, and the answer had to be found rather than assumed.4

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The material is intractable, the interior circularity of the best pieces is measured rather than asserted, and the "they only had sand" reply is itself too glib — the toolkit was more capable than the textbook cartoon of it. An argument that makes the defender produce the actual tool-marks, the actual workshops, and an actual replication has done real work even when its own conclusion fails.

The attested toolkit is documented, not conjectured, and it is a hand toolkit. Petrie devoted a chapter of The Pyramids and Temples of Gizeh to the "mechanical methods of the pyramid builders," and it is the founding description of Egyptian hard-stone working: tubular copper drills, solid drills, and straight and circular saws, used with a loose abrasive, leaving cores and tool-marks he collected and drew. The stone-vessel drill is so characteristic that its image became the hieroglyph for "craftsman." The specific bits are known from the objects and from workshop finds: hand-held stone borers with sand for the hardest stones, crescent-shaped flint drills and figure-of-eight stone borers weighted with a forked shaft for hollowing, copper tubes for the lug-holes. Unfinished vessels survive at every stage — one andesite-porphyry jar in the British Museum abandoned when its handle broke, others still carrying the manufacturing foot they were held by — which is what a hand industry leaves behind and a delivered machine technology does not.5

The method has been replicated, hard stone included. Denys Stocks, an experimental archaeologist with an engineering background, spent decades reconstructing the toolkit from Petrie's cores and the tool-marks. A bow-driven copper tube charged with dry quartz sand, worked by a small team, cut granite at about 120 revolutions a minute and produced cores whose striations — generally about 0.25 mm wide and deep — match those on the ancient cores closely; the copper barrel wore away at a ratio of less than one part in a hundred of the depth cut. His full replication of a Manchester Museum vessel used stone hammers, flint chisels, tube-drilling with sand, and sandstone and fine-sand finishing, and he estimated about 800 hours for the piece. Olga Vdovina, working for a Russian science-outreach group and using only stone, bone, and wood, made a diorite vase by hand — diorite being among the hardest stones the Egyptians used — over about two years. The material-removal rate for granite runs roughly ten times slower than for alabaster, which is exactly why the hard stone was prestige work; it is not why it was impossible.6

The precision test cuts against the machine, once it is done on provenanced objects. This is the load-bearing objection and it is positive evidence, not an appeal to what Neolithic people "must" have done. In 2025 Max Fomitchev-Zamilov published a peer-reviewed metrological study in npj Heritage Science that did what the viral scans had not: it measured a controlled sample and compared it. Nineteen Predynastic vessels from the Petrie Museum were scanned on a coordinate-measuring machine and scored on two independent quantities — circularity (how round each cross-section is) and concentricity (how well the cross-sections stack on a common axis) — against twenty-five modern lathe-made stone vases and twenty-four modern hand-made ones. The lathe-made vessels formed a tight, well-separated cluster. The Predynastic vessels did not fall in it. On the outer surface their quality distribution was statistically indistinguishable from the hand-made class (p = 0.87). And the interior told the same story once both numbers were read together: the Predynastic bores are excellently circular but poorly concentric — each slice is round, but the slices wander off a common axis by tens of thousandths of an inch. A lathe produces the opposite signature, because a single axis of rotation fixes concentricity for free. Rounds-but-wanders is the fingerprint of a workpiece turned by hand on a rotating base while a borer is worked against it — grinding, not machining.7

The "aerospace" figures come from the wrong tail of the corpus. The ultra-precise vessels that carry the thousandths-of-an-inch claims are a tiny minority — on one count about four of some fifty-six scanned — and every one of them comes from a private collection without an excavation history, not from a museum with a findspot. This is not a side point; it is the whole point. Van Kerkwyk himself concedes the selection: the most precise pieces, he says, are in private hands because collectors sought out the finest examples, while museums hold the thousands of ordinary and broken ones. But that is precisely the survivorship problem stated as a virtue. Tens of thousands of vessels made over centuries will, by ordinary variation, throw up a handful of superb outliers with no machine required — and a market that filters for "the most precise and beautiful" will concentrate exactly those outliers, together with any modern forgeries good enough to pass, in private collections. The independent metrologist Stine Gerdes, whose open-source scanning of Petrie Museum pieces the peer-reviewed study credits as the pioneering prior work, finds the same split: museum vessels at surface tolerances around 70–100 micrometres, consistent with pre-industrial craft, and the ultra-precise private pieces standing apart as unprovenanced outliers. Mark Qvist, the software engineer whose 2023 analysis of one private "OG" vase first popularized the "radial traversal" design-intent reading, revisited the question after higher-resolution 2025 scans and reportedly reversed it, concluding that the object in its current form is not 5,000 years old at all — a modern replica, a contemporary piece, or an ancient vessel later reworked.8

The Core No. 7 prop has come apart in the proponent's own hands. Petrie's "spiral groove" is the whole basis of the 0.100-inch feed-rate claim, and it is disputed on the object. Reid and Brownlee, examining core No. 7, reported that the grooves are not a single continuous spiral but individual rings of the kind found on cores in any modern quarry; Lawton and Ogilvie-Herald press the same distinction between separate horizontal striations, common to all cores, and a true connected spiral, which Petrie claimed for this one core alone. When Dunn inspected the core himself he found Petrie's 0.100-inch spacing wrong — he measured 0.040 to 0.080 inch — and conceded he could not sustain an advanced-machining reading on the feed rate as Petrie had stated it. The pressure figure sometimes attached to the claim rests on treating the drill as a solid bit; the Egyptian drill was a hollow tube, which needs a small fraction of that force, easily supplied by hand and a weighted capstone. Stocks's copper-tube-and-sand cores carry striations that match No. 7. The one artifact the machining case rested on turns out to be consistent with the toolkit everything else attests.9

What is genuinely still open. No full replication of a closed, narrow-necked hard-stone jar at the interior quality of the best museum pieces has been published; the metrology corpus is still small (tens of vessels, not hundreds); and the exact bit that finished the finest interiors — a weighted stone borer, a shaped grinding head on a shaft — is inferred from the surface signature rather than recovered in the act. These are open details inside a process that is understood, and an unanswered question about which borer sat in the artisan's hand is not evidence that no artisan's hand was involved.7

The dating sub-claim, kept separate

The manufacturing question and the antiquity question are different, and conflating them is how this class of argument usually goes wrong. Suppose the manufacturing claim failed entirely, as it does; one could still ask whether the vessels — or the technique — are much older than the conventional date, far enough to reach a pre-Flood horizon. What would have to hold? The real precedents that make such a move live elsewhere are monument usurpation, renovation counted as construction, and reused foundations: mechanisms that work on architecture, where a later ruler can inherit and re-inscribe someone else's building.

None of them transfers here. These are small, portable objects recovered from sealed burials across a well-ordered sequence — Badarian to Naqada I–III to Early Dynastic — each grave dated by its associated organic material and the whole typology internal to that sequence, with unfinished pieces and workshop debris fixing manufacture to the same horizon. There is no earlier structure to appropriate, and a grave-good cannot be older than its grave without leaving the stratigraphy visibly disturbed. The one route the argument does have — that a given ultra-precise piece is not ancient at all — runs the opposite way from a pre-Flood horizon: the unprovenanced outliers are suspected of being too young, modern work passed off as Predynastic, which is the reverse of the antiquity claim. The dating sub-claim is weak — not because the consensus says so, but because the specific mechanisms that could unseat a date do not apply to portable, stratified, workshop-attested grave goods.1

Assessment

Weak — as the claim that predynastic Egypt's hard-stone vases required a lathe, machine tooling, or a technology beyond their own capacity. The process is understood in its main lines and attested at every step by material that has been dug up: abrasion rather than carving, with loose quartz sand harder than the copper; tubular drilling and sawing that leave diagnostic cores and striations; hollowing of closed forms with crescent flints and weighted stone borers; grinding and polishing with sand and sandstone. The toolkit is in Petrie's chapter and in the workshops; the method has been replicated in granite and in diorite; the unfinished vessels and the manufacturing feet are on museum shelves. And when the precision is measured on provenanced objects rather than on hand-picked ones, the museum vessels cluster with modern hand-made work and not with lathe-made work — round in section, wandering off-axis, which is the signature of the hand and not of the machine.

Two things are owed to the argument. Its premise about the difficulty is correct, and pressing it is what produced the serious metrology — the coordinate-measuring studies and the open-source scanning that a complacent defence would never have bothered with. And its central observation about the best interiors stands: two or three thousandths of an inch of circularity, cut without iron, is a genuine and surprising number. What the evidence will not support is the inference drawn from it. The "aerospace precision" case is built on a handful of unprovenanced pieces selected for being extraordinary and plausibly not ancient at all, while the objects that actually come from Predynastic graves measure like superb handwork, because that is what they are. The correct reading of the finest vase is not that someone else made it; it is that these people were extraordinarily good at what they did, over many generations, with sand and copper and patience — a more interesting result than the one the argument was after, and, unlike it, what the ground actually shows.
