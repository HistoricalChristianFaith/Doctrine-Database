The giant-skeleton hoax lineage on the Flood and the Nephilim in the material record

Dates: 1869–present (the Cardiff Giant of October 1869 to the "Smithsonian admits" fabrication of December 2014 and its still-circulating local variants)
Summary: A witness whose evidential value is precisely that it is fraud. Every famous "giant skeleton" is either a manufactured object, a manufactured image, or a manufactured news story, and in most cases the maker is known by name and the confession is on record. This page documents the lineage as documented fraud, and then sets the honest ceiling against it: the tallest human being for whom irrefutable evidence exists, Robert Wadlow, at 2.72 m, by a pathology of the pituitary gland. It is the last witness on this shelf before the modern scientific record, and it feeds the material-trace arguments — where it is the principal reason the question "do any authenticated remains attest gigantism at Nephilim scale?" has the answer it has.

A note on why a page of frauds belongs on an evidential timeline at all. This reconstruction grants its premise for the sake of exploration and then tests it; testing means reporting what turns up, and along this particular line of inquiry what turns up is a two-century chain of deliberate deceptions. Naming them as deceptions is not scepticism imported from outside — it is what the perpetrators themselves said, in court and in print. Nothing here bears on whether God could make a giant. It bears on whether anyone has ever produced one.

Claims & evidence

The Cardiff Giant (1869) — a hoax built out of Genesis 6:4

The archetype of the genre began as an argument about this very verse. George Hull, a New York tobacconist, fell into a dispute with a Methodist revivalist named Turk over whether Genesis 6:4 meant there had literally been giants in the earth. Hull, who thought it absurd and thought people credulous, decided to demonstrate the credulity.

He had a block of gypsum quarried at Fort Dodge, Iowa, and shipped to Chicago, where it was carved into a ten-foot-four-inch male figure weighing nearly three thousand pounds. He aged it: sand and water rubbed over the surface, steel knitting needles punched in to simulate skin pores, sulphuric acid to weather it. In late November 1868 he buried it behind the barn of his cousin William Newell at Cardiff, New York. On 16 October 1869 Newell hired well-diggers, who struck it, and Newell put up a tent and charged admission.

The exposure was immediate on the expert side and slow on the popular side. Othniel C. Marsh, the Yale palaeontologist, inspected it and pronounced it "a most decided humbug," observing that gypsum is soluble and that a figure buried in wet ground for the claimed ages could not have retained fresh tool marks. P. T. Barnum, refused at $50,000, simply commissioned a plaster copy and exhibited it as the real one; when the syndicate sued, the judge declined to intervene unless the original giant would swear to its own genuineness. Hull confessed on 10 December 1869, and the fraud was established in open court on 2 February 1870. The object still exists and is on public display at Cooperstown, New York.

— The Cardiff Giant, with the quarrying, carving, artificial ageing, discovery and confession dates, Marsh's verdict, and the Barnum litigation.

The Solid Muldoon (1877) — the same man, the second time

Hull did it again. In 1877 he manufactured a second figure — seven feet six inches, moulded from mortar, rock dust, clay, plaster, ground bones, blood and meat, and fitted with a small tail so it could be sold as evolution's missing link as well as a giant. It was shipped to Colorado in a crate labelled "machinery," buried near Beulah, and left in the ground for months so the soil would settle. The site was pre-seeded: a hired local was paid to "find" smaller fake fossils nearby, duly reported in a newspaper whose editor was the son of Hull's front man. William Conant and his son "discovered" it on 16 September 1877. Barnum, older and wiser, played the publicity from the other side. The New York Times exposed it in 1878.

The Muldoon matters here for one reason: it establishes the pattern as a business rather than a mistake. The salted site, the shipping crate, the tame newspaper and the primed local rumour are a method, and the method recurs.

— "Solid Muldoon," Colorado Encyclopedia; Solid Muldoon.

The nineteenth-century newspaper "giant" reports and the mound-builder myth

The stock of "giant skeleton unearthed" clippings that circulates today is drawn overwhelmingly from nineteenth-century American local newspapers reporting on Native American burial mounds. Those reports have a documented context, and it is not a suppressed archaeology. They belong to the mound-builder myth: the long-running claim that the earthworks of the Ohio and Mississippi valleys were built not by the ancestors of living Native peoples but by a vanished superior race — variously Israelites, Welshmen, Atlanteans, or biblical giants — whose land had therefore been vacant for the taking. The myth had presidential endorsement and legislative consequences, and the "giants" were one of its recurring proofs.

The myth was demolished from inside the federal scientific establishment. Cyrus Thomas, after two decades of excavation for the Bureau of Ethnology, concluded in his 1894 report that the mounds were built by the ancestors of the historic tribes. That is the relevant irony for this page: the Smithsonian's actual role in the giant story was to disprove the lost-race framework the giants belonged to — which is exactly the role a later conspiracy theory would invert into a cover-up.

— Cyrus Thomas, Report on the Mound Explorations of the Bureau of Ethnology (Twelfth Annual Report, Washington, 1894); Jason Colavito, The Mound Builder Myth: Fake History and the Hunt for a "Lost White Race" (Norman: University of Oklahoma Press, 2020), on the newspaper record and its political function.

The manufactured image (2002–2007)

The single most widely circulated "giant skeleton" photograph — a partly excavated humanoid skeleton several times human size, with a man in a trench beside it for scale — is a digital composite made for a contest. It was entered on the design site Worth1000 under the alias IronKite, took third place in a 2002 competition called "Archaeological Anomalies 2," and was built by taking a published photograph of a Cornell University mastodon excavation in New York State and replacing the mastodon bones. The artist also recoloured a bystander's clothing to tie the two human figures together and exaggerate the scale.

It escaped the contest in early 2004 with a caption attached — found during gas exploration in the Arabian desert, with a gesture at the Qur'anic people of ʿĀd for authority — and was printed as news by The New Nation in Bangladesh in April 2004 and by Indian papers after it; a 2007 version in Hindu Voice credited the find to a National Geographic Society team. National Geographic itself has stated it has found no ancient giant humans. The artist, who had nothing to do with the hoax his picture became, told the magazine he preferred to stay anonymous because the online arguments over the image had turned religious.

— "Find Out How the Giant Skeleton Hoax Started," National Geographic (2007); "Giant Human Skeleton," Museum of Hoaxes.

The manufactured news story: "the Smithsonian destroyed the giant skeletons" (2014)

On 3 December 2014 a site called World News Daily Report published "Smithsonian Admits to Destruction of Thousands of Giant Human Skeletons in Early 1900's." The story: an organisation called the American Institution of Alternative Archaeology had accused the Smithsonian of destroying prehistoric skeletons up to twelve feet tall to protect the accepted chronology of human evolution; the Smithsonian sued for defamation; the case reached the Supreme Court in 2014, where whistleblowers confirmed the destruction and the Court ordered the documentation declassified.

Every element of it is invented. World News Daily Report carried a disclaimer stating that it was a satirical publication and its articles fiction. No such organisation as the AIAA exists. No such case exists in the Supreme Court's docket. The Smithsonian's own account is flat: "This fanciful account never had a shred of truth to it. The AIAA does not exist, there was never any evidence of biblical giants in North America that the Smithsonian could conceal and, of course, no such case was ever brought to the Supreme Court."

The images that travel with the story are themselves recycled fakes — among them photographs of Édouard Beaupré, a French-Canadian man with genuine pituitary gigantism who died in 1904, and a 47½-inch "femur" that the curator of the Mt. Blanco Fossil Museum has acknowledged sculpting. The story has been rebutted by the Associated Press, Reuters, PolitiFact, USA Today and Snopes, and it keeps returning in fresh local dress — an Ohio version, an Australian version — each time with the same fabricated core.

— Snopes, "Did the Smithsonian Destroy Thousands of Giant Human Skeletons?"; "Busting 13 of the Smithsonian's Most Persistent Myths," Smithsonian Magazine (myth 11), for the quoted denial.

The honest ceiling: Robert Wadlow

Against all of that stands the one figure in this area that is fully documented, repeatedly measured in life by physicians, and uncontested. Robert Wadlow (22 February 1918 – 15 July 1940) of Alton, Illinois reached 8 ft 11.1 in / 2.72 m — the tallest human being in recorded history for whom there is irrefutable evidence.

Two features of the case are the ones that matter for this timeline. First, his height was pathological: hypertrophy of the pituitary gland producing an abnormally high level of growth hormone, growth that never stopped. Second, it was disabling: he had little feeling in his legs and walked in braces, and he died at twenty-two of an infection from an ankle sore caused by a faulty brace. Extreme human height is a rare endocrine disorder in a single individual, and it carries a severe cost. It is not a heritable population trait, and there is no documented instance of it being one.

— Robert Wadlow; Guinness World Records, "Tallest man ever".

What this witness establishes, and what it does not

  • It establishes that the claimed material trace of biblical giants is, so far as the record goes, entirely manufactured — carved, moulded, composited, or written — and that in the leading cases the maker confessed or the publisher disclaimed.
  • It establishes a documented ceiling and a documented mechanism: 2.72 m, once, by pituitary pathology, at severe cost to the man.
  • It does not establish that no giant ever lived. Fraud in the evidence offered for a claim refutes the evidence, not the claim; a hoax proves that someone wanted the thing to be true, which is a fact about the hoaxer. The relevant conclusion is narrower and firmer: on the present record there is no authenticated skeletal evidence of a giant population, and the well-known items adduced as such are known frauds.
  • It does not rest on ruling out the supernatural. A miracle needs no bones; but a claim that a population of nine-foot men lived and died in a known territory is a claim about bones, and the bones offered are gypsum, mortar, pixels, and prose.

Notes

  • The Cardiff Giant's origin deserves to be kept in view when the hoax lineage is used polemically. It was made by a sceptic to embarrass believers, and it worked. Reproducing its descendants as evidence for the giants is therefore a peculiar reversal: the object was designed from the start to make credulity visible.
  • A recurring confusion in this material is between an individual and a population. Wadlow, Beaupré, and the other documented cases are single individuals with an identifiable endocrine disorder; the biblical roster describes peoples with descendants and territories. Evidence of the first is not evidence of the second, and it is the second that this timeline's arguments are testing.
