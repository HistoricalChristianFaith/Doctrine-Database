The Sumerian King List on the Flood and the Nephilim in the material record

Dates: c. 1817 BC (the Weld-Blundell prism, WB 444; the composition is older, and an Ur III recension survives without the antediluvian section) · dating method: epigraphic — the prism's own year-name, its text running down to the Isin dynasty and closing in the eleventh year of Sîn-māgir, which the Mesopotamian king-lists and date-formula lists place at c. 1817 BC on the middle chronology; the older Ur III manuscript is placed by script and archival context rather than by any date-formula. The 241,200 years of antediluvian reign are not a dating method and are not chronological data — see §the sexagesimal reigns · ETCSL 2.1.1
Summary: A Sumerian document that lists the kings of Mesopotamia city by city and dynasty by dynasty, and divides them with a flood. Before the flood, eight kings in five cities reign 241,200 years between them; "then the flood swept over"; then "the kingship had descended from heaven" a second time, at Kish, and the reigns begin their long slide down toward humanly plausible lengths. It is the oldest surviving document that treats a deluge as the hinge of chronology rather than as a story — the same thing Genesis 5–11 does — and it is the source from which that frame reached the Greek and then the Christian world, through Berossus.

A note on where this sits. This timeline is ordered by the age of the evidence, and the King List's evidence-date is genuinely awkward. The composition is older than any copy of it; the oldest copy we have is older still than the flood-frame, because it does not contain it. The date given above is the date of the best-preserved copy that does — which is the earliest hard datum for the antediluvian section specifically.

Claims & evidence

Eight kings, five cities, 241,200 years, then the flood

After the kingship descended from heaven, the kingship was in Eridug. In Eridug, Alulim became king; he ruled for 28800 years. Alaljar ruled for 36000 years. 2 kings; they ruled for 64800 years. Then Eridug fell and the kingship was taken to Bad-tibira. In Bad-tibira, En-men-lu-ana ruled for 43200 years. En-men-gal-ana ruled for 28800 years. Dumuzid, the shepherd, ruled for 36000 years. 3 kings; they ruled for 108000 years. Then Bad-tibira fell (?) and the kingship was taken to Larag. In Larag, En-sipad-zid-ana ruled for 28800 years. 1 king; he ruled for 28800 years. Then Larag fell (?) and the kingship was taken to Zimbir. In Zimbir, En-men-dur-ana became king; he ruled for 21000 years. 1 king; he ruled for 21000 years. Then Zimbir fell (?) and the kingship was taken to Curuppag. In Curuppag, Ubara-Tutu became king; he ruled for 18600 years. 1 king; he ruled for 18600 years. In 5 cities 8 kings; they ruled for 241200 years. Then the flood swept over.

After the flood had swept over, and the kingship had descended from heaven, the kingship was in Kic.

— The Sumerian King List, ETCSL 2.1.1 (Oxford's Electronic Text Corpus of Sumerian Literature; the transliteration conventions are the corpus's own — Curuppag is Shuruppak, Zimbir is Sippar, Kic is Kish). Four features matter evidentially and are worth separating: the flood is a datable event in a regnal chronology, not an episode in a story; kingship is re-founded after it, exactly as Genesis restarts the generations from Noah; the last pre-flood city is Shuruppak, the very city whose king is the flood hero in Atrahasis and Gilgamesh; and the last pre-flood king is Ubara-Tutu, named in Gilgamesh XI as the father of the flood survivor. The King List and the flood poems are telling the same chronology.

Every antediluvian reign is a sexagesimal round number

Not one of the eight figures is an arbitrary number. Babylonian arithmetic counted in base 60, with named units: the ner = 600 and the šár = 3,600. Every antediluvian reign in the list above is a whole multiple of the šár, or of the šár plus one ner:

  • 28,800 = 8 × 3,600 (Alulim, En-men-gal-ana, En-sipad-zid-ana)
  • 36,000 = 10 × 3,600 (Alalgar, Dumuzid the shepherd)
  • 43,200 = 12 × 3,600 (En-men-lu-ana)
  • 21,000 = 5 × 3,600 + 5 × 600 (En-men-dur-ana)
  • 18,600 = 5 × 3,600 + 1 × 600 (Ubara-Tutu)

The total, 241,200, is likewise 67 šár. The same artificiality persists (in weaker form) after the flood, where the early Kish reigns are round multiples of sixty and some are perfect squares — Ilku's 900 is thirty squared. This does not by itself prove the numbers are fictions; a scribe can round a remembered figure as well as invent one. What it does establish is that the numbers are generated within a numerical system, so they cannot be treated as raw data. Any argument that the reigns preserve a memory of a real pre-Flood span has to explain the arithmetic first — which is the work of the antediluvian-reigns argument.

The oldest copy has no flood in it

This is the sharpest evidential point on the page, and it cuts against the King List's value as a witness to the flood. The list survives in roughly sixteen tablets and fragments, nearly all Old Babylonian and mostly from Nippur. Only one manuscript is older: the Ur III recension published by Piotr Steinkeller, copied under Shulgi in the twenty-first century BC. It begins with Kish. It has no antediluvian kings and no flood. Nor does the antediluvian section appear in every Old Babylonian copy, including copies from Nippur. The best-preserved manuscript that does carry it is the Weld-Blundell prism (WB 444) in the Ashmolean, whose text runs down to the Isin dynasty and was written in the eleventh year of Sîn-māgir of Isin — c. 1817 BC.

That date is epigraphic and internal, and it is worth saying what kind of datum it is. The prism carries its own year-name; the year-name belongs to a king whose place in the Isin sequence is fixed by the king-lists and date-formula lists; and the sequence is anchored to absolute years by the conventional Babylonian chronology, whose middle option is the one used here. So the copy's date is about as firm as a cuneiform date gets — harder than the palaeographic estimate that carries the Eridu Genesis, and moved only by the low/middle/high chronology debate, which shifts second-millennium dates by decades to about a century rather than by millennia. What the prism's date does not fix is the age of the composition, still less of the tradition, and the Ur III manuscript — placed by script and by the archival context of its find, not by any date-formula — is the reminder that the composition had a history before this copy. For the Ur III manuscript and the absence of the antediluvian prologue from it and from several Nippur copies, see P. Steinkeller, "An Ur III Manuscript of the Sumerian King List," in Literatur, Politik und Recht in Mesopotamien: Festschrift für Claus Wilcke (Wiesbaden: Harrassowitz, 2003), 267–92, summarized with the manuscript census at Sumerian King List. The natural reading is that the antediluvian section is a prefix, welded onto an existing dynastic list — the same weld visible from the other side in the Eridu Genesis, where a flood story is set inside a creation-cities-kingship frame. That does not make the flood tradition young; it makes the King List's flood a borrowing into a chronicle, not an independent chronicle of a flood.

The list is a political argument, not an archive

Jacobsen's central finding was that the King List presents as a single succession — kingship passing from city to city, one dynasty at a time — what was in fact a set of overlapping and often rival dynasties. The document's thesis is that legitimate kingship over Sumer is single and indivisible and is held by one city at a time, which is a claim its Isin-period compilers had every reason to make. Of the pre-Sargonic rulers it names, only a handful are corroborated by contemporary inscriptions, and current handbooks warn that the list's value as a chronological source for that era is severely limited. Its real value is as evidence for how Old Babylonian scribes narrated their own deep past — which is precisely the question this page is asking of it.
