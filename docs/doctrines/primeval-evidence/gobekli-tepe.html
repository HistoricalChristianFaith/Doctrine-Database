Göbekli Tepe on the Flood and the Nephilim in the material record

Dates: c. 9600–8000 BC (Pre-Pottery Neolithic A into the middle PPNB; the earliest hard radiocarbon determinations from Enclosure D calibrate to c. 9664–9311 BC) · dating method: radiocarbon at the early end, lithic typology at the late end — two different kinds of argument that should not be conflated (below) · K. Schmidt, Paléorient 26 (2000)
Summary: A hill of superimposed stone enclosures above the Harran plain in south-eastern Turkey, ringed with carved T-shaped monoliths up to five and a half metres tall and sixteen tonnes in weight, raised by people who had no pottery, no metal, no writing, and — on the excavators' reading of the faunal and botanical remains — no domesticated crops or animals. It is the oldest monumental architecture known, and it is the earliest witness on this shelf: everything else here, tablet or stratum, is younger by millennia. Its evidential weight is not that it says anything about a flood — it says nothing about one — but that it is a fixed, well-dated horizon of organized human society that any reconstructed Flood chronology has to accommodate somewhere.

Claims & evidence

Monumental building before agriculture

Klaus Schmidt, who directed the excavation from 1995 until his death in 2014, reported enclosures of the lower layer (Layer III) built as roughly circular arrangements of T-pillars set into a bench wall, with two larger free-standing pillars at the centre — the whole raised, quarried, carved, and transported by a population whose tool-kit is Pre-Pottery Neolithic A and whose food remains are those of hunters and gatherers. The pillars carry relief carving of foxes, boars, snakes, cranes, scorpions and vultures, and several bear arms, hands and belts, so that the T is a stylized human or supra-human figure. Schmidt's own inference — that cult drove sedentism rather than the reverse — has been contested (see below); what is not contested is the sequence: the megaliths precede domestication in this region rather than following it.

— Klaus Schmidt, "Göbekli Tepe, Southeastern Turkey. A Preliminary Report on the 1995–1999 Excavations," Paléorient 26/1 (2000): 45–54.

Karahan Tepe and the Taş Tepeler complex

Göbekli Tepe is no longer a solitary marvel. Since 2019 Necmi Karul has excavated its sister mound Karahan Tepe, some thirty-five kilometres to the east, under the Turkish Ministry of Culture's Taş Tepeler ("Stone Hills") programme, which now coordinates the study of Göbekli Tepe and more than a dozen related Neolithic sites across the Şanlıurfa region, the settlement wave placed "around the middle of the 10th millennium B.C." Karahan Tepe's signature find is a chamber — Structure AB — cut seven metres by six and three and a half deep into the living bedrock, from which ten pillars (some reports say eleven) were left standing as the rock around them was carried away, shaped like phalluses, with a human head sculpted from the long wall so that it seems to gaze across at them. The chamber is subtractive rock-cutting rather than assembled masonry, and it, like the Göbekli enclosures, is the work of people with no pottery, no metal, and no writing. What it establishes is that the pre-agricultural monumentality of Göbekli Tepe was not a single anomaly but a regional culture — which sharpens both the datum this witness supplies and the interpretations built on it.

— Necmi Karul, "Buried Buildings at Pre-Pottery Neolithic Karahantepe," Türk Arkeoloji ve Etnografya Dergisi 82 (2021); with the official "Karahantepe," Taş Tepeler project page (Structure AB's dimensions, the bedrock-cut pillars, and the wall-head), and "11,000-year-old carving of a man… discovered at Karahan Tepe," Live Science.

The dating, and why it is harder than it looks

The commonly quoted span c. 9600–8000 BC rests on two different kinds of argument, and they should not be conflated. The lower end comes from radiocarbon: a sample of wall plaster from Enclosure D returned 9984 ± 42 14C BP, calibrating to roughly 9664–9311 BC, which is the earliest direct measurement tied to a standing structure rather than to fill. The upper end is typological: projectile points from the site run through PPNA forms (el-Khiam, Helwan, Aswad) into common PPNB forms (Byblos, Nemrik) but stop short of the characteristic latest-PPNB types, which places abandonment around 8000 BC.

The complication is the site's formation process. The Layer III enclosures were, at the end of their use, cleaned, partly dismantled, and packed with a fill of rubble, sediment, flint and animal bone. Charcoal from that fill dates the filling, not the building — and the fill may itself contain older material moved from elsewhere, so the stratigraphy can invert. Any confident single date for "when Göbekli Tepe was built" is therefore a date for one enclosure and one event in its life, and the enclosures are not all contemporary: the single determination from Enclosure C (9700 ± 30 14C BP, c. 9261–9139 BC) suggests work on it was under way while D was being buried.

— O. Dietrich and K. Schmidt, "A Radiocarbon Date from the Wall Plaster of Enclosure D of Göbekli Tepe," Neo-Lithics 2/10 (2010): 82–83; O. Dietrich et al., "Establishing a Radiocarbon Sequence for Göbekli Tepe: State of Research and New Data," in Neolithic Corporate Identities (2013), PDF; summarized by the excavation team at "How old is it? Dating Göbekli Tepe," The Tepe Telegrams (German Archaeological Institute).

The excavators' interpretation is itself disputed

Schmidt read the enclosures as sanctuaries — "the world's first temple," in the phrase the press adopted — built by dispersed hunter-gatherer bands who had no settlement there. E. B. Banning argued the opposite reading from the same material: that the buildings are houses, or house-like structures with domestic debris in them, that the sharp separation of "sacred" from "domestic" is a modern import, and that identifying a Neolithic temple requires evidence the site does not supply. Later excavation seasons have strengthened his side of the argument by turning up domestic-looking architecture and water cisterns on the mound. Nothing in this dispute moves the dates; it moves what the dates are evidence of.

— E. B. Banning, "So Fair a House: Göbekli Tepe and the Identification of Temples in the Pre-Pottery Neolithic of the Near East," Current Anthropology 52/5 (2011): 619–660.

Why it sits on this timeline

Göbekli Tepe is the datum that a Flood chronology has to place, and it is awkward to place. Take the two commonest placements in turn, without prejudging either.

  • Flood later than Göbekli Tepe (the placement implied by any reconstruction that anchors the deluge in the Mesopotamian alluvial deposits, c. 3500–2900 BC — see the flood deposits). Then Göbekli Tepe is antediluvian, and a global deluge passed over it. The mound would then have to show a flood horizon; what it shows instead is a fill placed by human hands, containing knapped flint and butchered bone, with a continuous cultural sequence running through it.
  • Flood earlier than Göbekli Tepe. Then the deluge lies more than five thousand years before the first writing, and every text on this shelf — the King List, Atrahasis, Gilgamesh, Genesis — is separated from the event by a gap that no documentary chain spans.

Whether either placement can be made to work, and on what model, is the business of the Göbekli Tepe argument, where it is weighed adversarially. The point of the witness block is the datum, not the verdict.

Notes

  • A widely circulated claim connects Göbekli Tepe's Pillar 43 ("the Vulture Stone") to a dated comet impact at the Younger Dryas onset (Sweatman and Tsikritsis, "Decoding Göbekli Tepe with Archaeoastronomy," Mediterranean Archaeology and Archaeometry 17/1 [2017]: 233–250). The excavation team rejected it — among other objections, the proposed date for Pillar 43 runs some 700–1,000 years ahead of the radiocarbon evidence, and reliefs on the pillar's narrow sides were left out of the reading: J. Notroff et al., "More than a vulture: A response to Sweatman and Tsikritsis," MAA 17/2 (2017): 57–63. It is not relied on here, and it is a separate question from the site's dating.
  • The backfill problem — fill charcoal dating the burial rather than the building, and capable of inverting the sequence — is a real limitation of a real kind, published by the excavators themselves. On how far it and the other methods behind this shelf's dates can be pushed, see are these witnesses securely dated?; the short answer at this site is that the genuine uncertainty moves it by centuries within the Neolithic, not out of it.
  • The site is also a fixture of popular arguments for a lost advanced civilization — that megaliths of this scale and finish imply techniques, or a builder, that the conventional account does not allow, read variously as the biblical Eden, as the work of the Watchers of 1 Enoch, or as a deliberately buried antediluvian world. That is a claim about manufacture and identity, and it is a separate question from the site's date; it is weighed at Do Göbekli Tepe and Karahan Tepe attest a lost pre-Flood civilization? (assessment: weak; the underlying early-complexity datum sound but modest), among the out-of-place-artifact arguments. The separate question of where the site falls on a Flood chronology is its own argument. The witness block here reports the datum and leaves the verdict there.
  • Early monumental building is not a solitary anomaly that calls for a lost civilization; it is a recurring, well-dated pattern. In the New World the same surprise arrives again and again inside the ordinary chronology. Watson Brake in Louisiana — eleven mounds joined by a ring embankment — was raised by mobile hunter-gatherers around 5,400–5,000 years ago, the oldest known earthwork complex in the Americas and proof that monumental construction does not wait on farming or on the state (Joe W. Saunders et al., "A Mound Complex in Louisiana at 5000–5400 Years Before the Present," Science 277 [1997]: 1796–1799); the later Poverty Point earthworks (c. 3,700–3,100 years ago) were built by hunter-fisher-gatherers on the same principle. And airborne lidar has lately exposed dense early complexity where forest hid it: a two-thousand-year-old network of platform towns, plazas and roads in the Upano Valley of Amazonian Ecuador (Stéphen Rostain et al., "Two thousand years of garden urbanism in the Upper Amazon," Science 383 [2024]: 183–189) and low-density urbanism with stepped platforms, causeways and reservoirs in the Bolivian Llanos de Mojos (Heiko Prümers et al., "Lidar reveals pre-Hispanic low-density urbanism in the Bolivian Amazon," Nature 606 [2022]: 325–328). None of these is an out-of-place artifact: each is securely dated within the conventional timeline and needs no lost technique. They are cited here only as calibration — that a society building at monumental scale earlier or more elaborately than a textbook expected is normal across the record, so Göbekli Tepe's farmerless builders are one instance of a widespread phenomenon rather than a unique riddle.
