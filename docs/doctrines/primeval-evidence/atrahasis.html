Atrahasis on the Flood and the Nephilim in the material record

Dates: c. 1635 BC (the dated copy) · Wikipedia
Summary: The Old Babylonian poem Atra-ḫasīs — "when the gods were man" — carries the oldest substantially preserved flood narrative with an ark and a named survivor, and it is the only one of the three Mesopotamian flood texts whose age rests on a dated colophon rather than on palaeography. Its evidential value on this page is that of a fixed point: it puts an ark-and-survivor flood story in writing in southern Mesopotamia in the seventeenth century BC, centuries before the Genesis text as we have it. What it does not supply, and what the corroboration arguments have to weigh, is any independent witness to the flood it describes — the text is a datum about the antiquity of the tradition, not directly about the antiquity of the event.

A note on where this sits. This timeline is ordered by the age of the evidence, not the age of the event the evidence reports. Atrahasis leads the sequence because its tablets are dated, not because it describes the earliest flood.

Claims & evidence

The copy is dated: the twelfth year of Ammi-ṣaduqa, c. 1635 BC

The principal Old Babylonian manuscript is a three-tablet set written out by a scribe who signed and dated his work. The colophons name him and fix the year:

Month Nisan, 21st day, the year when Ammi-saduqa, the king, …

Written by Ku-Aya, the junior scribe

— Atra-ḫasīs: The Babylonian Story of the Flood, ed. W. G. Lambert and A. R. Millard (Oxford: Clarendon, 1969), the date-formula and scribal signature of the Tablet I colophon (edition online). Lambert and Millard place the writing of Tablet I on the 21st of Nisan in Ammi-ṣaduqa's twelfth year — c. 1635 BC on the middle chronology — and Tablet III on the 28th of Shebat; Ammi-ṣaduqa was the great-great-grandson of Hammurabi. This is why Atrahasis anchors the sequence: a dated tablet is a harder datum than a palaeographic estimate.

The flood is sent because mankind is too loud — not because mankind is wicked

The land was bellowing like a bull,
The god got disturbed with their uproar.
Enlil heard their noise
And addressed the great gods,
"The noise of mankind has become too intense for me,
With their uproar I am deprived of sleep."

— Atra-ḫasīs II.i.3–8, in Lambert and Millard's edition, p. 73. The motive matters for the comparison with Genesis. Enlil's grievance is rigmu and ḫubūru — noise, clamour, the racket of an over-multiplied species — and the flood is the last in an escalating series of population controls (plague, then famine, then deluge). Genesis 6:5–13 gives a moral cause, not a demographic one; the divergence is the standard exhibit for those who read the resemblance between the two accounts as literary rather than testimonial.

The ark: a house pulled down and rebuilt as a boat, roofed "like the Apsû"

Wall, listen to me!
Reed wall, observe all my words!
Destroy your house, build a boat,
Spurn property, and save life…
Roof it over like the Apsû.
So that the sun shall not see inside it
Let it be roofed over above and below.

— Atra-ḫasīs III.i.20–31, in Lambert and Millard's edition, p. 89. Enki is under oath with the other gods not to warn mankind, so he speaks to a reed wall and lets the hero overhear — a device kept, almost word for word, in Tablet XI of Gilgamesh. The vessel is not a ship of stated cubit dimensions as in Genesis 6:15; it is a house converted, sealed above and below.

Seven days of deluge, and the gods swarming to the survivor's sacrifice

For seven days and seven nights
Came the deluge, the storm, [the flood].

[The gods sniffed] the smell,
They gathered [like flies] over the offering.

— Atra-ḫasīs III.iv.24–25 and III.v.34–35, in Lambert and Millard's edition, pp. 96–99 (square brackets are the editors' restorations of damaged text). The seven-day duration, the survivor's sacrifice on disembarking, and the gods' hungry converging on it recur in Gilgamesh XI.128–133 and 161–163; Genesis keeps the sacrifice and the pleasing odour (Gen 8:20–21) but not the divine famine that motivates it here. In Atrahasis the mother-goddess then rounds on the assembly for having "decided on total destruction," and the gods' answer to the overpopulation problem is not another flood but stillbirth, infertility, and celibate priestesses — an ending with no counterpart in Genesis at all.

Notes

  • The hero's name, Atra-ḫasīs ("exceedingly wise"), is the same epithet applied to the flood hero in the Assyrian recension of Gilgamesh XI.49, where the crowd assembles "at Atar-hasis's gate" — one of the seams showing the flood episode in Gilgamesh was taken over from this poem.
  • Later recensions (Middle Babylonian, Assyrian, Neo-Babylonian) diverge from Ku-Aya's text, so the c. 1635 BC date fixes a written form of the story, not the story's origin, which is older and unrecoverable.
