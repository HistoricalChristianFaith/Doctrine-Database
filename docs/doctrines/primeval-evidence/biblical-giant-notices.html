The biblical giant-tribe notices on the Flood and the Nephilim in the material record

Dates: c. 7th–5th centuries BC (the notices as texts; the events they report are set some seven centuries earlier, and the oldest physical copies are the Qumran scrolls of the 2nd–1st centuries BC) · dating method: source-critical for the notices as compositions — the least secure kind of date on this shelf, since it is an argument about the growth of a text rather than a measurement of an object, and the ranges scholars give for the Deuteronomistic and Priestly material are wide and disputed. The physical copies are a separate and much firmer matter: the Qumran manuscripts are dated palaeographically, with radiocarbon on the skins broadly confirming the script-based ranges — but they date copies, not the composition
Summary: The Anakim, Rephaim, Emim, Zamzummim, and Og of Bashan — the Hebrew Bible's roster of oversized peoples in and around Canaan, and the only body of text that claims a post-Flood giant population still walking around in a datable historical period. This page treats the roster as what it demonstrably is, a datable textual witness to the tradition, and then asks the question this timeline exists to ask: is any of it a physical datum? The doctrinal question — how the Church read these peoples as the Nephilim's descendants — belongs to the Nephilim belief-history; the readings of Genesis 6:4's "and also afterward" that the roster forces belong to the post-Flood Nephilim problem.

On the placement. This witness sits by the date of the notices, not by the date of the conquest they narrate — the shelf on this page is ordered by the age of the evidence, and the evidence here is a text. One external attestation discussed below is considerably older than the notices themselves; it is treated here rather than given its own earlier slot because its whole interest is what it does or does not corroborate in them. And the placement is deliberately loose-jointed: a source-critical date is an argument, not a measurement, and nothing on this page turns on the notices belonging to one century rather than another within that range. What matters evidentially is the gap — centuries wide on any dating — between the events narrated and the earliest text narrating them. On what each dating method behind this shelf does and does not establish, see are these witnesses securely dated?

Claims & evidence

What the texts actually say

The roster is smaller and more repetitive than its reputation. Five passages carry nearly all of it.

  • The spies' report. "The land, through which we have gone to search it, is a land that eateth up the inhabitants thereof; and all the people that we saw in it are men of a great stature. And there we saw the giants, the sons of Anak, which come of the giants: and we were in our own sight as grasshoppers, and so we were in their sight" (Numbers 13:32–33). The narrative itself labels this an "evil report" — a false one, in the story's own judgement — and Caleb and Joshua, who walked the same ground, do not repeat the giants.
  • The Deuteronomic parentheses. "The Emims dwelt therein in times past, a people great, and many, and tall, as the Anakims; which also were accounted giants, as the Anakims; but the Moabites call them Emims" (Deuteronomy 2:10–11); and, of the Ammonite country, "That also was accounted a land of giants: giants dwelt therein in old time; and the Ammonites call them Zamzummims" (Deuteronomy 2:20–21). Both are antiquarian asides in a travel itinerary, each ending in a philological gloss of the form "but the X call them Y."
  • Og's bedstead. "For only Og king of Bashan remained of the remnant of giants; behold, his bedstead was a bedstead of iron; is it not in Rabbath of the children of Ammon? nine cubits was the length thereof, and four cubits the breadth of it, after the cubit of a man" (Deuteronomy 3:11).
  • The conquest and the remnant. "Joshua destroyed them utterly with their cities. There was none of the Anakims left in the land of the children of Israel: only in Gaza, in Gath, and in Ashdod, there remained" (Joshua 11:21–22) — the verse that hands the giant line to Philistia, and so to Goliath "of Gath" (1 Samuel 17:4) and the four Gittite champions "born to the giant," one of them polydactyl, of 2 Samuel 21:19–22.
  • The oldest layer, and the register. The Rephaim, Zuzim, and Emim appear as ordinary defeated peoples in the campaign notice of Genesis 14:5, with no comment on their size at all; and Amos, describing the same Amorite enemy, writes that his "height was like the height of the cedars, and he was strong as the oaks" (Amos 2:9) — an explicit simile, which is the biblical corpus labelling its own register in the one place it cannot be mistaken.

Two literary features are worth naming before any archaeology, because they set what a physical trace would have to match. First, no giant in the roster is measured except Og's furniture and Goliath, and Goliath's number is itself unstable in the manuscript tradition (see how tall was Goliath?). Second, the giant notices are concentrated in passages doing antiquarian work — explaining who used to hold a territory and what its neighbours called them — which is a scribe's register, not a surveyor's.

— Texts as above, King James Version.

The one possible external attestation of the name: the execration texts

Egyptian Middle Kingdom execration texts — hieratic curses written on figurines and bowls that were then ritually smashed, the Berlin group of the 20th century BC and the Brussels group of the 19th–18th — list Canaanite enemies of pharaoh by ruler and people. Among them is a group read as ʾiy-ʿanaq, "the people of Anaq," with three named rulers: Erum, Abiyamimu, and Akirum. If the identification with the biblical ʿanaq holds, it is the earliest external trace of the roster by some eight centuries, and it puts a real Canaanite population group behind the name.

The identification is argued rather than settled, and its evidential yield is narrow even if granted. It attests a people-name and three rulers' names — not a stature, not a burial, not a bone. Nothing in the Egyptian material describes the Iy-ʿanaq as large; the Egyptians cursing them had every rhetorical motive to say so and did not. What the texts would establish is that Numbers and Joshua are naming a real Bronze Age Canaanite entity, which is a point about the tradition's rootedness, not about anyone's height.

The Rephaim are attested — as the dead

The one name on the roster with unambiguous extra-biblical standing is the Rephaim, and the attestation cuts against the giant-population reading rather than for it. The Ugaritic texts know the rpʾum: a company of deified ancestral kings, summoned at royal funerals and hailed with a divinized patron, "Rapiu, King of Eternity," enthroned at ʿṯtrt and hdrʿy — Ashtaroth and Edrei, which are precisely Og's two royal cities in Deuteronomy 1:4. This is a hard and near-consensus result, and it explains the biblical Rephaim who lie in Sheol rather than march to war (Isaiah 14:9).

Its bearing here is precise. The material and epigraphic trace of "Rephaim" that we actually possess is a cult of the royal dead located in exactly the Transjordanian geography the giant-notices attach to Og. That is a genuine external corroboration of the biblical text at the level of names, places, and institutions — and it is corroboration of a mortuary cult, not of living giants.

— Both readings are weighed on this wiki at the Rephaim as the spirits of dead kings (KTU 1.161) and Og's cities Ashtaroth and Edrei as the seat of the Ugaritic Rapiu (KTU 1.108), each rated sound.

Og's bed: the roster's only measurable object, and it is a monument

Deuteronomy 3:11 is the single place where the giant tradition offers a number attached to a thing that could in principle be gone and looked at. Nine cubits by four — roughly 4 by 1.8 metres — of iron, on display "in Rabbath of the children of Ammon," which is to say in a foreign capital, as a curiosity, generations after Og. The object is offered as a public exhibit, not as a grave.

What it is not is skeletal. The older commentary reading it as a basalt sarcophagus has been superseded, and the stronger case now on the table reads the dimensions against the cuneiform record of the bed of Marduk — a cult-bed of comparable proportions — making Og's ʿeres a monument in the idiom of royal and divine furniture rather than a bedstead scaled to a sleeper. Either way the datum is monumental and rhetorical: an object in a treasury, doing the work of a trophy. The Golan does hold real megalithic architecture that could sustain that idiom — the dolmen fields and the concentric ring of Rujm el-Hiri — and nothing in it is a bed or a burial of extraordinary size.

The skeletal record where the roster puts the remnant

Joshua 11:22 makes a locatable claim: after the conquest the Anakim survive "only in Gaza, in Gath, and in Ashdod." That is Philistia, and Philistia is where the roster's one measured champion comes from. It is therefore the one place where the tradition can be tested against bones rather than against texts — and, unusually for this kind of question, there are bones.

The Leon Levy Expedition's 2013–2016 seasons at Ashkelon recovered some 231 individuals from the first indisputably Philistine cemetery ever excavated, Iron Age IIA. Sherry Fox measured maximum crown–heel lengths on the sixteen adults (eight male, eight female) complete and extended enough to take directly, rather than estimating from regression formulae built on other populations. Mean male stature: 154.6 cm, range 144.9–164.5; mean female 147.2 cm. Roughly five foot one. Fox's own summary is blunt: contrary to the stature of the most famous Philistine, the Philistines were relatively short.

The caveats are real and should be stated rather than buried. The measured sample is sixteen, not 231. The cemetery is Iron IIA, a few centuries after the conquest the notices narrate. And the low sexual dimorphism and the dental growth-arrest lines in the same population point to nutritional stress, so this is a stressed population's stature, not a regional baseline. What the finding cannot be made to mean is that a giant lineage was there and went unnoticed: a population with hereditary gigantism in it does not produce a cemetery whose tallest measured adult is 164.5 cm.

— Sherry C. Fox, "The Stature of the Philistines" (Leon Levy Expedition to Ashkelon), abstract and related abstract, 81st Annual Meeting of the Society for American Archaeology (Vancouver, 2016); reporting of the cemetery and its osteology in Bruce Bower, "Ancient cemetery provides peek into Philistines' lives, health," Science News (2016), and "Unearthed bones bring Philistines to life," Harvard Gazette (2016).

What this witness contributes to the reconstruction

Set out plainly, the roster yields three things and withholds one.

  • It yields names and places that are externally real — a Canaanite Iy-ʿanaq in the execration texts, if the reading holds; a Rephaim tradition at Ugarit anchored to Og's own two cities.
  • It yields one measurable object, and that object is a monument on display in a foreign capital, read most strongly as royal-cultic furniture.
  • It yields a literary register that the corpus itself flags: an "evil report" the narrative disowns, antiquarian glosses, and an explicit cedar-simile.
  • It withholds a body. There is no stratum, no burial, and no skeleton anywhere in the roster's geography that attests the population it describes — and in the one district where the text says the remnant survived, the excavated dead are short.

That is a witness with real evidential content. It is not, on its own, a material trace of a giant population, and the arguments on this timeline take up whether anything else supplies one.

Notes

  • The roster is not uniform in what it claims. Deuteronomy 2 says the Emim and Zamzummim were "accounted giants" and goes on to say the Lord destroyed them long before Israel arrived — a claim about a vanished people, made in passing, in a text explaining place-names. Numbers 13 says the spies saw them. These are different kinds of assertion, and flattening them into one "giant tribes" datum overstates the corpus.
