The Black Sea infill stratum on the Flood and the Nephilim in the material record

Dates: c. 5600 BC (the shelf-drowning horizon as dated by Ryan and Pitman — 7,150 radiocarbon years BP; the same team later revised the timing toward c. 8.4 ka BP, and rival readings put the reconnection at c. 9,400 cal BP and make it gradual) · Ryan et al., Marine Geology 138 (1997)
Summary: A change of facies in the sediments of the Black Sea shelf — freshwater molluscs below, marine molluscs above, with a drowned shoreline and river channels preserved beneath — which William Ryan and Walter Pitman interpreted in 1997 as the trace of a catastrophic inundation: the Mediterranean rising to the Bosporus sill and pouring through, submerging more than 100,000 km² of exposed continental shelf, and driving the farmers living on it outward into Europe and Anatolia. Their 1998 popular book put the suggestion in its title: Noah's Flood. This is the most-cited candidate for a real regional catastrophe large enough to seed a flood-tradition — and it is also the most heavily contested item on this shelf.

Claims & evidence

The observation: a freshwater lake that becomes a sea

The evidential core is not in dispute even where the interpretation is. During the last glaciation the Black Sea was cut off from the world ocean and became a freshwater or brackish lake whose surface, on Ryan and Pitman's reconstruction, drew down more than a hundred metres below its outlet. Cores from the shelf show freshwater molluscan assemblages overlain by marine ones, and the seismic profiles show a former shoreline, wave-cut terraces, and incised river valleys now under water. Something reconnected the basin to the Mediterranean, and the fauna changed when it did. The questions are how fast and when.

— W. B. F. Ryan, W. C. Pitman III, C. O. Major, K. Shimkus, V. Moskalenko, G. A. Jones, P. Dimitrov, N. Görür, M. Sakınç, and H. Yüce, "An abrupt drowning of the Black Sea shelf," Marine Geology 138 (1997): 119–126; popularized in W. Ryan and W. Pitman, Noah's Flood: The New Scientific Discoveries About the Event That Changed History (New York: Simon & Schuster, 1998).

The catastrophic reading

On Ryan and Pitman's account the reconnection at c. 7,150 14C yr BP was a breach, not a rise: seawater cut through the Bosporus with enormous discharge, the lake level climbed fast enough to be lethal to settlement on the shelf, and the drowning was measured in months, not millennia. That reading is what makes the event a candidate memory-seed: a slow transgression displaces people over generations and leaves no story, whereas a breach displaces them in a single season and does. The proposed date also sits in roughly the right place to be ancestral to a Mesopotamian tradition that is first written down in the early second millennium BC.

The gradualist reading, and why it has the stronger recent literature

Ali Aksu, Richard Hiscott and colleagues advanced an "Outflow Hypothesis" on directly contrary evidence, and their case has three legs. First, a delta at the southern exit of the Bosporus, radiocarbon-dated to 9–10,000 years ago, which requires Black Sea water to have been flowing south for millennia before the proposed breach. Second, the absence, in the mud drape on the floor of the Sea of Marmara, of the erosion a violent northward surge would have cut. Third, a reinterpretation of the mollusc change itself: not a basin suddenly filled with seawater, but the gradual rise of the density interface between fresh surface water and saline bottom water above shelf depth, which changes what lives on the shelf without any catastrophe. Their conclusion was that the Black Sea "has been at or above Bosphorus sill depth, flowing into the world ocean unabated since ~10.5 ky BP," with two-way exchange thereafter.

— A. E. Aksu, R. N. Hiscott, P. J. Mudie, A. Rochon, M. A. Kaminski, T. Abrajano, and D. Yaşar, "Persistent Holocene Outflow from the Black Sea to the Eastern Mediterranean Contradicts Noah's Flood Hypothesis," GSA Today 12/5 (2002): 4–10. For the state of the dispute across the wider geological, palaeontological and archaeological record, see V. Yanko-Hombach et al., "Controversy over the great flood hypotheses in the Black Sea in light of geological, paleontological, and archaeological evidence," Quaternary International 167–168 (2007): 91–113.

What the witness actually supplies

Three things, and it is worth keeping them apart.

  • A real event. The Black Sea did reconnect to the Mediterranean in the early Holocene and its shelf was drowned. That is not in question.
  • A contested rate. Whether the drowning was catastrophic or progressive is genuinely open, with the weight of the post-2002 literature on the gradualist side. A gradual reconnection is a much poorer candidate for a memory-seed, because it gives nobody anything to remember.
  • No textual chain. Even on the catastrophic reading, nothing connects the Black Sea shelf to the Mesopotamian flood texts except plausibility. The earliest of those texts — Atrahasis — is written some four thousand years later, several hundred miles away, and describes a river-and-rain flood in an alluvial plain, not the sea coming in over a coast.

The adjudication is the business of the historical-kernel argument. Note also that the witness is doubled-edged for the reconstruction: a Black Sea event dated to c. 5600 BC would be a kernel for the tradition, but it is not the Flood of a young single-date global model, and it is far too late to be relevant to Göbekli Tepe, which is four thousand years older still.

Notes

  • The dating has moved even within the catastrophist camp: Ryan and co-workers later revised the drowning toward c. 8.4 ka BP. Any argument that leans on the c. 5600 BC figure should be stated as leaning on the 1997 version specifically.
  • This item attracts partisan citation from both directions — it is quoted by creationist writers as a scientific concession to a flood and by their critics as a refutation of one. Neither is what the literature says: the dispute is a technical one about Holocene sea level and Bosporus hydrography, and its participants are not arguing about Genesis.
