The modern scientific record on the Flood and the Nephilim in the material record

Dates: twentieth and twenty-first centuries (the youngest witness on this shelf; the findings gathered here range from Wallace's zoogeography of 1876 to a genomic bottleneck study of 2023 and the technical replies to it published through 2025)
Summary: Not a document or a stratum but a body of findings — stratigraphic, genomic, biogeographic — that the predictive arguments on this timeline are tested against. It is placed last because it is the youngest evidence, not because it is the final word. This page reports what the findings say and what their own literature disputes; it does not adjudicate. The adjudication, with the falsification conditions stated in advance and any failure scoped to the specific model that generated the prediction, belongs to the predictive arguments.

A word on method, since this is the witness most likely to break a model. Nothing here rests on ruling miracles out: an act of God is not excluded by any of it, and "no natural process allows this" is not the objection being made. The objection these findings can make is narrower and harder — that a proposed event should have left a particular physical trace, and the trace is looked for and is not there, or the trace that is there has the wrong date. That is an evidential argument, and it is answerable on evidential terms.

Claims & evidence

Stratigraphy: no synchronous worldwide deposit

The sedimentary record is not a single sequence but many local ones, correlated by fossil content, radiometric dates, and magnetic reversals; it contains, at innumerable places, features that require long quiet intervals interleaved with the deposition. Three examples of the kind of thing at issue, chosen because each is a positive datum rather than an absence:

  • Layered ice. The EPICA Dome C core from Antarctica preserves an unbroken accumulation sequence through eight glacial cycles, some 740,000 years, with the layering and isotopic cycling intact from top to bottom — a column that a global inundation would have to have left undisturbed while covering it.
  • Successive fossil forests. At Joggins in Nova Scotia, upright lycopsid trunks stand rooted in place in dozens of stacked horizons through hundreds of metres of section, each horizon a forest floor that grew, was buried, and was grown over again.
  • Evaporites. Thick salt and gypsum sequences require the repeated evaporation of standing water to dryness — the opposite condition to inundation — and are interbedded within the same columns.

The point these carry for this timeline is specific: there is no one contemporaneous worldwide deposit that the sequences share. Flood layers of the kind found in Mesopotamia are real, common, and locally severe — and each is dated to its own century (see the Mesopotamian flood deposits).

— EPICA community members, "Eight glacial cycles from an Antarctic ice core," Nature 429 (2004): 623–628; on the Joggins section, Joggins Fossil Cliffs (UNESCO World Heritage List, 2008).

Population genetics: the bottleneck that is there, and the one that is not

A survival of the human line through eight individuals a few thousand years ago is a genetic claim, and genetics can test claims of that shape: a sharp recent crash to a handful of founders leaves a distinctive loss of allelic diversity and a datable coalescence. Human genomes do carry bottleneck signals, but the well-attested ones are the out-of-Africa founder effect (tens of thousands of years old, and confined to non-African populations) rather than a species-wide crash in the Holocene.

The most striking recent claim runs the other way from a young model and is worth stating precisely because it is not the convenient result. Wangjie Hu and colleagues, applying a coalescent method (FitCoal) to modern genomes, inferred a severe ancestral bottleneck around 930,000–813,000 years ago reducing breeding individuals to roughly 1,280 for some 117,000 years. That is a bottleneck — but it is a thousand times older than a Holocene flood and more than a hundred times larger than eight people.

Honesty requires the rest of the story: that finding is itself contested in its own literature. Deng, Nielsen and colleagues argue the signal is likely a statistical artifact, noting that no comparable bottleneck appears in non-African populations, which is hard to reconcile with an event at 900 kya that should predate every modern population split; another group reports that they could reproduce the result with FitCoal on Yoruba data but not with an independent method (mushi) on the same site-frequency spectrum, and that a no-bottleneck model was never shown to fit worse. The Hu group has replied in turn. So the state of play is: a proposed deep-Pleistocene bottleneck, disputed; and no detected recent species-wide crash to a founding handful, which is the datum the predictive argument actually turns on.

— Wangjie Hu et al., "Genomic inference of a severe human bottleneck during the Early to Middle Pleistocene transition," Science 381 (2023): 979–984; contra, Yuxin Deng et al., "A previously reported bottleneck in human ancestry 900 kya is likely a statistical artifact," Genetics 229/1 (2025), and "Insufficient Evidence for a Severe Bottleneck in Humans During the Early to Middle Pleistocene Transition".

Biogeography: distributions that do not radiate from one point

If every land animal alive descends from a pair released at a single landing place a few thousand years ago, then the present distribution of animals is the result of a radiation outward from that point — and it should look like one. The pattern the discipline has documented since Wallace instead partitions the world into faunal regions whose boundaries track ancient geography rather than distance from any centre: marsupials essentially confined to Australasia and the Americas, with the fossil marsupials in the same regions; lemurs on Madagascar and nowhere else; the sharp faunal break at the Wallace Line between Bali and Lombok, two islands twenty miles apart; endemic radiations on oceanic islands whose nearest relatives are on the nearest mainland. Each of these is a distribution that a single-point dispersal must explain by selective migration — every marsupial reaching Australia and leaving no trace en route, and nothing else making the same journey.

— Alfred Russel Wallace, The Geographical Distribution of Animals, 2 vols. (London: Macmillan, 1876) — the founding statement of the faunal regions and of the line that carries his name; the framework has been much revised in detail and not overturned in outline.

What this witness does not settle

Three limits, stated so the arguments that use this page do not overclaim it.

  • None of these findings speaks to whether a local catastrophic flood occurred in the ancient Near East. They constrain a global one.
  • None of them rules out divine action. They rule out particular physical consequences of particular proposed events — which is a claim about traces, not about what God may do.
  • The genomic item is live and disputed within genetics, and any argument leaning on Hu et al. must lean on the disputed version or not at all. The undisputed part is the negative: no recent crash to eight has been detected.

Notes

  • An "effective population size" is not a head-count. It is the size of an idealized population that would lose diversity at the observed rate, and it can be substantially smaller than the true number of individuals — a point the critics of Hu et al. press, and one that cuts in both directions when a figure like 1,280 is compared with a narrative number like eight.
