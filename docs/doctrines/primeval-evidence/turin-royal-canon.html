The Turin Royal Canon on the Flood and the Nephilim in the material record

Dates: c. 1250 BC (a hieratic papyrus written under Ramesses II, r. c. 1279–1213 BC, on the reverse of a discarded tax register; now Museo Egizio, Turin, Papyrus 1874) · dating method: archaeological and palaeographic — the hand and the reused Ramesside recto, which fix the age of the copy firmly in the New Kingdom. The tens of thousands of years the papyrus assigns to its divine reigns are not a dating method and are not chronological data — see §the 36,620 years · Turin King List
Summary: An Egyptian king-list — the fullest one Egypt left, and the only one written on papyrus rather than carved on a wall — that lists the pharaohs dynasty by dynasty and prefaces the human kings with a former age of gods, demigods and "spirits". Before Menes and the First Dynasty reign the great gods, then the Shemsu Hor, the "Followers of Horus," with reigns of schematic, superhuman length. It is the Egyptian counterpart to the Sumerian King List's antediluvian god-kings: the same architecture of a divine golden former age of enormous reigns standing before ordinary human history — and, read honestly, the same lesson, that those huge numbers are ideology and schema, not a chronological record.

A note on where this sits. This timeline is ordered by the age of the evidence. The Turin Canon's copy is Ramesside, about 1250 BC, which lands it beside Gilgamesh Tablet XI in the sequence — not because the events it reports are of that age (its divine reigns claim tens of millennia) but because that is when this manuscript was written. What the papyrus reports about the deep past is exactly what the shelf is here to test, and the ordering keeps the two apart.

Claims & evidence

Gods, then the Followers of Horus, then Menes

The Turin Canon runs, in its surviving eleven columns, from a divine prologue down through the human dynasties as far as the New Kingdom — and its first columns give Egypt a pre-human government. A register of gods (the ennead — the reading of the broken names is debated, but Geb, Osiris, Seth, Horus, Thoth and their fellows stand in it) is followed by a class of lesser divine or ancestral rulers and then by the Shemsu Hor — the šmsw ḥr, "Followers" or "Companions of Horus" — who reign as the last pre-dynastic power, handing kingship on to Menes and the First Dynasty. Each name in the divine register is written in a cartouche exactly as a historical pharaoh's would be, and each was given a reign-length, though most of those figures are now lost with the papyrus. The document, in other words, does for Egypt precisely what the Sumerian King List does for Mesopotamia: it treats a heroic former age as the opening chapter of a continuous chronology, not as a separate myth.

— On the structure (gods → spirits/mythical kings → human dynasties, in eleven columns) and the physical document, Turin King List; on the Shemsu Hor as the predynastic "Followers of Horus" bridging the divine and human ages, Shemsu Hor. The standard critical editions are Alan H. Gardiner, The Royal Canon of Turin (Oxford: Griffith Institute, 1959), and Kim Ryholt, The Political Situation in Egypt during the Second Intermediate Period c. 1800–1550 B.C. (Copenhagen: Museum Tusculanum Press, 1997), whose re-reading of the traces revised many of Gardiner's entries.

The 36,620 years — and where the number comes from

The reigns in the divine prologue are not human lengths; they run to thousands of years apiece, and a summation is often quoted for the whole mythological section:

Venerables Shemsu-Hor, 13,420 years; Reigns before the Shemsu-Hor, 23,200 years; Total 36,620 years.

Two things have to be said about that tidy figure, and they are the honest core of this entry. First, its provenance: the reading is R. A. Schwaller de Lubicz's, from his Sacred Science, and it circulates almost entirely from him and his readers — it is an esoteric reconstruction, not the reading of Gardiner's or Ryholt's critical editions, and the numbers wobble from one retelling to the next (13,400 for 13,420, and so on). Second, the state of the evidence: the papyrus disintegrated in transit in the 1820s and survives as some 160 fragments with roughly half the original lost, so the divine reign-figures are among the most heavily reconstructed lines on it. A total offered to the exact year, off a document that is half gone, is carrying more precision than the object can bear. None of this depends on ruling the supernatural out: the point is not that gods cannot have reigned but that this papyrus does not attest that they did to any particular count of years — the number is a scribal and then a modern-esoteric artefact before it is a datum.

— The 36,620-year summation is quoted from R. A. Schwaller de Lubicz, Sacred Science: The King of Pharaonic Theocracy (New York: Inner Traditions, 1982), pp. 86–87; on the fragmentary state and the mainstream reading of the divine section as mythological rather than chronological, Turin King List.

The same architecture as the Sumerian list — and the same limit

Set beside the Sumerian King List, the parallel is structural and hard to miss. Both documents open the national chronology with a divine or heroic former age of enormous reigns; both then step down, through a transitional class, into humanly plausible kingship; both write the god-kings in the same formal register as the historical ones. The Sumerian list divides its two ages with a flood; the Turin Canon divides its with the passage from the Followers of Horus to Menes. And both encode their long reigns within a scheme rather than reporting them as data — the Sumerian reigns are whole multiples of the sexagesimal šár (3,600), the Egyptian ones are round and vast in the same spirit. That two unrelated Near-Eastern cultures independently gave themselves a golden former age of superhuman reigns is a genuine and interesting convergence.

But it is a convergence of traditions, and that is exactly its limit. A shared narrative pattern — "before ordinary history there was an age of vast reigns" — is strong evidence that the pattern was widespread and old; it is not evidence that the age itself was real, still less that it lasted the tens of thousands of years the numbers name. Convergent myths of a golden former age tell us about the shape of ancient memory and ideology, not about elapsed time. This is the same discipline the Sumerian entry applies to its own antediluvian reigns, and it is why the antediluvian-reigns argument — "do these reigns preserve a memory of a real pre-Flood/former age?" — has to reckon with the arithmetic and the ideology before it can treat any figure as a datum. The Turin Canon strengthens that argument only in the modest way an independent witness to a tradition can, and it inherits the argument's ceiling.

Notes

  • The Turin Canon is the antediluvian-longevity motif in its Egyptian dress — superhuman reigns before recorded history — and so parallels the vast pre-Flood lifespans of Genesis 5 and the like traditions noted elsewhere on this shelf. The comparison is to the motif, not to any shared count of years.
