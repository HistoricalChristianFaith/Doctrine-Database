The New Testament on Simon the Zealot

Dates: c. 30–90 (the events, and the writing of the Gospels and Acts) · Wikipedia
Summary: Simon appears only in the four lists of the Twelve, always eleventh — next to last, just before Judas Iscariot — and is given nothing but a surname to tell him apart from Simon Peter. Matthew and Mark call him “the Cananaean”; Luke and Acts, in the same slot, “the Zealot.” The two are one epithet in two languages, not two men or two facts, and neither means “from Cana” or “Canaanite.” What the “zeal” signified — the revolutionary party, or devotion to the Law — is the only question the New Testament raises about him.

Claims & evidence

“Simon the Cananaean” in Matthew and Mark

In the first two Gospels the eleventh apostle — standing in the third group of four, after James the son of Alphaeus and Thaddaeus — is Simon the Cananaean (the KJV, following the later spelling, has “Canaanite”):

… Simon the Canaanite, and Judas Iscariot, who also betrayed him.

— Matthew 10:4 (cf. Mark 3:18, “and Simon the Canaanite”). The Greek is ho Kananaios, a transliteration of the Aramaic qan’ana — “the zealous one.” It looks like a place-name to an English eye, but it is not: it neither means “from Cana” of Galilee nor “Canaanite” of the old nation, both of which would be spelled otherwise. It is the very word Luke will translate.

“Simon called Zelotes” in Luke and Acts — the same slot, translated

Luke, writing for Greek readers who would not catch the Aramaic, renders the epithet into Greek in both of his lists:

Matthew and Thomas, James the son of Alphaeus, and Simon called Zelotes.

— Luke 6:15 (cf. Acts 1:13, “and Simon Zelotes”). Zēlōtēs is the Greek for “zealous one” — the exact sense of the Aramaic Matthew and Mark preserved. So the two surnames are a translation-pair for a single description of one man, not two apostles and not two episodes in a life. That Luke bothers to distinguish this Simon by his epithet, as the others distinguish him as “the Cananaean,” is only because the Twelve already held one Simon of great prominence — Simon Peter — and a second Simon needed a tag.

What the epithet does not settle

The lists agree on the word and leave its force open. In first-century usage “zealous” (zēlōtēs) could name a temper — a man ardent for God and the Law, as Paul calls himself “zealous toward God” and “exceedingly zealous of the traditions” — or, later, a party: the armed nationalist faction Josephus calls “the Zealots,” which drove the revolt against Rome. The New Testament itself gives no hint which is meant here, and the two readings pull in opposite directions: on the one hand a former insurgent brought into the circle of Jesus alongside Matthew the tax-gatherer, who had served Rome; on the other simply a devout Galilean. The chronology of the party — which Josephus dates to the war of the 60s, a generation after the call of the Twelve — is the hinge of the question, and it is weighed on its own page.

Notes

  • One epithet, two languages. “Cananaean” (Matthew, Mark) and “Zelotes” (Luke, Acts) are the Aramaic and the Greek for the same word, “zealous” — a settled point, and the reason the two names never appear together for one man. Of Simon’s life, mission, or death the New Testament records nothing at all.
  • Not “of Cana” or “Canaanite.” The look-alike English “Canaanite” (KJV) is a spelling accident, not a claim about nationality or hometown; the underlying Kananaios is the zeal-word. Some later Eastern traditions nonetheless made him “Simon of Cana” and even the bridegroom at the wedding there — a folk etymology the word will not bear.
  • Three Simons to keep apart. Simon Peter, Simon the Zealot, and — outside the Twelve — Simeon son of Clopas, the Lord’s kinsman who became second bishop of Jerusalem. The apostle-lists would later confuse the last two; the confusion is traced on the apostle-lists page.
