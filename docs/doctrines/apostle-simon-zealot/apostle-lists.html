The apostle-lists on Simon the Zealot

Dates: uncertain (3rd–7th c. and later; mutually dependent) · Catholic Encyclopedia
Summary: Where the Gospels give Simon only a surname, the late catalogues of the apostles supply a mission and a death — and cannot agree on either, nor even keep his person distinct from another Simon. This page gathers what the apostle-list literature (Pseudo-Hippolytus, Pseudo-Dorotheus, and their Byzantine and Latin kin) says of him, and shows why it is church memory, not history. For the shared reliability key on this whole layer of sources, see the apostles hub.

Claims & evidence

A mission scattered across three continents

The lists send Simon almost everywhere, and rarely to the same place twice. The Greek menologies and the tradition behind Pseudo-Dorotheus give him a northern and western field — the coasts of the Black Sea, then Egypt and North Africa (Mauretania and “Africa the less”), and at last Britain, where one strand has him crucified. The Latin tradition, by contrast, keeps him in the East, pairing him with Jude/Thaddaeus and sending both to Persia. Later Georgian tradition plants his tomb in the Caucasus, at Nikopsia in Abkhazia. The Catholic Encyclopedia sums up the disorder plainly: “According to the Greeks, he preached on the Black Sea, in Egypt, Northern Africa, and Britain, while, according to the Latin Passio Simonis et Judae… Simon laboured in Persia, and was there martyred at Suanir.” No two of these can stand together, and none rests on an early or independent witness.1

The person confused: Simon the Zealot for Simeon of Jerusalem

The catalogues cannot even hold Simon steady as a single man. Pseudo-Hippolytus’s On the Twelve Apostles gives, at the apostle’s slot:

Simon the Zealot, the son of Clopas, who is also called Jude, became bishop of Jerusalem after James the Just, and fell asleep and was buried there at the age of 120 years.

— but this is not the apostle at all. It is Simeon son of Clopas, a kinsman of the Lord whom the early church remembered as the second bishop of Jerusalem, chosen to succeed James the Just, and whom Hegesippus (preserved in Eusebius) reports arrested and crucified under Trajan as an old man of a hundred and twenty. The list-maker has fused two different men — the silent apostle and the long-lived bishop — because both were called Simon/Simeon and one source made the bishop “son of Clopas.” The fusion is instructive: it shows the machinery by which a wordless apostle acquires a biography, here by absorbing the well-attested life and death of another.2

Death: sawn asunder, crucified, or hewn down

The manner of death is as unfixed as the field. Some passions have Simon sawn asunder — the grisly tradition that gave Western art its emblem, the saw, by which Simon is known in altarpieces to this day. Others report him crucified: at Suanir in Persia, or (in the British strand) among the Britons. The Golden Legend has the temple priests simply hack Simon and Jude to death together, and then hedges as to the cross. That the same figure is remembered as sawn in half, nailed to a cross, and hewn down by a mob — in three different countries — is again the mark of legend accreting around a name, not testimony converging on a fact.3

Notes

  • Mutually dependent, unreliable as history. The apostle-lists copy and contradict one another; they are valuable as evidence of how the church came to remember and commemorate Simon — feast-days, burial-claims, emblems — not as record of where he actually went or how he died. The house treatment of this layer is set out once on the apostles hub.
  • The Simeon confusion cuts both ways. Because Pseudo-Hippolytus lends the apostle Simeon of Jerusalem’s Jerusalem episcopate and death-at-120, some later lists make Simon the Zealot bishop of Jerusalem and a centenarian — details that belong by origin to the Lord’s kinsman, not to the apostle. Simeon son of Clopas is a distinct figure, and the two are best kept apart. Whether the fusion is a mere list-maker’s error, or whether the apostle and the bishop were genuinely one man — a thesis some ancient and modern sources actually advance — is weighed on its own argument page, “Was Simon the Zealot the same man as Simeon son of Clopas?” (assessment contested).
  • Why the missions matter. The clash of Persia, Britain, Egypt, and the Caucasus is itself the story: it is weighed as an argument on its own page (assessment weak).
