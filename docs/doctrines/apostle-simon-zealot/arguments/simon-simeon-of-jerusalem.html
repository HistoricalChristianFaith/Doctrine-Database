Was Simon the Zealot the same man as Simeon son of Clopas, the second bishop of Jerusalem?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — the identification is genuinely advanced (by the apostle-lists, the Chronicon Paschale, an apocryphal apostle-Preaching, and modern reference works that argue it), and a real family argument gives it a spine; against it stands the earliest good witness, Hegesippus, who presents Symeon son of Clopas as the Lord’s cousin, chosen to succeed the apostolic leadership precisely because a kinsman, not as one already of the Twelve. Strong enough to persist in the tradition, weak in its sources, with no decisive verdict either way · advanced by the apostle-lists (Pseudo-Hippolytus)

The claim

Simon the Zealot, the son of Clopas, who is also called Jude, became bishop of Jerusalem after James the Just, and fell asleep and was buried there at the age of 120 years.

— Pseudo-Hippolytus, On the Twelve Apostles 11. In one sentence the catalogue makes the apostle Simon the Zealot identical with Simeon son of Clopas, the second bishop of Jerusalem whom Hegesippus reports martyred under Trajan at 120. The claim under test is that identity: that the silent eleventh apostle and the long-lived kinsman-bishop are one man, so that Simon the Cananaean was the Lord’s cousin who took the Jerusalem chair after James the Just.1

Who advances it

The identity reading is old and recurring, not a modern guess. Pseudo-Hippolytus’s On the Twelve Apostles states it outright; the Chronicon Paschale “and elsewhere” identifies the apostle with “Simon Clopas”; and an apocryphal apostle-Preaching heads its hero “the blessed Simon, the son of Cleopas, who was surnamed Judas, which is interpreted Nathanael, who became bishop of Jerusalem after James the brother of our Lord” — heaping the apostle, the bishop, Jude, and Nathanael onto one figure. Modern reference works keep the thesis in play: the International Standard Bible Encyclopedia lays out the supporting case, and the Catholic Encyclopedia notes that Simon “is identified with Simon Clopas” in the tradition, “the same as Simeon of Jerusalem, or Simon the brother of the Lord, or both.”2

The evidence for

The steelman is stronger than the confused-catalogue origin suggests. The names align: both men are Simon/Simeon, both Galilean, both anchored to the Jerusalem church — and the apostle’s own dossier already drifts toward the bishop’s biography (his Jerusalem episcopate and death-at-120). A family argument gives it a spine: if Alphaeus is the same name as Clopas/Cleopas — a common equation — then Simon son of Clopas would be the brother of James son of Alphaeus, which would neatly explain why Luke and Acts set “James son of Alphaeus” and “Simon the Zealot” side by side in the lists: they were brothers. The Zealot epithet even fits the recruiting scene: a follower of the zealot cause, staking everything on the fulfilment of Messianic prophecy, is exactly the man to whom Philip’s “we have found him of whom Moses wrote” would appeal — the ground on which the same tradition further folds in Nathanael. And a kinsman among the Twelve is not far-fetched: the Lord’s brothers and cousins were prominent in the first church, so an apostle who was also a cousin, and who later held Jerusalem, would tie the apostolic and the dynastic leadership into one line.3

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The identification is coherent and cannot be ruled out on metaphysical or chronological grounds alone: a man born in the last years BC could have been called by Jesus and still have died, very old, under Trajan, and this wiki does not reject a claim merely because it requires a long life or strains a round number. That a cousin of the Lord sat among the Twelve, and later led Jerusalem, is a perfectly possible shape for the early church, and the Alphaeus–Clopas equation, if granted, would give the lists’ juxtaposition of James and Simon a tidy explanation.

What weighs against. The main resistance comes from the identification’s own best witness. Hegesippus — the earliest historical (not legendary) reporter of the Lord’s kindred, and the source Eusebius leans on — presents Symeon son of Clopas not as one of the Twelve but as the Lord’s cousin, put forward to succeed James “because he was a cousin of the Lord.” On the natural reading that qualification tells against his already being an apostle: the logic of the succession is that, the apostolic leader having been martyred, the church turned to a kinsman to carry on — a point no one would trouble to state of a man who was himself of the Twelve. Eusebius, who knew Hegesippus intimately, nowhere equates this bishop with the apostle Simon the Zealot, and never calls Symeon an apostle. The pro-sources, for their part, are the layer the record trusts least: Pseudo-Hippolytus and its kin are the mutually-dependent catalogues that also scatter Simon across three continents and cannot hold his person steady, so their fusion may be a wordless apostle absorbing a better-attested life rather than a memory of identity. The spine of the modern case, the Alphaeus–Clopas equation, is itself the contested Hieronymian move, not a fixed premise; and the further layer that Simon was also Nathanael (usually Bartholomew) and also Jude strains the reading further. (The divergent death-traditions — the apostle sent to Persia and sawn or crucified there, the bishop crucified at Jerusalem under Trajan — cut only weakly here, since the apostle’s mission-and-death traditions are themselves worthless.) None of this refutes the identity outright: Hegesippus’ silence is an argument from silence, the succession-logic is suggestive rather than probative, and a cousin who was also an apostle remains possible. What it does is leave the two sides balanced — a recurring tradition with a genuine family argument on one hand, a sober early witness that keeps cousin-bishop and apostle apart on the other.4

Assessment

Contested. The equation of Simon the Zealot with Simeon son of Clopas is genuinely advanced and cannot be dismissed: it is old (Pseudo-Hippolytus, the Chronicon Paschale, an apocryphal apostle-Preaching) and carried by a real family argument — if Alphaeus is Clopas, Simon son of Clopas is the brother of James son of Alphaeus, explaining their juxtaposition in the lists. But it leans on the least reliable stratum of the apostolic record, and the earliest trustworthy witness, Hegesippus in Eusebius, presents Symeon as the Lord’s cousin chosen to succeed the apostolic leadership because a kinsman — more naturally a bishop who was not himself of the Twelve. That tells against the identity without disproving it. With a persistent tradition and a genuine family argument on one side, and a sober early witness pulling the other way, the question has no decisive answer: the mainstream keeps the two Simons apart, but the identification remains defensible.5
