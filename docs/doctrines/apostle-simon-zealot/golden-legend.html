The Golden Legend on Simon the Zealot

Dates: c. 1260 (compiled by Jacobus de Voragine; drawing on the Latin Passio Simonis et Iudae) · Golden Legend (Caxton)
Summary: The most colourful and most-read Western life of Simon is the one Jacobus de Voragine gave in the Golden Legend, retelling the Latin passion of Simon and Jude. It sends the two apostles together into Persia, pits them against a pair of sorcerers, and martyrs them side by side — the source of their joint feast on 28 October and of Western art’s pairing of Simon’s saw with Jude’s club. It is late legend, a millennium and more after the events, and is cited here as such.

Claims & evidence

Into Persia together

The Golden Legend makes Simon and Jude a missionary pair, each with his own first field, meeting in Persia:

Judas preached first in Mesopotamia and in Pontus, and Simon preached in Egypt, and from thence came they into Persia…

— where they found “two enchanters, Zaroes and Arphaxat, whom S. Matthew had driven out of Ethiopia.” The tale thus stitches Simon’s story to two others already in the legendary: to Jude, whose partner he becomes, and to Matthew, whose old adversaries he inherits. The joining of Simon and Jude is the durable part — it is why the Western calendar keeps them on one day — but it is a literary pairing, not an independent memory, and the parallel Jude/Thaddaeus timeline tells the same scene from the other apostle’s side.1

The martyrdom at Suanir

After many wonders and disputations, the priests of the sun-temple turn on the apostles:

… the bishops and the people took them and brought them to the temple of the sun… [and] they ran upon the apostles and hewed them to death anon.

This is the death the Western Church commemorates: a joint martyrdom in a pagan temple in Persia. From it the 28 October feast of “SS. Simon and Jude” descends, and from it the emblems of Western art — though the instrument the art gives Simon, a saw, comes not from this scene of hewing but from the parallel tradition, older than the Golden Legend, that Simon was sawn asunder.2

The legend’s own hesitation

Even Jacobus will not vouch for the manner of Simon’s death. Reporting the rival crucifixion tradition, he adds a striking caution:

… it is founden in divers places, of S. Simon, that he was nailed to the cross… And some say verily that it was not this Simon that suffered the martyrdom of the cross, but it was another.

A medieval compiler openly conceding that the sources disagree, and that the crucified “Simon” may be another man entirely, is a rare glimpse of the legend doubting itself — and a fair warning of how little weight any single detail of Simon’s death can bear.3

Notes

  • Late legend, not early witness. The Golden Legend (c. 1260) is hagiography a millennium and more after the apostle, drawing on the Latin Passio Simonis et Iudae; it is cited here as evidence for how Simon was remembered in the medieval West, never as a record of events. See the shared key on the apostles hub.
  • Simon and Jude as a pair. The pairing gave the two apostles their common feast and their paired emblems (Simon’s saw, Jude’s club). The same joint martyrdom is treated, from Jude’s side, on the Thaddaeus/Jude Golden Legend page.
  • Where he died is unsettled. Persia (Suanir) here; Britain, Egypt, and the Caucasus elsewhere; and the manner — hewn, sawn, or crucified — is no firmer. The whole tangle is weighed on the mission-and-death argument page (assessment weak).
