Adela Yarbro Collins and Leonard Thompson on the Date of Revelation

Dates: 1984 (Yarbro Collins) / 1990 (Thompson) · Wikipedia
Summary: Two late-date scholars who reshaped the late-date rationale itself. Both keep the Domitianic date (c. 95) while dismantling the old picture of a savage empire-wide "Domitianic persecution," relocating the book's setting in a perceived crisis and the pressure of the imperial cult rather than an objective reign of terror.

Claims & evidence

Yarbro Collins: a crisis perceived, not a persecution documented

In Crisis and Catharsis (1984), Adela Yarbro Collins argues that the crisis to which Revelation responds was in large part a matter of perception — a sense of relative deprivation and of the clash between Christian faith and social experience, focused on the imperial cult in the cities of Asia Minor — rather than a systematic, documented persecution under Domitian. The book works as apocalyptic rhetoric that names and resolves (catharsis) that perceived crisis.
— Adela Yarbro Collins, Crisis and Catharsis: The Power of the Apocalypse (Philadelphia: Westminster Press, 1984).

Thompson: Domitian's "reign of terror" as a hostile construct

In The Book of Revelation: Apocalypse and Empire (1990), Leonard Thompson argues that the image of Domitian as a bloodthirsty tyrant is largely the creation of later, hostile senatorial writers (Tacitus, Suetonius, Pliny, Dio), and that the contemporary evidence shows no systematic persecution of Christians. The Domitianic setting therefore stands, but the book answers the tensions of life under the imperial cult and John's own rhetoric of crisis, not a literal empire-wide massacre.
— Leonard L. Thompson, The Book of Revelation: Apocalypse and Empire (New York: Oxford University Press, 1990).

Notes

  • A late date that survives its old proof. The nineteenth-century early-date case leaned partly on the weakness of the Domitianic-persecution tradition; Robinson pressed the same point. Yarbro Collins and Thompson concede the history — there was no great Domitianic persecution — yet keep the late date, grounding it in the imperial cult and social setting instead. The result is that "no persecution under Domitian" no longer decides the date either way.
  • Why they sit on the timeline. They are not primarily arguing the date; their contribution is to the rationale for the late date, which is why their work feeds the late-date internal arguments (imperial cult, the seven churches) rather than the external-witness cluster.
