Clement of Alexandria on the Date of Revelation

Dates: c. 200 (Quis dives salvetur?) · Wikipedia
Summary: Telling the story of the apostle John and the young robber, Clement places John's return from Patmos to Ephesus "on the tyrant's death." He names no emperor; the tradition later identified the tyrant as Domitian, making Clement an early, if unspecific, witness for the late date.

Claims & evidence

John returned from Patmos "on the tyrant's death"

"For when, on the tyrant's death, he returned to Ephesus from the isle of Patmos, he went away, being invited, to the contiguous territories of the nations"

— Who Is the Rich Man That Shall Be Saved? 42, trans. ANF, source. The clause is incidental — Clement's interest is the pastoral tale that follows — which makes it valuable: he is not arguing a date but assuming one, that John's exile ended when a "tyrant" died.

Notes

  • Which tyrant? Clement leaves the emperor unnamed. Read alongside Irenaeus, whom Clement's contemporary readers knew, "the tyrant" is naturally Domitian — and that is how Eusebius took it, quoting this very passage in his Church History (3.23) as evidence for John's Domitianic exile and release. An early-date reader could in principle supply Nero instead, since Clement supplies no name; but nothing in Clement points that way, and the received identification is Domitian.
  • An independent strand of the same tradition. Writing c. 200 in Alexandria, Clement attests the exile-and-return story apart from Irenaeus's Antichrist-number argument — a second, geographically distinct early witness that John was banished to Patmos and freed at an emperor's death.
