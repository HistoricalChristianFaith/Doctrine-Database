Kenneth L. Gentry Jr. on the Date of Revelation

Dates: 1989 (Before Jerusalem Fell) · Wikipedia
Summary: The standard modern monograph for the early (Neronian) date. Gentry argues Revelation was written c. AD 65–66, under Nero and before Jerusalem's fall, resting the case on internal evidence — the temple still standing, the "sixth king" as Nero — and working to blunt the Irenaeus passage the late date depends on.

Claims & evidence

The internal case for a Neronian date

Gentry's central thesis is that the book's own contents date it before AD 70: Revelation 11:1–2 has John measure a temple that is still standing; Revelation 17:10's seven kings, of whom "one is," he identifies with the Julio-Claudian line down to Nero as the sixth; and the pervasive language of imminent judgment points at the coming destruction of Jerusalem. He gives internal (inspired) evidence priority over the external patristic testimony.
— Kenneth L. Gentry Jr., Before Jerusalem Fell: Dating the Book of Revelation (Tyler, TX: Institute for Christian Economics, 1989).

Disarming the Irenaeus passage

Gentry concedes that Irenaeus, Against Heresies 5.30.3, is the late date's strongest external prop, then argues three things against it: that the subject of "was seen" is ambiguous and may refer to John himself — who lived on into Domitian's day — rather than to the vision, so it need not date the writing; that Irenaeus is elsewhere an unreliable chronicler (e.g. his claim that Jesus lived to nearly fifty); and that his notices on Revelation are not wholly consistent. He enlists earlier scholars, such as S. H. Chase, for the "it was John who was seen" reading.
— Gentry, Before Jerusalem Fell (on the "was seen" construction and the reliability of Irenaeus).

Notes

  • Where the argument is strong, and where contested. The internal observations (especially the standing temple and the seven-kings count) are the substance of the early-date case and are treated on their own argument pages. The Irenaeus rebuttal is more contested: the "John was seen" reading is grammatically possible but is not the natural sense, and was not how any ancient reader — Eusebius included — took the sentence. Even J. A. T. Robinson, who shares Gentry's early date, reads Irenaeus the standard way and merely judges him mistaken. The sentence is weighed in full on the argument page Does Irenaeus (5.30.3) date the seeing of Revelation to Domitian's reign? (assessed sound as a reading of what Irenaeus meant).
  • A confessional starting point. Gentry writes as a postmillennial preterist, for whom an early date underwrites reading much of Revelation as fulfilled in AD 70. That motive does not settle whether the internal arguments succeed, which is why each is weighed separately rather than dismissed or accepted wholesale.
