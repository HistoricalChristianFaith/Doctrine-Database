Jerome on the Date of Revelation

Dates: c. 393 (De viris illustribus / Lives of Illustrious Men) · Wikipedia
Summary: Jerome hands on the Domitianic tradition in fixed, dated form: John was banished to Patmos and wrote the Apocalypse "in the fourteenth year… after Nero" — i.e. under Domitian — and, freed at Domitian's death, returned to Ephesus and lived until Trajan.

Claims & evidence

John was banished to Patmos and wrote the Apocalypse under Domitian

"In the fourteenth year then after Nero Domitian having raised a second persecution he was banished to the island of Patmos, and wrote the Apocalypse, on which Justin Martyr and Irenaeus afterwards wrote commentaries. But Domitian having been put to death and his acts, on account of his excessive cruelty, having been annulled by the senate, he returned to Ephesus under Pertinax and continuing there until the time of the Emperor Trajan, founded and built churches throughout all Asia, and, worn out by old age, died…"

— Lives of Illustrious Men 9 (on the apostle John), trans. NPNF, source. "The fourteenth year after Nero" and "a second persecution" both place the banishment in Domitian's reign (Nero's persecution being the first), agreeing with Irenaeus and Eusebius.

Notes

  • The tradition now carries a hard date. Where Irenaeus said only "towards the end of Domitian's reign," Jerome reckons from Nero — "the fourteenth year after Nero" — and frames the exile as the second imperial persecution, after Nero's. The Domitianic date has by his day hardened into a fixed chronological datum handed on as settled fact.
  • Dependent on the earlier chain. Jerome is a transmitter, not an independent witness: his notice rests on Eusebius (whom he abridges throughout the De viris illustribus), who rests in turn on Irenaeus. His value lies in showing how firmly the late date had set by the end of the fourth century in the Latin West.
