The Syriac Subscription on the Date of Revelation

Dates: 6th century (the Syriac Apocalypse; superscription/title) · Wikipedia
Summary: The heading prefixed to the Apocalypse in the Syriac tradition names Nero, not Domitian, as the emperor who banished John to Patmos — the earliest explicit witness to the early (Neronian) date, though it stands centuries after the events and against the whole Greek and Latin line.

Claims & evidence

John was banished to Patmos "by Nero Caesar" — ⚠ unverified

The title-line carried with the Apocalypse in the Syriac version is reported to read: "The Apocalypse of St. John, written in Patmos, whither John was sent by Nero Caesar." An early-date witness cited repeatedly in the debate — but the exact primary wording has not been confirmed here against the manuscript: the standard edition of the Crawford Apocalypse (John Gwynn, The Apocalypse of St. John in a Syriac Version Hitherto Unknown, Dublin, 1897) prints the Syriac and a reconstructed Greek but no English rendering of the heading, and the "by Nero Caesar" form reaches the modern reader through nineteenth-century and later early-date scholarship (Moses Stuart; Kenneth Gentry). It is recorded here on that secondary authority, pending confirmation of the exact primary text.

Notes

  • The earliest explicit early-date witness. Unlike the Greek and Latin Fathers, who say Domitian (or, for Epiphanius, Claudius), the Syriac heading says Nero — placing the exile before AD 68. Early-date advocates prize it as the one strand of ancient external testimony that agrees with their internal-evidence case.
  • Late and editorial. A book's superscription is a scribal title, not the author's, and the Syriac Apocalypse belongs to the sixth-century (Philoxenian/Harklean) stratum of the Syriac New Testament — five centuries after John. As a witness to what actually happened it is weak; its interest is that a distinct textual tradition preserved, or invented, a Neronian memory against the dominant Domitianic one.
  • Weighed as an argument. Whether this subscription evidences a genuine early (Neronian) tradition, or is only a late scribal inference, is assessed on the argument page Does the Syriac subscription naming Nero evidence an early (Neronian) date for Revelation? (assessed weak as a witness that settles the date).
