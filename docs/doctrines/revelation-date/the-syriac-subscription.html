The Syriac Subscription on the Date of Revelation

Dates: 6th century (the Syriac Apocalypse; superscription/title) · Wikipedia
Summary: The heading prefixed to the Apocalypse in the Syriac tradition names Nero, not Domitian, as the emperor who banished John to Patmos — the earliest explicit witness to the early (Neronian) date, though it stands centuries after the events and against the whole Greek and Latin line.

Claims & evidence

John was banished to Patmos "by Nero Caesar"

The title-line carried with the Apocalypse in the Syriac version reads, in Moses Stuart's direct quotation of the title page, that "it was written in Patmos, whither John was sent by Nero Caesar." (Stuart, A Commentary on the Apocalypse, 1845, 1:267) Stuart also traces the edition: the Syriac Apocalypse in the printed Syriac New Testament was copied in western Asia by Caspar, passed to Scaliger the younger and the library of Leyden, and was published by L. de Dieu in 1627 — the text the London and Paris Polyglots then took. This is the later (Philoxenian/Harklean) Syriac stratum, not the second-century Peshitta, which never carried the book. A fuller English form — "The Revelation which was made by God to John the Evangelist on the island of Patmos, into which he was thrown by Nero Caesar" — circulates through the modern early-date literature (Kenneth Gentry). John Gwynn's later critical edition of a different Syriac copy (The Apocalypse of St. John in a Syriac Version Hitherto Unknown, Dublin, 1897, from the Crawford manuscript) prints the Syriac and a reconstructed Greek but supplies no English of the heading — its opening leaves were damaged — so the "by Nero Caesar" title is anchored to the De Dieu/Polyglot edition Stuart used, not to Gwynn's.

Notes

  • The earliest explicit early-date witness. Unlike the Greek and Latin Fathers, who say Domitian (or, for Epiphanius, Claudius), the Syriac heading says Nero — placing the exile before AD 68. Early-date advocates prize it as the one strand of ancient external testimony that agrees with their internal-evidence case.
  • Late and editorial. A book's superscription is a scribal title, not the author's, and the Syriac Apocalypse belongs to the sixth-century (Philoxenian/Harklean) stratum of the Syriac New Testament — five centuries after John. As a witness to what actually happened it is weak; its interest is that a distinct textual tradition preserved, or invented, a Neronian memory against the dominant Domitianic one.
  • Weighed as an argument. Whether this subscription evidences a genuine early (Neronian) tradition, or is only a late scribal inference, is assessed on the argument page Does the Syriac subscription naming Nero evidence an early (Neronian) date for Revelation?
