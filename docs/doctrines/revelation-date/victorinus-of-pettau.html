Victorinus of Pettau on the Date of Revelation

Dates: c. 300 (d. c. 304) · Wikipedia
Summary: In the earliest surviving commentary on Revelation, Victorinus states plainly that John saw the Apocalypse while "condemned to the labour of the mines by Cæsar Domitian" on Patmos — the first witness to attach the vision itself, not just John's presence on the island, to Domitian by name.

Claims & evidence

John was condemned to the mines on Patmos by Domitian

"He says this, because when John said these things he was in the island of Patmos, condemned to the labour of the mines by Cæsar Domitian."

— Commentary on the Apocalypse of the Blessed John, on Revelation 10:11, trans. ANF, source. Victorinus adds that after Domitian's death John was released and published the Apocalypse he had received — so, on his account, the vision was seen under Domitian and the book issued just after.

Notes

  • The earliest Revelation commentary. Writing in Latin at the end of the third century, Victorinus is the first to comment on the Apocalypse verse by verse, and he takes the Domitianic setting as settled. His note fixes the tradition a step further than Irenaeus: not merely that the vision was seen "towards the end of Domitian's reign," but that John was a condemned exile in the Patmos mines under that emperor.
  • Dependent on, and confirming, the Irenaean line. Victorinus knew and used Irenaeus, so he is not a wholly independent source for the date; his value is as an early Western echo showing the Domitianic reading already fixed and elaborated well before Eusebius gathered the testimonies.
