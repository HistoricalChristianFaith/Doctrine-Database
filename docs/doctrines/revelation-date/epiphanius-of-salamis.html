Epiphanius of Salamis on the Date of Revelation

Dates: c. 375 (Panarion) · Wikipedia
Summary: The lone patristic outlier. Refuting the Alogi (who rejected John's writings), Epiphanius twice dates John's prophecy on Patmos to the reign of the emperor Claudius — decades before Domitian, and even before Nero. His date is almost universally judged a confusion; but it is the one ancient witness that does not place the vision under Domitian.

Claims & evidence

John returned from Patmos "under Claudius Caesar"

"the Holy Spirit compelled John to issue the Gospel in his old age when he was past ninety, after his return from Patmos under Claudius Caesar, and several years of his residence in Asia."

— Panarion 51.12,2, trans. Frank Williams (Brill), source.

John prophesied on Patmos "during the time of Claudius Caesar"

"He foretold it prophetically by the mouth of St. John, who prophesied before his falling asleep, during the time of Claudius Caesar and earlier, when he was on the isle of Patmos."

— Panarion 51.33,9, trans. Frank Williams (Brill), source.

Notes

  • An internally strained date. Epiphanius pairs "under Claudius" (who died in AD 54) with John being "past ninety" when he then wrote the Gospel after returning — a chronology that does not cohere, since it would push John's Gospel absurdly early. Most scholars read the "Claudius" notice as a slip or a garbled tradition rather than a considered rival to the Domitianic date.
  • Did he mean Nero? Some early-date advocates note that Nero's full imperial name was Nero Claudius Caesar, and suggest Epiphanius (or his source) meant Nero — which would make him an early-date witness. This is a modern conjecture, not what the text says: Williams' translation reads "Claudius Caesar" without qualification. It is worth recording as a proposed rescue of the passage, not as its plain sense.
  • Why it still matters. Even as an error, Epiphanius shows the Domitianic dating was not literally unanimous in antiquity — a single dissenting voice the early-date case can point to, though it cuts toward Claudius, not Nero, and is generally treated as unreliable.
