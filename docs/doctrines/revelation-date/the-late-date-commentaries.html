The mainstream late-date commentaries on the Date of Revelation

Dates: 1997–2014 (Aune, Beale, Koester) · Wikipedia
Summary: The major reference commentaries of the last generation converge on the late (Domitianic) date, c. AD 95–96 — the current scholarly majority. David Aune, G. K. Beale, and Craig Koester each weigh both cases and settle on the late date, though Aune allows that earlier material was folded into the final book.

Claims & evidence

Aune: a final edition c. 95, over earlier material

David Aune's three-volume Word Biblical Commentary holds the standard late date (c. 95, under Domitian) but by way of a two-edition theory: a first edition as early as c. AD 70, incorporating still older material, expanded into a final edition near the end of the century. Aune is thus a useful bridge figure — he grants that Neronian-era material may underlie the book while dating its finished form to Domitian.
— David E. Aune, Revelation, Word Biblical Commentary 52A–C (Dallas: Word, 1997–98), Introduction, § "Date."

Beale: no Neronian emperor-cult enforcement

G. K. Beale's New International Greek Testament Commentary lays out both cases and settles on the Domitianic date, arguing among other things that the systematic pressure of the imperial cult that Revelation presupposes fits the end of the century better than Nero's reign, whose persecution was tied to the fire of AD 64 rather than to refusal of emperor-worship.
— G. K. Beale, The Book of Revelation, New International Greek Testament Commentary (Grand Rapids: Eerdmans, 1999), Introduction.

Koester: c. 95–96, Domitianic

Craig Koester's Anchor Yale Bible commentary dates the book c. AD 95–96 under Domitian, setting it against the Greco-Roman and Jewish-Christian world of the late first century, with the imperial cult and the social and economic tensions of the Asian cities as its backdrop.
— Craig R. Koester, Revelation: A New Translation with Introduction and Commentary, Anchor Yale Bible 38A (New Haven: Yale University Press, 2014), Introduction.

Notes

  • The present majority. These commentaries represent the mainstream verdict today: the late date is the default, with the early date a serious minority. That majority rests on the external patristic testimony (Irenaeus and the chorus after him) together with the internal fit of the imperial cult and the seven churches — the arguments weighed separately on this page.
  • Not unanimous on the mechanism. Even within the late-date camp the rationale varies: Aune allows early strata, Beale and Koester argue chiefly from the imperial-cult setting, and Yarbro Collins and Thompson have unsettled the old persecution premise. The convergence is on the date, not on why.
