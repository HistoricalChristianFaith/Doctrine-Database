Eusebius of Caesarea on the Date of Revelation

Dates: c. 324 (Church History) · Wikipedia
Summary: The great church historian assembles the earlier testimonies into a single chronology: John was banished to Patmos under Domitian, released after Domitian's death under Nerva, and returned to Ephesus, where he lived until Trajan. Eusebius quotes Irenaeus's "almost in our own generation, at the end of the reign of Domitian" as his proof.

Claims & evidence

John was banished to Patmos under Domitian

"It is said that in this persecution the apostle and evangelist John, who was still alive, was condemned to dwell on the island of Patmos in consequence of his testimony to the divine word."

— Church History 3.18.1, trans. NPNF (McGiffert), source. "This persecution" is the one Eusebius has just assigned to Domitian's later years.

The proof from Irenaeus

"If it were necessary for his name to be proclaimed openly at the present time, it would have been declared by him who saw the revelation. For it was seen not long ago, but almost in our own generation, at the end of the reign of Domitian."

— Church History 3.18.3, quoting Irenaeus, Against Heresies 5.30.3, source. Eusebius reproduces the linchpin passage as the authority for his own dating.

Release under Nerva and residence at Ephesus to Trajan

"It was at this time that the apostle John returned from his banishment in the island and took up his abode at Ephesus, according to an ancient Christian tradition."

— Church History 3.20.9, source (the return follows Domitian's death and the annulling of his acts under Nerva). Eusebius adds that at Ephesus John "remained among them until the time of Trajan" (Church History 3.23.4).

Notes

  • The tradition made a chronology. Eusebius is the writer who welds the scattered notices — Irenaeus's dating, Clement's exile-and-return tale, the memory of John at Ephesus — into a continuous sequence: banished under Domitian, freed under Nerva, alive to Trajan. This is the frame Jerome would abridge and hand to the Latin West.
  • A transmitter of Irenaeus, not an independent check. Eusebius's date rests avowedly on Irenaeus, whom he quotes. His witness strengthens the reception of the late date but does not add a source independent of the linchpin passage — a point the early-date camp presses when it argues that the whole Domitianic chorus traces back to one ambiguous sentence.
