Irenaeus on the Date of Revelation

Dates: c. 180 (Against Heresies Book 5) · Wikipedia
Summary: The linchpin of the late (Domitianic) date. Arguing that the number of the Antichrist's name would have been revealed had it been needful, Irenaeus remarks that the apocalyptic vision "was seen… almost in our day, towards the end of Domitian's reign" — the earliest surviving external testimony to when Revelation was seen, and the anchor of the whole late-date case.

Claims & evidence

The apocalyptic vision was seen "towards the end of Domitian's reign"

"We will not, however, incur the risk of pronouncing positively as to the name of Antichrist; for if it were necessary that his name should be distinctly revealed in this present time, it would have been announced by him who beheld the apocalyptic vision. For that was seen no very long time since, but almost in our day, towards the end of Domitian's reign."

— Against Heresies 5.30.3, trans. ANF, source. Domitian reigned 81–96, so "towards the end of" his reign points to c. 95–96 — the date the great majority of the Fathers, and mainstream criticism, still assign to the book.

Notes

  • The grammatical crux. The English "that was seen" renders the Greek ϡἐωράθη (heōrathē), whose unexpressed subject is disputed. The natural reading — followed by Eusebius, Jerome, and nearly all the ancients — is that the vision (or John himself) "was seen" near the close of Domitian's reign. Early-date advocates (Moses Stuart, and at length Kenneth Gentry) urge instead that the subject is the person who beheld the vision, i.e. that it was John, not the Apocalypse, who "was seen" living on into Domitian's day — leaving the date of the book itself open. The point is contested; the plain sense, and the whole ancient reception, take Irenaeus to date the vision.
  • How much weight one witness bears. Irenaeus wrote c. 180, within living memory of the apostolic age, and as a hearer of Polycarp (a disciple of John) stood unusually close to the Johannine tradition. His statement is the headwater from which Eusebius, Victorinus, Jerome, and the later chorus draw; the late date rests very largely on it. Whether a single — if early and well-placed — external notice should outweigh the bundle of internal inferences marshaled for the early date is the pivot of the whole dispute.
