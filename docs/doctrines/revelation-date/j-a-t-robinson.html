J. A. T. Robinson on the Date of Revelation

Dates: 1976 (Redating the New Testament) · Wikipedia
Summary: A mainstream (not preterist) critic who argued that the whole New Testament, Revelation included, was written before AD 70. Robinson dated the Apocalypse to c. AD 68 and dismissed the traditional Domitianic-persecution rationale as historically thin.

Claims & evidence

The New Testament's silence on the fall of Jerusalem

"One of the oddest facts about the New Testament is that what on any showing would appear to be the single most datable and climactic event of the period—the fall of Jerusalem in AD 70, and with it the collapse of institutional Judaism based on the temple—is never once mentioned as a past fact."

— J. A. T. Robinson, Redating the New Testament (London: SCM Press, 1976), 13, source. This is the load-bearing premise of Robinson's whole project, not a claim about Revelation in particular; he applied it across the canon, Revelation among the rest, to argue a pre-70 date.

Revelation dated before AD 70

In his chapter on the Apocalypse (ch. VIII), Robinson placed the book c. AD 68 and read the seven-kings sequence of Revelation 17:9–11 against the emperors of the year of the four emperors (68–69). He regarded the Domitianic persecution behind the standard late date as historically insubstantial — there is, he urged, no good evidence of a systematic Domitianic persecution of Christians for Revelation to answer.
— Robinson, Redating the New Testament, ch. VIII.

Notes

  • Not a preterist. Robinson was a liberal Anglican bishop and critic, not a confessional preterist; his early dating rested on general historical argument, which is why it is often cited as an independent, non-partisan vote for the pre-70 date.
  • The persecution premise. Robinson's dismissal of the Domitianic persecution anticipates the very point Adela Yarbro Collins and Leonard Thompson would press from the late-date side — that the "reign of terror" was overstated. The two camps thus partly agree on the history while dividing on the date: for Robinson the weak persecution tells against Domitian; for Thompson it merely reframes what a Domitianic Revelation was responding to.
