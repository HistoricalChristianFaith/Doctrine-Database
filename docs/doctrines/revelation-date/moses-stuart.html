Moses Stuart and the nineteenth-century early-date scholars on the Date of Revelation

Dates: 1845 (Stuart's Commentary on the Apocalypse) · Wikipedia
Summary: The early (Neronian) date was, for much of the nineteenth century, the leading critical view — not a fringe position. Moses Stuart argued it fully in America in 1845; in England the Cambridge scholars Westcott, Lightfoot, and Hort, and the popularizer F. W. Farrar, inclined the same way, dating the book to the years around Nero's death.

Claims & evidence

Stuart: Revelation was written c. AD 68, before Jerusalem fell

In his two-volume Commentary on the Apocalypse (Andover / New York, 1845), Moses Stuart argued for a date near the end of Nero's reign (c. AD 68) and before the fall of Jerusalem in 70, reading much of the book as a prophecy of that fall and of Rome's own convulsions. Drawing on the German critics Eichhorn, Ewald, and Lücke, he pressed that the visions had to be intelligible to their first-century readers — which, he held, pointed to the Neronian setting.
— Moses Stuart, A Commentary on the Apocalypse, 2 vols. (Andover: Allen, Morrill & Wardwell, 1845), Introduction.

The Cambridge line: Westcott, Lightfoot, Hort, and Farrar

The same early date was held by the leading English New Testament scholars of the era. B. F. Westcott, J. B. Lightfoot, and F. J. A. Hort each assigned the Apocalypse to Nero's reign or the years immediately following — a convergence noted by later commentators as near-unanimous among the Cambridge three. F. W. Farrar gave the fullest popular case in The Early Days of Christianity (1882), reading the beast and its numbered name against Nero and the turmoil of AD 68–69.
— on the Cambridge trio, see Robert H. Mounce, The Book of Revelation (NICNT; Grand Rapids: Eerdmans, 1977), 32; F. W. Farrar, The Early Days of Christianity (London: Cassell, 1882), ch. xxviii.

Notes

  • The early date was once the critical mainstream. It is easy to read the modern debate as a settled late-date consensus challenged by a preterist minority. The nineteenth-century picture was the reverse: internal-evidence dating had made the Neronian view the dominant scholarly position, and the later swing back to Domitian (with Aune, Beale, Koester) is itself a development to be weighed, not a fixed baseline.
