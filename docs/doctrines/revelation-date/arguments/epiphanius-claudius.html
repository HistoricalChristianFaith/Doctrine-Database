Does Epiphanius's "Claudius Caesar" date witness an early (pre-Domitianic) date for Revelation?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak (as a witness to an early date) — the notice is real and does break the patristic unanimity for Domitian, so it is not nothing; but read plainly it names the emperor Claudius (41–54), a date almost universally judged a chronological muddle, and read charitably (as Nero Claudius Caesar) it is an imprecise, disputed emendation buried in an otherwise unreliable chronology. Either way it does not deliver a clean witness to the Neronian early date · pressed as an early-date witness by preterist writers (Kenneth Gentry and others); its plain "Claudius" reading is what Epiphanius actually wrote

Related arguments: the external case for the early date — see also Does the Syriac subscription naming Nero evidence an early (Neronian) date for Revelation?; and, on the external case for the late date, Does Irenaeus (5.30.3) date the seeing of Revelation to Domitian's reign? and Do the later Fathers independently confirm the Domitianic date, or merely echo Irenaeus?

Almost the whole ancient external testimony to when Revelation was written names Domitian. Epiphanius of Salamis (c. 375) is the one Father who breaks ranks in the other direction from the book's own internal signs: twice, refuting the Alogi (who rejected John's Gospel and Apocalypse), he places John's prophecy and exile on Patmos not under Domitian, nor even under Nero, but "in the time of Claudius Caesar" — the emperor who died in AD 54. Early-date advocates sometimes enlist him as a patristic witness against the late date. The question is what that notice can actually evidence: a genuine memory of an early writing, or a confusion — and, if the former, an early writing under whom.

The claim

the Holy Spirit compelled John to issue the Gospel in his old age when he was past ninety, after his return from Patmos under Claudius Caesar, and several years of his residence in Asia.

— Epiphanius, Panarion 51.12,2 (trans. Frank Williams); paired with 51.33,9, where John "prophesied… during the time of Claudius Caesar and earlier, when he was on the isle of Patmos."1 The inference offered: if a fourth-century Father dated John's exile to Claudius, then the Domitianic tradition was not the only ancient memory, and the door to a pre-Domitianic (hence possibly Neronian) writing stands open.

Who advances it

Epiphanius is not a preterist's ally by intent — his aim is to defend Revelation's apostolic authorship against the Alogi, and the date is incidental to that. The use of his notice as an early-date witness is modern: preterist and early-date writers (Kenneth Gentry among them) cite him as evidence that the Fathers were not unanimous for Domitian, and some go further, proposing that "Claudius Caesar" here means Nero, whose full imperial name was Nero Claudius Caesar Augustus Germanicus — which would convert Epiphanius into a (garbled) witness for the Neronian date itself.2 He is not quite alone: the sixth-century Latin commentator Apringius of Beja likewise names Claudius — "at the time of Claudius Caesar… he ordered John, the apostle of our Lord Jesus Christ, to be transported into exile" — so a minor "Claudius" strand did persist beside the dominant Domitianic one.3

The evidence for

What genuinely holds is narrow but real. The patristic testimony is not literally unanimous: against Irenaeus and the chorus that names Domitian, here is a fourth-century bishop — a diligent, if credulous, collector of traditions — twice, deliberately, in a work of controversy, dating John's Patmos prophecy earlier, and a sixth-century Latin witness (Apringius) agreeing on Claudius. A head-count of "all the Fathers say Domitian" therefore overstates; the ancient reckoning had at least a thin dissenting current, and early-date writers are entitled to say so. And the notice is at any rate pre-Domitianic in its plain sense: whatever emperor Epiphanius meant, he did not mean Domitian.

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The dissent is real, and it should not be waved away: Epiphanius (and after him Apringius) is an ancient voice that does not say Domitian. The "the Fathers unanimously date Revelation to Domitian" claim is, strictly, false; the honest statement is that the Fathers preponderantly say Domitian.

What is overstated or imprecise. Nearly everything else. Take the plain reading first: the emperor Claudius reigned 41–54, which puts John's Patmos vision before most of the seven churches Revelation addresses had well been founded and a full generation before the internal signs the early date itself relies on (a "sixth king," a Nero cipher) — a date no camp, early or late, actually holds. Read plainly, then, Epiphanius does not support the Neronian early date; he contradicts it too. And his own chronology is visibly broken: he has John, "past ninety," writing the Gospel only after returning from this Claudian exile, which would drag the Fourth Gospel absurdly early and is internally incoherent — the mark of a garbled tradition, not a considered rival reckoning. Epiphanius is elsewhere notoriously unreliable on dates and chronology, so an isolated, self-contradicting notice is weak evidence for any precise date.4

The "he meant Nero" rescue. The proposal that "Claudius Caesar" denotes Nero (via the regnal name Nero Claudius Caesar) is the strongest available reading for the early-date purpose — and it is a conjecture the text will not confirm. Epiphanius writes "Claudius Caesar" without qualification, and he distinguishes Claudius from Nero as separate emperors elsewhere in the same work; nothing in the passage signals the longer regnal name. The emendation is possible — ancient writers do sometimes name emperors by a subsidiary name — but it is a rescue read onto Epiphanius to save him for the early date, not the sense he states. And even if granted, it buys only a garbled, imprecise Neronic notice inside a chronology we have just seen collapse: an approximate witness, not a clean one.

Assessment

Weak — as a witness to an early date for Revelation. Two readings, and neither yields what the early date needs. On the plain reading Epiphanius means the emperor Claudius (41–54), which is chronologically almost impossible, is universally judged a confusion, and in fact undercuts the Neronian date as much as the Domitianic one; on the charitable reading he means Nero Claudius Caesar, which is an unconfirmed emendation against his own usage, and which even if accepted only supplies an imprecise notice lodged in a demonstrably incoherent chronology. What the passage does establish is modest and worth conceding: the patristic tradition was not unanimous for Domitian — a "Claudius" current ran beside it, carried by Epiphanius and Apringius. But breaking the unanimity is not the same as evidencing an early date: a strained, isolated, self-contradicting notice pointing to an emperor no one dates the book under cannot bear that weight. With the Syriac subscription it makes up the whole of the external case against Domitian — and, like it, corroborates nothing so much as the rule that the early date's real strength lies in the book's internal signs, not in the Fathers.
