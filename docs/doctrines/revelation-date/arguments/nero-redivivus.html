Does the Nero-redivivus legend (Revelation 13:3; 17:8,11) require a post-Nero, Domitianic date?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — a genuine late-date consideration, but double-edged and non-decisive. The healed death-wound of the beast (Rev 13:3) and the beast that "was, and is not, and is to come… the eighth, and is of the seven" (Rev 17:8,11) are widely read as invoking the first-century Nero redivivus legend — the belief that Nero had not truly died, or would return from the East. Late-date advocates argue that the developed form of this legend (a resurrected, returning Nero-monster, and an emperor cast as Nero come again) presupposes a vantage well after Nero's death in June 68 — i.e. Domitian's reign. The inference cuts, but not cleanly: the expectation of Nero's return is attested immediately after his death (a false Nero already in 69; edicts circulated as if he lived), so the legend's mere presence does not require the 90s, only its most elaborated eschatological form fits a later date more comfortably; and whether Revelation invokes a matured redivivus myth at all — rather than the empire's own death-and-revival in the civil war of 68–69, or a general parody-resurrection of the beast — is itself disputed. · the late-date reading advanced by Richard Bauckham, David Aune, and Craig Koester; the early-date counter by Kenneth Gentry and J. A. T. Robinson

Related arguments: the internal case for the late date — see also Does Revelation's emperor-worship setting fit Domitian's imperial cult, dating the book to the 90s? and Does the settled condition of the seven churches (Laodicea's wealth) require a late date?

The claim

"And I saw one of his heads as it were wounded to death; and his deadly wound was healed: and all the world wondered after the beast… The beast that thou sawest was, and is not; and shall ascend out of the bottomless pit… And the beast that was, and is not, even he is the eighth, and is of the seven, and goeth into perdition."

— Revelation 13:3; 17:8, 11 (KJV); cf. 13:14, the image made "to the beast, which had the wound by a sword, and did live."1 Late-date advocates read these three touches — a head wounded to death and healed, a beast that was, and is not, and is to come, and an eighth king who is of the seven — as allusions to the Nero redivivus legend: the popular first-century belief that Nero, who died by his own hand in June 68, had not really perished but had fled to the Parthians in the East and would return at the head of their armies to reclaim his throne and take vengeance on Rome. The death-and-return of the beast, on this reading, is Nero's; the phrase "was, and is not, and is to come" is a deliberate demonic parody of the divine title "which was, and is, and is to come" (Rev 1:4; 4:8), casting the returning Nero as a counterfeit, resurrected antagonist. And the argument for a date follows: a legend takes time to arise and mature, so a developed returning-Nero myth — especially one fused with eschatological, quasi-resurrection expectation — presupposes a standpoint well after 68. By Domitian's reign the legend had grown a long tail: a third Nero-pretender appeared c. AD 88 with Parthian backing, and hostile tradition would soon remember Domitian himself as "a bald Nero," a Nero come again. The myth's developed presence, late-date advocates conclude, points past the 60s toward the 90s.2

Who advances it

The redivivus reading of the beast is near-universal among modern commentators; its use as a dating argument belongs to the late-date camp. Richard Bauckham's The Climax of Prophecy gives the fullest treatment of the Nero-redivivus material in Revelation, tracing the "was, and is not, and is to come" and the "eighth… of the seven" to the returning-Nero expectation documented in the Sibylline Oracles — and, tellingly, holds a thoroughly Nero-saturated Revelation on a Domitianic date, taking the legend's developed form as evidence of the later vantage. David Aune and Craig Koester read the beast's death-wound and revival the same way and likewise date the book c. 95. The argument is rarely run alone; it is deployed as reinforcement, the mirror-image of the early date's own appeal to the seven kings — where the early-date reading needs the sixth king to be the reigning Nero, the late-date reading needs a returned one.3

The legend arose at once — it does not by itself require the 90s

The argument's structural weakness as a dating inference is that the expectation of Nero's return did not need decades to form: it is attested within a year of his death. Suetonius reports that some long decorated Nero's tomb and "produced… his edicts, as if he were still alive and would shortly return and deal destruction to his enemies," and that Nero's death was greeted in the East with fear that he would come back. Tacitus records that "about this time Achaia and Asia Minor were terrified by a false report that Nero was at hand," and that a first impostor claiming to be Nero arose already in 69, the year after his death, in the turmoil of the civil war. Two further pretenders followed — one c. AD 79/80 under Titus (Terentius Maximus, backed by the Parthians, in Cassius Dio) and a third c. 88/89 under Domitian. So an "expectation of Nero's return" was a live, circulating thing from 68–69 onward. Its bare presence in a text therefore cannot discriminate a book of the late 60s from one of the 90s: John could have drawn on the freshly-minted legend within a year or two of Nero's death as readily as a generation later. What can be said is narrower and softer — that the most developed form of the myth, in which the returning Nero is fused into an eschatological, quasi-resurrected super-antagonist (the stage represented by the later Sibylline Oracles), fits a later standpoint more comfortably; but "more comfortably" is not "requires," and the line between an early and a matured form of a fast-moving legend is too soft to fix a decade.4

Whether Revelation invokes redivivus at all is disputed

The dating inference also rests on a prior reading that is itself contested. Early-date advocates deny that Rev 13:3 and 17:8,11 require the returning-Nero myth in the first place. Kenneth Gentry reads the mortally wounded head that "was healed" not of a resurrected Nero but of the Roman imperial house itself, which in the chaos of 68–69 — the "year of the four emperors," Nero's suicide ending the Julio-Claudian line, three emperors falling in quick succession, the empire seeming to bleed to death — was "wounded to death" and then "healed" when Vespasian secured the throne and restored the state. On this reading the death-and-revival is the empire's, in real and recent events, and no matured legend is needed at all. Others grant a Nero-allusion but read the beast's death-and-return as a general parody-resurrection — the beast aping the slain-and-risen Lamb (Rev 5:6) — without importing the full Parthian-return legend or its chronology. The proof-texts under-determine the question: "was, and is not, and is to come" and "the eighth… of the seven" are compatible with a returning Nero, with a reviving empire, and with a mythic beast that mimics the resurrection, and the choice among them is a matter of interpretation, not of a date the text stamps on itself.5

A legend with a long, elastic life

That the returning-Nero speculation neither began nor ended at any tidy date is confirmed at the far end by Augustine, who records the same live speculation still circulating in the early fifth century. Discussing Paul's "man of sin," he reports the conjecture that the apostle "alluded to Nero, whose deeds already seemed to be as the deeds of Antichrist. And hence some suppose that he shall rise again and be Antichrist. Others, again, suppose that he is not even dead, but that he was concealed that he might be supposed to have been killed, and that he now lives in concealment in the vigor of that same age which he had reached when he was believed to have perished, and will live until he is revealed in his own time and restored to his kingdom." Augustine, writing c. AD 425 — more than three and a half centuries after Nero's death — still finds both branches of the legend (Nero-will-rise and Nero-is-not-dead) in living circulation, and marvels "that men can be so audacious in their conjectures." A tradition this elastic, alive from 69 to 425, is a poor instrument for fixing a book to a single decade: its presence is compatible with almost any point on that long arc.6

Adversarial assessment

What holds. Revelation's beast really is Nero-shaped, and the touches at 13:3, 13:14, and 17:8,11 really do resonate with the returning-Nero expectation — the death-wound healed, the "was, and is not, and is to come" mocking the divine name, the eighth king who "is of the seven." The mainstream is right that the redivivus legend stands somewhere behind this material, and the more elaborated, eschatologically-charged form of that legend does sit more naturally at a later vantage than at the moment of Nero's death. As a consideration among others, it genuinely leans late.

What is overstated. The leap from "the redivivus legend is present" to "therefore Domitian, not Nero" fails on two counts. First, the legend is not a late growth: the expectation of Nero's return is documented from 68–69, so a writer of the late 60s had it ready to hand, and the myth's mere presence dates nothing. The dating force reduces to a claim about the degree of the legend's development — a soft, contestable judgment, not a fixed marker. Second, the inference presupposes that Revelation invokes the matured myth at all, which the early-date reading (the empire's own death-and-revival in the civil war) and the parody-resurrection reading both deny. And there is a real tension the internal-evidence camps must each face: the early date's own seven-kings and 666 arguments want the beast to be the living, reigning Nero of the mid-60s, while this argument wants a dead and returning one — so the same beast is claimed, in incompatible tenses, by both sides, and neither can help itself to both readings at once.

Assessment

Contested (as a dating argument). This rates the inference "the Nero-redivivus legend in Revelation ⇒ a Domitianic date," not the identification of the beast with Nero (which is sound — see 666 = "Nero Caesar") nor Revelation's date as a whole (which turns chiefly on the external witness of Irenaeus, weighed elsewhere). The legend genuinely stands behind the beast's death-and-revival, and its most developed form fits a later standpoint more comfortably, so this is a reasonable card for the late date. But it is not decisive: the return-expectation is attested from 68–69, so the myth's presence cannot by itself exclude the 60s; the dating weight collapses to a soft judgment about how "developed" the legend in Revelation is; and whether the text invokes the matured redivivus myth at all — rather than the empire's civil-war wound or a general parody of the resurrection — is itself disputed. Double-edged, then: a consideration that leans late where it is granted, but that an early-date reading can accommodate without strain — strong by the lights of the history-of-religions reader who sees a matured myth, inconclusive by the chronologist's, since the legend was born the year Nero died.
