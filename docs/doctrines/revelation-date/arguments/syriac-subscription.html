Does the Syriac subscription naming Nero evidence an early (Neronian) date for Revelation?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak (as a witness that settles the early date) — the subscription is real, explicit, and the earliest unambiguous Neronic notice, so it is not to be dismissed as nonexistent; but it is a scribal title, carried in Syriac versions of the sixth and seventh centuries, standing some five hundred years after the events, with an opaque tradition-history of its own — a lone late colophon that cannot outweigh a second-century witness (Irenaeus, c. 180). It corroborates the early date within one late textual stream; it does not evidence an independent early tradition · advanced by Moses Stuart, James M. MacDonald, and Kenneth Gentry; its lateness pressed by the late-date scholarship (Mark Hitchcock)

Related arguments: the external case for the early date — see also Does Epiphanius's "Claudius Caesar" date witness an early (pre-Domitianic) date for Revelation?, the one Father who dates John's Patmos vision earlier still — to Claudius; with this subscription, he makes up the whole of the external case against Domitian.

See also: the external case for the late date — Does Irenaeus (5.30.3) date the seeing of Revelation to Domitian's reign? and Do the later Fathers independently confirm the Domitianic date, or merely echo Irenaeus?

The entire ancient external testimony to when Revelation was written favors the late (Domitianic) date, almost with one voice. The one strand that names Nero instead is not a Father at all but a subscription — the heading or title-line carried with the Apocalypse in the Syriac tradition, which states that John was banished to Patmos by Nero Caesar. Early-date advocates prize it as their single piece of ancient external corroboration, the one witness outside the book's own contents that agrees with their internal-evidence case. The question is what a subscription of this kind can actually evidence.

The claim

The Revelation which was made by God to John the Evangelist on the island of Patmos, into which he was thrown by Nero Caesar.

— the Syriac title of the Apocalypse in the fuller form that circulates through the modern early-date literature (Kenneth Gentry). The wording anchored to a page-cited source is Moses Stuart's: quoting the printed Syriac title page directly, he gives it as "…written in Patmos, whither John was sent by Nero Caesar."1 The inference is straightforward: if a Syriac copy of Revelation headed the book with Nero's name, then a line of tradition — distinct from the Greek and Latin one that says Domitian — remembered the exile as Neronian, and that memory is external corroboration of the early date.

Pinning the wording

The substance of the heading — Patmos… by Nero Caesar — is stable across the reports, though the reported English forms vary from writer to writer ("thrown," "sent," "cast," "exiled"). It is a genuine title of the printed Syriac Apocalypse: Moses Stuart quotes it from the title page and traces its edition — the Syriac text first published by L. de Dieu in 1627 (from the Scaliger/Leyden manuscript) and thence carried into the London and Paris Polyglots.2 The later critical edition of a different Syriac copy — John Gwynn's The Apocalypse of St. John in a Syriac Version Hitherto Unknown (1897), from the Crawford manuscript — prints the Syriac text and a reconstructed Greek but furnishes no English rendering of the heading (its opening leaves were damaged), so the familiar "by Nero Caesar" sentence is anchored to the De Dieu/Polyglot line, not to Gwynn.

Dating it honestly

Two dates must be kept apart, and a common secondary gloss runs them together. Revelation was not part of the earliest Syriac New Testament (the Peshitta), which omits it; it was supplied only in the later Syriac versions — the Philoxenian (AD 507) and the Harklean (616) — so a Syriac Apocalypse with any heading at all belongs to the sixth–seventh centuries. (That a Syriac Revelation of some form circulated earlier is likely — Ephrem the Syrian, d. c. 373, quotes the book — but that pushes back the text, not the demonstrable age of the heading.) The subscription is therefore a scribal colophon, not a second-century tradition: it is the title a copyist prefixed to the book, five centuries after John, and it must not be dressed — as some early-date citations imply — in the antiquity of "the Syriac version" as though that were apostolic. Mark Hitchcock, pressing exactly this point for the late date, calls the heading "the first clear, accepted, unambiguous witness to the Neronic date," and dates that witness to the sixth–seventh centuries — late enough, he argues, that had the early date been the true one, some earlier and more local witness (in Asia Minor, where the book was written) ought to survive.3

Adversarial assessment

What the subscription really is. It is genuine, explicit, and unique: alone among ancient external notices it names Nero, and it is the earliest such notice that does. It is not the invention of modern preterists, and it should not be waved away as though it did not exist — a real, distinct textual tradition set John's exile under Nero, and the Syriac stream is consistent with itself in doing so (the Syriac History of John the son of Zebedee likewise places the exile under Nero).4 So the honest early-date claim is not "there is no external evidence" — there is this.

Why it cannot carry the date. Three things hold its weight down, and none depends on ruling the reading out. First, it is late. A sixth–seventh-century colophon stands four centuries downstream of Irenaeus and further still from the events; on any ordinary weighing of witnesses, the earlier testimony governs, and even F. J. A. Hort — who inclined to the early date — granted that "if external evidence alone could decide, there would be a clear preponderance for Domitian."5 Second, its own history is opaque. We do not know whether the heading transmits an independent early memory or is itself a late inference — a scribe reasoning back from the book's Nero-saturated imagery (the 666 cipher, the wounded-and-healed beast) to a Neronian setting is at least as economical an explanation as an unbroken sixth-century-old tradition, and a heading cannot show which it is. Third, it is a title, not a testimony. Superscriptions are the most fluid, least authorial part of a manuscript (Hoskier catalogued dozens of divergent Greek prologues to Revelation), and they routinely record a copyist's harmonizing guess rather than received fact.

What it does and does not evidence. The distinction the early-date use blurs is between a witness and a tradition. The subscription witnesses that some Syriac Christians, by the sixth century, believed the exile was Neronian; it does not evidence that this belief descends from an early and independent line, still less that it is correct. Set beside a single grammatically-disputed but second-century sentence in Irenaeus, a lone late colophon of unknown pedigree is the weaker card — which is why the early date's real strength was always its internal arguments (the standing temple, the seven kings), not this external one. The subscription confirms the early date within one late stream; it does not settle it.

Assessment

Weak — as a witness that settles, or even substantially evidences, an early date. The claim to weigh is not whether the Syriac heading exists (it does) or whether it names Nero (it does), but whether it evidences a genuine early tradition of a Neronian writing, and that it cannot bear: it is a scribal title of the sixth–seventh centuries, five hundred years after the events, its own descent unknown, standing alone against the whole earlier external line. It is real and worth stating — the earliest explicit Neronic notice, and with Epiphanius the sum of the external case against Domitian — but the honest verdict is the one even a sympathetic critic (Hort) conceded: the external evidence, this included, favors the late date, and the early date must be carried, if at all, on the internal signs. A late title records a belief; it does not date a book.
