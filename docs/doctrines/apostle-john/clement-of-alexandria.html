Clement of Alexandria on John, son of Zebedee

Dates: c. 150–215 (writing c. 200) · Wikipedia
Summary: Clement independently attests John's old age at Ephesus. In the famous story of the young robber he has John return from Patmos “after the tyrant's death” and, in extreme age, govern and reorder the churches of Asia — even riding out himself into the mountains to reclaim a convert who had fallen away. It is an early line of the Ephesus tradition, separate from Irenaeus's, and it presupposes the same picture: the last surviving apostle, released from exile, overseeing the churches of Asia to the end of a long life.

Claims & evidence

“A tale, which is not a mere tale”: John returns from Patmos to govern Asia

Clement tells a story he vouches for as true history, not fable, about John's last years. Its opening fixes the setting: on the death of the emperor who had banished him, John comes back from Patmos to Ephesus and takes up the oversight of the surrounding churches — appointing bishops, ordering whole congregations, and ordaining ministers.

Listen to a tale, which is not a mere tale, but a narrative concerning John the apostle, which has been handed down and treasured up in memory. For when, after the tyrant's death, he returned from the isle of Patmos to Ephesus, he went away upon their invitation to the neighboring territories of the Gentiles, to appoint bishops in some places, in other places to set in order whole churches, elsewhere to choose to the ministry some one of those that were pointed out by the Spirit.

— Clement of Alexandria, Who is the Rich Man that Shall Be Saved? 42, quoted in Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History 3.23.

The rest of the story shows the aged apostle at work. Visiting one of these cities, John entrusts a promising youth to the care of the local bishop, who baptizes and instructs him. But the young man falls in with bad company, drifts into crime, and at last becomes the captain of a band of robbers in the mountains. Returning later, John demands of the bishop the deposit he had left — “I demand the young man and the soul of the brother” — and, learning the youth is “dead to God,” tears his clothes and calls for a horse: “But let a horse be brought me, and let some one show me the way.” The old man rides straight to the robbers' haunt, lets their outpost seize him — “For this did I come; lead me to your captain” — and, when the young chief turns to flee in shame, pursues him “forgetting his age,” crying, “Why, my son, dost thou flee from me, thine own father, unarmed, aged?” The robber throws down his arms, weeps bitterly, and is led back and restored to the church.

John recalled to episcopal work in extreme old age

Clement's anecdote presupposes what the tradition says elsewhere: that John, released from exile, lived on at Ephesus as the overseer of the churches of Asia into extreme old age, still able — however improbably for his years — to travel the region and even to ride after a lost convert. Eusebius introduces the story with exactly this note, naming the emperor Clement leaves anonymous.

At that time the apostle and evangelist John, the one whom Jesus loved, was still living in Asia, and governing the churches of that region, having returned after the death of Domitian from his exile on the island.

— Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History 3.23. Eusebius adduces the story precisely as evidence that John was still alive and active into the reign of Trajan, corroborating it from “two witnesses… Irenæus and Clement of Alexandria.”

Notes

  • Which tyrant? Clement names no emperor — only “the tyrant.” The later tradition, and Eusebius here, identify him as Domitian, placing John's return from Patmos about the year 96. This dovetails with Victorinus and Jerome, who likewise put the exile under Domitian. See the summary timeline.
  • An independent line of the Ephesus tradition. Clement writes at Alexandria, drawing on his own store of church memory rather than on Irenaeus; his testimony is therefore a separate strand of the same tradition. That two witnesses of different provenance agree strengthens the case that John died of old age at Ephesus rather than as an early martyr.
  • Marriage, and the exile. Clement elsewhere (per Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History 3.30) notes that some of the apostles married. And the “after the tyrant's death → return from Patmos” detail assumes the very exile that Revelation reports its author suffering on the island (Revelation 1:9).
