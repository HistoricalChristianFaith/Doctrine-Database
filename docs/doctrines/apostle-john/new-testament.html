The New Testament on John, son of Zebedee

Dates: c. 30–95 (the events, and the writing of the Gospels, Acts, and Revelation) · Wikipedia
Summary: The bedrock. John is a Galilean fisherman, son of Zebedee and younger brother of James, called with him from the nets and surnamed a “son of thunder.” With Peter and James he is one of the inner three. The Fourth Gospel calls one unnamed figure “the disciple whom Jesus loved,” entrusted with Jesus's mother at the cross and named as the witness behind the Gospel; the tradition identifies him with John. In Acts he is Peter's constant companion, and Paul names him a “pillar” of the Jerusalem church. Revelation is written by a “John” exiled on Patmos.

Claims & evidence

A fisherman, son of Zebedee, brother of James, called from the nets

John and his elder brother James are fishermen of the Sea of Galilee, partners of Simon Peter, called by Jesus straight from their boat as they mended their nets with their father Zebedee.

And when he had gone a little further thence, he saw James the son of Zebedee, and John his brother, who also were in the ship mending their nets. And straightway he called them: and they left their father Zebedee in the ship with the hired servants, and went after him.

— Mark 1:19–20 (cf. Matthew 4:21–22; Luke 5:10, partners of Simon).

“Boanerges, the sons of thunder”

In naming the Twelve, Mark records the surname Jesus gave the two brothers — a temper the Gospels elsewhere illustrate, when James and John would call down fire on a Samaritan village, and when John forbids an exorcist who did not follow their company.

And James the son of Zebedee, and John the brother of James; and he surnamed them Boanerges, which is, The sons of thunder…

— Mark 3:17 (cf. the fire from heaven, Luke 9:54; the strange exorcist, Mark 9:38). John is named among the first four in every apostle-list, always with Peter, James, and Andrew (Matthew 10:2; Mark 3:17; Luke 6:14; Acts 1:13).

One of the inner three

With Peter and James, John belongs to the three disciples Jesus repeatedly kept nearest him — admitted where the rest were not: to the raising of Jairus's daughter, to the Transfiguration, and to the agony in Gethsemane.

And he suffered no man to follow him, save Peter, and James, and John the brother of James.

— Mark 5:37 (Jairus's daughter); the Transfiguration, “Peter, and James, and John” (Mark 9:2); Gethsemane, “Peter and James and John” (Mark 14:33). He and James ask for the seats at Jesus's right and left, and are told they will “drink of the cup” he drinks (Mark 10:35–40) — a saying that figures in the debate over how John died.

“The disciple whom Jesus loved”

The Fourth Gospel never names the sons of Zebedee (it mentions them only once, collectively, at John 21:2), but repeatedly speaks of an unnamed “disciple whom Jesus loved” — who leans on Jesus's breast at the last supper, stands by the cross, outruns Peter to the empty tomb, and is named at the close as the witness whose testimony the Gospel records. From Irenaeus onward the church identified this disciple with John.

Now there was leaning on Jesus' bosom one of his disciples, whom Jesus loved.

— John 13:23 (cf. the empty tomb, John 20:2–8; and the closing attestation, “This is the disciple which testifieth of these things… and we know that his testimony is true,” John 21:24).

Entrusted with the mother of Jesus at the cross

Of the Twelve, this disciple alone is placed at the crucifixion, where the dying Jesus commits his mother to his care.

When Jesus therefore saw his mother, and the disciple standing by, whom he loved, he saith unto his mother, Woman, behold thy son! Then saith he to the disciple, Behold thy mother! And from that hour that disciple took her unto his own home.

— John 19:26–27. The tradition read this charge as the reason John's later home was said to be Ephesus, and linked it to the legends of Mary's last years.

Peter's companion in Acts, and a “pillar” of the church

In the earliest church John appears repeatedly at Peter's side — healing the lame man at the Beautiful Gate, arraigned together before the Sanhedrin as “unlearned and ignorant men,” and sent down together to lay hands on the Samaritan converts. Paul, recounting his visit to Jerusalem, names John with Peter (Cephas) and James the Lord's brother as those “who seemed to be pillars.”

And when James, Cephas, and John, who seemed to be pillars, perceived the grace that was given unto me, they gave to me and Barnabas the right hands of fellowship; that we should go unto the heathen, and they unto the circumcision.

— Galatians 2:9 (cf. the Beautiful Gate, Acts 3:1–11; before the council, Acts 4:13–19; sent to Samaria, Acts 8:14).

“John” the exile of Patmos

The Revelation names its author simply “John,” a servant and brother in tribulation, who received the vision while banished to the island of Patmos.

I John, who also am your brother, and companion in tribulation… was in the isle that is called Patmos, for the word of God, and for the testimony of Jesus Christ.

— Revelation 1:9. Whether this “John” is the son of Zebedee, and how the exile fixes the date of the book, are weighed on the timeline for the date of Revelation.

Notes

  • The one death the New Testament does not record. Acts reports the martyrdom of John's brother James (Acts 12:2) but says nothing of John's own end — the silence into which the later Ephesus tradition, and the minority early-martyrdom thesis, both step. See the arguments on the end of John.
  • The beloved disciple = John. The Fourth Gospel keeps its chief witness anonymous; the identification with the son of Zebedee is an inference from the internal evidence (he is one of the inner circle, present where only the Twelve were, yet never named while the other prominent disciples are) that the church made from the second century on. It is examined further on the summary timeline and on the argument over Papias's “two Johns.”
  • Authorship. The tradition ascribes to this John a Gospel, three epistles, and the Revelation. The New Testament texts themselves name no author for the Gospel and epistles and only “John” for the Revelation; the ascriptions, and the question whether one hand wrote all, belong to the later witnesses.
