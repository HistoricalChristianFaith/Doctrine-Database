Irenaeus of Lyons on John, son of Zebedee

Dates: c. 130–202 (writing c. 180) · Wikipedia
Summary: Irenaeus, who as a boy had heard Polycarp of Smyrna — himself a hearer of John — is the firm early anchor of the Ephesus tradition: John published his Gospel and lived to old age at Ephesus, dying under Trajan. He also preserves the vivid story of John fleeing the bath-house rather than share it with the heretic Cerinthus.

Claims & evidence

John published a Gospel at Ephesus, “who had leaned upon His breast”

Setting out the origins of the four Gospels, Irenaeus places the writing of the Fourth Gospel at Ephesus and names its author as the disciple who reclined next to Jesus at the supper.

Afterwards, John, the disciple of the Lord, who also had leaned upon His breast, did himself publish a Gospel during his residence at Ephesus in Asia.

— Against Heresies 3.1.1. (Note Irenaeus identifies the evangelist John with the beloved disciple of the Fourth Gospel and places the writing at Ephesus.)

He remained at Ephesus until the times of Trajan

Appealing to the elders of Asia who had known John, Irenaeus dates the apostle's survival to the reign of Trajan — a great age reached at the very turn of the century.

those who were conversant in Asia with John, the disciple of the Lord, [affirming] that John conveyed to them that information. And he remained among them up to the times of Trajan.

— Against Heresies 2.22.5. Trajan reigned 98–117, so this places John's death at a great age around the turn of the century. (Irenaeus repeats the point at 3.3.4: John “remained among them permanently until the times of Trajan.”)

The flight from Cerinthus at the bath-house

Among the traditions Irenaeus received from Polycarp is this anecdote of John's later Ephesian years, when he recoiled from sharing a bath-house with the Gnostic Cerinthus.

There are also those who heard from him that John, the disciple of the Lord, going to bathe at Ephesus, and perceiving Cerinthus within, rushed out of the bath-house without bathing, exclaiming, “Let us fly, lest even the bath-house fall down, because Cerinthus, the enemy of the truth, is within.”

— Against Heresies 3.3.4. Irenaeus has the story from Polycarp; it is the earliest anecdote of John's later Ephesian years and shows the polemical “son of thunder” temper the Gospels record.

Notes

  • The Polycarp link. Irenaeus's testimony matters because of its short chain — Irenaeus heard Polycarp, and Polycarp had heard John (see also Irenaeus's Letter to Florinus, preserved in Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History 5.20). See the summary timeline.
  • The load-bearing witness for old age at Ephesus. Irenaeus is the earliest and firmest support for the argument that John died of old age at Ephesus, not early as a martyr.
  • The Gospel against Cerinthus. Irenaeus (Against Heresies 3.11.1) also credits John with writing the Gospel against Cerinthus's error — the same heretic of the bath-house story.
