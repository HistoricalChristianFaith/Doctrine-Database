Eusebius of Caesarea on John, son of Zebedee

Dates: c. 260–340 (the Ecclesiastical History, c. 325) · Wikipedia
Summary: Eusebius gathers the scattered notices into the first connected account of John's later life: banished to Patmos under Domitian, released under Nerva, governing the churches of Asia from Ephesus into the reign of Trajan. He quotes Irenaeus and Clement, preserves Polycrates of Ephesus's witness that John lies buried at Ephesus, and — reading Papias — is the first to argue for two Johns and two Ephesian tombs, a move that let him detach the Revelation from the apostle.

Claims & evidence

Patmos under Domitian, release under Nerva, old age at Ephesus

Eusebius fixes the chronology the later church inherited. He dates John's banishment to the fifteenth year of Domitian, has him released on the tyrant's death, and follows Irenaeus and Clement in keeping him at Ephesus into Trajan's reign.

It is said that in this persecution the apostle and evangelist John, who was still alive, was condemned to dwell on the island of Patmos in consequence of his testimony to the divine word.

— Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History 3.18.1; the release and return under Nerva, 3.20.8–9; the old age at Ephesus (quoting Irenaeus and Clement), 3.23.

Polycrates of Ephesus: John buried at Ephesus, “who reclined upon the bosom of the Lord”

Eusebius preserves a letter of Polycrates, bishop of Ephesus (c. 190), listing the great luminaries who “fell asleep” in Asia. Among them is John — identified with the beloved disciple and given a priestly dignity — whose grave is at Ephesus.

…and moreover John, who was both a witness and a teacher, who reclined upon the bosom of the Lord, and, being a priest, wore the sacerdotal plate. He fell asleep at Ephesus.

— Polycrates, in Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History 5.24.3 (cf. 3.31.3). Polycrates is a local, late-second-century witness — from Ephesus itself — to John's burial there, an independent strand of the old-age tradition.

The two Johns and the “two tombs in Ephesus”

Reading Papias's preface, in which the name John appears twice, Eusebius argues that a second John — “the presbyter” — lived at Ephesus alongside the apostle. He enlists the local tradition of two tombs as corroboration.

This shows that the statement of those is true, who say that there were two persons in Asia that bore the same name, and that there were two tombs in Ephesus, each of which, even to the present day, is called John's.

— Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History 3.39.6. Eusebius had a motive: doubtful of the Revelation's apostolic authorship (a doubt he draws from Dionysius of Alexandria, 7.25), he could assign it to “the other” John. The argument, and the tombs, are weighed on the argument page.

Notes

  • A compiler, only as strong as his sources. Eusebius's value is that he preserves earlier witnesses — Irenaeus, Clement, Polycrates, Papias — but the traditions he relays are only as good as those sources; where he reasons for himself (the two Johns) he has an interest in the outcome. See the apostles hub on his place in the ladder.
  • He supports both the old-age reading and the two-Johns puzzle. His Patmos-to-Trajan chronology and his preservation of Polycrates strengthen the case that John died of old age at Ephesus; his reading of Papias opens the question of the “two Johns” (contested), which bears on authorship.
