Papias of Hierapolis on John, son of Zebedee

Dates: c. 60–130 (writing c. 110–130; surviving only in fragments) · Wikipedia
Summary: Papias, bishop of Hierapolis in Asia and a collector of the elders' living memory, gives the oldest post-apostolic notice of John — and two of the knottiest puzzles about him. In the one surviving sentence of his preface he seems to name a John twice, an apostle and a “presbyter,” the seed of the “two Johns” question; and a lost passage of his second book was later reported (by writers many centuries after him) to have said that John, like his brother, “was killed by the Jews” — the seed of the early-martyrdom thesis.

Claims & evidence

The preface: John named twice — apostle and “presbyter”

Papias explains that he prized the “living and abiding voice” of those who had known the apostles. In doing so he lists two ranks of informant — and the name John falls in both.

…what Andrew or what Peter said, or what was said by Philip, or by Thomas, or by James, or by John, or by Matthew, or by any other of the disciples of the Lord, and what things Aristion and the presbyter John, the disciples of the Lord, say. For I did not think that what was to be gotten from the books would profit me as much as what came from the living and abiding voice.

— Papias, preface to the Expositions of the Oracles of the Lord, quoted in Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History 3.39.4. The first “John” stands among the apostles (past tense, “said”); the second, “the presbyter John,” stands after Aristion among those still speaking in Papias's own day. Whether these are two men or one, named twice in different capacities, is weighed on the argument page.

The report that John “was killed by the Jews”

A ninth-century Byzantine chronicler, George Hamartolus (“the Monk”), reports — citing the lost second book of Papias — that John, far from dying peacefully, was martyred.

After Domitian, Nerva reigned one year, who recalled John from the island (i.e. Patmos), and allowed him to dwell in Ephesus. He was at that time the sole survivor of the twelve Apostles, and after writing his Gospel received the honour of martyrdom. For Papias, bishop of Hierapolis, who was an eye-witness of him, in the second book of the Oracles of the Lord says that he was killed by the Jews, and thereby evidently fulfilled, together with his brother, Christ's prophecy concerning them.

— George Hamartolus, Chronicon (9th c.), a fragment of Papias, in J. B. Lightfoot, The Apostolic Fathers (1891), Fragments of Papias. A similar note is ascribed to the fifth-century epitomator Philip of Side. Both are weighed as the basis of the early-martyrdom thesis.

Notes

  • Only fragments survive. Papias's five-book work is lost; every notice comes to us secondhand, chiefly through Eusebius, who thought Papias “a man of very small intelligence” and was glad to read his two Johns as denying the apostle the authorship of Revelation (Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History 3.39.6, 13).
  • Two puzzles, both live. The preface feeds the question of Papias's “two Johns” (assessment: contested), which bears on who wrote the Gospel, the epistles, and Revelation; the martyrdom fragment feeds the question whether John was martyred early (assessment: weak). See the grouped arguments on the end of John.
  • The self-contradiction in George's notice. George's own sentence has John recalled from Patmos, dwelling at Ephesus as sole survivor of the Twelve, and writing his Gospel — before it adds that he “received the honour of martyrdom.” The internal strain is one reason the report is held to be a garbled memory rather than a clean witness to Papias.
