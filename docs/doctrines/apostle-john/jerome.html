Jerome on John, son of Zebedee

Dates: c. 347–420 (writing 393) · Wikipedia
Summary: Jerome, in On Illustrious Men, condenses the Greek tradition for the Latin West: John wrote his Gospel last of the four, against Cerinthus and the Ebionites; was exiled to Patmos under Domitian and wrote the Apocalypse there; returned to Ephesus on Domitian's death; and died worn out by old age under Trajan, buried near Ephesus.

Claims & evidence

The last-written Gospel, against Cerinthus and the Ebionites

John, the apostle whom Jesus most loved, the son of Zebedee and brother of James, the apostle whom Herod, after our Lord's passion, beheaded, most recently of all the evangelists wrote a Gospel, at the request of the bishops of Asia, against Cerinthus and other heretics and especially against the then growing dogma of the Ebionites, who assert that Christ did not exist before Mary. On this account he was compelled to maintain His divine nativity.

— Jerome, On Illustrious Men 9.

Jerome makes explicit the identification the tradition assumed — the beloved disciple, the evangelist, and the son of Zebedee are one man — and dates the Gospel last of the four.

Patmos under Domitian, and death of old age under Trajan

In the fourteenth year then after Nero Domitian having raised a second persecution he was banished to the island of Patmos, and wrote the Apocalypse, on which Justin Martyr and Irenaeus afterwards wrote commentaries. But Domitian having been put to death and his acts, on account of his excessive cruelty, having been annulled by the senate, he returned to Ephesus under Pertinax and continuing there until the time of the emperor Trajan, founded and built churches throughout all Asia, and, worn out by old age, died in the sixty-eighth year after our Lord's passion and was buried near the same city.

— Jerome, On Illustrious Men 9.

“The fourteenth year after Nero” ≈ Domitian's persecution of c. 95–96; “the sixty-eighth year after our Lord's passion” places John's death c. 98–100, at the start of Trajan's reign — the standard Ephesus chronology. (Note: “under Pertinax” is Jerome's slip; the emperor who recalled the exiles was Nerva.)

Notes

  • A channel, not an independent witness. Jerome is dependent on Eusebius and the earlier Greek tradition rather than reporting anything of his own, but he is the channel by which the Ephesus chronology reached the Latin West. See the summary timeline.
  • Weight on the Ephesus end. Jerome's dating supports the mainstream reading that John died of old age at Ephesus, against the rival tradition of an early martyrdom.
