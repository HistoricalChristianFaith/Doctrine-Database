The Acts of John on John, son of Zebedee

Dates: c. 150–200 (the “Leucian” apocryphal Acts) · Wikipedia
Summary: The apocryphal Acts of John is a second-century romance of low biographical value, marked by encratite and docetic tendencies and condemned as heretical in antiquity. It is the wellspring of John's most famous legendary scenes — the destruction of the temple of Artemis at Ephesus, the raisings of the dead, and his own peaceful “Metastasis,” a death in a grave he digs himself — and is cited here only as an early witness to how John was venerated, never as history.

Claims & evidence

The collapse of the temple of Artemis at Ephesus

The book's set-piece has John stand in the temple of Artemis (Diana) at Ephesus on the goddess's feast day and pray that God will overthrow her cult. As he prays, the great temple gives way — the altar shatters, the idols fall, and half the building comes down, killing the priest — and the crowd turns to John's God on the spot.

And as John spake these things, immediately the altar of Artemis was parted into many pieces, and all the things that were dedicated in the temple fell, and [MS. that which seemed good to him] was rent asunder, and likewise of the images of the gods more than seven. And the half of the temple fell down, so that the priest was slain at one blow by the falling of the (?roof, ? beam).

— M. R. James, The Apocryphal New Testament (Acts of John), §42 (within §§37–45). The crowd's response follows at once: “One is the God of John, one is the God that hath pity on us, for thou only art God: now are we turned to thee, beholding thy marvellous works!”

Raisings of the dead (Drusiana and others)

Across the book John raises several people to life; the most developed episode is that of Drusiana, a chaste married woman who dies of grief. John comes to her tomb, takes her hand, prays, and calls her out — a scene that also serves the book's insistent theme of continence, since her would-be violator is struck dead and then himself restored.

And after these words John said unto Drusiana: Drusiana, arise. And she arose and came out of the tomb; and when she saw herself in her shift only, she was perplexed at the thing…

— ibid., §80 (within §§63–86). These raisings are miracle-romance rather than reportage, but they are why later tradition remembered John above all as a worker of wonders at Ephesus.

The “Metastasis”: John's peaceful death in a self-dug grave

The book closes with John's death — called the Metastasis (“passing over”). At the last, an aged John goes out from the gathering, has the brethren dig a trench beside a brother's tomb (bidding them make it deeper), lays aside his garments, prays a long farewell prayer, and then lies down in the grave and quietly gives up his spirit — a serene old-age death, with no violence and no executioner. Some copies are not content with so plain an ending and add that when the brethren came the next day his body was not found, only his sandals or a fine dust (“manna”) welling up from the grave — the seed of the later legend that John never truly died but was translated alive.

— ibid., §§106–115 (the account of John's death; wording varies across the Greek recensions). The point that matters for the tradition is its character: even this legendary source remembers John's end as a peaceful death, not a martyrdom.

Notes

  • Reliability — romance-hagiography, condemned in antiquity. The Acts of John belongs to the second-century “Leucian” corpus of apostolic romances (with the Acts of Peter, Andrew, and Thomas). It carries a frankly docetic Christology — most notoriously in the “Hymn of Jesus” and the vision on the mount, where the crucified Lord's body seems unreal (§§87–105) — along with encratite tendencies; it was rejected by the church and explicitly condemned at the Second Council of Nicaea (787). It has little value as biography, but is early and vivid evidence of John's cult at Ephesus. On the house treatment of the apocryphal Acts, see the apostles hub.
  • The poisoned cup is not here. The famous ordeal in which John drinks poison unharmed — the origin of the chalice-and-serpent in his iconography — does not belong to the surviving Acts of John at all. It was attached to John later, by the Byzantine and Latin apostle-lists and the medieval Golden Legend; readers meeting it in art or legend should not credit it to this second-century book. See the summary timeline.
  • A peaceful death is itself a datum. The Metastasis matters to the question of how John died: even the legendary tradition remembers him dying quietly of old age at Ephesus, not as a martyr. That the earliest romance about him already tells a natural death, not a killing, weighs against the rival tradition of an early martyrdom — see the arguments on the end of John.
