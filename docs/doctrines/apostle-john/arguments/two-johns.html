Was Papias's “John the elder” a second John?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — whether Papias's preface distinguishes the apostle John from a separate “John the elder” at Ephesus, and if so which Johannine writings belong to which man. The evidence genuinely divides serious scholars: strong by the lights of literary criticism (the Greek of Revelation is not the Greek of the Gospel), weaker by the lights of early attestation (the witnesses nearest Papias knew only one John in Asia). No clear winner.

The claim

In the one surviving sentence of his preface, Papias names a John twice — once among the apostles whose words the elders reported, and again, after Aristion, among “the disciples of the Lord” still speaking in his own day, whom he calls “the presbyter John.” The claim weighed here is Eusebius's inference, revived by many moderns: that these are two men, an apostle John and a distinct “John the elder,” both at Ephesus — with the corollary that the Johannine corpus may be divided between them (the Gospel to the apostle, the Revelation and/or the short epistles, whose author calls himself “the elder,” to the presbyter).1

The evidence for

Four supports carry the two-Johns reading. The text of Papias: the double naming is real, and Papias sets the second John apart — after an interval, in a different tense, behind Aristion, and with the distinguishing title “the presbyter.” Eusebius's reading: the earliest interpreter of the sentence, Eusebius, took it to mean two men, and adduced the local report of “two tombs in Ephesus, each … called John's.” The self-designation of the epistles: the author of 2 and 3 John calls himself simply “the elder” (ho presbyteros) — the very word Papias attaches to the second John. The style of Revelation: already in the third century Dionysius of Alexandria observed that the rough, Semitizing Greek of Revelation cannot be from the same hand as the polished Gospel and First Epistle — a stylistic gulf that a two-author hypothesis neatly explains.2

Adversarial assessment

The case against two Johns. The preface can as naturally name one John twice: first in a past-tense list of apostolic authorities whose words the elders had reported, then among those whose teaching was still being passed on — the apostle John, uniquely long-lived, belonging to both ranks at once. Papias calls both figures “disciples of the Lord,” a phrase awkward for a mere second-generation elder. Crucially, the witnesses closest to Papias know only one John at Ephesus and call him the apostle: Irenaeus, who had heard Polycarp (a hearer of John), never hints at two. The “two tombs” prove little — a city may raise two shrines to one saint, or a tomb and a cenotaph. And Eusebius was not a disinterested reader: uneasy about the Revelation's chiliasm, he had a motive to find a second John to whom it could be assigned.

Why it stays contested. Yet the case for two Johns will not simply dissolve. The stylistic difference between Revelation and the Gospel is a hard fact, felt by every reader of the Greek, and the epistolary “the elder” is a genuine echo of Papias's language; a single ambiguous sentence is doing a great deal of work on both sides. The reading is strong by one discipline's standards (the internal evidence of language and self-designation, which pushes toward more than one author) and weak by another's (the external attestation, which knows one apostolic John in Asia). Modern scholarship remains split: some assign the Gospel and epistles to “John the elder” and not the apostle at all; some keep the apostle for the Gospel and give Revelation to another John; some hold to a single John throughout. The one sentence of Papias cannot settle what it started.3

Assessment

Contested. Whether Papias's “John the elder” is a second John — and how, if so, the Gospel, epistles, and Revelation divide between the two — is genuinely disputed, with weighty considerations and real proponents on each side and no consensus. The internal, stylistic evidence favours more than one hand; the external, early attestation favours one apostolic John in Asia; and the decisive text is a single sentence patient of both readings. It is left contested, not resolved.
