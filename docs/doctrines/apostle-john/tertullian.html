Tertullian on John, son of Zebedee

Dates: c. 155–220 (writing c. 200) · Wikipedia
Summary: Tertullian gives the earliest witness to the Roman “boiling oil” legend: arguing that the apostolic churches still keep the apostles' teaching, he points to Rome, where (he says) John was plunged unhurt into boiling oil before being sent to his island exile.

Claims & evidence

Plunged unhurt into boiling oil at Rome, then exiled

Tertullian is listing the great churches that can show an unbroken apostolic pedigree; of Rome he writes:

How happy is its church, on which apostles poured forth all their doctrine along with their blood! where Peter endures a passion like his Lord's! where Paul wins his crown in a death like John's; where the Apostle John was first plunged, unhurt, into boiling oil, and thence remitted to his island-exile!

— Prescription against Heretics 36.

This is the first mention of the boiling-oil ordeal, later localized by tradition at the Latin Gate in Rome (the feast “St John before the Latin Gate”). Tertullian tells it as a deliverance survived, in the manner of the martyr-ordeal stories, and pairs it with the Patmos exile that the Revelation itself attests (Revelation 1:9).

Notes

  • The ordeal is unattested before Tertullian and absent from the Greek fathers who otherwise chronicle John's life (Irenaeus, Clement, Eusebius); it is best treated as a Roman tradition of uncertain historical value, valuable as evidence of how John was remembered. See the summary timeline.
  • The medieval Golden Legend expands it into the cauldron before the Latin Gate; see the summary timeline.
