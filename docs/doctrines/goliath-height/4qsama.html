4QSama — the Dead Sea Samuel Scroll — on How Tall Was Goliath?

Dates: copied mid-1st century BC (c. 50–25 BC); the oldest extant Hebrew manuscript of this verse · Wikipedia
Summary: The Qumran Samuel scroll gives Goliath's height as four cubits and a span (~6'9″) at 1 Samuel 17:4 — the decisive Hebrew witness. By a thousand years the oldest surviving Hebrew copy of the verse, it shows the shorter number is not a Greek peculiarity but the reading of the oldest Hebrew manuscript we possess, agreeing with the Septuagint against the far later Masoretic codices' "six cubits and a span" (~9'9″).

Claims & evidence

4QSama reads "four cubits and a span" at 1 Samuel 17:4

The scroll is fragmentary at this point, so the height is read and reconstructed by its editors rather than surviving whole; but their reading is four cubits and a span, not the six of the later Masoretic Text. This is the pivotal datum for the whole question: it is the earliest Hebrew evidence for Goliath's stature by roughly a millennium, and it stands with the Greek against the medieval Hebrew codices. The scroll was first edited by Frank Moore Cross; its bearing on Goliath's height, and the underlying manuscript relations, are set out by Eugene Ulrich, The Qumran Text of Samuel and Josephus (Harvard Semitic Monographs 19; Missoula: Scholars Press, 1978), and surveyed for this verse specifically by J. Daniel Hays, "Reconsidering the Height of Goliath," Journal of the Evangelical Theological Society 48/4 (2005): 701–714, at 703–704 (PDF). See also the overview of the manuscript at the Samuel Scroll.

Notes

  • It aligns the Hebrew with the Greek and Josephus. 4QSama's "four cubits and a span" matches the Septuagint and the height Josephus reports, and stands against the later Masoretic codices. The shorter number is therefore attested across the oldest strands of the textual tradition — Hebrew, Greek, and Josephus alike.
  • Its discovery reopened the question. Before the Qumran scrolls came to light from 1947 onward, the Greek "four" could be waved away as a secondary or softened reading. After 4QSama, the shorter figure has the oldest Hebrew manuscript we possess behind it, and the burden shifts: the six-cubit reading of the medieval codices now looks like the later, expanded one.
  • A text-type close to the Greek and Josephus. Ulrich showed that 4QSama's text-type is close to the Hebrew that lies behind both the Old Greek and Josephus — so the scroll does not merely happen to agree with them at this one number, but represents the family of Samuel text from which their shorter reading descends.
