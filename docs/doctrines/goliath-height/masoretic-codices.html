The Masoretic Codices on How Tall Was Goliath?

Dates: Aleppo Codex c. 930; Leningrad Codex 1008/1010 — the earliest complete Hebrew Bibles · Aleppo Codex · Leningrad Codex
Summary: The great medieval Masoretic manuscripts — the base text of every printed Hebrew Bible — give Goliath's height as "six cubits and a span" (Hebrew shesh ammot wa-zaret, roughly 9 feet 9 inches). This is the reading standardized in the printed Hebrew Bible and inherited by the English versions that print "six" (usually with a footnote for the ancient "four"). It is a thousand years younger than the Dead Sea Samuel scroll, which reads "four."

Claims & evidence

1 Samuel 17:4 in the Masoretic Text reads "six cubits and a span"

"And there went out a champion out of the camp of the Philistines, named Goliath, of Gath, whose height was six cubits and a span."

— 1 Samuel 17:4, Masoretic Text (Hebrew shesh ammot wa-zaret), Jewish Publication Society translation, source. The medieval Masoretic codices — the Aleppo Codex (c. 930) and the Leningrad Codex (1008/1010), the two oldest surviving near-complete Hebrew Bibles — carry this "six" (shesh), and every printed Hebrew Bible descends from them. As J. Daniel Hays notes, the Masoretic tradition here stands against the older witnesses, which read "four"; Reconsidering the Height of Goliath, Journal of the Evangelical Theological Society 48/4 (2005): 701–714, at 705.

Notes

  • The base of the printed Hebrew Bible. The Leningrad Codex is the manuscript reproduced in the standard scholarly editions of the Hebrew Bible (Biblia Hebraica), and the Aleppo Codex is the model text of others; so the Masoretic "six cubits and a span" is the reading that stands in effectively every printed Hebrew Bible and, through it, in the translations made from the Hebrew.
  • A thousand years younger than the "four" witnesses. These codices post-date the Dead Sea Samuel scroll 4QSama by roughly a millennium — and 4QSama, the Septuagint, and Josephus all give Goliath's height as four cubits and a span (about 6 feet 9 inches). The Masoretic "six" is thus the later and taller reading, which is why modern English Bibles that print "six" usually add a footnote recording the ancient "four."
  • What the "six" carried downstream. The Masoretic "six" also stands behind Jerome's Vulgate and the medieval Western allegory built on it by Bede — the giant's height read as a figure of worldly pride — so the higher number shaped the Latin West's text and its exegesis alike.
