Jerome and the Vulgate on How Tall Was Goliath?

Dates: c. 405 (the Vulgate translation of Samuel–Kings, made from the Hebrew) · Wikipedia
Summary: Translating from the Hebrew of his day, Jerome renders Goliath "six cubits and a span" — the Masoretic figure, roughly 9'9" — and the Vulgate fixed that number for a thousand years of Western readers, over against the older Greek and Qumran "four cubits and a span" (roughly 6'9").

Claims & evidence

The Vulgate reading: "six cubits and a span"

"And there went out a man baseborn from the camp of the Philistines named Goliath, of Geth, whose height was six cubits and a span."

— Douay-Rheims, 1 Kings (= 1 Samuel) 17:4, source. The Latin of the Vulgate here reads that Goliath's height was sex cubitorum et palmo — "six cubits and a palm/span." The Douay-Rheims English follows the Vulgate directly, and so preserves Jerome's "six." At roughly a foot and a half to the cubit plus a span, that yields a giant of about nine feet nine inches.

Notes

  • Jerome translated from the Hebrew. For Samuel–Kings Jerome worked from a Hebrew (proto-Masoretic) text, which read "six," and he shows no sign of following the Greek "four" here. The Vulgate's number is the Hebrew number, carried into Latin.
  • A note on the book title. In the Vulgate and the Douay-Rheims that follows it, the books we call 1–2 Samuel are titled 1–2 Kings (with our 1–2 Kings becoming 3–4 Kings). So the verse cited above as "1 Kings 17:4" is the same passage as 1 Samuel 17:4.
  • Where his "six" stands in the tradition. Jerome's figure aligns with the later Masoretic codices and against the shorter "four cubits and a span" of the Septuagint, the Qumran scroll 4QSama, and Josephus. Because the Latin West read Scripture through the Vulgate, it was Jerome's "six" — not the older "four" — that became the standard Goliath of Western Christendom.
