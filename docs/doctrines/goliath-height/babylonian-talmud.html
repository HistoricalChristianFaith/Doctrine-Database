The Babylonian Talmud on How Tall Was Goliath?

Dates: c. 500 (redaction of the Babylonian Talmud) · Wikipedia
Summary: An indirect witness to the taller "six" reading. The rabbinic Goliath haggadah works entirely from the Masoretic Hebrew of 1 Samuel 17 — the text that reads "six cubits and a span" — and elaborates Goliath as a mighty but human-scaled warrior: a formidable champion of Gath, not a colossus. When the sages reach for genuinely cosmic, mountain-uprooting giant-scale, they attach it not to Goliath but to Og of Bashan. The Talmud nowhere quotes a height figure for Goliath, and it shows no awareness of the shorter Greek/Qumran "four cubits and a span."

Claims & evidence

The Goliath haggadah is built on the Masoretic text of 1 Samuel 17

In the aggadic sweep of Sotah 42a–42b, the sages read Goliath straight out of the received Hebrew of 1 Samuel 17 and embroider it. They derive his name and pedigree from the wording of the chapter, identify his mother with Orpah — Ruth's sister-in-law, who "returned" to her people (playing on oref, the "back of the neck") — and gloss the taunting champion who "presented himself forty days, morning and evening" (1 Sam 17:16) as forty days set against the forty days of the giving of the Torah. The giant of this legend is drawn in epic but recognizably human terms: a warrior "built…perfect and free of any blemish," felled at last by David. See Sotah 42b, Sefaria (Koren–Steinsaltz translation); and the discussion of the passage in Jeremy Brown, "Sotah 42 — Goliath, Polydactyly, and Hereditary Gigantism," Talmudology.

The point for height is what the passage presupposes rather than what it states: the entire elaboration rests on the Masoretic Hebrew of 1 Samuel 17, the very recension whose verse 4 gives Goliath's stature as six cubits and a span. The Hebrew tradition the Talmud transmits is thus a standing witness to the taller reading, even though the sages here are chasing Goliath's ancestry and downfall, not his measurements.

Cosmic giant-scale is reserved for Og of Bashan, not Goliath

Where the rabbis do tell a genuinely mountainous giant-story, the giant is Og, king of Bashan — not Goliath. In Berakhot 54b the sages recount Og resolving to uproot a mountain "three parasangs" long to hurl upon the camp of Israel, only to have it drop over his own head; Moses, himself reckoned ten cubits tall, leaps ten cubits with a ten-cubit axe to strike Og in the ankle. The tradition frankly notes that for these dimensions "there is no biblical reference, but rather a tradition was transmitted." See Berakhot 54b, Sefaria (Koren–Steinsaltz translation). That the sages spend their extravagant, extra-scriptural giant-scale on Og and not on Goliath underscores how the Goliath of the Hebrew tradition stays within the bounds of its text — a huge man, but a man.

Notes

  • An indirect vote for "six." Because the rabbinic tradition transmits and expounds the Masoretic Hebrew, it implicitly stands on the "six cubits and a span" reading of 1 Samuel 17:4. It is not, however, an independent witness to the number: it inherits the figure with the Hebrew text rather than attesting it afresh, and it never cites the measurement.
  • No awareness of the shorter text. Nothing in the Goliath haggadah shows the sages knew of the Septuagint's "four cubits and a span," or of the like reading in the Qumran scroll 4QSama. The rabbinic discussion never registers a textual variant in Goliath's height at all — it simply works from the Hebrew before it.
  • Haggadic, not metrological. The interest of Sotah 42b is squarely aggadic — Goliath's lineage (his mother Orpah, his hundred fathers), his forty days of blasphemy, and his fall by David's hand. It is not trying to fix his stature, and it offers no independent measurement; its value here is as evidence for the text the Hebrew tradition carried, not for a height figure of its own.
