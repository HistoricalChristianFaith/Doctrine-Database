Bede on How Tall Was Goliath?

Dates: c. 716 (the Commentary on Samuel, In primam partem Samuhelis) · Wikipedia
Summary: Bede treats the six-cubit Goliath as a plain fact of the text and allegorizes it — the giant who "boasts to be similar to the Most High" — tying the six cubits to the six days of creation and to Ezekiel's measuring-reed; he betrays no knowledge of the shorter "four cubits" reading.

Claims & evidence

The allegory of "six cubits and a palm" (commenting on 1 Samuel 17:4)

"He is well described as being six cubits and a palm high, because he who boasts to be similar to the Most High, promises to preach perfect works and a blessed end through his philosophers. For God made the world in six days and rested on the seventh; and he established for mortals through the law six days of work, the seventh of rest. Indeed, Ezekiel saw in the hand of the man measuring the new city, a reed of six cubits and a palm; because the Lord measures the perfection of work in His chosen ones, as if in six cubits; and He contemplates the hope of eternal rest, which will be completed in the future, as if the part of the seventh."

— Bede, Commentary on Samuel, on 1 Samuel 17:4, source. For Bede the height is not a textual question but a spiritual one: the six cubits become the six days of creation and labour, and the giant himself figures the devil who "boasts to be similar to the Most High."

Notes

  • No variant in view. Bede takes "six" as simply the text and spends no word on any alternative measurement; his interest is the spiritual sense — Goliath as the devil, his city Gath read as "winepress" — not the arithmetic. Nothing in the commentary suggests he had ever met a copy reading "four."
  • Which text-form he inherits. His "six cubits and a palm" matches the Masoretic reading carried into the Latin West by Jerome's Vulgate — against the shorter "four cubits" of the Septuagint, the Qumran scroll 4QSama, and Josephus.
  • The Ezekiel reed. The measuring-reed "of six cubits and a palm" he cites is Ezekiel 40:5, which he reads as a warrant for taking Goliath's stature as a figure of works measured and rest awaited.
