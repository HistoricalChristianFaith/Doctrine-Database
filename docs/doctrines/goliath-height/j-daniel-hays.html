J. Daniel Hays on How Tall Was Goliath?

Dates: 2005 (“Reconsidering the Height of Goliath,” Journal of the Evangelical Theological Society 48/4) · JETS 48/4 (2005): 701–714
Summary: Hays, professor of Old Testament at Ouachita Baptist University, is the leading modern advocate that the original reading of 1 Samuel 17:4 is “four cubits and a span” (about 6′9″), and that the Masoretic “six cubits and a span” (about 9′9″) is a later scribal inflation. He argues the older witnesses — 4QSama (the oldest Hebrew), the Septuagint (Vaticanus, Alexandrinus, and the Lucianic recension), and Josephus — preserve “four,” while “six” is secondary. Even the shorter Goliath, he notes, towered over an Israelite army averaging roughly 5′0″–5′6″.

Claims & evidence

“Four cubits and a span” is the original reading

Hays marshals the manuscript evidence for the shorter figure: 4QSama, the oldest surviving Hebrew text of Samuel (late first century BC), reads “four cubits and a span”; so do the major Greek witnesses — Codex Vaticanus (the oldest complete Greek Bible), Codex Alexandrinus, and the Lucianic recension — and so does the first-century historian Josephus. The Masoretic “six cubits and a span,” by contrast, is attested only in medieval Hebrew codices; the earliest, the Aleppo Codex, dates to about AD 935. Hays concludes that the convergence of the oldest and independent witnesses on “four” makes it the original, with the Masoretic “six” a secondary enlargement of the giant. “Reconsidering the Height of Goliath,” JETS 48/4 (2005): 701–714 (also at Galaxie).

Even at ~6′9″ the giant still fits the narrative

A shorter Goliath, Hays stresses, is in no way an anticlimax. Drawing on the work of Victor Matthews and on skeletal evidence such as the crucified man from Giv’at ha-Mivtar, he puts the average Iron-Age Israelite male at roughly 5′0″–5′6″ — so a man of nearly 6′9″ would still have loomed a full head or more above the ranks he defied. Hays adds a narrative point: 1 Samuel 9:2 singles out Saul as the exceptionally tall Israelite, head and shoulders above his people — precisely the man who, by his own stature, should have gone out to meet the Philistine champion, and did not. “Reconsidering the Height of Goliath,” JETS 48/4 (2005): ~710–713.

The text-critical mainstream — and the pushback

Hays’s conclusion sits with the broad text-critical mainstream: P. Kyle McCarter’s Anchor Bible I Samuel (1980), Emanuel Tov, and Eugene Ulrich all favor the shorter reading as original. The dissent comes from within evangelical scholarship. Clyde E. Billington argued that the Greek “four” reflects a conversion of the height into Egyptian royal cubits (longer than the common cubit), which would put Goliath back near eight feet, and defended the Masoretic “six.” Clyde E. Billington, “Goliath and the Exodus Giants,” JETS 50/3 (2007): 489–508. William D. Barrick likewise defended the received Hebrew text against the shorter reading. William D. Barrick, “Goliath’s Height”. Hays answered his critics in the same volume. J. Daniel Hays, reply in JETS 50/3 (2007): 509–516.

Notes

  • This is the doctrine’s live argument. Whether “four” or “six” is the original reading is the open question of “How Tall Was Goliath?”, not a settled fact. Hays’s conclusion tracks the older witnesses — 4QSama, the Septuagint, and Josephus — against the Masoretic codices.
  • A genuine dispute, left open here. Hays makes a strong external-evidence case (older, independent witnesses converging on “four”), and Billington and Barrick answer with an internal one (the cubit-conversion reading of the Greek, and the integrity of the received Hebrew). Both sides are represented on this timeline; this page does not declare a winner.
  • Provenance. Quotations of measurements here follow Hays’s own figures in the JETS article; the cubit is reckoned at about 18 inches and the span at about 9 inches, yielding the 6′9″ / 9′9″ contrast he draws.
