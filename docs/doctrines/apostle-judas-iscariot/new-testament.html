The New Testament on Judas Iscariot

Dates: c. 30 (the betrayal), the Gospels and Acts written c. 50–95 · Wikipedia
Summary: Judas is named last in every list of the Twelve, always with the note that he betrayed Jesus. The New Testament gives him a surname (“Iscariot”), a father (Simon), a role (keeper of the common purse), a crime (handing Jesus over for thirty pieces of silver), and two accounts of his end — Matthew’s hanging and the field bought by the priests, and Acts’ falling headlong in a field of his own. The two death-notices are the seed of everything weighed on this page’s arguments.

Claims & evidence

The name: “Iscariot,” son of Simon

In all four lists of the Twelve Judas comes last, and always with the same tag — the betrayer. John twice gives his patronymic, “the son of Simon”:

He spake of Judas Iscariot the son of Simon: for he it was that should betray him, being one of the twelve.

— John 6:71 (cf. Matthew 10:4, Mark 3:19, Luke 6:16, each closing the list with Judas “who also was the traitor”). The surname Iskariōtēs is most often read as the Hebrew ’îsh-Qêriyôth, “man of Kerioth” — a town of southern Judah (Joshua 15:25) — which, if right, would make Judas the one Judean among a company of Galileans. Older rival guesses (from the Latin sicarius, “dagger-man,” or an Aramaic root for “the false one”) are weaker on the philology; the “man of Kerioth” reading is now standard.

The role: keeper of the bag — and a thief

John alone tells us Judas carried the disciples’ money, and puts the darkest gloss on it. When Mary of Bethany anoints Jesus and Judas objects that the ointment might have been sold for the poor, the evangelist adds:

This he said, not that he cared for the poor; but because he was a thief, and had the bag, and bare what was put therein.

— John 12:6. The “bag” (glōssokomon) is the common purse of Jesus’ band; the charge of pilfering from it is John’s, not the Synoptics’, and colours the later tradition of Judas as a man moved by greed.

The betrayal for thirty pieces of silver

Matthew fixes the price, and in a form that will matter for the prophecy he later cites:

Then one of the twelve, called Judas Iscariot, went unto the chief priests, and said unto them, What will ye give me, and I will deliver him unto you? And they covenanted with him for thirty pieces of silver.

— Matthew 26:14–16. At the last supper John has Jesus mark the traitor with a morsel of bread: “And after the sop Satan entered into him. Then said Jesus unto him, That thou doest, do quickly” (John 13:26–27). In Gethsemane Judas identifies Jesus with a kiss, and the deed is done.

The end, first account: Matthew — remorse, the temple, the hanging

Matthew alone narrates Judas’s death, and does it in one dense scene of remorse, restitution refused, and suicide:

Then Judas, which had betrayed him, when he saw that he was condemned, repented himself, and brought again the thirty pieces of silver to the chief priests and elders, saying, I have sinned in that I have betrayed the innocent blood. And they said, What is that to us? see thou to that. And he cast down the pieces of silver in the temple, and departed, and went and hanged himself. And the chief priests took the silver pieces, and said, It is not lawful for to put them into the treasury, because it is the price of blood. And they took counsel, and bought with them the potter’s field, to bury strangers in. Wherefore that field was called, The field of blood, unto this day.

— Matthew 27:3–8. Matthew then binds the scene to prophecy: “Then was fulfilled that which was spoken by Jeremy the prophet… And they took the thirty pieces of silver… and gave them for the potter’s field” (Matthew 27:9–10) — a citation whose wording is mostly Zechariah 11:12–13, not Jeremiah, and which is weighed on its own argument page. Here Judas hangs himself; and it is the priests who buy the potter’s field, called “the field of blood” because bought with blood-money.

The end, second account: Acts — a field of his own, and falling headlong

Peter, proposing that Judas’s place be refilled, gives a different-sounding end:

Now this man purchased a field with the reward of iniquity; and falling headlong, he burst asunder in the midst, and all his bowels gushed out. And it was known unto all the dwellers at Jerusalem; insomuch as that field is called in their proper tongue, Aceldama, that is to say, The field of blood.

— Acts 1:18–19. Peter too anchors the fate in Scripture, but to the Psalms: “Let his habitation be desolate… and his bishoprick let another take” (v. 20, quoting Psalms 69:25 and 109:8). Here Judas himself buys the field; he dies not by hanging but by falling headlong and bursting open; and the field is “field of blood” for a different reason — the blood of his own death on it.

Notes

  • Two death-notices, several divergences. Matthew and Acts agree that Judas died violently, that his betrayal-money went into a field, and that the field was ever after called “the field of blood.” They differ on how he died (hanging vs. falling headlong), who bought the field (the priests vs. Judas), and why it bears its name (bought with blood-money vs. site of a bloody death). Whether these are irreconcilable is weighed on the manner-of-death page and the Field-of-Blood page.
  • “Man of Kerioth.” If “Iscariot” is ’îsh-Qêriyôth, Judas is the only member of the Twelve whose surname marks him a Judean rather than a Galilean — a small but suggestive datum, since it is also the reason the epithet distinguishes him from “Judas… not Iscariot” (John 14:22), i.e. Thaddaeus/Jude.
  • The prophecy problem. Matthew’s ascription of the thirty-pieces oracle to “Jeremy the prophet” when the words are chiefly Zechariah’s is a separate, long-noticed crux, treated on its own page.
