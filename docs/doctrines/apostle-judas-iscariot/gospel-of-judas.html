The Gospel of Judas on Judas Iscariot

Dates: composed c. 130–170 (Greek original, lost); surviving in a single Coptic copy of c. 300 (Codex Tchacos) · Wikipedia
Summary: A 2nd-century Gnostic text — denounced by Irenaeus about 180 and lost until a Coptic copy surfaced and was published in 2006 — that reimagines Judas not as the arch-traitor but as the one disciple who truly understood Jesus, entrusted with secret knowledge. It is a window onto how one Gnostic sect recast the villain of the canonical story, not a source for the historical Judas.

Claims & evidence

The earliest notice: Irenaeus condemns a “Gospel of Judas” (c. 180)

A century before our surviving copy was written, Irenaeus of Lyons already knew — and denounced — a text of this name, ascribing it to a Gnostic group he calls the Cainites:

They declare that Judas the traitor was thoroughly acquainted with these things, and that he alone, knowing the truth as no others did, accomplished the mystery of the betrayal; by him all things, both earthly and heavenly, were thus thrown into confusion. They produce a fictitious history of this kind, which they style the Gospel of Judas.

— Irenaeus, Against Heresies 1.31.1 (ANF vol. 1). This fixes the text’s existence, and its Judas-as-knower theme, no later than about 180 — though whether Irenaeus’s Cainite book is precisely the Sethian text we now possess is debated.

The recovered text: Judas the favoured disciple

The Coptic Gospel of Judas is a secret dialogue set in the days before the crucifixion. Judas alone is able to stand before Jesus and confess his true, heavenly origin — “I know who you are and where you have come from. You are from the immortal realm of Barbelo” — and to him alone Jesus discloses the Gnostic cosmology hidden from the other eleven: a transcendent God above the flawed creator of the material world, and salvation by knowledge (gnosis) for an elect few.1 On the reading first published in 2006, Judas’s handing-over of Jesus is a service commanded by Jesus himself — freeing the divine spirit from its mortal body — not a crime.

A contested hero: the revisionist reading

The “exonerated Judas” of the first translation has since been challenged. April DeConick and others argue the Sethian author in fact damns him: Jesus names Judas the “thirteenth daimōn” — in Sethian usage a title for the world-ruling archon, not a compliment — and tells the disciples they are not of the holy generation and will not ascend.2 Whether the text lionizes or condemns Judas is thus itself disputed; what is not disputed is that it serves a 2nd-century Gnostic theology, not first-century memory.

Notes

  • Late, Gnostic, and dependent. The Greek original is dated by its language to the 2nd century, roughly a hundred years after Judas’s death, and presupposes the canonical Gospels. It is of no biographical value for the historical Judas; its worth is as evidence of how a Gnostic sect reimagined him — treated here the same way as the apocryphal Acts, an early witness to religious imagination, never laundered into fact.3
  • Manuscript vs. composition. The single surviving copy — in the Coptic Codex Tchacos, found near El Minya in Egypt in the 1970s and radiocarbon-dated to about 300 — is centuries younger than the 2nd-century text it preserves. It was restored and published, amid much publicity, by the National Geographic Society in 2006.4
