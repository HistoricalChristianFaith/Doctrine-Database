The Golden Legend on Judas Iscariot

Dates: c. 1260 (Jacobus de Voragine’s Legenda Aurea); the underlying legend medieval · Wikipedia
Summary: By the high Middle Ages the spare Gospel villain had grown a whole fabulous biography — an Oedipus-like tale of an exposed infant who unknowingly kills his father and marries his mother before coming to Christ. Jacobus de Voragine gathers it into the Golden Legend, telling it as the preamble to the life of Matthias (the apostle chosen to replace Judas) — and himself twice brands it apocryphal and “not authentic.” It is the clearest case on this timeline of legend accreting around a name.

Claims & evidence

The Oedipus of Judas: exposed, a parricide, and his mother’s husband

The Golden Legend tells Judas’s early life before it reaches Matthias, and opens by warning that the story is not Scripture:

It is read in a history, though it be named apocrypha, that there was a man in Jerusalem named Reuben…

— Jacobus de Voragine, the Golden Legend, life of St. Matthias (tr. William Caxton). The tale runs: Judas’s mother, Cyborea, dreams she “had conceived a child that should destroy their people,” so the parents set the newborn adrift — “they put him to a little fiscelle or basket well pitched… and set it in the sea.” The waves carry him to “an island named Scarioth” (whence, the legend says, his surname), where the queen finds and rears him. Grown and having learned he is a foundling, “Judas slew the child that he had supposed to be his brother, and was son to the king,” then “fled anon and came into Jerusalem, and entered into the court of Pilate.” Sent by Pilate to steal fruit from an orchard, he quarrels with its owner — his own unrecognized father — and “Judas smote his father with a stone on the head that he slew him.” Pilate then seizes the dead man’s goods and “gave his wife to Judas in marriage” — so that Judas weds his own widowed mother. Learning the double crime of parricide and incest, Judas “went to Jesu Christ, which did so many miracles, and prayed him of mercy and forgiveness.”

Jacobus’s own disavowal

Voragine ends the legend by disowning it as history, exactly as he had opened it:

Thus far it is read in the history which is not authentic.

— the Golden Legend, life of St. Matthias. The compiler flags his own material — a rare and telling honesty: the whole Oedipus-tale is medieval invention, and he knows it, before he returns to the canonical betrayal and the election of Matthias.

Dante: Judas in the mouth of Satan

The other great medieval elaboration is Dante’s. In the Inferno the deepest zone of Hell — the ninth circle’s innermost ring — is named Giudecca, after Judas, for traitors to their benefactors; and at the very centre Judas is the worst-punished soul of all, held head-first in the central mouth of the three-faced Satan, his back forever flayed:

“That soul up there which has the greatest pain,” the Master said, “is Judas Iscariot; with his head inside, he plies his legs without.”

— Dante, Inferno 34.61–63 (tr. Longfellow). Medieval imagination has travelled a long way from the two spare death-notices of the New Testament.

Notes

  • Legend, by the author’s own admission. The infancy-romance is a piece of high-medieval storytelling — the Oedipus pattern (exposed child → parricide → mother-marriage) transferred onto the arch-traitor — more than a millennium after the events, and Voragine twice says so (“named apocrypha”; “not authentic”). It is cited here as late legend, the same house treatment given the Golden Legend throughout the apostles section, never as evidence about the historical Judas.
  • The name, folk-etymologized. The legend derives “Iscariot” from the island “Scarioth” — a storyteller’s guess with no philological basis; the standard derivation is “man of Kerioth” (see the New Testament page).
