Did Matthew misattribute a Zechariah prophecy to “Jeremy the prophet”?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: plausible — the reading that Matthew’s citation is a deliberate composite, whose words come mainly from Zechariah 11 but whose imagery (potter, purchased field, a place renamed for innocent blood) is drawn from Jeremiah 18–19 and 32, and which names the senior/governing prophet Jeremiah on purpose, is coherent, fits Matthew’s demonstrable habit with fulfilment citations, and is the mainstream scholarly explanation — enough to defeat the charge of a simple blunder. It falls short of certainty only because why Jeremiah is named has several competing sub-explanations and none is decisive.

The claim

Then was fulfilled that which was spoken by Jeremy the prophet, saying, And they took the thirty pieces of silver… and gave them for the potter’s field, as the Lord appointed me.

— Matthew 27:9–10. The wording is substantially that of Zechariah 11:12–13 (thirty pieces of silver; cast to the potter) — not Jeremiah. The charge: Matthew has misremembered his source and named the wrong prophet, a plain error in the text.1

The evidence for a deliberate composite

No single passage contains what Matthew quotes. Zechariah supplies the words (thirty pieces, the potter, the house of the LORD) but no field and no purchase; Jeremiah supplies the motifs — the potter at his wheel (Jeremiah 18), an earthen vessel and a valley renamed for “innocent blood” near the Potsherd Gate (Jeremiah 19, strikingly close to Matthew’s Field of Blood), and the prophet buying a field for weighed-out silver (Jeremiah 32). Matthew’s quotation is a mosaic of both. Matthew does this elsewhere. His fulfilment formulae routinely fuse texts and name one source — as at Matthew 21:4–5 (Isaiah + Zechariah) — exactly as Mark 1:2–3 names “Isaiah” while quoting Isaiah and Malachi. Naming the more prominent prophet for a blended citation was an accepted convention. Jeremiah is Matthew’s paradigm rejected prophet. Matthew alone among the evangelists names Jeremiah (also 2:17; 16:14), and builds a rejected-prophet typology around him; naming him here is a redactional signal, not a slip — the argument of Michael Knowles’s standard monograph. The best patristic readers saw this. Augustine argued the Spirit “introduced the name of Jeremiah, who spoke of the purchase of the field, instead of… Zechariah… to whom we are indebted for the notice of the thirty pieces of silver” — a deliberate prompt to collate the two prophets. Jerome, who actually handled a Nazarene “apocryphal book of Jeremiah” containing the words, nonetheless judged it “more like a testimony taken from Zechariah,” cited by sense in the Evangelists’ usual manner.2

The evidence against, and the weaker fallbacks

The simplest reading is still “he erred.” Matthew names a prophet, and the words are another prophet’s; a critic may fairly say he misremembered, and that the composite-citation account is a post-hoc defence. That ancient scribes already felt the problem is shown by the variants — some manuscripts drop the name (“the prophet”), others substitute “Zechariah” or even “Isaiah” — but these are near-universally judged secondary corrections of the harder original “Jeremiah,” so the text-critical route defends the reading’s originality while conceding the difficulty was felt. Two popular fallbacks are weak. That “Jeremiah” names the head-scroll of the prophetic collection (Jeremiah standing first among the Latter Prophets in the order of b. Bava Batra 14b) is possible but underdetermined — it assumes that ordering was fixed and known in Matthew’s day, and does not explain why he names a prophet at all rather than “the prophets.” And the appeal to a lost Jeremiah prophecy (the Nazarene apocryphon) is the weakest of all: even Jerome, its one eyewitness, declined it.3

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The deliberate-composite reading is well-grounded: no one text yields Matthew’s quotation; the fusion of Zechariah’s words with Jeremiah’s potter-and-field imagery is demonstrable; the convention of naming the senior prophet is paralleled in Mark and in Matthew himself; and Matthew’s special interest in Jeremiah gives a positive reason for the name. This is the majority view among Matthean commentators (Carson, France, Davies & Allison, Blomberg, Knowles), and it is strong enough that “flat blunder” is not the most reasonable verdict.

What it cannot reach. The account explains that the citation is composite better than it fixes why Jeremiah in particular is named: the “more prominent prophet,” “rejected-prophet typology,” and “Jeremiah 19 governs the fulfilment” explanations overlap but none is decisive, and Gundry, granting Jeremiah 19’s role, doubts how much Jeremiah 18 and 32 really contribute. So the charge of a careless error does not hold, but neither is any single mechanism proven. The interpretation is coherent, majority, and defensible — short of demonstrated.4

Assessment

Plausible. Matthew’s ascription to “Jeremy” is best read as a deliberate composite citation — Zechariah’s words within a Jeremian frame of potter, field, and innocent blood, credited to the governing prophet — not as a misremembered source; the reading is coherent and mainstream and defeats the bare-error charge, while the precise reason Jeremiah is named remains genuinely underdetermined. The scroll-head and lost-apocryphon defences are weaker and unnecessary.
