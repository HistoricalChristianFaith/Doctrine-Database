The Field of Blood — who bought it, and why so named?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — that the priests bought the potter’s field (Matthew) and that Judas “purchased” it (Acts) can be reconciled by agency — they bought it with his money, in effect his purchase — and both name-etymologies (blood-money; a bloody death) can stand together; the harmonization is linguistically defensible and arguably the more probable reading of the field. But Acts plainly gives an independent reason for the name, and a strong critical school reads the two as separate popular etiologies. Genuinely disputed.

Related arguments: the two accounts of Judas’s end — see also Do Matthew and Acts contradict on how Judas died?

The claim

[Matthew:] And they [the chief priests] took counsel, and bought with them the potter’s field… Wherefore that field was called, The field of blood… [Acts:] Now this man [Judas] purchased a field with the reward of iniquity… that field is called… The field of blood.

— Matthew 27:7–8 and Acts 1:18–19. Two divergences are in view: who bought the field (the priests, or Judas), and why it is called “the field of blood” (because bought with blood-money, or because Judas bled to death on it).1

The evidence for harmonization

“Bought” by agency is ordinary usage. The priests took Judas’s returned money and bought the field with it; it is unforced to say both that they bought it and that Judas acquired it, since he furnished the price. The commentators state the principle flatly: “A man is said often to do a thing when he furnishes means for doing it,” and the field “was bought by the chief priests… with the money which Judas returned.” Luke’s verb (ektēsato, “acquired”) suits an acquisition through Judas’s money as much as by his own hand. Both etymologies can be true at once. A field bought with “the price of blood” and also stained by a bloody death is doubly “the field of blood” — the two reasons reinforce rather than exclude each other. The site fits both. Akeldama, on the southern slope of the Hinnom valley, was a potter’s clay-ground turned burial place; its identification is ancient (Eusebius’s Onomasticon; the Bordeaux Pilgrim of 333), and its red clay and quarry-edge suit a burial field named for blood on either account.2

The evidence against

Acts supplies a different reason for the name. Matthew is explicit that the name comes from the blood-money (“because it is the price of blood,” 27:6–8); Acts ties it to the notoriety of Judas’s death (“it was known unto all… insomuch as that field is called… The field of blood”), i.e. the blood of Judas on the ground. These are not the same etiology, and the “both are true” move is a harmonizer’s synthesis neither text makes. The buyer really does differ on the surface. Matthew’s priests buy the field precisely because they may not put blood-money in the treasury; Acts’ Judas buys it himself — the agency reading smooths this, but it is smoothing. Each account is shaped for its own book. Matthew’s potter’s field serves his Zechariah/Jeremiah fulfilment citation (the potter, the thirty pieces, a renamed field of blood, cf. Jeremiah 19); Acts’ desolate field serves Peter’s Psalm citation (“let his habitation be desolate”). The divergence looks like two independent Jerusalem traditions about the same notorious plot, each pressed into a different scriptural mould.3

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The buyer-divergence is the weaker half of the contradiction charge: purchase by agency is normal Greek and normal speech, and no one doubts the money was Judas’s. On this point the harmonization is genuinely strong — arguably more probable than not — and the dual name (blood-money and a bloody death) is coherent rather than forced.

What it cannot reach. The naming-divergence is the harder half: Acts states its own reason for “field of blood,” and it is not Matthew’s, so declaring both operative is an addition to the text. The most economical reading of the whole is that two authentic popular traditions attached to one real plot of ground — the potter’s field bought with the betrayal-money, remembered also as the place of the traitor’s ghastly end — and each evangelist tells it toward his own prophetic point. That is neither a clean harmony nor a fatal contradiction. On the evidence the field-question is disputed, though it leans more harmonizable than the manner-of-death.4

Assessment

Contested. “The priests bought it” and “Judas purchased it” reconcile easily by agency, and a field can bear a blood-name on two counts at once; but Acts gives an independent reason for the name that Matthew does not, and the mainstream reading takes the two as separate etiologies of one site. Reconcilable, and more plausibly so than the death-manner divergence — but not established as a single authorial account.
