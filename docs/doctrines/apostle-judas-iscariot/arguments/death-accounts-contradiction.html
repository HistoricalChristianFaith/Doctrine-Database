Do Matthew and Acts contradict on how Judas died?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — Matthew’s hanging and Acts’ falling-headlong-and-bursting are reconcilable in principle (hanged, then the body fell; or the two describe different phases), and both are shaped by Old Testament type rather than reported as forensic detail; but the harmonizations are ad hoc, the accounts pull in different directions, and a strong critical school reads them as two independent symbolic traditions. Genuinely disputed, with no clear winner.

Related arguments: the two accounts of Judas’s end — see also Field of Blood — who bought it, and why so named?

The claim

[Matthew:] And he cast down the pieces of silver in the temple, and departed, and went and hanged himself… [Acts:] and falling headlong, he burst asunder in the midst, and all his bowels gushed out.

— Matthew 27:5 and Acts 1:18. The question under test is whether these are two accounts of one death that can stand together, or a genuine contradiction. A third witness, Papias, adds a swelling so gross that Judas “could not pass where a chariot could pass easily” and burst — neither hanging nor a simple fall.1

The evidence for harmonization

The classic reconciliation is mechanically possible. If Judas hanged himself and the rope or branch later gave way — or the body, undiscovered, swelled and fell — then he both “hanged himself” (Matthew) and, in the fall, “burst asunder” (Acts). The standard commentators put it plainly: “If in such a place the suicide first hanged himself and the cord which he used gave way, it is easy to understand how in the fall all the consequences described in this verse would be the result.”2 The two verbs describe different things. Matthew reports the act (a hanging); Acts, the state of the corpse (fallen, burst) — not necessarily the immediate cause of death, and not incompatible. An early harmonizing tradition exists. Apollinaris of Laodicea, citing Papias, already tried to hold the accounts together — on his reading Judas was cut down alive and lived on, dying later of the monstrous swelling — which, whatever its own value, shows the reconciling impulse is not a modern invention.3

The evidence against

The harmonization is ad hoc. Neither text mentions a broken rope or a delay; the “fell-because-the-cord-snapped” bridge is supplied entirely from outside, to force agreement the texts do not offer. The two accounts are pulling in opposite narrative directions. Matthew’s Judas repents, returns the money, and hangs himself in shame — a scene modelled on Ahithophel, the counsellor who betrayed David and “hanged himself” (2 Samuel 17:23), the only other Old Testament suicide by the same rare verb. Acts’ Judas shows no remorse, keeps the money to buy a field, and dies by a divine-seeming rupture on his own ground — a death of the wicked drawn from the Psalms Peter cites. These are theologically shaped endings, each fitting its own book, not two halves of one police report. Papias makes a third, not a bridge. His swelling-and-crushing is yet another manner of death, so the sources are diverging, not converging. And the strongest text-critical prop is weak. The suggestion that Acts’ prēnēs (“headlong”) should be read prēsthēs (“swollen,” to match Papias) rests on late Syriac, Georgian, and Armenian witnesses generally judged secondary corrections, not the original text.4

Adversarial assessment

What holds. There is no logical contradiction: a man can hang himself and, in the event, also fall and burst, so the two verbs are not strictly exclusive, and the wiki does not treat a difficulty as a falsehood merely because a bridge must be supplied. Read as complementary snapshots — act and aftermath — Matthew and Acts can be held together, and the site of Akeldama (a steep quarry-edge above the Hinnom valley) even makes a fall physically plausible.

What it cannot reach. Possibility is not probability. The reconciling detail is imported, not attested; the two narratives are built for different theological ends and even disagree on Judas’s inner state (penitent in Matthew, unrepentant in Acts); and Papias’s independent horror shows the tradition multiplying rather than settling. A serious body of scholarship (Zwiep, Ehrman, and cautiously Marshall) concludes the accounts are two symbolic traditions of a traitor’s fitting end, not to be fused into a third harmonized story. The honest verdict is that the harmonization is available but not compelling, and the contradiction-reading is respectable but not proven — a genuine standoff, on the evidence, not on any bar against the miraculous.5

Assessment

Contested. That Matthew’s hanging and Acts’ falling-and-bursting describe one death is possible and has a long harmonizing pedigree, but only by importing a broken rope the texts never mention; that they are two independently-shaped traditions is the mainstream critical reading, but it too is an inference. Neither “one reconcilable death” nor “flat contradiction” wins outright. What is firm is only the shared core: Judas betrayed Jesus and died soon after, violently.
