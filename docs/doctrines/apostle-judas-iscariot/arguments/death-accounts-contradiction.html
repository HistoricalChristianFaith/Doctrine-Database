Matthew’s hanging and Acts’ bursting: one death, told twice

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: plausible — Acts does not say Judas fell from a height. Prēnēs genomenos means “having become prostrate,” a description of the body’s state, and Matthew supplies the means; a hanged body in Jerusalem at Passover would in any case have been taken down within the day. So the two notices can describe one death without importing anything, the reading is ancient rather than a modern patch, and a major commentator on Acts defends it. It falls short of settled because most commentators decline it, because Acts’ wording tracks a known scriptural pattern for the ruin of the wicked, and because Papias soon supplies a third version.

Related arguments: the two accounts of Judas’s end — see also Matthew’s priests and Acts’ Judas: one purchase, one name

The claim

[Matthew:] And he cast down the pieces of silver in the temple, and departed, and went and hanged himself… [Acts:] and falling headlong, he burst asunder in the midst, and all his bowels gushed out.

— Matthew 27:5 and Acts 1:18. The reading weighed here is that these are two descriptions of a single death — Matthew giving how Judas died, Acts the state in which his body was found — and not two incompatible reports. A third witness, Papias, adds a swelling so gross that Judas “could not pass where a chariot could pass easily” and burst — neither hanging nor a fall.1

What can be said for it

Acts does not describe a fall. The sharpest form of the contradiction charge — a man cannot both hang and plunge from a height — depends on rendering prēnēs genomenos as “falling headlong.” The Greek says no such thing. Prēnēs is an adjective meaning “prone, face-downward,” and the phrase is “having become prostrate”; it names a posture, not a trajectory. In 3 Maccabees 5:50 the same word describes people who “threw themselves down prēneis” — face-down on the ground, from no height at all. The ancient versions read it that way: the Old Latin has him “cast down on his face,” the Peshitta and Coptic likewise. “Headlong” enters the English tradition with the Geneva Bible of 1560 and has been read as a plunge ever since. Strip the translator’s gloss and Acts is describing a corpse lying face-down and ruptured — which is a description of the aftermath, not a rival account of the cause.2

The body was going to come down. The usual objection is that the reconciling step — the body leaving the rope — is invented to order. It is not: it is required by the law and the season. Deuteronomy forbids leaving a hanged body overnight — “his body shall not remain all night upon the tree, but thou shalt in any wise bury him that day” — and this is Passover in Jerusalem, the very concern that governs the removal of the bodies at the crucifixion. Whoever found Judas would have cut him down within hours. Craig Keener, who does not himself harmonize, states the point plainly: Matthew’s hanging “could fit Luke’s (then posthumous) spattering if the rope eventually broke or was cut (Judeans did not leave corpses hanging).” The step that is supposed to be imported is the one step the situation compels.3

Acts’ silence is not a denial. Luke is not narrating Judas’s death; Peter is explaining, in one clause, why the twelfth place is empty. Darrell Bock, in the standard evangelical commentary on Acts, presses this against C. K. Barrett’s flat verdict:

Barrett (1994: 93) claims that no harmonization of these accounts is possible. But things are not so clear.

On the manner of death he adds that Conzelmann’s reading of a fall from a roof or cliff aside, “whether there was an earlier hanging is not precluded by what Acts does say,” and concludes that “different purposes and concerns lead to the differing details, which are reconcilable.” Two writers selecting different details from one event is the ordinary condition of ancient narrative, not a special pleading.4

The reading is ancient, not a modern repair. It is sometimes said that reconciling the two notices is a post-Enlightenment expedient. The record runs the other way. About 380 the Apostolic Constitutions already tell the story as one sequence, without a hint that anything needs smoothing — Judas “repented, and hanged himself, and burst asunder in the midst, and all his bowels gushed out.” About the same date Apollinaris of Laodicea, quoting Papias, offers a worked reconciliation with a different mechanism: “Judas did not die by hanging, but lived on, having been cut down before he was suffocated.” The Latin text of Acts known to Augustine had absorbed the hanging into the verse itself — collum sibi alligavit, et deiectus in faciem, disruptus est medius, “he bound his own neck, and cast down on his face, burst asunder in the midst” — and Jerome’s Vulgate fixed it there (et suspensus crepuit medius, “and being hanged he burst in the middle”). The Greek commentary on Acts transmitted under the name of Oecumenius states the modern form of the harmony outright:

And when they say that he used a rope and hung himself, the sacred Gospels also record that if he now fell forward and everything that follows, there is no contradiction. For both happened: the cord by which the hanging occurred broke, and therefore he was carried forward onto the ground and had fallen.

Bede in the eighth century and Theophylact in the twelfth take the two as one death without argument. Whatever its merits, the reading is the church’s long-standing one.5

The two notices converge more than they diverge. Matthew and Acts show no knowledge of each other, yet agree that Judas betrayed Jesus, that the betrayal-money went into a field, that he died violently and soon, that the field became a burial ground, and that it was known in Jerusalem by an Aramaic name meaning “field of blood.” Barrett — who denies the accounts can be harmonized — nonetheless judges it “most unlikely that Matthew and Luke should both, independently, have made the association.” Bock makes the same point from Luke’s side: the aside that the field “was known unto all the dwellers at Jerusalem” is a strange thing to write about an invention. Even Bart Ehrman takes the place-name to be pre-Christian and historical. Whatever the manner of death, both writers are handling a remembered event, not each spinning a fable.6

What weighs against it

Acts’ wording tracks a scriptural pattern. This is the serious objection, and it is lexical rather than mechanical. Wisdom of Solomon 4:19, describing God’s ruin of the ungodly, uses the two words of Acts 1:18 together — rhēxei (“he shall rend them”) and prēneis (“prostrate”), “that they shall be speechless.” Kirsopp Lake in 1933 made this the key to Acts, and Barrett follows him. The ruin of a traitor by bursting and spilled entrails is a well-worn convention besides — Antiochus in 2 Maccabees 9, Amasa in 2 Samuel 20, Herod in Acts 12, the traitor Nadan in the Ahiqar legend — just as Matthew’s hanging echoes Ahithophel, the counsellor who betrayed David and “hanged himself,” the only other figure in the Greek Bible described by the same verb apēnxato. If the manner-details in each account are supplied by a scriptural template, then the question of how they fit together is the wrong question: neither is reporting the mechanics. Arie Zwiep puts the methodological form of it — conflating the two produces a third narrative that “neither story was ever meant” to yield.7

Papias makes a third, not a bridge. Within a generation or two the swelling-and-crushing story is circulating as its own version. Candida Moss reads it as a dropsy narrative — edema and worms being, in ancient medicine, the somatic signature of greed — and as a development downstream of Acts rather than an independent memory. Either way the tradition is multiplying, which is what one expects when the manner of death was not firmly known.

Most of the props usually offered for the harmony do not bear weight. Three in particular should be set aside, and the case is stronger without them. (i) The forensic argument. That a suspended body bloats with putrefaction gases and ruptures on falling is not fantasy — gas distension can split skin and herniate organs, and a systematic review of suicidal hangings records a case where advanced putrefaction, not the fall, caused the body to come apart. But no case of decompositional evisceration in a hanged body being cut down is recorded anywhere in the literature; in warm dry air a body may mummify rather than bloat; scavengers are the ordinary explanation for an opened abdomen outdoors; and the “three to seven days” textbook quotation on which the popular version rests is untraceable to any medical work. Nothing in either text supplies an interval anyway. (ii) The “swollen” reading. F. H. Chase’s 1912 proposal that prēnēs means “swollen” (as if from prēthō), which would align Acts with Papias, was dismantled by Lake — “there is too much extant Greek literature for us lightly to accept a new meaning for a well-known word” — and the sense is not registered by the standard lexicon. The variant prēstheis in late Syriac, Georgian and Armenian witnesses is itself a harmonizing correction toward Papias. It also cuts against the harmony it is meant to serve: a swelling is not a fall. (iii) The cliff. The southern Hinnom slope at Akeldama is genuinely quarried and terraced, with rock faces of thirty feet and more surveyed in the nineteenth century — but no scholar has actually argued the case from the terrain, and the classic travel-writers regularly cited for it say nothing of the kind.8

And the weight of commentary is against it. Marshall allows the combination and Bock defends it, but Lake, Barrett, Haenchen, Conzelmann, Zwiep, Pervo, and Robertson all decline to harmonize. A reading held by a minority of specialists is not thereby wrong, but it is not established either.

Assessment

Plausible. Two questions have to be kept apart. The popular apologetic case — a decomposing body, a snapped rope, a cliff above the Hinnom — is the weaker one: its forensic prop rests on a citation no one can find, its lexical prop was refuted in 1933, and its topographical prop has no advocate in the literature. The interpretation those props are meant to serve stands on its own without them. Acts does not say Judas fell from a height; it says his body lay prostrate and ruptured, which is a statement about the state in which he was found, and Matthew’s hanging supplies the means. That a hanged body came down the same day is not a convenient supposition but a requirement of the law at Passover. Acts is not narrating the death at all, so its silence about a rope denies nothing. And the reading is not a modern rescue: it is embedded in the Latin text of Acts from the fourth century and stated outright in the Greek commentary tradition.

What holds it short of probable is that Acts’ two operative words are the two words of Wisdom 4:19, and the ruin of the traitor is a pattern with a long literary pedigree, so the description may be shaped rather than observed; that Papias is already telling a third version; and that most who work closely on Acts read the two notices as independent traditions of a death whose manner was not remembered in common. The accounts can describe one death, coherently and without importing anything the situation does not already supply — but that they do is not demonstrated. What is firm is the core both attest: Judas betrayed Jesus, his money bought a field known in Jerusalem as the field of blood, and he died violently and soon after.
