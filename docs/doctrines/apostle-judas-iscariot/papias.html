Papias of Hierapolis on the death of Judas

Dates: c. 100–130 (Papias’s Exposition of the Oracles of the Lord, surviving only in fragments) · Wikipedia
Summary: Papias, a hearer of the elders and among the earliest post-apostolic writers, preserves a third and grislier account of Judas’s end — not hanging, not a simple fall, but a monstrous bodily swelling ending in his being crushed and bursting. Surviving only as a quotation in later writers (chiefly Apollinaris of Laodicea), it shows the tradition already growing more lurid within a generation or two of Acts.

Claims & evidence

Judas swollen monstrous, and burst

The fragment, preserved by Apollinaris of Laodicea and printed among the remains of Papias, reads:

Judas walked about in this world a sad example of impiety; for his body having swollen to such an extent that he could not pass where a chariot could pass easily, he was crushed by the chariot, so that his bowels gushed out.

— Papias, Fragments III (ANF vol. 1, tr. Roberts–Donaldson). The detail that Judas grew so bloated he “could not pass where a chariot could pass” and was then crushed is peculiar to Papias; it keeps the “bowels gushed out” of Acts 1:18 but supplies a cause — a swelling, not a fall — the New Testament does not give.

The later embellishments (Theophylact, after Papias)

The tradition did not stop with Papias. The ANF editors note that the Byzantine commentator Theophylact, quoting this passage, “adds other particulars, as if they were derived from Papias”: that Judas’s eyes swelled shut beyond a physician’s probing, that his body ran with worms, and that he died in a solitary spot so foul that “no one could pass the place without stopping up his nose with his hands.”

— editorial note to Papias, Fragments III (ANF vol. 1). These are later accretions layered onto Papias’s already vivid notice — the mechanism by which a spare Gospel report grows, across the centuries, into full-blown horror.

Notes

  • A third death, not simply Acts retold. Papias is sometimes read as harmonizing Acts, but he in fact reports a distinct manner of death — a swelling and a crushing — that neither Matthew (hanging) nor Acts (falling headlong) contains. His value here is as an early witness to how quickly, and how divergently, the story of Judas’s end multiplied. The relation of the three accounts is weighed on the manner-of-death argument page.
  • Early, but fragmentary and secondhand. Papias wrote within living memory of the apostolic age, which lends his notice weight; but the death-fragment survives only through later quotation (Apollinaris, Theophylact, and the catenae), so its exact original wording and context are not fully recoverable. Eusebius, who knew Papias’s five books, thought him a man “of very limited understanding” and credulous about marvels — a caution that applies to this fragment too.
