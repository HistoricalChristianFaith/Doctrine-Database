Aerius of Sebaste on the Ministerial Priesthood — presbyter and bishop, one order

Dates: c. 355 · Wikipedia
Summary: The earliest named figure recorded as flatly asserting that presbyter and bishop are one and the same order — the position Jerome would argue more moderately a generation later, here pressed to the point of schism. A presbyter of Sebaste in Pontus, passed over for the bishopric that went to Eustathius, Aerius held that "one is no different from the other … one order, and one honor and one rank," reciting a list of functions the two share — and, tellingly for this doctrine, he keeps the eucharistic offering firmly within the ordained clergy ("the bishop performs the eucharistic liturgy, the presbyter likewise"). He levels the ranks inside the ministry; he does not hand the offering to the laity. Epiphanius catalogued the position as the seventy-fifth heresy and answered it by making ordination the power that distinguishes the bishop — the very hinge Jerome would later grant.

Claims & evidence

"A bishop is no different from a presbyter — one order, one honor, one rank"

… his teaching was more insane than is humanly possible, and he says, "What is a bishop compared with a presbyter? The one is no different from the other. There is one order," he said, "and one honor and one rank. A bishop lays on hands," he said, "but so does a presbyter. The bishop administers baptism, and the presbyter does too. The bishop performs the eucharistic liturgy, the presbyter likewise. A bishop occupies the throne, and the presbyter also occupies one." With this he misled many, < who > regarded him as their leader.

— Aerius, as quoted by Epiphanius, Panarion 75 (Against the Aerians), §3.3 (Frank Williams trans.), archive.org. Aerius argues from the New Testament's interchange of titles — the apostle writes "to bishops and deacons" (Philippians 1:1) and tells a bishop he received his gift "at the hands of the presbytery" (1 Timothy 4:14) — "so that, as Aerius says, bishops and presbyters are the same" (§4.4). It is the same lexical case Jerome would build from the same texts; Aerius simply draws from it the radical conclusion that the episcopal rank is illegitimate.1

What Aerius does not deny: the offering stays with the ordained

It would be easy to read Aerius as a proto-congregationalist — and he is often enrolled among the "proto-Protestants" — but his own list forbids it. Every function he recites belongs to presbyter and bishop alike; not one is handed to the layman. In the same breath that levels the two clerical ranks he takes for granted that "the bishop performs the eucharistic liturgy, the presbyter likewise" — the offering is the act of an ordained minister, whether styled bishop or presbyter. His quarrel is with the gradation of the clergy, not with the reservation of the Eucharist to it. In that he is the exact forerunner of Jerome, who likewise equated presbyter and bishop while raging that the offering belongs only to those "through whose prayers the body and blood of Christ are produced." Aerius is thus a counter-witness to the hierarchy strand of the doctrine, and an unwitting witness for its who-may-offer core.

Epiphanius's reply: ordination is the bishop's distinguishing power

< From > his saying that a bishop and a presbyter are the same, it is plain to people with sense that he is simply foolish. How can this be? The one is an order that generates fathers. For the episcopate produces fathers for the church. But the presbyterate, which cannot produce fathers, produces children through the laver of regeneration, but surely not fathers or teachers. And since he is not ordained for the purpose of ordaining, how could a presbyter consecrate a bishop, or say that he is equal to a bishop? Aerius' quarrel and his jealousy have deceived him.

— Epiphanius, Panarion 75 §4.2–3, archive.org. Epiphanius rests the whole distinction on the power to ordain: the bishop "generates fathers" by consecrating other clergy, which the presbyter cannot do. This is the same fulcrum Jerome grants from the other side — "what function, excepting ordination, belongs to a bishop that does not also belong to a presbyter?" Where Epiphanius makes ordination the mark of a distinct, superior order, Jerome makes it the single reserved episcopal power and levels everything else; both agree it is the one function that separates bishop from presbyter.2

Notes

  • The natural pairing with Jerome. Aerius (c. 355) and Jerome (c. 393) make the same equation — presbyter = bishop — from the same New-Testament proof-texts (Philippians 1:1; 1 Timothy 4:14; and, in Jerome, Acts 20:28; Titus 1:5–7). The difference is temper and conclusion: Jerome historicizes the monarchical episcopate as a post-apostolic remedy for schism while remaining inside the Church and reserving ordination to the bishop; Aerius denounces the episcopal rank as such and broke communion over it, for which Epiphanius catalogued him among the heresies. On the framing dispute their testimony is weighed on the "distinct ministerial priesthood, or only the priesthood of all believers?" argument (assessment: contested): both bear on the hierarchy half of that question, not on the reservation of the offering.
  • Attested only through a hostile witness. No work of Aerius survives; his words reach us only as Epiphanius reproduces them in order to refute them, and Epiphanius prefaces the quotation by calling the teaching "more insane than is humanly possible." The bracketed insertions (< >) are the translator's. The report is nonetheless a direct quotation, and Augustine repeats the charge (De Haeresibus 53: the Aerians held "that a presbyter ought not to be distinguished from a bishop") — though, as with the parallel charge on prayer for the dead, Augustine "merely epitomises Epiphanius," so the ancient record of the position traces to this one witness.3
  • The same man, a second tenet. The Panarion 75 that records this presbyter-bishop equation is the same chapter that preserves Aerius's better-known rejection of prayer and offering for the dead — treated on his purgatory-timeline page. He is one figure carrying two distinct dissents, each catalogued by Epiphanius and each surviving only through him.
  • Where he sits on the timeline. Aerius falls between the First Council of Nicaea (325), which presupposes the reserved offering as already fixed "by canon and custom," and Ambrose (c. 391), who expounds why the offering belongs to the priest — a dissenting voice bracketed by the settled practice he disputes. His floruit is fixed by his break with Eustathius, made bishop of Sebaste c. 355, under whom Aerius had been a presbyter and a rival for the same see.
