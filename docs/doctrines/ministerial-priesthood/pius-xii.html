Pius XII on the Ministerial Priesthood

Dates: 20 November 1947 (the encyclical Mediator Dei, on the sacred liturgy) · Eugenio Pacelli, Pope Pius XII, 1876–1958; reigned 1939–1958 · Wikipedia
Summary: In Mediator Dei Pius XII gives the modern papal statement of the doctrine in the vocabulary the tradition had been moving toward: the ordained priest offers the Eucharistic sacrifice "in the person of Christ" (in persona Christi), because his minister "represents" the sacred Person of Jesus Christ and, by his consecration, "possesses the power of performing actions in virtue of Christ's very person." Against a then-current tendency to blur the ordained priesthood into the "common priesthood" of all the baptized, Pius insists that though the faithful truly "offer" the sacrifice in their own way, they do not thereby possess priestly power: "Only to the apostles, and thenceforth to those on whom their successors have imposed hands, is granted the power of the priesthood." He is the immediate papal antecedent of Vatican II's formula that the two priesthoods differ "in essence and not only in degree."

Claims & evidence

The priest offers in the person of Christ (Mediator Dei 69)

Explaining why the Mass is the same sacrifice as Calvary, Pius locates the identity in the priest, who at the altar acts not in his own name but in Christ's:

The priest is the same, Jesus Christ, whose sacred Person his minister represents. Now the minister, by reason of the sacerdotal consecration which he has received, is made like to the High Priest and possesses the power of performing actions in virtue of Christ's very person.

— Mediator Dei (1947), § 69, source.1 This is Cyprian's "that priest truly discharges the office of Christ, who imitates that which Christ did" and Ambrose's "the priest… uses the words of Christ," now stated as the settled reason of the modern magisterium: the consecration is Christ's act, performed through a minister who bears his Person.

The power of the priesthood is given only by the laying on of hands in succession (Mediator Dei 40)

Pius grounds the reservation of that power in the apostolic succession, exactly the chain Clement of Rome and Irenaeus had traced:

Only to the apostles, and thenceforth to those on whom their successors have imposed hands, is granted the power of the priesthood, in virtue of which they represent the person of Jesus Christ before their people, acting at the same time as representatives of their people before God.

— Mediator Dei (1947), § 40, source.2 The priest is here cast as a double representative — of Christ before the people, and of the people before God — the two directions the fathers had held together.

The ministerial priesthood distinguished from the common priesthood of the faithful (Mediator Dei 43)

The encyclical's most pointed contribution is its guard against dissolving the ordained priesthood into the priesthood of all believers. The faithful genuinely "offer" the sacrifice, Pius grants, but not by a priestly power of their own; ordination sets the priest apart:

In the same way, actually that baptism is the distinctive mark of all Christians, and serves to differentiate them from those who have not been cleansed in this purifying stream and consequently are not members of Christ, the sacrament of holy orders sets the priest apart from the rest of the faithful who have not received this consecration.

— Mediator Dei (1947), § 43, source.3 This is the seam Leo the Great had held in one sentence — the whole people "consecrated priests by the oil of the Holy Spirit," yet "beyond the special service of our ministry as priests" — here defended against a modern flattening of the distinction, and it feeds directly into the argument the timeline turns on (a distinct ministerial priesthood, or only the priesthood of all believers?).

Notes

  • The modern papal statement. Mediator Dei is the magisterial text that fixes the phrase in persona Christi as the reason for the reserved offering, the idiom Aquinas had systematized. It carries the patristic conviction into the twentieth century without altering its substance.
  • Guarding the distinction, not denying the common priesthood. Like Leo, Pius affirms that the faithful truly share in Christ's priesthood and truly "offer" — he does not deny the priesthood of all believers; he denies only that it makes them priests in the sacrificing sense, so that the ordained and the baptized "offer" in two different modes. That is the precise point Vatican II would sharpen into "differ… in essence and not only in degree."
  • Provenance. Mediator Dei is quoted from the official English translation published by the Vatican; the section numbering follows that edition.
