Thomas Aquinas on the Ministerial Priesthood

Dates: c. 1274 AD (the Summa Theologiae, Third Part, left unfinished at his death) · Thomas Aquinas, c. 1225 – 1274; Dominican friar and the pre-eminent scholastic theologian of the Latin West · Wikipedia
Summary: Aquinas gives the doctrine its mature scholastic form. Asking "whether the consecration of this sacrament belongs to a priest alone," he reasons that because the Eucharist "is performed only as in the person of Christ," and "whoever performs any act in another's stead, must do so by the power bestowed by such a one," the priest can consecrate only because "the power of consecrating this sacrament on Christ's behalf is bestowed upon the priest at his ordination" — the very point at which he "is put upon a level with them to whom the Lord said… 'Do this for a commemoration of Me'" (Luke 22:19). What Cyprian had stated as the priest "discharging the office of Christ," Aquinas systematizes: the celebrant acts in persona Christi by an instrumental power conferred at ordination, so that "it belongs to priests to accomplish this sacrament."

Claims & evidence

The power to consecrate is conferred at ordination (Summa Theologiae III, Q. 82, a. 1)

In the Third Part of the Summa, treating "the minister of this sacrament," Aquinas asks whether the consecration belongs to a priest alone. He answers from the principle that the Eucharist is performed in the person of Christ, and that to act in another's stead one must hold a power bestowed for it:

As stated above, such is the dignity of this sacrament that it is performed only as in the person of Christ. Now whoever performs any act in another's stead, must do so by the power bestowed by such a one.

— Summa Theologiae III, Q. 82, a. 1, source (Fathers of the English Dominican Province translation).1 From this he draws the conclusion that the lay believer is given the power to receive the sacrament in baptism, but the power to consecrate it is given only at ordination:

But as the power of receiving this sacrament is conceded by Christ to the baptized person, so likewise the power of consecrating this sacrament on Christ's behalf is bestowed upon the priest at his ordination: for thereby he is put upon a level with them to whom the Lord said (Luke 22:19): "Do this for a commemoration of Me."

— Summa Theologiae III, Q. 82, a. 1, source.2 The argument fastens the doctrine to the Last-Supper command: the priest may consecrate because ordination places him among those to whom Christ said "Do this" — the "this do in remembrance of me" charge (Luke 22:19) read as committed to the apostles and, through them, to the ordained. Aquinas's verdict is flat:

Therefore, it must be said that it belongs to priests to accomplish this sacrament.

— Summa Theologiae III, Q. 82, a. 1, source.3

Notes

  • What it establishes. Aquinas turns the patristic conviction into a systematic mechanism. Cyprian had said the priest "discharges the office of Christ"; Ignatius had bound the valid Eucharist to the bishop's authority. Aquinas supplies the metaphysics: the consecration is an act done in persona Christi by an instrumental power that Christ confers on the priest at ordination — so the reservation of the offering to the ordained is not a matter of church order alone but follows from what the sacrament is.
  • Ties to the Luke 22 proof-text. Aquinas rests the priest's power directly on the "do this in remembrance of me" command (Luke 22:19), read as addressed to the apostles and transmitted by ordination — the institution-narrative strand weighed in the summary's Arguments.
  • Provenance. The Summa Theologiae is not among the works held in the collections surveyed here, which carry Aquinas's biblical commentaries but not the Summa; the text is quoted from the standard Fathers of the English Dominican Province translation as published online.
