Hippolytus of Rome on the Ministerial Priesthood

Dates: c. 215 AD (the Apostolic Tradition) · Hippolytus, c. 170 – c. 235; presbyter (and rival bishop) at Rome, the most learned Roman churchman of his generation · Wikipedia
Summary: The Apostolic Tradition preserves the earliest surviving Christian ordination prayers, and they draw the ministerial priesthood as sharply as any ancient text. The bishop is consecrated in frankly high-priestly terms — to "serve as thy high priest," to "offer thee the gifts of thy holy church," and by "the Spirit of high-priesthood to have authority to remit sins." The deacon, by contrast, "is not ordained to the priesthood, but to serve the bishop," and receives none of "that Spirit that is possessed by the presbytery." The document thus grades the orders by their relation to the offering: bishop and presbyter share the priestly Spirit; the deacon does not.

Note on attribution: this church-order, long ascribed to Hippolytus of Rome and dated c. 215, survives only in later Latin, Coptic, and other versions, and a number of modern scholars regard it as a composite text of uncertain authorship. The dating and Roman provenance are debated; what is not in doubt is that it transmits very early liturgical usage. It is cited here from Burton Scott Easton's 1934 English edition.

Claims & evidence

The bishop is ordained as "high priest" to offer the Church's gifts (Apostolic Tradition 3)

At the consecration of a bishop, one of the assembled bishops lays on hands and prays that God pour out the "royal Spirit," in language saturated with the offering and the high priesthood:

Thou who knowest the hearts of all, grant to this thy servant, whom thou hast chosen to be bishop, [to feed thy holy flock] and to serve as thy high priest without blame, ministering night and day, to propitiate thy countenance without ceasing and to offer thee the gifts of thy holy church. And by the Spirit of high-priesthood to have authority to remit sins according to thy commandment…

— Apostolic Tradition, ch. 3, source (Burton Scott Easton, The Apostolic Tradition of Hippolytus, Cambridge, 1934).1 The prayer welds the two strands of the doctrine into one act: the bishop is made a "high priest" whose proper work is "to offer… the gifts of thy holy church" — the Eucharistic offering — and the same "Spirit of high-priesthood" carries the authority to remit sins. Where Ignatius had asserted that the valid Eucharist hangs on the bishop, here the Church's own liturgy confers the offering-power by ordination, in explicitly sacerdotal words.

The deacon "is not ordained to the priesthood" (Apostolic Tradition 8–9)

The rite grades the three orders precisely by whether they share the priestly Spirit. Presbyters join in laying hands on a new presbyter "because of the common and like Spirit of the clergy," but the deacon is set apart:

When the deacon is ordained, this is the reason why the bishop alone shall lay his hands upon him: he is not ordained to the priesthood but to serve the bishop and to carry out the bishop's commands. … He does not receive that Spirit that is possessed by the presbytery, in which the presbyters share; he receives only what is confided in him under the bishop's authority. For this cause the bishop alone shall make a deacon.

— Apostolic Tradition, ch. 9 (with ch. 8 on the presbyter), source (Easton translation).2 The distinction is the very one the doctrine turns on. Bishop and presbyter share the priestly Spirit — hence the presbyters lay on hands at a presbyter's ordination — whereas the deacon "is not ordained to the priesthood," but only to service, and so is ordained by the bishop alone. The document even limits the presbyter: he "has only the power to receive; but he has no power to give," so "a presbyter does not ordain the clergy." The offering belongs to the priestly orders; the ministry of the table and of service does not.

Notes

  • What it establishes. The Apostolic Tradition gives the earliest text in which the Church's liturgy itself grades the clergy by their relation to the offering: the bishop is consecrated "high priest" to offer the Church's gifts, the presbyter shares his priestly Spirit, and the deacon is expressly "not ordained to the priesthood." It moves the doctrine from assertion (Ignatius) to enacted rite — the offering-power is given at ordination, in sacerdotal words.
  • Authorship and date. The ascription to Hippolytus and the c. 215 dating are the traditional view; modern liturgical scholarship (Bradshaw, Johnson, Phillips and others) treats the work as a layered compilation whose original shape is hard to recover. This does not affect its value here — the ordination prayers are agreed to be very early — but the reader should not lean on it as a securely datable single-author witness. On this wiki Hippolytus is anchored elsewhere at c. 204–205 for his Commentary on Daniel; the later date here reflects the church-order, not his floruit.
  • Relation to the timeline. Hippolytus stands between Tertullian's ordo/laity distinction (c. 208) and Cyprian's priest who "discharges the office of Christ" (c. 253), supplying the liturgical link the two flank: the moment the offering-power is conferred by an ordination prayer in high-priestly terms.
