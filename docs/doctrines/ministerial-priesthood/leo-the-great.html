Leo the Great on the Ministerial Priesthood

Dates: c. 450 AD (the teaching spans his pontificate, 440–461 — the anniversary Sermons 1–4, preached on the day of his consecration in the early 440s, and the Holy-Week Sermon 59 on the Passion; Leo c. 400–461) · Wikipedia
The fifth-century Roman synthesis, seated between Augustine and the schoolmen. Preaching year by year on the anniversary of his own consecration, Pope Leo I gathers the earlier convictions into a single frame: the priesthood is not a human inheritance but an office "handed on in perpetuity" from Christ — the eternal High Priest "after the order of Melchizedech" — through Peter and the ministry, so that it is "the condescension of Divine grace," not birth or the people's choice, "which creates the bishop." Leo holds this together with the fullest patristic statement of the corporate priesthood: all the regenerate are "a royal race and… sharers in the office of the priesthood" — yet, he adds in the same breath, that common priesthood stands "beyond the special service of our ministry as priests," the sacramental office kept distinct from the priesthood all the baptized share. And on the altar he sets the New-Covenant sacrifice: "the one offering of… Body and Blood" that has replaced "the variety of fleshly sacrifices," presented now by "priests of holier anointing." He is the bridge from Augustine's office-not-merit principle to the mature medieval doctrine Aquinas would systematize.

Claims & evidence

The priesthood is handed on from Christ, through Peter, in perpetuity — and Divine grace, not birth, creates the bishop

Leo's anniversary sermon on his elevation to the papacy takes as its theme that the ministry is a succession from Christ the eternal priest, not a human office men confer on themselves. Reading "Thou art a priest for ever after the order of Melchizedech" of Christ — "in whom was prefigured the eternal High Priest" — he presses that "now that the mystery of this Divine priesthood has descended to human agency, it runs not by the line of birth," but is given to "those men… whom the Holy Ghost prepares":1

…now that the mystery of this Divine priesthood has descended to human agency, it runs not by the line of birth, nor is that which flesh and blood created, chosen, but without regard to the privilege of paternity and succession by inheritance, those men are received by the Church as its rulers whom the Holy Ghost prepares: so that in the people of God's adoption, the whole body of which is priestly and royal, it is not the prerogative of earthly origin which obtains the unction, but the condescension of Divine grace which creates the bishop.

The title of the section states the principle outright — "From Christ and Through S. Peter the Priesthood is Handed on in Perpetuity" — and Leo binds the ordained ministry to that unbroken line, grounding it, as Clement and Irenaeus had, in an apostolic succession, but now fixed on the Petrine see he holds. Where the earlier witnesses argued that the offering belongs to a succeeding order, Leo names its source: the office is created by the Spirit's grace at consecration, not seized by any man for himself.

A royal and priestly people — yet the offering is "the special service of our ministry as priests"

Leo gives the fathers' fullest affirmation of the priesthood of all believers, and in the same sentence marks off the ordained ministry from it. Preaching again on his consecration day, he tells the whole congregation that their baptism has made them royal and priestly — and then draws the distinction the doctrine turns on:2

For all, regenerated in Christ, are made kings by the sign of the cross; they are consecrated priests by the oil of the Holy Spirit, so that beyond the special service of our ministry as priests, all spiritual and mature Christians know that they are a royal race and are sharers in the office of the priesthood.

The clause "beyond the special service of our ministry as priests" is the hinge: the common priesthood of the baptized is real and full — Leo will not soften it — but it is set alongside, not in place of, "the special service" that the ordained perform. This is the same both-and already seen in Justin, Irenaeus, Tertullian, and Augustine: a corporate priesthood affirmed without surrendering the reserved ministry of the altar. Because the two priesthoods are held together in one sentence, Leo is a witness cited on both sides of the framing question weighed in the summary's Arguments.

The one offering of Christ's Body and Blood, presented by "priests of holier anointing"

That the eucharistic action is a true sacrifice — the premise the reserved ministry rests on — Leo states in his Holy-Week preaching on the Passion, where the cross is called "the fount of all blessings" that has turned the old rites into the new:3

…what before was done in the one temple of the Jews in dark signs, was now to be celebrated everywhere by the piety of all the nations in full and open rite. For now there is a nobler rank of Levites, there are elders of greater dignity and priests of holier anointing… Now, too, the variety of fleshly sacrifices has ceased, and the one offering of Thy Body and Blood fulfils all those different victims: for Thou art the true "Lamb of God, that takest away the sins of the world," and in Thyself so accomplishest all mysteries, that as there is but one sacrifice instead of many victims, so there is but one kingdom instead of many nations.

Where Ambrose located the priest's act in his speaking Christ's words and Cyprian in his "discharging the office of Christ," Leo frames it in the language of a superseded and fulfilled sacrificial order: the Levitical priesthood is gone, but a "nobler rank" of ministers with a "holier anointing" now presents "the one offering" of the Lord's Body and Blood in every place — the same reading of Malachi's worldwide pure offering that runs from the Didache onward. His change-of-order language on the Eucharist is cited here for the ministerial point; the sacrificial realism of these Passion sermons also bears on the Real Presence.

Notes

  • Dating. The quotations come from sermons preached across Leo's pontificate (440–461), not from a single datable work: the succession sermon and the "royal race" sermon are among the anniversary sermons on his consecration (traditionally assigned to his early years, the first delivered 29 September 440 and its successors on the following anniversaries), and the "one offering" passage is from a Holy-Week Passion sermon of the same period. The timeline anchors him at a representative c. 450, seating him in the long gap between Augustine (c. 400) and Aquinas (c. 1274).
  • The corporate priesthood is genuinely his, and does not undo the reservation. Leo's "royal race… sharers in the office of the priesthood" is a favourite proof-text for the priesthood of all believers, and rightly — it should not be softened. But Leo himself, in the same breath, sets it "beyond the special service of our ministry as priests," and everywhere assumes that the offering of the altar is presented by the ordained; the corporate priesthood names a dignity all the baptized share, not a licence to preside at the Eucharist. He is a doubled witness here for exactly that reason, like Tertullian before him.
  • Where he sits in the development. Leo does not add a new mechanism so much as gather the tradition into the classic Roman form: the priesthood as a Christ-derived office handed on in succession (Clement, Irenaeus), created by the Spirit at consecration and not by the man (Augustine's office-not-merit principle), presenting the one New-Covenant sacrifice (Cyprian, Ambrose). It is this synthesis that the medieval schools inherit and Aquinas systematizes as a power conferred in persona Christi at ordination.
  • Texts used. Sermon 3 (on the anniversary of his elevation) and Sermon 59 (on the Passion) are quoted in the NPNF translation (Feltoe, Nicene and Post-Nicene Fathers, ser. 2, vol. 12); Sermon 4 (on the anniversary of his consecration, Sermo 4.1 in PL 54) is quoted in the modern translation carried in the Roman Office of Readings. Leo also appears on the intermediate state and astrology timelines.
