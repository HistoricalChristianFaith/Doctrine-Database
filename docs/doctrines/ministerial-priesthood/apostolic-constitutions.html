The Apostolic Constitutions on the Ministerial Priesthood

Dates: c. 380 AD (a Syrian — probably Antiochene — compilation of earlier church-orders, redacted late in the fourth century) · Wikipedia
Summary: The largest surviving early church-order gathers the tradition this timeline traces into a developed liturgy. It keeps the Didache's reading of Malachi's "pure sacrifice," sets the eucharistic offering in the hands of the bishop it repeatedly names the "high priest," preserves in full the ordination prayers and the great thanksgiving he presides over, and presses — as sharply as Clement of Rome had — that no one may seize the sacerdotal office who has not received it. It is the fullest fourth-century witness that the offering belongs, name and act, to the ordained.

Claims & evidence

The Sunday Eucharist is the "pure sacrifice" Malachi foretold

The seventh book, an expansion of the Didache, directs the Lord's-day assembly and grounds its offering in the same prophecy the church had read eucharistically from the sub-apostolic age — Malachi 1:11:1

On the day of the resurrection of the Lord, that is, the Lord's day, assemble yourselves together, without fail, giving thanks to God, and praising Him for those mercies God has bestowed upon you through Christ, and has delivered you from ignorance, error, and bondage, that your sacrifice may be unspotted, and acceptable to God, who has said concerning His universal Church: "In every place shall incense and a pure sacrifice be offered unto me; for I am a great King, saith the Lord Almighty, and my name is wonderful among the heathen."

The offering is the whole Church's, and it is a "sacrifice" — but, as the book goes on to show, one made through an ordained ministry. The reading is exactly the one the Didache gave and Justin and Irenaeus pressed; here it is set inside a complete order of worship. (Whether Malachi's verse will bear this weight is weighed on the argument page.)

The bishop is the "high priest" who offers at the altar

Throughout the eighth book the presiding minister is styled the "high priest" (archiereus). The prayer for a bishop's consecration asks that God make him a high priest who presents the Church's gifts:2

…grant that this Thy servant whom Thou hast chosen to the holy office of Thy bishop, may discharge the duty of a high priest to Thee, and minister to Thee unblameably night and day; that he may appease Thee unceasingly, and present to Thee the gifts of Thy holy Church, and in the spirit of the high-priesthood have power to remit sins according to Thy commandment…

And when the book sets out the great thanksgiving itself — the earliest full eucharistic anaphora to survive — it is this same high priest who stands at the altar and leads it:3

Let the high priest, therefore, together with the priests, pray by himself; and let him put on his shining garment, and stand at the altar, and make the sign of the cross upon his forehead with his hand, and say: The grace of Almighty God, and the love of our Lord Jesus Christ, and the fellowship of the Holy Ghost, be with you all.

Where Hippolytus had preserved the earliest ordination prayers, the Constitutions preserves the earliest full liturgy that those prayers ordain a man to celebrate — and it is throughout the bishop, "the high priest," who offers, with the presbyters concelebrating and the deacons in subordinate service. The office Ignatius had made the ground of a valid Eucharist is here written into the rite word by word.

No one may seize the offering who has not received the office

The compilation is emphatic that the sacerdotal office is received, never usurped — the very plank Clement had laid down against the Corinthian revolt. Warning against those who intrude on the offering, the eighth book invokes the Old Testament's punished usurpers:4

…the neglect about the sacrifices and eucharistical offices which will arise from their being impiously offered by those who ought not to offer them; who think the honour of the high-priesthood, which is an imitation of the great High Priest Jesus Christ our King, to be a matter of sport…

The book's second book had already headed a whole chapter with the same warning — "That It is a Horrible Thing for a Man to Thrust Himself into Any Sacerdotal Office, as Did Corah and His Company, Saul and Uzziah" — and the eighth draws the constructive rule from it: the priesthood descends by orderly appointment, not self-election, as the apostles themselves were appointed by Christ and their ministers by them:5

…no one snatches the sacerdotal dignity to himself, but either receives it from God, as Melchisedec and Job, or from the high priest, as Aaron from Moses.

This is the same succession-and-reservation logic Clement and Irenaeus had drawn, now embedded in a liturgical constitution: the offering is the act of a "high priest" who holds an office handed on, and the layman who would seize it stands with Corah and Uzziah.

Notes

  • What it is, and how it is dated. The Apostolic Constitutions is a composite church-order in eight books, compiled in Syria (most likely Antioch) around 375–380 from earlier sources: books 1–6 rework the third-century Didascalia Apostolorum, book 7 the Didache, and book 8 an ordination-and-liturgy source akin to the tradition ascribed to Hippolytus, closing with the Apostolic Canons. It writes in the voice of the apostles through Clement of Rome, a literary fiction; its value here is as a late-fourth-century snapshot of a developed Antiochene liturgy and its theology of the ordained offering. The pseudonymous framing does not touch the point cited: a church that arranges its whole Sunday worship around a bishop-"high priest" who alone offers.
  • Both priesthoods, as elsewhere on this timeline. Like Justin, Irenaeus, and Leo, the Constitutions can speak of the whole Church's sacrifice while reserving the celebration to the ordained; the "high priest" language marks the celebrant off from the assembly without denying that the offering is presented in the people's name. The book's later reception was mixed — the Trullan council (692) flagged it as interpolated by heretics even while its Canons were received — but its liturgical and ordination material is prized as an early and detailed witness.
  • Texts used. Quotations are from the Roberts–Donaldson translation in the Ante-Nicene Fathers, vol. 7: Book VII ch. 30 (the Lord's-day assembly and Malachi), Book VIII chs. 5 (the bishop's consecration prayer), 12 (the anaphora), and 46 (the reserved offering and succession), with the parallel warning at Book II ch. 27. The Apostolic Constitutions also underlies the appended Apostolic Canons, which appear on the Old Testament canon timeline.
