Jerome on the Ministerial Priesthood

Dates: c. 393 AD (the position stated in the Commentary on Titus, c. 386–387, and Letter 146 to Evangelus, date uncertain; Jerome c. 347–420) · Wikipedia
The timeline's sharpest ancient qualifier — but not a denial. Jerome is the strongest patristic witness that the distinction between bishop and presbyter is not of apostolic institution but a later development: "a presbyter is the same as a bishop," and "before… the churches were governed by a common council of presbyters," the single bishop being raised over the rest only afterward, "to remedy schism." That historicizes the monarchical episcopate the doctrine's earlier witnesses (Ignatius, Cyprian) treat as apostolic. Yet Jerome is no congregationalist, and he does not touch the doctrine's core: in the very sentence that levels bishop and presbyter he takes for granted that the Eucharist is confected only through ordained hands — a deacon who exalts himself over the presbyters is "insane," for it is "through their prayers" that "the body and blood of Christ are produced." He reserves ordination to the bishop, holds the bishop rightly set "over" the presbyters "by the custom of the Church," and never lets a layman near the offering. So he qualifies the hierarchy strand of the doctrine while affirming its who-may-offer heart.

Claims & evidence

"The presbyter is the same as the bishop"

Expounding Paul's charge to Titus to "ordain presbyters in every city… for a bishop must be blameless" (Titus 1:5–7), Jerome fastens on the apostle's own slide between the two titles and draws the lexical conclusion the priesthood-of-all-believers case would later press: the New Testament uses presbyteros and episkopos for one office. His Commentary on Titus states it flatly:1

Therefore, the presbyter is the same as the bishop, and before, by the instigation of the devil, competitions arose in religion and it was said among the people, "I am of Paul," "I am of Apollo," "I am of Cephas," the governance of the church was conducted by the joint counsel of the presbyterate. But when each person began to consider those whom he had baptised as his own rather than Christ's, it was decided throughout the world that one chosen from among the presbyters should be placed in charge of the others and have care of the general well-being of the church, so that the seeds of schism might be rooted out.

He restates the whole argument, with the same proof-texts, in Letter 146 to Evangelus — written to rebuke someone who had ranked deacons above presbyters. There the lexical case is marshalled from Philippians 1:1 ("with the bishops and deacons"), Acts 20:28 (Paul calls the Ephesian presbyters "bishops"), Titus 1:5–7, and 1 Peter 5:1: "when the apostle clearly teaches that presbyters are the same as bishops."2

The single bishop is a remedy for schism, not an apostolic order

From the identity of the two titles Jerome draws the historical thesis that makes him a counter-witness. The monarchical bishop — one man set over the presbyters of a city — is, on his account, a post-apostolic arrangement adopted everywhere to check faction, not a rank the apostles instituted. He even supplies the local memory: at Alexandria the presbyters themselves made their bishop from among their own number down to the mid-third century.2

When subsequently one presbyter was chosen to preside over the rest, this was done to remedy schism and to prevent each individual from rending the church of Christ by drawing it to himself. For even at Alexandria from the time of Mark the Evangelist until the episcopates of Heraclas and Dionysius the presbyters always named as bishop one of their own number chosen by themselves and set in a more exalted position, just as an army elects a general… For what function, excepting ordination, belongs to a bishop that does not also belong to a presbyter?

The last question is the hinge. Jerome grants the bishop exactly one exclusive power — ordination — and no other; everything else the bishop does, the presbyter may do too, the Eucharist included. This is the exact reverse of the trajectory that runs from Ignatius, for whom the monarchical bishop is the God-given hinge of the Eucharist's validity, through Cyprian, who reads the bishop as successor of the apostles by divine appointment. Where they see the threefold hierarchy as apostolic, Jerome sees a two-order ministry (presbyter-bishop and deacon) over which a presiding bishop was later raised — "by the custom of the Church," as he says in the Titus commentary, closing the passage by binding both sides to that custom:3

Therefore just as the presbyters know that by the custom of the Church they are subject to him who has been placed over them as their head, so also let the bishops know that according to the custom of the Church they are greater than the presbyters and ought to regulate the whole church by common council, imitating Moses…

What Jerome does not deny: the Eucharist is confected by ordained hands alone

It would be easy to over-read Jerome into a flat denial of the ministerial priesthood, and popular appropriations sometimes do. But his own words forbid it. The occasion of Letter 146 is a man who set deacons over presbyters, and Jerome's indignation rests squarely on the presbyter's eucharistic dignity: it is the presbyters "through whose prayers the body and blood of Christ are produced," and for a deacon — "a mere server of tables and of widows" — to lord it over them is madness.1

For when the apostle clearly teaches that presbyters are the same as bishops, must not a mere server of tables and of widows be insane to set himself up arrogantly over men through whose prayers the body and blood of Christ are produced?

The Latin of that clause — ad quorum preces Christi corpus sanguisque conficitur, "through whose prayers the body and blood of Christ is confected" — uses the very verb (conficere) the developed Latin doctrine, and this doctrine's title, would use for the priest's act.4 Jerome takes for granted, that is, exactly what the doctrine asserts: the Eucharist is made by an ordained minister's prayers, and the line runs between the presbyter-bishop who may offer and the deacon and layman who may not. His quarrel is not with reserving the offering to the ordained — he assumes it — but with the further claim that the bishop's rank over the presbyter is of apostolic, divine institution. He affirms an ordained sacerdotal ministry (he elsewhere applies the language of "priesthood" to every bishop alike, "whether it be at Rome or at Engubium… his priesthood is one"); he denies only that the monarchical episcopate is more than a churchly development. He is a witness for the ministerial priesthood who prunes back one of its later overgrowths.

Notes

  • Counter-witness to the hierarchy, not to the reservation. Jerome cuts against the strand that treats the three-order, monarchical episcopate as apostolic — the Ignatian–Cyprianic line the timeline otherwise runs. He does not cut against the doctrine's core claim that only an ordained minister may confect the Eucharist; on that he is an emphatic witness for, sharpening the presbyter's eucharistic dignity against the deacon. Reading him as a proto-congregationalist — "no clergy, every believer a celebrant" — inverts him: he reserves ordination to bishops, ranks bishops above presbyters (if only "by the custom of the Church"), and never once lets a layman offer.
  • The one power he reserves to the bishop: ordination. Jerome's rhetorical question — "what function, excepting ordination, belongs to a bishop that does not also belong to a presbyter?" — is the fulcrum of the whole position. It concedes a real, exclusive episcopal power (so he is no leveller of all distinction) while denying the bishop any monopoly on the Eucharist. His picture is thus of a single ordained order (presbyter = bishop) within which, by later custom, one man presides and alone ordains.
  • An earlier, more radical voice for the same equation. Jerome was not the first to say "presbyter = bishop." Aerius of Sebaste (c. 355) had pressed the identity a generation before, from the same New-Testament interchange of titles — "a bishop is no different from a presbyter… one order, and one honor and one rank" (Epiphanius, Panarion 75.3). The difference is temper and outcome: Aerius denounced the episcopal rank as illegitimate and broke communion over it, and Epiphanius catalogued him as a heretic, answering that ordination is the bishop's distinguishing power. Jerome makes the identical lexical case and grants the identical exception (ordination), but historicizes the episcopate as a churchly remedy for schism while staying inside the Church — the moderate to Aerius's schismatic. Notably, Aerius too kept the offering with the ordained (bishop and presbyter alike "perform the eucharistic liturgy"), so the pairing sharpens the point: the ancient presbyter-bishop levellers reserved the Eucharist to the clergy no less firmly than their opponents.
  • How he is used, and misused. Jerome, Ep. 146 is the classic patristic prooftext for those — Reformers, later Presbyterians and other non-episcopal traditions — who argue that the bishop/presbyter distinction, and by extension a sharply set-apart hierarchical order, is a human development rather than divine institution. The appeal is fair to Jerome's historical thesis. It overreaches when it enlists him against the ministerial priesthood as such: Jerome would confine the Eucharist to ordained presbyters as firmly as any of his opponents, and his target is the rank of bishop over presbyter, not the rank of the ordained over the laity.
  • Bearing on the framing argument. Jerome's testimony is weighed as a live consideration on the "distinct ministerial priesthood, or only the priesthood of all believers?" argument page (assessment: contested). It cuts cleanly along that page's (a)/(b) seam: it supports (a), that from early on an ordained ministry alone offered the Eucharist, while it complicates the succession-of-a-distinct-episcopal-order case — the very hiereus/hierarchy development the page marks as third-century and contested. That the West's own great biblical scholar, translating and expounding the New Testament, found no apostolic bishop/presbyter distinction in its pages is a data-point the "apostolic from the first" narrative must absorb.
  • Texts used. The quotations are from Jerome's Commentary on Titus (on Titus 1:5–7, c. 386–387) and Letter 146 to Evangelus (date uncertain; the NPNF English of W. H. Fremantle). The timeline anchors his ecclesiological position at c. 393. Jerome also appears on the Old Testament canon, rabbinic corruption, perpetual virginity, prayer to the saints, infant baptism, and Nephilim timelines.
