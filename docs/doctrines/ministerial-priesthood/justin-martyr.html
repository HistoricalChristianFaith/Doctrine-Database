Justin Martyr on the Ministerial Priesthood

Dates: c. 155 (the First Apology addressed to Antoninus Pius, c. 155; the Dialogue with Trypho c. 155–160) · Wikipedia
Summary: Justin gives the earliest surviving description of the Christian Sunday liturgy, and in it a single minister — "the president of the brethren" — leads the prayers and "offers" the thanksgiving over the bread and cup, while "those who are called by us deacons" distribute the elements and carry a portion to the absent. The eucharistic food, he insists, is "not common bread and common drink" but "the flesh and blood of that Jesus who was made flesh," and admission to it is reserved to the baptized believer. And in the Dialogue with Trypho he reads Malachi's prophecy of a "pure offering… in every place" as fulfilled "in the Eucharist of the bread and the cup… presented by Christians in all places throughout the world." Three strands meet in him: a presiding minister who offers, a real presence, and the Eucharist as the Malachi sacrifice.

Note: this page treats Justin's witness to the ministerial shape of the rite; his testimony to the Real Presence and to the Eucharist as sacrifice are quoted here because they are bound up in the same passages.

Claims & evidence

One "president" offers the thanksgiving; deacons distribute (First Apology 65, 67)

There is then brought to the president of the brethren bread and a cup of wine mixed with water; and he taking them, gives praise and glory to the Father of the universe, through the name of the Son and of the Holy Ghost, and offers thanks at considerable length for our being counted worthy to receive these things at His hands. And when he has concluded the prayers and thanksgivings, all the people present express their assent by saying Amen… And when the president has given thanks, and all the people have expressed their assent, those who are called by us deacons give to each of those present to partake of the bread and wine mixed with water over which the thanksgiving was pronounced, and to those who are absent they carry away a portion.

— First Apology, ch. 65, source (Roberts–Donaldson translation, Ante-Nicene Fathers; cf. New Advent).1 The Sunday gathering follows the same pattern:

And on the day called Sunday, all who live in cities or in the country gather together to one place, and the memoirs of the apostles or the writings of the prophets are read, as long as time permits; then, when the reader has ceased, the president verbally instructs, and exhorts to the imitation of these good things. Then we all rise together and pray, and, as we before said, when our prayer is ended, bread and wine and water are brought, and the president in like manner offers prayers and thanksgivings, according to his ability, and the people assent, saying Amen; and there is a distribution to each, and a participation of that over which thanks have been given, and to those who are absent a portion is sent by the deacons.

— First Apology, ch. 67, source (cf. New Advent).2 What Justin shows is a differentiated assembly: not every believer offers, but one designated "president" (Greek ho proestōs, "the one presiding") presides over the prayers and thanksgiving, and a distinct order of "deacons" administers the elements. The act of offering the thanksgiving is his; the people's part is the "Amen." This is the structural plank the doctrine rests on, now attached to the rite itself: by the mid-second century the Eucharist already has a single presiding celebrant and an assisting diaconate. Justin does not, however, call the president a "priest" (hiereus) or "bishop" — those identifications are later inferences from the office he describes (see Notes).

Not common bread: "the flesh and blood of that Jesus who was made flesh" (First Apology 66)

And this food is called among us the Eucharist, of which no one is allowed to partake but the man who believes that the things which we teach are true, and who has been washed with the washing that is for the remission of sins, and unto regeneration, and who is so living as Christ has enjoined. For not as common bread and common drink do we receive these; but in like manner as Jesus Christ our Saviour, having been made flesh by the Word of God, had both flesh and blood for our salvation, so likewise have we been taught that the food which is blessed by the prayer of His word, and from which our blood and flesh by transmutation are nourished, is the flesh and blood of that Jesus who was made flesh.

— First Apology, ch. 66, source (cf. New Advent).3 Two points bear on the ministerial question. First, the realism: the consecrated food is "the flesh and blood of that Jesus who was made flesh," a thing too holy to be at every hand — which is why the same passage restricts admission to the baptized believer in right standing. Second, the food becomes the Eucharist when it is "blessed by the prayer of His word" (the eucharistic prayer offered, in chs. 65 and 67, by the president). The realism and the reserved office reinforce each other: a sacrament held to be the Lord's flesh is guarded as to who may confect and who may receive it. Justin's witness to the Real Presence is treated on its own at the Real Presence page.

The Malachi "pure offering," fulfilled "in the Eucharist of the bread and the cup" (Dialogue 117)

Accordingly, God, anticipating all the sacrifices which we offer through this name, and which Jesus the Christ enjoined us to offer, i.e., in the Eucharist of the bread and the cup, and which are presented by Christians in all places throughout the world, bears witness that they are well-pleasing to Him. But He utterly rejects those presented by you and by those priests of yours, saying, "And I will not accept your sacrifices at your hands; for from the rising of the sun to its setting my name is glorified among the Gentiles… but ye profane it."

— Dialogue with Trypho, ch. 117, source (Roberts–Donaldson translation, Ante-Nicene Fathers; cf. New Advent).4 Following the Didache, Justin reads Malachi 1:10–12 as a prophecy of the Christian Eucharist: the "pure offering" foretold "in every place" is "the Eucharist of the bread and the cup," offered by Christians the world over, against the rejected sacrifices of the Levitical priests. He thus joins the two threads the doctrine needs — the Eucharist is a sacrifice, and it is the New-Covenant fulfilment that supersedes the temple's. This argument is weighed on its own (Malachi as the eucharistic sacrifice) in the summary's Arguments section.

Notes

  • What it establishes. Justin supplies the earliest detailed picture of the rite in practice. By c. 155 the Sunday Eucharist already has (a) a single presiding minister who alone "offers" the prayers and thanksgiving, (b) an assisting order of deacons who distribute, (c) a real-presence understanding of the elements, and (d) admission restricted to the baptized. The offering is plainly the act of a designated officer, not of the congregation at large — the concrete liturgical correlate of the structural claim Clement and the Didache had stated, and the rule Ignatius had laid down.
  • What it does not say. Justin never calls the president a "priest" (hiereus) or "bishop," and never describes his ordination; he simply records the office as it functioned. The identification of the "president" with the bishop/presbyter is a reasonable inference from the threefold ministry already attested by Ignatius a generation earlier, but it is an inference, not Justin's own word. Honest weighing of the proponent's reading (president = priest/bishop) should note that the sacerdotal title is supplied from elsewhere, not from this text.
  • The "high priestly race." Cutting the other way, in the immediately preceding chapter Justin calls the whole believing people "the true high priestly race of God," for whom "in every place… sacrifices are presented to Him well-pleasing and pure," adding that "God receives sacrifices from no one, except through His priests" (Dialogue 116).5 Here the priestly language attaches to the body of believers, not to the president alone — a datum the "priesthood of all believers" side fairly presses. Justin holds both together without strain: a corporately priestly people whose worldwide offering is nonetheless made, in each assembly, through one who presides. The tension between these two readings is exactly the dispute the doctrine turns on, weighed in the summary's Arguments.
  • Same author, the Real Presence and the sacrifice. Justin is also a primary witness that the Eucharist is the true "flesh and blood" of Christ (First Apology 66) and that it is the Malachi "pure offering" (Dialogue 117); both strands are quoted above and feed the Real Presence page and the Malachi-sacrifice argument respectively.
