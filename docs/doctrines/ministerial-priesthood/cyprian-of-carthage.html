Cyprian of Carthage on the Ministerial Priesthood

Dates: c. 253 AD (the letter to Caecilius, Epistle 62; On the Unity of the Church c. 251) · Cyprian, c. 200 – 258; bishop of Carthage from c. 248 and martyr under Valerian, the leading Latin churchman of the mid-third century · Wikipedia
Summary: Cyprian gives the most explicit ante-Nicene statement that the one who offers the Eucharist acts in the place of Christ: "that priest truly discharges the office of Christ, who imitates that which Christ did; and he then offers a true and full sacrifice in the Church to God the Father." The offering is bound to the priest who reproduces what Christ did at the Supper, and the rite is not to be "changed by human tradition into anything else than what was divinely appointed." In On the Unity of the Church he draws the corollary that "the flesh of Christ… cannot be sent abroad," for "there is… no other home to believers but the one Church" — one Eucharist within the one episcopal communion. Cyprian is thus the bridge from Ignatius's bishop-bound Eucharist to the developed Latin doctrine of the priest who offers in persona Christi.

Note: this page treats Cyprian's witness to the ministerial shape of the offering; his testimony that the Eucharist is a true sacrifice is quoted here because the two are inseparable in the same passage, and it also feeds Cyprian's Real Presence page (same Epistle 62) and the Malachi 1:11 "pure offering" argument in the summary's Arguments.

Claims & evidence

"That priest truly discharges the office of Christ" (Epistle 62, to Caecilius)

Writing to correct those who used water alone, without wine, in the cup, Cyprian grounds the rite's unchangeable form in the priest's reproduction of what Christ himself did and offered:

For if Jesus Christ, our Lord and God, is Himself the chief priest of God the Father, and has first offered Himself a sacrifice to the Father, and has commanded this to be done in commemoration of Himself, certainly that priest truly discharges the office of Christ, who imitates that which Christ did; and he then offers a true and full sacrifice in the Church to God the Father, when he proceeds to offer it according to what he sees Christ Himself to have offered.

— Epistle 62 (to Caecilius, "On the Sacrament of the Cup"), § 14, source (Roberts–Donaldson translation, Ante-Nicene Fathers; cf. New Advent; some editions number this letter 63).1 This is the most direct ante-Nicene articulation of the principle later Latin theology calls acting in persona Christi: the priest "discharges the office of Christ" precisely by "imitating that which Christ did," and the validity of his offering hangs on his fidelity to Christ's own act. Where Ignatius had tied the valid Eucharist to the bishop's authority, Cyprian explains why the celebrant matters — he stands in the place of the one high priest and represents his sacrifice.

"Not… changed by human tradition into anything else than what was divinely appointed"

In the same letter Cyprian frames the offering as a divine institution no human custom may alter, appealing to Christ's warning against breaking "the least of the Lord's commandments":

But if we may not break even the least of the Lord's commandments, how much rather is it forbidden to infringe such important ones, so great, so pertaining to the very sacrament of our Lord's passion and our own redemption, or to change it by human tradition into anything else than what was divinely appointed!

— Epistle 62, § 14, source (cf. New Advent).2 The argument is structural to the doctrine: the Eucharist has a form "divinely appointed" by Christ, which the priest is bound to reproduce, not to recompose at will. The same logic that forbids substituting water for wine secures the wider claim that the offering belongs to the one who keeps Christ's institution — the ordained celebrant — rather than to private innovation.

One Eucharist within the one Church (On the Unity of the Church 8)

In On the Unity of the Church, expounding the Passover lamb that "shall not be sent abroad from the house," Cyprian draws the ecclesial corollary: the Lord's body is confined to the one Church, outside which there is no valid offering.

The flesh of Christ, and the holy of the Lord, cannot be sent abroad, nor is there any other home to believers but the one Church.

— On the Unity of the Church (Treatise 1), § 8, source (Roberts–Donaldson translation, Ante-Nicene Fathers; cf. New Advent).3 For Cyprian the Eucharist and the Church's unity are one question: because the "holy of the Lord" cannot be carried outside the "one house," there is no true Eucharist apart from the bishop's communion. This is the eucharistic side of his famous rule that "he can no longer have God for his Father who has not the Church for his mother" — the offering belongs not to the schismatic assembly but to the one ordained ministry within the one Church.

Notes

  • What it establishes. Cyprian supplies the explicit rationale that Ignatius and Justin had left implicit: the celebrant offers because he "discharges the office of Christ," imitating the act of the one high priest. By the mid-third century the Latin church speaks of the eucharistic minister in frankly sacerdotal and representative terms — sacerdos who offers sacrificium in Christ's place — and ties the validity of that offering to fidelity to Christ's institution and to the unity of the Church.
  • Sacrifice and ministry together. Cyprian routinely calls the bishop/presbyter sacerdos ("priest") and the Eucharist a sacrificium offered to God the Father; the two convictions are a single thought in Epistle 62. His witness therefore stands behind both the ministerial-priesthood claim and the eucharistic-sacrifice reading of Malachi adduced from the Didache and Justin onward (weighed in the summary's Arguments).
  • The cup controversy. The immediate occasion of Epistle 62 was the "Aquarians," who offered water alone. Cyprian's insistence that the mixed cup is "divinely appointed" is the premise from which the broader sacerdotal statement follows — useful context for weighing how far his language is a deliberate doctrine of priesthood and how far an argument from Christ's example pressed for a particular liturgical point.
