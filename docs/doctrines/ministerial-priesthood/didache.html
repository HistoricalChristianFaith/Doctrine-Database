The Didache on the Eucharist as the "Pure Sacrifice"

Dates: c. 100 AD (The Teaching of the Twelve Apostles; compiled c. AD 100, range c. 50–150; anonymous, no single author) · Wikipedia
Summary: The Didache — the earliest surviving church-order manual — gives the first post-apostolic text to call the Sunday Eucharist a sacrifice and to read it as the fulfilment of Malachi's prophecy of a "pure offering" made "in every place" among the nations. Gathering on "every Lord's day" to "break bread and give thanksgiving," the assembly is told to confess its transgressions and be reconciled first, "that your sacrifice may be pure… For this is that which was spoken by the Lord: In every place and time offer to me a pure sacrifice." It is the seed of the whole later argument that the Eucharist is an offering — though the Didache itself does not yet say who alone may offer it, the very next chapter directs the church to "appoint… bishops and deacons" who "render you the service of the prophets and teachers."

Claims & evidence

The Sunday Eucharist is a "sacrifice" that fulfils Malachi's prophesied pure offering (Didache 14)

But every Lord's day do ye gather yourselves together, and break bread, and give thanksgiving after having confessed your transgressions, that your sacrifice may be pure. But let no one that is at variance with his fellow come together with you, until they be reconciled, that your sacrifice may not be profaned. For this is that which was spoken by the Lord: In every place and time offer to me a pure sacrifice; for I am a great King, saith the Lord, and my name is wonderful among the nations.

— Didache (The Teaching of the Twelve Apostles), ch. 14, source (Roberts–Donaldson translation, Ante-Nicene Fathers; cf. New Advent).1 Three things make this the headwater of the eucharistic-sacrifice tradition. First, the Eucharist is plainly called a sacrifice (Greek thysia) — named so three times in as many sentences. Second, that sacrifice must be kept "pure," which is why confession of sin and reconciliation with one's brother are required before it is offered, exactly as the prophet demanded a pure offering. Third, the warrant cited is Malachi 1:11, "In every place… offer to me a pure sacrifice" — the very text the later tradition (Justin, Irenaeus, and onward) makes the standing proof that the Eucharist is the New-Covenant offering foreseen among the Gentiles.2

The church is to appoint bishops and deacons for its ministry (Didache 15)

Appoint, therefore, for yourselves, bishops and deacons worthy of the Lord, men meek, and not lovers of money, and truthful and proved; for they also render to you the service of prophets and teachers. Despise them not therefore, for they are your honoured ones, together with the prophets and teachers.

— Didache, ch. 15, source (cf. New Advent).3 The instruction follows immediately on the sacrifice passage, and is read by the tradition as joining the two strands the doctrine later weaves together: the assembly's offering (ch. 14) and the appointed ministers who preside over it (ch. 15). The Didache does not yet spell out that the offering belongs to the bishops alone — but it sets the appointed ministry beside the offering in the same breath, the configuration that Clement of Rome and Ignatius will state explicitly within a generation.

Notes

  • Earliest witness on this timeline. Whatever its precise date (estimates run c. 50–150; many settle near c. 100), the Didache stands at or before the head of the post-apostolic record, alongside Clement of Rome (c. 96). It supplies the doctrine's oldest datapoint for the sacrifice plank — that the Eucharist is a true offering, not merely a fellowship meal — which the later witnesses build the ministerial restriction upon.
  • What it does and does not establish. The Didache firmly attests the Eucharist-as-sacrifice and the Malachi reading; it does not yet restrict the offering to ordained ministers. That further step — that only a bishop or presbyter may offer — is first made explicit in Ignatius ("Let that be deemed a proper Eucharist, which is [administered] either by the bishop, or by one to whom he has entrusted it," Smyrnaeans 8). The honest reading of the Didache is the modest one: it is the premise (the Eucharist is an offering), seated next to the appointed ministry, not yet the full conclusion.
  • The "spiritual sacrifice" caveat — weigh it in. The Ante-Nicene Fathers editor notes that the Didache's "sacrifice" carries the same spiritual sense found in Romans 12:1, Philippians 2:17, Hebrews 13:15, and 1 Peter 2:5 — the offering of praise, thanksgiving, and a reconciled heart.4 The text's own logic supports this: what keeps the sacrifice "pure" is confession and reconciliation, not a theory of the elements. So the Didache shows the language of eucharistic sacrifice present from the start, but does not by itself decide between a "sacrifice of praise" reading and the fuller propitiatory sense the medieval tradition gives it. Whether Malachi 1:11 carries that fuller weight is weighed on its own argument page (forthcoming).
