Clement of Rome on the Ministerial Priesthood

Dates: c. 96 AD (1 Clement, written c. 95–96; Clement floruit as bishop of Rome, d. c. 99) · Wikipedia
Summary: Writing to quell a revolt at Corinth that had deposed its presbyters, Clement argues that the church's ministry is a divinely ordered office, not an open function. God himself, he says, fixed "where and by whom" the offerings are to be presented and at what times; just as the old covenant assigned distinct ministrations to high priest, priests, and Levites — while "the layman is bound by the laws that pertain to laymen" — so each Christian must keep "the rule of the ministry prescribed to him." The apostles, in turn, appointed bishops and deacons and arranged that "when these should fall asleep, other approved men should succeed them in their ministry." It is the earliest explicit witness that the offering belongs to an appointed, succeeding order — the seed of both the ministerial priesthood and apostolic succession.

Claims & evidence

God fixed "where and by whom" the offerings are presented; the layman keeps the layman's place (1 Clement 40)

These things therefore being manifest to us… it behoves us to do all things in [their proper] order, which the Lord has commanded us to perform at stated times. He has enjoined offerings [to be presented] and service to be performed [to Him], and that not thoughtlessly or irregularly, but at the appointed times and hours. Where and by whom He desires these things to be done, He Himself has fixed by His own supreme will… For his own peculiar services are assigned to the high priest, and their own proper place is prescribed to the priests, and their own special ministrations devolve on the Levites. The layman is bound by the laws that pertain to laymen.

— First Epistle to the Corinthians, ch. 40, source (Roberts–Donaldson translation, Ante-Nicene Fathers; cf. New Advent).1 Clement's premise is that the presenting of "offerings" (Greek prosphorai) is not left to every worshipper's discretion: God has determined both the persons and the occasions. He reads the Levitical order — high priest, priests, Levites, and laymen, each in a distinct station — as the model of how God orders worship, and the "layman" (laikos, the word's first surviving Christian use) is by name not in the offering station. The Eucharist is not explicitly named here, but the argument is being applied to the deposed Corinthian ministers, so the offering in view is the church's.

Each must keep "the rule of the ministry prescribed to him" (1 Clement 41)

Let every one of you, brethren, give thanks to God in his own order, living in all good conscience, with becoming gravity, and not going beyond the rule of the ministry prescribed to him. Not in every place, brethren, are the daily sacrifices offered, or the peace-offerings, or the sin-offerings and the trespass-offerings, but in Jerusalem only. And even there they are not offered in any place, but only at the altar before the temple, that which is offered being first carefully examined by the high priest and the ministers already mentioned.

— First Epistle to the Corinthians, ch. 41, source.2 The phrase "in his own order" (en tō idiō tagmati) governs the whole appeal: worship has assigned stations, and to overstep one's is to "go beyond the rule of the ministry prescribed." The Jerusalem-only restriction of sacrifice is offered as the principle's proof — that God limits not only the act of offering but its place and its ministers.

The apostles appointed bishops and deacons as a deliberate order of ministers (1 Clement 42)

Christ therefore was sent forth by God, and the apostles by Christ. Both these appointments, then, were made in an orderly way, according to the will of God… And thus preaching through countries and cities, they appointed the first-fruits [of their labours], having first proved them by the Spirit, to be bishops and deacons of those who should afterwards believe.

— First Epistle to the Corinthians, ch. 42, source.3 Clement traces an unbroken line of authorization — God → Christ → apostles → bishops and deacons — so that the ministry is not self-appointed but derives from the divine sending. This is the chain the later doctrine builds on: those who preside over the offering hold a transmitted commission, not a merely congregational license.

The apostles arranged for ministers to succeed one another in office (1 Clement 44)

Our apostles also knew, through our Lord Jesus Christ, that there would be strife on account of the office of the episcopate. For this reason, therefore, inasmuch as they had obtained a perfect fore-knowledge of this, they appointed those [ministers] already mentioned, and afterwards gave instructions, that when these should fall asleep, other approved men should succeed them in their ministry. We are of opinion, therefore, that those appointed by them, or afterwards by other eminent men, with the consent of the whole Church… cannot be justly dismissed from the ministry. For our sin will not be small, if we eject from the episcopate those who have blamelessly and holily fulfilled its duties.

— First Epistle to the Corinthians, ch. 44, source.4 This is the load-bearing sentence for apostolic succession: the apostles, foreseeing conflict, both appointed ministers and "gave instructions" for an ongoing succession — "when these should fall asleep, other approved men should succeed them." The deposed Corinthian presbyters therefore cannot be lawfully removed, because they stand in that succession; to eject them is sin. The ministry is thereby shown to be an enduring office, charged (per chs. 40–41) with the offerings, and continued by orderly succession rather than re-created by each congregation.

Notes

  • What it establishes — and what it doesn't. Clement firmly attests an ordered, appointed, succeeding ministry charged with presenting the church's offerings, with the layman expressly outside the offering station. He does not spell out a sacramental theology of the Eucharist, nor use the word "priest" (hiereus) of the Christian minister — the Levitical priesthood is his analogy, not his title for the bishop. The honest reading is that 1 Clement supplies the structural plank (a divinely ordered ministry that alone presents the offering, transmitted by succession), which the later witnesses — Ignatius, Justin, Cyprian — fill out with the eucharistic and sacerdotal content.
  • Bishop and presbyter used interchangeably. Clement appoints "bishops and deacons" (ch. 42) yet speaks of the deposed officers as "presbyters" and of ejecting men "from the episcopate" (ch. 44) without distinguishing the two — the same fluid usage found in the New Testament (Acts 20:17, 28; Titus 1:5–7). So 1 Clement attests a two-order ministry (bishops/presbyters and deacons), not yet the later three-order, monarchical episcopate; the succession he describes is of the presbyteral-episcopal college, not of a single monarch-bishop per city.
  • Anchor for the succession argument. Because ch. 44 grounds the ministry in an apostolically arranged succession, this page is the earliest witness adduced for the apostolic-succession reading weighed in the Arguments section of the summary.
  • Same author, other doctrines. Clement also figures as an early witness on the Old Testament canon (his use of Wisdom and Judith).
