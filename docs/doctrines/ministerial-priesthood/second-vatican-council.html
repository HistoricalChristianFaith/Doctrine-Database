The Second Vatican Council on the Ministerial Priesthood

Dates: 21 November 1964 (the dogmatic constitution Lumen Gentium, on the Church, promulgated by Pope Paul VI at the twenty-first ecumenical council) · Wikipedia
Summary: Lumen Gentium 10 gives the doctrine its settled modern shape and the phrase that most precisely states it. The council affirms in full the common priesthood of all the baptized — "a holy priesthood" who "offer spiritual sacrifices" — and then distinguishes it from the ordained ministry not by degree but by kind: "Though they differ from one another in essence and not only in degree (essentia et non gradu tantum), the common priesthood of the faithful and the ministerial or hierarchical priesthood are nonetheless interrelated." The ministerial priest "acting in the person of Christ… makes present the Eucharistic sacrifice, and offers it to God in the name of all the people." This is the text the framing argument on this timeline — ministerial priesthood versus the priesthood of all believers — most directly engages.

Claims & evidence

The common priesthood of all the baptized, affirmed in full (Lumen Gentium 10)

Before distinguishing the ordained ministry, the council states the priesthood of all believers as strongly as any father had:

Christ the Lord, High Priest taken from among men, made the new people "a kingdom and priests to God the Father." The baptized, by regeneration and the anointing of the Holy Spirit, are consecrated as a spiritual house and a holy priesthood, in order that through all those works which are those of the Christian man they may offer spiritual sacrifices and proclaim the power of Him who has called them out of darkness into His marvelous light.

— Lumen Gentium (1964), § 10, source.1 This is the corporate priesthood that Justin ("the true high priestly race of God"), Irenaeus ("all the righteous… are priests"), and above all Leo the Great had affirmed — here restated as the ground on which the ministerial priesthood is then distinguished, not displaced.

The two priesthoods differ "in essence and not only in degree" (Lumen Gentium 10)

The council then draws the distinction in the words that give the doctrine its most precise modern statement:

Though they differ from one another in essence and not only in degree, the common priesthood of the faithful and the ministerial or hierarchical priesthood are nonetheless interrelated: each of them in its own special way is a participation in the one priesthood of Christ. The ministerial priest, by the sacred power he enjoys, teaches and rules the priestly people; acting in the person of Christ, he makes present the Eucharistic sacrifice, and offers it to God in the name of all the people. But the faithful, in virtue of their royal priesthood, join in the offering of the Eucharist.

— Lumen Gentium (1964), § 10, source.2 The phrase essentia et non gradu tantum answers the framing dispute exactly: the ministerial priesthood is not merely a higher rung of the one priesthood of all believers but differs from it in kind, the ordained alone "acting in the person of Christ" to make the sacrifice present — Cyprian's and Pius XII's in persona Christi carried into the council's own definition.

Notes

  • The modern terminus. Lumen Gentium 10 is where the doctrine reaches its settled contemporary form. It holds together, in a single article, the two things the whole timeline has been tracing: the common priesthood of the baptized affirmed without reserve, and the ministerial priesthood marked off as differing "in essence" — the ordained alone offering the Eucharist in persona Christi in the name of all.
  • Directly on the framing argument. Because it states both priesthoods so precisely, LG 10 is the text the argument page Does the New Testament institute a distinct ministerial priesthood, or only the priesthood of all believers? engages most directly: it is the fullest magisterial claim that the New Testament institutes a distinct sacrificing priesthood alongside — not instead of — the priesthood of all believers.
  • Continuity with Trent and Pius XII. The council does not withdraw Trent's definition of a visible, sacrificing priesthood; it restates it in terms of participation in Christ's one priesthood, and gives Pius XII's distinction of the ministerial from the common priesthood its conciliar formula.
  • Provenance. Lumen Gentium is quoted from the official English translation published by the Vatican; the Latin essentia et non gradu tantum is from the promulgated Latin text.
