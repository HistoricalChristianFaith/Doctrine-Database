John Chrysostom on the Ministerial Priesthood

Dates: c. 392 · Wikipedia
Summary: Chrysostom's Treatise on the Priesthood (De Sacerdotio, c. 386–392) is the classic patristic exaltation of the ordained priest's office. Book 3 argues that the priesthood is instituted not by any created power "but the Paraclete Himself"; that the priest at the altar "makes prolonged supplication, not that some flame … may consume the offerings" but that the Holy Spirit descend on the sacrifice; and that priests have "received an authority which God has not given to angels or archangels" — to confect the Eucharist, to bind and loose, and to forgive sins, so that "what priests do here below God ratifies above." Where his eucharistic realism grounds the Real Presence, here the weight falls on who may offer: salvation itself, he says, is "accomplished only by means of those holy hands, I mean the hands of the priest."

Claims & evidence

The priesthood is instituted by the Holy Spirit and ranks among heavenly ordinances (On the Priesthood 3.4)

For the priestly office is indeed discharged on earth, but it ranks amongst heavenly ordinances; and very naturally so: for neither man, nor angel, nor archangel, nor any other created power, but the Paraclete Himself, instituted this vocation, and persuaded men while still abiding in the flesh to represent the ministry of angels. Wherefore the consecrated priest ought to be as pure as if he were standing in the heavens themselves in the midst of those powers.

— Treatise on the Priesthood (De Sacerdotio) 3.4, New Advent (NPNF Series I, vol. 9; also at this edition).1 The office is not a human arrangement but a divine institution, which is why for Chrysostom it is no more possible to despise it out of humility than to despise the rank of the angels.

At the altar the priest calls down not fire but the Holy Spirit (On the Priesthood 3.4)

Picture Elijah and the vast multitude standing around him, and the sacrifice laid upon the altar of stones … then the sudden rush of fire from Heaven upon the sacrifice … Now then pass from this scene to the rites which are celebrated in the present day … There stands the priest, not bringing down fire from Heaven, but the Holy Spirit: and he makes prolonged supplication, not that some flame sent down from on high may consume the offerings, but that grace descending on the sacrifice may thereby enlighten the souls of all, and render them more refulgent than silver purified by fire.

— On the Priesthood 3.4, New Advent.2 This is the sharpest patristic statement of the priest's epicletic role: the eucharistic sacrifice is effected through the ordained minister's invocation of the Spirit upon the gifts — the same conviction attested in the Liturgy that bears Chrysostom's name. It supplies the mechanism behind the reservation: the offering is made by the one whose prayer draws down the Spirit on the victim.

Priests have an authority "not given to angels or archangels" — to bind, loose, and confect (On the Priesthood 3.5)

For they who inhabit the earth and make their abode there are entrusted with the administration of things which are in Heaven, and have received an authority which God has not given to angels or archangels. For it has not been said to them, "Whatsoever ye shall bind on earth shall be bound in Heaven, and whatsoever ye shall loose on earth shall be loosed in Heaven." … and what priests do here below God ratifies above, and the Master confirms the sentence of his servants.

Chrysostom then makes salvation itself depend on the priestly hands:

For if no one can enter into the kingdom of Heaven except he be regenerate through water and the Spirit, and he who does not eat the flesh of the Lord and drink His blood is excluded from eternal life, and if all these things are accomplished only by means of those holy hands, I mean the hands of the priest, how will any one, without these, be able to escape the fire of hell, or to win those crowns which are reserved for the victorious?

— On the Priesthood 3.5, New Advent.3 Here the reservation of the doctrine is stated at its highest pitch: baptismal regeneration and the eucharistic gift of Christ's flesh and blood — the means of salvation — are dispensed "only by means of" the priest's hands. The office is grounded not on church order alone but on the delegated, Christ-derived power to bind and loose (Matthew 18:18; John 20:23).

The priest actually takes away sin, and begets to God (On the Priesthood 3.6)

The Jewish priests had authority to release the body from leprosy, or, rather, not to release it but only to examine those who were already released … But our priests have received authority to deal, not with bodily leprosy, but spiritual uncleanness—not to pronounce it removed after examination, but actually and absolutely to take it away. … For not only at the time of regeneration, but afterwards also, they have authority to forgive sins.

— On the Priesthood 3.6, New Advent.4 Chrysostom explicitly transfers the language of the Levitical priesthood — but with an escalation: the Christian priest does not merely declare a cleansing (as the Aaronic priest examined the healed leper) but effects it, "actually and absolutely." Where Clement of Rome had used the Levitical orders only as an analogy and never named the minister a priest, Chrysostom freely calls the Christian minister hiereus ("priest") and grounds his dignity on the powers to confect, to forgive, and to regenerate — the developed Greek form of the sacerdotal conviction.

Notes

  • Placement on the timeline. Chrysostom wrote the Treatise early, probably in the later 380s or early 390s, before his ordination to the priesthood at Antioch and long before he became bishop of Constantinople (398). It is dated here c. 392, consistent with the dating on his Real Presence page, and seats him between Nicaea (325) and Aquinas (c. 1274) — the mature patristic articulation of the office whose validity Ignatius had tied to the bishop and whose in-persona-Christi rationale Cyprian had supplied.
  • Two sides of one treatise. The same Book 3 is a principal witness on the Real Presence — "when thou seest the Lord sacrificed, and laid upon the altar." There the emphasis falls on what is offered; here on who may offer it. For Chrysostom the two are inseparable: it is precisely because the enthroned Lord is "held in the hands" at the altar that the hands must be the priest's.
  • Bearing on the framing dispute. Chrysostom is the strongest patristic voice for the reading that the New Covenant has a distinct, awesome priestly office — he uses the sacerdotal title without hesitation and hangs both Eucharist and absolution on the priest's hands. That very freedom illustrates the historical point weighed on the argument page: by the late fourth century the term "priest" (hiereus/sacerdos) is fully naturalized for the Christian minister, a development downstream of reading the Eucharist as sacrifice. See Does the New Testament institute a distinct ministerial priesthood, or only the priesthood of all believers? (contested).
