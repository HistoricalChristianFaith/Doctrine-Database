Was Ignatius the child Jesus took in His arms? (Matthew 18:2–4)

Type: historical argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — a late fable: no writer for the better part of eight centuries knows it, Ignatius never claims it, and it is best explained as a medieval re-reading of his epithet Theophoros ("God-bearer") in the passive sense ("God-carried") — not because a boy meeting Jesus is impossible, but because the tradition fails every historical test of attestation and dating · advanced by the medieval Greek hagiographers (Anastasius the Librarian, Symeon Metaphrastes) and later endorsed by Francis de Sales; held fabulous by Jean Daillé and John Pearson.

The claim

Many say that this child was St. Ignatius the martyr, whose feast we celebrated yesterday and whose Office is transferred to tomorrow. Oh, how blessed was this glorious St. Ignatius, since he was taken up into Our Lord's arms and given as an example to the Apostles! How precious and sweet was that kiss!

— Francis de Sales, sermon on The Purification of the Blessed Virgin Mary (1622), source.1 The tradition being asserted is a literal identification: that the anonymous little child whom Jesus "set in the midst" of the quarrelling disciples and "took… in his arms" as the model of humility — Matthew 18:2–4; Mark 9:36; Luke 9:47–48 — grew up to be Ignatius of Antioch, and that this is why he was surnamed Theophoros, "God-bearer."2 De Sales himself records that the identification was not settled even in devotional use — "many say that this child was St. Martial, who later became Bishop of Limoges" — before giving Ignatius as "the more common opinion."

Why it bears on this doctrine

The legend is not idle hagiography: in its earliest form it does polemical work for the standing of the bishop's office. Anastasius reports the Greeks saying that, because Christ had touched and sanctified him, "not even the apostles dared to lay their hands on him again" when he was made bishop — i.e. the child sanctified by Christ's own embrace needed no ordinary imposition of hands. That is a claim about the origin and dignity of the episcopate, resting on the same Ignatius whose letters are this doctrine's earliest witness that a Eucharist is valid only under the bishop. So the legend's historical value bears directly on how the episcopal office was exalted — and, more broadly, on how much weight the late Ignatian hagiography can carry wherever Ignatius is cited (here, and on the Real Presence and intermediate state timelines).

The evidence for

The name is a genuine hook. Ignatius really did bear the epithet Theophoros, and the Greek is ambiguous: accented on the penult (Θεοφόρος) it is active, "he who bears God"; read as Θεόφορος it is passive, "he who is borne by God." The passive sense — "carried by God" — invites the picture of a child literally carried in the Lord's arms, and it is from that reading that the identification grows.3 The tradition then has real, datable attestation. Its earliest known witness is Anastasius the Librarian (c. 878), who in a gloss on his Latin translation of the Acts of the Eighth Council reports that "the Greeks relate" the child of Matthew 18 to have been this Ignatius, "and therefore they call him God-bearer."4 A century later Symeon Metaphrastes (c. 987) built it into his widely-copied Life of Ignatius, and from there it passed into the Byzantine liturgical books — the Greek Menaion and the office for his feast (Dec. 20) — where the story that the Lord "lifted him up while still a toddler" and embraced him is sung to this day; Francis de Sales repeats it in 1622 as "the more common opinion."5 A tradition attested from the ninth century, embedded in the liturgy, and endorsed by a Doctor of the Church is not nothing; and nothing in it requires the impossible — a boy in first-century Syria could in principle have met Jesus.

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The bare possibility stands: the wiki does not rule a claim out merely because it involves a first-hand encounter with Christ, and the identification is internally coherent. The name Theophoros is authentic, its passive reading is grammatically available, and the tradition is genuinely old enough (ninth century) and widespread enough (hagiography and liturgy alike) to be a real historical datum rather than a modern invention.

What does not hold. Every historical test the identification must pass, it fails. (1) Silence. Not one writer before Anastasius — not Ignatius's own disciple Polycarp, not Irenaeus, not Origen, not Eusebius, not Jerome, none of the ancient Acts of his martyrdom — shows the faintest awareness of it. A boy singled out and embraced by Christ, then grown to be a great bishop and martyr, is precisely the sort of thing the early church would have treasured and repeated; its total absence for the better part of eight centuries is decisive against antiquity. The Catholic Encyclopedia grants as much: the earliest writers who record it do so "apparently without good reason."6 (2) A positive counter-tradition, from Antioch itself. The silence is not even neutral: the earliest datable memory of Ignatius runs positively the other way. John Chrysostom, preaching his Eulogy on Saint Ignatius at Antioch — Ignatius's own see — around 386–397, some five centuries before Anastasius, says outright that Ignatius "had not even seen" Christ in the flesh, "nor enjoyed his companionship," and that he rose to the episcopate not by any touch of the Lord but because "the hands of the blessed apostles touched his sacred head." A boy whom Jesus took up in His arms is precisely one who had seen Him; Chrysostom, at the very church that would have treasured such a memory, flatly denies it and grounds Ignatius's dignity in apostolic ordination instead.7 Theodoret (c. 447) and the later witnesses carry the same ordination tradition — Ignatius "received episcopal grace by the hand of the great Peter" — whose whole point (the office handed on by apostolic hands) is the exact reverse of the legend's own polemical payoff, that "not even the apostles dared to lay their hands on him again" because Christ's embrace had already consecrated the child. So the counter-tradition is not merely earlier than the legend; it contradicts it at just the point the legend was built to make. (3) Self-silence. Ignatius, who in his letters calls himself Theophoros and dwells freely on his own unworthiness and hope of martyrdom, never once claims to have been that child — an astonishing omission if it were true and known to him. (4) The name points the other way. John Pearson's Vindiciae Epistolarum S. Ignatii (1672) showed that the ancients uniformly accented the word as active, Theophóros, "God-bearer" — Ignatius's own self-description of one who carries Christ in his soul — and that the passive accentuation that suits the fable is the later shift, not the original. The active sense is exactly how Ignatius uses it of himself, and it needs no legend to explain it.8 (5) A better explanation of the story's rise. Pearson further proposed that the fable crystallized around the ninth century by confusion with a contemporary namesake — Ignatius, Patriarch of Constantinople (d. 877), nicknamed the "new God-bearer" — which fits its sudden appearance in precisely Anastasius's generation. Whether or not that particular mechanism is right, it accounts for the timing the antiquity-hypothesis cannot.

Keeping two things apart. The chief refuters overreach in one direction and must be read with care. Jean Daillé (1670) rightly called the legend "clearly fabulous," but he leaned on that lateness to argue that the Ignatian letters themselves are pseudonymous — a non sequitur. The fable's ninth-century origin says nothing against the seven middle-recension letters, whose authenticity Pearson defended in the same breath and which have been held genuine since Zahn and Lightfoot. A late legend can attach to a real and early figure; the datum to carry forward is that the child tradition is late, not that Ignatius is.9

Assessment

Weak. The identification of Ignatius with the child of Matthew 18 does not hold on its best available case. It is not weak because it asks us to believe a boy met Jesus — that is possible, and possibility is not the issue — but because it fails on the ordinary historical grounds: an unbroken silence of every source for some eight hundred years — and, from Ignatius's own see, a positive early counter-tradition (Chrysostom, Theodoret) that he never saw Christ and had his office from the apostles' hands — Ignatius's own failure to claim it, and a name whose original (active) sense already accounts for itself and points away from the passive reading the fable needs. The best explanation is the one Daillé and Pearson gave: a late Byzantine legend, grown from a re-accented epithet and perhaps a confusion with the ninth-century patriarch of the same name, then fixed in liturgy and passed on in good faith by later writers such as Francis de Sales. The one caution is to keep the fable's lateness from being pressed, as Daillé pressed it, into a case against the genuineness of Ignatius's letters — that is a separate and far stronger question, and the letters stand.
