Did Christ delegate his own authority to the apostles, to be handed on to ordained successors? (John 20:21–23; Luke 10:16)

Type: interpretive argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — that Christ conferred his own authority on the apostles is the plain sense of the "sending" texts, and that the church should hand its ministry on in orderly succession is attested within living memory of the apostles (Clement of Rome, c. 96); but the further claim the doctrine needs — that this authority descends only through a line of episcopal ordination, carrying a sacramental power to confect the Eucharist — is an inference the texts underdetermine, that the Reformers read instead as the gospel-ministry given to the whole church, and that even Catholic theology concedes is a selective succession of one part of an office whose foundational part cannot be transmitted · the transmissible-authority reading advanced by Clement of Rome, Irenaeus, and the Catholic magisterium; the gospel-ministry reading advanced by Calvin and the Reformed tradition, with the historical development of monepiscopacy described by J. B. Lightfoot.

Related arguments: Christ's commission to the apostles — see also Was "Do this in remembrance of me" a commission to the apostles alone?

The interpretation to weigh

The "why only priests" case needs a mechanism by which the eucharistic ministry is restricted, and the institution-narrative argument ("Do this in remembrance of me") supplies only half of it: that Christ first charged the apostles. This argument supplies the other half — that Christ delegated to the apostles his own divine authority, and that the authority so delegated is transmissible, handed on to the bishops and presbyters who succeed them. Its proof-texts are the risen Lord's "As the Father hath sent me, even so send I you … Receive ye the Holy Ghost: Whose soever sins ye remit, they are remitted unto them" (John 20:21–23), and the charge to the seventy, "He that heareth you heareth me; and he that despiseth you despiseth him that sent me" (Luke 10:16; cf. Luke 10:1).1 On the restrictive reading these establish a chain — the Father sends the Son, the Son the apostles, the apostles their ordained successors — down which Christ's authority, including the power to forgive sins and to offer the Eucharist, passes only through ordination. The objection grants the delegation but denies the chain: the texts commission the apostles, but they neither name a succession nor make the authority a priestly power reserved to an ordained line; the authority is the gospel and its ministry, given to the whole church, and the specific descent through episcopal ordination is a later development read back into the words.

The case for a delegated, transmissible authority

The "sending" language is explicit and strong. John 20:21 sets the apostles' mission in direct parallel to Christ's own — "as the Father hath sent me, even so send I you" — and immediately joins to it the gift of the Spirit and the power to remit and retain sins (20:22–23). This is not a general exhortation but a commissioning of named men with a specific, Christ-derived authority; the Catholic reading takes "even so send I you" as the charter of an apostolate (Greek apostolē, a "sending") that carries real power to govern, to forgive, and to celebrate.2 Luke 10:16 sharpens the point of identity: to hear the sent ones is to hear Christ, and to reject them is to reject him and the Father — the authority of the sender resting on the sent.

Orderly succession appears within living memory of the apostles. The strongest support is not a proof-text but the speed and uniformity with which the church institutionalized succession. Clement of Rome (c. 96), writing while men who had known the apostles were still alive, already traces the ministry's authority through an unbroken chain — "God sent forth Christ … Christ therefore came from God, and the apostles from Christ" — and reports that the apostles, foreseeing "strife on account of the office of the episcopate," "appointed [bishops and deacons], and afterwards gave instructions, that when these should fall asleep, other approved men should succeed them in their ministry" (1 Clement 42, 44).3 Irenaeus, a century later, makes apostolic succession an explicit rule of faith: the truth is preserved where the apostles, "delivering up their own place of government to these men," are succeeded by an unbroken line of bishops — and he lists the Roman succession from Peter and Paul down to his own day as the test of authentic teaching (Against Heresies III.3).4 The magisterium gathers this into doctrine: the apostles "took care to appoint successors," consigning "to their immediate collaborators … the duty of completing and consolidating the work they had begun" and ruling "that likewise on their death other proven men should take over their ministry" (Catechism 861).5

The case for a gospel-ministry given to the whole church

The texts commission, but they do not specify succession. Neither John 20 nor Luke 10 mentions ordination, episcopal lines, or a transmission of office; they record a sending of the apostles, not a constitution for the church's future government. That the church came to hand on its ministry in orderly succession is historical fact (Clement); that John 20:21–23 establishes such a succession, and confines its powers to an ordained class, is a further inference the words do not carry on their face. The Reformed reading takes the authority conferred to be the ministry of the gospel itself. Calvin, on John 20:23, holds that "the only subject which is handled in this passage is the preaching of the Gospel … he makes no allusion in this passage to anything which is peculiar to himself, but only appoints ministers and pastors to govern the Church; and on this condition, that he alone keeps possession of the whole power, while they claim nothing for themselves but the ministry." The "keys," on this view, have no power "separate from the preaching of the gospel," and the forgiveness Christ authorizes is the announcement of forgiveness through the word, entrusted to the church, not a sacerdotal power held by a caste.6

The episcopal mechanism is a development, not a datum of the texts. The specific claim — that the authority descends through a line of bishops distinct from presbyters — is undercut by the earliest evidence itself. Clement uses "bishop" and "presbyter" interchangeably and attests a two-order ministry, not the monarchical episcopate the later doctrine presupposes. J. B. Lightfoot's classic study concluded that monepiscopacy "was not … imposed upon the churches from without" but "grew up … out of the presbyteral order by the elevation of one of its members," emerging only in the second century — so the channel through which the authority is said to flow is a post-apostolic arrangement, however early and providential, not something Christ instituted in John 20.7

Even the doctrine concedes the succession is selective. The Catholic position itself holds that the apostolic office has "one aspect that cannot be transmitted: to be the chosen witnesses of the Lord's Resurrection and so the foundation stones of the Church" — only its "permanent aspect" passes to successors (Catechism 860), and "the bishops are not simply a continuation of the office of apostle" but receive its governing charge while the apostolate as such ceases. That concession is telling: which powers transmit and which do not is a theological judgment about the church's permanence, not a distinction the proof-texts draw. Once it is granted that part of what John 20 confers does not descend, the claim that the eucharistic and penitential powers specifically do descend, and only through ordination, can no longer be read straight off the "sending" language; it rests on the prior doctrine of the ministry, not on the texts alone.8

Adversarial assessment

What holds. Two things are well-grounded. First, that Christ delegated his own authority to the apostles is the natural sense of John 20:21 and Luke 10:16 — the parallel "as … even so" and the identity "he that heareth you heareth me" make the apostles bearers of a Christ-derived commission, not self-appointed teachers. Second, the principle that this ministry should be handed on in orderly succession is not a late Catholic overlay but is attested by Clement of Rome within a generation of the apostles and made an explicit rule by Irenaeus — early, widespread, and plausibly continuous with apostolic practice. A reading on which the apostles held a real delegated authority and deliberately provided for its continuance is on solid ground.

What is overstated. The doctrine needs more than that, and the surplus is where the argument strains. (a) From commission to succession. John 20 and Luke 10 commission the apostles; they do not, on their face, institute a succession or restrict the conferred powers to an ordained line. The succession has to be supplied from elsewhere — from later practice and the church's self-understanding — and where a proponent makes the "sending" texts by themselves prove an episcopally-transmitted priesthood, the proof-texts are loaded beyond what they say. (b) From authority to sacerdotal power. That the authority is specifically a power to confect the Eucharist, reserved to the ordained, is read into "whose soever sins ye remit"; the Reformed alternative — that the authority is the gospel-ministry and its announced forgiveness, given to the whole church — is a live and textually economical reading, not a desperate evasion. (c) From succession to episcopal ordination. The channel the doctrine specifies, descent through bishops as an order above presbyters, is a second-century development (Lightfoot), and the earliest witness (Clement) does not yet know it. (d) The doctrine's own concession. Catholic theology grants that the foundational part of the apostolic office is non-transmissible; this concedes that "succession" is selective, which means the texts do not settle which powers carry on — that is decided by the doctrine of ministry the argument is meant to establish, so the reasoning is partly circular.

Two claims kept apart. (a) Did the apostles hold a delegated authority, and provide for its orderly continuance? — yes, well-attested. (b) Do John 20 / Luke 10 establish that a sacramental priesthood descends only through episcopal ordination? — that is the contested step, underdetermined by the words, met by a coherent rival reading, and resting on a succession-mechanism the earliest sources do not yet attest. As with the parallel "Do this in remembrance of me" argument, the restriction the doctrine needs is downstream of the prior conviction that the Eucharist is a sacrifice offered by a priestly order; grant that, and the transmitted-authority reading follows, deny it, and the texts read as the commissioning of a gospel-ministry for the whole church.

Assessment

Contested. The reading is strong by the lights of the tradition and weak by the lights of bare exegesis, and the two do not converge. As a claim that Christ delegated his own authority to the apostles, and that the church rightly handed its ministry on in orderly succession, it is well-grounded — the "sending" texts say the first, and Clement of Rome and Irenaeus attest the second within living memory of the apostles. As the load-bearing claim the doctrine makes of it — that John 20:21–23 and Luke 10:16 by themselves establish a sacramental authority descending only through episcopal ordination — it is an inference the texts do not compel: they commission the apostles without naming a succession or a priestly caste, the Reformed reading (the gospel-ministry and its announced forgiveness, given to the whole church) is coherent and not ruled out, the episcopal channel is a second-century development the earliest witness does not yet know, and even Catholic theology concedes the succession is selective — which leaves it to the prior doctrine of ministry, not the proof-texts, to decide which powers transmit. Hence contested: a genuine and early principle of succession-in-ministry that the texts are consistent with, but not the transmissible priesthood the doctrine reads off them.
