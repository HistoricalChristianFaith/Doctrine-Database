Ambrose of Milan on the Ministerial Priesthood

Dates: c. 390 AD (On the Mysteries and On the Sacraments, catechetical addresses to the newly baptized) · Ambrose, c. 339 – 397; bishop of Milan from 374, the towering Latin churchman of the West · Wikipedia
Summary: Ambrose sets the ordained ministry at the centre of the eucharistic act. Instructing the newly baptized, he points them to the threefold ministry they have just seen — "the deacon… the priest… the chief priest [i.e. the bishop]" — and grounds the consecration not in the minister's own words but in Christ's: "that sacrament which you receive is made what it is by the word of Christ," so that "when one comes… to celebrating the venerable sacrament, the priest no longer uses his own words, but he uses the words of Christ." The priest offers by speaking Christ's institution over the elements; that is why the confecting belongs to him.

Claims & evidence

The threefold ministry at the altar (On the Mysteries 2.6)

Reminding the newly baptized of what they beheld at the font and the altar, Ambrose names the three orders in ascending rank, bidding them look past the men to the grace of the mysteries:

You saw there the deacon, you saw the priest, you saw the chief priest [i.e. the bishop]. Consider not the bodily forms, but the grace of the Mysteries. You spoke in the presence of the angels, as it is written: "For the priest's lips keep knowledge, and they seek the law at his mouth, for he is the angel of the Lord Almighty."

— On the Mysteries, ch. 2, § 6, source (Nicene and Post-Nicene Fathers, ser. 2, vol. 10; cf. New Advent).1 Ambrose takes the graded ministry — deacon, priest, bishop — entirely for granted as the order the initiate has just witnessed, and reads the priest's very lips as the mouthpiece of divine knowledge (Malachi 2:7). The mysteries are administered through that ordained order; the newly baptized are catechized to see the offering as its proper act.

The consecration is by the word of Christ, spoken by the priest (On the Mysteries 9.52–54; On the Sacraments 4)

The heart of Ambrose's eucharistic teaching is that the elements are changed not by any natural process nor by the minister's own authority, but by the priest uttering Christ's own words of institution over them:

…what are we to say of that divine consecration where the very words of the Lord and Saviour operate? For that sacrament which you receive is made what it is by the word of Christ. … Before the blessing of the heavenly words another nature is spoken of, after the consecration the Body is signified.

— On the Mysteries, ch. 9, §§ 52, 54, source (NPNF ser. 2, vol. 10; cf. New Advent).2 In the parallel catechesis On the Sacraments he says it more pointedly still: at the moment of consecration "the priest no longer uses his own words, but he uses the words of Christ. Therefore, the word of Christ completes this sacrament." Here is the mechanism the whole doctrine presses toward: the priest confects the sacrament precisely by lending his voice to Christ's institution, "no longer… his own words." The offering is Christ's, made present through the ordained minister who speaks the dominical words — the Latin seed of the later teaching that the priest consecrates in persona Christi.

Notes

  • What it establishes. Ambrose supplies the developed Latin account of why the offering belongs to the ordained: consecration is effected by Christ's own words of institution, and it is the priest who speaks them over the elements. He takes the threefold ministry as the settled order of the Milanese church and centres the eucharistic change on the priest's utterance of the dominical word.
  • Bridge to Aquinas. Where Cyprian said the priest "discharges the office of Christ," Ambrose locates the act in the priest's speaking Christ's words — the very point Aquinas will systematize as consecration in persona Christi. His stress that "the word of Christ completes this sacrament" is one of the patristic texts the medieval doctrine of the form and minister of the Eucharist builds on.
  • Overlap with the Real Presence. Ambrose's change-of-nature language ("Before the blessing… another nature… after the consecration the Body") is quoted here only for the ministerial point — that the change comes by the priest's speaking Christ's words — but it is also a major witness for the eucharistic realism weighed on the Real Presence page.
