The Council of Nicaea on the Ministerial Priesthood

Dates: AD 325 (the council convened by Constantine; its disciplinary canons) · the first ecumenical council, c. 318 bishops gathered chiefly against Arianism, which also issued twenty canons of church order · Wikipedia
Summary: Canon 18 of Nicaea legislates against deacons in some districts who were giving the Eucharist to the presbyters and even touching it before the bishops, on the express ground that "neither canon nor custom permits that they who have no right to offer should give the Body of Christ to them that do offer." The canon assumes — as already ancient ("neither canon nor custom") — that the right to offer the Eucharist belongs to bishops and presbyters, never to deacons, who are "the ministers of the bishop and the inferiors of the presbyters." It is the earliest conciliar witness that the confecting and offering of the Eucharist is reserved to the higher orders, ordering the deacon back "within their own bounds."

Claims & evidence

"They who have no right to offer should not give the Body of Christ to them that do offer" (Canon 18)

The council corrects districts where deacons had overstepped their order — administering the Eucharist to presbyters, and handling it before the bishops — grounding the correction in a settled rule about who may offer:

It has come to the knowledge of the holy and great Synod that, in some districts and cities, the deacons administer the Eucharist to the presbyters, whereas neither canon nor custom permits that they who have no right to offer should give the Body of Christ to them that do offer. And this also has been made known, that certain deacons now touch the Eucharist even before the bishops. Let all such practices be utterly done away, and let the deacons remain within their own bounds, knowing that they are the ministers of the bishop and the inferiors of the presbyters. Let them receive the Eucharist according to their order, after the presbyters, and let either the bishop or the presbyter administer to them. Furthermore, let not the deacons sit among the presbyters, for that is contrary to canon and order. And if, after this decree, any one shall refuse to obey, let him be deposed from the diaconate.

— Canons of the Council of Nicaea, Canon 18, source (Nicene and Post-Nicene Fathers, ser. 2, vol. 14, ed. Schaff & Wace).1 The decisive phrase is the parenthetical rationale: a deacon may not "give the Body of Christ" to the presbyters because he is one of "they who have no right to offer," whereas the presbyters are "them that do offer." The canon thus distinguishes two functions — offering (reserved to bishop and presbyter) and distributing/ministering (the deacon's proper, subordinate role) — and treats the reservation of the first as already fixed "by canon" and "by custom," not as a new ruling of 325. The deacon is restored "within their own bounds": he receives after the presbyters, and the bishop or presbyter administers to him, never the reverse.

Notes

  • What it establishes. By AD 325 the reservation of the eucharistic offering to bishops and presbyters is treated by an ecumenical council as so settled that its violation is a disciplinary scandal correctable by deposition. The canon does not argue the point; it presupposes it ("neither canon nor custom permits"), which makes it a strong witness that the conviction Ignatius, Justin, and Cyprian express was, by the early fourth century, the universal order of the church and not a local or contested opinion.
  • Offering vs. distributing. Nicaea draws explicitly the distinction implicit in Justin Martyr, where the "president" offers and "those who are called by us deacons" distribute. Canon 18 makes the line a matter of law: the deacon distributes but does not offer, and so may not "give the Body of Christ" to those who do.
  • The "right to offer." The phrase "they who have no right to offer" (Greek tois mē exon prospherein) is the canon's own language for the ministerial priesthood: the offering of the Eucharist is a right attached to the higher orders by ordination, not a function any minister may assume. This is the earliest conciliar statement of that reservation in so many words.
  • A council as a witness. A council is treated here as a single timeline "witness" dated to its sitting (AD 325). Among the fathers seated so far it is the latest voice; the medieval magisterial witnesses (Innocent III, Aquinas) come later still.
