Irenaeus of Lyons on the Ministerial Priesthood

Dates: c. 189 AD (Against Heresies, esp. Books III–IV; the work is generally dated c. 180–189) · Irenaeus of Lyons, c. 130 – c. 202; bishop of Lugdunum (Lyons) in Gaul, a hearer of Polycarp who had himself known the apostle John · Wikipedia
Summary: Against the Gnostics, Irenaeus welds together the two planks the ministerial priesthood rests on. First, he names the Eucharist "the new oblation of the new covenant" — the very "pure sacrifice" Malachi foresaw — which "the Church receiving from the apostles, offers to God throughout all the world." Second, he makes apostolic succession the visible test of authentic ministry: the bishops "instituted… by the apostles" in an unbroken line are those to obey, and the presbyters "who… possess the succession from the apostles" hold "the certain gift of truth." He thus binds the eucharistic offering to a ministry defined by descent from the apostles. Like Tertullian and Justin after and before him, though, Irenaeus can also call "all the righteous" and "all the apostles of the Lord" priests — so he affirms the reserved offering and the corporate priesthood together, and does not yet expound a distinct sacrificing hiereus-class in so many words.

Claims & evidence

The Eucharist is "the new oblation of the new covenant," Malachi's pure sacrifice (Against Heresies IV.17.5)

Arguing that the same God gave both covenants, Irenaeus reads the Last-Supper institution as the Church's standing sacrifice, offered "throughout all the world" as the prophet Malachi foretold:

Again, giving directions to His disciples to offer to God the first-fruits of His own, created things—not as if He stood in need of them, but that they might be themselves neither unfruitful nor ungrateful—He took that created thing, bread, and gave thanks, and said, "This is My body." And the cup likewise, which is part of that creation to which we belong, He confessed to be His blood, and taught the new oblation of the new covenant; which the Church receiving from the apostles, offers to God throughout all the world, to Him who gives us as the means of subsistence the first-fruits of His own gifts in the New Testament, concerning which Malachi, among the twelve prophets, thus spoke beforehand: "I have no pleasure in you, saith the Lord Omnipotent, and I will not accept sacrifice at your hands. For from the rising of the sun, unto the going down [of the same], My name is glorified among the Gentiles, and in every place incense is offered to My name, and a pure sacrifice."

— Against Heresies IV.17.5, source (Roberts–Donaldson translation, Ante-Nicene Fathers; cf. New Advent).1 This is the earliest full statement of the eucharistic reading of Malachi 1:11 that the timeline later leans on: the Eucharist is a true oblation, not merely a memorial meal, and the Church holds it "receiving from the apostles" — so the offering is bound from the outset to the apostolic transmission. (In the next section, IV.17.6, Irenaeus also glosses the verse's "incense" as "the prayers of the saints" from Revelation 8:4 — a spiritualizing note weighed on the Malachi argument page.)

"The oblation of the Church… is accounted with God a pure sacrifice" (Against Heresies IV.18.1–2)

The following chapter draws out that the Church's offering is the genuine successor to the Old-Covenant sacrifices — the class of oblation continues, only its character has changed:

The oblation of the Church, therefore, which the Lord gave instructions to be offered throughout all the world, is accounted with God a pure sacrifice, and is acceptable to Him; not that He stands in need of a sacrifice from us, but that he who offers is himself glorified in what he does offer, if his gift be accepted.… And the class of oblations in general has not been set aside; for there were both oblations there [among the Jews], and there are oblations here [among the Christians]. Sacrifices there were among the people; sacrifices there are, too, in the Church: but the species alone has been changed, inasmuch as the offering is now made, not by slaves, but by freemen.

— Against Heresies IV.18.1–2, source (Roberts–Donaldson translation; cf. New Advent).2 The sacrifice-language is emphatic and repeated ("sacrifices there are, too, in the Church"), supplying the premise on which the whole doctrine turns: if the Eucharist is an offering, the question of who may offer it follows. Irenaeus himself frames the answer here corporately — it is "the oblation of the Church" — and it is the succession texts below that tie that Church-offering to the ordained who stand in the apostolic line.

Apostolic succession as the test of true ministry (Against Heresies III.3.1–3)

To refute the Gnostics' claim of a secret tradition, Irenaeus appeals to the public, traceable succession of bishops the apostles themselves installed — and lists the Roman line as his showcase:

We are in a position to reckon up those who were by the apostles instituted bishops in the Churches, and [to demonstrate] the succession of these men to our own times… The blessed apostles, then, having founded and built up the Church, committed into the hands of Linus the office of the episcopate. Of this Linus, Paul makes mention in the Epistles to Timothy. To him succeeded Anacletus; and after him, in the third place from the apostles, Clement was allotted the bishopric.

— Against Heresies III.3.1, 3, source (Roberts–Donaldson translation; cf. New Advent).3 Where Clement of Rome had asserted that the apostles appointed bishops and arranged for their orderly succession, Irenaeus turns that succession into an explicit rule of faith — the criterion by which a genuine ministry is known from a heretical one. This is the plank the doctrine's second argument, Did Christ delegate his own authority to the apostles, to be handed on to ordained successors?, weighs.

"Obey the presbyters… who possess the succession from the apostles" (Against Heresies IV.26.2)

The same principle governs presbyters as well as bishops: authority attaches to the line of succession, and those outside it are to be held in suspicion:

Wherefore it is incumbent to obey the presbyters who are in the Church,—those who, as I have shown, possess the succession from the apostles; those who, together with the succession of the episcopate, have received the certain gift of truth, according to the good pleasure of the Father. But [it is also incumbent] to hold in suspicion others who depart from the primitive succession, and assemble themselves together in any place whatsoever.

— Against Heresies IV.26.2, source (Roberts–Donaldson translation; cf. New Advent).4 Irenaeus here uses "presbyter" and "the succession of the episcopate" almost interchangeably — the same two-order fluidity Clement shows and Jerome will later press. What is firm is that legitimate ministry is defined by apostolic descent: the offering of the Church belongs to the ministry that stands in that succession, "those who… possess the succession from the apostles."

Notes

  • What it establishes. Irenaeus supplies two of the doctrine's load-bearing planks at once: the Eucharist as a true oblation fulfilling Malachi's "pure sacrifice," and apostolic succession as the public mark of the authentic ministry that holds it. He is the hinge between Clement's bare succession-of-appointment and the developed doctrine of an ordained ministry that alone offers.
  • The corporate-priesthood counter-note. In the same work Irenaeus writes that "all the righteous possess the sacerdotal rank. And all the apostles of the Lord are priests, who do inherit here neither lands nor houses, but serve God and the altar continually" (Against Heresies IV.8.3).5 Like Justin's "high priestly race" and Tertullian's "are not even we laics priests?", this shows he holds the priesthood of all believers alongside the reserved offering; he does not yet single out a distinct sacrificing hiereus-class in the way later writers will. His witness therefore grounds the succession and the sacrifice without settling, of itself, whether the reservation is more than the Church's apostolic order — the question weighed on the ministerial-priesthood-vs-all-believers page.
  • Load-bearing in the arguments. Irenaeus is already cited on two of this doctrine's argument pages — for the eucharistic reading of Malachi's pure offering (IV.17–18) and for apostolic succession (III.3; IV.26). This page gives those citations their timeline home.
