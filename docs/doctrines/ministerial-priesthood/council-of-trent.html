The Council of Trent on the Ministerial Priesthood

Dates: 15 July 1563 (Session 23 of the Council of Trent, the nineteenth ecumenical council, under Pope Pius IV; the True and Catholic Doctrine concerning the Sacrament of Order, with its canons) · Wikipedia
Summary: Answering the Reformers, Trent's twenty-third session gives the Roman dogmatic terminus of the doctrine this timeline traces. It defines that in the New Testament there is "a new, visible, and external priesthood," instituted by Christ, to which was delivered "the power… of consecrating, offering, and administering His Body and Blood, as also of forgiving and of retaining sins"; that this order is a true sacrament that imprints an indelible character, so that "he who has once been a priest" cannot "again become a layman"; and that the church's ministry is "a hierarchy… by divine ordination instituted, consisting of bishops, priests, and ministers." Canon 1 anathematizes reducing the priesthood to "only an office and bare ministry of preaching the Gospel." This is the point on which the conviction of Ignatius, Cyprian, and Aquinas is set as defined dogma against the Reformation.

Claims & evidence

A visible priesthood instituted by Christ, holding the power to offer the Eucharist and remit sins (Chapter 1)

The decree opens by tying the priesthood to the sacrifice: because the church has received "the holy visible sacrifice of the Eucharist" (defined at Session 22), there must be a priesthood to offer it. It then grounds that priesthood in Christ's institution and hands it the twin powers of offering and absolving:

Sacrifice and priesthood are, by the ordinance of God, in such wise conjoined, as that both have existed in every law. Whereas, therefore, in the New Testament, the Catholic Church has received, from the institution of Christ, the holy visible sacrifice of the Eucharist; it must needs also be confessed, that there is, in that Church, a new, visible, and external priesthood, into which the old has been translated. And the sacred Scriptures show, and the tradition of the Catholic Church has always taught, that this priesthood was instituted by the same Lord our Saviour, and that to the apostles, and their successors in the priesthood, was the power delivered of consecrating, offering, and administering His Body and Blood, as also of forgiving and of retaining sins.

— Council of Trent, Session 23 (1563), Doctrine on the Sacrament of Order, ch. 1, trans. J. Waterworth, source.1 This is the patristic conviction stated as defined doctrine: where Cyprian had said the priest "discharges the office of Christ" and Aquinas that the power to consecrate "is bestowed upon the priest at his ordination," Trent makes the offering-and-absolving power a dogma delivered by Christ to the apostles and their successors.

The priesthood is not a bare preaching office (Canon 1)

The attached canons anathematize the Reformed reduction of the priesthood to a ministry of the word alone:

If any one saith, that there is not in the New Testament a visible and external priesthood; or that there is not any power of consecrating and offering the true body and blood of the Lord, and of forgiving and retaining sins; but only an office and bare ministry of preaching the Gospel, or, that those who do not preach are not priests at all; let him be anathema.

— Council of Trent, Session 23 (1563), Canon 1, trans. Waterworth, source.2 This canon is aimed squarely at the framing question the timeline turns on — whether the New Testament institutes a distinct sacrificing priesthood, or leaves the act to the whole priestly people. Trent answers by defining the sacrificing priesthood and condemning the "bare ministry of preaching" alternative.

Order is a sacrament that imprints a character; a priest cannot become a layman again (Canons 3–4)

Trent defines ordination as a true sacrament instituted by Christ, through which the Holy Spirit is given and an indelible character imprinted — the point Augustine had first pressed from the baptism/orders parallel against the Donatists:

If any one saith, that order, or sacred ordination, is not truly and properly a sacrament instituted by Christ the Lord… let him be anathema.

If any one saith, that, by sacred ordination, the Holy Ghost is not given; and that vainly therefore do the bishops say, Receive ye the Holy Ghost; or, that a character is not imprinted by that ordination; or, that he who has once been a priest, can again become a layman; let him be anathema.

— Council of Trent, Session 23 (1563), Canons 3 and 4, trans. Waterworth, source.3

A hierarchy of divine ordination: bishops, priests, and ministers (Canons 6–7)

Finally, against Aerius's ancient levelling and the Reformers' parity of ministers, Trent defines the graded ministry and the bishop's superiority, with the reserved power of ordination:

If any one saith, that, in the Catholic Church there is not a hierarchy by divine ordination instituted, consisting of bishops, priests, and ministers; let him be anathema.

If any one saith, that bishops are not superior to priests; or, that they have not the power of confirming and ordaining; or, that the power which they possess is common to them and to priests… let him be anathema.

— Council of Trent, Session 23 (1563), Canons 6 and 7, trans. Waterworth, source.4 Here the seam that Aerius and Jerome had cut along — whether the bishop truly outranks the presbyter — is closed on the side of gradation, with ordination named as the bishop's reserved power, exactly the hinge Jerome had granted from the other side.

Notes

  • The dogmatic terminus. Trent 23 answers the Reformers on precisely the doctrine this timeline traces. Each strand the fathers supplied is here defined as dogma under anathema: the visible sacrificing priesthood (Chapter 1, Canon 1), the priest acting in Christ's stead to offer and absolve (the patristic in persona Christi), the indelible character (Augustine's baptism/orders parallel), and the graded, apostolic hierarchy (against Aerius and the Reformed parity of ministers).
  • Priesthood joined to sacrifice. The decree's opening move — that sacrifice and priesthood are "conjoined… in every law" — makes the whole rest on the prior Session 22 definition of the Mass as a true sacrifice (1562). That is the half of the framing dispute the argument page Does the New Testament institute a distinct ministerial priesthood, or only the priesthood of all believers? flags as decisive: whether there is a distinct sacrificing priesthood turns on whether the Eucharist is a sacrifice.
  • Not "invented at Trent." The council presents the reserved, sacrificing priesthood as what "the tradition of the Catholic Church has always taught," pointing back up the timeline to Ignatius, Cyprian, and the rest; what Trent adds is dogmatic definition and the anathema, in answer to a denial that had not been raised in the earlier centuries.
  • Translation provenance. The English is J. Waterworth's, The Canons and Decrees of the Sacred and Oecumenical Council of Trent (London: Dolman, 1848), the standard nineteenth-century English edition of the conciliar acts.
