Tertullian on the Ministerial Priesthood

Dates: c. 208 AD (On Exhortation to Chastity; On Baptism c. 200–206) · Tertullian, c. 155 – c. 220; the first great Latin theologian, a Carthaginian whose vocabulary shaped Western doctrine · Wikipedia
Summary: Tertullian is the earliest Latin writer to name in so many words the difference between the "Order" (ordo) and the laity, and to reserve the offering, baptism, and priestly acts to the ordained — while in the same breath voicing the counter-truth that "are not even we laics priests?" He is thus a doubled witness: he grounds the clergy/laity distinction the doctrine rests on, yet grounds it in the Church's authority rather than a separate consecration, and lets the layman "offer, and baptize, and are priest" where no clergy are present. In On Baptism he ranks the right to baptize — chief priest (the bishop), then presbyters and deacons, then laymen only in necessity — and warns that a layman's "emulation of the episcopal office is the mother of schisms."

Claims & evidence

"The difference between the Order and the laity" (On Exhortation to Chastity 7)

Arguing that clergy must be men of a single marriage, Tertullian anticipates the objection that what is forbidden to priests is allowed to laymen, and answers it by drawing the line between the ordained Order and the laity — even while conceding the priesthood of all believers:

Vain shall we be if we think that what is not lawful for priests is lawful for laics. Are not even we laics priests? It is written: "A kingdom also, and priests to His God and Father, hath He made us." It is the authority of the Church, and the honour which has acquired sanctity through the joint session of the Order, which has established the difference between the Order and the laity. Accordingly, where there is no joint session of the ecclesiastical Order, you offer, and baptize, and are priest, alone for yourself. But where three are, a church is, albeit they be laics.

— On Exhortation to Chastity, ch. 7, source (Thelwall translation, Ante-Nicene Fathers; cf. New Advent).1 This is the earliest surviving Latin statement of the ordo/laity distinction as a settled category. Its logic is precisely the structural plank the ministerial priesthood rests on: normally the offering and baptizing belong to the Order, and a layman acts as priest only "where there is no joint session of the ecclesiastical Order." Yet Tertullian's grounding is notably ecclesial rather than sacramental — it is "the authority of the Church" and the "honour" of the Order that "established" the difference, and he freely calls the laity "priests" on the strength of Revelation 1:6 — so the passage supplies both the reserved-ministry reading and its sharpest early qualification.

The right to baptize is ranked by order (On Baptism 17)

Closing his treatise on baptism, Tertullian sets out who may confer it, in a descending order that places the layman last and hedges his right with the demand for reverence:

Of giving it, the chief priest (who is the bishop) has the right: in the next place, the presbyters and deacons, yet not without the bishop's authority, on account of the honour of the Church, which being preserved, peace is preserved. Beside these, even laymen have the right; for what is equally received can be equally given. … But how much more is the rule of reverence and modesty incumbent on laymen—seeing that these powers belong to their superiors—lest they assume to themselves the specific function of the bishop! Emulation of the episcopal office is the mother of schisms.

— On Baptism, ch. 17, source (Thelwall translation, Ante-Nicene Fathers; cf. New Advent).2 The ladder is exact: bishop first, then presbyters and deacons "not without the bishop's authority," and only then the layman — and even the layman's right is bounded by "necessity" and the warning that to usurp "the specific function of the bishop" breeds schism. Though the sacrament in view is baptism rather than the Eucharist, the principle is the same one the doctrine turns on: the sacred acts belong by right to the ordained, the layman's competence is exceptional and derivative, and the "honour of the Church" is preserved by keeping the line.

Notes

  • What it establishes. Tertullian gives Latin theology the very word — ordo, "Order" — for the clergy set apart from the plebs, and the earliest explicit Western account of the clergy/laity distinction as the Church's settled discipline. The offering, the baptizing, and the priestly office belong normally to that Order; the layman may step in only where no clergy are present, and even then must not "emulate" the bishop's proper office.
  • The counter-witness in the same texts. Tertullian is also the strongest early Latin voice for the priesthood of all believers — "Are not even we laics priests?" — and for the layman's genuine (if emergency) right to offer and baptize. He roots the ordo/laity difference in the Church's authority and honour rather than in a distinct consecration, which is why the same chapter is cited on both sides of the ministerial-priesthood-vs-all-believers question. His witness therefore supports the reserved ministry as a matter of order while leaving open whether that reservation is more than ecclesiastical discipline.
  • Montanist period. On Exhortation to Chastity belongs to Tertullian's rigorist, Montanist-leaning years, when he was increasingly hostile to a lax clergy; the passage's stress on the laity's own priesthood is partly polemical. That colouring is worth weighing, but it does not blunt the plain historical datum: the ordo/laity distinction was already so established that he can argue from it. Whether that rigorist hostility to a lax clergy was heresy or merely harsh discipline is weighed on the argument “Was it doctrine, or only discipline?”, part of the timeline “Was Tertullian a heretic?”.
