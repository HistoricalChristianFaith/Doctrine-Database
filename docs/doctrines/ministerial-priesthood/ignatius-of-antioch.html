Ignatius of Antioch on the Ministerial Priesthood

Dates: c. 108 (the seven letters written en route to martyrdom at Rome, c. 107–110) · Wikipedia
Summary: Writing to the churches of Asia Minor on his way to die at Rome, Ignatius gives the earliest explicit rule that a Eucharist is valid only when the bishop, or one he authorizes, presides. Where Clement had grounded an ordered, succeeding ministry, Ignatius ties that ministry directly to the Eucharist itself: "Let that be deemed a proper Eucharist, which is administered either by the bishop, or by one to whom he has entrusted it"; "It is not lawful without the bishop either to baptize or to celebrate a love-feast." The congregation is to gather around "one altar" and "one bishop, along with the presbytery and deacons." This is the single most direct ante-Nicene statement that the offering belongs to the ordained ministry and not to the body at large.

Claims & evidence

A valid Eucharist requires the bishop or his delegate (Smyrnaeans 8)

See that ye all follow the bishop, even as Jesus Christ does the Father, and the presbytery as ye would the apostles; and reverence the deacons, as being the institution of God. Let no man do anything connected with the Church without the bishop. Let that be deemed a proper Eucharist, which is [administered] either by the bishop, or by one to whom he has entrusted it. Wherever the bishop shall appear, there let the multitude [of the people] also be; even as, wherever Jesus Christ is, there is the Catholic Church. It is not lawful without the bishop either to baptize or to celebrate a love-feast; but whatsoever he shall approve of, that is also pleasing to God, so that everything that is done may be secure and valid.

— Epistle to the Smyrnaeans, ch. 8, source (Roberts–Donaldson translation, Ante-Nicene Fathers; cf. New Advent).1 This is the load-bearing text. Ignatius does not merely commend the bishop's oversight; he makes the bishop's presidency (or his express delegation of it) the condition of a proper — in the Greek, bebaia, "valid/secure" — Eucharist, and extends the same rule to baptism and the love-feast. The act is not at the disposal of any believer who would perform it; it is "not lawful" apart from the one who holds the office. The phrase "or by one to whom he has entrusted it" already supplies the principle of delegation — the presbyter presides by the bishop's commission — on which the later distinction between the order and its license is built.

One Eucharist, one altar, one bishop with the presbytery and deacons (Philadelphians 4)

Take ye heed, then, to have but one Eucharist. For there is one flesh of our Lord Jesus Christ, and one cup to [show forth] the unity of His blood; one altar; as there is one bishop, along with the presbytery and deacons, my fellow-servants: that so, whatsoever ye do, ye may do it according to [the will of] God.

— Epistle to the Philadelphians, ch. 4, source (Roberts–Donaldson translation, Ante-Nicene Fathers; cf. New Advent).2 Here the unity of the Eucharist is anchored in the unity of the ministry: one altar corresponds to one bishop set among the presbytery and deacons. The "one altar" language treats the Eucharist as an offering made at a fixed station, not a meal celebrated at large — and the station is defined by the threefold order. A second Eucharist apart from that order is, by implication, no Eucharist of the church at all.

Ignatius held the see by ordination, not by Christ's own hand — the later witnesses that he was made bishop by the apostles, succeeding Peter at Antioch

Ignatius never describes his own consecration; but a firm patristic tradition holds that he did not seize the Antiochene chair, nor hold it by any direct touch of Christ — he received it through the laying-on of apostolic hands, standing in succession to Peter, who had first held that see. The point bears directly on the doctrine his letters serve: the episcopate is an office conferred by ordination and handed on by succession, not self-generated. The three chief witnesses give the datum in ascending explicitness — and one of them is routinely quoted for rather more than it says.

For I do not wonder at the man alone that he seemed to be worthy of so great an office, but that he obtained this office from those saints, and that the hands of the blessed apostles touched his sacred head. … But since I have mentioned Peter, I have perceived a fifth crown woven from him, and this is that this man succeeded to the office after him. … [W]hen Peter was about to depart from here, the grace of the Spirit introduced another teacher equivalent to Peter, so that the building already completed should not be made more unsound by the insignificance of the successor.

— John Chrysostom, Eulogy on Saint Ignatius (delivered at Antioch, c. 386–397), §§2, 4, source (Nicene and Post-Nicene Fathers, Series I, Vol. IX).3 Chrysostom's whole encomium leans on the point that Ignatius, who "had not even seen" Christ in the flesh, drew his office not from the Lord's own hand but from the apostles': "they…ordained him," "the hands of the blessed apostles touched his sacred head," and he "succeeded to the office after" Peter at Antioch. The episcopate is thus, for Chrysostom, a dignity handed on — a reading flatly opposed to the later legend that "not even the apostles dared lay hands on" Ignatius because Christ's embrace had already consecrated him (see the "child in Christ's arms" legend, whose whole payoff this tradition undercuts).

You have no doubt heard of the illustrious Ignatius, who received episcopal grace by the hand of the great Peter, and after ruling the church of Antioch, wore the crown of martyrdom.

— Theodoret of Cyrus, Eranistes, Dialogue I ("The Immutable"), c. 447, source (cf. New Advent).4 Theodoret makes the ordination explicit and personal: episcopal grace conferred "by the hand of the great Peter." A note in the Nicene and Post-Nicene Fathers edition records the competing early traditions — the Apostolic Constitutions (vii. 46) has Ignatius ordained by Paul, while John Malalas, like Theodoret, names Peter, ordaining him on the death of Euodius — so the fact of an apostolic ordination is well attested even where the ordaining apostle is disputed.5

… qui ornarunt sedem beatae memoriae multum triumphantis Ignatii martyris, cujus tu indignus existens, nescio quomodo insiluisti inchoare novitatem in catholica Ecclesia… imo ille qui vertex est omnium pastoralium sedium… apostolorum vere optimus Petrus.

— Felix III of Rome, synodical letter deposing Peter the Fuller of Antioch, c. 485 (they "adorned the see of the blessed memory of the greatly-triumphant Ignatius the martyr, of whom, being unworthy, you have somehow leapt in to bring novelty into the Catholic Church… nay, he who is the summit of all pastoral sees… truly the best of the apostles, Peter").6 Felix roots the dignity of the Antiochene see in Ignatius the martyr who "adorned" it, and the whole rebuke in Peter the apostle, "the summit of all pastoral sees" — so an intruder like Peter the Fuller holds the chair unworthily. He does not, however, say what he is often quoted (e.g. by John Pearson) as saying — that Ignatius "was ordained bishop by Peter's right hand." That explicit "right hand" wording is Theodoret's, folded back into Felix by later apologists; the letter itself attests only the connected point that the see is Ignatius's and Ignatius's honour is Peter's.7

Notes

  • What it establishes. Ignatius is the earliest surviving witness to say in so many words that the validity of the Eucharist depends on the presiding minister's office. With him the structural plank Clement laid — a divinely ordered, appointed ministry — is fastened explicitly to the Eucharist: the offering belongs to the bishop and those he authorizes. This is why the lead that prompted this page calls it "the single most direct patristic statement" of the doctrine's thesis.
  • The monarchical episcopate. Unlike Clement, who uses "bishop" and "presbyter" interchangeably, Ignatius presupposes the three-order, monarchical structure — one bishop over a presbytery and deacons in each city — and it is on that single bishop that he fastens the Eucharist. Whether this represents a development beyond the New Testament's two-order usage, or the drawing-out of what was implicit in it, is itself part of the dispute the doctrine raises; what is not in doubt is that Ignatius reads the offering as the ordained ministry's charge.
  • Same author, the Real Presence. Ignatius is also a key witness that the Eucharist is the true "flesh of our Lord Jesus Christ" — see his detail page on the Real Presence, where the very realism of the sacrament reinforces why he guards who may confect it.
  • Ordination and succession. That Ignatius was made bishop by apostolic hands, and succeeded Peter in the see of Antioch, is a datum for the older doctrines of episcopal ordination and apostolic succession — the office received by the laying-on of hands and handed on in an unbroken line. It cuts against the Baptist-successionist thesis of a parallel, non-episcopal chain of true churches: here the earliest catholic memory of Antioch is precisely of a bishop ordained by, and succeeding, an apostle.
  • The "child in Christ's arms" legend is late. A medieval Greek tradition identified Ignatius as the child whom Jesus set in the disciples' midst and embraced (Matthew 18:2–4) — in its earliest form a story exalting the bishop's office. It is a late fable, unattested before the ninth century and pointing away from the ancient (active) sense of his name Theophoros; it is weighed on its own page, Was Ignatius the child Jesus took in His arms? Its lateness bears on the weight the legendary Ignatian material can carry, but tells nothing against the genuineness of his letters, which are the witness this page rests on.
