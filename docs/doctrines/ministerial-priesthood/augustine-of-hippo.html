Augustine of Hippo on the Ministerial Priesthood

Dates: c. 400 AD (the anti-Donatist sacramental works — On Baptism c. 400–401 and Answer to the Letters of Parmenian c. 400 — with the Tractates on John, c. 407, and City of God X, c. 417; Augustine 354–430) · Wikipedia
The bridge from Cyprian's "office of Christ" to the medieval sacramental character. Pressed by the Donatist controversy — which claimed a sacrament administered by an unworthy or schismatic minister was void — Augustine forged the principle that would carry the whole later doctrine: the sacrament's power and the offering's validity rest on Christ, working through the ordained office, not on the holiness of the man who holds it. "Peter may baptize, but this is He that baptizeth… Judas may baptize, still this is He that baptizeth." From the same controversy he drew the parallel between baptism and orders: as a validly baptized man who secedes does not lose baptism, so a validly ordained man who secedes does not lose the sacrament of his ordination — the earliest clear statement of what the schools would call the indelible character of orders, the seed of Aquinas's teaching that the power to consecrate is conferred at ordination. And in the City of God he names the Eucharist the Church's own sacrifice, offered through the "great High Priest" in "the sacrament of the altar." He is thus a hinge witness: the reservation of the offering to the ordained is grounded not in the minister's merit but in the office Christ instituted and the character ordination imprints.

Claims & evidence

The sacrament's validity rests on Christ, not the minister's worth

Against the Donatists, who held that a sacrament conveyed by a traditor or an unworthy minister was no sacrament, Augustine located the sacrament's whole efficacy in Christ, the true agent who acts through whatever hands administer it. Expounding John the Baptist's testimony — "This is He that baptizeth with the Holy Ghost" — he presses that the virtue of the rite is "attributed to Him alone," so that the standing of the minister neither adds to nor subtracts from it:1

…there was to be a certain peculiarity in Christ, such that, although many ministers, be they righteous or unrighteous, should baptize, the virtue of baptism would be attributed to Him alone on whom the dove descended, and of whom it was said, "This is He that baptizeth with the Holy Ghost"? Peter may baptize, but this is He that baptizeth; Paul may baptize, yet this is He that baptizeth; Judas may baptize, still this is He that baptizeth. For if the sanctity of baptism be according to the diversity of merits in them that administer it, then as merits are diverse there will be diverse baptisms…

The point is the exact obverse of Cyprian's "that priest truly discharges the office of Christ, who imitates that which Christ did": where Cyprian grounds the offering in the celebrant's standing in the place of Christ, Augustine guards the same conviction from the Donatist inflation of it, insisting that because the minister acts in Christ's stead, it is Christ's holiness, not the minister's, on which the sacrament rests. The office is what Christ instituted; the man is its instrument.

Ordination imprints a permanent character, retained even by the schismatic

Augustine drew the sacramental principle out to orders explicitly, and this is his distinctive contribution to this doctrine. Arguing that a schismatic's baptism is valid because the sacrament, once rightly given, is Christ's and cannot be un-given, he sets baptism and ordination in strict parallel: neither may be repeated, because both leave a standing mark. In On Baptism he states it flatly:2

For as those who return to the Church, if they had been baptized before their secession, are not rebaptized, so those who return, having been ordained before their secession, are certainly not ordained again; but either they again exercise their former ministry, if the interests of the Church require it, or if they do not exercise it, at any rate they retain the sacrament of their ordination; and hence it is, that when hands are laid on them, to mark their reconciliation, they are not ranked with the laity.

The seceding minister keeps "the sacrament of conferring baptism" just as the seceding believer keeps the sacrament of baptism; both are Christ's seal, not the Church's transferable licence. This is the principle Augustine had already pressed against the Donatist bishop Parmenian — that as there is one baptism, so the ordained retain their orders across the schism — and it is the earliest clear Western statement of what the schoolmen would name the indelible character of orders.3 For the ministerial priesthood the corollary is exactly the one Aquinas would systematize: the power to celebrate the sacrament is not the layman's, nor the good man's as such, but is conferred at ordination and abides in the one ordained — that "the power of consecrating this sacrament… is bestowed upon the priest at his ordination."

The Eucharist is the Church's sacrifice, offered through the great High Priest

That the offering is a true sacrifice — the premise the reserved ministry rests on — Augustine states in the City of God, where he defines the Church's whole self-oblation and then identifies it with what is done at the altar:4

…the whole redeemed city, that is to say, the congregation or community of the saints, is offered to God as our sacrifice through the great High Priest, who offered Himself to God in His passion for us… This is the sacrifice of Christians: we, being many, are one body in Christ. And this also is the sacrifice which the Church continually celebrates in the sacrament of the altar, known to the faithful, in which she teaches that she herself is offered in the offering she makes to God.

The one High Priest, Christ, is the ground of the offering; the Church makes it "in the sacrament of the altar," the rite the ordained celebrate. Augustine keeps the corporate note strong — it is the whole body that is offered, and offers — without surrendering the reserved celebration: the sacrifice the community presents is confected at the altar, and Augustine, like the tradition before him, never lets a layman preside there. His teaching feeds forward on two lines at once: the Real Presence (the elements the altar makes Christ's body and blood) and the ministerial priesthood (the offering the ordained present in the Church's name).

The one sacrifice, foretold of old, is offered now "from the rising of the sun to its setting"

Preaching on Easter morning to the newly baptized — who had just received the Eucharist for the first time — Augustine names that Eucharist the one, worldwide sacrifice the prophets foretold, no longer bound to the Temple but offered everywhere, its victim Christ the priest "after the order of Melchizedek":5

What does such a great and divine sacrament mean, such a clear and noble remedy, such a pure and easy sacrifice, which is now offered not in one earthly city of Jerusalem, nor in that tabernacle made by Moses, nor in that temple built by Solomon, which were shadows of things to come, but from the rising of the sun to its setting, as foretold by the Prophets, it is immolated, and according to the grace of the New Testament is offered to God as a victim of praise… the sacrifice of our time is the body and blood of the priest himself. For of him it was foretold long before in the Psalms: You are a priest forever according to the order of Melchizedek.

Here the eucharistic-oblation strand of the City of God is stated in the plainest liturgical terms and joined to the two prophecies the tradition read of the Christian offering: Malachi's "pure offering… from the rising of the sun to its setting" (the proof-text weighed on the Malachi argument page) and the Melchizedekian priesthood of Psalm 110, the same text Leo the Great would preach of the one High Priest. The sacrifice is "offered" — the premise the reserved ministry rests on — and Augustine, catechizing his own newly-baptized flock, takes for granted that it is the clergy who offer it.

Notes

  • What the Donatist crucible forged. Augustine's ministerial theology is not a fresh speculation but the answer he was forced to give the Donatists. Their case — a sacrament from an unworthy minister is void — would have made the offering hang on the celebrant's holiness. Augustine's counter (Christ is the true minister; the sacrament and the orders are His seal, not the man's merit) is precisely what lets the reserved offering stand on the office rather than the occupant. It is the theological hinge between Cyprian's priest who "discharges the office of Christ" and Aquinas's priest who consecrates by an instrumental power conferred at ordination.
  • The seed of the indelible character of orders. Augustine does not use the later term character indelebilis, and his interest in On Baptism is chiefly to defeat rebaptism, not to build a theology of orders. But his flat statement that the ordained "retain the sacrament of their ordination" across secession — so that on return they are "not ranked with the laity" and are not re-ordained — is the datum the medieval doctrine of an indelible priestly character is built from. He is cited here for that seed, not for a developed scholastic account he never wrote.
  • The corporate emphasis is real, and does not undo the reservation. The City of God passage is a favourite of those who read Augustine toward the priesthood of all believers: the whole city is priest and sacrifice. That emphasis is genuinely his and should not be softened — but it sits, in Augustine, alongside a wholly ordinary assumption that the "sacrament of the altar" is celebrated by the clergy, and it is the same both-and (a corporate priesthood and a reserved offering) already seen in Justin, Irenaeus, and Tertullian. The corporate self-offering names what is offered; it does not hand the celebration to the assembly at large.
  • Texts used. The quotations are from the Tractates on the Gospel of John 6 (on John 1:32–33, c. 407), On Baptism, Against the Donatists I.1.2 (c. 400–401), and the City of God X.6 (c. 417), in the NPNF translations, and the Easter sermon Sermon 228B (On the Sacraments, on Easter Day; also numbered Denis 3), an English translation of the Latin. The baptism/orders parallel was first pressed in Answer to the Letters of Parmenian (Contra epistolam Parmeniani) II, c. 400, Augustine's earliest sustained anti-Donatist treatise; the same argument is stated in Augustine's own words in On Baptism, quoted above. The timeline anchors him at c. 400. Augustine also appears on the Real Presence, infant baptism, intermediate state, purgatory, prayer to the saints, perpetual virginity, Old Testament canon, and Nephilim timelines, among others.
