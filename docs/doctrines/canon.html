The Biblical Canon

How the Church came to recognize which books are Scripture — the Old Testament (and the disputed deuterocanon) and the New — is a story of gradual reception, not a single decree. The earliest lists are partial and regional: the Muratorian fragment (late 2nd c.), Origen, Eusebius's sorting of the books into "acknowledged," "disputed," and "spurious" (Church History 3.25), and Athanasius's Festal Letter 39 of 367 — the first catalogue to match the present twenty-seven-book New Testament — followed by the African councils of Hippo (393) and Carthage (397), with the East long hesitant over the Apocalypse and the West and East still divided over the deuterocanon at the Reformation. Around this real history a popular legend grew: that the canon was decided in one stroke at the Council of Nicaea in 325 — voted into being, even sorted by a miracle — the claim this page first takes up below.

Related cruxes — the Nicene-canon legend

The popular legend that the biblical canon was settled in one stroke at the Council of Nicaea (325) — its miraculous mechanism, and its New-Testament-apocrypha face. Three facets of one story, weighed together: the general thesis that Nicaea fixed the canon, the altar-ordeal by which the legend has the books sorted, and the twin claim that the excluded gospels were actively suppressed — banned, gathered up and burned.

Did the Council of Nicaea (325) fix the biblical canon? — the popular legend (assessment: unsupported)

The Nicaea-fixed-the-canon legend, in its popular modern form — the claim, bestselling in Dan Brown's The Da Vinci Code (2003) but Enlightenment-rationalist in origin (Paine, Voltaire), that the contents of the Bible were decided at Nicaea: a canon voted, a "new Bible" commissioned and financed by Constantine "which omitted those gospels that spoke of Christ's human traits," the losing gospels "outlawed, gathered up and burned." The interpretation is unsupported on every part. The council's twenty surviving canons and acts say nothing of the canon of Scripture; Eusebius, who was present, records the canon as still disputed in his own day (Church History 3.25); Athanasius, also present, in 367 still frames listing the books as his own undertaking of "necessity," the canon "handed down" rather than decreed — from which Breen rightly infers no Nicene catalogue was ever promulgated; and Constantine's one real Bible-project, the fifty copies ordered from Eusebius (Life of Constantine 4.36), was the transcription of an existing collection, not its selection, with no trace of suppression or burning. The legend distorts a real but smaller history — an emperor who convened a council that legislated on doctrine and discipline, and who later paid for Bibles. (A far narrower, genuinely scholarly question — whether Nicaea used or recognized particular disputed books in passing — is separate, and even its defenders deny the council drew up an official list.)1

The altar-ordeal miracle — were the inspired books sorted onto the altar at Nicaea? (assessment: unsupported)

The miraculous "book-ordeal" — the strand of the legend that supplies its mechanism: the disputed writings laid by the altar, the council at prayer, and the inspired books found on top while the spurious sank beneath. The interpretation is unsupported. The tale rests on a single source — §35 of the anonymous Synodicon Vetus, compiled around AD 900, nearly six centuries after the council — a work crowded with details found nowhere else and recounting synods scholars doubt ever met; no contemporary witness and none of the council's own acts or twenty canons know of it. Its result, a canon closed by divine sign in 325, is contradicted by Eusebius and Athanasius, both present at Nicaea, who go on treating the canon as still open. The anecdote entered Western letters only when its Greek manuscript was carried west by Andreas Darmasius and printed in 1601 by Johannes Pappus (hence later writers' "Pappus, in his Synodicon"), whence Voltaire relayed it — the source of the modern embellishment that the canon was at Nicaea "even sorted by a miracle."2

Were the infancy gospels suppressed — banned, voted out, gathered up and burned? (assessment: unsupported)

The same legend in New-Testament-apocrypha dress — the popular framing that the gospels left out of the New Testament, the infancy gospels above all (the Infancy Gospel of Thomas, the Protoevangelium of James), were not merely passed over but suppressed: outlawed, voted down, "gathered up and burned." The interpretation is unsupported. The documented history is gradual non-reception: from the second century on the proto-orthodox never widely received these texts as apostolic, and the formal exclusion-lists, when they come, speak in the language of books "not to be received" — Innocent I (405) pronounces the pseudo-apostolic gospels "rejected and condemned," and the Decretum Gelasianum (c. 500) is literally titled On books to be received and not to be received — not destroyed. The one real decree-and-bonfire near Nicaea burned a heretic's doctrinal treatises — Constantine's order to torch Arius's writings on pain of death — not the apocryphal gospels, and even that suppression failed; meanwhile the excluded gospels went on circulating freely for a thousand years, the Protoevangelium surviving in 140-odd Greek manuscripts and a dozen languages, the very footprint of free circulation rather than eradication. A milder, true claim survives: they were never received as Scripture. The "banned and burned" image is the Nicaea-fixed-the-canon legend on the New Testament side.3

Related cruxes — the scholarly case for a Nicene canon-action

The sober, scholarly cousin of the popular legend: not a voted "new Bible," but the claim — built by the Jesuit introduction-scholar Rudolph Cornely on three textual props — that the council of 325 genuinely took up the canon and enumerated the sacred books, deuterocanon included. All three of his props are weighed here; they run from his strongest to his weakest.

Did Jerome's Preface to Judith show the Council of Nicaea counted Judith among the Scriptures? (assessment: weak)

The one early text adduced for a real Nicene action on the canon — set apart from the popular legend above, and Cornely's strongest prop. Jerome (c. 405) writes that Judith "is found by the Nicene Council to have been counted among the number of the Sacred Scriptures," and Cornely reads this as proof that the council of 325 enumerated the sacred books in a decree, deuterocanon included. The interpretation is weak. Jerome's hedged "is found to have been counted" attests at most a viva voce use of Judith in the council's deliberations — a book quoted as Scripture in argument, as Ephesus later leaned on Hebrews without decreeing a New Testament canon — not a promulgated list; he never cites a Nicene catalogue, the council's twenty surviving canons say nothing of Scripture, and Jerome himself, in the same prefaces, expressly rules Judith out of the canon ("not in the canon … among the Apocryphal writings"), which he could not do had he taken Nicaea to have decreed it in. Athanasius — present at Nicaea — likewise excludes Judith in 367, and Hefele treats a Nicene canon-decree as a later misattribution. The modest reading (Nicaea cited Judith in argument) is plausible; the decree-reading does not hold.4

The Council of Hippo's canon 36 under a "Nicaea" heading — does the Vallicellian codex prove the council drew its canon from Nicaea? (assessment: unsupported)

Cornely's third prop. A single eighth/ninth-century codex (Vallicelliana B.58, fol. 77) prefaces the canon-list of the Council of Hippo (393) — "that besides the canonical Scriptures nothing be read in church" — with the rubric "Chapter XXIV of the Council of Nicaea," then runs out a catalogue intermixing the proto- and deuterocanonical books; Cornely concludes the Fathers of Hippo drew their list from the decrees of Nicaea (carried to Africa by Caecilian of Carthage), so that Nicaea decreed the wider canon. The interpretation is unsupported. The list beneath the rubric is the securely-dated African canon of Hippo — reaffirmed at Carthage in 397 and 419 — not a Nicene one; the "of Nicaea" heading survives in this lone late manuscript and is a textbook case of the mislabelling Hefele documents, later conciliar canons drifting under Nicaea's name because the genuine Nicene canons always headed the early corpus (the Rome copy welding Nicaea and Sardica sub uno titulo; Pope Zosimus quoting a Sardican canon as Nicene). The very Nicene exemplar Cornely invokes — Caecilian's copy at Carthage — is on record as having held no such canon when the African bishops searched it in the Apiarius affair, forcing them to send to Alexandria and Constantinople for authentic texts. Breen grants the prop "what force it may have" yet still refuses to "refer the Council of Nice among the proofs of the Canon," noting it "exists but in one sole codex"; the council's own twenty canons are silent.5

Cassiodorus's Institutiones I.14 — does listing Nicaea among the dividers of Scripture show the council took up the canon? (assessment: unsupported)

Cornely's weakest prop. Cassiodorus (c. 543) names "the Councils of Nicaea and Chalcedon," with Hilary, Rufinus, and Epiphanius, among those who "by their divisions, fitted the sacred books to the appropriate mysteries"; Cornely infers the Nicene Fathers must have drawn up a division of the canon. The interpretation is unsupported. Cassiodorus's chapter is about how the already-received books are divided and numbered — reconciling Jerome's, Augustine's, and the Septuagint's schemes — not about which books are Scripture; his sentence yokes Nicaea to Chalcedon, which decreed no canon, so the inference proves too much; and as sixth-century, second-hand testimony to 325 it is best read as an instance of the later drift of canon-material under Nicaea's name — the very drift Hefele documents, and Hefele is the source from which Cornely (Breen notes) borrowed the citation. Breen's verdict — "obscure words" that "form no forcible proof" — stands; the council's own twenty canons are silent.6
