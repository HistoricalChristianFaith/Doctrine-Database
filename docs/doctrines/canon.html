The Biblical Canon

How the Church came to recognize which books are Scripture — the Old Testament (and the disputed deuterocanon) and the New — is a story of gradual reception, not a single decree. The earliest lists are partial and regional: the Muratorian fragment (late 2nd c.), Origen, Eusebius's sorting of the books into "acknowledged," "disputed," and "spurious" (Church History 3.25), and Athanasius's Festal Letter 39 of 367 — the first catalogue to match the present twenty-seven-book New Testament — followed by the African councils of Hippo (393) and Carthage (397), with the East long hesitant over the Apocalypse and the West and East still divided over the deuterocanon at the Reformation. Around this real history a popular legend grew: that the canon was decided in one stroke at the Council of Nicaea in 325 — voted into being, even sorted by a miracle — the claim this page first takes up below.

Related cruxes — the Nicene-canon legend

The popular legend that the biblical canon was settled in one stroke at the Council of Nicaea (325) — and the miraculous mechanism a later strand of it supplies. Two facets of one story, weighed together: the general thesis that Nicaea fixed the canon, and the altar-ordeal by which the legend has the books sorted.

Did the Council of Nicaea (325) fix the biblical canon? — the popular legend (assessment: unsupported)

The Nicaea-fixed-the-canon legend, in its popular modern form — the claim, bestselling in Dan Brown's The Da Vinci Code (2003) but Enlightenment-rationalist in origin (Paine, Voltaire), that the contents of the Bible were decided at Nicaea: a canon voted, a "new Bible" commissioned and financed by Constantine "which omitted those gospels that spoke of Christ's human traits," the losing gospels "outlawed, gathered up and burned." The interpretation is unsupported on every part. The council's twenty surviving canons and acts say nothing of the canon of Scripture; Eusebius, who was present, records the canon as still disputed in his own day (Church History 3.25); Athanasius, also present, in 367 still frames listing the books as his own undertaking of "necessity," the canon "handed down" rather than decreed — from which Breen rightly infers no Nicene catalogue was ever promulgated; and Constantine's one real Bible-project, the fifty copies ordered from Eusebius (Life of Constantine 4.36), was the transcription of an existing collection, not its selection, with no trace of suppression or burning. The legend distorts a real but smaller history — an emperor who convened a council that legislated on doctrine and discipline, and who later paid for Bibles. (A far narrower, genuinely scholarly question — whether Nicaea used or recognized particular disputed books in passing — is separate, and even its defenders deny the council drew up an official list.)1

The altar-ordeal miracle — were the inspired books sorted onto the altar at Nicaea? (assessment: unsupported)

The miraculous "book-ordeal" — the strand of the legend that supplies its mechanism: the disputed writings laid by the altar, the council at prayer, and the inspired books found on top while the spurious sank beneath. The interpretation is unsupported. The tale rests on a single source — §35 of the anonymous Synodicon Vetus, compiled around AD 900, nearly six centuries after the council — a work crowded with details found nowhere else and recounting synods scholars doubt ever met; no contemporary witness and none of the council's own acts or twenty canons know of it. Its result, a canon closed by divine sign in 325, is contradicted by Eusebius and Athanasius, both present at Nicaea, who go on treating the canon as still open. The anecdote entered Western letters only when its Greek manuscript was carried west by Andreas Darmasius and printed in 1601 by Johannes Pappus (hence later writers' "Pappus, in his Synodicon"), whence Voltaire relayed it — the source of the modern embellishment that the canon was at Nicaea "even sorted by a miracle."2

Did Jerome's Preface to Judith show the Council of Nicaea counted Judith among the Scriptures? (assessment: weak)

The one early text adduced for a real Nicene action on the canon — set apart from the popular legend above. Jerome (c. 405) writes that Judith "is found by the Nicene Council to have been counted among the number of the Sacred Scriptures," and the Jesuit introduction-scholar Rudolph Cornely reads this as proof that the council of 325 enumerated the sacred books in a decree, deuterocanon included — the strongest of three props he marshals (with an obscure phrase in Cassiodorus and a heading in one late codex of Hippo's canon). The interpretation is weak. Jerome's hedged "is found to have been counted" attests at most a viva voce use of Judith in the council's deliberations — a book quoted as Scripture in argument, as Ephesus later leaned on Hebrews without decreeing a New Testament canon — not a promulgated list; he never cites a Nicene catalogue, the council's twenty surviving canons say nothing of Scripture, and Jerome himself, in the same prefaces, expressly rules Judith out of the canon ("not in the canon … among the Apocryphal writings"), which he could not do had he taken Nicaea to have decreed it in. Athanasius — present at Nicaea — likewise excludes Judith in 367, and Hefele treats a Nicene canon-decree as a later misattribution. The modest reading (Nicaea cited Judith in argument) is plausible; the decree-reading does not hold.3
