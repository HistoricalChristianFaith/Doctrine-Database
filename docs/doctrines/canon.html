The Biblical Canon

How the Church came to recognize which books are Scripture — the Old Testament (and the disputed deuterocanon) and the New — is a story of gradual reception, not a single decree. The earliest lists are partial and regional: the Muratorian fragment (late 2nd c.), Origen, Eusebius's sorting of the books into "acknowledged," "disputed," and "spurious" (Church History 3.25), and Athanasius's Festal Letter 39 of 367 — the first catalogue to match the present twenty-seven-book New Testament — followed by the African councils of Hippo (393) and Carthage (397), with the East long hesitant over the Apocalypse and the West and East still divided over the deuterocanon at the Reformation. Around this real history a popular legend grew: that the canon was decided in one stroke at the Council of Nicaea in 325 — voted into being, even sorted by a miracle — the claim this page first takes up below.

Timeline

The chronological witnesses to the canon's reception, earliest first. The cross-cutting legends and historical theses they bear on are weighed below under Arguments & cruxes.

Justin Martyr (c. 155)

The earliest witness on the timeline attests not a list but a function: at Rome around 155, Justin Martyr already treats the Gospels as Scripture. He calls them the apostles' "memoirs composed by them, which are called Gospels," citing them for the words of institution (First Apology 66), and reports that in the Sunday assembly "the memoirs of the apostles or the writings of the prophets are read, as long as time permits" (67) — the apostolic records read aloud beside the prophets, the practical mark of a book held to be Scripture. The boundary is not yet drawn, though: Justin never enumerates the four, never names the evangelists, and his Gospel citations sometimes harmonize the texts — so he marks the moment the Gospels function as Scripture, a generation before Irenaeus fixes their number and the Muratorian list first counts the books.1

Irenaeus of Lyons (c. 180)

The earliest datable notice of infancy-gospel material is also the earliest rejection of it. Writing against the Gnostic followers of Marcus, Irenaeus files the tale of the boy Jesus and his alphabet-teacher — "Pronounce Alpha … Do thou first tell me what Alpha is, and then I will tell thee what Beta is" — among the Marcosians' "unspeakable number of apocryphal and spurious writings, which they themselves have forged," branding it a "false and wicked story" (Against Heresies 1.20.1).2 The episode is the same alphabet-lesson that later fills chapters 6 and 14 of the Infancy Gospel of Thomas; in Irenaeus it is already outside the rule of truth. A caution belongs on the witness, though: he reports a single circulating story the Gnostics expound, never naming a book and never calling it "Thomas" or "a gospel" — the same saying travels independently in the Epistle of the Apostles — so he attests the early rejection of the tradition, not yet a fixed infancy text.3

Origen of Alexandria (c. 248)

Two generations on, Origen is the first to name the apostolic-pseudonym and infancy gospels and to set them outside the four. Reading Luke's "many have taken in hand to draw up a narrative" as a veiled criticism, he ranks the rival gospels as mere "attempts": "those who wrote the Gospel according to the Egyptians and the Gospel according to the Twelve 'attempted' to write … for there is also the Gospel according to Thomas and the Gospel according to Matthias and several others. These are attempts; the church of God has approved only the four" (Homilies on Luke 1).4 And in the Commentary on Matthew he can cite "a tradition in the Gospel according to Peter … or The Book of James" — the Protoevangelium — for the view that the Lord's brethren were Joseph's sons by an earlier marriage, reporting only what "some say" and never granting the book the standing of Scripture (X.17, on Matt 13:55).5 His boundary is drawn by reception and authority — the church has "approved only the four" — not, in these passages, by a charge of heresy; the sharper "forgeries of heretics" language waits for Eusebius. And because his list of titles is bare, it cannot by itself fix which "Gospel of Thomas" he meant, the Gnostic sayings gospel or the Infancy text (see the crux below).

Eusebius of Caesarea (c. 324)

On the eve of Nicaea, Eusebius draws the most deliberate early sorting of the books — accepted, disputed-but-widely-recognized, and rejected — and then sets the apostolic-pseudonym gospels of the heretics a step below even the rejected. It is here that the backbone names of the New-Testament apocrypha stand together: among "those that are cited by the heretics under the name of the apostles" are "the Gospels of Peter, of Thomas, of Matthias, or of any others besides them, and the Acts of Andrew and John," books that "no one belonging to the succession of ecclesiastical writers has deemed worthy of mention" and whose style and matter "clearly show themselves to be the fictions of heretics," "to be cast aside as absurd and impious" (Church History 3.25.6–7). His is the sharpest pre-Athanasian language of exclusion — and a contemporary's testimony cutting against the later legend: present at the council of 325, he still records the canon itself as disputed, not decreed, which is why this same chapter recurs among the Nicene-canon cruxes below.6

Cyril of Jerusalem (c. 350)

A generation after Eusebius, and within his own province, Cyril of Jerusalem turns the sorting into a pastoral rule for the newly baptized. Catechizing the candidates, he tells them to learn the canon "from the Church," read "the twenty-two books of the Old Testament" and the recognized New, and "read none of the apocryphal writings … for why dost thou, who knowest not those which are acknowledged among all, trouble thyself in vain about those which are disputed?" (Catechetical Lecture 4.33). His New-Testament list runs the four Gospels — "for the rest have false titles and are mischievous" — Acts, the seven Catholic Epistles, and "the fourteen Epistles of Paul," conspicuously stopping short of the Apocalypse (the Eastern hesitancy over Revelation), and naming a single apocryphal example: a "Gospel according to Thomas" he ascribes to the Manichaeans (4.36). The boundary, as with Eusebius, is drawn by church reception and the register is repudiation, not destruction: the apocrypha are to be "put aside in a secondary rank," and "whatever books are not read in Churches, these read not even by thyself" (4.35–36).7

Innocent I of Rome (c. 405)

By the turn of the fifth century the Western boundary is drawn by a sitting bishop of Rome. Answering an inquiry from Exuperius of Toulouse, Innocent I appends a canon-list matching the present twenty-seven-book New Testament, then turns to the apocrypha: "the rest of the books, which appear under the name of Matthias … or under the name of Peter and John (which were written by a certain Leucius) … or under the name of Andrew … or under the name of Thomas, and whatever others there may be," he writes, "you should know they are not only to be rejected but also condemned" (Ep. 6, 405). It is the sharpest Western papal exclusion before the Gelasian Decree, naming the same apostolic-pseudonym gospels and acts Eusebius had bracketed and tagging them to their reputed forgers (the Petrine and Johannine acts to "a certain Leucius," the Andrean to two named philosophers). Yet his verb tells against the legend: the books are damnanda — condemned, repudiated — not gathered and burned; this is exclusion by formal repudiation, the documented register of the late rejection-lists. (The "apocrypha" he condemns are the pseudo-apostolic New-Testament writings, not the Old-Testament deuterocanon, which his list keeps.)8

The rejection-lists (c. 500–810)

By the sixth century the boundary is drawn not book-by-book but as a formal catalogue of rejection — a list of titles "not to be received." The Western Decretum Gelasianum (De libris recipiendis et non recipiendis, c. 500) is the one early list that names the infancy texts by name: among some sixty rejected apocrypha it registers "the book on the infancy of the saviour" (the boy-Jesus material of the Infancy Gospel of Thomas) and "the book of the nativity of the saviour and of Mary or the midwife" (the Protoevangelium of James), alongside — and kept distinct from — "the Gospel in the name of Thomas which the Manichaeans use," each tagged "apocryphal" and "to be avoided by catholics." The Greek catalogues run a parallel Eastern record but a narrower one: the List of Sixty Books (c. 650) and the Stichometry of Nicephorus (c. 810) reject a "Gospel of Thomas" and a "Circuit of Thomas" without ever naming the infancy gospels or the Protoevangelium — and the "Gospel of Thomas" they list, over-long by its own line-count, is most likely not the infancy book at all. Two things follow. The exclusion of the infancy gospels as such is, on the surviving lists, a Western datum; and the register on both sides is repudiation — books "to be avoided," "not received" — not the "banned and burned" of the legend. (The Decretum's separation of the Manichaean "Gospel of Thomas" from "the book on the infancy of the saviour" is itself evidence on the which-Thomas crux below.)9

Arguments & cruxes

Sub-claims marshaled for (or against) a reading, which cut across the timeline rather than sitting at one date — each weighed adversarially on its own page, with an assessment of the interpretation it serves.

Related cruxes — the Nicene-canon legend

The popular legend that the biblical canon was settled in one stroke at the Council of Nicaea (325) — its miraculous mechanism, and its New-Testament-apocrypha face. Three facets of one story, weighed together: the general thesis that Nicaea fixed the canon, the altar-ordeal by which the legend has the books sorted, and the twin claim that the excluded gospels were actively suppressed — banned, gathered up and burned.

Did the Council of Nicaea (325) fix the biblical canon? — the popular legend (assessment: unsupported)

The Nicaea-fixed-the-canon legend, in its popular modern form — the claim, bestselling in Dan Brown's The Da Vinci Code (2003) but Enlightenment-rationalist in origin (Paine, Voltaire), that the contents of the Bible were decided at Nicaea: a canon voted, a "new Bible" commissioned and financed by Constantine "which omitted those gospels that spoke of Christ's human traits," the losing gospels "outlawed, gathered up and burned." The interpretation is unsupported on every part. The council's twenty surviving canons and acts say nothing of the canon of Scripture; Eusebius, who was present, records the canon as still disputed in his own day (Church History 3.25); Athanasius, also present, in 367 still frames listing the books as his own undertaking of "necessity," the canon "handed down" rather than decreed — from which Breen rightly infers no Nicene catalogue was ever promulgated; and Constantine's one real Bible-project, the fifty copies ordered from Eusebius (Life of Constantine 4.36), was the transcription of an existing collection, not its selection, with no trace of suppression or burning. The legend distorts a real but smaller history — an emperor who convened a council that legislated on doctrine and discipline, and who later paid for Bibles. (A far narrower, genuinely scholarly question — whether Nicaea used or recognized particular disputed books in passing — is separate, and even its defenders deny the council drew up an official list.)10

The altar-ordeal miracle — were the inspired books sorted onto the altar at Nicaea? (assessment: unsupported)

The miraculous "book-ordeal" — the strand of the legend that supplies its mechanism: the disputed writings laid by the altar, the council at prayer, and the inspired books found on top while the spurious sank beneath. The interpretation is unsupported. The tale rests on a single source — §35 of the anonymous Synodicon Vetus, compiled around AD 900, nearly six centuries after the council — a work crowded with details found nowhere else and recounting synods scholars doubt ever met; no contemporary witness and none of the council's own acts or twenty canons know of it. Its result, a canon closed by divine sign in 325, is contradicted by Eusebius and Athanasius, both present at Nicaea, who go on treating the canon as still open. The anecdote entered Western letters only when its Greek manuscript was carried west by Andreas Darmasius and printed in 1601 by Johannes Pappus (hence later writers' "Pappus, in his Synodicon"), whence Voltaire relayed it — the source of the modern embellishment that the canon was at Nicaea "even sorted by a miracle."11

Were the infancy gospels suppressed — banned, voted out, gathered up and burned? (assessment: unsupported)

The same legend in New-Testament-apocrypha dress — the popular framing that the gospels left out of the New Testament, the infancy gospels above all (the Infancy Gospel of Thomas, the Protoevangelium of James), were not merely passed over but suppressed: outlawed, voted down, "gathered up and burned." The interpretation is unsupported. The documented history is gradual non-reception: from the second century on the proto-orthodox never widely received these texts as apostolic, and the formal exclusion-lists, when they come, speak in the language of books "not to be received" — Innocent I (405) pronounces the pseudo-apostolic gospels "rejected and condemned," and the Decretum Gelasianum (c. 500) is literally titled On books to be received and not to be received — not destroyed. The one real decree-and-bonfire near Nicaea burned a heretic's doctrinal treatises — Constantine's order to torch Arius's writings on pain of death — not the apocryphal gospels, and even that suppression failed; meanwhile the excluded gospels went on circulating freely for a thousand years, the Protoevangelium surviving in 140-odd Greek manuscripts and a dozen languages, the very footprint of free circulation rather than eradication. A milder, true claim survives: they were never received as Scripture. The "banned and burned" image is the Nicaea-fixed-the-canon legend on the New Testament side.12

Related cruxes — the scholarly case for a Nicene canon-action

The sober, scholarly cousin of the popular legend: not a voted "new Bible," but the claim — built by the Jesuit introduction-scholar Rudolph Cornely on three textual props — that the council of 325 genuinely took up the canon and enumerated the sacred books, deuterocanon included. All three of his props are weighed here; they run from his strongest to his weakest.

Did Jerome's Preface to Judith show the Council of Nicaea counted Judith among the Scriptures? (assessment: weak)

The one early text adduced for a real Nicene action on the canon — set apart from the popular legend above, and Cornely's strongest prop. Jerome (c. 405) writes that Judith "is found by the Nicene Council to have been counted among the number of the Sacred Scriptures," and Cornely reads this as proof that the council of 325 enumerated the sacred books in a decree, deuterocanon included. The interpretation is weak. Jerome's hedged "is found to have been counted" attests at most a viva voce use of Judith in the council's deliberations — a book quoted as Scripture in argument, as Ephesus later leaned on Hebrews without decreeing a New Testament canon — not a promulgated list; he never cites a Nicene catalogue, the council's twenty surviving canons say nothing of Scripture, and Jerome himself, in the same prefaces, expressly rules Judith out of the canon ("not in the canon … among the Apocryphal writings"), which he could not do had he taken Nicaea to have decreed it in. Athanasius — present at Nicaea — likewise excludes Judith in 367, and Hefele treats a Nicene canon-decree as a later misattribution. The modest reading (Nicaea cited Judith in argument) is plausible; the decree-reading does not hold.13

The Council of Hippo's canon 36 under a "Nicaea" heading — does the Vallicellian codex prove the council drew its canon from Nicaea? (assessment: unsupported)

Cornely's third prop. A single eighth/ninth-century codex (Vallicelliana B.58, fol. 77) prefaces the canon-list of the Council of Hippo (393) — "that besides the canonical Scriptures nothing be read in church" — with the rubric "Chapter XXIV of the Council of Nicaea," then runs out a catalogue intermixing the proto- and deuterocanonical books; Cornely concludes the Fathers of Hippo drew their list from the decrees of Nicaea (carried to Africa by Caecilian of Carthage), so that Nicaea decreed the wider canon. The interpretation is unsupported. The list beneath the rubric is the securely-dated African canon of Hippo — reaffirmed at Carthage in 397 and 419 — not a Nicene one; the "of Nicaea" heading survives in this lone late manuscript and is a textbook case of the mislabelling Hefele documents, later conciliar canons drifting under Nicaea's name because the genuine Nicene canons always headed the early corpus (the Rome copy welding Nicaea and Sardica sub uno titulo; Pope Zosimus quoting a Sardican canon as Nicene). The very Nicene exemplar Cornely invokes — Caecilian's copy at Carthage — is on record as having held no such canon when the African bishops searched it in the Apiarius affair, forcing them to send to Alexandria and Constantinople for authentic texts. Breen grants the prop "what force it may have" yet still refuses to "refer the Council of Nice among the proofs of the Canon," noting it "exists but in one sole codex"; the council's own twenty canons are silent.14

Cassiodorus's Institutiones I.14 — does listing Nicaea among the dividers of Scripture show the council took up the canon? (assessment: unsupported)

Cornely's weakest prop. Cassiodorus (c. 543) names "the Councils of Nicaea and Chalcedon," with Hilary, Rufinus, and Epiphanius, among those who "by their divisions, fitted the sacred books to the appropriate mysteries"; Cornely infers the Nicene Fathers must have drawn up a division of the canon. The interpretation is unsupported. Cassiodorus's chapter is about how the already-received books are divided and numbered — reconciling Jerome's, Augustine's, and the Septuagint's schemes — not about which books are Scripture; his sentence yokes Nicaea to Chalcedon, which decreed no canon, so the inference proves too much; and as sixth-century, second-hand testimony to 325 it is best read as an instance of the later drift of canon-material under Nicaea's name — the very drift Hefele documents, and Hefele is the source from which Cornely (Breen notes) borrowed the citation. Breen's verdict — "obscure words" that "form no forcible proof" — stands; the council's own twenty canons are silent.15

Which "Gospel of Thomas" did the Fathers condemn — the sayings gospel or the Infancy Gospel? (assessment: weak)

A two-text problem on the New-Testament side. One title, "Gospel of Thomas," belongs to two unrelated works — the Gnostic sayings gospel (114 logia, recovered at Nag Hammadi) and the Infancy Gospel of Thomas (the miracle-working boy Jesus) — so the early condemnations of "the Gospel of Thomas" (Hippolytus, Origen, Eusebius, Cyril of Jerusalem, the Gelasian Decree) are routinely read as the rejection of the infancy text. That equation is weak. Where the target is identifiable it is the sayings gospel or a Manichaean book of that title — Hippolytus quotes its saying 4 and ties it to the Gnostic Naassenes; Cyril names a "Gospel of Thomas" written by "one of the three wicked disciples of Manes"; Eusebius brackets it among the heretics' apostolic-pseudonym forgeries (Peter, Thomas, Matthias). Only Origen's bare list and Eusebius's catalogue-name are loose enough to admit the infancy text, and an undescribed title identifies nothing. The witness who does attest the infancy material — Irenaeus, on the boy-Jesus alphabet tale (Against Heresies 1.20.1) — never calls it "Thomas" or a "gospel"; and the Gelasian list itself registers the Manichaean "Gospel of Thomas" and "the book on the infancy of the saviour" as separate entries. The infancy gospel was excluded too — but on its own, partly different evidence; the famous "Gospel of Thomas" rejections should not do double duty.16
