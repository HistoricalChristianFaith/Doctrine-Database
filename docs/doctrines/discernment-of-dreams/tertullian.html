Tertullian on the discernment of dreams

Dates: c. 210 · Wikipedia
Summary: In A Treatise on the Soul (De Anima) 45–49 Tertullian gives the earliest sustained Christian anatomy of sleep and dreams, sorting them by source into a fourfold division — from demons (most), from God, from the soul itself, and from the pure ecstatic state — and warns that a dream's turning out true does not fix its origin.

Claims & evidence

Most dreams are sent by demons — yet may still come true

We declare, then, that dreams are inflicted on us mainly by demons, although they sometimes turn out true and favourable to us.

— A Treatise on the Soul 47, ANF 3 (also newadvent.org). The concession that demonic dreams "sometimes turn out true" is the doctrinal hinge of the whole tradition: truth of content is no proof of divine source. Earlier (ch. 46) he notes even the pagans knew this — "Aristotle, while expressing his opinion that dreams are in most cases untrue, yet acknowledges that there is some truth in them" — and that the diabolical spirits "beset their human prey with their fantasies not only in their chapels but also in their chambers."

God sends dreams — He pours out His Spirit that His servants should see visions

But from God—who has promised, indeed, "to pour out the grace of the Holy Spirit upon all flesh, and has ordained that His servants and His handmaids should see visions as well as utter prophecies"—must all those visions be regarded as emanating, which may be compared to the actual grace of God, as being honest, holy, prophetic, inspired, instructive, inviting to virtue…

— A Treatise on the Soul 47 (conflating Joel 2:28 / Acts 2:17). Tertullian adds that "it was, indeed by an inspiration from God that Nebuchadnezzar dreamt his dreams; and almost the greater part of mankind get their knowledge of God from dreams" — the God-source is real and, he thinks, common.

A third source: the soul creating dreams for itself — and a residual ecstatic class

The third class of dreams will consist of those which the soul itself apparently creates for itself from an intense application to special circumstances.

— A Treatise on the Soul 47. Beyond these three he leaves a fourth, residual category: dreams "which evidently proceed neither from God, nor from diabolical inspiration, nor from the soul… will have to be ascribed in a separate category to what is purely and simply the ecstatic state." He had already defined that state (ch. 45): "This power we call ecstasy, in which the sensuous soul stands out of itself, in a way which even resembles madness."

Because sources differ, dreams must be tested, not taken at face value

So much for the dreams to which credit has to be ascribed even by ourselves, although we must interpret them in another sense.

— A Treatise on the Soul 46. Tertullian's own settled allegiance is stated flatly at ch. 49 — "We, however, derive dreams from God" — but the fourfold scheme is exactly what makes discernment necessary: since the demonic dream can wear the look of the divine and even come true, its content cannot certify it.

Notes

  • Tertullian is also the earliest witness on the sibling question of dream-apparitions of the dead, where the same book (De Anima 57) argues that what necromancy "brings up" is a demonic counterfeit, not the departed soul — and states the parallel epistemic rule: "things are not true because they appear to be so, but because they are fully proved to be so."
  • The fourfold division (demons / God / the soul / pure ecstasy) is stated entirely within ch. 47; the surrounding chapters (45–49) supply the theory of sleep and ecstasy that frames it.
  • His contested orthodoxy. The De Anima is a work of Tertullian's Montanist years, and whether his turn to the “New Prophecy” made him a heretic — he was never tried or condemned by any synod — is weighed on the timeline “Was Tertullian a heretic?”.
