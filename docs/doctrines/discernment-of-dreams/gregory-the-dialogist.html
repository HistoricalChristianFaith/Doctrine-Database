Gregory the Great on the discernment of dreams

Dates: c. 593 · Wikipedia
Summary: In Dialogues IV.48 Gregory gives the tradition's most-cited classification — six kinds of dream — and makes its decisive point: the evil source mixes truth into falsehood to win credit, so that because dreams "grow from such divers roots" they are the harder to trust, and only holy men discern true revelation from illusion "by a certain inward spiritual taste."

Claims & evidence

Six kinds of dream

Concerning this point, Peter, you must understand that there are six kind of dreams. For sometime they proceed of too much fulness or emptiness of the stomach: sometime by illusion: sometime both by thought and illusion: sometime by revelation: and sometime both by thought and revelation. The two first all by experience know to be true: and the four latter we find mentioned in holy scripture.

— Dialogues IV.48, tr. P.W. 1608 (ed. Gardner, 1911) (also historicalchristian.faith). Gregory's six collapse into the tradition's three sources: the body (a full or empty stomach), the devil (illusion, and thought joined to illusion), and God (revelation, and thought joined to revelation). He anchors the revelation-dreams in Scripture — Joseph's dream of exaltation (Gen. 37), the angel warning Joseph to flee to Egypt (Matt. 2:13), Nebuchadnezzar (Dan. 2) — and the thought-mixed dreams in "Dreams follow many cares" (Eccles. 5:2).

The wicked spirit foretells true things — to ensnare the soul with one lie

But seeing dreams do grow from such divers roots, with so much the more difficulty ought we to believe them: because it doth not easily appear unto us, from what cause they do proceed. Holy men, indeed, by a certain inward spiritual taste, do discern betwixt illusions and true revelations… so that they know what they receive from the good spirit, and what they suffer by illusion from the wicked: and therefore, if our mind be not herein very attentive and vigilant, it falleth into many vanities, through the deceit of the wicked spirit: who sometime useth to foretell many true things, that, in the end, he may by some falsehood ensnare our soul.

— Dialogues IV.48. This is the doctrine's sharpest single formulation of why a true-seeming dream cannot be trusted on its content: the deceiver deliberately salts his illusions with truths to earn belief, then springs "some falsehood." The safeguard is not a rule of thumb but the trained spiritual discernment of the holy — an "inward spiritual taste."

Scripture itself warns against trusting and observing dreams

For if dreams did not sometime proceed by illusion from our secret enemy, never would the wise man have said: Dreams have made many to err, and hoping in them have they been deceived: and again: [Ye] shall not be soothsayers, nor observe dreams: by which words we see how they are to be detested, that are compared with sooth-sayings.

— Dialogues IV.48, quoting Ecclesiasticus (Sirach) 34:7 and the Law's prohibition of observing dreams (cf. Lev. 19:26). Gregory sets the deuterocanonical warning "Dreams have deceived many" beside the Torah's ban on dream-divination to make dream-observance a species of forbidden soothsaying — the con-side of the scriptural tension, weighed against the many God-sent dreams he had just catalogued.

Notes

  • Gregory's use of Sirach 34:7 puts a deuterocanonical text at the load-bearing centre of the doctrine — a datum for the wiki's interest in the Old Testament canon and the authority the Fathers granted the "ecclesiastical" books in practice.
  • In the same book of the Dialogues Gregory takes the departed to appear as themselves and solicit the living's prayers; see his detail page on dream-apparitions of the dead. The two treatments sit together: dreams are a real channel, and even the true ones must be sifted from the illusory.
