Athanasius of Alexandria on the discernment of dreams

Dates: c. 318 · Wikipedia
Summary: In Against the Heathen (Contra Gentes) 31–33 Athanasius makes the dreaming soul's own activity — ranging beyond the sleeping body — a proof of its rationality and immortality, which grounds the tradition's "natural" source of dreams: much of what we dream is simply the soul's own operation, not a message from without.

Claims & evidence

In sleep the soul ranges beyond the body, contemplating what is not before the eyes

Often, for example, when the body is lying on the earth, man imagines and contemplates what is in the heavens.

— Against the Heathen 31, NPNF2 4. Athanasius is arguing that the soul is a rational substance distinct from the body: it thinks and beholds things the bodily senses cannot reach.

The sleeping soul travels, meets acquaintances, and forecasts — evidence it outlives the body

Often when the body is quiet, and at rest and asleep, man moves inwardly, and beholds what is outside himself, travelling to other countries, walking about, meeting his acquaintances, and often by these means divining and forecasting the actions of the day.

— Against the Heathen 31. The dream is here treated as the soul's own motion while the body lies still — and (§§32–33) offered as demonstration that the rational soul is immortal, since it acts independently of the body it inhabits. For the discernment tradition this supplies the third, natural source in Tertullian's and Gregory's schemes: not every vivid or even predictive dream need be ascribed to God or to a demon; the immortal soul, loosed in sleep, ranges on its own.

Notes

  • Athanasius does not himself set out to sort dreams by source; his interest is the soul's immortality. But his account of the dreaming soul's independent activity is precisely what later writers (Tertullian's "the soul itself apparently creates for itself," Gregory's stomach-born dreams, Aquinas's "inward cause") mean by the natural or bodily origin of dreams — the source that is neither divine revelation nor demonic illusion.
  • The same passage, in taking the soul's dream-ranging as proof it survives death, bridges to dream-apparitions of the dead and the intermediate state.
