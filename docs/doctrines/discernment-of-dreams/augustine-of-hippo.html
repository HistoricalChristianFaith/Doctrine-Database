Augustine of Hippo on the discernment of dreams

Dates: c. 415 · Wikipedia
Summary: In The Literal Meaning of Genesis (De Genesi ad litteram) Book XII Augustine sets out the classic threefold division of vision — corporeal, spiritual, intellectual — locates dreams in the middle, "spiritual" (imaginative) kind, and there states the discernment problem at its sharpest: a good or an evil spirit may impress the same images, and telling them apart is "exceedingly difficult," because a deceiving spirit can be calm, truthful, and useful precisely in order to seduce.

Book XII of De Genesi ad litteram survives in free form only in the original Latin (linked below); the standard English translations are J. H. Taylor, The Literal Meaning of Genesis (ACW 41–42, 1982), and E. Hill, On Genesis (WSA I/13, 2002). The blockquotes below are the verbatim Latin; a literal English rendering follows each.

Claims & evidence

Three kinds of vision: corporeal, spiritual, intellectual

Primum ergo appellemus corporale, quia per corpus percipitur et corporis sensibus exhibetur. Secundum spiritale; quidquid enim corpus non est et tamen aliquid est, iam recte spiritus dicitur… Tertium vero intellectuale, ab intellectu.

— De Genesi ad litteram XII.7.16, Latin Wikisource. In English: "Let us then call the first corporeal, because it is perceived through the body and presented to the bodily senses. The second, spiritual; for whatever is not a body and yet is something is rightly called spirit… The third, truly intellectual, from the intellect." Augustine illustrates the three with a single verse (XII.6.15): reading "love your neighbour as yourself," the letters are seen corporeally, the neighbour thought of spiritually, love beheld intellectually. Dreams and ecstasies belong to the middle, spiritual (that is, imaginative) kind — images of bodies seen when the bodily senses are at rest.

Spiritual (dream) images can be impressed by a spirit, evil or good

Cum autem… vel commixtione cuiusquam alterius spiritus seu mali seu boni, ita corporalium rerum in spiritu exprimuntur imagines, tamquam ipsis corporis sensibus corpora praesententur…

— De Genesi ad litteram XII.12.25. In English: "But when — whether by too intense a concentration of thought, or by some force of illness… or by the admixture of some other spirit, whether evil or good — images of corporeal things are so expressed in the spirit that bodies seem presented to the very senses of the body…" Augustine explicitly lists the causes of such dream-images: the mind's own overwork, bodily illness, and the action of a spirit (good or evil). And these visions, "whether in dreams or in ecstasy" (sive in somnis sive in ecstasi, XII.12.26), when they show what a body could not show, must be shown "by some spirit."

The discernment is "exceedingly difficult" — the deceiving spirit speaks true to seduce

Discretio sane difficillima est, cum spiritus malignus quasi tranquillius agit… quando etiam vera dicit, et utilia praedicat, transfigurans se, sicut scriptum est, velut angelum lucis, ad hoc ut cum illi in manifestis bonis creditum fuerit, seducat ad sua.

— De Genesi ad litteram XII.13.28. In English: "The discernment, however, is exceedingly difficult, when a malign spirit acts as it were more tranquilly, and… says what it can; when it even says true things and foretells useful things, transfiguring itself, as it is written, as an angel of light, so that, once it has been believed in these manifest goods, it may seduce [men] to its own ends." This is the pivotal admission of the whole doctrine: Augustine concedes that a demonic dream can be calm, true, and even accurately predictive — so neither serenity nor fulfilment certifies its source. When a good spirit gives such visions "there is no doubt at all… it is the gift of God"; the trouble is that the counterfeit is built to look exactly like the gift.

Notes

  • The "angel of light" figure is 2 Corinthians 11:14, which Augustine, Cassian, and the monastic tradition all lean on: the deceiver's tactic is not crude falsehood but mixing true and useful things into the deception — the same mechanism Gregory names ("foretell many true things, that… he may by some falsehood ensnare our soul").
  • Augustine's fuller word on whose act a dream-figure is — and why the dead ordinarily do not appear of their own power — is in On Care to Be Had for the Dead; see his detail page on dream-apparitions of the dead. There too the rule is that a true dream-vision is worked "by angelical operations… permitted from above," a false one by demonic deception.
