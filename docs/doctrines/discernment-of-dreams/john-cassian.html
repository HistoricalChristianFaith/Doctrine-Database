John Cassian on the discernment of dreams

Dates: c. 426 · Wikipedia
Summary: In the Conferences Cassian carries the three-source scheme into the monastery as the "discernment of thoughts": every thought and nocturnal impression comes from God, the devil, or ourselves, and the monk must be a "good money-changer" testing each — for the devil counterfeits the true coin, "transforming himself into an angel of light."

Claims & evidence

Three origins of our thoughts: from God, from the devil, from ourselves

Above all we ought at least to know that there are three origins of our thoughts, i.e., from God, from the devil, and from ourselves… a whole range of thoughts springs from the devil, when he endeavours to destroy us either by the pleasures of sin or by secret attacks, in his crafty wiles deceitfully showing us evil as good, and transforming himself into an angel of light to us… But they arise from ourselves, when in the course of nature we recollect what we are doing or have done or have heard.

— Conferences I.19 (First Conference of Abbot Moses), NPNF2 11 (also newadvent.org). This is Tertullian's and Gregory's taxonomy transposed from dreams to the monk's inner life — and it turns on the same danger, the devil "deceitfully showing us evil as good."

Be a "good money-changer": test which coin bears the true king's image

WE ought then carefully to notice this threefold order, and with a wise discretion to analyse the thoughts which arise in our hearts… so that we may… become good money-changers, whose highest skill and whose training is to test what is perfectly pure gold and what is commonly termed tested… and not only shrewdly to recognize coins stamped with the heads of usurpers, but with a still shrewder skill to detect those which have the image of the right king, but are not properly made…

— Conferences I.20. The counterfeit that most endangers the soul is not the obvious falsehood but the coin that bears "the image of the right king" and yet is "not properly made" — the demonic dream or thought that looks holy and true. Cassian illustrates it with Abbot John of Lycon, who took the devil "in the shape of a filthy Ethiopian" for a genuine command and so "was deceived by a counterfeit coin."

The monk Heron: an angel of Satan received "as an angel of light"

the old man Heron, only a very few days ago was cast down by an illusion of the devil from the heights to the depths… And deceived by this presumption he received with the utmost reverence an angel of Satan as an angel of light and with blind slavishness obeyed his commands and cast himself down a well… nothing doubting of the promise of the angel who had assured him that the merits of his virtues and labours were such that he could not possibly run any risk.

— Conferences II.5 (Second Conference of Abbot Moses), NPNF2 11 (also newadvent.org). The nocturnal illusion is not harmless: believed, it kills. This is why Cassian makes discretion "the mother of all virtues."

The safeguard: never trust your own judgment — submit every thought to the elders

True discretion… is only secured by true humility. And of this humility the first proof is given by reserving everything (not only what you do but also what you think), for the scrutiny of the elders, so as not to trust at all in your own judgment… For a man cannot possibly be deceived, who lives not by his own judgment but according to the example of the elders…

— Conferences II.10. Where Augustine had called the discernment "exceedingly difficult," Cassian offers the practical remedy: because "Satan himself transforms himself into an angel of light" (Conf. XVI.11), the dream or thought must be "kept for the consideration of some more experienced brother or approved Elder," lest "we… venerate in our thoughts an angel of darkness instead of an angel of light."

Notes

  • Cassian treats dreams chiefly under the broader head of logismoi (thoughts/impressions), of which nocturnal illusions are a species; the Conferences' dedicated conference "On Nocturnal Illusions" (Abbot Theonas, Conf. XXII) concerns chastity in sleep and was left untranslated by the Victorian editors, so his usable teaching on dream-discernment is the three-origins / money-changer material of Conferences I–II and XVI.
  • The "angel of light" motif (2 Cor. 11:14) links Cassian directly to Augustine's transfigurans se… velut angelum lucis and forward to Gregory's wicked spirit who "foretell[s] many true things" to ensnare.
