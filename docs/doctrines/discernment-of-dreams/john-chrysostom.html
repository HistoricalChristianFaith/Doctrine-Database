John Chrysostom on the discernment of dreams

Dates: c. 390 · Wikipedia
Summary: Preaching on Matthew's infancy narrative, Chrysostom affirms the God-sent dream as a real vehicle of revelation but observes that God calibrates the mode of revelation to the recipient's faith — the strong believer gets a dream, the weaker or more doubtful an open vision.

Claims & evidence

God guides Joseph by a dream because his faith needed no more

And why not openly, as to the shepherds, and to Zacharias, and to the Virgin? The man was exceedingly full of faith, and needed not this vision. Whereas the Virgin, as having declared to her very exceeding good tidings, greater than to Zacharias, and this before the event, needed also a marvellous vision; and the shepherds, as being by disposition rather dull and clownish.

— Homilies on the Gospel of St. Matthew, Homily IV.10 (on Matt. 1:20–21), NPNF1 10. Chrysostom takes the dream not as a lesser or doubtful channel but as one fitted to a strong faith: precisely because Joseph "was… full of faith," a quiet dream sufficed where the doubting Zacharias and the "dull and clownish" shepherds were met with open angelic appearances. Revelation is graded to its hearer.

The dream comes upon Joseph mid-thought — a guided, not a random, dream

While he thought on these things, the angel appears in a dream.

— Homilies on Matthew, Homily IV.10. Chrysostom stresses the timing: the angel comes as Joseph is turning the matter over, so the dream answers a real deliberation. Where Matthew's Joseph is repeatedly steered by dreams (1:20; 2:13, 19–22), Chrysostom reads each as a genuine divine communication — the pro-revelation pole of the tradition — while his "fitted to the recipient" principle quietly implies the corollary the wider doctrine insists on: a dream carries authority by its source and its fruit, not merely by being a dream.

Notes

  • In the homilies on the flight into Egypt (Hom. VIII, on Matt. 2:11–16) Chrysostom's stated reason for God working "hiddenly" — by dreams and quiet providences rather than open miracle — is to safeguard belief in Christ's true humanity: "For if from His earliest infancy He had shown forth wonders, He would not have been accounted a Man." The dream is thus part of the economy of the hidden Incarnation, not a second-class revelation.
  • Chrysostom does not here supply an explicit "test every dream" caution; his contribution is on the God-sent side of the ledger — that even genuine divine dreams are apportioned by God to the measure of the recipient. The discernment against demonic and bodily dreams is carried most sharply by Augustine, Cassian, and Gregory.
