Thomas Aquinas on the discernment of dreams

Dates: c. 1271 · Wikipedia
Summary: In the Summa Theologiae II-II, q. 95, a. 6 Aquinas gathers the patristic taxonomy into a decision procedure: dreams have an inward cause (the soul's own thoughts, or the body's disposition) and an outward cause, "corporal and spiritual" — the spiritual either from God through the angels or from the demons — and divining by dreams is lawful only so far as they come from God or nature, unlawful when it rests on the demons.

Claims & evidence

The inward cause of dreams: the soul's thoughts and the body's disposition

The inward cause of dreams is twofold: one regards the soul… those things which have occupied a man's thoughts and affections while awake recur to his imagination while asleep… the inward cause of dreams regards the body: because the inward disposition of the body leads to the formation of a movement in the imagination consistent with that disposition; thus a man in whom there is abundance of cold humors dreams that he is in the water or snow.

— Summa Theologiae II-II, q. 95, a. 6, Benziger tr.. This is Athanasius's dreaming soul and Gregory's stomach-born dreams made precise: the two natural sources, the mind's residue and the body's humoral state.

The outward cause: corporal (the heavens) and spiritual (God's angels, or the demons)

The outward cause of dreams is twofold, corporal and spiritual. It is corporal in so far as the sleeper's imagination is affected either by the surrounding air, or through an impression of a heavenly body… The spiritual cause is sometimes referable to God, Who reveals certain things to men in their dreams by the ministry of the angels… Sometimes, however, it is due to the action of the demons that certain images appear to persons in their sleep.

— Summa Theologiae II-II, q. 95, a. 6. Aquinas thus preserves the whole tradition's roster of sources — God (through angels), demons, and nature (bodily and celestial) — and orders them under the inward/outward distinction.

The rule: lawful from God or nature, unlawful from the demons

There is no unlawful divination in making use of dreams for the foreknowledge of the future, so long as those dreams are due to divine revelation, or to some natural cause inward or outward… But it will be an unlawful and superstitious divination if it be caused by a revelation of the demons.

— Summa Theologiae II-II, q. 95, a. 6. The taxonomy that began as description becomes a moral criterion: it is the source, not the accuracy, that makes reliance on a dream licit or forbidden — the scholastic settlement of the epistemic worry the Fathers had pressed from Tertullian onward, that a demonic dream may come true.

Notes

  • Aquinas cites Scripture on both sides in the article — the God-sent dreams (Job 33:15, Numbers 12:6) against the prohibitions — and resolves them exactly by the source-test: the ban falls on dreams "caused by a revelation of the demons," not on those God or nature sends.
  • The same source-and-permission logic governs Aquinas's answer on dream-apparitions of the dead: the separated soul is "naturally cut off from communication with the living," appearing only "according to the disposition of Divine providence" — nature versus the extraordinary act of God, mapped onto the dream's sources here.
