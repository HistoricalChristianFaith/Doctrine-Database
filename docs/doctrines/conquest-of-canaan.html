The Historicity of the Conquest of Canaan

The book of Joshua tells of Israel entering Canaan after the Exodus and taking the land — Jericho, Ai, the southern and northern campaigns, and the settlement of the tribes. The New Testament and the whole Christian tradition treat this as real history (Acts 7:45; 13:19; Hebrews 11:30–31). This page gathers the external, non-biblical evidence adduced for it — Egyptian inscriptions and the archaeology of the Levant — and weighs, honestly, the proposed synchronisms with Joshua and Judges. It is a companion to the belief-timelines elsewhere on this wiki but a different kind of page: the subject is not how a doctrine developed across the Fathers, but what the ground and the monuments of the ancient Near East can and cannot tell us about whether the events happened — closer in kind to the historicity questions taken up on Noah's Flood and the height of Goliath.

Seven data points anchor the case, and the timeline below sets them out earliest-first, each dated by its own artifact rather than by a person: the destruction of Jericho (~1550 BC on the mainstream date, contested), the fiercely burnt "City IV" at Tell es-Sultan whose fallen mudbrick wall and untouched grain stores are pressed against Joshua 6 — though its date, early (~1400) or Middle Bronze (~1550), is the crux of whether it is Joshua's Jericho at all; the Soleb inscription of Amenhotep III (~1400 BC), whose topographical list names a "land of the Shasu of Yhw" — read by many as the earliest extra-biblical appearance of the divine name; the Amarna Letters (~1350 BC), the Egyptian diplomatic archive in which Canaanite city-kings beg Pharaoh for help against roving ʿApiru (Habiru) bands; the destruction of Hazor (~1230 BC), the violent, human-caused Late Bronze burn layer at the greatest Canaanite city — the one city Joshua is said to have "burned with fire"; the Merneptah Stele (~1208 BC), the earliest extra-biblical mention of "Israel" as a people already in Canaan; the Iron I highland-settlement horizon (~1200 BC), the sudden appearance of hundreds of small new villages in the central hill country that many read as the material footprint of early Israel; and, within that horizon, the Mount Ebal structure (~1200 BC), a ramp-approached, unhewn-stone cultic installation on the mountain of the covenant curses, filled with the burnt bones of young sacrificial animals, which its excavator identified as the altar of Joshua 8.

The honest shape of the case must be stated with it. The strongest correlations — above all the identification of the Habiru with the Hebrews, and the reading of Shechem, handed over in Joshua without a recorded battle (Joshua 8; 24), against the archaeological picture — depend on accepting a contested early-date (~1400 BC) chronology for the Exodus and conquest, which is not the mainstream scholarly position; the more widely held late-date (~1250 BC) scheme and, more sharply, the dominant secular reconstruction read Joshua largely as a later origin narrative rather than as reportage, and take the highland villages as the settling of an indigenous population rather than an invading one. The individual synchronisms, and the early-date chronology the tightest of them rest on, are weighed adversarially in the Arguments section below; the timeline carries the attestations themselves, and the arguments adjudicate what they do and do not establish.

Related doctrines: Biblical history & archaeology — see also the historicity of Sennacherib's siege of Jerusalem (the same kind of page: the external, non-biblical evidence weighed against a biblical historical event — there, the Assyrian annals, the Lachish reliefs, and Hezekiah's siege-preparation archaeology of 701 BC).

Timeline

The dated external attestations are set out earliest first. Each carries its artifact's date on its own detail page; the contested synchronism theses they bear on are weighed below under Arguments.

The destruction of Jericho — a fortified city burned, its granaries full (c. 1550 BC, disputed)

The timeline opens with a burn layer in the ground — and with the fiercest dating dispute in the whole file. Tell es-Sultan, beside the perennial spring above the Jordan valley, is biblical Jericho beyond dispute, and its excavators — John Garstang (1930–36) and Kathleen Kenyon (1952–58) — agree that a heavily fortified Bronze Age city there, the stratum labelled "City IV," ended in a fierce fire: walls and floors blackened, every room filled with fallen brick and timber, a burnt-debris layer a yard or more thick across the dig. Two features are what conquest-readers press — a stretch of the mudbrick city wall found fallen outward down the slope, and storage jars still full of grain, marking a city taken quickly and, strikingly, not plundered (cf. the ban on spoil, Joshua 6:17–18) — a profile of walls down, city burned, and nothing taken that lines up point-for-point with Joshua 6.1 That much of the destruction is common ground. What is not — and it is the whole question of whether this is Joshua's Jericho — is the date: Kenyon redated City IV's fall to c. 1550 BC (the end of the Middle Bronze Age), and radiocarbon on short-lived grain from the very burn layer returned c. 1562 ± 38 BC, against the c. 1400 BC that Garstang and, later, Bryant Wood defend for a conquest correlation. It is slotted here at the mainstream ~1550 BC figure — the earliest datum on the page — and early-date readers place it at ~1400 BC instead; either way the destruction is the same, and it is the date that decides everything. That dating fight is weighed below under Arguments, not settled here.2

The Soleb inscription — "the land of the Shasu of Yhw" (c. 1400 BC)

The earliest inscribed datum is a name carved in stone in Nubia. On a column of Amenhotep III's temple at Soleb (c. 1400–1390 BC), a list of "lands of the Shasu" — the semi-nomads Egyptian sources place in the Edom–Midian–Negev fringe — includes one ring read tŕ šŕsw yhwŕ, "the land of the Shasu of Yhw." It is widely held to be the oldest extra-biblical appearance of the divine name YHWH anywhere, roughly four centuries before any biblical text was written, and a second, abbreviated occurrence turns up at the related temple of Amarah West under Ramesses II.3 Read soberly it is external, independently-dated evidence that a Semitic group tied to the name Yahweh existed in the right geographical zone in the Late Bronze Age — shared ground among scholars. The further step from "Shasu of Yhw" to early Israel or the conquest (and, conversely, the rival thesis that Yahwism originated among these desert Shasu) is an interpretive one, weighed below under Arguments, not asserted here.4

The Amarna Letters — the Habiru, and Shechem handed over (c. 1360–1332 BC)

The next window is an Egyptian filing cabinet. The Amarna Letters — some 380 cuneiform tablets found in 1887 at Tell el-Amarna, the diplomatic archive of Akhenaten's court — preserve the appeals of the Canaanite city-kings to Pharaoh across roughly 1360–1332 BC, as their towns fall to roving ʿApiru (Habiru) bands. ʿAbdi-Ḥeba of Jerusalem reports flatly that "the land of the king deserted to the Ḫapiru" and that "the ʿApiru have plundered all the lands of the king," begging repeatedly for archers; and he denounces Labʾayu, ruler of Shechem, for "giving the land of Šakmu to the Hapiru" — the great central-hill city passing to the raiders without a recorded Egyptian battle, just as Shechem is never stormed in Joshua.5 That much is common ground: the archive attests a real, broad collapse of the Egyptian vassal order in mid-fourteenth-century Canaan. The further steps — that these Habiru are the biblical Hebrews, and that Shechem's handover answers to the covenant assembly of Joshua 8 and 24 — are contested inferences, resting on the same early-date chronology, and are weighed below under Arguments, not asserted here. Mainstream scholarship treats "Habiru" as a social designation for uprooted, stateless, and outlaw people used across the whole ancient Near East, not an ethnonym and not a name for Israel.6

The destruction of Hazor — the one city Joshua "burned with fire" (c. 1230 BC)

Between the Amarna archive and the Merneptah Stele lies a burn layer in the ground. Tel Hazor, in Upper Galilee, was the greatest Canaanite city in the land — "the head of all those kingdoms" (Joshua 11:10) — and both its excavators, Yigael Yadin (1955–58) and Amnon Ben-Tor (from 1990), found that Late Bronze Hazor ended in a single, ferocious fire, hot enough to crack its basalt masonry, after which the vast lower city was permanently abandoned. Statues of Canaanite gods and Egyptian kings had been deliberately decapitated and their hands cut off — the mark, Ben-Tor argues, of a human assault, not an accident.7 This is the one thing the text predicts of Hazor and of Hazor alone: of the northern cities Israel took, "Israel burned none of them, save Hazor only" (Joshua 11:13) — so it is the single place Scripture calls for a burn layer at the largest Canaanite city, and a massive, human-caused burn layer at the largest Canaanite city is exactly what was found. That correlation is the agreed datum. The two further steps — who caused the fire, and which of the site's Late Bronze destructions (the well-attested 13th-century one, or an earlier ~15th-century burn) answers to Joshua — are contested inferences, bound up with the early-date chronology, and are weighed below under Arguments, not asserted here.8

The Merneptah Stele — "Israel is laid waste, its seed is not" (c. 1208 BC)

The first time Israel is named in any source outside the Bible is on a slab of black granite. On the Merneptah Stele — a victory monument of Pharaoh Merneptah, carved in the fifth year of his reign (c. 1208 BC) and found by Flinders Petrie at Thebes in 1896 — a hymn celebrating a Libyan war closes with a strophe on a Canaan campaign that names Ashkelon, Gezer, and Yanoam, and then: "Israel is laid waste, its seed is not."9 The datum that carries the weight is grammatical: where Ashkelon, Gezer, and Yanoam take the Egyptian determinative for a city, the name Israel takes the determinative for a people — so the stele attests, from an external and independently dated source, a people called Israel already in Canaan by the late thirteenth century BC. What it does not supply is a battle report for Joshua: it is a terminus, not evidence of the conquest event itself, and its date sits after both the early-date (~1400 BC) and late-date (~1250 BC) conquest schemes alike. All mainstream views agree Israel was in Canaan by Merneptah's day; the dispute — how it got there and when — is weighed below under Arguments, not settled here.10

The Iron I highland-settlement horizon — hundreds of new villages in the hill country (c. 1200 BC)

The last datum is not an inscription but a fact of demography and dirt. Around the collapse of the Late Bronze Age, a wave of small, unwalled new villages springs up across the central hill country between Jerusalem and Shechem — from roughly 25 highland villages in the Late Bronze Age to over 300 by the end of Iron I, the settled population doubling from about 20,000 to 40,000. Their material culture shows a recurring cluster of traits — near-absence of pig bones (sharpest beside the pork-eating Philistine cities of the coast), the four-room "Israelite" house, collared-rim storage jars, and a striking lack of temples and idols.11 That much is agreed: a hill country thinly held in the Late Bronze Age is, within a few generations, thickly settled by villagers whose plain, egalitarian pattern differs from both the Canaanite towns and the coastal Philistines. The contested step is what the cluster means. Its pottery develops out of Late Bronze Canaanite forms rather than importing a foreign repertoire, so the dominant reconstruction reads the settlers as substantially indigenous — Israel emerging from within Canaan rather than invading it — and no single trait is exclusively "Israelite." Whether the horizon is the material footprint of the conquest, of a peaceful settling, or of an internal social upheaval is weighed below under Arguments, not asserted here.12

The Mount Ebal structure — a ramp-approached altar filled with sacrificial bone (c. 1200 BC)

The last datum is a stone installation within that same highland horizon. On the north-eastern slope of Mt. Ebal — the mountain Deuteronomy 27 assigns to the covenant curses and on which Joshua 8:30 has Joshua build an altar — Adam Zertal excavated (1982–1989) a large rectangular structure of unhewn field-stones, roughly 9 × 7 m, filled with earth and ash rather than floored, approached by a stone ramp rather than steps, built over an earlier round installation and then deliberately buried around 1140 BC after less than a century of use. In and around it lay a heavy ash layer and more than three thousand burnt animal bones — young, mostly male sheep, goats, and cattle, with no pig — which the zooarchaeologist Liora Kolska Horwitz read as the debris of sacrifice.13 That the site is an early Iron I cultic installation, not a dwelling, is broadly agreed — even Amihai Mazar and, reportedly, Israel Finkelstein grant its ritual character. What is not agreed is the identification: is this the specific altar of the covenant ceremony of Joshua 8:30–35, a more generic highland cult place (Finkelstein; Na'aman's cult site of Shechem), or — on the minority readings it once drew — a watchtower (Kempinski) or farmstead? Unlike Jericho and Ai, the case does not hinge on the early date — an Iron I altar fits the mainstream late date — but the step from “a highland altar c. 1200 BC” to “Joshua's altar” is drawn from the text, and is weighed below under Arguments, not asserted here.14

Arguments

The load-bearing claims cut across the timeline rather than sitting at one date, and each is weighed adversarially on its own page with an assessment of the interpretation it serves: whether the Habiru of the Amarna Letters are the Hebrews; whether Shechem's battle-less handover in Joshua answers to the Amarna archive; whether Hazor's Late Bronze burn is the fingerprint of the Israelite conquest or of an internal revolt; whether the Iron I highland villages bear a distinct material "signature" of early Israel; whether the ramp-approached stone installation on Mt. Ebal is the covenant altar of Joshua 8; whether Soleb's "Shasu of Yhw" preserves the divine name; whether the broken third name-ring on the Berlin statue-pedestal reads an "Israel" two centuries before Merneptah; whether the Deir ‘Alla plaster inscription's "Balaam son of Beor" corroborates the biblical seer of Numbers, and through him the conquest narrative; whether Jericho's burnt "City IV" fell c. 1400 BC (as an early-date Joshua needs) or c. 1550 BC; whether Joshua's Ai is to be relocated from the empty mound at et-Tell to nearby Khirbet el-Maqatir; whether the Late Bronze destruction layers at the cities Joshua names cluster into one synchronized campaign at all; and, foundational to the tightest correlations, whether the early-date (~1400 BC) Exodus and conquest chronology holds against the mainstream late-date and non-historical readings. That foundational question leads — two grouped rival advocacy pages, the early date and the late date, read first because the tightest synchronisms hold only on the early one; the two Amarna synchronisms that inherit it follow, grouped with it; then the single-datum arguments — who destroyed Hazor, the Iron I material "signature," the Mount Ebal altar, Soleb's "Shasu of Yhw," the disputed "Israel" name-ring on the Berlin pedestal, and the Deir ‘Alla "Balaam son of Beor" attestation; and last among the historicity arguments the two site-correlation cases that do hinge on the early date — the dating of Jericho's destruction and the identification of Ai — and the general multi-site test they feed, whether the Late Bronze destruction layers cluster into one Joshua campaign at all. A final argument shifts axes entirely — from whether the conquest happened to how the Church answered its moral problem, the God-commanded ḥerem (Origen's allegory and Augustine's just war) — and is kept separate at the end.

Related arguments — the conquest date

The foundational chronology dispute, told as two rival advocacy cases. Read literally, the biblical numbers (1 Kings 6:1; Judges 11:26) place the conquest c. 1400 BC; the store-city name "Rameses" (Exodus 1:11) and the Egyptological consensus place it c. 1250 BC. Each camp brings its own proof-text and its own defenders, and each is weighed on its own best case; a shared mainstream doubt — that no external invasion happened at all — is a defeater folded into both. This question leads the doctrine because the tightest synchronisms below hold only on the early date, so their force is conditional on how it is answered.

Does the conquest date to c. 1400 BC (the early date)? (assessment: contested)

The early-date case — contested: biblical chronology, read at face value — the 480 years of 1 Kings 6:1 and Jephthah's 300 years in Judges 11:26 — favours a fifteenth-century entry (Exodus c. 1446 BC, conquest c. 1406 BC), and the plain sense of those numbers does not dissolve into symbolism. But it is a minority position, its one archaeological prop (Wood's re-dated Jericho) is weak, and it stands against the Egyptology behind the late date. Advanced by Bryant Wood and Associates for Biblical Research, Titus Kennedy, Douglas Petrovich, John Bimson, and Eugene Merrill. This is the linchpin the Amarna synchronisms below depend on.

Does the conquest date to c. 1250 BC (the late date)? (assessment: contested)

The late-date case — contested: the store-city name "Rameses" (Exodus 1:11) is a nineteenth-dynasty toponym pointing the oppression to the thirteenth century, and this is the majority position among scholars who grant a historical Exodus — a real point in its favour. But its field-archaeology backbone (the thirteenth-century destruction "cluster") largely dissolves site by site, the "Rameses" reading has a live rebuttal, and it must set aside the plain biblical numbers that favour the early date. Advanced by Kenneth Kitchen and the broad Egyptological consensus.

Related arguments — the Amarna synchronisms

Two proposed synchronisms drawn from a single archive: the Amarna Letters' picture of a Canaan collapsing to the ʿApiru, and of Shechem's king Labʾayu handing his city over to them without an Egyptian battle. One asks whether those Habiru are Israel; the other whether Shechem's battle-less handover answers to Joshua's un-besieged Shechem and its covenant assembly. They rest on the same two premises — the Habiru identification and the early date above — and are best read as a set.

Are the Habiru of the Amarna Letters the biblical Hebrews? (assessment: weak)

The Habiru = Hebrews identification — weak as external proof of the invasion: the strong equation rests on a contested etymology, treats a trans-regional social term as though it were an ethnonym, and holds only on the early date; the softer reading — that Israel emerged from the ʿapiru milieu — is plausible on its own merits but points toward emergence within Canaan, not an invasion of it.

Was Shechem handed over without a battle — Labʾayu (EA 289) and Joshua 8 & 24? (assessment: contested)

The Shechem-handover synchronism — contested: Joshua never fights for Shechem, yet Israel holds the city and convenes the whole nation there for covenant renewal (Joshua 8:30–35; 24:1), and the Amarna archive independently shows Shechem's own king siding with the ʿApiru rather than resisting them (EA 289) — so the letters plausibly illuminate the silence. But the strong claim that EA 289 records the Joshua-24 assembly reads a rival king's hostile accusation as a covenant transfer, and stacks the (weak) Habiru identification on the (contested) early date.

Who destroyed Canaanite Hazor — an Israelite conquest, or an internal revolt? (assessment: contested)

The Hazor-destroyer question — contested: that Hazor's violent Late Bronze burn, with its methodically beheaded and hand-severed Canaanite and Egyptian statues, is the fingerprint of an external Israelite conquest is coherent and dovetails with the one city Joshua singles out to burn (Joshua 11:13). Ben-Tor, the excavator, reaches "Israel" by elimination — ruling out Egypt (its own royal images were defaced), the Sea Peoples (no coastal pottery inland), a rival Canaanite city, and an internal revolt. But his own co-director, Sharon Zuckerman, reads the same selective, battle-debris-free destruction as an internal "termination ritual," not an invasion — and beneath the whole dispute lies a ceiling: the ground can narrow the suspects but cannot itself name the destroyers "Israel"; that comes from Joshua 11 alone. A genuine, impressive correlation with the text; not an independent proof of the agent.

Do the Iron I highland villages bear a material "signature" of early Israel? (assessment: contested)

The material-culture "signature" — contested: the recurring Iron I trait-cluster (near-absence of pig bones, the four-room house, collared-rim jars, no temples or idols) is sound as evidence that a distinct highland group — plausibly early Israel — existed by c. 1200 BC, and it dovetails with the Merneptah "Israel"; but it is weak as evidence of an invasion, because the pottery's continuity with Late Bronze Canaan and the non-exclusivity of every single trait are the mainstay of the rival emergence-within-Canaan models. "Marks early Israel" (defensible) is not "proves the conquest" (unsupported by the cluster alone).

Is the Iron I structure on Mount Ebal the covenant altar of Joshua 8? (assessment: contested)

The Mount-Ebal-altar identification — contested, splitting cleanly at two joints: that Zertal's ramp-approached, unhewn-stone installation on Mt. Ebal, filled with the burnt bones of young sacrificial animals, is a genuine early Iron I cult place is well grounded and near-consensus (Mazar, and reportedly Finkelstein, grant it; the "watchtower" and "farmstead" readings do not survive the bone deposit), and, unlike Jericho and Ai, it needs no early date — an Iron I altar fits the mainstream late date. But that it is specifically Joshua's altar of Joshua 8:30–35 is disputed: the identification is read in from the text (nothing in the stones says "Joshua"), the northern-slope location sits awkwardly against the Deuteronomy 27 blessing-and-cursing ceremony, and serious scholars (Finkelstein, Na'aman's cult site of Shechem) grant the cult place while denying the specific altar. A genuine, impressive correlation on a firmly cultic site; the ground shows Israel worshipped here, but "Joshua's altar" comes from Joshua 8, not the stones. (The separate "curse tablet" recovered from the excavation dump is rated weak and is no part of the altar case.)

Does Soleb's "Shasu of Yhw" attest the divine name — and early Israel? (assessment: contested)

The Soleb "Shasu of Yhw" argument — contested, splitting at its two joints: that the name-ring preserves the divine name YHWH (c. 1400 BC, the oldest extra-biblical occurrence) is plausible, arguably sound — accepted by Astour, van der Toorn, and Redford alike; but the leap to Israel/the conquest is weak — the ring names Shasu bedouin of the Edom–Midian fringe, not Israel, and the same datum grounds the rival Midianite/Kenite origin of Yahwism, which points the other way.

Does the Berlin pedestal relief (ÄM 21687) name "Israel" before Merneptah? (assessment: weak)

The Berlin-pedestal "Israel" reading — weak: on a broken Egyptian statue-pedestal in Berlin, two intact name-rings read Ashkelon and Canaan — two of the three Levantine names that recur on the Merneptah Stele — and Görg, van der Veen, and Theis reconstruct the damaged third ring as an archaic Israel, dated on orthographic grounds two centuries before Merneptah. The hook (the Merneptah-like sequence) is real, but as proof of a pre-Merneptah Israel the reading fails on its best case: it needs a reconstructed sign in the broken ring (unique, even restored); its sibilant is š where Merneptah's Israel has ś (Hoffmeier's objection); the object is a 1913 dealer purchase with no find-spot, so its "pre-Merneptah" dating is uncontrolled; and Robert Ritner's 2020 refutation reads the name as Assyria instead. A live minority reading defended by serious scholars, not a falsehood — but it cannot bear the weight of "earliest Israel." The firm earliest attestation remains the Merneptah Stele (c. 1208 BC).

Does the Deir ‘Alla inscription's "Balaam son of Beor" corroborate the conquest narrative? (assessment: weak)

The Deir ‘Alla "Balaam son of Beor" argument — weak as evidence for the conquest, though it splits cleanly at its joint. A plaster wall-inscription from Tell Deir ‘Alla in the Jordan Valley (c. 880–770 BC) opens with "the account of Balaam son of Beor, the man who was a seer of the gods" — the same name, patronymic, calling, and Transjordan region as the seer of Numbers 22–24, and it even shares the divine title Shadday(in) with Balaam's own oracles. As an external attestation of the figure and a regional Balaam tradition behind the biblical episode this is plausible-to-sound. But as proof of the conquest it is weak: the text postdates any conquest by ~500 years, recounts decidedly different events (a night-vision of a divine council, not the Balak episode), and portrays a pagan seer of the gods, so it confirms neither the biblical narrative nor whether Israel took Canaan. Bryant Wood's leap — that a real Balaam makes "Balak, Moses, Joshua, and… the events described authentic" — is a non-sequitur the scholarship on the inscription rejects: the text's literary character bars adding Balaam to the "biblical people confirmed by archaeology," and what it really shows is a shared Transjordanian storytelling tradition. A genuine touchpoint of the Numbers world; not a proof of the conquest.

Was Jericho's "City IV" destroyed c. 1400 BC (Wood) or c. 1550 BC (Kenyon)? (assessment: contested)

The Jericho-dating question — contested, but leaning against the c. 1400 BC date: everyone agrees the fortified "City IV" at Tell es-Sultan was violently burned, and the fight is purely when. Bryant Wood's revival of Garstang's c. 1400 BC date rests on a real but minority reading of the pottery; three of his four props are weak or withdrawn (the scarabs date a cemetery not the city, the stratigraphy only restates the pottery call, and his 1410 BC radiocarbon figure collapsed on a known lab calibration error), while high-precision radiocarbon of short-lived charred grain from the burn layer itself returned c. 1562 ± 38 BC — the single strongest, most independent datum on the site, and it points to the Middle Bronze Age. The flagship instance of the chronology dispute below: if City IV fell c. 1550 BC, no fortified Jericho stood for any proposed conquest, early or late, to destroy.

Is Joshua's Ai to be identified with Khirbet el-Maqatir rather than et-Tell? (assessment: weak)

The Ai-identification question — weak: here the starting datum is a problem for the historicity case, not evidence for it. The mound long identified as Ai, et-Tell, had no Middle or Late Bronze city for Joshua 7–8 to destroy — its own excavators (Albright, Marquet-Krause, Callaway) found only a ruined Early Bronze town, which the dominant reading takes to make the Ai narrative an etiological legend for a heap whose name means “the ruin.” The conservative answer relocates Ai to nearby Khirbet el-Maqatir, a small Late Bronze I fortress that was burned; its correspondences are real hooks (a small site, smaller than Gibeon, a north gate, an LB I burn in the right region), but the identification stands only by importing the contested early (~1400 BC) date and an ad hoc migrating-toponym harmonization for Genesis 12:8, it names a border fortress rather than a royal city, and even sympathetic supporters (Joel Kramer) have defected. The companion site-correlation case to the dating of Jericho's City IV, and, like it, conditional on the chronology weighed last.

Do the Late Bronze destruction layers cluster into a single Joshua campaign? (assessment: weak)

The destruction-layers-cluster question — weak for the strong claim that the burnt Late Bronze cities Joshua names line up into one synchronized conquest. This is the empirical backbone of W. F. Albright's 1930s "13th-century conquest" model — four sites with violent burn layers (Tell Beit Mirsim = Debir, Beitin = Bethel, Lachish, and later Yadin's Hazor) — and the general, multi-site test that the Jericho, Hazor, and Ai cases all feed. Once the identifications and dates are corrected the cluster comes apart: Debir (Tell Beit Mirsim) is misidentified and drops out (biblical Debir is Khirbet Rabûd, untouched by these waves); Bethel (Beitin) is a disputed site whose burn is early-twelfth-century and plausibly Philistine; Lachish fell ~1130 BC as a still-Egyptian city its own excavator credits to the Sea Peoples; and the destructions as a set scatter across three waves and a century-plus, with mostly non-Israelite causes. The early-date (~1400 BC) camp does not rescue the synchronism — it concedes the 13th-century cluster and relocates the conquest, inheriting the contested chronology and the weak Jericho/Ai correlations. So the layers are not positive proof of a unified conquest event; the individual burns keep whatever weight their own pages give them.

Do the patristic defenses answer the moral problem of the ḥerem? (assessment: contested)

A different axis from all the arguments above: not whether the conquest happened, but how the Church answered the moral difficulty it raises. The moral-problem-of-the-ḥerem argument — contested: the book of Joshua has God command the ḥerem, the ban or devotion-to-destruction of the Canaanite cities (Deuteronomy 20:16–17; Joshua 6:21), and the objection that this is irreconcilable with a good God runs from Marcion and the Manichaean Faustus to modern critics. The two most influential Christian replies were given early — Origen's allegorical reading, which transposes the slaughter into the believer's interior warfare against sin and so declines to affirm a literal extermination, and Augustine's divine-command / just-war reading (Contra Faustum 22), which defends the commanded wars as "not cruelty but righteous retribution," locates the real evils of war in the dispositions of the heart, and grounds legitimacy in proper authority (the fountainhead of Western just-war theory). Both are serious and coherent, and this wiki does not count the divine-command defense out on the premise that God cannot righteously command death; but the two answers pull against each other, the allegorical route sidesteps the literal history this page otherwise weighs, and the sharpest edge of the objection — the killing of non-combatants on the authority of the command — is where ancient and modern critics still press. A weighty, still-live defense, genuinely disputed; not settled either way.
