Clement of Alexandria on universal restoration (apokatastasis)

Dates: c. 200 · Wikipedia
Summary: Clement, head of the Alexandrian catechetical school and Origen's predecessor in it, supplies the scriptural and philosophical seed of the later hope of restoration. He does not state a finished doctrine that all will be saved, but he holds two things that the doctrine is built from: that all God's punishments are corrective, never merely retributive — medicine aimed at the sinner's conversion, not vengeance — and that this discipline continues after death, the wayward being chastised “till they become ashamed and repent.” He reads the descent of Christ to Hades as a saving preaching to those who died before, and holds that God “saves all,” some by teaching and some by correction. The universal terminus that Origen and Gregory of Nyssa would draw is left implicit; the mechanism is already here.

Claims & evidence

God's punishments are corrective and saving, aimed at repentance, not death

… then all who believe shall be saved, although they may be of the Gentiles, on making their profession there; since God's punishments are saving and disciplinary, leading to conversion, and choosing rather the repentance than the death of a sinner; and especially since souls, although darkened by passions, when released from their bodies, are able to perceive more clearly, because of their being no longer obstructed by the paltry flesh.

— Stromata VI.6, source. The setting is the descent of Christ to Hades to preach the Gospel to the dead. Clement's premise — that God chastises “choosing rather the repentance than the death of a sinner” (an echo of Ezekiel 18:23, 33:11) — makes divine punishment therapeutic in principle: it exists to convert, not to destroy. Once that is granted, and granted that the dead can still perceive and repent, the door to a post-mortem recovery of even the unbaptized stands open. This is the corrective-punishment axiom on which the whole later apokatastasis rests.

Even after death, the wayward are chastised by “paternal admonitions” until they repent

But if, like the deaf serpents, they listen not to the song called new, though very old, may they be chastised by God, and undergo paternal admonitions previous to the Judgment, till they become ashamed and repent, but not rush through headlong unbelief, and precipitate themselves into judgment.

— Stromata VII.16, source. Clement is writing of the heretics he has been refuting; his wish for them is not damnation but that they be corrected “previous to the Judgment.” The chastisement is paternal — the discipline of a father for a child — and its stated aim is that they “become ashamed and repent.” He goes on to distinguish these “partial corrections, which are called chastisements,” from the final judgment, treating remedial suffering as a distinct and prior thing. This is the seed Origen would develop into a graded, age-long purification of every rational creature.

Notes

  • Seed, not finished doctrine. Clement nowhere states in so many words that all will finally be saved, and it would overreach to enlist him as a flat universalist. What he supplies is the two load-bearing premises — corrective (not vindictive) punishment, and its continuation beyond death — from which the later hope is drawn. Whether the developed patristic doctrine was already the church's reading in the second century, or was a later construction read back onto authors like Clement who “state far less,” is exactly the point contested by Ilaria Ramelli and Michael McClymond; see the summary's arguments.
  • Clement appears on this wiki also as a witness on the purgatory and intermediate-state timelines, where the same corrective-fire and Hades-preaching passages do related work.
  • The corrective-punishment reading was pressed the other way by later opponents from Matthew 25:46 (“these shall go away into eternal punishment”); on that counter-text see Basil of Caesarea and Augustine.
