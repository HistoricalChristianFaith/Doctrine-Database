Basil of Caesarea on universal restoration (apokatastasis)

Dates: c. 375 · Wikipedia
Summary: Basil the Great is enlisted as the counter-witness from within the same Cappadocian circle that produced the doctrine's boldest advocate, his brother Gregory of Nyssa. In a monastic rule he answers those “who say that the punishment will be without end” by pressing the parallel grammar of Matthew 25:46: if “eternal punishment” has an end, so must “eternal life,” and he calls the notion of an end to punishment “a demonic deceit.” But the case is doubly hedged: the passage's authenticity is scholarly-contested (Ilaria Ramelli argues it is a later interpolation), and the same Basil, with Gregory of Nazianzus, compiled the Philocalia that preserved Origen. So he is a real but qualified witness that the eternalist reading was pressed against the restoration in the fourth century.

Claims & evidence

“If the punishment should once end, the heavenly life would necessarily have to end too”

…it is a demonic deceit that many people forget so frequent and clear words and statements of our Lord and invent a kind of an end of the punishments so that they may have more courage to sin. For if the punishment should once end, the heavenly life would necessarily have to end too. But if we cannot bear the mere thought of it when speaking about the life in heaven, how then can we speak about the end of the eternal suffering, if as the former so the latter is preceded by the word ‘eternal’ — ‘and these will go away,’ says the Lord, ‘into eternal punishment, but the righteous into eternal life.’

— Regulae brevius tractatae (Shorter Rules), Q. 267, source (Greek: PG 31.1264–65). This is the classic eternalist proof from Matthew 25:46: the two clauses share the one adjective (Greek aiōnios), so a temporary hell forces a temporary heaven. Basil adds that the varying “severe” and “light” beatings of Luke 12:47–48 mark a difference among torments, “not an end” of them. It is the argument every later opponent of the restoration would return to.

Notes

  • The authenticity caveat is essential, not decorative. Ilaria Ramelli argues that Reg. brev. 267 is most likely a later interpolation into Basil's Asceticon — a corpus whose question-and-answer transmission is demonstrably prone to accretion — noting that its charge (that those who deny eternal punishment are under “a demonic deceit”) would implicitly condemn Basil's own revered teacher Origen and his own siblings Macrina and Gregory of Nyssa, all of whom held the restoration. (Ramelli, “St Basil and Apokatastasis”) Others regard the interpolation thesis as special pleading. Either way, Basil-as-opponent rests on this one contested text, and the page presents it as contested.
  • The Philocalist. With Gregory of Nazianzus, Basil compiled the Philocalia of Origen, the anthology preserving the Greek of parts of Peri Archōn. (Lewis's 1911 introduction) His relation to Origen was thus that of an editor and admirer who nonetheless (if the rule is genuine) drew the line at universal restoration.
  • The Matthew 25:46 parallelism Basil presses is the same counter-text that Augustine and the later councils would set against the restoration; the doctrine's advocates answer that “eternal” (aiōnios) can denote an age-long, not strictly endless, duration.
