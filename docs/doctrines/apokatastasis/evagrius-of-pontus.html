Evagrius of Pontus on universal restoration (apokatastasis)

Dates: c. 345–399 · Wikipedia
Summary: Evagrius, the desert monk and disciple of the Cappadocians who systematized the tradition of contemplative prayer, gives the restoration its most speculative form. In the Kephalaia Gnostica (Gnostic Chapters) he teaches that all rational beings (the logikoi), fallen from an original unity, will at the last be reunited in the knowledge of the Trinity — worlds dissolved, distinctions abolished, “all… gods.” It is this Evagrian scheme, more than anything in Origen's own text, that the sixth-century councils actually anathematized. Evagrius was among those condemned by name in the reception of the Second Council of Constantinople (553), alongside Origen and Didymus.

Claims & evidence

In the final Unity, distinctions of rank vanish and “all will be gods”

In second natural contemplation, it is said that some are leaders, and others are submissive to leaders, of necessity. But, in the Unity there will not be those who are leaders, neither those who are submitted to leaders; but all will be gods.

— Kephalaia Gnostica IV.51 (unreformed Syriac S2), tr. Luke Dysinger, source. Evagrius's cosmology runs from an original Unity (henad), through a fall or “movement” that shatters it into the graded world of bodies and ranks, to a final reunification in which every rational being is restored to equality in the knowledge of God. The end here answers the beginning — Origen's structure — but pressed to a stricter equality: at the consummation the hierarchy of “leaders” and “submissive” that governs the present order simply ceases.

At the end, worlds are destroyed and bodily distinctions dissolved into equality of knowledge

Accompanying the knowledge that concerns the logikoi are the destruction of worlds, the dissolution of bodies, and the abolition of names; while [there] remains the equality of knowledge according to the equality of substances.

— Kephalaia Gnostica II.17 (unreformed Syriac S2), tr. Luke Dysinger, source. The Kephalaia Gnostica survives complete only in Syriac, and in two recensions: an expurgated version (S1) with the Origenist content softened away, and the unreformed version (S2), recovered and edited by Antoine Guillaumont, which preserves statements like this one. Greek fragments confirm S2's priority. This is the text on which the reconstruction of Evagrian “Origenism” chiefly rests.

Notes

  • Reserve about the outcome. As Dysinger observes, Evagrius “appears to have avoided ever stating openly that ‘all will be saved’”; the restoration is carried implicitly, through the logikoi returning to their primordial state, rather than announced as a doctrine of universal salvation. Ilaria Ramelli nonetheless reads him as a central heir of Origen's apokatastasis who speaks of it “rather overtly” (Ramelli, The Christian Doctrine of Apokatastasis, Brill 2013; and her translation of the unreformed Kephalaia Gnostica, SBL 2015).
  • The condemned system. The fifteen anti-Origenist anathemas of 553 describe a scheme — undifferentiated minds, a fall into graded bodies, a final state in which every spirit is equal — that matches the Kephalaia Gnostica far more closely than it matches Peri Archōn. Whether the councils therefore condemned an Evagrian system rather than Origen himself is weighed on the condemnation timeline's argument on that question.
  • Condemned by name. Evagrius is not named in the surviving conciliar canons of 553 (which name Origen), but the council's reception — Cyril of Scythopolis, and the definitions of the Third Council of Constantinople (680–81) and Nicaea II (787) — condemns “Origen, Didymus, and Evagrius” together. See Didymus the Blind, condemned with him.
