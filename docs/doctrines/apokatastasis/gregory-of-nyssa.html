Gregory of Nyssa on universal restoration (apokatastasis)

Dates: c. 380 · Wikipedia
Summary: Gregory of Nyssa, the youngest of the three great Cappadocians, gives the fullest and boldest patristic statement of universal restoration — and he is the decisive witness for the doctrine's respectability, because he taught it in the same terms as Origen, drew it from the same verse (1 Corinthians 15:28), carried it to its hardest edge (the restoration even of the evil spirits), and was never condemned: canonized a Father of the Church, named among the “holy Fathers” by the Seventh Ecumenical Council. On his account evil has no eternal existence; the purgative fire is medicine, not vengeance; and “after long periods of time” the whole rational creation is restored to its primal state and joins in one harmony of thanksgiving.

Claims & evidence

“God all in all” means the complete annihilation of evil — nothing left outside the good

But He that becomes “all” things will be “in all” things too; and herein it appears to me that Scripture teaches the complete annihilation of evil. If, that is, God will be “in all” existing things, evil, plainly, will not then be amongst them… The excepting of that one thing, evil, mars the comprehensiveness of the term “all.”

— On the Soul and the Resurrection, source. This is the exegetical heart of the doctrine, and it is Origen's: read 1 Corinthians 15:28 at full strength, and a province of the rational creation permanently outside God would leave God less than “all in all.” Gregory takes the phrase to require that evil, having no independent substance, be finally and wholly consumed — not that any nature be destroyed, but that the corruption mixed into it be burnt away.

Even the evil spirits will at last confess Christ's Lordship

…when evil shall have been some day annihilated in the long revolutions of the ages, nothing shall be left outside the world of goodness, but… even from those evil spirits shall rise in harmony the confession of Christ's Lordship.

— On the Soul and the Resurrection, source. This is the “hardest edge” that later councils would anathematize when they met it in Origen — the restoration reaching even the demonic powers — and Gregory states it without flinching, reading Philippians 2:10 (“things under the earth”) alongside 1 Corinthians 15. That a canonized Father held it in these words, and was never censured, is the single strongest datum that as a doctrine the restoration was tolerated long after Origen.

Purgation is medicine: the surgeon's fire, not the executioner's

…when, after long periods of time, the evil of our nature, which now is mixed up with it and has grown with its growth, has been expelled, and when there has been a restoration of those who are now lying in Sin to their primal state, a harmony of thanksgiving will arise from all creation…

— The Great Catechism 26, source. Gregory's controlling image is the physician who cauterizes: the patient “may be angry with the doctors, and wince with the pain of the incision; but if recovery of health be the result… they will feel grateful.” The suffering is real and can be agonizing, but its end is healing, and in the same chapter he applies it even to “the author of evil” himself, whose purgation “becomes a blessing to that nature, though the separation is agonizing.” This is the therapeutic frame that distinguishes his restoration from mere release of the damned.

Notes

  • The decisive uncondemned witness. The argument that universal restoration was condemnable in Origen's own day founders on Gregory: he teaches the same conclusion, from the same verse, to the same extremity, more than a century later and is honoured as a saint. See the summary's argument on whether the doctrine kept reputable company and, on the condemnation timeline, “Did Origen teach universal restoration as a doctrine, and was it condemnable in his day?”
  • Medicinal, not satisfactory. Gregory's purgative fire heals a nature; it does not discharge a debt of punishment for the elect alone. He therefore grounds the fire strand of later purgatory while diverging sharply from the Latin satisfactory doctrine — the caveat spelled out on his purgatory page.
  • Gregory's sister Macrina, his interlocutor in On the Soul and the Resurrection, voices the doctrine in the dialogue; his brother Basil is the family's apparent counter-voice, though the authenticity of Basil's one anti-universalist text is itself disputed.
