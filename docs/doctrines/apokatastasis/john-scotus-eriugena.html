John Scotus Eriugena on universal restoration (apokatastasis)

Dates: c. 800–877 · Wikipedia
Summary: Eriugena, the Irish philosopher of Charles the Bald's court and the boldest speculative mind of the Carolingian age, is the last of the patristic universalists — the figure at whom Ilaria Ramelli ends her eight-century history of the doctrine. His masterwork, the Periphyseon, builds a whole cosmology on the movement of all things out from God (exitus) and their return to God (reditus): the entire created order at last flows back to its source, God becomes “all in all,” and nothing of created nature is annihilated — only evil, which has no substance, is excluded. Read (rightly or wrongly) as pantheism, the Periphyseon was condemned in the thirteenth century and burned by papal order — a late echo of the sentence on Origen.

Claims & evidence

The universal return: all nature flows back to God, who becomes “all in all”

There is a general return of all things to God. Corporeal things will return to their incorporeal causes, the temporal to the eternal, the finite will be absorbed in the infinite.

— summarizing Periphyseon Book V, Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, source. For Eriugena all of nature is a single dynamic process: it proceeds from God through the primordial causes into the world of effects, and it returns by the same steps to God. Beyond this general return, the blessed attain a special return — deification (theosis), the soul merging with God “as lights blend into the one light… as a droplet of water merges with the stream.” The consummation is Origen's and Gregory's read cosmologically: God will be all in all, “when there will be nothing except God alone.”

God annihilates no nature; only substance-less evil is excluded

The return does not destroy what returns. God, on Eriugena's account, annihilates nothing of nature: the integrity of each created thing is preserved in its return, as air is not destroyed when it is wholly suffused with sunlight. Hell and heaven are accordingly not places but states — the real hell being the deprivation of the vision of God — and punishment falls on the perverse will, trapped in its own false images (phantasiai), not on any nature; for evil has no true substance and, having no substance, cannot itself endure into the restored creation.1 This is the same instinct as Gregory of Nyssa's “complete annihilation of evil” — no created thing lost, no corruption kept — carried through with philosophical rigor.

Notes

  • Verbatim caution. No free, stable English translation of the Periphyseon circulates online; the standard translation (Sheldon-Williams / O'Meara) is in copyright. The doctrine is therefore presented here from the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy's account of Book V rather than from a primary blockquote, and the wording of any Latin line (e.g. the omnia in omnibus passage) has not been verified against the critical edition. The substance — the universal reditus, the non-annihilation of nature, God all in all — is not in scholarly doubt.
  • The last patristic universalist. Ramelli closes The Christian Doctrine of Apokatastasis: A Critical Assessment from the New Testament to Eriugena (Brill, 2013) with him, placing him at the end of the line Origen → the Cappadocians → Evagrius → Pseudo-Dionysius → Maximus → Eriugena.2
  • Condemned late. The Periphyseon was linked with the pantheism of Amalric of Bène and condemned at the Council of Paris (1210); in 1225 Pope Honorius III ordered copies burned; and it was later placed on the Index of Prohibited Books, where it remained until the Index was abolished.3 The charge was pantheism rather than universal restoration as such — but the reditus is inseparable from the system condemned, and the sentence is the medieval West's last act against the Origenian hope.
