Maximus the Confessor on universal restoration (apokatastasis)

Dates: c. 630 · Wikipedia
Summary: Maximus, the greatest Greek theologian of the seventh century, is the master of reserve. Writing after 553, when the bolder forms of the restoration stood condemned, he neither affirms nor denies it but keeps what he calls an “honourable silence.” He carefully distinguishes three senses of apokatastasis, endorsing the restoration of the soul's powers (the sense “Gregory of Nyssa especially loves”) while declining to commit the fuller doctrine to writing — “a better and more secret explanation… we… will honour by silence.” Whether that reticence conceals a quiet universalism or a genuine agnosticism is one of the liveliest disputes in modern patristics.

Claims & evidence

Three senses of restoration — the soul's powers restored, as Gregory taught

The third meaning [of apokatastasis] is used by Gregory especially in reference to the qualities of the soul that had been corrupted by sin and then are restored to their original state.

— Quaestiones et dubia (PG 90:796A–C), as rendered by Hans Urs von Balthasar, source. Maximus lists three things the church means by “restoration”: the return of each person to virtue in this life; the resurrection restoration of human nature to incorruption; and the restoration of the fallen powers of the soul to their created condition — the sense he assigns to Gregory of Nyssa. Read carefully, he grants that every soul will at the end regain the knowledge of the good without necessarily regaining participation in it — a distinction that lets him honour Gregory while stopping short of asserting that all are saved. (The passage is numbered I.13 in Declerck's edition, 19 in the older Deconinck numbering; it is one text, not two.)

The fuller doctrine he will “honour by silence”

…a better and more secret explanation, which is kept in the minds of the mystics, but we, as well, will honour by silence.

— Quaestiones ad Thalassium (on the restoration), tr. Andreas Andreopoulos, source. This is the “honourable silence” that gives the reading its name. Elsewhere in the same work (Ad Thalassium 43) Maximus says outright that “the deeper secrets of the divine doctrines must not be committed to writing.” He plainly knows a fuller teaching about the end; he equally plainly declines to set it down — a posture that only makes sense if the fuller teaching is one that could not, after 553, be safely taught in the open.

Notes

  • A genuinely contested case. Scholars divide four ways. Brian Daley reads the silence as real reserve — Maximus neither was nor meant to be a universalist (Daley, “Apokatastasis and ‘Honorable Silence’…,” 1982; The Hope of the Early Church, 1991). Hans Urs von Balthasar reads him as hopeful but not assertoric (Cosmic Liturgy; Dare We Hope). Ilaria Ramelli enrolls him in the apokatastasis tradition outright (The Christian Doctrine of Apokatastasis, 2013). A minority (e.g. Ignatius Green) denies any universalism at all. The page presents his position as reserve, and the debate over what the reserve conceals as unsettled.
  • Verbatim caution. The three-senses passage is quoted in Balthasar's rendering (a verbatim English translation of PG 90:796); the securely-attested wording of the “honourable silence” and “not… committed to writing” lines follows Andreopoulos and Constas respectively. The Quaestiones et dubia and the Quaestiones ad Thalassium are distinct works and are kept distinct here.
  • Maximus stands in the same generation as Isaac of Nineveh — the Greek reserve and the Syriac boldness are the two seventh-century faces of a hope the councils had driven underground but not out. Both feed the tradition Ramelli traces to its terminus in Eriugena. The same reserve underlies his refutation of the Origenist fall of minds in Ambiguum 7, told on the timeline of Origen's condemnation: there he dismantles the pre-existence of souls while keeping an “ever-moving rest” in which the end surpasses the beginning — an eschatology built to answer the anathemas from within.
