Gregory of Nazianzus on universal restoration (apokatastasis)

Dates: c. 381 · Wikipedia
Summary: Gregory the Theologian is the guarded witness — neither an advocate like his friend Gregory of Nyssa nor a flat opponent. Preaching on baptism he lists the fires of Scripture and then allows, without endorsing, that “some may prefer… to take a more merciful view” of the last fire — a door left deliberately ajar rather than a doctrine taught. Elsewhere he speaks of a “last Baptism… more painful and longer” that “consumes the stubble of every evil,” language a purgative reading can lean on but which he does not draw out to universal restoration. With Basil he compiled the Philocalia, the anthology that preserved much of Origen in the original Greek — so his reticence sits within a lifelong, careful engagement with the very source of the hope.

Claims & evidence

A “more merciful view” of the last fire may be taken — permitted, not asserted

…and one even more fearful still than these, the unquenchable fire which is ranged with the worm that dieth not but is eternal for the wicked. For all these belong to the destroying power; though some may prefer even in this place to take a more merciful view of this fire, worthily of Him That chastises.

— Oration 40.36 (On Holy Baptism), source. The careful grammar is the whole point. Gregory has just described the unquenchable fire as “eternal for the wicked” and belonging to “the destroying power”; then, rather than close the question, he concedes that a reader may “prefer… a more merciful view… worthily of Him That chastises” — that is, a view on which the fire is corrective, fitting a God who disciplines. He neither adopts that view nor forbids it. This studied openness — the hope held as permissible speculation, not preached as doctrine — is Nazianzen's characteristic posture, and it is why he is claimed cautiously by later universalists and just as cautiously by their opponents.

Notes

  • Do not conflate the two fire-texts. The harsher passage is a different oration: in Oration 39.19 (On the Holy Lights, source) Gregory warns that the unrepentant “will be baptized with Fire, in that last Baptism which is more painful and longer, which devours wood like grass, and consumes the stubble of every evil.” That the fire “consumes the stubble of every evil” can be read purgatively, but in context it is a threat, not a promise of restoration; the two orations should be weighed together, not merged.
  • The Philocalia. Gregory and Basil, during their retreat in Pontus (late 350s), compiled the Philocalia of Origen, selections from Origen's works that preserve the Greek of parts of Peri Archōn (notably Book III, on free will) otherwise surviving only in Latin. (Lewis's 1911 introduction) The two chief guardians of Origen's text thus stand on either side of the restoration question — Gregory guardedly open, Basil (in one contested text) opposed.
  • He is the brother-in-arms of the doctrine's boldest advocate, Gregory of Nyssa, and of its family counter-voice, Basil.
