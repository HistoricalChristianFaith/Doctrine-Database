Didymus the Blind on universal restoration (apokatastasis)

Dates: c. 313–398 · Wikipedia
Summary: Didymus, blind from early childhood and for some fifty years the head of the catechetical school of Alexandria, was in his own day a celebrated teacher — Jerome sat at his feet and called him “the Seer.” He was also a thoroughgoing Origenist who held the pre-existence of souls and the final restoration of all. For a long time his restoration teaching could only be reported at second hand; but the commentaries recovered in the 1941 Tura papyrus find let it be read in his own words, and they show him reading the “eternal” punishment of Scripture as a bounded, remedial correction and the end as a return of all rational creatures to God. After the Origenist controversy his memory was repudiated: he was anathematized, with Origen and Evagrius, in the reception of the sixth-century condemnation and by name at the councils of 680–681 and 787.

Claims & evidence

“Eternal” punishment is bounded, not everlasting

In his Commentary on Job — one of the works recovered at Tura, and so a securely genuine text — Didymus takes apart the word the whole doctrine of an endless hell rests on. Aiōnios, he argues, does not by itself mean “everlasting”: it means “beginningless and endless” only of God, and elsewhere carries a bounded sense, so that the “aiōnios” punishment of the Gospel need not be eternal at all:

It must be noted that αιώνιος has several meanings: in the expression, ‘αιώνιος God,’ it means ‘beginningless and endless’; for the divinity is called αιώνιος by virtue of having neither a beginning nor an end of its existence… Indeed, it is laid down concerning the Hebrew who did not wish to be freed in the seventh year, that ‘he will be your slave unto the αιών’: for no slave of a human being remains one forever, even after his death.

— Didymus the Blind, Commentary on Job 76, in Ilaria Ramelli's translation.1 He draws the corollary directly: punishment and death are only “aiōnioi,” belonging to the ages, whereas salvation is “hyper-aiōnios” — it outlasts all the ages; and the fire of judgement, he says elsewhere, will consume not the creatures but the evil in them.

The restoration reaches every rational creature, the wicked included

What the correction is for is a universal return. Commenting on the Father's giving all things into the Son's hand (John 3:35), Didymus states that even the rational beings who have become wicked will finally be restored, once they have put off their evil and put on virtue:

This is said about rational creatures [τῶν λογικῶν]. Since, among all of them, there are also some who have become wicked, know how these will have a restoration [κατάστασιν] once they have arrived in the hands of the Son, obviously after rejecting the evilness [κακίαν] that they had, and assuming virtue [ἀρετήν].

— Didymus the Blind, Commentary on John, fragment 2, in Ramelli's translation.2 This is Origen's conclusion in Origen's own school: a katástasis — a restoration — of the fallen, not merely of the holy. (The John material is a catena fragment, and so a shade less secure than the Tura Job; the two read together, with the Psalms and Zechariah commentaries, make the doctrine unmistakable.)

Jerome's corroboration: Didymus followed Origen on the restoration

The recovered text confirms what his most famous pupil had said with hostility. Jerome, who had studied under Didymus and praised him warmly, turned against him during the Origenist controversy and listed him among those who had swallowed Origen's errors whole. He grants that Didymus was sound on the Trinity and faults him only where he trusted Origen — “we extol… the purity of his faith in the Trinity, while on the other point in which he erred in trusting to Origen we withdraw from him” — and names among those errors the final restoration (apokatastasis) and the pre-existence of souls.3 This is a former pupil turned prosecutor, and should be weighed as such; but it is early, informed, and now confirmed by Didymus's own commentaries.

“In the liberation of all, no one remains a captive” — a commentary of disputed authorship

Didymus is widely quoted, on his teaching of Christ's descent to Hades, as having written that “in the liberation of all no one remains a captive” — that at the Lord's passion the devil alone was injured, losing all the captives he held. The line is genuine to a work that travels under Didymus's name — the Brief Commentary on the Catholic Epistles (In epistolas canonicas brevis enarratio), in its notes on 1 Peter — but that work survives only in the Latin translation Epiphanius Scholasticus made for Cassiodorus, and its Didymian authorship is genuinely disputed: it is frequently cited as Pseudo-Didymus, since the same commentary brands 2 Peter a forgery (falsata) whereas the securely genuine Didymus quotes that epistle as canonical. So the sentence cannot carry the weight of a firsthand witness the way the Tura commentaries can; it is flagged here (⚠) as belonging to a text of contested authenticity, not verified against a securely genuine work.4 It changes nothing that matters, however: the remedial reading of punishment and the restoration of the wicked are already established above from Didymus's own hand, in the Tura Job and Commentary on John.

Notes

  • Scholarly attestation. That Didymus taught remedial punishment and a final universal restoration is the settled reading of modern scholarship — Ilaria Ramelli treats him as a link in the Origenian chain, noting that “like Origen and then Gregory Nyssen… Didymus also included the fallen angels and Satan in the eventual restoration,” and the point is not seriously contested even by those who dispute the maximal “mainstream” thesis.5 With the Tura commentaries on Job and the Psalms now in hand, his witness stands on firsthand footing beside Gregory of Nyssa's, no longer resting on report alone.
  • Condemned with Origen and Evagrius. Didymus's name travels with Origen's and Evagrius's in the anti-Origenist reception. The extant acts of the Second Council of Constantinople (553) do not in fact name him; the report that the assembled fathers anathematized him with Origen and Evagrius is Cyril of Scythopolis's, and his name is then fixed by the definitions of the Third Council of Constantinople (680–681) and Nicaea II (787). The condemnation of the three together is traced on the condemnation of Origen, and it is the sharpest case on the wiki's test of who else held the condemned views and what became of them — a teacher who held both anathematized doctrines, died in the Church's honour, and is still kept as “St. Didymus the Blind” on 18 October in the Prologue of Ohrid.
