Didymus the Blind on universal restoration (apokatastasis)

Dates: c. 313–398 · Wikipedia
Summary: Didymus, blind from early childhood and for some fifty years the head of the catechetical school of Alexandria, was in his own day a celebrated teacher — Jerome sat at his feet and called him “the Seer.” He was also a thoroughgoing Origenist who held the pre-existence of souls and the final restoration of all, and after the Origenist controversy his memory was repudiated: he was condemned, with Origen and Evagrius, in the reception of the Second Council of Constantinople (553). His witness to the restoration rests mainly on the testimony of others — above all the disillusioned Jerome — and on modern scholarship reading his recovered commentaries; a clean firsthand statement is harder to pin down.

Claims & evidence

Jerome: Didymus followed Origen's restoration and pre-existence

Jerome, who had studied under Didymus and praised him warmly, turned against him during the Origenist controversy and listed him among those who had swallowed Origen's errors whole. On Jerome's account Didymus gave an orthodox reading of the Trinity but “accepted without hesitation” Origen's other opinions — the sin of the pre-existent minds, the soul's pre-existence, and the final restoration (apokatastasis).1 This is hostile testimony from a former pupil turned prosecutor, and should be weighed as such; but it is early, informed, and consistent with what the recovered commentaries show.

“In the liberation of all, no one remains a captive” — ⚠ unverified

Didymus is widely quoted, on his teaching of Christ's descent to Hades, as having written that “in the liberation of all no one remains a captive” — that at the Lord's passion the devil alone was injured, losing all the captives he held. The line is transmitted at second hand (partly through Ambrose's Latin) and its modern circulation runs heavily through nineteenth-century Universalist historians; the authenticity of the commentary fragments it is drawn from is disputed. It is reported here, not verified against a critical edition of a securely genuine work, and is best treated as an open lead rather than a firsthand quotation.2

Notes

  • Scholarly attestation. That Didymus taught remedial punishment and a final universal restoration is the settled reading of modern scholarship — Ilaria Ramelli treats him as a link in the Origenian chain, and the point is not seriously contested even by those who dispute the maximal “mainstream” thesis.3 The most secure primary base is the body of commentaries (on Zechariah, Genesis, Psalms, Job) recovered in the 1941 Tura papyrus find; a firsthand apokatastasis passage located there would put his witness on the same verified footing as Gregory of Nyssa's.
  • Condemned with Origen and Evagrius. Didymus's name travels with Origen's and Evagrius's in the anti-Origenist reception — Cyril of Scythopolis, and the definitions of the Third Council of Constantinople (680–81) and Nicaea II (787). The condemnation of the three together is traced on the condemnation of Origen.
