Origen of Alexandria on universal restoration (apokatastasis)

Dates: c. 229 · Wikipedia
Summary: Origen is the fountainhead. In Peri Archōn (On First Principles, c. 229) he builds the whole doctrine on 1 Corinthians 15:28 — that God will finally be “all in all” — and argues that the goodness of God will “recall all His creatures to one end,” the end answering the beginning, so that after “countless and unmeasured ages” every rational nature is restored. He offers it under an announced caution, as one of the questions the apostolic preaching left open for inquiry — and yet argues it, in places, as the necessary consummation of all things. The one thing he insisted he never taught was the restoration of the devil as devil: that, he wrote, only a madman could ascribe to him.

Claims & evidence

The goodness of God will recall all creatures to one end — God “all in all”

We think, indeed, that the goodness of God, through His Christ, may recall all His creatures to one end, even His enemies being conquered and subdued.

— De Principiis I.6.1, source. The whole argument of the chapter “On the End or Consummation” hangs on 1 Corinthians 15:28: when “all enemies will be subdued to Christ, when death — the last enemy — shall be destroyed,” then the kingdom is delivered up to the Father and God is “all and in all.” Origen reads that subjection as a saving submission — the same by which the saints wish to be subject to Christ — not a crushing; a rational creation left permanently divided against God would leave God less than “all in all.”

Restoration comes slowly, over “countless and unmeasured ages,” the end answering the beginning

And this result must be understood as being brought about, not suddenly, but slowly and gradually, seeing that the process of amendment and correction will take place imperceptibly in the individual instances during the lapse of countless and unmeasured ages…

— De Principiis III.6.6, source. In this chapter “On the End of the World” the restoration is not merely floated but described as the term of a long process: “when the end has been restored to the beginning,” all rational souls restored, the body changed “into the glory of a spiritual body,” “then verily God will be ‘all in all.’” The grammar here is the grammar of description, not of a bare question — which is why the claim that Origen held restoration only as a tentative hope is itself contested: he announces caution over the whole class of questions, then argues this one hard.

He denied ever teaching the salvation of the devil as devil

…they declare that I hold that the father of wickedness and perdition, and of those who are cast forth from the kingdom of God, that is the devil, is to be saved, a thing which no man can say even if he has taken leave of his senses and is manifestly insane.

— Origen, letter to friends at Alexandria, preserved in Rufinus, On the Falsification of the Works of Origen, source. Origen complained that opponents had garbled his teaching in public debate and circulated a doctored transcript. The restoration he taught is of rational natures healed of the evil that is no part of their created being; the persistence of the devil as devil — wickedness saved while remaining wickedness — he repudiates as self-contradictory. The distinction is the crux of the two argument pages on the point: whether the later charge misrepresents him, and whether his system nonetheless requires it.

Notes

  • Announced caution, then hard argument. Origen's preface assigns the origin of the soul and the nature of the devil to the questions the Church “has not explained with sufficient clearness,” open for inquiry — and he treats the restoration “in the manner rather of an investigation and discussion, than… of fixed and certain decision.” Whether that reserve makes the restoration a mere hope is weighed on the condemnation timeline's apokatastasis argument. The honest reading: more than a bare hope, less than a fixed dogma.
  • The keystone text is 1 Corinthians 15:28; the doctrine takes its name from the “restitution of all things” (apokatastasis pantōn) of Acts 3:21, and is paired with the reconciliation of “all things” of Colossians 1:20.
  • Origen appears across this wiki on some sixteen timelines — the pre-existence of souls on the origin of the soul, the cleansing fire on purgatory — and his verdicts are traced on the condemnation of Origen. His restoration is the doctrine Gregory of Nyssa would carry uncondemned, and the one the sixth-century councils anathematized.
