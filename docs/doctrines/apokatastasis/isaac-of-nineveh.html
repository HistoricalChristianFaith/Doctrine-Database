Isaac of Nineveh on universal restoration (apokatastasis)

Dates: c. 613–700 · Wikipedia
Summary: Isaac the Syrian, a seventh-century East-Syriac bishop of Nineveh and one of the most beloved of all ascetic writers, carries the restoration in a distinctively mercy-based key. Gehenna, on his account, is real and terrible but not the last word and not God's vengeance: those punished there “are scourged by the scourge of love,” the torment being the anguish of having sinned against a love that never withdraws. To think God created rational beings only to hand them over to endless affliction would be, he says, blasphemy; the kingdom and Gehenna alike “are matters belonging to mercy.” That a saint venerated across every Eastern church — and, since November 2024, in the Roman Martyrology — taught this openly shows the hope survived, and remained holy, centuries after the Greek and Latin condemnations.

Claims & evidence

Those in Gehenna are “scourged by the scourge of love”

I also maintain that those who are punished in Gehenna are scourged by the scourge of love. For what is so bitter and vehement as the punishment of love? I mean that those who have become conscious that they have sinned against love suffer greater torment from this than from any fear of punishment. For the sorrow caused in the heart by sin against love is sharper than any torment that can be.

— The Ascetical Homilies of Saint Isaac the Syrian, Homily 28, source. Isaac relocates hell's fire inside the sinner: it is not that God's love is withdrawn from the damned but that it is present and unbearable to those who have set themselves against it. “It would be improper,” he continues, “for a man to think that sinners in Gehenna are deprived of the love of God.” The same love that is joy to the saved is torment to the lost — a single divine reality met two ways — and the torment is therefore remedial in character rather than merely retributive.

Gehenna is a matter of mercy; to think otherwise is blasphemy

The Kingdom and Gehenna are matters belonging to mercy, which were conceived of in their essence by God as a result of His eternal goodness.

That we should further say or think that the matter is not full of love and mingled with compassion would be an opinion full of blasphemy.

— The Second Part (Discourse 39), tr. Sebastian Brock, source. In the discourses of his Second Part Isaac reasons from the nature of the Creator: “It is not the way of the compassionate Maker to create rational beings in order to deliver them over mercilessly to unending affliction.” God “is not one who requites evil, but He sets evil aright.” Punishment is real but purposive — ordered, like a physician's cautery, to “the advantage that is going to come from all these things” — so that the outcome he expects is the triumph of the kingdom over Gehenna, all creation drawn at last into thanksgiving.

Notes

  • A holy witness, East and West. Isaac is a canonized saint of the Byzantine, Oriental, and Assyrian churches, and in November 2024 was added to the Roman Martyrology of the Latin Church. (Wikipedia) His case parallels Gregory of Nyssa's: a teacher of the restoration whom the church venerates rather than condemns — a datum the “condemnable” framing has to reckon with.
  • Verbatim caution. The “scourge of love” passage is quoted from the Holy Transfiguration Monastery (Miller) translation, where it stands as Homily 28; the older Wensinck numbering places the same homily at 27 or 85, and renders the phrase “scourging of love.” The Second Part quotations follow Sebastian Brock's edition (CSCO 554–555, Peeters 1995), Discourse 39.
  • Isaac wrote in the Church of the East, outside the reach of the Greek anathemas of 553; his transmission of the hope, alongside Maximus the Confessor in the Greek world, shows the doctrine continuing after the councils rather than being extinguished by them.
