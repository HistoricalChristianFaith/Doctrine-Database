Is the pillars-of-Seth legend a displaced Enoch tradition?

Type: source-critical / tradition-history argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: plausible — that Josephus's two-pillars story carries roles Jewish lore elsewhere gives to Enoch — the antediluvian sage who receives heavenly astronomy, writes it down, and preserves it across the coming fire-and-water destruction — is a well-grounded, mainstream observation; the closest formal parallel, in which the sage inscribes his knowledge on two durable media so it survives either catastrophe, is told of Enoch, not the Sethite line. But the stronger claim, that the Sethite version is specifically a displacement of a prior Enochic pillars-legend, does not clearly win out over the reading that Sethite, Enochic, and Egyptian antediluvian-sage strands ran in parallel and cross-pollinated; and the named "pillars of Enoch" is a late (post-1723 Masonic) back-formation, not ancient attestation · advanced in the comparative scholarship on Second Temple "antediluvian sage" traditions (Andrei A. Orlov).

The claim

…although Josephus refers to Seth and his progeny, some features of the "two stelae" story allude to peculiar roles and situations which Jewish lore traditionally associates with the seventh antediluvian patriarch Enoch.

— Andrei A. Orlov, "Overshadowed by Enoch's Greatness: 'Two Tablets' Traditions from the Book of Giants to Palaea Historica," marquette.edu/maqom. The thesis is not that Josephus muddled the two names — he plainly writes "Seth" — but that the functions his Sethites perform (discovering "the science of the heavenly bodies and their order," writing that science down, and inscribing it on two media so it outlasts the twin destructions by fire and water) are the very roles that the Enochic literature reserves for Enoch. On this reading the pillars-of-Seth notice is a Sethite recasting of an older Enochic tradition.1

Who advances it

The observation is a commonplace of the scholarship on Second Temple "antediluvian sage" traditions: several scholars have noted the Enochic coloring of Josephus's Sethite story, and Andrei Orlov sets the Sethite and Enochic aetiologies side by side at length, tracing the "two tablets" motif from the Qumran Book of Giants through to the Byzantine Palaea Historica. It belongs to the same body of comparative work — VanderKam, Kvanvig, Orlov — that this wiki already draws on for Enoch as the seventh antediluvian sage patterned on the Mesopotamian king Enmeduranki (see the Nephilim timeline's Sumerian King List ≅ Enoch argument).2

The evidence for

Every role Josephus gives the Sethites belongs, in the older sources, to Enoch. The Enochic literature is emphatic and early that Enoch is the first man to receive and record heavenly knowledge. Jubilees 4:17 (c. 100 BC) calls him "the first among men that are born on earth who learnt writing and knowledge and wisdom and who wrote down the signs of heaven according to the order of their months in a book"; the Astronomical Book of 1 Enoch (chs. 72–82) has the angel Uriel show Enoch the courses of the luminaries and the reckoning of the calendar; and the Qumran Book of Giants names him "Enoch the distinguished scribe" (4Q203). So the two things Josephus credits to Seth's posterity — astronomy and its inscription — are, in the tradition Josephus's own century inherited, Enoch's signature roles.3

The closest formal parallel is sharper still. Josephus's distinctive detail — two pillars, one of brick and one of stone, so that whichever destruction came (fire or water) the knowledge would survive — recurs in the Enochic stream attached to Enoch himself. The Byzantine Palaea Historica has Enoch warn the giants "that the earth would either be destroyed by fire or by water," and depicts him "sitting and writing on marble (tablets) and on bricks" — the marble to outlast a fire, the bricks to outlast a flood: the same paired-media, dual-catastrophe survival logic Josephus gives the Sethites, but here worked by Enoch. That the identical mechanism is told of Enoch elsewhere is the strongest single support for reading Josephus's Sethite pillars as a recasting of an Enochic original.4

And the datum that Enoch discovered the heavenly science — not merely recorded it — is older still. The sharpest and one of the earliest witnesses is Pseudo-Eupolemus, an anonymous Samaritan writer of c. 200–150 BC whose fragment Alexander Polyhistor preserved (under the misattributed name of the Jewish historian Eupolemus) and Eusebius in turn quotes in the Praeparatio Evangelica. It traces "the first discovery [of astrology] to Enoch, and saying that he, and not the Egyptians, had first invented astrology" — Abraham, in this telling, merely transmits the science to the Egyptian priests at Heliopolis. It even syncretizes Enoch with the Greek Titan who bears up the heavens: "the Greeks say that Atlas invented astrology, and that Atlas is the same as Enoch: and that Enoch had a son Methuselah, who learned all things through angels of God." This is generally reckoned the oldest datable Jewish or Samaritan text to credit Enoch with the discovery of astronomy, and it pushes the "Enoch is the antediluvian astronomy-figure" datum back some three centuries before Josephus. It tightens the page's core observation from the front end: the very first role Josephus gives the Sethites — discovering "the science of the heavenly bodies and their order" — is one an early strand assigns pointedly to Enoch.5

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The narrow, descriptive claim is sound and mainstream: the roles Josephus assigns the Sethites are Enochic roles, the celestial-scribe function is Enoch's across Jubilees, the Astronomical Book, and the Book of Giants, and the two-media / fire-and-water survival device is attested of Enoch in the Palaea stream. The Sethite and Enochic aetiologies are genuinely entangled: they are two tellings of one antediluvian-sage complex, and Josephus's telling wears its Enochic inheritance on its sleeve. This also sharpens a point the summary makes — the moral valence of the celestial knowledge flips between the strands: in 1 Enoch 8 the same astronomy is illicit, taught by rebel Watchers to enslave mankind, while in the Sethite/Enochic-scribe tradition it is licit, received by a righteous seer and piously preserved. Same astronomy, opposite origin.

What is overstated or underdetermined. The step from "the roles are Enochic" to "the pillars-of-Seth legend is a displaced Enoch tradition" outruns what the evidence fixes. (a) The Sethite half is not merely a mask for Enoch: a generation before Josephus, Philo already dignifies Seth as "author" of the race of "those who love virtue and piety" — the virtuous line enlarged through Noah, Abraham, and Moses — with no pillars, no astronomy, and no Enoch in view, so an independently old Sethite theme was available for Josephus to build on. (b) The pillar-and-stele motif is not uniquely Enochic either: the Babylonian Berossus has the flood-hero bury the antediluvians' writings at Sippar and dig them up after, and the Egyptian Manetho / Hermetic Book of Sothis tradition tells of sacred pillars in the "Seiriadic land" inscribed by Thoth-Hermes before the flood and re-transcribed after (a fragment preserved only by the Byzantine chronographer George Syncellus, c. AD 800) — the very region-name and before-and-after logic Josephus reports (weighed on the historicity page). The material Josephus wove together plainly had more than one source, so "Seth displaced Enoch" competes with "Sethite, Enochic, and Egyptian sage-traditions ran in parallel and cross-fertilized," and the texts do not settle which. (c) The reception must not be back-read: a named "pillars of Enoch" is late, and its own pedigree tells against antiquity. The medieval Craft "Old Charges" already carry the two-pillars-survive-fire-and-water story, but credit the pillars neither to Seth nor to Enoch: the Matthew Cooke Manuscript (c. 1410–1450), citing Ranulf Higden's Polychronicon (c. 1350), assigns them to the children of Lamech — Jabal, Jubal, Tubal-cain, and Naamah — and has them recovered after the Flood by Pythagoras and Hermes; the only Enoch in that legend is Cain's son the city-builder. The name "pillars of Enoch" enters Freemasonry only when Anderson's Constitutions (1723) reassigns them to "godly ENOCH … (tho' some ascribe them to Seth)," an attribution the 1738 second edition elaborates and the nineteenth-century Royal Arch / George Oliver material builds into the porphyry-and-brass "pillars of Enoch." The same physical pillars thus migrate from Seth to Lamech's sons to Enoch as the story is retold — the signature of a back-formation, not of an independent ancient tradition. That whole line is downstream of Josephus; it cannot be cited as antique attestation.6 (d) And where a Father actually had occasion to reach for a pillars-or-plates preservation device, one declined it: Augustine, discussing "Enoch the seventh from Adam" and the antediluvian writings ascribed to him, explains their non-survival not by any lost archive but by suspicion of their authenticity — they "could not be held as authoritative … on account of their too great antiquity, which made it seem needful to regard them with suspicion" — so the very mechanism the displacement thesis needs to have been traditional is one a well-placed witness passes over.7

Assessment

Plausible. Take the two questions apart. That Josephus's Sethite pillars carry roles that Jewish lore gives to Enoch — heavenly astronomy, its inscription, and its preservation through fire and water on paired durable media — is well-grounded and effectively mainstream: the celestial-scribe function is Enoch's in Jubilees, the Astronomical Book, and the Book of Giants, and the two-media survival device is told of Enoch himself in the Palaea Historica. On that narrow point the reading is sound, and the pious counter-aetiology this page's doctrine follows is, in substance, Enochic. But the stronger thesis — that the Sethite legend is specifically a displacement of a prior Enochic pillars-story — is only plausible, not established: an independently ancient virtuous-Seth theme (Philo) and a non-Enochic Egyptian/Babylonian stele-tradition (Manetho, Berossus) were equally available, so parallel, cross-pollinating branches of one antediluvian-sage complex fit the evidence as well as a clean Enoch→Seth substitution. And the named "pillars of Enoch" is a modern Masonic back-formation, not an ancient rival label — it belongs to the legend's reception, not its roots.
