Is Josephus's pillars-of-Seth report historical?

Type: historicity critique (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — the underlying tradition — antediluvian wisdom inscribed on paired pillars against a coming catastrophe, discovered by a righteous line — is genuinely ancient and coherent, with close Babylonian and Egyptian parallels; but Josephus's specific claim, that the actual stone pillar of Seth's own descendants still stood "in the land of Siriad" in his day, is weak as history. Whiston's two objections cut unevenly: his charge that Josephus confused Seth with the pharaoh Sesostris rests on a mistranslation and does not land, while his objection that no such pillar could survive a universal flood has real force against the literal monument but not against the tradition · critique advanced by William Whiston (1737), against the report of Josephus.

Related arguments: the origins of the legend — see also Is the pillars-of-Seth legend a displaced Enoch tradition?, Is the fire-and-water double-destruction an ancient tradition?, Is the pillars-of-Seth legend the pious inversion of the Watchers' antediluvian astrology?, Is the Surid pyramid-legend a cognate of the Sethite tradition?, and Is the Sethian-Gnostic Seth tradition a cognate of the legend?

The claim

Of Josephus's mistake here, when he took Seth the son of Adam, for Seth or Sesostris, king of Egypt, the erector of this pillar in the land of Siriad, see Essay on the Old Testament, Appendix, p. 159, 160.

— William Whiston, translator's footnote to Antiquities of the Jews I.2.3 (1737 translation), ccel.org. Whiston, having translated Josephus's report that the Sethite stone pillar "remains in the land of Siriad to this day," appends two objections to it — the one quoted here, that Josephus mistook Seth son of Adam for the pillar-erecting Egyptian king Sesostris, and a second, that a stone pillar could not in any case have survived the Deluge:

Although the main of this relation might be true, and Adam might foretell a conflagration and a deluge, which all antiquity witnesses to be an ancient tradition… yet it is no way credible that they could survive the deluge, which has buried all such pillars and edifices far under ground in the sediment of its waters, especially since the like pillars of the Egyptian Seth or Sesostris were extant after the flood, in the land of Siriad…

— Whiston, same note, gutenberg.org. Two distinct charges, then: a person-conflation charge (Seth ↔ Sesostris) and a physical-survival charge (no pillar outlasts a world-drowning flood).1

Who advances it

The critique is Whiston's own, embedded in the most widely-read English Josephus. Its philological engine is a chain of identifications: Josephus's stone pillar stands in Greek Seiris / Seirida (the "Seiriad land"); Whiston took that to mean Syria; the Greek historian Herodotus (Histories 2.102–106) reports that the conquering pharaoh Sesostris set up inscribed victory stelae across the lands he subdued, and claims to have seen surviving examples "in Syria-Palestine"; so Whiston inferred that the "surviving pillar" Josephus reports is really one of Sesostris's Syrian stelae, and that Josephus (or his source) slid the Egyptian Seth-name of the pharaoh onto Seth son of Adam. The reading was carried forward by later commentators and popularizers of the "pillars of Seth."2

The evidence for

The tradition is not free invention, and its Egyptian coloring is real. The strongest thing the critique has hold of is that Josephus's pillar-legend belongs to a widespread Hellenistic-era motif of antediluvian knowledge inscribed on durable stelae and recovered after the flood — and that the surviving-monument half of it has a specifically Egyptian pedigree. The Babylonian priest Berossus (3rd c. BC) has the flood-hero Xisouthros bury the writings of the antediluvians at Sippar before the deluge and dig them up after; the Egyptian priest Manetho (and the Hermetic Book of Sothis tradition attached to his name) tells of sacred pillars in the "Seiriadic land" (Terra Seriadica), inscribed by the first Thoth-Hermes before the flood and re-transcribed after (a fragment preserved only because the Byzantine chronographer George Syncellus, c. AD 800, quotes it — the same Christian chronographic channel that transmits the Berossus material) — the same before-and-after-the-flood stelae, in the same region-name, that Josephus reports. The Hermetic corpus carries the same device a second, independent time: in the Kore Kosmou (the "Virgin of the World," an excerpt preserved by Stobaeus), Hermes seals his sacred books "beyond the reach of destruction and of decay, invisible and concealed… until the day shall come" for worthy souls to recover them, and has the teachings "best suited for the benefit of mortal men… engrave[d]… on tablet and on obelisk" — the same durable-or-hidden inscription that outlasts a coming catastrophe and is recovered later, now told of Hermes's own writings rather than a righteous line's pillars. Two things to hold together here: this deepens the case that the motif is old and cross-cultural (a point that stands whatever one makes of Josephus's monument), but it is more of the same Egyptian Hermes-material that Josephus's "land of Siriad" points at, not a Sethite corroboration — the Hermetic stelae fit the Egyptian Hermes-frame better than the biblical Seth-frame, which is the very seam the tradition-borrowing runs along. That Josephus's "Siriad" most naturally denotes Egypt — the land of the worshippers of Seirios (Sirius, the Dog-Star, whose heliacal rising governed the Egyptian calendar) — is the reading of the standard modern editors, and it aligns Josephus's surviving pillar with the Manethonian obelisks rather than with anything in Genesis. So the report does look like a Jewish adaptation of a real Near-Eastern stele-lore: the motif is old, cross-cultural, and coherent, and the "still stands in Egypt to this day" detail plausibly reflects a genuine Egyptian monumental tradition.3

Adversarial assessment

What holds. Two things. First, the tail of the story — Josephus's vouching for a specific, still-standing, Sethite pillar — is historically weak on any reading. It has him certifying a physical antediluvian monument he did not see and cannot have verified, in a form that matches the Egyptian Hermes-stelae of Manetho far better than anything in the Sethite genealogy he is narrating; the likeliest explanation is exactly the kind of tradition-borrowing the critique gestures at, a foreign stele-legend folded into the line of Seth (a move that also served the apologetic purpose of claiming the antiquity of the sciences for the biblical patriarchs rather than for Egypt). Second, Whiston's survival objection has a genuine internal bite: the whole logic of the two pillars is that the stone one outlasts the water — yet if the Flood was the universal, world-covering deluge the tradition requires and Josephus elsewhere affirms, a free-standing inscribed pillar left intact, legible, and locatable afterward is hard to credit on the story's own terms. (This is partly a flood-model question: a merely local or anthropological flood leaves the survival unproblematic but guts the two-catastrophe cosmology; a geographically global one preserves the cosmology but strains the surviving-pillar claim — see the flood timeline's global-vs-anthropological-extent argument.)

What is overstated or mistaken. The conflation charge — the headline of Whiston's note — does not hold up, and rests on a demonstrable slip. (a) Josephus never names Sesostris, never mentions a conquering pharaoh, and embeds the whole notice inside the righteous Sethite genealogy and Adam's fire-and-water prophecy; there is no textual sign of a garbled royal name, only a legend attributed, as the surrounding narrative attributes everything here, to Seth's line. (b) Whiston's philological bridge fails at its first plank: Josephus's Seiris/Seirida is not "Syria." In late antiquity the term denoted Egypt — the land of the Sirius-worshippers — and it is Whiston who imported "Syria," then reached for Herodotus's Sesostris stelae (which Herodotus placed in Syria-Palestine) to supply an erector for a pillar the mistranslation had relocated. The Herodotus stelae are, moreover, a separate misidentification: the reliefs Herodotus took for Sesostris's (at Nahr el-Kalb and Karabel) are now read as Ramesses II and a Hittite-era carving, so the chain is a misreading resting on a misattribution. (c) Even granting that "Siriad" is Egypt and that Josephus drew on an Egyptian stele-tradition, that yields tradition-appropriation, not name-confusion: Josephus need not have "mistaken Seth for Sesostris" to have Judaized a Hermetic pillar-legend, and in fact he would have been the last writer to concede that the Sethites' science was really Egypt's. So the specific error Whiston alleges is a conjecture built on his own rendering, not a fault in Josephus.4

Assessment

Contested — strong as tradition-history, weak as a report of fact, and its best-known refutation misfires. Distinguish the two questions the story runs together. As a tradition — antediluvian discoveries preserved through catastrophe on paired durable pillars, kept by a righteous line — the legend is genuinely ancient and coherent, with independent Babylonian (Berossus) and Egyptian (Manetho / the Hermetic Book of Sothis) analogues; on that level it is sound, and Whiston himself grants its antiquity. As Josephus's specific claim — that the very stone pillar of Seth's descendants still stood in Egypt in the first century — it is weak: it certifies an unseen antediluvian monument that matches the Egyptian Hermes-stelae far better than the Sethite frame, and its internal survival-logic strains against the universal Flood the tradition presupposes. But Whiston's marquee objection, that Josephus conflated Seth with Sesostris, does not carry: it depends on reading Josephus's Seiris as "Syria" (it denotes Egypt, the land of Sirius-worshippers) and on Herodotus's separately misidentified Sesostris stelae, so the alleged confusion is Whiston's inference, not Josephus's blunder. The honest verdict is therefore split: trust the antiquity and coherence of the underlying motif; treat the surviving-pillar report as a Judaized borrowing of Egyptian stele-lore rather than reliable history; and set aside the Seth-for-Sesostris conflation as unproven. The relevant overreach to flag on the proponent's page is Josephus's own — the "remains… to this day" certification — not a name-confusion he did not commit.
