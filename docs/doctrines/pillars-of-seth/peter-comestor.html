Peter Comestor on the Pillars of Seth

Dates: c. 1170 (the Historia Scholastica, composed c. 1169–1173); Peter Comestor d. 1178, chancellor of the cathedral school of Notre-Dame, Paris · Wikipedia
Summary: Comestor's Historia Scholastica — the "school history" of the Bible that became the standard Bible-narrative of the medieval Latin West, surviving in some 800 manuscripts and the great conduit for Josephus's material into that world — retells the two-pillars legend and cites Josephus by name. But he makes the decisive change: he ascribes the pillars not to Josephus's righteous line of Seth but to the Cainite musician Jubal, son of Lamech, whom he treats as the inventor of music. With Comestor the "pillars of Seth" become, in the West, the pillars of Cain's line.

Claims & evidence

The two columns against flood and fire — ascribed to Jubal, "as Josephus says"

In the Genesis section, in the chapter on the descendants of Cain, Comestor treats Jubal (whom the Vulgate of Genesis 4:21 makes "the father of all such as handle the harp and organ") as the inventor of music — then attaches the pillars to him. Adam's prophecy of two judgments, the two media, the survival of one against each catastrophe: the whole Josephan device is here, but its bearer is a son of Lamech in the line of Cain.

Nomen fratris ejus Tubal, pater canentium in cithara, et organo. Non instrumentorum quidem, quae longe post inventa fuerunt, sed inventor fuit musicae, id est consonantiarum, ut labor pastoralis quasi in delicias verteretur. Et quia audierat Adam prophetasse de duobus judiciis, ne periret ars inventa, scripsit eam in duabus columnis, in qualibet totam, ut dicit Josephus, una marmorea, altera latericia, quarum altera non diluetur diluvio, altera non solveretur incendio.

— Historia Scholastica, Liber Genesis, chapter on the generations of Cain ("De Iubal et Tubalcain," cap. 28 in the printed editions), Latin text at Wikisource (standard citation: Migne, Patrologia Latina 198, col. 1079; critical edition A. Sylwan, Petri Comestoris Scolastica Historia: Liber Genesis, CCCM 191, Brepols 2005). In English: "The name of his brother was Tubal [Jubal], father of those who play on the harp and organ — not [the inventor] of instruments, which were devised long after, but he was the inventor of music, that is, of harmonies… And because he had heard that Adam had prophesied of two judgments, lest the art he had invented should perish, he wrote it on two columns, the whole of it on each, as Josephus says — one of marble, the other of brick — of which the one would not be washed away by the flood, the other would not be dissolved by fire." Comestor adds, still following Josephus, that the marble column was said to remain "in the land of Syria" (in terra Syriaca).1

The Cainite drift: Seth's pillars become Jubal's

The change is not a slip. Josephus assigns the pillars to Seth's virtuous posterity; Comestor, citing "ut dicit Josephus," nonetheless welds them onto Jubal — the Cainite culture-hero whom Isidore had named inventor of music "of the stock of Cain, before the flood" (Etymologies III.16.1), and whom Isidore's Chronica had placed one entry above the pillars notice. Comestor closes that gap: the antediluvian art that must be preserved is now music, its inventor is now Jubal, and the pillars are now his. The "counter-aetiology of astronomy" that Josephus built around the righteous line of Seth has, in the Latin schools, migrated to the line of Cain and shifted from astronomy to the liberal art of music.

This is the reception fact that most complicates the legend's name. What the West inherited under Comestor's immense influence was not quite "the pillars of Seth" but the pillars of Jubal — a divergence that later chroniclers would carry forward, sometimes alongside the older Sethite/generic form.2

Notes

  • Comestor's manuscripts spell the musician "Tubal" where the Vulgate reads "Jubal" (Genesis 4:21); the two names are confused throughout the tradition (Higden's apparatus records "Tubal (Iubal, D.)"). Either way the figure is the son of Lamech in the line of Cain, not Seth — distinct from Tubal-Cain, the metalworker of the next verse.
  • Comestor is the single most important channel by which Josephus reached the medieval Latin West (the Historia Scholastica carries dozens of Josephus citations). That authority is exactly why his Cainite reassignment stuck: the legend travels onward in his form.
  • The reassignment feeds the Enochic-displacement and pious-inversion questions from a new angle: the legend proves mobile across its antediluvian cast — Enoch, Seth, Jubal — which is easier to square with a floating "antediluvian-sage" motif than with a fixed historical report.
  • Directly downstream: Ranulf Higden's Polychronicon (c. 1350) carries both a generic/ Sethite Josephan form and, in its Jubal chapter, Comestor's Cainite version verbatim ("Petrus…").
