Ranulf Higden on the Pillars of Seth

Dates: c. 1350 (the Polychronicon, Latin recensions c. 1327–1360; John Trevisa's Middle-English translation 1387); Ranulf Higden d. 1364, Benedictine monk of St Werburgh's, Chester · Wikipedia
Summary: Higden's Polychronicon — the most widely read universal chronicle in later-medieval England, Englished by John Trevisa in 1387 — retells the two-pillars legend in its antediluvian section, crediting Josephus. Its main text keeps the older, undifferentiated form ("men" who foresaw fire and flood, per Josephus); but in the chapter on Jubal several manuscripts also splice in Peter Comestor's Cainite version. So both branches of the legend — the generic/Sethite-Josephan and the Cainite-Jubal — sit within a single chronicle, which the later Masonic tradition would in turn quarry.

Claims & evidence

The two pillars against fire and water — the main text, "as Josephus reports"

In Book II, under the marginal rubric De duabus columnis contra diluvium et ignem ("On the two columns against flood and fire"), Higden gives the legend in its plain Josephan form: not Seth's line or Cain's by name, but "men" who knew the double destruction was coming and wrote their arts on a marble and a brick column.

His temporibus scientes homines, sicut per Adam praedictum fuerat, quod aut aquis aut igne forent perituri, studia sua et artes quas excogitaverant scripserunt in duabus columnis, lapidea et lateritia, ne tolleretur a memoria quod sapienter excogitaverant; in marmore quidem contra diluvium aquas, in lateritia contra incendium. E quibus ferunt columnam lapideam diluvium evasisse, et adhuc in Syria permansisse.

— Polychronicon Ranulphi Higden, Book II, ed. Churchill Babington (Rolls Series 41), vol. II, p. 233, archive.org. Trevisa's 1387 Middle English, printed on the facing page, renders it:

þat tyme men wiste, as Adam hadde i-seide, þat þey schulde be destroyed by fuyr, oþer by water, þerfore bookes þat þey hadde i-made by grete trauaille and studie þey closede hem in tweie greet pileres i-made of marbyl and of brend tyle. In a piler of marbyl for water, and in a pyler of tyle for fuyre; for hit schulde be i-saued in þat manere to helpe [of] mankynde. Me seiþ þat þe piler of stoon scaped þe flood and is &yogh;it in Siria.

— John Trevisa, trans., same edition, vol. II, p. 233, archive.org. Both texts open by naming Josephus as the source ("Iosephus."), keep the fire-and-water premise credited to Adam's prophecy, and repeat the survival of the stone pillar "in Syria" — the legend in the same undifferentiated shape Isidore had transmitted, unattached to either Seth or Cain.1

Comestor's Cainite version, spliced into the Jubal chapter

Higden did not only preserve the generic form. In the chapter on Jubal and Tubal-Cain, two manuscripts (the editor's C and D) insert Comestor's Cainite retelling almost word for word, flagged "Petrus" — so within the one chronicle the pillars belong at once to anonymous antediluvian "men" (per Josephus) and to the Cainite musician Jubal (per Comestor).

Petrus. Tubal (Iubal, D.) fuit inventor consonantiarum, non instrumentorum… Et quia audierat Adam prophetasse de duobus judiciis, ipse scripsit hanc artem in duabus columnis, in utraque totam; in marmorea contra diluvium, in lateritia contra incendium, ne periret ars inventa.

— editor's apparatus to vol. II (note to p. 232), reproducing the addition of MSS C and D, archive.org. This is Comestor's sentence carried intact: the Cainite Jubal, the invented art of music, the two columns against the two judgments. Higden thus documents, in one place, the two streams the legend had split into — the older Josephan/generic and the medieval Latin Cainite.2

Notes

  • Higden also carries a doublet of the legend: Zoroaster of Bactria, who "wrote the seven sciences in fourteen pillars, seven of brass and seven of burnt tile, to save them against either flood [or fire]." The paired-durable-media-against-two-catastrophes device had by his day attached itself to more than one antediluvian sage — further evidence that it is a mobile motif, not a fixed report (cf. the Enochic-displacement argument).
  • The Polychronicon is the bridge to the legend's best-known afterlife: the Masonic "Old Charges." The Cooke Manuscript (c. 1420) names Higden's chronicle — "Polychronicon" (as "Pilicronicon") — as a source for its own account of the two pillars that preserve the sciences through the Flood. That Masonic reception is a distinct thread of its own.
  • Higden stands third in the Latin-West chain that carries the legend from Josephus without passing through the doctrinal Fathers: Josephus → Isidore (chronicle) → Comestor (Bible-history) → Higden (chronicle) — the vehicle throughout being chronography and biblical history, not dogmatic theology.
