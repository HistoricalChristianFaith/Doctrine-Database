George Kedrenos (Cedrenus) on the Pillars of Seth

Dates: fl. c. 1050 (Byzantine chronicler; the Synopsis Historion reaches to 1057) · Wikipedia
Summary: George Kedrenos (Latin Cedrenus) gives the pillars legend its fullest surviving Greek form — and gives it, pointedly, back to Seth himself. In the primeval-history opening of his world-chronicle he credits Seth with naming the seven planets, comprehending the courses of the heavens, and setting up two pillars, one of stone and one of brick, on which he wrote his discoveries — the classic Josephan legend kept intact on the Byzantine side at the very moment the Latin West was reassigning the same pillars to the Cainite musician Jubal.

Claims & evidence

Seth named the planets, comprehended the heavens, and set up the two pillars — the Sethite form kept whole

Seth gave names to the seven planets, and comprehended the lore of the movements of the heavens. He also prepared two pillars, one of stone and one of brick, and wrote these things upon them… He also devised the Hebrew letters.

— George Kedrenos, Compendium Historiarum (Synopsis Historion) (c. 1050), as translated by M. R. James, The Lost Apocrypha of the Old Testament (1920), "Seth," from Migne Patrologia Graeca 121, col. 8 (wikisource.org; Greek/Latin in Bekker's Bonn edition, archive.org).1 Where Josephus credits "the posterity of Seth" collectively with the astronomy and the two pillars, and where Isidore tells it generically of "some men," Kedrenos names Seth himself as the discoverer, planet-namer, pillar-builder, and inventor of the Hebrew alphabet — the pious counter-aetiology of astronomy in its most concentrated form. James notes that the same primeval-history notices about Seth run through the Byzantine chronographic tradition Kedrenos inherits, the earlier George Syncellus included.

He keeps the pillars with Seth just as the Latin West was moving them to Cain's line

The interest of Kedrenos on the timeline is the contrast he draws with the contemporary Latin chronicles. In the same mid-eleventh-to-twelfth-century window, the Western "school Bible" of Peter Comestor (c. 1170) took the pillars away from Seth and gave them to the Cainite musician Jubal — the drift that Ranulf Higden would carry into English. Kedrenos, writing a little earlier on the Greek side, does the opposite: he keeps the whole apparatus — the celestial science, the two durable pillars, even the invention of writing — attached to the righteous line of Seth. The Byzantine branch of the tradition thus preserved the pious Sethite legend in the form Josephus gave it, at the very moment the Latin branch was recasting it as the pillars of Cain's posterity.2

Notes

  • Enochic coloring. Kedrenos's Seth also "was caught away by an angel and instructed in what concerned the future transgression of his sons… and concerning the Flood and the coming of the Saviour" — the ascent-and-revelation role that Jewish lore more usually reserves for Enoch, the seventh from Adam. That the antediluvian-sage functions slide between Seth and Enoch in these late notices is exactly the entanglement weighed on the summary's Enochic-displacement argument (assessment: plausible); M. R. James records that R. H. Charles already read some of Cedrenus's Seth-material as transferred from Enoch.

  • Another doctrine, same chronicler. Kedrenos surfaces elsewhere on this wiki for a different late legend — the Byzantine claim that the Simeon of Luke 2 was one of the Septuagint's seventy translators, kept alive three centuries to see the Christ he had doubted (weighed on the Septuagint timeline's George Kedrenos page and its Simeon-the-Just argument).
