Josephus on the Pillars of Seth

Dates: c. AD 100 · Wikipedia
Summary: In the Antiquities of the Jews (I.2.3, c. AD 93–94), Josephus gives the classic form of the tradition that the righteous line of Seth invented astronomy and, foreseeing a double destruction of the world by fire and water, inscribed their discoveries on two pillars — one of brick, one of stone — so the knowledge would survive the Flood. The stone pillar, he reports, still stood "in the land of Siriad" in his own day.

Claims & evidence

Seth's descendants invented the science of the heavenly bodies

They also were the inventors of that peculiar sort of wisdom which is concerned with the heavenly bodies, and their order.

— Antiquities of the Jews, Book I, Chapter 2, Section 3, trans. William Whiston, gutenberg.org. Josephus credits the origin of astronomy not to revelation from above but to the virtue of Seth's line — a pious human discovery.

The two pillars against fire and water, and the pillar "in the land of Siriad"

And that their inventions might not be lost before they were sufficiently known, upon Adam's prediction that the world was to be destroyed at one time by the force of fire, and at another time by the violence and quantity of water, they made two pillars, the one of brick, the other of stone: they inscribed their discoveries on them both, that in case the pillar of brick should be destroyed by the flood, the pillar of stone might remain, and exhibit those discoveries to mankind; and also inform them that there was another pillar of brick erected by them. Now this remains in the land of Siriad to this day.

— Antiquities of the Jews, Book I, Chapter 2, Section 3, gutenberg.org. The two pillars answer the two prophesied catastrophes: brick could survive fire, stone the flood, so between them the antediluvian science would outlast either. Whiston's "Siriad" renders Josephus's Greek Seiris, taken by most readers to mean Egypt (the "Seiriad land"), though the identification is Josephus's own detail, not Scripture's.

Notes

  • Josephus's account is a pious counter-aetiology of astronomy: the same celestial knowledge that the Watcher tradition (1 Enoch 8; Tatian; Tertullian) traced to the fallen angels, he traces to the righteous line of Seth. See the Astrology and the Stars timeline and its fallen-angels argument.
  • Strikingly, the rival angelic aetiology sits in the same book: at Antiquities I.3.1 Josephus reports the "many angels of God" who begat unjust giants, likened to the giants of Greek myth — treated on Josephus on the Nephilim. So both origins of celestial knowledge, the Sethite and the angelic, stand side by side in Josephus's own narrative.
  • The pillars belong to the wider antediluvian-wisdom lore: knowledge preserved through the Flood (cf. Noah's Flood) and the tradition of the antediluvian sages, with Enoch the seventh from Adam (cf. the Nephilim). The motif has close analogues — the Babylonian antediluvian writings buried at Sippar and dug up after the flood (Berossus), and the Egyptian sacred pillars in the "Seiriadic land" inscribed by Thoth-Hermes before the flood and re-transcribed after (Manetho / the Hermetic Book of Sothis) — which is why "Siriad" is most naturally read as Egypt (the land of the worshippers of Seirios, Sirius the Dog-Star, whose rising governed the Egyptian calendar).
  • The fire-and-water framing is not Josephus's invention: the expectation of a world destroyed once by fire and once by water is an ancient tradition attested well before him — Plato's Egyptian priest presents it as hoary lore (Timaeus 22c, "the greatest [destructions] are by fire and water"), Berossus and the Stoics fix it as the great-year cycle of ekpyrosis and kataklysmos, and the first-century Jewish Life of Adam and Eve (49–50) has Seth inscribe the family testament on paired tablets of stone and clay against a judgment "first by water and second by fire" — the closest parallel of all to this notice. So Josephus inherited the legend's premise rather than fabricating it: weighed on the argument page Is the fire-and-water double-destruction an ancient tradition? (assessment: sound).
  • Where the report overreaches. Josephus's one historically vulnerable move is the "Now this remains in the land of Siriad to this day" — certifying an unseen, still-standing antediluvian monument, whose form matches the Egyptian Hermes-stelae of Manetho far better than the Sethite frame he sets it in, and whose survival strains against the world-covering Flood the story presupposes. That certification, not the legend as such, is the overstatement to trust least. William Whiston's translator's-note charge that Josephus went further and confused Seth with the pharaoh Sesostris does not hold — it rests on reading Josephus's Seiris as "Syria" and on Herodotus's separately-misidentified Sesostris stelae. Both are weighed on the argument page Is Josephus's pillars-of-Seth report historical? (assessment: contested).
