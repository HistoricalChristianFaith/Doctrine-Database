George Syncellus on the Pillars of Seth

Dates: c. 810 · Wikipedia
Summary: George Syncellus (d. c. 810/811), a monk of Constantinople, compiled the Ecloga Chronographica, a universal chronicle stitched from older sources — and it is the single conduit through which the two closest pagan cognates of the pillars legend reach us: the Egyptian Seiriadic columns of Thoth-Hermes (inscribed before the Flood, re-transcribed after) that Josephus's "land of Siriad" points at, and the Babylonian Sippar tablets that Xisouthros buried before the deluge and dug up after. Syncellus heads the Byzantine chronographic tradition that also kept the pious Sethite form of the legend intact — the form later given in full by George Kedrenos.

Claims & evidence

He alone preserves Manetho's Seiriadic columns — the Egyptian cognate Josephus's "Siriad" resembles

…copied from the sacred books, written by Thoth, the first Hermes, in the sacred language and in hieroglyphic characters… upon the columns set up in the Seriadic land… and after the deluge… translated from the sacred language into the Greek tongue in hieroglyphic characters, and committed to writing in books… by Agathodaemon, the son of the second Hermes.

— the preface to the (pseudo-Manethonian) Book of Sothis, quoted in Syncellus's Chronography; this fragment survives only because Syncellus copied it. It is the sharpest pagan parallel to Josephus: sacred antediluvian knowledge inscribed on stone columns, in the Seriadic land — the same region-name Josephus gives his surviving stone pillar — and recovered after the Flood. Because "Siriad" most naturally denotes Egypt (the land of the worshippers of Seirios, the Dog-Star), Syncellus's Manethonian fragment aligns Josephus's monument with the Egyptian Hermes-stelae rather than with anything in the Sethite genealogy — the seam the tradition-borrowing runs along, weighed on the historicity argument.1

He also preserves Berossus's Sippar tablets — the Babylonian cognate

The same chronicle is the sole conduit for the Babylonian half of the parallel. Quoting the priest Berossus (3rd c. BC), Syncellus reports that the flood-hero Xisouthros, warned of the coming deluge, buried "the beginning, middle, and end of all writing" at Sippar, the city of the sun, so that after the waters receded the survivors dug up the antediluvian writings and gave them back to mankind. Antediluvian knowledge deliberately hidden in durable form against a coming flood and recovered afterward is the identical device Josephus gives Seth's descendants — here worked by the Babylonian sages. Both of the "independent Babylonian and Egyptian analogues" the pillars tradition leans on, then, come through this one ninth-century Byzantine monk.2

He heads the Byzantine tradition that kept the pillars with Seth

Beyond the pagan cognates, Syncellus stands at the head of the Greek chronographic line that preserved the pious Sethite legend itself. The primeval-history notices this tradition carries — that Seth named the seven planets, comprehended the courses of the heavens, and set up two pillars of stone and brick — are given in their fullest form by George Kedrenos two centuries later, and M. R. James records that both Syncellus and Cedrenus preserve them. So where the Latin West would drift the pillars onto Cain's line (Comestor's Jubal), the Byzantine chronicle-tradition Syncellus anchors kept them attached to the righteous line of Seth.3

Notes

  • He carries the impious mirror too. On the Nephilim timeline Syncellus is the witness who transcribes the Greek 1 Enoch "Book of the Watchers" as antediluvian history — the rebel angels who "taught" astronomy to enslave mankind. His chronicle thus holds both origins of celestial knowledge that this doctrine sets against each other: the impious revelation of the Watchers and the righteous discovery of the Sethites. His Alexandrian source Panodorus (c. AD 400) even fused the two, making the Watchers the real originators of antediluvian astronomy and, on his editors' reading, likely identifying them with the Sethites — noted on the summary's pious-inversion argument.
  • Conduit, not proponent. Syncellus does not himself argue for the pillars legend; he transmits, in one place, the Egyptian and Babylonian analogues, the Enochic Watchers text, and the Sethite notices. That concentration is exactly what makes him a hinge on this timeline — almost the whole comparative apparatus the argument pages use for the pillars legend passes through his Chronography.
