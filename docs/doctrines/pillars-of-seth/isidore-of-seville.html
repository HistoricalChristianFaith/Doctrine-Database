Isidore of Seville on the Pillars of Seth

Dates: c. 620 (the Chronica Maiora, first recension c. 615, revised c. 626); Isidore c. 560–636, archbishop of Seville · Wikipedia
Summary: Isidore is the writer who carries the two-pillars legend into the Latin West. His universal chronicle, the Chronica, sets Josephus's report of the antediluvians who wrote their discoveries on a brick and a stone pillar against the flood-and-fire into the standard sequence of world history — but he tells it generically, of "some men," and places it in the same breath as the Cainite line of Lamech, whose son Jubal his Etymologies names as the inventor of music "before the flood." Isidore keeps the pillars and the Cainites side by side without yet fusing them; that fusion is Peter Comestor's later move.

Claims & evidence

The two pillars against fire and water — "as Josephus reports"

In the antediluvian entries of the Chronica, at the generation of Lamech and Noah, Isidore inserts the pillars notice. He credits Josephus by name, but does not say whose descendants raised them — only "some men" who foresaw the double destruction.

In these times, as Josephus reports, some men, who knew that they were about to die by either fire or water, inscribed their discoveries on two columns made of brick and stone, so that the memory of those things which they had discovered in their wisdom might not be erased. Their stone columns are reported to have withstood the flood and remain in Syria to this very day.

— Chronica (Chronicon) §5, trans. Kenneth B. Wolf, tertullian.org. The report is Josephus's (Antiquities I.2.3), compressed to its essentials: the paired media, the fire-and-water premise, and the survival of the stone pillar "in Syria to this very day." Isidore neither names Seth nor corrects Josephus — he simply lodges the legend in the Latin chronicle tradition, from which it passes to the medieval Bible-histories.1

The Cainite next door: Jubal the inventor of music "before the flood"

The pillars entry sits immediately after Isidore's notice of Lamech's sons — the Cainite Jubal and Tubal-Cain — whom he credits with music and metalwork. In the Etymologies he fixes the same point in a lapidary formula that the Middle Ages copied endlessly.

In this age also, Jubal, from the line of Cain, discovered the art of music and his brother Tubal Cain was the inventor of the arts of copper and iron.

— Chronica §4, tertullian.org; compare Etymologies III.16.1: "Moyses dicit repertorem musicae artis fuisse Tubal, qui fuit de stirpe Cain ante diluvium" ("Moses says that the discoverer of the art of music was Tubal, who was of the stock of Cain before the flood"). This is the raw material for the later drift: a Cainite culture-hero, credited with an antediluvian art, planted one line above the antediluvian pillars — two ingredients waiting to be welded.2

What the Etymologies does not carry

Isidore's encyclopedia, the natural place to look for the legend, does not in fact contain it. Its astronomy chapters (III.24–27, on the astronomy/astrology distinction) discuss the science of the heavens without the pillars, and its music chapter gives only the Cainite-Tubal note above — no brick-and-stone columns, no fire-and-flood. The pillars legend is carried by Isidore the chronicler, not Isidore the encyclopedist — a small but telling sign that the legend travelled in the chronicle/Bible-history genre, not the doctrinal one.

This is the honest negative result: readers sometimes assume the pillars sit in the Etymologies; they do not. The located passage is in the Chronica.3

Notes

  • Isidore is the hinge of the Latin reception. He transmits Josephus's pillars faithfully (generic "men," crediting Josephus) while, one entry earlier, pinning music on the Cainite Jubal. He keeps the two formally apart; it is Peter Comestor (c. 1170) who fuses them, writing the pillars onto Jubal himself — and Ranulf Higden (c. 1350) then carries both versions. The drift from Josephus's righteous line of Seth to a Cainite line begins here, in the adjacency Isidore leaves unresolved.
  • The generic wording matters for the historicity question: Isidore repeats Josephus's claim that the stone pillar still stands "in Syria to this very day" without independent knowledge of it — a certification passed hand to hand down the chronicle tradition, not a fresh sighting.
  • The fire-and-water framing Isidore inherits is itself older than Josephus (Plato, Berossus, the Life of Adam and Eve): weighed on Is the fire-and-water double-destruction an ancient tradition? (assessment: sound).
  • Isidore appears elsewhere on this wiki as a witness on astrology (the astronomy/astrology distinction), the Old Testament canon, the origin of the Septuagint, the integrity of the text, and the first language.
