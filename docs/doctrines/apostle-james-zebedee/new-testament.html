The New Testament on James, son of Zebedee

Dates: c. 30–90 (the events, and the writing of the Gospels and Acts) · Wikipedia
Summary: The bedrock. James is a Galilean fisherman, son of Zebedee and elder brother of John, called with him from the nets and surnamed with him a “son of thunder.” With Peter and John he is one of the inner three. He and John ask for the chief seats and are told they will “drink of the cup” Jesus drinks. He is the only apostle whose death the New Testament records: beheaded by Herod Agrippa I about the year 44, the first of the Twelve to be martyred.

Claims & evidence

A fisherman, son of Zebedee, brother of John, called from the nets

James and his younger brother John are fishermen of the Sea of Galilee, partners of Simon Peter, called by Jesus straight from their boat as they mended their nets with their father Zebedee.

And when he had gone a little further thence, he saw James the son of Zebedee, and John his brother, who also were in the ship mending their nets. And straightway he called them: and they left their father Zebedee in the ship with the hired servants, and went after him.

— Mark 1:19–20 (cf. Matthew 4:21–22; Luke 5:10, partners of Simon).

“Boanerges, the sons of thunder”

In naming the Twelve, Mark records the surname Jesus gave the two brothers — a name the Gospels elsewhere illustrate, when James and John would call down fire on a Samaritan village.

And James the son of Zebedee, and John the brother of James; and he surnamed them Boanerges, which is, The sons of thunder…

— Mark 3:17 (cf. the fire from heaven, Luke 9:54). James is named in the first group of four in every apostle-list, always with Peter, John, and Andrew (Matthew 10:2; Mark 3:17; Luke 6:14; Acts 1:13).

One of the inner three

With Peter and John, James belongs to the three disciples Jesus repeatedly kept nearest him — admitted where the rest were not: to the raising of Jairus's daughter, to the Transfiguration, and to the agony in Gethsemane.

And he suffered no man to follow him, save Peter, and James, and John the brother of James.

— Mark 5:37 (Jairus's daughter); the Transfiguration, “Peter, and James, and John” (Mark 9:2); Gethsemane, “Peter and James and John” (Mark 14:33).

“Ye shall indeed drink of the cup” — the request for the seats

James and John ask Jesus for the places at his right and left hand in his glory. He answers with a question about a cup and a baptism — and a promise the later church read as foretelling James's martyrdom.

But Jesus said unto them, Ye know not what ye ask: can ye drink of the cup that I drink of? and be baptized with the baptism that I am baptized with? And they said unto him, We can. And Jesus said unto them, Ye shall indeed drink of the cup that I drink of; and with the baptism that I am baptized withal shall ye be baptized…

— Mark 10:35–40 (cf. Matthew 20:20–23, where their mother makes the request).

Killed by Herod — the first apostle martyred

Acts records, without elaboration, the death that makes James unique among the Twelve: about the year 44 Herod Agrippa I, grandson of Herod the Great, moved against the Jerusalem church and had James beheaded. He is the only apostle whose death the New Testament reports.

Now about that time Herod the king stretched forth his hands to vex certain of the church. And he killed James the brother of John with the sword.

— Acts 12:1–2. The “Herod” here is Agrippa I, who died in 44 (Acts 12:20–23), fixing James's death at or a little before that year.

Notes

  • The one recorded apostolic death. Of all the Twelve, only James son of Zebedee has his death told in the New Testament (Acts 12:2). This is the firm anchor to which all later tradition about him — the accuser's conversion, the mission to Spain, the shrine at Compostela — is attached, and against which it must be measured. See the summary timeline.
  • His mother, and Salome. The mother of James and John, who asks for the two seats in Matthew (Matthew 20:20), is among the women at the cross; comparing the lists (Matthew 27:56; Mark 15:40) she is commonly identified as Salome. This is an inference from the parallel notices, not a statement of any one text.
  • “The Great.” He is called James the Great (or the Elder) only to distinguish him from James son of Alphaeus and James the Lord's brother; the New Testament does not use the epithet.
