Clement of Alexandria on the death of James

Dates: c. 200 (Clement's Hypotyposes; the words survive only as quoted by Eusebius, c. 325) · Wikipedia
Summary: The earliest tradition about James beyond the bare notice of Acts. Clement, in the seventh book of his lost Hypotyposes, told — on the authority of “those who were before him” — that the man who brought James to the judgment-seat was converted by his testimony, confessed himself a Christian, and was beheaded together with him; James, asked for forgiveness on the way, answered “Peace be with thee” and kissed him. Eusebius preserves the passage in Book 2 of his Church History.

Claims & evidence

The accuser converted, and beheaded with James

Eusebius, recounting the death of James under Herod Agrippa, pauses to relay a story he found in Clement. It is the one early expansion of the martyrdom — and Clement is careful to mark it as received tradition, “the account … which has been given by Clement, who moreover adds a tradition worthy of mention … as he received it from those who lived before him.”

He says that the one who led James to the judgment seat, when he saw him bearing his testimony, was moved, and confessed that he was himself also a Christian. They were both therefore, he says, led away together; and on the way he begged James to forgive him. And he, after considering a little, said, “Peace be with thee,” and kissed him. And thus they were both beheaded at the same time.

— Clement of Alexandria, Hypotyposes Book 7, quoted in Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History 2.9.

Notes

  • What kind of source this is. Clement (c. 150–215) is an early and serious witness, but the Hypotyposes is lost, so the story reaches us only through Eusebius; and Clement himself frames it not as first-hand knowledge but as a tradition handed down. It is early, it is not implausible, and it is uncorroborated — the strengths and the limits are weighed on the argument page, Was James's accuser converted and beheaded with him?
  • The name Josias. Clement gives the accuser no name; the later Golden Legend calls him Josias and weaves him into a longer romance of James in Spain and a magician named Hermogenes. That embellishment belongs to the medieval layer, not to Clement's spare notice.
