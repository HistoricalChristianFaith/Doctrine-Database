Augustine of Hippo on dream-apparitions of the dead

Dates: c. 421 (On Care to Be Had for the Dead / De cura pro mortuis gerenda, c. 421–424; Augustine 354–430) · Wikipedia
Summary: Augustine wrote the classic Christian treatment of the folk phenomenon, occasioned by the very scenario that opens this question — a dead father appearing in his son's dream to reveal where a cancelled debt-note lay. His verdict is the tradition's most careful: the figure of a dead man in a dream is not his soul but a likeness, exactly as a living friend is seen in dreams without knowing it; the departed are ordinarily unaware of the affairs of the living; and true, useful dream-visions are effected by angelic operation under God's providence, false ones by demonic deception. He proves the dead's unawareness from his own mother. But he keeps two doors open: God may, by special dispensation, send a soul (Samuel, Moses), and the martyrs really do aid us — never "by their proper nature," always "by a Divine power."

Claims & evidence

The occasion: a dead father reveals a hidden debt-note in his son's dream

The treatise answers Paulinus of Nola, who had asked whether burial near a saint's tomb profits the dead; but Augustine widens it into the general question of apparitions, prompted by cases he had heard at Milan. The paradigm case is precisely the folk scenario — the dead disclosing hidden, true information:

…when we were at Milan, we heard tell of a certain person of whom was demanded payment of a debt, with production of his deceased father's acknowledgment, which debt unknown to the son the father had paid… Then in this exceeding anxiousness of his, his said father appeared to him in a dream, and made known to him where was the counter acknowledgment by which that acknowledgment was cancelled. Which when the young man had found and showed, he not only rebutted the wrongful claim of a false debt, but also got back his father's note of hand…

— On Care to Be Had for the Dead §13, source. "Here then the soul of a man is supposed to have had care for his son," Augustine writes — and the rest of the treatise is his patient refusal of that supposition.

The dead in a dream are a likeness, no more their soul than a living friend seen in a dream is his

Augustine's decisive move is to deny any special status to a dream of the dead. Living people are constantly seen in others' dreams without the least awareness of it — he gives his own case, a former pupil who dreamed Augustine solved a passage of Cicero for him while the real Augustine, "far away beyond the sea," knew nothing of it. If the living appear so, unknowing, why not the dead?

Such, however, is human infirmity, that when in a dream a person shall see a dead man, he thinks it is the soul that he sees: but when he shall in like manner dream of a living man, he has no doubt that it is not a soul nor a body, but the likeness of a man that has appeared to him: just as if it were not possible in regard of dead men, in the same sort unconscious of it, that it should not be their souls, but their likenesses that appear to the sleepers.

— On Care to Be Had for the Dead §13, source. This is the same argument Tertullian had made two centuries before ("apparitions of dead persons in dreams [are] not more really true than those of living persons"); Augustine develops it into a full account.

The agent is angelic, under God's providence — the departed themselves are unconscious

Where the vision is true and serviceable, Augustine assigns it not to the dead but to angels, permitted or commanded by God for the living's instruction or solace:

By angelical operations, then, I should think it is effected, whether permitted from above, or commanded, that they seem in dreams to say something about burying of their bodies, when they whose the bodies are are utterly unconscious of it… Sometimes however, by fallacious visions, men are cast into great errors, who deserve to suffer this.

— On Care to Be Had for the Dead §12, source. The dead reveal where their bodies lie, and it proves true, yet "they whose the bodies are are utterly unconscious of it": the information is real, the agency is not the deceased's.1

The proof from Monica: if the dead attended us, my mother would visit me every night

Augustine's most personal argument is that the ordinary holy dead simply do not come. If they could and did, no one would come sooner than his own mother, who had followed him across the world in life:

If the souls of the dead took part in the affairs of the living, and if it were their very selves that, when we see them, speak to us in sleep; to say nothing of others, there is my own self, whom my pious mother would no night fail to visit, that mother who by land and sea followed me that she might live with me. Far be the thought that she should, by a life more happy, have been made cruel, to that degree that when any thing vexes my heart she should not even console in his sadness the son whom she loved with an only love…

— On Care to Be Had for the Dead §16, source. He backs it with Scripture — Isaiah's "Abraham hath not known us, and Israel is not cognizant of us" (Isa 63:16), and Josiah gathered to his fathers "in peace" precisely so that "thine eyes shall not see all the evils" to come. "There then are the spirits of the departed, where they see not whatever things are doing… in this life to men."

The two exceptions: souls sent by special dispensation, and the martyrs' aid "by a Divine power"

Augustine is not a bare sceptic. He grants that God may, by special act, send a soul from the dead — Samuel appearing to Saul, Moses with Elijah at the Transfiguration — just as Paul was "rapt into Paradise" from among the living:

…that some from the dead are sent to the living, as, on the other hand, Paul from the living was rapt into Paradise, divine Scripture doth testify. For Samuel the Prophet, appearing to Saul when living, predicted even what should befall the king: although some think it was not Samuel himself… but that some spirit, meet for so evil works, did figure his semblance…

— On Care to Be Had for the Dead §18, source. (On which side of the Endor question Augustine finally stands — he reports both, and elsewhere leans that Samuel really was seen — see the Endor argument page.) And the martyrs, whose apparitions and aid are real, help "by a Divine power," never of their own nature — the exact distinction the developed cult of the saints would rest on:

…we are to understand that it must needs be by a Divine power that the Martyrs are interested in affairs of the living, from the very fact that for the departed to be by their proper nature interested in affairs of the living is impossible.

— On Care to Be Had for the Dead §19, source. So the fault line is drawn: the ordinary dead do not naturally perceive us and are not the agents of their apparitions; the glorified martyrs truly aid us, but by God's power, not their own.2

Notes

  • This is the same treatise, and the same distinction ("by a Divine power… [not] by their proper nature"), that Augustine's page on prayer to the saints reads as "the careful brake" — there the point qualifies the invocation of the martyrs; here it answers the folk apparition of the ordinary dead. The two are one coherent teaching: the dead do not naturally attend us, and whatever traffic there is between the worlds is God's doing.
  • The tension with Gregory the Great is the pivot of this timeline. Gregory's Dialogues takes the apparitions to be the dead themselves, soliciting prayers; Augustine takes them to be likenesses worked by angels while the dead lie unconscious. Aquinas later harmonizes the two: naturally cut off (Augustine), but sent by divine dispensation (Gregory).
  • Augustine appears across this wiki as a load-bearing witness to the intermediate state, purgatory and prayer for the dead, and prayer to the saints — the doctrines that frame the "living and the dead" family this one belongs to.
