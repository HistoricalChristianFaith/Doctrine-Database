Tertullian on dream-apparitions of the dead

Dates: c. 210 (A Treatise on the Soul / De Anima, c. 210–213; Tertullian c. 155–220) · Wikipedia
Summary: The earliest sustained Christian verdict on the phenomenon, and a flat denial that the dead return. Because every soul is detained in Hades until the resurrection, no departed soul can be "brought up" by necromancy or seen as itself in a dream; what appears is a demonic counterfeit, and the test of a dream is never its vividness but whether the thing dreamed is "fully proved" true. Tertullian anticipates Augustine's central argument — that the image of a dead man in a dream is no more the man than the image of a living one — by two centuries.

Claims & evidence

No soul leaves Hades before the resurrection, so the dead cannot be summoned back

Tertullian's whole treatment rests on his eschatology: Hades is a real holding-place that detains every soul until the end (his fuller doctrine of the intermediate state is traced on that timeline). If no soul may leave, then the souls that magicians and mediums claim to "call up" cannot be the dead at all. He grounds the impossibility on the rich man and Lazarus — the gulf none may cross — and makes the point directly against the necromancers:

Moreover, the fact that Hades is not in any case opened for (the escape of) any soul, has been firmly established by the Lord in the person of Abraham, in His representation of the poor man at rest and the rich man in torment. No one, (he said,) could possibly be despatched from those abodes to report to us how matters went in the nether regions…

— A Treatise on the Soul 57, source. The one apparent exception he grants — "The power of God has, no doubt, sometimes recalled men's souls to their bodies, as a proof of His own transcendent rights" — he immediately walls off from necromancy: a true resurrection restores a soul to a solid, palpable body, and by that very contrast exposes "the fraudulence of every incorporeal apparition of dead persons."

What the medium raises is a demon impersonating the dead — even at Endor

If the dead cannot be summoned, the figure that answers the summons must be something else. Tertullian identifies it as a demon — the same unclean spirit "lying concealed in the persons of the dead" that exorcism unmasks — and he applies this even to the hardest case, the witch of Endor calling up Samuel (1 Sam 28), which he reads as a demonic representation, not the prophet's returning soul:

…it was no less than this that was anciently permitted to the Pythonic (or ventriloquistic) spirit — even to represent the soul of Samuel, when Saul consulted the dead, after (losing the living) God. God forbid, however, that we should suppose that the soul of any saint, much less of a prophet, can be dragged out of (its resting-place in Hades) by a demon.

— A Treatise on the Soul 57, source. Satan "is transformed into an angel of light," so it was "easy" for one and the same spirit — in the sorceress and in the apostate king — to counterfeit Samuel and make Saul believe he saw him. This is the minority-turned-influential reading weighed on the Endor argument page; it is the opposite of Origen's, who held the figure genuinely was Samuel.

A dream of the dead is worth no more than a dream of the living

Tertullian then makes the move Augustine would later make the center of De cura: he refuses to grant that a dream of a dead person is any more "the person" than a dream of a living one, and relocates the test of truth from the vividness of the vision to its verification:

Well, we admit apparitions of dead persons in dreams to be not more really true than those of living persons; but we apply the same estimate to all alike — to the dead and to the living, and indeed to all the phenomena which are seen. Now things are not true because they appear to be so, but because they are fully proved to be so. The truth of dreams is declared from the realization, not the aspect.

— A Treatise on the Soul 57, source. The tomb-oracles of the Nasamones and the Celts, who sleep at their ancestors' graves to receive dreams, are for Tertullian simply this same imposture dressed as piety — the dead do not come, whatever the sleeper sees.1

Notes

  • Tertullian's earlier chapters on dreams (De Anima 45–49) sort dreams into those from God, those from demons, and those arising naturally from the soul's own restlessness — the framework within which his verdict here belongs: a dream can be sent, but not by the dead.
  • The through-line to Augustine is close enough to be almost a citation: both deny that the dead are the agents of their own apparitions, both compare the dream-of-the-dead to the dream-of-the-living to prove it, both allow that God may nonetheless send a soul by special act. The difference is emphasis — Tertullian stresses the demonic counterfeit, Augustine the neutral, God-permitted "angelical operation."
  • He appears elsewhere on this wiki as a load-bearing witness to the intermediate state (the conscious, structured Hades that his argument here presupposes) and to prayer and oblation for the dead.
