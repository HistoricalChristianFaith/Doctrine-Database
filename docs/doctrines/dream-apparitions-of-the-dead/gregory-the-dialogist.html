Gregory the Great on dream-apparitions of the dead

Dates: c. 593 (Dialogues Book IV; Gregory c. 540–604, pope 590–604) · Wikipedia
Summary: Gregory turns the Latin tradition away from Augustine's angel-mediated caution and toward taking the apparitions of the dead at face value. Book IV of the Dialogues is a dense catalogue of deathbed visions and revenant stories in which the departed appear as themselves — conscious of their own post-mortem state, naming the sins for which they suffer, and begging the living for the prayers and the Eucharistic sacrifice that will release them. Gregory does weigh dreams cautiously in the abstract, but his verdict on the concrete cases is that "the spirits of them that be dead desire it of the living."

Claims & evidence

The deacon Paschasius, met alive in the baths, begs the bishop's prayers

Gregory's most-cited case is Paschasius, a holy Roman deacon who had erred in a church schism. After his death Bishop Germanus of Capua, taking the waters for his health, finds Paschasius himself standing in the hot baths, serving — and the dead man explains his own state and asks to be prayed for:

…he found there standing in those hot waters the foresaid Paschasius, ready to do him service. At which sight being much afraid, he demanded what so worthy a man as he was did in that place: to whom Paschasius returned this answer: "For no other cause," quoth he, "am I appointed to this place of punishment, but for that I took part with Lawrence against Symmachus: and therefore I beseech you to pray unto our Lord for me, and by this token shall you know that your prayers be heard, if, at your coming again, you find me not here."

— Dialogues IV.40, source. Germanus prays; returning, he finds Paschasius gone — "purged from that sin." For Gregory the apparition is the deacon, aware and asking; there is no suggestion of a mere likeness or an angel wearing his face.

The spirit at the baths of Centumcellis — and Gregory's general rule

A parallel story fixes the doctrine. A priest of Centumcellis is repeatedly served at the baths by a helpful stranger; offering him blessed bread in thanks, he learns the man is dead and suffering, and is asked to offer the sacrifice for him. When the man later vanishes, Gregory draws the general moral outright:

…whereby it appeareth what great profit the souls receive by the sacrifice of the holy oblation, seeing the spirits of them that be dead desire it of the living, and give certain tokens to let us understand how that by means thereof they have received absolution.

— Dialogues IV.55, source. This is the hinge of the whole timeline: where Augustine had said the departed are "utterly unconscious" of their apparitions and the agency is angelic, Gregory states as a rule that the dead themselves desire and solicit the living's prayers, and that their appearing is the "token" of it.1

Dreams weighed cautiously in the abstract — but the deathbed visions trusted

Gregory is not naïve about dreams as such. Asked directly "whether dreams are to be believed," he sorts them into kinds — from the stomach's fullness or emptiness, from diabolical illusion, from thought mixed with illusion, from revelation, and from thought mixed with revelation — and warns that because they "grow from such divers roots, with so much the more difficulty ought we to believe them." Only the discerning can tell them apart: "Holy men, indeed, by a certain inward spiritual taste, do discern betwixt illusions and true revelations," for the deceiving spirit "sometime useth to foretell many true things, that, in the end, he may by some falsehood ensnare our soul."2 Yet the caution he applies to dreams he does not extend to the waking apparitions and deathbed visions of Book IV, where the dying see dead saints and kinsmen come to meet them, and the dead return to name their state. His frame is the doctrine of an immediate particular judgment and a purifying fire before the last day — the setting in which souls have a state to report and a need the living can meet, traced on the purgatory timeline.

Notes

  • Gregory is the counter-pole to Augustine on this timeline, and the two frame its central tension: are the apparitions the dead themselves (Gregory) or likenesses worked by angels while the dead lie unaware (Augustine)? Aquinas later reconciles them — naturally cut off, but sent by divine dispensation — and cites "the fourth book of the Dialogues" as his evidence that the dead do appear.
  • The same Book IV is a load-bearing source for Gregory on purgatory and prayer for the dead (the cleansing fire and the profit of the oblation) and on the intermediate state (each soul judged at death, entering rest or punishment at once). The revenant stories here are the narrative face of that same eschatology.
