Thomas Aquinas on dream-apparitions of the dead

Dates: c. 1274 (Summa Theologiae, Supplement, compiled posthumously from earlier work; Aquinas 1225–1274) · Wikipedia
Summary: Aquinas gives the question its settled scholastic answer by harmonizing the two poles of the timeline. Following Augustine, he holds that "according to the natural course" the separated souls are "utterly cut off from communication with the living"; following Gregory's Dialogues, he grants that "according to the disposition of Divine providence" they do sometimes come forth and appear. And he sharpens the fault line the tradition had been drawing: the glorified saints "can appear when they will," the damned and ordinary dead only as God permits.

Claims & evidence

Naturally cut off, yet by divine dispensation the dead do appear

The article asks "whether the souls who are in heaven or hell are able to go from thence." Aquinas answers by granting the ordinary rule and the extraordinary exception together:

…according to the natural course, the separated souls consigned to their respective abodes are utterly cut off from communication with the living. Nevertheless, according to the disposition of Divine providence separated souls sometimes come forth from their abode and appear to men, as Augustine, in the book quoted above, relates of the martyr Felix who appeared visibly to the people of Nola when they were besieged by the barbarians.

— Summa Theologiae, Supplement q. 69, a. 3, source. This is exactly Augustine's structure — the departed do not "by their proper nature" attend the living, but God may send them — now stated as a formal rule-and-exception. Aquinas adds that souls sometimes appear "to seek our suffrages, as to those who are detained in purgatory, as evidenced by many instances related in the fourth book of the Dialogues" — folding Gregory's revenants into the scheme as the evidentiary base.

The saints appear at will; the damned and the ordinary dead only by permission

Aquinas then draws the distinction that governs the whole question — the difference between the glorified dead and the rest, which is precisely the fault line between this doctrine and prayer to the saints:

There is, however, this difference between the saints and the damned, that the saints can appear when they will to the living, but not the damned…

— Summa Theologiae, Supplement q. 69, a. 3, source. The glorified, sharing in God's own freedom, may appear at their own will; the damned and the ordinary dead only as God permits. So they never come of their own initiative — the tradition's core answer to the folk apparition — while the saints, and they alone, retain a liberty to appear.1

Notes

  • Aquinas is the synthesis of this timeline: he keeps Augustine's "utterly cut off… by the natural course" and Gregory's real, prayer-seeking apparitions, subordinating both to divine providence and sorting the cases by the state of the soul. What in Augustine was a tension and in Gregory a mass of anecdote becomes in Aquinas a rule with clean exceptions.
  • The saints-appear-at-will clause is the same distinction that grounds Aquinas's defence of invoking the saints, treated on the prayer-to-saints and purgatory timelines. Here it does the opposite work — explaining why the ordinary dead of the folk apparitions do not simply come when they please.
