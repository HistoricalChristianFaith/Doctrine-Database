Irenaeus of Lyons on Infant Baptism

Dates: c. 180–189 (Against Heresies, composed at Lyons) · Wikipedia
Summary: Irenaeus never discusses the baptism of infants directly — there is no passage in which he weighs whether babies should be baptized. What he does say, in a famous sentence of Against Heresies II.22.4, is that Christ came to save every stage of human life, "all… who through Him are born again to God — infants, and children, and boys, and youths, and old men." Because Irenaeus elsewhere calls baptism "the new birth unto God," paedobaptist writers read this line as counting infants among the baptized-and-reborn — which would make him, c. 180, the earliest (implicit) witness to infant baptism, a generation before Tertullian. The vocabulary link is genuinely his; whether this particular sentence documents the practice, as against asserting that the incarnation sanctifies infancy, is contested and is weighed on its own argument page.

Claims & evidence

Infants are named among "all… who through Him are born again to God" (Against Heresies II.22.4)

For He came to save all through means of Himself—all, I say, who through Him are born again to God—infants, and children, and boys, and youths, and old men. He therefore passed through every age, becoming an infant for infants, thus sanctifying infants; a child for children, thus sanctifying those who are of this age, being at the same time made to them an example of piety, righteousness, and submission; a youth for youths… So likewise He was an old man for old men, that He might be a perfect Master for all, not merely as respects the setting forth of the truth, but also as regards age, sanctifying at the same time the aged also.

— Against Heresies II.22.4, source (cf. New Advent).1 The sentence sits inside Irenaeus's argument for recapitulation: against Gnostics who confined Christ's saving work (and, on one reading of theirs, his very ministry) to a narrow span, Irenaeus insists that the Son "passed through every age" — was himself infant, child, youth, and old man — so that he might sanctify each age from within. The catalogue of ages ("infants… and old men") is therefore, in the first place, a list of the life-stages Christ redeemed, not a note on who was being brought to the font. The Latin behind "born again to God" is renascuntur in Deum.

For Irenaeus, "the new birth unto God" is baptism (Demonstration 3; Against Heresies III.17.1)

First of all it bids us bear in mind that we have received baptism for the remission of sins, in the name of God the Father, and in the name of Jesus Christ… And that this baptism is the seal of eternal life, and is the new birth unto God, that we should no longer be the sons of mortal men, but of the eternal and perpetual God.

— The Demonstration of the Apostolic Preaching 3, source.2 This is the crux of the paedobaptist reading. Irenaeus expressly equates baptism with "the new birth unto God" — the very phrase (renasci in Deum) he applies to infants in II.22.4. He does the same in Against Heresies III.17.1, where "the power of regeneration into God" is glossed by the baptismal command of Matthew 28:19: "Go and teach all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Ghost."3 The nineteenth-century Ante-Nicene Fathers editor accordingly annotated the II.22.4 phrase: the reference "is doubtless to baptism, as clearly appears from comparing book iii. 17, 1." So "born again to God" is, in Irenaeus's own idiom, baptismal language — and he names infants among those it describes.

Notes

  • What the passage does and does not settle. Two things are true at once and should be kept apart. First, Irenaeus's vocabulary is baptismal: "the new birth unto God" is his own definition of baptism, so when he says infants are "born again to God" he is, on the natural reading, including them among the baptized. Second, the burden of II.22.4 is recapitulation, not a survey of baptismal practice — Irenaeus is proving that Christ sanctified every age by living through it, and the reference to infants being "born again" may describe the redemption the incarnation brings to infancy rather than report that infants were, as a matter of custom, carried to the font. Even Kurt Aland — a defender of infant baptism — judged the text less than decisive, while Joachim Jeremias read it as evidence for the practice's antiquity; Everett Ferguson allows it "could be the earliest reference to infant baptism," but only cautiously.4 That live disagreement is adjudicated on the "reborn to God" argument page; the honest verdict is contested — strong by the lights of Irenaeus's own baptismal vocabulary, weaker as direct documentary evidence that infants were being baptized.
  • Relation to Tertullian. If the reading holds, Irenaeus (c. 180) pushes the implicit evidence a generation earlier than Tertullian's explicit c. 203 objection. The two do not conflict: Tertullian confirms that infant baptism was a real, current practice (he argues against it), while Irenaeus — if he is speaking of baptism here — shows the assumption that infants belong among the reborn already in place in the preceding generation. Neither father gives us a narrated infant baptism; Tertullian's is the earliest firm evidence, Irenaeus's the earliest possible one.
