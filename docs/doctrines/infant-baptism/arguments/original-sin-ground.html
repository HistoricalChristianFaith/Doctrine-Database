Does infants’ inherited sin (Psalm 51:5, Romans 5:12) require their baptism for the remission of sin?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — as an account of the practice’s oldest positive rationale it is on firm ground: from Cyprian and Origen to Augustine and the anti-Pelagian councils, the Latin West grounded the urgency of baptizing the newborn in inherited sin cleansed “for the remission of sins,” and Scripture does teach a real inherited sinful condition (Romans 5; Psalm 51:5; Ephesians 2:3). But the further step the argument needs — that infants bear damnable guilt requiring sacramental remission — is exactly what the Christian East (which baptizes infants on a different ground) and the later credobaptist tradition deny; the two load-bearing proof-texts carry real cruxes (Romans 5:12’s eph’ hō and the Latin “in whom all sinned”; Psalm 51:5 as penitential hyperbole); and the strict form “no baptism, therefore lost” proves too much — softened by the very tradition that pressed it. Strong by Augustinian/Latin lights, weak by Eastern and credobaptist ones: a genuinely divided question · advanced by Cyprian, Origen, and Ambrose, systematized by Augustine and made conciliar law at Carthage (418)

Related arguments: the saving ground of baptism — see also baptism’s regenerating necessity (John 3:5)

The claim

Set beside the covenant argument (which reasons from the infant’s covenant membership) and the proof-texts of example (Mark 10:14, 1 Corinthians 7:14), this is the oldest positive rationale the fathers give for baptizing an infant, and it reasons from the infant’s need. Its structure is simple:

1. The infant is not innocent. Though it has done no personal act, a newborn contracts from Adam a real sin or defilement — David’s “in sin did my mother conceive me,” Paul’s “in Adam all sinned”:

Behold, I was shapen in iniquity; and in sin did my mother conceive me. (Psalm 51:5)

Wherefore, as by one man sin entered into the world, and death by sin; and so death passed upon all men, for that all have sinned. (Romans 5:12)

2. Baptism is “for the remission of sins.” The apostolic formula (Acts 2:38) makes remission of sin the thing baptism effects.

3. Therefore the infant needs it, and needs it early. If the newborn carries Adam’s sin and baptism remits sin, the infant is a fit — indeed an urgent — subject: not to wait the eighth day of the circumcision type, but to be brought “without delay,” lest anything hinder the grace it needs.1

Who advances it

This is the engine of the Western case, and it runs earlier than Augustine. Origen (c. 248) reasons from the custom of baptizing infants “for the remission of sins” to the “stains of birth” the rite must be putting away, asking why else the Law would command a sin-offering for a newborn (Leviticus 12). Cyprian’s council of sixty-six bishops (c. 253) grounds baptizing the newborn without delay in the fact that, though the infant “has not sinned” by any act of its own, it has “contracted the contagion of the ancient death” from Adam. Ambrose (c. 387) argues that no age is exempt from the sacrament because no age is exempt from sin. Augustine (c. 412) welds it into system, making the universal practice the decisive proof of original sin: since the Church everywhere baptizes infants for the remission of sins, and infants commit no actual sins, “it is the guilt of original sin which is healed in them by the grace of Him who saves them by the laver of regeneration.” The anti-Pelagian synods and the plenary Council of Carthage (418) make it binding doctrine, anathematizing any who say the newborn is baptized but “derive[s] from Adam no original sin, which needs to be removed by the laver of regeneration.” Jerome presses the same in his Dialogue Against the Pelagians. Modern Catholic apologetics restates it as the biblical ground of the practice.2

The evidence for

The steelman drops the strictest form (see below) and rests on two solid points.

  • Scripture really does teach an inherited sinful condition. The argument is not built on one disputed verse. Psalm 51:5 sets sin at the point of conception; Romans 5:12–19 makes Adam the head in whom “death passed upon all men”; Paul elsewhere calls all men “by nature the children of wrath” (Ephesians 2:3); Job asks “Who can bring a clean thing out of an unclean? not one” (Job 14:4), which the fathers pair with the Septuagint’s “no one is clean from stain, not even if his life on earth be but one day.” That the newborn is not a moral blank slate is not an Augustinian novelty: the oldest exegetes of Psalm 51:5 — Origen, Augustine, Jerome, and later Aquinas — all read it as inherited iniquity drawn from Adam, not merely as the circumstances of one conception.3
  • It is the practice’s actual, and earliest, positive rationale. Where the covenant and household arguments are Reformation-era systematizations read back onto the texts, the sin-remission ground is the one the ancient church itself gives — and gives while treating the practice as beyond dispute. Its antiquity (Origen and Cyprian, pre-Augustinian) and its power to explain the urgency of infant baptism (Cyprian’s “without delay,” the foundling canon’s anxiety lest a child die unbaptized) are real: no other rationale accounts as well for why the early church hurried the newborn to the font.

Adversarial assessment

What holds. Two things are secure. First, the historical claim: original sin, not any child-proof-text, is the oldest positive engine of Western paedobaptism, and it is genuinely pre-Augustinian. Second, the biblical premise in its broad form: humanity inherits from Adam a sinful, death-bound condition — a teaching of Romans 5 that survives every reading of its disputed clause, and that even the traditions which reject the argument’s conclusion (the Christian East, most credobaptists) affirm in some form. If inherited guilt is real and baptism remits it, the infant’s need follows, and follows urgently.

What is contested or overstated. The weight rests on inferential steps that a large part of the church has always refused:

  • Inherited guilt vs. inherited corruption. The argument needs the infant to bear not merely Adam’s mortality and corruption but a personal, damnable guilt that a sacrament must remit. That is precisely the step the Christian East declines. Preaching a generation before Augustine, Chrysostom baptized infants as a matter of course yet called them “sinless” and pointedly left the remission of sins off the list of what their baptism confers, grounding the rite in the abundance of grace instead; Eastern Orthodoxy to this day speaks of an inherited “ancestral sin” — mortality and a bent toward sin — while denying inherited guilt. So a church that has baptized infants unbrokenly for as long as the West grounds the very same practice on a rationale this argument treats as its foundation. The ground is not the practice; it is one reading of it.4
  • The Romans 5:12 crux. The strongest transmission-of-guilt reading historically leaned on the Latin: Ambrosiaster and then the Vulgate rendered Paul’s Greek eph’ hō (ἐφ’ ὅ) as in quo — “in whom [Adam] all sinned” — the very phrase Augustine builds on (“in Adam all have sinned”). But most modern exegetes (Fitzmyer, Cranfield) render eph’ hō “because” / “inasmuch as all sinned,” not “in whom” — so the sharpest form of the inherited-guilt reading rests partly on a translation the Greek does not compel. This does not overturn Romans 5 (the passage still teaches a real solidarity in Adam’s death, on any construal); it weakens the specific move from that solidarity to an imputed guilt the infant must have washed away.5
  • Psalm 51:5 as penitential hyperbole. The verse is a great sinner’s confession, not a treatise on infancy, and it can be read as David tracing his adultery back to the sinful condition he has had from the first — a representative, hyperbolic self-accusation — rather than as the doctrine that every newborn bears remissible guilt at conception. Augustine himself concedes the difficulty: David, born of lawful marriage, is not literally “conceived in” anyone’s sin, so Augustine has him speak “in the person of mankind.” That representative reading is available to the argument — and equally available to its critics, who take the verse as witnessing the human condition without licensing the inference to sacramental necessity in infancy.6
  • It proves too much. If baptism is necessary to remit a damnable guilt the infant already bears, the unbaptized infant who dies is lost — the inference Pelagius resisted and Augustine pressed (to a “mildest” punishment). The tradition itself flinched at the conclusion: Ambrose would not say whether such infants “have the honor of the kingdom”; Gregory of Nazianzus held that infants who die unbaptized without any fault of their own are “neither glorified nor punished”; the medieval West devised the limbo of infants to spare them hell’s pain; a canon assigning them no happy “middle place,” sometimes cited under Carthage 418, is of doubtful authenticity; and modern Catholic teaching entrusts them to God’s mercy. A conclusion the holding tradition so persistently softened is a sign the strict “need → necessity → damnation” chain overreaches its evidence.7
  • The credobaptist grants the premise and denies the conclusion. The Reformed and Baptist traditions affirm original sin as strongly as Augustine, yet deny that it warrants infant baptism: remission is applied, they hold, through faith and regeneration, not by a rite worked on a passive infant, and every baptism-for-remission the New Testament narrates attends repentance and belief. On this view the doctrine of inherited sin is true and the inference to the font simply does not follow — the infant’s real need is met in Christ by faith, in God’s time, not by sacramental application in the cradle. The argument therefore does not settle the question against them; it presupposes a baptismal realism (baptism remits original guilt ex opere operato) that is itself one of the things in dispute.8

Assessment

Contested. As history, the argument is sound: inherited sin is the oldest and most powerful positive reason the ancient church gave for baptizing the newborn, it is genuinely pre-Augustinian, and it best explains the practice’s urgency. As biblical theology in its broad form it stands too: Scripture teaches a real inherited sinful condition that even the argument’s opponents concede. But the interpretation the argument serves — that infants bear a damnable Adamic guilt which baptism must sacramentally remit, so that the rite is their necessary remedy — is genuinely disputed, and disputed along the deepest fault lines in the church: the Christian East baptizes the same infants while denying inherited guilt and grounding the rite in grace; the credobaptist affirms original sin while denying it warrants infant baptism; and the two proof-texts carry real cruxes (the Latin “in whom” of Romans 5:12; Psalm 51:5’s hyperbole), while the strict necessity-form proves too much and was softened by the very tradition that held it. Strong by Augustinian and Latin-sacramental lights, weak by Eastern and credobaptist ones — the signature of a contested claim rather than a sound or weak one. One proponent overreach attaches to the strict form: Augustine’s inference that unbaptized infants who die suffer condemnation — the “proves too much” edge his own tradition would not follow to the end — is flagged on his page. Its companion in the saving-ground pair is baptism’s regenerating necessity (the remedy side — John 3:5 / Titus 3:5 — to this argument’s need side); see also the covenant-circumcision argument (the covenant-membership ground, also contested), with which this positive rationale is often paired.
