Does Romans 6’s dying-and-rising — buried with Christ, walking in newness of life — require a believing subject, excluding infants?

Type: argument against infant baptism (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — a double-edged text, adduced on both sides. The credobaptist reads Paul’s death-burial-resurrection imagery (“we are buried with him by baptism into death… even so we also should walk in newness of life”) as a conscious dying-to-self and rising, which a newborn cannot enact, so the rite presupposes a believing, repentant subject. As an account of how an adult convert comes to the font that is sound — the passage really does read baptism as a willed appropriation of Christ’s death issuing in a changed life, and a baptism that meant nothing and bore no fruit would empty the sign. But as a bar against baptizing a believer’s infant it overreaches at three points. The governing verbs are passive — “we were baptized,” “we are buried with him” — a divine act done to the subject (union with Christ effected by God), not a psychological feat the recipient performs, so the infant’s incapacity is no more a bar than the adult’s. “Walk in newness of life” is a purpose-clause — the obligation and fruit baptism sets in motion, realized as the baptized grows — not a precondition already met (the adult convert, too, walks newly only after the font). And Paul’s own next breath ties this same burial-with-Christ to “the circumcision made without hands” (Colossians 2:11–12), the covenant sign given to eight-day-old infants — so the apostle who wrote Romans 6 did not treat “buried with Christ” as excluding the non-professing covenant member. Yet the paedobaptist counter does not simply win the other way: Colossians 2:12 grounds the rising “through faith,” and its covenant-continuity inference is itself disputed. Genuinely two-edged — strong for the credobaptist as far as the conversion of adults reaches, no exclusion of covenant children · the credo use appears in the popular catalogs (GotQuestions; John MacArthur), the paedo counter in the Reformed and Lutheran treatments of the passage (OPC; Ligonier; the covenant-circumcision case)

Related arguments: the order of faith and baptism — see also the pattern of faith-then-baptism in Acts, the immersion-mode argument (the mode read off the same burial imagery), baptism as the new-covenant circumcision (the Colossians 2 tie), and the paedobaptist reply, baptism’s ground in God’s Word, not the recipient’s faith

The claim

Paul reads baptism as a dying, burial, and rising with Christ that issues in a new manner of life:

Know ye not, that so many of us as were baptized into Jesus Christ were baptized into his death? Therefore we are buried with him by baptism into death: that like as Christ was raised up from the dead by the glory of the Father, even so we also should walk in newness of life. (Romans 6:3–4)

The credobaptist presses the subject this presupposes. Baptism here is not a bare washing but a dramatized death-to-self and resurrection to “newness of life” — a dying and rising the one baptized undergoes and then lives out. A newborn can neither die to a self it has not formed nor rise to walk a life it cannot yet lead; the “newness of life” the passage aims at is a moral turning no infant has made. So (the argument runs) the rite as Paul defines it fits only a conscious, repentant believer, and to pour it over an infant is to enact a burial-and-resurrection in someone who has died and risen to nothing. This is the distinct claim about the subject — kept separate here from the cognate mode argument, which reads the same burial imagery to fix how the water is applied (immersion, a going-under and rising), not to whom.1

Who advances it

In its popular form the argument is a staple of the credobaptist catalogs, usually run alongside the faith-precedes-baptism case: GotQuestions cites Romans 6:3–4 for baptism as the believer’s “identification with Christ in His death, burial, and resurrection,” and concludes that since “an infant cannot repent and place his or her faith in Christ,” baptism “should only be performed on those who have chosen to believe.” John MacArthur states it as the plain sense of the chapter — “Christian baptism is a picture of the union of a believer in the death, burial and resurrection of Christ.” The reading is old: it is already implicit in Hubmaier’s and Schleitheim’s insistence that baptism belongs to those who “believe truly that their sins are taken away.” On the paedobaptist side the same verses are turned back: the Reformed and Lutheran treatments read Romans 6 as God’s objective act on the recipient and yoke it to the covenant sign of Colossians 2 — the ground on which the Orthodox and the Reformed baptize the same infants the credobaptist would exclude.2

The evidence for

Steelmanned, the credobaptist reading rests on real features of the text and its earliest exposition:

  • The passage is unmistakably about a death and a resurrection, not a mere rinsing. Paul does not say the baptized are cleansed; he says they are “buried with him… into death” and raised to “walk in newness of life.” The rite images the most drastic personal reversal there is — the end of one life and the beginning of another.1
  • The fathers who expound it draw out its moral, willed dimension. On these very verses Chrysostom says that as Christ’s death and burial were real, so is the baptized person’s death to sin — and therefore “what is of our part again must be contributed”: the resurrection Paul intends is enacted “in the present life by a change of habits,” “when the fornicator becomes chaste, the covetous man merciful.” A death to sin one contributes to, and a changed life that proves it, are the acts of an agent, not of a newborn.3
  • It coheres with the whole shape of the New Testament’s baptisms. Everywhere a baptism is narrated it follows the hearing and believing of the word, and Peter calls the rite “the answer of a good conscience toward God” (1 Peter 3:21) — a conscience answering, like a life freely made new, being the act of one who can believe. Romans 6 is not a stray proof-text but the theological statement of what those conversions enacted.4

Adversarial assessment

What holds. For the conversion of an adult the reading is right and is granted. Baptism is not a bare ritual; it signs a real union with Christ in his death and resurrection, and it lays on the baptized the obligation to “walk in newness of life.” A theology that baptized adults for whom the rite meant nothing, and from whom no new life was expected, would run flat against Romans 6. So far the credobaptist has the text with him.

What is overstated. The leap from “baptism enacts a dying-and-rising” to “therefore only one who can consciously enact it may be baptized” outruns what Paul says, at three points.

  • The governing verbs are passive — God’s act, not the recipient’s feat. Paul writes that we “were baptized into his death” and “are buried with him” — the baptized are the object acted upon, and it is God who buries them with Christ (the “divine passive”). The dying-and-rising is union with Christ effected from outside, not a psychological transaction the recipient generates; as the Reformed and Lutheran expositors of the passage put it, Paul “does not say we are buried with Christ because we are baptized,” and the verbs describe what God “accomplishes… through” the sacrament. On that reading the infant’s inability to introspect is no more a bar to being buried-with-Christ than the adult’s is — both are buried by the same divine act. This is the Word-not-faith point applied to Romans 6: the credobaptist premise that a valid baptism requires the recipient’s own present, conscious appropriation is exactly what the paedobaptist denies, with reasons.5
  • “Walk in newness of life” is a purpose and fruit, not a precondition. The clause is final — baptized into his death that we should walk newly — naming the end baptism serves and the obligation it imposes, realized after and because of the rite. The adult convert, too, does not walk in newness of life before he is baptized; the new walk is the sequel, not the entrance requirement. So the passage no more demands a prior conscious walk of an infant than of an adult: to make “newness of life” a threshold the candidate must already have crossed is to read a consequence as a credential.6
  • Paul’s own parallel yokes this burial to the infant covenant sign. The same “buried with him in baptism” recurs a chapter’s breath later set beside circumcision: “in whom also ye are circumcised with the circumcision made without hands… buried with him in baptism” (Colossians 2:11–12). Circumcision was the covenant sign given by command to the eight-day-old infant who could not profess — and Paul draws the two together, as Chrysostom notes on the spot: “what he calls circumcision, he again calls burial… When and where? In Baptism.” So the apostle who wrote “buried with him… walk in newness of life” placed that very burial next to a sign applied to those who could not consciously enact anything. The credobaptist reads Romans 6’s burial as excluding the non-professing subject; Paul’s own development of the image in Colossians does not.7

Where the paedobaptist must not overclaim. The Colossians tie does not simply settle the matter the other way. Colossians 2:12 grounds the rising “through faith of the operation of God,” and its earliest expositor stresses just that — Chrysostom: “he hath well said, ‘of faith,’ for it is all of faith.” And the covenant-continuity inference that carries the circumcision parallel from an ancient sign to a modern infant — that new-covenant membership tracks descent as the Abrahamic sign did — is itself disputed and is weighed contested on its own page. So the paedobaptist who quotes Colossians 2 as a knock-down for infant baptism claims more than the verse gives, just as the credobaptist who quotes Romans 6 as a knock-down against it does.8

Assessment

Contested. Romans 6:3–4 is genuinely double-edged, adduced on both sides, and neither side carries it outright. The credobaptist reading — baptism as a dying-to-self and rising to a new life — is true to the text as an account of how an adult convert comes to the font, and it rightly resists any picture of baptism as an empty rinsing that expects no changed life; a father as central as Chrysostom draws out exactly its moral, willed dimension. But as a bar against the baptism of a believer’s infant it overreaches. The governing verbs are passive — a burial-with-Christ God works on the subject, not a feat the recipient performs — so the infant’s incapacity is no more disqualifying than the adult’s; “walk in newness of life” is the purpose and fruit baptism sets going, not a threshold already crossed; and Paul’s own next use of the image sets this burial beside “the circumcision made without hands” (Colossians 2:11–12), the covenant sign given to infants, so the apostle himself did not read “buried with Christ” as excluding the non-professing covenant member. Yet the paedobaptist counter does not simply reverse the verdict: Colossians 2:12 anchors the rising “through faith,” and its covenant-continuity premise is itself contested. Like the broader pattern-of-Acts argument to which it is kin, the passage describes the conversion of adults and is silent on the covenant status of believers’ children; it neither commands nor forbids their baptism. Strong for the credobaptist as far as the conversion of adults reaches, weak as the exclusion of covenant infants it is pressed to be — and where either side quotes it as though it settled the subject question, the argument claims more than the evidence gives.
