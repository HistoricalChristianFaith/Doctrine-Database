Does the New Testament order — repentance and faith, then baptism — exclude infants?

Type: argument against infant baptism (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — the sequence the argument reads off the New Testament is real: wherever a baptism is narrated it follows the hearing and believing of the word (“Repent, and be baptized,” Acts 2:38; “when they believed… they were baptized,” Acts 8:12), and no paedobaptist reverses that order on the mission field — an adult convert is catechized before the font. But every clear instance is a first-generation adult convert, and the texts are simply silent on the children of those already believing; as a bar against baptizing a believer’s infant the argument overreaches at three points. Two of its four stock proof-texts are textually disputed — Mark 16:16 stands in the longer ending absent from the earliest Greek codices, and the Ethiopian’s confession (Acts 8:37) is a Western interpolation missing from the oldest manuscripts. The covenant sign it appeals to was itself given, under the old covenant, to a believer’s eight-day-old son (Isaac) who could not yet believe, so “faith, then the sign” was already the adult-convert order, not a universal rule. And the paedobaptist tradition answers the “he that believeth and is baptized” text head-on — an infant, said Bede, “believes by others,” through the faith of the Church and its sponsors, while Luther relocates a valid baptism onto God’s Word and makes the recipient’s faith receptive rather than constitutive. Strong as an account of how converts come to baptism, weak as the knock-down exclusion of covenant infants · adduced by the Schleitheim Confession (1527), John Smyth (1609), and the popular credobaptist catalogs

Related arguments: the order of faith and baptism — see also the Great Commission’s order (Matthew 28:19)

The claim

The credobaptist reads a fixed order off the New Testament’s baptismal texts: the word is preached, the hearer repents and believes, and then he is baptized. Peter’s Pentecost charge puts repentance first:

Repent, and be baptized every one of you in the name of Jesus Christ for the remission of sins, and ye shall receive the gift of the Holy Ghost. (Acts 2:38)

The same sequence, the argument runs, is written across the record: “He that believeth and is baptized shall be saved” (Mark 16:16); the Samaritans “when they believed Philip preaching… were baptized” (Acts 8:12); the Ethiopian eunuch is told “If thou believest with all thine heart, thou mayest,” and confesses his faith before Philip baptizes him (Acts 8:36–37).1 Since repentance and personal faith are things a newborn cannot exercise, the infant is not a proper subject of baptism at all. The Schleitheim Confession made the point confessional — baptism belongs to those “who have learned repentance and amendment of life, and who believe truly that their sins are taken away by Christ… and who with this significance request it… This excludes all infant baptism” — appealing to exactly these texts as “the foundation and testimony of the apostles.”2 In its popular modern form the claim is that baptism is “an act of obedience subsequent to faith,” so that one must be old enough to believe before being baptized.3

Who advances it

This is the oldest and broadest of the credobaptist arguments — the pattern-of-the-New-Testament claim that underlies the more particular exegetical cases. Schleitheim (1527) states it as bare principle; John Smyth (1609) sharpens the Great-Commission half of it into a syllogism (weighed on its own page); and the modern Baptist confessions and apologetics catalogs run it as the plain sense of Acts — every narrated baptism following a professed faith. It is the reasoning behind the very name believers’ baptism.3

The evidence for

Steelmanned — set aside the disputed proof-texts and the case still stands on secure ground:

  • The order is uniform wherever the New Testament narrates a baptism. Those who “gladly received his word were baptized” (Acts 2:41); the Samaritans “when they believed… were baptized” (Acts 8:12); Lydia, “whose heart the Lord opened,” then her household (Acts 16:14–15); the Corinthians “hearing believed, and were baptized” (Acts 18:8). No narrated instance reverses the order, and none names an infant. Peter at Pentecost does not even say believe before he says be baptized — he says repent; as Chrysostom notes, the charge specifies “the conditions” a hearer must meet.4
  • The meaning of the rite presupposes a conscious subject. Baptism is a burial and rising with Christ — “we are buried with him by baptism into death… even so we also should walk in newness of life” (Romans 6:4) — and Peter calls it “the answer of a good conscience toward God” (1 Peter 3:21). A dying-and-rising freely willed, and a conscience answering, are acts of a person who can believe, not of a newborn.5
  • The whole apostolic mission runs in this direction. The Great Commission subordinates baptism to disciple-making, and the book of Acts is a record of that mission: preaching, response, baptism, in that order, from Pentecost to Ephesus. The credobaptist reading is not a stray inference from one verse but the grain of the narrative.6

Adversarial assessment

What holds. For the conversion of those outside the covenant the order is not in dispute. No paedobaptist baptizes an unevangelized adult before he professes faith; on the mission field the sequence “preach, believe, baptize” is simply how the church has always worked, and the pattern of Acts states it plainly. A theology that baptized professing adults without faith would run flat against the text. That much the credobaptist has right, and it is granted.

What is overstated. The leap from “converts believe before baptism” to “believers’ infants may not be baptized” outruns the evidence at three points, and two of the proof-texts marshalled for it will not bear weight.

  • Every clear instance is a first-generation adult convert. Acts 2, 8, 16, and 18 narrate the church’s expansion among those hearing the gospel for the first time — Jews at Pentecost, Samaritans, an Ethiopian traveller, Corinthians. Of course such people believe before they are baptized: there is no other way an adult outsider enters the church. But the texts describe how the unreached come to faith and baptism; they say nothing, one way or the other, about the newborn child of a believer already inside the covenant. The argument reads a rule for converts as if it were a rule for everyone — the same silence the Great-Commission argument founders on.6
  • The covenant sign was already given to a believer’s child who could not believe. Under the old covenant the order “faith, then the sign” held for the adult convert — Abraham believed and “received the sign of circumcision, a seal of the righteousness of the faith” (Romans 4:11) — yet the same Abraham circumcised the infant Isaac on the eighth day, with no personal faith of the child’s at all (Genesis 17:12). So the God who required a professed faith of the adult proselyte commanded the covenant sign for the believer’s infant regardless of it. “Faith precedes the sign” is therefore the adult-convert pattern, not a universal law — which is precisely the paedobaptist’s point (weighed on the covenant/circumcision page).7
  • The paedobaptist tradition answers the “believeth and is baptized” text directly. Confronted with Mark 16:16, Bede asks the objection’s own question — “What shall we say here about infants, who by reason of their age cannot yet believe?” — and answers: “In the Church… of our Saviour children believe by others, as also they drew from others the sins which are remitted to them in baptism.” This is Augustine’s doctrine that a speechless infant is baptized “through the sacrament of that faith,” his sponsors answering for him. And Luther cuts under the objection entirely: “Baptism is valid, even though faith be wanting. For my faith does not make Baptism, but receives it” — the ground of a valid baptism is God’s Word, not the recipient’s act. So the premise that a valid baptism requires the recipient’s own present faith is exactly what the paedobaptist denies, and denies with reasons; the argument assumes it rather than proving it.8
  • Two of the four stock proof-texts are textually disputed. Mark 16:16 belongs to the “longer ending” (Mark 16:9–20), absent from the two oldest Greek codices (Sinaiticus and Vaticanus) and reported by Eusebius and Jerome to be missing from nearly all the accurate copies known to them; the great majority of scholars judge it no part of Mark’s original. The Ethiopian’s confession (Acts 8:37) is a Western interpolation — a baptismal formula absent from the earliest and best witnesses (P45, P74, ℵ, A, B, C) and relegated to the margin or footnotes in modern critical editions. Both are ancient — Irenaeus and Cyprian already cite the eunuch’s words in the second and third centuries — so they testify to an early baptismal practice of professed faith; but as original words of Scripture they cannot carry the weight a proponent who quotes them as plain text supposes. The credobaptist pattern survives on the secure texts (Acts 2:38; 8:12; 18:8), but a case that leans on Mark 16:16 or Acts 8:37 as though textually settled overreaches.9

Assessment

Contested. The order the argument reads off the New Testament — repentance and faith, then baptism — is genuine, uniform in every narrated case, and conceded by paedobaptists for the conversion of those outside the covenant: on the mission field no one is baptized before he believes. As an account of how converts come to the font the credobaptist reading is sound. But it does not reach the conclusion pressed from it. Every clear instance is a first-generation adult convert, and the texts are silent on the children of believers; the covenant sign was, even under the old covenant, given to a believer’s infant who could not yet believe; and the premise that a valid baptism requires the recipient’s own faith is denied with reasons by the whole paedobaptist tradition, which holds that the infant believes through the faith of the Church (Bede, Augustine) or that the Word, not the recipient’s faith, makes the sacrament (Luther). Two of the four proof-texts most often cited — Mark 16:16 and Acts 8:37 — rest on disputed text and cannot be quoted as settled Scripture, though the pattern stands without them. So the New Testament’s faith-then-baptism order neither commands nor forbids the baptism of a believer’s infant: strong for the credobaptist as far as the conversion of adults reaches, weak as the exclusion of covenant children it is pressed to be. Where a proponent presents these texts as if they addressed believers’ children, or quotes Mark 16:16 or Acts 8:37 as textually secure, the argument claims more than the evidence gives.
