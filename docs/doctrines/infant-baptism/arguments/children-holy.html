Does Paul’s calling believers’ children “holy” (1 Corinthians 7:14) warrant baptizing them?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — as a warrant for baptizing. The verse really does set a believer’s children in a “holy,” not “unclean,” category — genuine covenant standing — but the step from that set-apartness to “a saint, therefore to be baptized” equivocates on hagios and is undercut within the same verse, which calls the pagan spouse equally “sanctified” though no one baptizes him; the whole ancient exegetical tradition reads the “holiness” as the legitimacy of the marriage and its offspring, not a baptismal title · advanced by Douglas Wilson (popular Reformed paedobaptist form)

The claim

In 1 Cor. 7:14, what does the apostle Paul call children of at least one believing parent? He says they are hagia, saints… It is not our custom to leave any saint unbaptized.

— Douglas Wilson, “All Saints Should Be Baptized”, dougwils.com. The argument is a compressed syllogism: in 1 Corinthians 7:14 Paul calls the children of at least one believing parent hagia (“holy”), the adjective whose substantive is hagioi, “saints”; saints are baptized; therefore believers’ infants should be baptized. Holy → a saint → saints are baptized → baptize the child.

Who advances it

Wilson states the popular Reformed-paedobaptist form of an old proof-text. The verse has been read as covenantal support for infant baptism at least since the Reformation — the Reformed tradition cites it among the passages showing that a believer’s children stand within the covenant — and it recurs in modern paedobaptist apologetics as a supplement to the main covenant-circumcision case. Wilson’s contribution is only the brisk syllogism (“hagia, saints… we leave no saint unbaptized”); the underlying covenant-standing reading is the serious version, and it is that version, not the syllogism, that this page weighs.

Wilson frames the syllogism inside a prior claim about what baptism is: “the foundational reality is that it is a statement that God makes… a statement of ownership, of possession,” not first a statement the believer makes. On that footing, he grants, “in the case of infants, we need to have warrant for saying that God in fact wants to make this statement” over a given child — and 1 Corinthians 7:14 is offered as exactly that warrant, since it is where Paul classes the believer’s child as hagia.1 The theological packaging changes nothing in the load-bearing step, though: the warrant still reduces to the inference from “the child is hagia” to “the child is a saint God means to claim at the font,” which is the very move the assessment below weighs. Grant Wilson’s premise that baptism is God’s statement of ownership and the case still stands or falls on whether 7:14’s “holy” is that ownership-title — and the same verse’s “sanctified” pagan spouse, whom God is not thereby said to claim at the font, is the standing obstacle.

The evidence for

Two things in the verse are real and in the proponent’s favor.

  • The word is genuinely covenant-holiness language. Paul does write that the children of a mixed marriage “are hagia” — the same root that names Christians “the saints” (hoi hagioi) throughout his letters. And he sets it in sharp antithesis: were it not so, “your children unclean (akatharta)” — the very word the Old Testament uses for what is outside the covenant and cannot approach God. So Paul is not making a throwaway remark: a believer’s child stands on the “holy” side of a holy/unclean divide, exactly the divide the covenant sign once marked.2
  • It supplies real covenant standing. This is why the verse has genuine force within the larger covenant argument: it is New Testament evidence that the children of believers are not simply “those afar off” but already occupy a set-apart status by virtue of a believing parent — the premise the covenant-circumcision case needs and that the credobaptist reading of the new covenant contests. As corroboration of covenant status, 7:14 pulls its weight.

Adversarial assessment

What holds. That believers’ children are called “holy” and not “unclean” is simply what the verse says, and it is a real datum for the covenant-standing of those children. Nobody disputes the lexical fact that hagios is the adjective behind “saints.”

Where it fails as a warrant for baptism. The syllogism turns on reading “holy” as “a saint — i.e. one of the baptized faithful,” and three things cut against that step.

  • The same verse sanctifies the pagan spouse. In the identical sentence the unbelieving husband “is sanctified (hēgiastai) by the wife,” and the unbelieving wife by the husband — the same holiness-vocabulary, of an idolater no one would dream of baptizing on that basis. Whatever “sanctified/holy” means here, it cannot mean “a person the church therefore baptizes,” or Paul would have licensed baptizing the heathen partner. The predicate that is supposed to carry the child to the font is applied, in the same breath, to someone it confessedly does not carry there. This is the decisive objection, and it is internal to the text.3
  • “Holy” here is relational set-apartness, not personal sainthood. The holy/unclean contrast in 7:14 is about the legitimacy of the marriage and the standing of its offspring, over against the “unclean” status Paul’s worried Corinthians feared a mixed union produced. The believing partner does not confer personal holiness on spouse or child — the spouse remains an unbeliever — but a relational set-apartness: the union is not defiling and the children are not illegitimate outsiders. To slide from that to “each child is a saint in the baptized-member sense” is to equivocate on hagios, trading its “set apart” sense for its “professing church member” sense.4
  • No ancient reader drew baptism from it — and the earliest explicitly refused to. Every early exegete of 7:14 reads the “holiness” as marital legitimacy / covenant set-apartness, not as a baptismal title. Chrysostom is emphatic that Paul “said not, He is holy; but, He is sanctified in his wife… not to signify that he is holy” but to free the wife from fear, and that the children are called “holy” only “by the intensity of the expression… that is, not unclean.” Theophylact repeats it: “holy” is a “superfluous expression” meaning “they are not unclean.” Aquinas glosses the children as “holy, i.e. become Christians” (or legitimate offspring), not as already baptized. Most tellingly, Tertullian — the earliest to expound the verse, and himself an opponent of infant baptism — expressly divides the child’s holiness from baptism: the children of believers are “designed for holiness, and thereby for salvation,” holy “as much by the prerogative of the (Christian) seed as by the discipline of the institution” — i.e. the seed’s holiness is one thing, the rite of baptism another. If 7:14 had plainly meant “these children are baptized saints,” the father who wanted to delay their baptism could not have quoted it so freely.5

The proponent’s overreach. The bare “hagia, saints → we baptize saints” move is the weak point: it treats an adjective of covenant set-apartness as though it were the roster-title of the baptized, and it would prove far too much (the sanctified pagan spouse). That specific misstep — resting the case on the lexical slide from “holy” to “a baptizable saint” — is flagged as overreach; the reading it is meant to serve survives only in its milder, covenant-standing form.

Assessment

Weak — as a warrant for baptizing. The verse genuinely places believers’ children in a “holy,” not “unclean,” category, and that covenant standing is a real datum — but standing is as far as it reaches. The inference to baptism runs through an equivocation on hagios (“set apart” → “a saint we baptize”) that the same verse refutes by applying the identical “sanctified” to the unbaptized pagan spouse, and no ancient exegete — least of all Tertullian, who both expounds the verse and opposes infant baptism — read it as a baptismal title. Its honest force is therefore corroborative of covenant status, feeding the covenant argument rather than warranting the font on its own; the proponent’s brisk “saint, so baptize” syllogism overreaches and is noted as such. For the covenant-standing case the verse actually supports, see the baptism-as-new-covenant-circumcision argument (assessment: contested) and the household baptisms (assessment: contested); like those, 7:14 corroborates without proving.
