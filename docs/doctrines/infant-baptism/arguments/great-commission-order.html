Does the Great Commission’s “make disciples… baptizing them” (Matthew 28:19) exclude infants from baptism?

Type: argument against infant baptism (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — the missionary order the argument reads off Matthew 28:19 is real and even patristically attested (Jerome: “first they teach all nations, then they immerse the instructed ones in water”), and the whole pattern of the commission-and-Acts presents baptism following a response to the preached word, so as an account of how the nations are first discipled the reading is well-grounded and no paedobaptist denies it; but as a proof that infants are excluded — still less Smyth’s stronger claim that the commission positively forbids their baptism — it overreaches: the one imperative is “make disciples [of] the nations,” with “baptizing” and “teaching” coordinate subordinate participles (not a fixed teach-then-baptize sequence — indeed the “teaching to observe” participle falls after baptism), the text addresses the church’s mission to unreached peoples, not the covenant status of believers’ children, on which it is silent, and the “an affirmative precept contains a negative” inference proves too much. Strong as an account of first-generation conversion, weak as a bar against covenant infants · adduced by John Smyth (1609) and the popular credobaptist catalogs

Related arguments: the order of faith and baptism — see also the pattern of faith-then-baptism in Acts

The claim

The risen Christ’s parting charge is the New Testament’s clearest command about who is to be baptized:

Go ye therefore, and teach all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Ghost: teaching them to observe all things whatsoever I have commanded you… (Matthew 28:19–20)

The credobaptist reading takes the sentence as fixing an order: one is first made a disciple (which requires teaching and a response of faith), and only then baptized. John Smyth, reasoning against paedobaptism in The Character of the Beast (1609), cast it as a syllogism and then pressed it into a prohibition:

They only are to be baptized that are made Disciples by teaching. Infants cannot be made Disciples by teaching. Ergo, Infants are not to be baptized… Every precept affirmative conteyneth a negative vnder it. Make Disciples by teaching & baptise them, is an affirmative conteyning vnder it, baptize not those that are not made Disciples by teaching. Ergo, those that are not by teaching made Disciples, are by Christ forbidden to be baptized.1

Because an infant cannot be taught or discipled, the argument runs, the Great Commission itself — the dominical warrant for baptism — withholds baptism from infants, and (on Smyth’s second, stronger form) positively forbids it. The same order underlies the popular credobaptist slogan that baptism is “an act of obedience subsequent to faith,” so that one must be old enough to believe before being baptized.2

Who advances it

The formal argument is Smyth’s, carrying the credobaptist case from Schleitheim’s bare confessional assertion (which had already cited Matthew 28 among “the foundation and testimony of the apostles”) into an explicit exegetical exclusion. But the exegesis was not new with Smyth: Balthasar Hubmaier, in On the Christian Baptism of Believers (1525), had already read Christ’s commission as fixing the order “the word, hearing, faith, baptism, works” and inferred that “since infants cannot be taught, they should not be baptised” — Smyth’s syllogism is the sharpened, formalized form of an argument the movement’s leading theologian had made eighty years earlier. In its modern form it is a staple of Baptist teaching and the popular apologetics catalogs, which read the commission’s sequence — disciple, then baptize — alongside the practice of Acts, where every narrated baptism follows the hearing and believing of the word.3

The evidence for

Steelmanned, the reading is not a bare word-order quibble. Three strands support it:

  • Baptism is grammatically subordinate to disciple-making. The sentence has one governing imperative — matheteusate, “make disciples” — and “baptizing” and “teaching” are subordinate participles describing how disciples are made. So baptism is not a free-standing rite but the seal of discipleship: one baptizes disciples. If discipleship is the thing of which baptism is the sign, a subject incapable of being discipled is, on the face of it, not a candidate.4
  • The pattern of Acts matches the order. Wherever the New Testament narrates a baptism, it follows the preaching of the word and a response of faith: those who “gladly received his word were baptized” (Acts 2:41); the Samaritans “when they believed… were baptized” (Acts 8:12); the Corinthians “hearing believed, and were baptized” (Acts 18:8). No narrated instance reverses the order, and none names an infant. The commission’s sequence and the church’s recorded practice agree.5
  • Even a paedobaptist father read the order this way. Jerome, commenting on the verse, is emphatic: “First they teach all nations, then they immerse the instructed ones in water. For it cannot be that the body receives the sacrament of baptism unless the soul has first embraced the truth with faith.” The Didache likewise sets instruction before the font (“having first said all these things, baptize”). So the teach-then-baptize sequence is not a Reformation novelty; it is the plain reading of ancient exegetes who practised infant baptism themselves.6

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The narrow point is sound and paedobaptists concede it: in the Great Commission baptism is subordinate to disciple-making, and the mission the text describes proceeds by preaching, response, and baptism — the order of Acts. No paedobaptist baptizes an adult convert from the nations before catechizing him; on the mission field the credobaptist sequence is simply how the church has always worked. That is real, and Jerome’s reading shows it is not in dispute.

What is overstated. As a proof that the verse excludes — still less forbids — the baptism of believers’ infants, the argument outruns its evidence at four points:

  • The participles are coordinate means, not a fixed sequence. “Make disciples” is the single imperative; “baptizing” (baptizontes) and “teaching” (didaskontes) are subordinate present participles “in co-ordination with each other,” both modes of disciple-making, not two ranked steps. Decisively, the “teaching” participle on which Smyth’s premise leans — “teaching them to observe all things whatsoever I have commanded you” — stands after “baptizing” in Matthew’s own order: it is post-baptismal catechesis, not the pre-baptismal instruction the syllogism needs. What actually precedes baptism is “make disciples,” and whether making a disciple requires the kind of cognitive teaching an infant cannot receive is precisely the point in dispute — assumed by the argument, not established by it.4
  • The subject is “the nations,” not covenant children. The imperative governs panta ta ethnē — the commission commissions the church’s mission to unreached peoples. That is exactly the point every patristic reader draws from it: Chrysostom, Theophylact, and Bede all read “go and teach all nations” as the extension of the gospel from the Jews to the Gentiles. The verse answers “whom shall we evangelize?” (all peoples, not Israel only), not “may a believer’s child be baptized?” It describes how nations are first discipled and is simply silent on the children of those already discipled — neither commanding nor forbidding their baptism.7
  • The “affirmative contains a negative” move proves too much. Smyth’s second syllogism — that a command to baptize the taught positively forbids baptizing the un-taught — is a logical overreach. A command to do X to a specified subject does not, of itself, forbid doing X to others: “teach all nations” did not forbid teaching individuals, and by the same expressio-unius logic Calvin observed one could bar women from the Lord’s Supper, since none is named partaking of it in the apostolic record. It is an argument from silence recast as a prohibition.8
  • Both camps have always claimed the text. Schleitheim cited Matthew 28 as its apostolic foundation; the paedobaptists read the same commission as carrying baptism into the household — the baptized “households” of Acts that Calvin pressed (see the household-baptisms argument). A proof-text both sides wield with equal facility does not settle the question; it underdetermines it.

Assessment

Contested. The disciple-before-baptism order that the argument reads off the Great Commission is genuine, matches the practice of Acts, and is granted even by paedobaptist exegetes — Jerome states it outright. As an account of how first-generation converts from the nations come to baptism, the credobaptist reading is sound, and a theology that baptized the un-evangelized adult heathen would indeed run against the text. But the leap from that missionary order to a bar against baptizing the infant children of believers is not carried by the verse: the two participles are coordinate means rather than a fixed sequence, the “teaching” the syllogism rests on is the passage’s post-baptismal catechesis, the commission’s subject is the mission to the nations rather than the covenant status of believers’ children, and the “forbids” inference is an argument from silence that proves too much. So the commission neither commands nor forbids infant baptism — it is genuinely contested ground: strong for the credobaptist as far as the pattern of conversion reaches, weak as the knock-down exclusion Smyth pressed it to be. Where a proponent presents Matthew 28:19 as itself forbidding the baptism of a believer’s infant, that reads a positive prohibition out of a text that is silent on the case.
