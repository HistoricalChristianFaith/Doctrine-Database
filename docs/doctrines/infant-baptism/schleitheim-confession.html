The Schleitheim Confession on infant baptism

Dates: c. 1527 (the Brotherly Union — Brüderliche Vereinigung — adopted by an assembly of Swiss Brethren at Schleitheim, on the Swiss–German border, on 24 February 1527; its chief author was Michael Sattler, c. 1490–1527, an ex-Benedictine prior turned Anabaptist, burned at the stake at Rottenburg less than three months later) · Wikipedia
Summary: The Schleitheim Confession is the earliest witness on this timeline to reject infant baptism outright. Where Tertullian and Gregory of Nazianzus had contested only the timing of a child’s baptism, and the whole preceding tradition took infants to be fit subjects, Article 1 restricts baptism to those “who have learned repentance and amendment of life” and who ask for it themselves — and so “excludes all infant baptism, the highest and chief abomination of the pope.” It is the foundational confessional statement of the credobaptist (believers’-baptism) position that would define the Anabaptist movement and, later, the Baptist tradition.

Claims & evidence

Baptism is for those who repent, believe, and request it — which “excludes all infant baptism” (Article 1)

The first of the confession’s seven articles opens the movement’s programme by defining the subject of baptism — and in the same breath rules out the newborn:

First. Observe concerning baptism: Baptism shall be given to all those who have learned repentance and amendment of life, and who believe truly that their sins are taken away by Christ, and to all those who walk in the resurrection of Jesus Christ, and wish to be buried with Him in death, so that they may be resurrected with Him, and to all those who with this significance request it [baptism] of us and demand it for themselves. This excludes all infant baptism, the highest and chief abomination of the pope. In this you have the foundation and testimony of the apostles. Mt. 28, Mk. 16, Acts 2, 8, 16, 19. This we wish to hold simply, yet firmly and with assurance.

— The Schleitheim Confession of Faith, Article 1.1 The order of the clauses is the whole argument: repentance, then faith, then a self-conscious request — each a thing an infant cannot do — are made prerequisites of the rite, so that a child is not a candidate at all. The proof-texts appealed to (the Great Commission of Matthew 28 and Mark 16, and the adult conversions of Acts 2, 8, 16, and 19) are all cases where baptism follows on preaching and belief, which the Brethren read as the apostolic pattern — “the foundation and testimony of the apostles” — against a custom they regarded as a papal corruption.

Notes

  • Authorship and setting. The confession’s German title is Brüderliche Vereinigung etzlicher Kinder Gottes siben Artickel betreffend (“Brotherly Union of a Number of Children of God Concerning Seven Articles”). It was hammered out at a gathering of Swiss Brethren at Schleitheim on 24 February 1527, at a moment when the two-year-old movement was near collapse under persecution, and is now widely accepted as chiefly the work of Michael Sattler, a former prior of the Benedictine abbey of St. Peter’s in the Black Forest who had joined the Anabaptists.2 Sattler was arrested within weeks, tried, and executed on 20 May 1527; the articles circulated rapidly in Swiss and South-German Anabaptist circles and drew written refutations from both Zwingli and Calvin — a measure of how sharply they broke with the magisterial Reformers, who retained infant baptism.3
  • The first reversal on the timeline. Every earlier witness here — even those who counselled delay — assumed infants were fit subjects of baptism and disputed only when it should be administered: Tertullian preferred to wait, Gregory of Nazianzus set a rough age of three for the ordinary case, but neither denied that a child could be baptized (and both allowed it in danger of death). Schleitheim is categorically different: it makes conscious faith and a personal request constitutive of a valid baptism, so that infant baptism is not merely ill-timed but no baptism at all. This is the pivot from a timing question to a subject question — the axis on which paedobaptists and credobaptists still divide.
  • The proof-texts are adult conversions, two on disputed text. The apostolic “foundation and testimony” Article 1 invokes — Mt. 28, Mk. 16, Acts 2, 8, 16, 19 — are all missionary conversions of adults, cases where a hearer of course believes before he is baptized; they establish the order for converts but say nothing directly about the newborn child of a believer already within the covenant, which is the disputed point. That limitation, and the paedobaptist replies (the covenant/circumcision parallel, the infant’s faith “through others,” and the Word — not the recipient’s faith — as what makes a baptism) are weighed on the argument page Does the New Testament order — repentance and faith, then baptism — exclude infants? Two of the cited texts rest on contested manuscripts besides: Mark 16:16 belongs to the “longer ending” of Mark (16:9–20) absent from the earliest Greek codices, and the eunuch’s confession in Acts 8 (v. 37) is a “Western” interpolation missing from the oldest witnesses — so they cannot be quoted as plain apostolic text.4
  • “Rebaptism” and the charge of Anabaptism. Because the Brethren themselves had all been baptized as infants in the Catholic Church, baptizing believing adults meant, in their opponents’ eyes, re-baptizing them — hence the name Anabaptist (“rebaptizer”), which was a capital charge under the Justinian Code revived by the 1529 Diet of Speyer. On the Brethren’s own logic, of course, the infant washing had never been a baptism, so believers’ baptism was simply baptism. Article 1 states the principle from which that whole conflict followed.
