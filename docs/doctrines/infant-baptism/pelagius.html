Pelagius on infant baptism

Dates: c. 415 (Pelagius, c. 354–418; British-born ascetic and biblical commentator, active at Rome and then in the East. The passages below belong to the Pelagian controversy of c. 411–418 — his confession of faith sent to Rome and the reports of his and his disciple Coelestius’s position gathered by Augustine; c. 415 is used here as the timeline anchor, roughly the Synod of Diospolis and the years his concession was pressed into the open) · Wikipedia
Summary: Pelagius is a witness to infant baptism precisely because he is Augustine’s great opponent. The man who denied that Adam’s guilt is inherited never denied that the Church baptizes infants; he conceded the universal practice outright — “according to the rule of the Church universal… we confess” — and disputed only its rationale, refusing to grant that what the rite remits in a newborn is original sin. That is a hostile witness to how settled the custom already was by c. 415: the leading theological dissident of the age could quarrel with the meaning of infant baptism but could not deny the practice, and had every motive to.

Claims & evidence

He concedes the practice: infants are baptized “for the remission of sins,” by “the rule of the Church universal”

Pelagius’s own confession of faith, quoted verbatim by Augustine, grants the practice in the plainest terms — and grants even the customary formula that it is “for the remission of sins”:

That infants, however, ought to be baptized for the remission of sins, according to the rule of the Church universal, and according to the meaning of the Gospel, we confess. For the Lord has determined that the kingdom of heaven should only be conferred on baptized persons; and since the resources of nature do not possess it, it must necessarily be conferred by the gift of grace.

— Pelagius, quoted in Augustine, On the Grace of Christ, and on Original Sin (Book 2, On Original Sin) 5.1 Augustine himself concedes that, taken alone, the words sound orthodox: “if he had not said anything elsewhere on this subject, who would not have supposed that he acknowledged the remission of original sin even in infants at their baptism?” The concession was public and pressed — Pelagius’s answer to Bishop Boniface that “infants are baptized with the same words of sacramental formula as adults” is what first made his questioner “rejoiced to hear the very thing which he was desirous of hearing.”

But he empties the rite of inherited guilt — the quarrel is over meaning, not practice (Coelestius states it openly)

What Pelagius kept guarded, his disciple Coelestius said outright before the Church at Carthage: infants are indeed to be baptized “for the remission of sins,” but the concession must not be read as admitting that any sin is transmitted from Adam. Augustine quotes him:

That infants, however, must be baptized for the remission of sins, was not admitted by us with the view of our seeming to affirm sin by transmission. This is very alien from the catholic meaning, because sin is not born with a man; it is subsequently committed by the man, for it is shown to be a fault, not of nature, but of the will. It is fitting, therefore, to confess this, lest we should seem to make different kinds of baptism.

— Coelestius, quoted in Augustine, On the Grace of Christ, and on Original Sin (Book 2) 6.2 The last sentence is the tell: they conceded the practice, Augustine notes, chiefly so as not “to make different kinds of baptism” — that is, to avoid the scandal of denying so universal a rite. The positive Pelagian rationale surfaces in the “novel opinion” Augustine says his ears were “suddenly offended” to hear at Carthage:

That infants are not baptized for the purpose of receiving remission of sin, but that they may be sanctified in Christ.

— reported in Augustine, On the Merits and Forgiveness of Sins, and on the Baptism of Infants 3.6.3 Baptism as consecration into Christ and entrance to the kingdom, then — not the washing-away of an inherited stain. Pelagius holds the same custom as Cyprian, Jerome, and Augustine, and reads it in nearly the opposite way — closer, on the point of infant sinlessness, to the Eastern rationale of Chrysostom than to the Western original-sin frame he was fighting.

Notes

  • The witness is strong because it is hostile. A friendly witness who baptizes infants tells us the practice existed in his circle; an opponent who would dearly like to be rid of the original-sin argument built on the practice, and still cannot deny the practice, tells us it was beyond dispute. This is the very lever Augustine pulls against him: the whole Church baptizes infants “for the remission of sins,” the Pelagians concede it, and are thereby “compelled to class baptized infants in the number of believers” — so, Augustine presses, what sin is remitted in one who has committed none? The concession Pelagius could not withhold becomes the premise of the doctrine he most opposed. That the fourth–fifth-century argument turned entirely on why infants are baptized, never on whether, measures how settled the custom was — the same shape the earlier disputes had taken over timing (Tertullian, Cyprian’s council, Gregory).
  • Dating. Pelagius’s exposition of Paul (his Commentary on Romans) belongs to his Roman years, c. 406–410, before the controversy broke; the concessions quoted here were dragged into the open during it — at the Synod of Diospolis (415) and in the confession of faith he sent to Rome (417) — and Augustine assembled them in On the Grace of Christ and on Original Sin in 418. c. 415 is used as the anchor, seating him within the same controversy as Augustine (whose own timeline anchor, c. 412, is the anti-Pelagian On the Merits); on the timeline he stands just after Augustine, the opponent to Augustine’s protagonist.
  • A minority reading holds that Pelagius opposed the practice. The Encyclopedia of Ancient Christianity (IVP Academic, 2014) is cited — chiefly by credobaptist writers arguing infant baptism is a late development — for the view that Pelagius was against baptizing infants. The mainstream judgment runs the other way: Pelagius retained and confessed the practice while denying its original-sin rationale, as the verbatim concessions above show and as the standard studies conclude — G. F. Wiggers noting that the Pelagians, pressed by Jerome’s charge that they had departed from the received creed, “conceded the object of infant baptism to be the remission of sins, only they denied that original sin was thereby forgiven,” and Philip Schaff that they “retained the traditional usage of infant baptism, but divested it of its proper import.”4 The likeliest source of the confusion is exactly the distinction this page turns on: mistaking Pelagius’s rejection of the original-sin meaning of the rite for a rejection of the rite. What is certain — and all that his value as a witness requires — is that even Augustine’s fiercest opponent could not deny that the Church everywhere baptized its infants.
