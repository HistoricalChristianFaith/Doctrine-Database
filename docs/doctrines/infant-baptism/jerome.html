Jerome on infant baptism

Dates: c. 403 (Jerome, c. 347–420; priest, translator of the Vulgate, and the West’s foremost biblical scholar. Letter 107, to Laeta on the upbringing of her infant daughter Paula, is dated by its NPNF editor to A.D. 403; the anti-Pelagian Dialogue below is c. 415) · Wikipedia
Summary: Jerome is a distinctive Western witness who does not argue for infant baptism so much as presuppose it and reframe the question as one of parental duty: in Letter 107 he warns Laeta that guilt attaches to Christian parents who withhold baptism from a child too young to ask for it, since “baptism ensures the salvation of the child.” A decade later, in the Dialogue Against the Pelagians, he puts that settled practice to doctrinal use — infants are baptized “that their sins may be forgiven them,” released from the Adamic “chain which bound” their father — enlisting Cyprian and his contemporary Augustine in the mature Western original-sin case.

Claims & evidence

Guilt falls on parents who withhold an infant’s baptism (Letter 107.6)

Laeta, daughter-in-law of Jerome’s friend Paula, had written from Rome asking how to raise her infant daughter as a virgin consecrated to Christ. Working through the child’s formation, Jerome turns to the objection that a child should not bear another’s sin (Ezekiel 18:20) — and answers that this holds only for those old enough for discretion; the parents are responsible for a child still too young to “discern between their right hand and their left.” Then he presses the point to baptism:

But perhaps you imagine that, if they are not baptized, the children of Christians are liable for their own sins; and that no guilt attaches to parents who withhold from baptism those who by reason of their tender age can offer no objection to it. The truth is that, as baptism ensures the salvation of the child, this in turn brings advantage to the parents. Whether you would offer your child or not lay within your choice, but now that you have offered her, you neglect her at your peril.

— Jerome, Letter 107 (to Laeta), §6.1 The frame is what makes Jerome distinctive. He is not proving that infants may be baptized (as Cyprian’s council answered Fidus, or as Ambrose argued from the circumcision type); the fitness of the infant is simply assumed. What is in view is the parents’ obligation — to withhold baptism from a speechless child is itself a sin that imperils them, because the rite secures the child’s salvation. The practice stands behind the exhortation as a settled given, invoked to load a duty onto the parent.

Why little children are baptized: released from “the chain which bound” their father (Dialogue Against the Pelagians 3.18–19)

In the third book of the Dialogue, Jerome’s orthodox speaker (Atticus) is pressed by the Pelagian (Critobulus) on the sinlessness of newborns. The exchange turns the settled custom of infant baptism into a proof of inherited sin:

Tell me, pray, and rid me of all doubts, why little children are baptized. A. That their sins may be forgiven them in baptism. C. What sin are they guilty of? How can any one be set free who is not bound? A. You ask me! The Gospel trumpet will reply, the teacher of the Gentiles… “Death reigned from Adam even unto Moses: even over those who did not sin after the likeness of the transgression of Adam…” But all men are held liable either on account of their ancient forefather Adam, or on their own account. He that is an infant is released in baptism from the chain which bound his father.

— Jerome, Dialogue Against the Pelagians 3.18–19.2 Jerome then names his authorities: he quotes at length from “the blessed martyr Cyprian… in the epistle addressed to Bishop Fidus on the Baptism of Infants” — the very council-letter weighed on Cyprian’s page — and points the reader to “that holy man and eloquent bishop Augustin,” who “not long ago wrote to Marcellinus… two treatises on infant baptism, in opposition to your heresy.”3 Here the rationale is fully Western: the infant is baptized for the remission of sin, and the sin in question is Adam’s, contracted by birth — the same original-sin logic Cyprian and Augustine pressed, and the opposite pole from the Eastern Chrysostom, who called infants “sinless” and grounded their baptism in grace’s abundance.

Notes

  • A witness to the settled custom, not an argument for it. Weighed honestly, Jerome’s value here is as evidence that infant baptism was, by c. 400 in the Latin West, wholly beyond dispute — so much so that a spiritual director could build a mother’s duty on it without a word of defense. Letter 107 never asks whether the infant is a fit subject; it assumes she is and rebukes the parent who would delay. That is a strong (because incidental) attestation of the practice, in the manner of Origen and Chrysostom — the custom is the premise, not the thing to be proved. It is not, however, a fresh argument for paedobaptism; for that Jerome simply borrows Cyprian and Augustine.
  • Parental-duty framing is Jerome’s distinctive contribution. Where the earlier witnesses debate the timing (Tertullian for delay, Cyprian’s council against it, Gregory of Nazianzus counselling age three) or the theology (Ambrose, Chrysostom, Augustine), Jerome relocates the question to the parents’ conscience: withholding baptism is a culpable neglect that endangers them. The move fits his correspondent — a mother anxious for her child’s consecration — and it presupposes, rather than establishes, that the rite “ensures the salvation of the child.”
  • Where he sits on the timeline. c. 403 places Jerome between John Chrysostom (c. 390, East) and Augustine (c. 412, West), the two men whose rationales he splits the difference against and with respectively. His anti-Pelagian Dialogue (c. 415) is roughly contemporary with Augustine’s own campaign, and Jerome writes as a conscious ally — deferring to Augustine’s treatises “lest… I carry firewood into a forest” — so the two together fix the mature Western case (infant baptism → original sin) at Bethlehem and Hippo alike within a few years of 415.
