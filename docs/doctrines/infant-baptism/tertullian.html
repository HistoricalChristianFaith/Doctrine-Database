Tertullian on infant baptism

Dates: c. 203 (the likely date of On Baptism / De Baptismo; Tertullian, c. 155 – c. 220, the first major Christian theologian to write in Latin, traditionally called a presbyter of Carthage — though his own works rather class him among the laity, the title descending only from Jerome; see the argument) · Wikipedia
Summary: Tertullian is the earliest Christian writer to discuss the baptism of little children explicitly — and he argues against it, counselling that baptism be delayed until a person can ask for it and knows Christ. His very objection is the earliest firm evidence that infants were in fact being brought for baptism in the church of his day.

Claims & evidence

Baptism should be delayed — "principally… in the case of little children"

Near the close of On Baptism, having defended the sacrament itself against a woman of the Cainite sect who denied it, Tertullian turns to the questions of who should baptize and when. He warns that baptism is "not rashly to be administered," and, weighing "the circumstances and disposition, and even age, of each individual," recommends postponement — above all for young children:

And so, according to the circumstances and disposition, and even age, of each individual, the delay of baptism is preferable; principally, however, in the case of little children. For why is it necessary—if (baptism itself) is not so necessary—that the sponsors likewise should be thrust into danger? Who both themselves, by reason of mortality, may fail to fulfil their promises, and may be disappointed by the development of an evil disposition, in those for whom they stood? The Lord does indeed say, "Forbid them not to come unto me." Let them "come," then, while they are growing up; let them "come" while they are learning, while they are learning whither to come; let them become Christians when they have become able to know Christ. Why does the innocent period of life hasten to the "remission of sins?" More caution will be exercised in worldly matters: so that one who is not trusted with earthly substance is trusted with divine! Let them know how to "ask" for salvation, that you may seem (at least) to have given "to him that asketh."

— Tertullian, On Baptism (De Baptismo) 18, trans. S. Thelwall (ANF vol. 3), full text (cf. New Advent).1 Two reasons drive the counsel. The first is the sponsors (godparents), who make the baptismal promises on the child's behalf and are "thrust into danger" — put at spiritual risk — should the child later fall away or should they themselves die before fulfilling what they vowed. The second is Tertullian's estimate of infancy itself: the "innocent period of life" has, on his reckoning, no urgent need to "hasten to the remission of sins," so baptism is better deferred until a person can "know Christ" and "ask" for salvation for himself. He applies the same logic of delay to the unmarried, "in whom the ground of temptation is prepared."

His objection presupposes that infants were already being baptized

The significance of the passage for the dating of the practice runs opposite to its author's intent. Tertullian is the first named opponent of infant baptism — but to argue that it should be delayed, he must be arguing against something that was actually being done. He is not proposing a novelty or heading off a hypothetical; he is answering a settled custom of bringing "little children" (with their sponsors) to the font, common enough to need a rebuttal. So the earliest explicit written reference to infant baptism is, paradoxically, this argument against it: it attests the practice as established in North Africa by c. 200.2

Notes

  • Tertullian's counsel of delay was a minority position that did not prevail: within two generations infant baptism is attested as settled, even apostolic, custom (the Apostolic Tradition ascribed to Hippolytus, c. 215; Origen, c. 248) — witnesses that would sit later on this timeline.
  • Note the tension he embodies: he is simultaneously a dissenting witness (against early/infant baptism) and the earliest witness that the practice existed. His page therefore bears on any argument about how early infant baptism can be traced.
  • His reason for delay is prudential and pastoral (the risk to sponsors; the "innocent" state of childhood), not a denial that baptism could be given to infants — he nowhere calls the practice invalid, only inadvisable.
  • The delay-counsel is sometimes ascribed to Tertullian's Montanism — the rigorist "New Prophecy" he embraced later in life — and from there pressed into a broader claim that the Montanist movement itself was anti-paedobaptist (a staple of Baptist-successionist genealogies, which enlist the Montanists as early believers'-baptism dissenters). Both steps are unsupported. On Baptism belongs to Tertullian's earlier "catholic" period — usually dated c. 198–203, before the Montanist sympathies that surface in his works from c. 204–207 — and the treatise's own strongly anti-heretical cast reads as mainstream, not sectarian; his reasons for delay are prudential (the risk to sponsors, the "innocence" of childhood), not a tenet of the New Prophecy.3 The one supposed Montanist trace in the work — that its opening rebukes a Montanist prophetess "Quintilla" — rests on a conjecture introduced by the 1550 Gelenius edition with no manuscript warrant. And there is no evidence that Montanism as such opposed infant baptism: the delay-counsel is Tertullian's own, and even his broader rigorism about post-baptismal sin is a separate question from any denial that infants may be baptized.4 The charge is weighed in full, in the frame of the verdict passed on Tertullian, on the argument “Was Tertullian’s opposition to infant baptism a Montanist or rigorist position?” (assessment: weak).
  • What his objection carries, and what it does not: it establishes that infant baptism is an established, hence ancient, custom by c. 200 — older than its first surviving mention, probably reaching into the second century. It does not by itself establish apostolic institution; the step from “an established custom by c. 200” to “instituted by the apostles” is a further, weaker inference that turns on the practice's later universality and the absence of any founding-controversy, not on this datum alone. A proponent who treats Tertullian's protest as proof the apostles baptized infants overreads it — the reach is to antiquity, weighed on the dating argument (plausible), not to apostolic origin, weighed on the antiquity & universality argument (contested).
  • His contested orthodoxy. The New-Prophecy question that the first note sets aside for this treatise is the whole subject of the timeline “Was Tertullian a heretic?” — where his rigorism, his never-conciliar “condemnation,” and even the presbyter-of-Carthage title of the head-note (see the argument) are weighed.
