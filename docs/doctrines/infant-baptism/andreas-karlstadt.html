Andreas Bodenstein von Karlstadt on infant baptism

Dates: c. 1486 – 24 December 1541 (his rejection of infant baptism dates from his Orlamünde pastorate, 1523–1524; the timeline anchors him at c. 1524, when he abolished the practice there and was in contact with the Zürich radicals) · GAMEO
Summary: A middle-position witness — Luther’s early Wittenberg colleague turned radical, and the first magisterial-generation Reformer to reject infant baptism. At Orlamünde (1523–1524) he stopped baptizing infants, and in his Dialogus… vom Tauff der Kinder (Worms, 1527) he argued that a child cannot be baptized on another’s faith. Yet, distinctively, he neither demanded rebaptism of those christened as infants nor was rebaptized himself — a stance between Zwingli, who defended the paedobaptist practice, and the Swiss Brethren, who made adult rebaptism their banner. His writings, printed in part by the Zürich radicals, fed the movement he stopped short of joining.

Claims & evidence

Infant baptism abolished at Orlamünde — an “outward thing” like circumcision (1523–1524)

Having quit Wittenberg for the parish of Orlamünde in 1523, Karlstadt carried through the plain, austere reform Luther had reined in: he stripped the images, simplified the liturgy — and stopped baptizing infants. But he raised the point differently from the Anabaptists who would soon follow. The standard reference records:

Against infant baptism Karlstadt, unlike the Anabaptists, spoke only incidentally, classing it as an “outward thing” with circumcision. He said, to be sure, that it was better to postpone baptism until the candidate was sure of his faith, and that it was superficial of Luther to baptize infants who do not understand their lusts, to say nothing of the death of their lusts through Christ.

— “Karlstadt, Andreas Rudolff-Bodenstein von,” Global Anabaptist Mennonite Encyclopedia.1 The reasoning is the credobaptist instinct in embryo — that a valid baptism answers to a faith the recipient himself holds, which a newborn cannot bring — but held loosely, as one implication of his spiritualizing bent rather than a doctrine to be pressed. It was no mere opinion: at Orlamünde the font fell silent, and in 1525 his own wife refused to have their son baptized.2

A child cannot be baptized on another’s faith — the Dialogus vom Tauff der Kinder (1527)

Karlstadt set out his baptismal thinking most fully in a dialogue printed anonymously at Worms in 1527, its full title naming its three subjects: vom fremden Glauben, Glauben der Kirche, Tauff der Kinder — “on alien faith, the faith of the Church, and the baptism of children.”3 The title states the argument: infant baptism rests on a fremder Glaube, a faith belonging to someone else — the sponsors, or the Church — and not to the child who receives the water, so the practice cannot stand on the child’s own account. It is the same objection that Luther would meet head-on two years later in the Large Catechism by relocating the ground of baptism from the recipient’s faith onto God’s Word, and the same one Menno would press from the un-teachability of infancy.

The middle position — rejecting infant baptism, but not rebaptizing

What sets Karlstadt apart from the Swiss Brethren is where he stopped. He rejected infant baptism, yet he did not take the defining Anabaptist step of rebaptizing adults who had been christened as infants, nor was he himself rebaptized — in this resembling the magisterial Reformers, who likewise held their Roman infant baptism valid. Calvin Pater titled his study of him Karlstadt as the Father of the Baptist Movements precisely on the strength of the baptismal thinking he seeded; but Karlstadt kept to a stance between the camps — preferring, like Zwingli’s own hesitant early view, to defer the sacrament, without making a second baptism the test of a true church.4 He also never shared the Anabaptists’ separatism or (at Orlamünde’s worst moments) their readiness for force — a transitional figure, not a party man.

The link to the Zürich radicals (1524)

Karlstadt’s ideas reached the Swiss radicals directly. In the summer of 1524 Conrad Grebel wrote to him as a fellow dissident who had “broken with Luther on somewhat similar grounds”; that October Karlstadt and his brother-in-law Gerhard Westerburg met the Grebel–Manz circle at Zürich, and Felix Manz and Westerburg arranged the Basel printing of Karlstadt’s tracts on the Lord’s Supper.5 The radicals read and distributed his eucharistic writings and, the reference tradition notes, moved to “very decided antipedobaptist positions” partly under the influence of his and Thüringen’s Thomas Müntzer’s letters and visits — though Karlstadt held back from Zürich’s decisive act, and after the October 1524 meeting the contact lapsed. He is thus a genuine forerunner of the believers’-baptism movement without being one of its founders: the man who first, in the Reformation, let a Christian’s infant go unbaptized on principle, and whose pen helped carry the question to the men who would rebaptize.6

Notes

  • Position reported from the reference tradition. Karlstadt’s own baptism tract survives — the 1527 Worms Dialogus vom Tauff der Kinder, translated in E. J. Furcha, The Essential Carlstadt: Fifteen Tracts (Herald Press, 1995) — but the account of his position given here rests on the standard encyclopedic and scholarly summaries rather than a verbatim line from that dialogue; his firsthand wording is a fit subject for a later, quote-level pass.
  • “Baptist, not Anabaptist.” The distinction is the hinge of his witness: he denied the infant a valid baptism (so a “baptist” in the credo sense) yet did not re-baptize (so not an Anabaptist). It places him, chronologically and doctrinally, at the pivot between Zwingli and the Swiss Brethren — a few months before the first rebaptism made the harder line the movement’s badge.
  • Dating. His rejection of infant baptism belongs to the Orlamünde years (1523–1524); the fullest surviving statement, the Dialogus, is 1527. The timeline anchors him at c. 1524 — the year the practice ceased at Orlamünde and his writings reached Zürich — placing him just before Reublin and the Swiss Brethren.
