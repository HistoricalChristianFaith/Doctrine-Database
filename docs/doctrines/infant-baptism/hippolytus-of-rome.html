Hippolytus of Rome (the Apostolic Tradition) on infant baptism

Dates: c. 215 (the conventional date of the Apostolic Tradition, the church order traditionally ascribed to Hippolytus of Rome, c. 170–235; a presbyter and the most learned theologian of the early-third-century Roman church, later reckoned a martyr) · Wikipedia
Summary: The Apostolic Tradition §21, describing the order of a baptism, directs that the little children be baptized first — those old enough answering the interrogations for themselves, and those too young having their parents or relatives answer in their stead. It is one of the earliest matter-of-fact descriptions of infants being baptized with sponsors speaking on their behalf, taken not as a novelty to be defended but as the settled shape of the rite. Its evidential force is tempered by real doubts about the document's date, authorship, and unity.

Claims & evidence

“First baptize the little ones… if not, their parents or other relatives shall speak for them”

In its description of the paschal baptismal rite — the blessing of the water at cockcrow, the renunciation of Satan, the stripping and threefold immersion with its interrogations — the Apostolic Tradition lays down the order in which the candidates are to be baptized. Children come first, and where they cannot yet answer the credal questions themselves, a family member answers for them:

And first baptize the little ones; if they can speak for themselves, they shall do so; if not, their parents or other relatives shall speak for them. Then baptize the men, and last of all the women…

— The Apostolic Tradition of Hippolytus §21.4, trans. Burton Scott Easton (Cambridge, 1934).1 The much-quoted Dix–Chadwick rendering carries the same sense: “And they shall baptize the little children first. And if they can answer for themselves, let them answer. But if they cannot, let their parents or someone from their family answer for them.”2 Two things make the passage notable. First, it is descriptive, not polemical: unlike Tertullian a decade or so earlier, who argues that little children's baptism should be delayed, the Apostolic Tradition simply records infant baptism as a fixed part of the liturgical order, needing neither defence nor apology. Second, it already presupposes the institution of sponsors: because an infant cannot make the baptismal renunciations and profession, “their parents or other relatives shall speak for them” — the very machinery of proxy-answering that Tertullian had worried put the sponsors “into danger.” The two witnesses thus corroborate each other: one opposing the custom, one prescribing it, both attesting that infants were in fact being brought to the font in the early third century.

Notes

  • Authorship and date are genuinely disputed. The Apostolic Tradition is only traditionally ascribed to Hippolytus; the ascription rests on a reconstruction, and the Greek original is lost, the text surviving mainly in a fourth-century Latin (Verona) palimpsest and in Coptic, Arabic, and Ethiopic versions. Much recent scholarship (Paul Bradshaw, Maxwell Johnson, L. Edward Phillips) treats it as a composite “living” church order, layered from the mid-second into the fourth century rather than the work of one author at one date — David Wright's summary is that “almost everything concerning this text remains the subject of lively scholarly argument.” So the c. 215 date is a convention, and one cannot be certain this particular rubric is that early rather than a later stratum.3
  • What it does and does not settle. Even granting the caveats, the directive is strong evidence that infant baptism — complete with proxy sponsors — was an established, unremarkable practice in the community whose rite this document preserves. What it cannot do by itself is date that establishment precisely: if the layer is early-third-century it corroborates Tertullian; if later, it still shows the practice fixed and normative by the time the text reached its surviving form.
  • Place on the timeline. At c. 215 the Apostolic Tradition falls just after Tertullian (c. 203) and a generation before Origen's claim (c. 248) that the church received infant baptism “from the apostles” — the first descriptive prescription of the rite, between the earliest opponent of the custom and its first appeal to apostolic warrant.
