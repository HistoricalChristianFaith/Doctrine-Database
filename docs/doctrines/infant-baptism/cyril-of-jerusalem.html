Cyril of Jerusalem on infant baptism

Dates: c. 350 (Cyril, c. 313–386; bishop of Jerusalem. His Catechetical Lectures — a Procatechesis and eighteen lectures to candidates preparing for the Easter font, with five later Mystagogical lectures to the newly-baptized — are generally dated to about 348–350, while he was still a presbyter) · Wikipedia
Summary: Cyril gives the fullest surviving picture of a fourth-century baptismal preparation — and, like the Didache and Justin before him, he is a neutral, double-edged witness. His whole catechetical machinery presupposes a candidate old enough to hear, repent, be exorcised, prepare through a forty-day Lent, and profess faith for himself before going down into the water: baptism follows an unfeigned personal faith and repentance. He nowhere discusses the baptism of infants — on that question he is silent — so the value of the witness is to what the adult road to the font looked like in mid-century Jerusalem, the order (faith, then baptism) the credobaptist presses, without settling whose children were also brought.

Claims & evidence

The catechumenate presupposes a conscious candidate — forty days to repent, on pain of a barren baptism (Procatechesis 4)

Addressing those whose names have been “enrolled” for the coming Easter baptism, Cyril treats the candidate as a moral agent who must actively repent and reform in the weeks before the font, warning that the water without a true change of purpose confers nothing:

A long notice is allowed thee; thou hast forty days for repentance: thou hast full opportunity both to put off, and wash, and to put on and enter. But if thou persist in an evil purpose, the speaker is blameless, but thou must not look for the grace: for the water will receive, but the Spirit will not accept thee.

— Cyril of Jerusalem, Procatechesis 4.1 The whole address is pitched to a hearer who can be taught, examined, and exhorted — “God, who knoweth your hearts, and observeth who is sincere, and who a hypocrite” — and who must “prepare thine own heart for reception of doctrine.” None of it can be said of a newborn.2

“The soul having been born again by faith” — faith and repentance precede the water (Catechetical Lecture 3)

In the lecture On Baptism, Cyril fixes the order plainly: the candidate first makes ready the soul by unfeigned faith and repentance, and only then does the water do its work — the two, faith and water, completing one another:

Make ready the vessels of the soul, cleansed by unfeigned faith, for reception of the Holy Ghost. Begin at once to wash your robes in repentance, that when called to the bride-chamber ye may be found clean.

— Cyril of Jerusalem, Catechetical Lecture 3.2.3 He states the sequence as a rule: “When going down, therefore, into the water, think not of the bare element, but look for salvation by the power of the Holy Ghost… the water cleanses the body, and the Spirit seals the soul,” so that baptism works “in order that, the soul having been born again by faith, the body also might by the water partake of the grace.” The virtuous unbaptized and the baptized-but-faithless are alike incomplete: “Neither doth he that is baptized with water, but not found worthy of the Spirit, receive the grace in perfection; nor if a man be virtuous in his deeds, but receive not the seal by water, shall he enter into the kingdom of heaven.”4

Notes

  • A witness to the adult road, silent on infants. Cyril is not an opponent of infant baptism; he simply never addresses it. What he documents is the shape of the adult catechumenate — enrollment, exorcism, a forty-day Lenten preparation, the renunciation of Satan and profession of faith at the font, and post-baptismal “mystagogy” — all of it built for a candidate who can understand, repent, and answer for himself. That is the same double edge as the earlier Didache and Justin: a description of the professing-adult rite that the credobaptist reads as the original norm and the paedobaptist reads as merely what a preparation for adult converts would say — silent, not exclusionary, about believers’ newborns.
  • Where he sits on the timeline. Cyril’s Jerusalem catechesis (c. 350) fills the long gap between Cyprian’s African council (c. 253) and the later Cappadocian and Antiochene witnesses (Nazianzus c. 381, Nyssa c. 383, Chrysostom c. 390). It belongs with the strand of fourth-century evidence — the vogue for deferring baptism to adulthood even among the Christian-born — that the dating-of-the-earliest-mention and antiquity-and-universality arguments weigh: real counterweight to an early-universal-norm reading, though (as Jeremias urged against Aland) equally readable as a temporary decline from an earlier custom.
  • Authorship caveat elsewhere. The five Mystagogical lectures (whose authorship between Cyril and his successor John of Jerusalem is disputed) are treated on a separate topic on this wiki; nothing here depends on them — the Procatechesis and the Catechetical Lectures proper are securely Cyril’s.
