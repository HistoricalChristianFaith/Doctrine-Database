Wilhelm Reublin on infant baptism

Dates: c. 1484 – after 1559 (his agitation against infant baptism around Zürich runs 1523–1525; the timeline anchors him at c. 1524, when parents in his parish first withheld their children from the font) · GAMEO
Summary: A credobaptist witness — and, on the record, the earliest of the Swiss radicals to act. A priest at Witikon in the villages around Zürich, Reublin was the first in the canton to preach against infant baptism, and on his advice parents began refusing to present their newborns — roughly two years before the Swiss Brethren’s first believers’ baptism of 21 January 1525, at which he was present, and after which he was expelled from the canton. That Easter he carried believers’ baptism outward, baptizing Hubmaier at Waldshut.

Claims & evidence

The first in the canton of Zürich to preach against infant baptism (c. 1524)

Reublin (Röubli), expelled from Basel for his evangelical preaching, became the peasants’ chosen pastor at Witikon near Zürich about 1523 — and there, the standard reference records, he

began to call in question the Scriptural authority and the propriety of infant baptism.

— The New Schaff–Herzog Encyclopedia of Religious Knowledge, “Reublin, Wilhelm”.1 The Global Anabaptist Mennonite Encyclopedia makes the priority explicit: Reublin was “the first in the canton of Zürich, in early 1524, to preach against the baptism of infants,” and by that Easter a number of parents in his congregation did not present their children.2 This is the earliest concrete anti-paedobaptist agitation in the Swiss Reformation — a full year before the Grebel circle’s decisive act — and it did not stay theoretical: it moved parents to withhold the sacrament.

Parents withhold their infants — and are punished (1524)

Acting on his advice several parents withheld their infants from christening and incurred severe punishment therefor.

— The New Schaff–Herzog Encyclopedia of Religious Knowledge, “Reublin, Wilhelm”.3 The refusal spread from Witikon to neighbouring Zollikon, and by August 1524 the Zürich council had noticed: it decreed a fine of one silver mark on parents who would not baptize their infants, had Reublin briefly imprisoned, and had the city’s three “people’s priests” warn him to stop preaching against the practice.4 The witness here is an act before it is a text: like the Swiss Brethren’s rebaptism a few months later, Reublin’s campaign enacts the credobaptist “No” — treating the infant as no fit subject of the font — before any of the movement’s confessions (Schleitheim) or treatises (Hubmaier, Menno, Smyth) had spelled it out.

Present at the birth of Anabaptism, then expelled (17–21 January 1525)

Reublin took part in the second Zürich baptism disputation of 17 January 1525, where he “was one of the antipedobaptist speakers”; when the council decided for Zwingli and ordered the radicals out, he, Grebel, and Manz were given eight days to leave the canton.5 Four days later the Swiss Brethren performed the first believers’ baptism at the house of Manz’s mother. Reublin thus stands both behind and beside that founding act — the pastor whose preaching had already emptied village fonts, present at the movement’s origin.

Carrying believers’ baptism outward — Hubmaier at Waldshut (Easter 1525)

Expelled from Zürich, Reublin went to Waldshut, where he “induced Hubmaier… already convinced against infant baptism, to lead his adherents in submitting to believers’ baptism”:

About Easter, 1525, he baptized Hubmaier and about sixty others and shortly afterward Hubmaier baptized about 300 more.

— The New Schaff–Herzog Encyclopedia of Religious Knowledge, “Reublin, Wilhelm”.6 He later baptized Michael Sattler near Rottenburg and evangelized through Hallau, Schaffhausen, Strasbourg, and Moravia — making him one of the human links between the Zürich origin and the movement’s several later centres.7

Notes

  • Dating — “as early as 1523.” Reublin settled at Witikon in 1523; the reference works place his actual preaching against infant baptism, and the resulting refusals, in early 1524, with the council’s mandate that August. The looser “from about 1523” (used on the Swiss Brethren page) reckons from his arrival and the beginnings of the ferment; the concrete campaign is 1524. Either way it predates the 21 January 1525 rebaptism.
  • No surviving first-person statement on baptism. Reublin left no extant tract of his own arguing the credobaptist case; his position is known from the record of his acts and the reports of the reference tradition (he was chiefly an agitator and evangelist, not a systematic writer like Hubmaier or Menno). He is on this timeline as the earliest Swiss actor against infant baptism, the way the Swiss Brethren entry stands for a deed rather than a doctrine.
  • Break with Zürich’s council-paced reform. Like the rest of the Grebel circle, Reublin broke with Zwingli precisely over letting the magistracy set the pace and terms of reform — refusing to wait on the council’s leave to stop baptizing infants. His imprisonment and expulsion mark the point where that disagreement turned from debate into penalty.
