Gregory of Nazianzus on infant baptism

Dates: c. 381 (Gregory, c. 329–390; one of the three Cappadocian Fathers and briefly archbishop of Constantinople. Oration 40, On Holy Baptism, was delivered at the Feast of Lights — Epiphany — in Constantinople, most likely 6 January 381, the day after his companion sermon Oration 39) · Wikipedia
Summary: Gregory is a fourth-century Eastern witness that infant and child baptism was an accepted practice, contested only as to timing. Preaching on baptism, he urges parents not to fear consecrating even an infant, and answers directly the question “are we to baptize [children] too?” — yes, at once if any danger of death presses; otherwise, in the ordinary case, his own counsel is to wait until about the third year, when a child can begin to hear and answer something of the mystery. He nowhere questions whether children are fit subjects for baptism; like Cyprian's council a century and a quarter earlier, the whole discussion presupposes the practice.

Claims & evidence

“Have you an infant child?” — consecrate it from its tenderest age (Oration 40.17)

In a passage exhorting each hearer according to his state of life, Gregory turns to parents of the very young and presses them not to let the “weakness of nature” hold them back from baptizing an infant:

Have you an infant child? Do not let sin get any opportunity, but let him be sanctified from his childhood; from his very tenderest age let him be consecrated by the Spirit. Fearest thou the Seal on account of the weakness of nature? O what a small-souled mother, and of how little faith!

— Gregory of Nazianzus, Oration 40 (On Holy Baptism) 17.1 He backs the exhortation with the example of Hannah, who “even before Samuel was born promised him to God, and after his birth consecrated him at once” — “not fearing anything in human nature, but trusting in God.” “Give your child the Trinity,” he concludes, “that great and noble Guard.”2

“Are we to baptize them too?” — yes if danger presses; otherwise wait for the third year (Oration 40.28)

Gregory then poses the objection head-on: granting the case of adults who ask for baptism, what of children too young to be conscious of either the loss or the grace? His answer distinguishes the emergency from the ordinary case, but in neither does he doubt that children are baptized:

Be it so, some will say, in the case of those who ask for Baptism; what have you to say about those who are still children, and conscious neither of the loss nor of the grace? Are we to baptize them too? Certainly, if any danger presses. For it is better that they should be unconsciously sanctified than that they should depart unsealed and uninitiated.

— Gregory of Nazianzus, Oration 40.28.3 He supports the emergency baptism of infants with the same Old-Testament types Cyprian's council had used — circumcision “on the eighth day… conferred on children before they had the use of reason,” and the blood on the doorposts that “preserved the firstborn, though applied to things which had no consciousness.”4 For the ordinary case (no danger of death), his own advice is to defer — not indefinitely, and not on principle, but so the child may grasp at least the outlines of the rite:

But in respect of others I give my advice to wait till the end of the third year, or a little more or less, when they may be able to listen and to answer something about the Sacrament; that, even though they do not perfectly understand it, yet at any rate they may know the outlines; and then to sanctify them in soul and body with the great sacrament of our consecration.

— Gregory of Nazianzus, Oration 40.28.5 The reasoning is pastoral, not a denial of infant baptism’s validity: since young children “have no account to give” for sins of ignorance, and only begin to be responsible “when reason is matured,” the ideal moment is early childhood — late enough to catechize a little, early enough to be “fortified by the Font, because of the sudden assaults of danger.”6

Notes

  • A witness to the practice, contested only as to timing. Gregory's counsel to wait to about age three is often quoted as if he opposed infant baptism; it does the opposite. He assumes children are baptized, prescribes immediate baptism whenever life is in danger, and defers the ordinary case only for a catechetical reason (so the child “may know the outlines”). The Nicene and Post-Nicene Fathers editor makes the point plainly: “That S. Gregory did not reject infant Baptism is clear, from the directions given late on in this Oration (c. xxviii; and cf. c. xvii. s. fin.).”7
  • Where he sits on the timeline. By c. 381 the live question in the East is no longer whether to baptize children but when — the same axis Cyprian's African synod had debated with Fidus (c. 253), and the reverse of Tertullian's c. 203 argument for delay. Gregory splits the difference: emergency baptism of infants without hesitation, elective baptism around the age of first understanding. His near-contemporary Gregory of Nyssa and the wider Cappadocian circle share the same settled acceptance of the practice.
  • Delay by whom? Gregory's own family is the cautionary backdrop: he and Basil were themselves baptized only as adults, a widespread fourth-century habit of postponing baptism (often till the deathbed) to have the maximal sweep of sins washed away at once. Oration 40 is in large part an argument against that adult procrastination — “let us be baptized today” — which throws his willingness to baptize endangered infants into relief: the delay he attacks is the adult's, not the child's.8
