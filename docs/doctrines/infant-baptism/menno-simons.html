Menno Simons on infant baptism

Dates: c. 1539 (Dat Fundament des Christelycken Leers — the “Foundation of Christian Doctrine” — first published in Dutch about 1539/1540 and revised thereafter; its section on baptism is his classic statement). Menno Simons, c. 1496–1561, born at Witmarsum in Friesland; ordained a Roman priest in 1524, he came over the course of the 1530s to reject infant baptism and the Mass, formally renounced Rome and was re-baptized in January 1536, and became the itinerant elder around whom the peaceable Dutch and North-German Anabaptists gathered — the movement that took from him the name Mennonites. He died at Wüstenfelde near Bad Oldesloe in 1561 · Wikipedia
Summary: Menno is the great Dutch codifier of the believers’-baptism position that Hubmaier had argued and Schleitheim had confessed a decade before him. In the baptism section of his Foundation of Christian Doctrine he grounds the rite in a fixed apostolic order — first the gospel is preached, then it is believed, and only then is the believer baptized — and rests that order on three pillars: Christ’s command, the apostles’ teaching, and the apostles’ practice. Because an infant “cannot be made to understand” and so cannot be taught or believe, Menno holds that to baptize it is to pervert Christ’s ordinance. It is the same teach-then-baptize case the timeline’s other credobaptist witnesses press — here given its most influential sixteenth-century vernacular form.

Claims & evidence

The apostolic order: preach, then believe, then baptize

Menno reads off the New Testament a fixed sequence in which faith always precedes the font. He takes Christ’s own commission in Mark as decisive for “when and how” baptism is to be given:

Go ye into all the world and preach the gospel to every creature, he that believeth and is baptized, shall be saved… Faith is before baptism.

— Menno Simons, Foundation of Christian Doctrine, “Christian Baptism” (citing Mark 16:15–16).1 He grounds the practice not on one proof-text but on three converging witnesses — command, teaching, and example:

There are necessarily three reasons why our faith accepts this baptism… First, because of the divine commandment of our beloved Lord Jesus Christ, which can never be broken. Secondly, because of the teaching of the holy apostles. Thirdly, because of the practice of these same apostles.

— Menno Simons, Foundation of Christian Doctrine, “Christian Baptism.”2 This is the broad faith-precedes-baptism argument — the whole pattern of the apostolic mission, in which the word is heard and believed before anyone is baptized — and, in the appeal to Christ’s commission, the Great-Commission-order argument that Smyth would later cast as a formal syllogism. Menno presses the same order Hubmaier had drawn from John’s and Christ’s baptism (word, hearing, faith, baptism, works).

An infant cannot be taught, so cannot be baptized without perverting the ordinance

From the order Menno draws the exclusion of infants directly: a rite that requires hearing and faith cannot belong to one incapable of either. Against the plea that children may be baptized on a faith they will one day have, he answers that a child cannot even grasp the visible, let alone the invisible:

If they cannot be made to understand any thing visible, how can they, then, prematurely, that is, before they can comprehend things, be taught and instructed in invisible, celestial matters of the Spirit?

— Menno Simons, Foundation of Christian Doctrine, “Christian Baptism.”3 Where the Schleitheim Confession makes repentance, faith, and a personal request constitutive of a valid baptism — and so bars the newborn — Menno reaches the same exclusion through the un-teachability of infancy: since baptism follows teaching and faith, and an infant can receive neither, to baptize it is to invert Christ’s appointed order and so to void the sign. The argument runs on the credobaptist order-of-faith reading rather than on any claim about the mode of the rite.

Notes

  • Named for him, but he built on those before him. Menno gave his name to the Mennonites, but on baptism he is a systematizer, not an originator: Hubmaier had argued the believers’-baptism case at treatise length in 1525, and the Swiss Brethren at Schleitheim had confessed it in 1527, both before Menno left the priesthood in 1536. His achievement was to fix the position in a durable, widely-read Dutch statement — the baptism section of the Foundation — whose ordering of preach–believe–baptize became standard in the tradition that carries his name.4
  • Against a settled Western consensus. Menno breaks not only with Rome but with the magisterial Reformers: Luther had grounded infant baptism in the Word that makes the sacrament, and Calvin in the covenant sign given to a believer’s seed — both retaining the paedobaptism the patristic witnesses on this timeline had handed down. Menno’s teach-then-baptize order is the direct counter to Calvin’s covenantal reading of the baptized “households” of Acts, weighed on the household-baptisms and covenant-circumcision argument pages.
