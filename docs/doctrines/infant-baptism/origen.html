Origen on infant baptism

Dates: c. 248 (Origen of Alexandria, c. 185–253/4; the greatest biblical scholar and most prolific writer of the pre-Nicene church, active at Alexandria and then Caesarea. The witnesses below run across his mature exegetical work — the Homilies on Luke (c. 233–4), the Homilies on Leviticus (c. 238–44), and the Commentary on Romans (c. 246) — anchored here at c. 248, the conventional date used for Origen across this wiki) · Wikipedia
Summary: Origen is the first writer to state plainly that the baptism of infants is a practice the Church received from the apostles. Across three works he treats infant baptism as universal and unremarkable, and reasons from it: since the Church baptizes “for the remission of sins” and yet baptizes newborns who have committed no actual sin, there must be an inherited defilement — the “stain” of birth — that the rite cleanses. He is thus a double witness: to the practice (universal in his day) and to its claimed apostolic antiquity.

Claims & evidence

“The church has received a tradition from the apostles to baptize even infants” (Commentary on Romans 5.9)

Commenting on Romans 6:6 (“our old self was crucified… that the sinful body might be destroyed”), Origen asks why the Law commands a sin offering for a newborn child (Leviticus 12) — how can an infant already have sin? — and answers by appeal to a defilement present from birth, then grounds infant baptism in apostolic tradition:

In the law it is commanded that sacrifices of a pair of turtledoves or two pigeons shall be offered for a newborn child. One of these is a sin offering and the other is a burnt offering. For what sin is this first pigeon offered? How can a newborn child have sinned already? And yet the child has sin, for which the sacrifice is commanded to be offered.… For the same reason the church has received a tradition from the apostles to baptize even infants. For they, to whom the secrets of the divine mysteries had been committed, knew that there are real stains of sin in everyone which must be cleansed by water and the Spirit.

— Origen, Commentary on the Epistle to the Romans 5.9 (on Romans 6:6), trans. Thomas P. Scheck.1 This is the sentence most often cited in the paedobaptism debate, and its force is twofold. First, Origen does not merely report that infants are baptized; he says the custom is a tradition from the apostles — the earliest surviving appeal to apostolic warrant for the practice, a generation after the Apostolic Tradition had prescribed it and two generations after Tertullian had opposed it. Second, the passage closes by refusing the transmigrationist reading he was sometimes accused of: the “stain” the child carries is not a soul's guilt from a previous life but a defilement of birth — a rationale that anticipates the Western doctrine of original sin without quite reaching it.2

“According to the usage of the Church, baptism is given even to infants” (Homilies on Leviticus 8.3)

Preaching on the Levitical purity laws, Origen makes the same move — the defilement of birth requires cleansing, and the Church accordingly baptizes the newborn:

Every soul that is born into flesh is soiled by the filth of wickedness and sin… In the Church, baptism is given for the remission of sins, and, according to the usage of the Church, baptism is given even to infants.

— Origen, Homilies on Leviticus 8.3.3 Here the practice is simply “the usage of the Church” — taken for granted, not defended — and it is tied directly to the remission of sins, which for a newborn can only mean the inherited stain.

“Through the mystery of Baptism, the stains of birth are put aside” (Homilies on Luke 14)

In his fourteenth homily on Luke, Origen presses the puzzle explicitly: if infants are baptized “for the remission of sins,” whose sins are these? His answer turns on a careful distinction between a stain and a sin:

Little children are baptized “for the remission of sins.” Whose sins are they? When did they sin? Or how can this explanation of the baptismal washing be maintained in the case of small children, except according to the interpretation we spoke of a little earlier? “No man is clean of stain, not even if his life upon the earth had lasted but a single day.” Through the mystery of Baptism, the stains of birth are put aside. For this reason, even small children are baptized.

— Origen, Homilies on Luke 14.5 (with the stain/sin distinction drawn at 14.3: “‘Stain’ and ‘sins’ do not mean the same thing. ‘Stain’ is one thing, ‘sin’ another”), trans. Joseph T. Lienhard.4 The reasoning is a syllogism run backwards from an accepted practice: the Church baptizes for remission of sins; the Church baptizes infants; therefore infants must have something requiring remission. That Origen argues from infant baptism as a fixed premise — rather than for it — is itself evidence of how settled the custom was by his day.

Notes

  • “5.9” is the section of the commentary, not Romans 5:9. The much-cited “Commentaries on Romans 5:9” of the popular catalogs refers to Book 5, section 9 of Origen's commentary; the passage actually expounds Romans 6:5–7, and the “apostolic tradition” sentence falls in his comment on Romans 6:6.5
  • A translation caveat on the Commentary on Romans. Origen's Greek commentary survives, for these books, only in the condensed Latin paraphrase of Rufinus of Aquileia (c. 405). Rufinus admits to abridging and adapting as he translated, so one cannot be certain the “from the apostles” formula is Origen's own wording rather than Rufinus's rendering — though the same apostolic-tradition claim is not needed to establish that Origen witnessed the practice, which the independent Leviticus and Luke homilies (preached in Greek and separately transmitted) already attest.6
  • Place on the timeline. Origen (c. 248) stands after Tertullian (c. 203) and the Apostolic Tradition (c. 215): where Tertullian opposed infant baptism and the Apostolic Tradition prescribed it as liturgical order, Origen is the first to claim it as a custom handed down from the apostles — the earliest witness to the antiquity-argument that would become central to the later paedobaptist case.
