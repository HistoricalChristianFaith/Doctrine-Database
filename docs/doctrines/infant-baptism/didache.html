The Didache on infant baptism

Dates: c. 100 (The Didache, or The Lord's Teaching Through the Twelve Apostles to the Nations — an anonymous Syrian or Antiochene church order; its layers are variously dated from c. 50 to c. 150, with the baptismal section commonly placed around the turn of the first century) · Wikipedia
Summary: The Didache preserves the earliest surviving Christian baptismal rubric, and on the infant-baptism question it is a neutral witness. It prescribes baptism after catechesis, with the candidate (and the baptizer) fasting beforehand — a picture of an instructed, professing candidate — yet it neither names nor excludes infants, and it expressly permits pouring where immersion-water is lacking. It is the baseline the later timeline argues over, not itself a voice for either side.

Claims & evidence

The earliest baptismal rubric: baptism follows catechesis, with candidate and baptizer fasting

The Didache opens with the “Two Ways” catechesis (chapters 1–6) — the way of life and the way of death that a candidate is taught — and only then, in chapter 7, turns to the rite itself. The baptismal instruction begins “Having first said all these things,” tying the font directly to the completed instruction that precedes it:

And concerning baptism, thus baptize ye: Having first said all these things, baptize into the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit, in living water. But if thou have not living water, baptize into other water; and if thou canst not in cold, in warm. But if thou have not either, pour out water thrice upon the head into the name of Father and Son and Holy Spirit. But before the baptism let the baptizer fast, and the baptized, and whatever others can; but thou shalt order the baptized to fast one or two days before.

— Didache (The Teaching of the Twelve Apostles) 7, trans. M. B. Riddle (Ante-Nicene Fathers vol. 7), full text (cf. New Advent).1 Two features of this rite describe a candidate old enough to take part in it. First, the baptism is the terminus of a course of teaching — “having first said all these things” points back to the Two Ways just laid out, so the person baptized is one who has been catechized. Second, the candidate is told to fast “one or two days before,” alongside the baptizer — a discipline a newborn cannot keep. The picture the rubric paints, then, is the conversion of a professing, instructed adult, the same portrait the later Justin Martyr (First Apology 61) gives of those who “are persuaded and believe” and are then “regenerated.”2

Mode is not made constitutive: pouring is permitted where immersion-water is lacking

The rubric ranks its options — living (running) water first, then still water, cold before warm — but where none of these can be had it plainly allows affusion: “pour out water thrice upon the head.” So the earliest church order already treats immersion as the preferred but not the required mode; a valid baptism does not stand or fall on being an immersion. This is why the Didache bears on the credobaptist immersion-mode argument: whatever the etymology of baptizō, the apostolic-age church did not make submersion constitutive of the sacrament, and the mode question is in any case orthogonal to the subject question — one can baptize an infant by immersion (as the Christian East does) or an adult by pouring.3

On the subjects of baptism it is neutral — and that neutrality is itself the contested datum

The Didache neither commands nor forbids the baptism of infants; it simply does not raise the question. What it describes — a catechized candidate who fasts before the font — fits an adult convert, and the credobaptist reads this as the original norm: the earliest baptismal instruction we possess portrays instructed, professing candidates, with no hint of infants. The paedobaptist replies that the genre settles nothing: a church order written to regulate the reception of adult pagans into a missionary church will naturally describe the adult-convert case, and its silence about believers’ newborns is what that genre predicts, not evidence they were excluded. The document is thus a terminus and a baseline — it fixes what the standard rite looked like around c. 100 — but it does not, on either reading, resolve whether the children of Christians were also brought. That is exactly the standoff weighed on the dating-of-the-earliest-mention argument.4

Notes

  • As a c. 100 witness the Didache sits at the head of this timeline — earlier than Irenaeus (c. 180) and the first explicit discussant, Tertullian (c. 203). It is a witness to the rite, not to a position in the infant-baptism debate, which had not yet been joined.
  • The dating is disputed and the text is composite: the Two Ways material (chs. 1–6) draws on older Jewish catechesis, and scholars variously date the whole from the mid-first to the mid-second century. Nothing on this page rests on a precise year — only on the Didache’s being a very early, pre-Tertullian description of the baptismal rite.
  • Its restriction of the Eucharist to “they who have been baptized into the name of the Lord” (ch. 9) shows baptism already functioning as the gate to communion — a datum for the sequence of Christian initiation, though again silent on the age of the baptized.
  • The Didache is cited across three argument pages on this doctrine — the dating case (pre-200 descriptions portray professing adults), the immersion-mode case (pouring permitted where water is scarce), and the Great-Commission-order case (baptism following instruction) — because its rubric touches all three questions at once. This detail page is where the document itself is set on the timeline; the arguments weigh what it is made to prove.
