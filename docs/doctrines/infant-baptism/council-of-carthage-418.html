The Council of Carthage (418) on infant baptism

Dates: c. 418 (the plenary Council of Carthage met at Carthage on 1 May 418, the culmination of a sequence of North African anti-Pelagian synods — councils at Carthage and Mileve (Milevum) in 416, whose synodal letters Pope Innocent I confirmed, and the condemnation of Coelestius at Carthage in 411. It comes about a century and a half after Cyprian’s African council of c. 253, and it makes conciliar law of the doctrine Augustine had pressed against Pelagius) · Wikipedia
Summary: The North African church condemned Pelagianism at a series of synods culminating in the plenary Council of Carthage of 1 May 418, whose canons — “entirely conformable to the Augustinian view” — made the original-sin rationale for infant baptism a test of communion. Its second canon anathematizes both the denial that newborns should be baptized and the distinctively Pelagian claim that they are baptized “for the remission of sins” while carrying no inherited guilt from Adam. As at Cyprian’s council, the practice itself is never in dispute; what the synod fixes is why the church baptizes infants.

Claims & evidence

The conciliar answer to Pelagius: Carthage, 1 May 418

The Pelagian controversy came to a head in North Africa through a chain of synods. Coelestius, Pelagius’s disciple, was condemned at Carthage in 411; in 416 councils at Carthage and Mileve (Milevum) — the latter’s synodal letter drafted with Augustine’s hand — wrote to Pope Innocent I, who confirmed their condemnation of Pelagius and Coelestius early in 417. After Innocent’s successor Zosimus wavered, a plenary council of more than two hundred African bishops assembled at Carthage on 1 May 418 and settled the matter with a series of canons that denounced the Pelagian teaching on human nature, original sin, and grace, and approved the contrary doctrine of Augustine; Zosimus then issued his Epistola tractoria condemning the Pelagians.1

Canon 2 — infant baptism grounded in inherited sin

The council’s second canon fastens infant baptism to original sin, making the very wording of the baptismal rite — “for the remission of sins” — the pivot of the argument. It anathematizes anyone who either refuses baptism to the newborn or keeps the rite while emptying it of its original-sin content:

Likewise it seemed good that whosoever denies that infants newly from their mother’s wombs should be baptized, or says that baptism is for the remission of sins, but that they derive from Adam no original sin, which needs to be removed by the laver of regeneration, from whence the conclusion follows, that in them the form of baptism for the remission of sins, is to be understood as false and not true, let him be anathema. For no otherwise can be understood what the Apostle says, “By one man sin is come into the world, and death through sin, and so death passed upon all men in that all have sinned,” than the Catholic Church everywhere diffused has always understood it. For on account of this rule of faith even infants, who could have committed as yet no sin themselves, therefore are truly baptized for the remission of sins, in order that what in them is the result of generation may be cleansed by regeneration.

— Council of Carthage (418), Canon 2 (also numbered Canon 110 [Greek cxxi], “That in Baptism sins are truly remitted,” in the African Code).2 The canon is aimed at two positions, both of which Pelagius’s circle had taken. The first — simply denying infant baptism — is the outright rejection; but the second is the one that actually mattered in the controversy: Pelagius and Coelestius kept the rite (Pelagius confessed that infants “ought to be baptized for the remission of sins, according to the rule of the Church universal”) while denying that the “sin” so remitted was any inherited Adamic guilt. Against that, the council rules that the liturgical formula must be taken at face value — the “remission of sins” over an infant is real remission of a real sin, and since the newborn has committed none of his own, it can only be the sin “the result of generation.” This is precisely Augustine’s argument from the practice of the church, now cast as a binding canon and anchored, as he had anchored it, in Romans 5:12.

Canon 1 — Adam was not created mortal

The canon on baptism rests on the synod’s first canon, which condemns the Pelagian premise that death is a fact of nature rather than the penalty of sin — the doctrine that made original sin thinkable in the first place:

That whosoever says that Adam, the first man, was created mortal, so that whether he had sinned or not, he would have died in body—that is, he would have gone forth of the body, not because his sin merited this, but by natural necessity, let him be anathema.

— Council of Carthage (418), Canon 1, “That Adam was not created by God subject to death.”3 If death entered by Adam’s sin (canon 1), and passes to all his descendants (Romans 5:12, canon 2), then even the newborn stands under a condemnation to be washed away — and the ancient custom of baptizing infants “for the remission of sins” is exactly what one would expect. The canons thus reproduce, as conciliar law, the very inference Augustine drew from the rite and Cyprian had already gestured toward with his “contagion of the ancient death.”

Notes

  • Eight canons or nine? Editions of the 418 canons print eight or nine. The variable canon — numbered 3 in the nine-canon arrangement — condemns the notion that unbaptized infants inhabit “a certain middle place” where they live happily though barred from the kingdom of heaven. It is of doubtful authenticity, “wanting in Isidore and Dionysius” (the two early canon-collections), and so is commonly dropped. Nothing in the infant-baptism witness rests on it: the binding, well-attested canon is the second, on original sin, not the disputed one on the fate of the unbaptized.4
  • How it sits beside Cyprian’s council. Both are North African synods, and both treat infant baptism as a settled custom rather than an open question — but they settle different things. Cyprian’s council of c. 253 ruled on when (baptize the newborn at once, don’t wait for the eighth day). The council of 418 rules on why (because the infant carries Adam’s sin to be remitted), turning the rationale into a term of communion. The two African councils thus bookend the patristic development: the practice fixed early, its doctrine defined at the end of the age.
  • Numbering across editions. These canons circulate in two forms: as “the canons of the Council of Carthage of 418” (numbered 1–8/9) and as canons of the Codex Canonum Ecclesiae Africanae (the African Code codified at Carthage in 419 and printed in the Nicene and Post-Nicene Fathers), where the infant-baptism canon appears as Canon 110 (Greek cxxi). The text is the same; only the number differs.
