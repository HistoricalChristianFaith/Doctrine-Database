Peter of Bruys on infant baptism

Dates: active c. 1117 – c. 1131 (burned at the stake by a mob at Saint-Gilles, near Nîmes; the timeline anchors him at c. 1131, the close of his ministry) · Wikipedia
Summary: A credobaptist witness — and the best-attested medieval opponent of infant baptism, roughly four centuries before the Anabaptists. An itinerant preacher in Provence and Languedoc, Peter of Bruys taught that baptism saves only where personal faith precedes it, so that the baptism of infants — who cannot yet believe — is worthless, and those baptized in infancy must receive the true baptism once they come to faith. His teaching survives only in the reports of his opponents, chiefly Peter the Venerable’s Contra Petrobrusianos (c. 1139–1140), but on the baptismal question it is a genuine anticipation of the believers’-baptism principle — not, as with the dualist Cathars, a rejection of the material sacrament as such.

Claims & evidence

Infant baptism is worthless, because baptism requires personal faith (c. 1117–1131)

Peter of Bruys left no writings of his own; his doctrine is known from the treatise the abbot of Cluny, Peter the Venerable, wrote against his followers. Its opening lists five Petrobrusian errors, of which the first is the rejection of infant baptism — summarized by Philip Schaff:

The baptism of persons before they have reached the years of discretion is invalid. Believers’ baptism was based upon Mark 16:16, and children, growing up, were rebaptized.

— Philip Schaff, History of the Christian Church, vol. 5, on Peter de Bruys.1 The Catholic Encyclopedia makes the underlying logic exact — and, importantly, shows that Peter did not reject water baptism as such but revalued it as a believer’s rite:

In his system baptism is indeed a necessary condition for salvation, but it is baptism preceded by personal faith, so that its administration to infants is worthless.

— N. A. Weber, “Petrobrusians,” Catholic Encyclopedia (1913).2 This is the same axis the credobaptist tradition would later contest: baptism belongs to the one who can believe for himself, so a newborn is no fit subject — the broad faith-precedes-baptism case, read here off Mark 16:16 (“He that believeth and is baptized shall be saved”) four hundred years before Menno rested the same exclusion on the same verse.

“Not rebaptism, but baptism” — the Petrobrusian reply (as reported)

The fullest report of the Petrobrusian argument, translated from Peter the Venerable’s treatise, puts the case in the dissenters’ own reported words — including their insistence (later echoed by the Anabaptists, from whom the very name comes) that what they did was not re-baptism at all, since the infant washing had never been a real baptism:

Hence infants, although they are baptized by you, nevertheless, because they cannot believe, since they are as yet too young, are not at all saved… But we wait for the time when a person, after he has known God, is ready to believe on Him, and so baptize him – not rebaptize him, as you accuse us – but baptize him, since he had not before been baptized with baptism which cleanses from sin.

— Peter the Venerable, Contra Petrobrusianos, trans. in J. C. Reagan, “Did the Petrobrusians Teach Salvation by Faith Alone?” The Journal of Religion 7 (1927), p. 89.3 Peter the Venerable’s own counter is the mirror-image of the whole paedobaptist case — if the martyrs are saved by faith without baptism, why can children not be saved “by baptism alone without faith?” — which concedes, in passing, that his opponents made faith the thing baptism answers to.4

Notes

  • A hostile-source witness. Everything we know of Peter of Bruys comes from his opponents — Peter the Venerable’s Contra Petrobrusianos (c. 1139–1140) and a passing notice in Peter Abelard. Like Pelagius on the other side of this timeline, he is attested chiefly by the men who wrote to refute him; but the report is detailed and consistent, and the baptismal error stands first in the list precisely because it was the most conspicuous.
  • Genuine believers’ baptism — not dualism. Peter of Bruys must be kept distinct from the roughly contemporary dualist Cathars, who also refused infant baptism but did so because they held all matter (water included) to be the work of an evil creator, replacing water baptism with a spiritual laying-on-of-hands (the consolamentum). Peter, by contrast, held baptism “a necessary condition for salvation” and merely required that personal faith precede it — a revaluation of the rite, not its abolition. Scholars note he and his successor Henry of Lausanne even forced clergy and monks to marry, the opposite of the Cathar rejection of matrimony on dualist grounds.
  • Henry of Lausanne. Peter’s preaching was carried on after his death by Henry of Lausanne, whose followers (the “Henricians”) held the same view of baptism; Bernard of Clairvaux preached against them in the Toulouse region in 1145. The movement was later absorbed into the broader current of southern-French dissent.
  • Bearing on “never contested.” Peter of Bruys is a standing qualification to the paedobaptist claim that infant baptism went essentially unchallenged from antiquity to the Reformation — the point weighed on the antiquity-and-universality argument. His dissent is real and explicit; but it is a twelfth-century protest, not evidence of a continuous underground believers’-baptism church, and it does not touch the earlier centuries in which the practice genuinely was uncontested.
  • Frequently conflated with the Waldenses. Successionist histories often merge Peter of Bruys with the Waldenses, whose founder Valdes actually affirmed infant baptism (1180). The genuinely anti-paedobaptist medieval group is the Petrobrusians; the Waldensian attribution is the weaker, contested one.
