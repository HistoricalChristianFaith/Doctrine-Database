The Council of Milevum (416) on infant baptism

Dates: c. 416 (the second Council of Milevum — Mileve, in Numidia, modern Mila in Algeria — met in the autumn of 416, one of a pair of North-African anti-Pelagian synods held that year, the other at Carthage. It comes two years before the plenary Council of Carthage of 418, whose canons made its doctrine binding law, and is not to be confused with the first Council of Milevum of 402) · Catholic Encyclopedia, “Pelagius and Pelagianism”
Summary: In the autumn of 416 the bishops of Numidia — Augustine’s own province — assembled at Milevum and, together with a parallel synod at Carthage, condemned the teaching of Pelagius and Coelestius and appealed to Pope Innocent I to confirm the condemnation. As at every stage of the Pelagian controversy, the practice of baptizing infants was never in dispute; what the synods rejected was the Pelagian re-reading of it — that the newborn is baptized for “sanctification” while carrying no inherited guilt from Adam. Milevum’s own act was its synodal letter to Rome, not a numbered canon; the famous anathema against denying infant baptism, sometimes cited as “Milevum, canon 3,” is in fact the second canon of the 418 Council of Carthage.

Claims & evidence

The two African synods of 416 and their appeal to Innocent I

When Pelagius was acquitted at the synod of Diospolis in Palestine (415), the African bishops moved to have the condemnation they had already pronounced against his disciple Coelestius (at Carthage in 411) confirmed by Rome. Two provincial synods met in the autumn of 416: about sixty-seven bishops of Proconsular Africa at Carthage under Aurelius, and some sixty bishops of the province of Numidia — the province of Augustine’s see of Hippo — at Milevum, presided over by Silvanus, the primate. In both places the doctrines of Pelagius and Coelestius were again rejected as contrary to the catholic faith.1

Each synod wrote to Pope Innocent I, seeking — as the Carthaginian letter put it — that the authority of the Apostolic See might be added to their own decrees; the appeal grounded that authority in Holy Scripture. To the two official synodal letters (the Carthaginian is Augustine’s Letter 175, the Milevitan his Letter 176) five bishops — Aurelius, Alypius, Augustine, Evodius, and Possidius — added a longer, familiar letter of their own (Letter 177), in which they set out at length the doctrine of original sin, infant baptism, and Christian grace, so that Innocent should have before him everything necessary to his decision.2

Infant baptism the shared premise, original sin the point at issue

What the Numidian bishops opposed was never the baptizing of infants, which the whole church — Pelagians included — took for granted. It was the Pelagian account of why the church baptized them. Coelestius had taught that infants are baptized not to wash away any guilt inherited from Adam but only for a higher sanctification; against this the Africans held, with Augustine, that the rite’s ancient formula “for the remission of sins” must be taken at face value, so that the sin remitted in a sinless newborn can only be the original sin contracted in generation. The 416 appeal thus carried to Rome exactly the argument-from-the-liturgy that Augustine was pressing and that the Council of Carthage of 418 would fix as conciliar law.3

Innocent answered the three letters in three separate epistles dated 27 January 417. He praised the bishops’ zeal, agreed with the African decisions, affirmed the necessity of inward grace, rejected the Pelagian theory of infant baptism, and declared Pelagius and Coelestius excluded from communion until they should return to orthodoxy — the ruling Augustine is popularly said to have greeted with the words “Rome has spoken; the case is closed.” After Innocent’s successor Zosimus briefly wavered, the plenary Council of Carthage of 1 May 418 settled the matter with the anti-Pelagian canons.4

Notes

  • “Milevum, canon 3” is really Carthage 418, canon 2. Popular patristic catenae on infant baptism sometimes quote the anathema “whoever says that infants fresh from their mothers’ wombs ought not to be baptized… let him be anathema” and attribute it to “the Council of Mileum II (416), canon 3.” The wording, however, is the second canon of the plenary Council of Carthage of 418 (Denzinger no. 102). The confusion is editorial: Denzinger prints the eight anti-Pelagian canons under a single joint heading — “Council of Mileum II 416, approved by Innocent, and Council of Carthage (XVI) 418, approved by Zosimus (against the Pelagians)” — because the same doctrine was affirmed at both. Milevum’s distinct 416 act was its synodal letter to Innocent, not a separate numbered canon; the numbered canons belong to the 418 synod. Nothing on the infant-baptism timeline is lost by the correction: the 416 councils supply the appeal, the 418 council supplies the canon.5
  • Milevum I (402) vs. Milevum II (416). Two councils met at Milevum. The first, in 402, dealt with African church discipline; the second, the anti-Pelagian synod of 416, is the one in view here. Only the second bears on infant baptism.
  • Where it sits on the timeline. Milevum 416 falls between Pelagius’s own confession (c. 415), which conceded the practice while denying its original-sin rationale, and the Council of Carthage of 418, which made that rationale binding. It is the conciliar hinge of the controversy: the moment the African church put the question formally to Rome.
