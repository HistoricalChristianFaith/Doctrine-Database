Justin Martyr on infant baptism

Dates: c. 155 (Justin Martyr, c. 100–c. 165 — the Palestinian-born philosopher who became Christianity’s foremost second-century apologist and was martyred at Rome under Marcus Aurelius; his First Apology is usually dated c. 155–157 and the Dialogue with Trypho a few years later) · Wikipedia
Summary: Justin gives the fullest surviving pre-Tertullian account of Christian baptism, and on the infant-baptism question he is a neutral — double-edged — witness. His set-piece description of the rite (First Apology 61) portrays instructed adults who “are persuaded and believe” and are then “regenerated,” the datum the credobaptist reads as the original norm; yet paedobaptists adduce two hints the other way — his mention of those who “have been Christ’s disciples from childhood” (First Apology 15) and his framing of baptism as the “spiritual circumcision” that replaces the carnal one (Dialogue 43), circumcision having been given to infants. Neither hint names an infant, and his own baptismal narrative describes professing believers, so Justin fixes what the standard rite looked like around the mid-second century without settling whose children were brought to it.

Claims & evidence

The baptismal set-piece describes instructed, professing believers

Justin’s First Apology 61 is the most detailed second-century description of Christian initiation we possess, written to explain the rite to a pagan emperor. It makes the candidate’s own persuasion and resolve the entry-point of the whole process:

As many as are persuaded and believe that what we teach and say is true, and undertake to be able to live accordingly, are instructed to pray and to entreat God with fasting, for the remission of their sins that are past, we praying and fasting with them. Then they are brought by us where there is water, and are regenerated in the same manner in which we were ourselves regenerated.

— The First Apology 61, trans. Dods & Reith (Ante-Nicene Fathers vol. 1), full text (cf. New Advent).1 The candidate is one who is “persuaded and believe[s],” who “undertake[s]” a manner of life, and who fasts and prays for the remission of past sins before the water — each of them an act a newborn cannot perform. This is the same portrait the earlier Didache paints of a catechized candidate who fasts before the font, and it is the pre-Tertullian datum the credobaptist reads as the original norm: the fullest second-century account of baptism describes an adult convert, with no mention of infants. Kurt Aland pressed exactly this material against Joachim Jeremias — the direct record before Tertullian is not silent about baptism but fairly full, and what it describes is adult, faith-professing baptism.2

“Disciples from childhood” — a hint the paedobaptist adduces

Defending the moral seriousness of Christians, Justin points to lifelong believers who have kept chastity into old age:

And many, both men and women, who have been Christ’s disciples from childhood, remain pure at the age of sixty or seventy years; and I boast that I could produce such from every race of men.

— The First Apology 15, full text (cf. New Advent).3 Jeremias adduced this line (1 Apology 15.6) as an early hint that children were incorporated into the church from the first — if these sexagenarians and septuagenarians had been Christ’s “disciples from childhood,” some, on the paedobaptist reading, were baptized as children. But the phrase will not bear much: “disciple from childhood” (ἐκ παίδων μαθητεύω) most naturally means catechized and raised in the faith from a young age — discipleship being precisely a matter of teaching, which presupposes an age of understanding — and says nothing of the age at baptism. Aland read it, consistently with 1 Apology 61, as describing those brought up as Christians who professed and were baptized when able, not baptized infants. The line is thus a genuine but double-edged datum: it shows children within the Christian community, not that they were baptized in infancy.4

Baptism as “spiritual circumcision” — corroboration for the covenant argument

In the Dialogue with Trypho, arguing against his Jewish interlocutor that the fleshly circumcision of the Law has given way to a spiritual one, Justin identifies that spiritual circumcision with baptism:

And we, who have approached God through Him, have received not carnal, but spiritual circumcision, which Enoch and those like him observed. And we have received it through baptism, since we were sinners, by God’s mercy; and all men may equally obtain it.

— Dialogue with Trypho 43, full text (cf. New Advent).5 This is the earliest explicit equation of baptism with circumcision — the backbone of the later Reformed covenant-circumcision argument: baptism now does what circumcision once did, and circumcision was administered to eight-day-old infants (Genesis 17), so the covenant sign belongs to a believer’s children. Justin supplies the premise (baptism = the new circumcision) that Cyprian, Ambrose, and Calvin would build the paedobaptist case upon — but he draws no such inference himself. His own gloss cuts the other way in one respect: those who receive this spiritual circumcision are said to have “approached God through Him” and to have received it “since we were sinners,” language of a conscious, believing convert, and the infant-circumcision half of the analogy is left implicit. The line is real corroboration for the covenant reading, not a witness to the practice.6

On the subjects of baptism he is neutral — and that is what both sides claim

Justin nowhere raises the question whether the infant children of Christians were baptized; the controversy had not yet been joined (the first explicit discussant, Tertullian, is a half-century later). What he describes — a persuaded, believing candidate who fasts before the water — fits an adult convert, which the credobaptist reads as the original norm. The paedobaptist replies that the genre settles nothing: an apology written to explain the faith to a pagan emperor, and a dialogue with a Jew about the abrogation of the Law, both naturally describe the adult-convert case and are silent, not exclusionary, about believers’ newborns. On this axis Justin is the same kind of witness as the Didache — a mid-second-century terminus fixing what the standard rite looked like, adduced by both sides on the dating-of-the-earliest-mention argument, and settling neither.7

Notes

  • As a c. 155 witness Justin sits between the Didache (c. 100) and Irenaeus (c. 180) on this timeline, before the first explicit discussant, Tertullian (c. 203). He is a witness to the rite as practised in the mid-second century, not to a position in a debate not yet begun.
  • Justin is cited on the dating argument on the critical side — his 1 Apology 61 is one of the pre-200 descriptions that portray professing adults — and this detail page keeps that reading: on balance his baptismal narrative describes believers, while the two paedobaptist hints (1 Apology 15; Dialogue 43) are real but double-edged. The two treatments are meant to agree, not to pull opposite ways.
  • The Dialogue 43 “spiritual circumcision” line is the earliest datum feeding the covenant-circumcision argument; it establishes the baptism–circumcision equation at the mid-second century but does not itself extend the sign to infants.
