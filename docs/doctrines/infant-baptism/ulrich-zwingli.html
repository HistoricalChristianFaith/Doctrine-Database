Huldrych Zwingli on infant baptism

Dates: c. 1525 (Von der Taufe, von der Wiedertaufe und von der Kindertaufe — “Of Baptism, Of Rebaptism, and Of Infant Baptism” — published at Zürich on 27 May 1525, the booklet Hubmaier answered; the fuller argument quoted below is from his Refutation of the Tricks of the Catabaptists, July 1527). Huldrych (Ulrich) Zwingli, 1484–1531, born at Wildhaus in the Toggenburg; from 1519 people’s priest at the Grossmünster in Zürich and leader of the Swiss (Reformed) Reformation. From 1523 his own former associates among the Zürich radicals — Conrad Grebel and Felix Manz — pressed him to give up infant baptism; after they began rebaptizing one another in January 1525 and the city council outlawed the practice, Zwingli defended paedobaptism in print, first in Of Baptism (May 1525) and then at length in the Refutation of the Tricks of the Catabaptists (In catabaptistarum strophas elenchus, 1527). He was killed at the battle of Kappel on 11 October 1531 · Wikipedia
Summary: Zwingli is the first magisterial Reformer to defend infant baptism against the Anabaptists, and the first to rest that defence on the covenant: baptism now does what circumcision once did, so the covenant sign belongs to a believer’s children as it belonged to Hebrew infants. The child is already “of the people of God” before he can profess anything, so — against the credobaptist demand for prior faith — he is not barred from the sign for being unable to hear or understand. It is the covenant-circumcision case Calvin would systematize a decade later, weighed on its argument page.

Claims & evidence

A believer’s children are already sons of God — so who forbids their baptism?

Answering the Swiss Brethren’s objection that no infant is named as baptized in the New Testament, Zwingli argues from the standing of the child within the covenant people rather than from any act of the child’s own:

The children of Christians are no less sons of God than the parents, just as in the Old Testament. Hence, since they are sons of God, who will forbid their baptism?

— Huldrych Zwingli, Refutation of the Tricks of the Catabaptists (1527).1

Circumcision was the counterpart of baptism — given to infants, so baptism should be too

On the same page he states the circumcision parallel that is the backbone of the whole Reformed case, and draws the paedobaptist conclusion directly from it:

Circumcision among the ancients (so far as it was sacramental) was the same as baptism with us. As that was given to infants so ought baptism to be administered to infants.

— Huldrych Zwingli, Refutation of the Tricks of the Catabaptists (1527).2 This is the argument — one covenant of grace, its sign passing from circumcision to baptism, so the believer’s infant is its natural subject — that Calvin would build into the settled shape of the Reformed defence, and that the timeline weighs adversarially on the covenant-circumcision argument page (assessment: contested).

Membership in God’s people precedes the sign — so the infant’s want of faith is no bar

Where Hubmaier and the Brethren made hearing, faith, and confession prior conditions of a valid baptism, Zwingli reverses the order of dependence: one belongs to the covenant body first, and the sign is added to mark a membership already held. An infant who cannot yet hear or understand is therefore no more excluded than a sleeping or speechless adult:

But since infants are of the people of God, they are not excluded because they cannot hear or understand. For that they are members of one and the same body of God’s people is clear from this, that circumcision, the sign of the covenant, is given them… by nature being of the body precedes bearing the mark of the body… The grace of the spirit by which we are admitted into union with the church precedes the sign of union. For no one is sealed unless he has first been enrolled in the army or service.

— Huldrych Zwingli, Refutation of the Tricks of the Catabaptists (1527).3 This is Zwingli’s answer to the credobaptist “faith first” argument (weighed on the faith-precedes-baptism page): baptism does not create covenant membership but seals it, so the recipient’s prior profession is not what the New Testament requires. Luther, defending the same practice two years later, would make a parallel move — that a valid baptism rests on God’s Word, not the recipient’s faith — from a sacramental rather than a covenantal starting point.

Notes

  • The first magisterial reply to the Anabaptists. Zwingli’s Of Baptism (May 1525) is the treatise Hubmaier answered in his On the Christian Baptism of Believers that July, and it opened the paedobaptist side of the Reformation baptismal controversy — before Schleitheim (1527), Luther’s Large Catechism (1529), and Calvin’s Institutes (1536). He anchors the timeline’s magisterial-Reformed wing at 1525.4
  • Zwingli before Calvin on the covenant. The covenant-circumcision argument is usually associated with Calvin, but Zwingli states it first and plainly (“circumcision… was the same as baptism with us”). His translator’s later judgment — that Zwingli never worked out a fully coherent theology of baptism, yet with his definition of it as a covenant sign “indicated the lines along which… profitable work was to be done” by later Reformed theologians — captures his place: the originator of the covenantal defence, not its systematizer.5
  • He had briefly leaned the other way. Zwingli concedes in Of Baptism that he had once thought it better to defer a child’s baptism until it could understand — the same position the radicals took — before concluding against it; the concession is part of why the Brethren felt he had betrayed a view he once shared.6
