John Chrysostom on infant baptism

Dates: c. 390 (Chrysostom, c. 349–407; the great preacher of Antioch, later archbishop of Constantinople. The passage is from his Third Baptismal Instruction — part of the eight-homily “Stavronikita series” recovered by Antoine Wenger and first published in 1957 — delivered to catechumens at Antioch; the series is dated to the period 389–398, most likely c. 390) · Wikipedia
Summary: Chrysostom takes infant baptism entirely for granted — “we baptize even infants” — but is a striking Eastern witness for the rationale: he says infants are baptized although they are sinless, and pointedly leaves the remission of sins off the list of what baptism gives them, grounding the practice instead in the positive gifts of grace (sanctification, adoption, membership in Christ). Where the Western tradition of Cyprian and Augustine ties infant baptism to inherited sin, Chrysostom attaches it to the abundance of grace, not to guilt to be washed away.

Claims & evidence

“We baptize even infants, although they are sinless” — for the further gifts of grace (Third Baptismal Instruction 3.6)

Having enumerated the many things baptism confers — not remission of sins only but a whole train of honors — Chrysostom turns to infants to prove the point that the rite gives far more than forgiveness:

Blessed be God, who alone does wonderful things! You have seen how numerous are the gifts of baptism. Although many men think that the only gift it confers is the remission of sins, we have counted its honors to the number of ten. It is on this account that we baptize even infants, although they are sinless, that they may be given the further gifts of sanctification, justice, filial adoption, and inheritance, that they may be brothers and members of Christ, and become dwelling places for the Spirit.

— John Chrysostom, Baptismal Instructions (Stavronikita series) 3.6, trans. Paul W. Harkins.1 The “ten honors” are the ones he has just been reciting: in the preceding section he tells the newly baptized they are “not only free, but also holy; not only holy, but also just; not only just, but also sons; not only sons, but also heirs; not only heirs, but also brothers of Christ; not only brothers of Christ, but also joint heirs; not only joint heirs, but also members; not only members, but also the temple; not only the temple, but also instruments of the Spirit.”2

Notes

  • The witness to the practice is incidental — and all the stronger for it. Chrysostom is not arguing for infant baptism; he assumes it, and reasons from it. His point is about the wealth of what baptism gives, and infants are his proof: since they plainly have no sins to remit, the fact that the Church baptizes them anyway shows that baptism must confer much more than remission. That the custom can be used as a settled premise in an Antiochene catechetical homily around 390 places it as firmly in the fourth-century East as Gregory of Nazianzus (c. 381) does.
  • An Eastern rationale, distinct from the Western original-sin framing. The most notable feature is the ground Chrysostom gives — or rather does not give. He calls infants “sinless” and lists what baptism gives them without including the remission of sins at all. The translator’s note draws out the significance: “sinless” renders a phrase literally meaning “having no sins”; if that refers to actual sins there is no difficulty (this is how Augustine read the text), but Wenger observes that Chrysostom omits remission of sin from the benefits granted to infants and takes this as “some foundation for the position that Chrysostom considered infants free from all sins, actual and original.”3 Either way the contrast with the Latin West is real: where Cyprian’s council roots the newborn’s baptism in the “contagion of the ancient death” contracted from Adam, and Augustine would soon make original guilt the very reason for the rite, Chrysostom grounds it in the sheer abundance of grace — the same practice, a different theology of why.
  • Not a denial of inherited fallenness. The point should not be overstated into a flat claim that Chrysostom held no doctrine of original sin. His teaching on the subject is famously less developed than the later Augustinian synthesis, and Wenger’s inference is offered as a tendency the passage “gives some foundation” to, not as a settled dogma; Chrysostom elsewhere speaks of the wound humanity inherits from Adam. What the passage firmly shows is narrower and secure: that in his practice and preaching infants are baptized as a matter of course, and that the remission of personal sin is conspicuously not the reason he gives for baptizing them.
