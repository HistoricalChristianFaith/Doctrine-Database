Petr Chelčický and the Unity of the Brethren on infant baptism

Dates: Petr Chelčický c. 1390 – c. 1460 (his Net of Faith, c. 1440–1443, is the timeline anchor, c. 1443); the Unity of the Brethren he inspired was organized at Kunvald in 1457, and abandoned its rebaptism of adult converts by act of the General Synod of 1534 · Wikipedia
Summary: A qualified credobaptist-leaning witness from the Bohemian (Hussite) reformation, filling the gap between Peter of Bruys and the Swiss Anabaptists. The lay theologian Petr Chelčický made personal, uncoerced faith central to the Christian life and preferred that baptism follow it — yet he did not demand rebaptism and did not flatly forbid infant baptism. His heirs, the Unity of the Brethren (Unitas Fratrum), went a step further in practice: they rebaptized adults who joined them from Rome, holding personal faith an essential condition of the baptismal covenant, while still baptizing their own infants — a practice they formally dropped in 1534, partly to distinguish themselves from the Anabaptists. The nuance is the point: this is a real anticipation of believers’ baptism, but a hedged one.

Claims & evidence

Chelčický: faith is not coerced, and should precede the sacrament (c. 1443)

Chelčický, a self-taught South-Bohemian layman who influenced the radical wing of the Hussite movement, grounded everything on a personal faith that cannot be compelled — the conviction from which his preference for a professed, later baptism followed. In The Net of Faith he writes:

By the use of force no man is brought to faith in Christ, as unlikely as that a man should acquire a knowledge of the Czech language by studying German.

— Petr Chelčický, The Net of Faith, trans. Enrico C. S. Molnár.1 From this stress on unforced, personal faith Chelčický preferred to baptize those of older age who could profess it — but, unlike the later Anabaptists, he stopped short of the two moves that would define the credobaptist break: he “did not completely forbid infant baptism, allowing it if the parents would assure their education in the faith,” and he “did not propose re-baptism.”2

The Unity of the Brethren: rebaptism required, then relinquished (1457–1534)

Where Chelčický hedged, the church that grew from his teaching — the Unitas Fratrum, organized at Kunvald in 1457 — put the principle into practice, making personal faith a condition of the baptismal covenant and rebaptizing those who came to it as adults, even while it continued to baptize infants:

They rebaptized converts from the Roman Catholic and national churches, because they deemed both to be idolatrous; and they extended this practice to the young, because they considered personal faith an essential condition of the baptismal covenant. But they did not on this account reject infant baptism. Children were baptized soon after their birth, and thus dedicated to God; then they were rebaptized, after a thorough course of instruction in the Catechism, when old enough to exercise personal faith… This practice, however, was relinquished by a formal act of the General Synod of 1534, and confirmation substituted in the place of rebaptism.

— “Moravian Brethren,” McClintock & Strong, Cyclopædia of Biblical, Theological, and Ecclesiastical Literature.3 The Brethren thus occupied a middle position no later party would hold together for long: infant baptism and an adult rebaptism upon profession. The 1534 abandonment of rebaptism — replacing it with confirmation — was itself partly a deliberate distancing from the Anabaptists then spreading in German lands, whose radicalism the Brethren did not wish to share.4

Notes

  • Why “qualified.” Chelčický is not a clean believers’-baptism witness in the way Peter of Bruys or Hubmaier is: he tolerated infant baptism and did not call for rebaptism. He belongs on this timeline as a witness to the instinct that faith should precede the font — an instinct his church half-institutionalized (rebaptizing adults) and then walked back. Told without the nuance, he would be miscast on either side.
  • Forerunner, not ancestor, of the Anabaptists. Historians see the Brethren and the sixteenth-century Anabaptists as sharing themes (personal faith, discipleship, nonresistance, separation from a coercive church) without a demonstrated direct line of descent. The 1534 repudiation of rebaptism cuts against any simple genealogy: at the very moment the Swiss and Dutch Anabaptists were making adult baptism their banner, the Brethren were laying theirs down.
  • Sources and their limits. Chelčický’s baptismal views reach us mainly through his broader writings and later summaries rather than a single set-piece on baptism; only Part I of The Net of Faith exists in English (Molnár), and it does not treat baptism directly. The load-bearing statement of the Brethren’s actual practice is the nineteenth-century reference account quoted above, corroborated by modern scholarship on the Bohemian reformation.
