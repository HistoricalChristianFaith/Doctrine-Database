Balthasar Hubmaier on infant baptism

Dates: c. 1525 (Von dem christlichen Tauf der Gläubigen — “On the Christian Baptism of Believers” — finished at Waldshut on 11 July 1525, in answer to Zwingli’s baptism booklet of that spring). Balthasar Hubmaier, c. 1480–1528, born at Friedberg near Augsburg; doctor of theology at Ingolstadt (1512) and a pupil of Johann Eck, cathedral preacher at Regensburg, then reforming pastor at Waldshut on the Rhine, where in 1525 he embraced believers’ baptism and became the ablest theologian of first-generation Anabaptism. He was baptized by Wilhelm Reublin at Waldshut about Easter 1525 and himself baptized some three hundred there. Burned at the stake in Vienna on 10 March 1528; his wife Elsbeth, who had urged him to hold firm, was drowned in the Danube with a stone about her neck three days later · Wikipedia
Summary: Two years before Schleitheim put the credobaptist position into a confession, Hubmaier had already argued it at length. His On the Christian Baptism of Believers (1525) — written in five days at Waldshut against Zwingli, and reckoned by historians the most effective Reformation-era defence of believers’ baptism — defines baptism as belonging to one who confesses his own sins, and reads off Scripture a fixed order (word, hearing, faith, baptism, works) that an infant, who cannot be taught or believe, is unable to meet. It is the same teach-then-baptize case Smyth would cast as a syllogism eighty years later, argued here first and in full by the movement’s leading theologian.

Claims & evidence

Baptism belongs to the confessor of his own sins

Hubmaier’s treatise opens by distinguishing many senses of “baptism” and settling on Christian water-baptism as an outward act that presupposes a personal, spoken confession — something no infant can make. His definition, as translated by Henry C. Vedder from the treatise:

To baptise in water is to pour outward water over the confessor of his sins, in accordance with the divine command, and to inscribe him in the number of sinners upon his own confession and acknowledgment.

— Balthasar Hubmaier, On the Christian Baptism of Believers (1525).1 By building the personal confession of the one baptized (“upon his own confession and acknowledgment”) into the very definition of the rite, Hubmaier makes the subject of baptism a self-confessing sinner — and so, like Schleitheim after him, excludes the newborn not by a separate rule but by what he takes baptism to be. This is the credobaptist move of grounding the exclusion in the nature of baptism itself, weighed on the faith-precedes-baptism argument page.

The order fixed in Scripture: word, hearing, faith, baptism — which an infant cannot meet

The heart of the treatise, across its seven chapters, is an argument from order. Vedder’s account of Hubmaier’s reasoning: examining the baptism of John, Hubmaier finds that “the order of John was: hearing of the word, repentance or conviction of sin, baptism, works” — John “baptised only those to whom he had first preached, who had therefore believed, confessed their sins, and promised amendment of life.” Turning to the baptism commanded by Christ, “the true order is found to be: the word, hearing, faith, baptism, works. His command is, ‘Go, teach all peoples, and baptise them…’ — Since infants cannot be taught, they should not be baptised.”2 Two of the credobaptist arguments later weighed on this timeline are already fully present here: the reading of Christ’s commission (Matthew 28:19) as a fixed sequence — teach, then baptize — that bars the un-teachable infant (the Great-Commission-order argument, which Smyth would later compress into a syllogism), and the broader appeal to the pattern by which the word is heard and believed before anyone is baptized (the faith-precedes-baptism argument). Hubmaier presses the point with a memorable image: to baptize a child as a future believer, he says, “is like hanging out a hoop as a sign of future wine.”3

Notes

  • The heavier theological witness behind Schleitheim. Where the Schleitheim Confession (1527) asserts the believers’-baptism position in a single article, Hubmaier — a trained doctor of theology — had already argued it at treatise length two years earlier, marshalling John’s baptism, Christ’s commission, and the apostolic practice. James Stayer judged On the Christian Baptism of Believers “the most effective defense of believers’ baptism in the Reformation era.” He anchors the timeline’s credobaptist wing at 1525, before Schleitheim and long before Smyth.4
  • Against Zwingli, and at the cost of his life. The treatise answers Zwingli’s baptism booklet directly, and Hubmaier begged the Zurich council to let Zwingli debate him: “If I err, I will gladly retract. If master Ulrich errs, he should not be ashamed to forsake his error, for the truth will ultimately conquer him.” The conviction is of a piece with the motto that became his emblem — Truth is immortal (Die Wahrheit ist untödlich). He did not retract: seized by Austrian authorities and, after torture, condemned, he was burned in Vienna on 10 March 1528, and his wife Elsbeth drowned in the Danube three days later.5
  • Mode, not only subject. Hubmaier’s own practice was affusion — on Easter 1525 he baptized some three hundred at Waldshut from a milk-pail of well-water set on the font — even as some Swiss Brethren were moving toward immersion. His case against paedobaptism thus turns on whom to baptize (the confessing believer), not on how, a reminder that the credobaptist argument and the immersion-mode argument are distinct axes — weighed separately on the immersion-mode page.6
