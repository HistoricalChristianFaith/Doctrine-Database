John Smyth on infant baptism

Dates: c. 1609 (The Character of the Beast, or The False Constitution of the Church, published at Amsterdam in March 1609). John Smyth, c. 1570–1612, an English Separatist minister — Christ’s College, Cambridge (M.A. 1593), sometime lecturer at Lincoln — who led his exiled congregation into believers’ baptism and, holding his own infant baptism void, baptized himself (hence “the Se-Baptist”), founding what is generally reckoned the first English Baptist church; he died of consumption at Amsterdam in August 1612 · 1911 Encyclopædia Britannica
Summary: Smyth carries the credobaptist case from Schleitheim’s confessional assertion to a formal argument from the Great Commission. Reasoning from Matthew 28:19, he holds that only those “made Disciples by teaching” are to be baptized, that infants cannot be so taught, and therefore that infants are not to be baptized — then presses the point logically: an affirmative precept contains a negative under it, so the command to “make Disciples by teaching & baptise them” itself forbids baptizing the un-taught. It is the seed of the “make-disciples-then-baptize excludes infants” reading of the Great Commission.

Claims & evidence

Only the taught are baptized (Matthew 28:19), so not infants — a syllogism and a precept

Answering the paedobaptist Richard Clifton, Smyth sets out two linked arguments against baptizing children — first a syllogism from the order of the Great Commission, then an argument from the logic of a command:

Hence therfor I reason against baptising infants.

1. They only are to be baptized that are made Disciples by teaching. Infants cannot be made Disciples by teaching. Ergo, Infants are not to be baptized. Secondly I reason thus.

2. Every precept affirmative conteyneth a negative vnder it. Make Disciples by teaching & baptise them, is an affirmative conteyning vnder it, baptize not those that are not made Disciples by teaching. Ergo, those that are not by teaching made Disciples, are by Christ forbidden to be baptized: & so infants are not to be baptized.

— John Smyth, The Character of the Beast (1609).1 The whole weight rests on reading Matthew 28:19 as a fixed order — disciple by teaching, then baptize — so that the subject of baptism is defined by a prior act (teaching) an infant cannot receive. The second argument sharpens the first from probability into prohibition: it is not merely that the commission fails to command infant baptism, but that, on Smyth’s logic of the affirmative precept, it positively forbids it. This is the same proof-text (the Great Commission of Matthew 28, with Mark 16) that Schleitheim had cited as “the foundation and testimony of the apostles,” now worked into an explicit exclusion — and the very text paedobaptists read the other way, taking the baptized “households” of Acts to carry the commission into the family.

Notes

  • From Separatist to Baptist to would-be Mennonite. Smyth had been an ordained Church of England minister before joining the Separatists and leading a congregation into exile in Amsterdam around 1608. The Character of the Beast (early 1609) marks his break with paedobaptism; concluding that the infant baptism he and his people had received was no baptism at all, and finding no one he counted a true administrator, he baptized himself and then the rest — the act that earned him the epithet Se-Baptist (“self-baptizer”). He soon doubted the propriety of the self-baptism, judged that he should have sought baptism from the Waterlander Mennonites, and applied for admission to their fellowship; he died before the union was completed. Thomas Helwys, who dissented from that turn, led a remnant back to England and founded the first Baptist church on English soil (Spitalfields, c. 1612).2
  • An argument, not just an assertion. Where the timeline’s first credobaptist witness, Schleitheim, had defined baptism so as to exclude infants (repentance, faith, and a personal request made constitutive of the rite), Smyth argues the exclusion from a shared proof-text. That makes his syllogism the seed of a discrete interpretive claim — that the Great Commission’s “make disciples… baptizing them” sequence bars the baptism of the un-taught — which cuts across the timeline and is weighed on its own terms rather than settled by who first voiced it, on its argument page.
  • Where the argument overreaches. Smyth’s second syllogism — “every precept affirmative conteyneth a negative vnder it,” so the command to baptize the taught positively forbids baptizing the un-taught — presses the case past what the text will bear. An affirmative command specifying a subject does not of itself prohibit the unmentioned case (“teach all nations” did not forbid teaching individuals; by the same logic Calvin noted one could bar women from the Lord’s Supper, none being named at it in the apostolic record). The Great Commission’s silence on the children of believers is thus turned into a positive prohibition it does not contain — an argument from silence, adjudicated on the argument page (assessment: contested). His first syllogism — that in the commission baptism follows disciple-making, as it does throughout Acts — is on far firmer ground; even the paedobaptist Jerome reads the same teach-then-baptize order off the verse without drawing any bar to infant baptism from it.
