The Swiss Brethren on infant baptism

Dates: c. 1525 (the first believers’ baptism took place at Zürich on the night of 21 January 1525, four days after the second Zürich baptism disputation of 17 January. The founders were Conrad Grebel, c. 1498–1526, of a prominent Zürich family, reckoned the chief founder of Swiss Anabaptism; Felix Manz, c. 1498–1527, son of a Grossmünster canon, drowned in the Limmat on 5 January 1527 as the first Anabaptist martyred by a Protestant government; and George Blaurock — Jörg vom Haus Jacob, “George of the house of Jacob” — c. 1491–1529, a former monk, burned at Klausen in the Tyrol on 6 September 1529) · GAMEO
Summary: A credobaptist witness — and the founding event of the whole movement, not a text but a deed. Under a Zürich council order to baptize their infants or be expelled, the radicals who had broken with Zwingli over the font baptized one another on their own confession of faith: Grebel baptized Blaurock “upon his faith and knowledge,” and Blaurock baptized the rest. It is the first known adult re-baptism since antiquity — the act that makes personal, professed faith the thing baptism answers to, so that no infant can be its subject, and thus the origin behind the confessions and treatises (Schleitheim, Hubmaier, Menno, Smyth) that later put the position into words.

Claims & evidence

The first believers’ baptism (21 January 1525)

Grebel, Manz, and their circle had pressed Zwingli since 1523 to abandon infant baptism; when he left the question to the Zürich council and the council decided against them, the break came. After the disputation of 17 January 1525 the council ordered that every unbaptized infant be baptized within eight days on pain of banishment — a mandate that fell directly on Grebel, who had refused to baptize his daughter Rachel, born on 5 January. On the evening of 21 January the group — some sixteen of them — met at the house of Felix Manz’s mother. The earliest narrative, in the Chronicle of the Hutterian Brethren (probably resting on Blaurock’s own account), tells what happened:

And it came to pass that they were together until anxiety came upon them, yes, they were so pressed within their hearts. Thereupon they began to bow their knees to the Most High God in heaven and called upon him as the Informer of Hearts, and they prayed that he would give to them his divine will and that he would show his mercy unto them. For flesh and blood and human forwardness did not drive them, since they well knew what they would have to suffer on account of it. After the prayer, George of the house of Jacob stood up and besought Conrad Grebel for God’s sake to baptize him with the true Christian baptism upon his faith and knowledge. And when he knelt down with such a request and desire, Conrad baptized him, since at that time there was no ordained minister to perform such work.

— The Chronicle of the Hutterian Brethren, p. 45. Blaurock, once baptized, then baptized the others present, and they pledged themselves as disciples to live separated from the world.1 Historians reckon this the moment Swiss Anabaptism was born: the first adult baptism-on-confession since the ancient church, performed by laymen because “there was no ordained minister” who would — itself a claim that the believing congregation, not the state church, holds the sacrament.2

Why it counts as a witness — the deed before the doctrine

The rebaptism is a witness on this timeline in the way an act can be: it does not argue against infant baptism, it makes it impossible. Blaurock asks to be baptized “upon his faith and knowledge”; the baptism answers to something the recipient professes, and a newborn professes nothing. The whole later credobaptist case is contained in that request as a practice before it is spelled out as a doctrine — two years before Schleitheim made personal repentance, faith, and a conscious request constitutive of baptism, and before Hubmaier fixed the order “word, hearing, faith, baptism, works,” Menno reasoned from the un-teachability of infants, and Smyth compressed it into a syllogism from Matthew 28:19. What those witnesses assert, the Swiss Brethren already did. It is also the decisive break with the magisterial Reformation: Zwingli answered the act in print within months (Of Baptism, May 1525), grounding paedobaptism in covenant standing, and Calvin and Luther likewise retained the infant baptism the patristic witnesses had handed down.

The seed before the meeting — Wilhelm Reublin

The 21 January baptism was not an isolated impulse. Wilhelm Reublin, a priest in the villages around Zürich, had been preaching against infant baptism as early as 1523, urging parents not to present their children — part of the ferment out of which the Grebel circle grew. Reublin later carried believers’ baptism outward: at Waldshut about Easter 1525 he baptized Balthasar Hubmaier, who in turn baptized some three hundred there. The Swiss Brethren’s act, in other words, stood at the head of a rapidly branching movement — the fountainhead of the Swiss Brethren proper and, through them, of the Mennonites, Hutterites, Amish, and later the Baptists.3

Notes

  • An act, not a person, on the timeline. This entry stands for a corporate event with three principal actors rather than a single author. Grebel is usually named the chief founder, but the baptism was mutual (Grebel baptized Blaurock; Blaurock baptized the others), and Manz’s home was its setting — so the witness is properly the Swiss Brethren as a body at their origin, distinct from the later individual voices (Hubmaier, Schleitheim/Sattler, Menno, Smyth) already on this timeline.
  • Mode. The first baptisms were almost certainly by affusion — water poured on the head — not immersion; some Swiss Brethren moved toward immersion only later, and Hubmaier himself baptized by pouring. The founding act thus turns on whom to baptize (the confessing believer), not on how — the two axes weighed separately on the immersion-mode argument page.
  • Cost. All three founders were dead within five years: Grebel of plague in 1526, Manz drowned by the Zürich authorities in 1527, Blaurock burned in the Tyrol in 1529 — a measure of how sharply the magisterial and Catholic authorities alike met the rejection of infant baptism.
