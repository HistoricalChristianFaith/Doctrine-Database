Martin Luther on infant baptism

Dates: c. 1529 (the Large Catechism — Der Große Katechismus — published at Wittenberg in 1529; its author, Martin Luther, 1483–1546, the Augustinian friar whose 1517 protest opened the Reformation) · Wikipedia
Summary: Two years after the Schleitheim Brethren made conscious faith a prerequisite of baptism, Luther answers the Anabaptist objection head-on in the fourth part of the Large Catechism. He defends infant baptism on two grounds. First, from its fruits: God has plainly sanctified many who were baptized as infants and given them the Holy Spirit — witness Bernard, Gerson, and Hus — which He would not do if their baptism displeased Him. Second, and more fundamentally, he relocates the ground of a valid baptism away from the recipient’s faith and onto God’s Word and command: “my faith does not make Baptism, but receives it,” so that a baptism is valid “even though faith be wanting.” This second point — that the Word, not the believer, constitutes the sacrament — is the distinctively magisterial-Reformation defense of the practice the whole patristic tradition had handed down.

Claims & evidence

Infant baptism is pleasing to God, proved by the Spirit’s fruits in those baptized as infants

Luther opens by refusing to make the infant’s faith the pivot of the question, then argues from what God has visibly done in those baptized in infancy:

That the Baptism of infants is pleasing to Christ is sufficiently proved from His own work, namely, that God sanctifies many of them who have been thus baptized, and has given them the Holy Ghost; and that there are yet many even to-day in whom we perceive that they have the Holy Ghost both because of their doctrine and life… But if God did not accept the baptism of infants, He would not give the Holy Ghost nor any of His gifts to any of them; in short, during this long time unto this day no man upon earth could have been a Christian. Now, since God confirms Baptism by the gifts of His Holy Ghost as is plainly perceptible in some of the church fathers, as St. Bernard, Gerson, John Hus, and others, who were baptized in infancy… they must acknowledge that such infant baptism is pleasing to God.

— Large Catechism, Part Fourth, Of Infant Baptism.1 The argument is an inference from effect to cause: God pours out the Holy Spirit — visibly, in doctrine and life — on people who received nothing but infant baptism, and “He can never be opposed to Himself, or support falsehood and wickedness” by lending His grace to a rite He rejected; therefore the rite pleases Him. Luther ties this to the creed’s article on the Church: were infant baptism false, then “during this long time unto this day no man upon earth could have been a Christian,” and “the holy Christian Church cannot perish until the end of the world.” He names Bernard of Clairvaux, Jean Gerson, and Jan Hus — figures even his opponents revered — as men whose sanctity is undeniable and whose only baptism was in infancy.

Baptism’s validity rests on the Word and command of God, not the recipient’s faith

Having answered from the fruits, Luther goes to the root of the Anabaptist premise — that a baptism without faith is no baptism — and denies it:

Further, we say that we are not so much concerned to know whether the person baptized believes or not; for on that account Baptism does not become invalid; but everything depends upon the Word and command of God. This now is perhaps somewhat acute but it rests entirely upon what I have said, that Baptism is nothing else than water and the Word of God in and with each other, that is when the Word is added to the water, Baptism is valid, even though faith be wanting. For my faith does not make Baptism, but receives it.

— Large Catechism, Part Fourth.2 This is the theologically decisive move. For Luther a sacrament is constituted by God’s Word joined to the element, and its validity therefore hangs on God’s act, not the recipient’s response: “my faith does not make Baptism, but receives it.” Faith is how one lays hold of the gift, but it is not what makes the gift real — so a baptism “does not become invalid even though it be wrongly received or employed.” He presses the point with a hard case: even a Jew baptized dishonestly “with evil purpose” would still receive a “genuine” baptism, “for here is the water together with the Word of God,” just as those who go unworthily to the Lord’s Supper “receive the true Sacrament even though they do not believe.”3 Where Schleitheim had made repentance, faith, and a personal request constitutive of baptism, Luther makes the Word constitutive and faith merely receptive — which removes at a stroke the objection that an infant, lacking faith, cannot be baptized.

Notes

  • Answering Schleitheim directly. Luther frames the whole discussion as a reply to “the devil through his sects” who “confuses the world… Of Infant Baptism, whether children also believe, and are justly baptized.” The “sects” are the Anabaptists, whose position the Schleitheim Confession had crystallized two years earlier (1527). This detail page and the Schleitheim page are thus the two sides of a single mid-Reformation exchange: Schleitheim makes conscious faith a prerequisite and so “excludes all infant baptism”; Luther denies that faith is the prerequisite at all and grounds the sacrament in the Word.
  • Where Luther stands in the longer story. On the timeline Luther is a paedobaptist witness who inherits and defends the practice the patristic writers above had handed down, but his rationale is neither the Western original-sin argument of Cyprian and Augustine nor the Eastern “abundance of grace” emphasis of Chrysostom. It is a sacramental-theological argument about what makes a baptism — the Word, not the faith of the one washed. Luther does not deny that faith belongs with baptism (he elsewhere speaks of an infant’s faith), but he refuses to let the presence or absence of faith decide the sacrament’s validity, and he explicitly counsels “the simple” to “dismiss this question… and refer it to the learned.”
  • “Word not faith” as a distinct rationale. Luther’s claim that baptism’s validity rests on the Word and command of God rather than the recipient’s faith is a discrete argument for the practice — one that answers the credobaptist objection on its own ground rather than by appeal to antiquity or original sin. It is weighed on its own argument page (assessed contested): the validity-vs-fruitful-reception distinction it turns on is genuine and ancient, and it rightly exposes the credobaptist objection as assuming the point in dispute, but it does not settle the question, because it rests on a sacramental realism the believers'-baptism tradition rejects.
  • The Bernard/Gerson/Hus argument overreaches. Luther’s first answer — that God has sanctified men baptized only in infancy, so the practice must please Him — is weighed separately (argument page, assessed weak), and his own statement of it claims more than it proves. That God sanctified Bernard, Gerson, and Hus shows He works in people who were baptized in infancy and later came to living faith — not that the infant baptism was the efficacious or pleasing act, which is the conclusion he presses (“they must acknowledge that such infant baptism is pleasing to God”). And his supporting move — that otherwise “no man upon earth could have been a Christian” and the Church “cannot perish” — is a “the Church could not have erred so long” form that proves too much (the same over-reach flagged on the antiquity-and-universality page). The move’s real force is defensive — rebutting the charge that God rejects the practice — not a positive demonstration that infancy is the fit time.
