Basil of Caesarea on infant baptism

Dates: c. 375 (Basil “the Great,” c. 330–379; bishop of Caesarea in Cappadocia and eldest of the three Cappadocian Fathers. His On the Holy Spirit dates to 375; his Exhortation to Holy Baptism [Homily 13] belongs to his episcopate, c. 370–378) · Wikipedia
Summary: Basil is a double-edged fourth-century witness. Born into a devout Christian family — grandson of a confessor, brother of Gregory of Nyssa — he was himself baptized only as an adult (c. 357, on his return from Athens), an instance of the very fourth-century deferral of baptism that the historical arguments weigh. His baptismal theology fixes the order the credobaptist presses: “first comes the confession… and baptism follows.” And his Exhortation to Holy Baptism is a sustained attack on adult procrastination — aimed at grown catechumens, many raised in the faith, who kept putting the font off. He nowhere argues against the baptism of infants; the witness is to the settled fourth-century adult road to baptism, not a verdict on whose children were brought.

Claims & evidence

“First comes the confession… and baptism follows” — the order of faith and the font (On the Holy Spirit 12)

Defending the baptismal invocation of the three Persons, Basil states the relation of faith to baptism as a fixed sequence in which the candidate’s confession comes first and the water sets its seal on his assent:

Faith and baptism are two kindred and inseparable ways of salvation: faith is perfected through baptism, baptism is established through faith, and both are completed by the same names. For as we believe in the Father and the Son and the Holy Ghost, so are we also baptized in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Ghost; first comes the confession, introducing us to salvation, and baptism follows, setting the seal upon our assent.

— Basil of Caesarea, On the Holy Spirit 12.28.1 The statement gives the same “confession, then font” order the faith-precedes-baptism argument turns on — from a father who belonged to a church that (as his fellow Cappadocian Nazianzus attests) still baptized infants in danger of death. It describes the road of the adult convert; it does not adjudicate the covenant standing of a believer’s child.

His own baptism as an adult: “to me my baptism was the beginning of life” (On the Holy Spirit 10)

Basil speaks of his own baptism — received as a grown man, after his schooling at Athens — as the conscious turning-point of his life, the “day of regeneration” on which he professed the faith for himself:

For if to me my baptism was the beginning of life, and that day of regeneration the first of days, it is plain that the utterance uttered in the grace of adoption was the most honourable of all… the tradition which guided me to the light, which bestowed on me the boon of the knowledge of God, whereby I, so long a foe by reason of sin, was made a child of God.

— Basil of Caesarea, On the Holy Spirit 10.26.2 He was baptized about 357 by Dianius, bishop of Caesarea, who also ordained him reader — the deferral of baptism to adulthood being, in his generation, common even in fully Christian households.3

“When will you become a Christian?” — against adult procrastination (Exhortation to Holy Baptism)

Basil’s baptismal sermon is not a defence of infant baptism but a rebuke to grown catechumens who keep deferring the font — some of them, he says, taught the faith since infancy yet still unbaptized in old age. He insists the whole of life is the time for baptism and that delay is a deadly gamble:

Although instructed in the divine word from your infancy, have you still not yet yielded to truth? always learning, have you not yet attained to knowledge? through life an inquirer, a seeker even to old age, when will you become a Christian? when shall we recognize you as our own? Last year you awaited the present time, and now again you put off to a future season.

— Basil of Caesarea, Exhortation to Holy Baptism (Homily 13).4 “The whole period of man’s life is the time for baptism,” he urges, warning against those who “await that baptism should be for you as a gift brought by a fever” — deferring it to the deathbed, when a man can no longer “confess accurately, or… enter into covenant with God, or… renounce the enemy.” His model is the Ethiopian eunuch, who, “when he had learned the gospel of the kingdom… embraced the faith with his heart, and did not delay to receive the seal of the Spirit.”5

Notes

  • Against the adult’s delay, not the child’s. The whole thrust of the Exhortation is that baptism should come sooner, not later — the delay Basil attacks is the grown catechumen’s, a habit so entrenched that even those “instructed… from infancy” put it off for decades. This is precisely the fourth-century vogue for deferred baptism that Nazianzus’s Oration 40 also combats. Read rightly, then, Basil neither commends nor condemns infant baptism; he presupposes the adult catechumenate and pleads with its members not to gamble their salvation on procrastination.
  • A counterweight, honestly weighed. That Basil, Nyssa, Nazianzus, Ambrose, Jerome, and Augustine — all children of Christian parents — were baptized as adults is real evidence against reading infant baptism as the universal fourth-century norm, and it is weighed on the dating-of-the-earliest-mention and antiquity-and-universality pages. But the datum cuts two ways: Jeremias read the deferral as a temporary decline from an earlier infant-baptism custom, Aland as showing the custom had not yet become normal — and the same fathers endorsed emergency infant baptism and, later, its theology. The deferral shows adult baptism remained ordinary; it does not show that infant baptism was rejected.
