John Spilsbury on infant baptism

Dates: c. 1638 (led the gathering of what is generally reckoned the first Particular — Calvinistic — Baptist church in London, and the body in which believers’ baptism by immersion was recovered). John Spilsbury (also spelled Spilsbery), c. 1593 – c. 1668, a London cobbler and Separatist who seceded from the Jacob–Lathrop–Jessey independent congregation over infant baptism; author of A Treatise Concerning the Lawfull Subject of Baptisme (1643) and a subscriber of the First London Baptist Confession (1644) · Wikipedia
Summary: Spilsbury represents the Particular (Calvinistic) strand of the credobaptist “No” — a different node from Smyth’s General Baptists — and the body in which believers’ baptism by immersion was recovered. He argued against infant baptism from the spirituality of the new covenant (only those who believe as Abraham did are Abraham’s seed, so the covenant’s sign is not for the infant), and set his name to the First London Baptist Confession of 1644, whose baptism articles make the credobaptist position confessional: baptism is “to be dispensed only upon persons professing faith… or taught,” its manner “dipping or plunging the whole body under water.”

Claims & evidence

Baptism belongs only to professing believers — the First London Confession, Article 39

By 1644 seven Particular Baptist congregations in London — three of the fifteen subscribers signing for Spilsbury’s own church — put their common position into the first Calvinistic Baptist confession. Its article on the subject of baptism restricts the rite to those who can profess faith for themselves:

That Baptism is an Ordinance of the new Testament, given by Christ, to be dispensed only upon persons professing faith, or that are Disciples, or taught, who upon a profession of faith, ought to be baptized.

— The Confession of Faith of those Churches which are commonly (though falsly) called Anabaptists (London, 1644), Article XXXIX.1 The first edition read “dispensed onely upon persons professing faith” — the emphatic exclusion the later 1646 revision quietly dropped, removing the word “onely.” The wording deliberately echoes the credobaptist reading of the Great Commission (“Disciples, or taught, who upon a profession of faith”) that Smyth had pressed into a syllogism, and by making a professed faith constitutive of a valid baptism it excludes the infant, who can profess none.

The manner is “dipping or plunging the whole body under water” — Article 40 and the recovery of immersion

Spilsbury’s people had at first baptized by affusion; it was among them, about 1638, that believers’ baptism by immersion was recovered, and the 1644 Confession made that mode confessional — the first English-language creed to do so. Its next article grounds immersion in the death-burial-resurrection imagery of Romans 6:

The way and manner of the dispensing of this Ordinance the Scripture holds out to be dipping or plunging the whole body under water: it being a sign, must answer the thing signified, which are these: first, the washing the whole soul in the blood of Christ: Secondly, that interest the Saints have in the death, burial, and resurrection; thirdly, together with a confirmation of our faith, that as certainly as the body is buried under water, and riseth again, so certainly shall the bodies of the Saints be raised by the power of Christ in the day of the resurrection, to reign with Christ.

— Confession of Faith (1644), Article XL.2 This is the confessional crystallization of the credobaptist immersion-mode argument — the sign “must answer the thing signified.” Weighed on its own page that argument comes out weak as a bar to infant baptism: it concerns how the water is applied, not to whom, and the two axes are independent (the Eastern Orthodox baptize infants by full immersion). The 1644 wording “the Scripture holds out to be” was itself softened out of the 1646 edition — a sign the compilers came to see the mode as less than absolutely determinative.

Why the infant is not a covenant subject — the 1643 treatise

Where the paedobaptist case for infant baptism rests on the covenant made with Abraham and his seed (the covenant-circumcision argument that Zwingli opened and Calvin systematized), Spilsbury’s A Treatise Concerning the Lawfull Subject of Baptisme (1643) answered it on its own ground. He held the covenant theology of the Reformed — one covenant of grace — but argued that under the new covenant its membership is spiritual, not fleshly: none are counted children of Abraham but as they believe as Abraham believed, so the promise runs to the believing, not to the infant by physical descent. It is the covenant, and not the outward sign, that constitutes the church, and the sign belongs only to one already in the covenant by faith. So the very text the paedobaptist reads as carrying the covenant sign to the believer’s children Spilsbury reads as carrying it only to those who share Abraham’s faith — the credobaptist answer to the covenant argument, off which the whole Particular-Baptist case is built.3

Notes

  • A different node from Smyth. John Smyth’s self-baptism at Amsterdam (1609) began the General Baptists — Arminian in theology, baptizing by affusion. Spilsbury’s church is the fountainhead of the Particular (Calvinistic) Baptists, and of the recovery of immersion: two distinct strands of the English credobaptist tradition, meeting in the common rejection of infant baptism but dividing on the extent of the atonement and (for a time) the mode. The immersion recovery is usually dated to about 1638, and the 1644 Confession is its formal statement.
  • “Murky origins.” The exact chronology of Spilsbury’s church is genuinely disputed among historians — the traditional 1633 founding date, the 1638 adoption of believers’ baptism by immersion, and the 1641 date some prefer for its becoming a “true” Baptist church all have their advocates, and the surviving records (chiefly the so-called Kiffin Manuscript) are thin and ambiguous. Following B. R. White, Stephen Wright argues that Spilsbury had adopted an early separatist baptism by affusion in the 1630s and that he and his people did not take up immersion until 1638. What is not in doubt is that by 1644 he was pastoring a settled Particular Baptist congregation and lending his name — and perhaps a hand in the drafting — to its confession.4
  • Where the confession overreaches on mode. Article 40’s “it being a sign, must answer the thing signified” presses the immersion case past what it will bear as an argument against infant baptism: the earliest church order already permits pouring where immersion-water is scarce (Didache 7), so the apostolic-age church never made mode constitutive of a valid baptism, and the mode charge in any case targets the wrong axis — how, not whom. The point is weighed on the immersion-mode argument page (assessment: weak against infant baptism, sound only as a case for immersion as the fuller sign). The confession’s own retreat from “the Scripture holds out to be” in 1646 registers the same qualification.
