Thomas Helwys on infant baptism

Dates: c. 1611–1612 (A Declaration of Faith of English People Remaining at Amsterdam in Holland, 1611, generally reckoned the earliest Baptist confession; and A Short Declaration of the Mistery of Iniquity, printed on his return to England, 1612). Thomas Helwys, c. 1575–c. 1616, a Nottinghamshire gentleman and Gray’s-Inn-trained lawyer who organized and funded the exile of John Smyth’s Separatist congregation to Amsterdam; when Smyth repudiated his own self-baptism and sought union with the Waterlander Mennonites, Helwys broke with him, led a remnant of about a dozen back to England, and c. 1611–1612 gathered at Spitalfields (London) what is generally reckoned the first Baptist church on English soil — a General (Arminian) Baptist congregation of believers’ baptism only. Imprisoned in Newgate for his plea for liberty of conscience, he died there about 1616 · Global Anabaptist Mennonite Encyclopedia Online (GAMEO)
Summary: Helwys plants Smyth’s believers’-baptism principle in English soil and fixes it in a confession. His 1611 Declaration of Faith — the first Baptist confession — makes the exclusion of infants an article: baptism is “the outward manifestation of dieing unto sin, and walking in newness of life… And therefore in no wise appertains to infants.” He grounds it on the same two proof-texts the credobaptist case turns on — the Great Commission (Matthew 28:19) and the Pentecost pattern of Acts 2:41, where confession of faith precedes the font — and on the Romans 6 symbolism of death and resurrection that a newborn cannot enact. His Mistery of Iniquity (1612) then couples believers’ baptism to the first English plea for total liberty of conscience.

Claims & evidence

Baptism “in no wise appertains to infants” (1611 Declaration, Art. 14)

The 1611 Declaration of Faith of English People Remaining at Amsterdam makes believers’ baptism a confessional article. Article 13 fixes the subject of baptism as the professing convert, on the twin authority of the Great Commission and apostolic practice; Article 14 then draws the exclusion of infants directly from the Romans 6 meaning of the rite:

13. That every Church is to receive in all their members by Baptism upon the Confession of their faith and sins wrought by the preaching of the Gospel, according to the primitive Institution, (Matthew 28:19) and practice, (Acts 2:41).

14. That Baptism or washing with water is the outward manifestation of dieing unto sin, and walking in newness of life. (Romans 6:2, 3, 4). And therefore in no wise appertains to infants.

— Thomas Helwys, A Declaration of Faith of English People Remaining at Amsterdam (1611).1 The argument compresses the whole credobaptist case into two articles. Article 13 defines a church’s members as those received “upon the Confession of their faith and sins” — the profession no infant can make — and anchors that on Matthew 28:19 (the Great-Commission order that Smyth had pressed into a syllogism two years earlier) and Acts 2:41, where the three thousand are baptized after they “gladly received his word” (the faith-precedes-baptism pattern). Article 14 adds a distinct ground: baptism signifies dying and rising with Christ (Romans 6), so it belongs only to one who can enact that death to sin and new life — “And therefore in no wise appertains to infants.” It is the first time the believers’-baptism exclusion is written into a standing English confession rather than argued in a treatise.

Notes

  • The first Baptist church on English soil. When Smyth, doubting the propriety of his own self-baptism, concluded he should have sought baptism from the Waterlander Mennonites and applied to join them, Helwys dissented — charging Smyth with apostasy for unchurching the congregation and for over-valuing an unbroken “succession,” and warning the Mennonites not to receive him. He kept the remnant (about a dozen members) on the principle that a true church may constitute itself on believers’ baptism without any prior line of administrators, led them back to England late in 1611 or early 1612, and gathered at White’s Alley, Spitalfields, the congregation generally reckoned the first Baptist church on English ground. It was General (Arminian) Baptist in theology — distinct from the Calvinistic Particular Baptist line that John Spilsbury would begin a generation later.2
  • Believers’ baptism and liberty of conscience. Almost as soon as he reached England, Helwys printed A Short Declaration of the Mistery of Iniquity (1612) — the first book published in English to plead for total liberty of conscience, extended (remarkably for its date) to “heretic, Turk, or Jew.” In a hand-written line to King James in the presentation copy he pressed the same logic that made baptism answer to personal faith into a limit on the crown’s reach over the soul: “The king is a mortall man and not God, therefore hath no power over the immortall soules of his subjects, to make lawes and ordinances for them, and to set Spiritual Lords over them.” The book cost him his freedom; he was imprisoned in Newgate, where he died about 1616.3
  • Where he stands on the timeline. Helwys is the hinge between Smyth’s exiled self-baptism (1609) and the settled English Baptist movement. Smyth had reasoned the exclusion of infants from the Great Commission; Helwys does not add a new syllogism so much as institutionalize the position — making it a church on English soil and an article in a confession, resting it on the shared proof-texts (Matthew 28:19, Acts 2:41) and on the Romans 6 death-and-resurrection symbolism later crystallized in the immersion-mode case. The same Romans 6 sign that Helwys reads as barring infants, Spilsbury’s Particular Baptists would read as also requiring immersion; Helwys’s General Baptists, like Smyth, still baptized by affusion, which shows the two arguments are separable — the whom of baptism and the how are distinct questions (the immersion-mode argument is weighed weak as a bar to infant baptism for just this reason).
