Cyprian of Carthage on infant baptism

Dates: c. 253 (Cyprian, c. 200/210–258; bishop of Carthage from c. 248/9 and the leading churchman of Latin Africa until his martyrdom under Valerian. The letter below — his reply to bishop Fidus, written in the name of a council of sixty-six African bishops — is dated c. 253, a few years after Origen and about half a century after his own predecessor Tertullian had objected to the practice in the same province) · Wikipedia
Summary: Cyprian gives the earliest conciliar attestation of infant baptism. A bishop named Fidus had proposed that a newborn should wait until the eighth day to be baptized, on the analogy of Old-Testament circumcision. Cyprian and sixty-five fellow bishops in council rejected the delay unanimously: not whether to baptize infants but only when was ever in question, and their answer was that grace must be denied to no one — “how much rather ought we to shrink from hindering an infant.” The practice is treated throughout as settled and unremarkable; the whole debate presupposes it.

Claims & evidence

A council of sixty-six bishops: baptize the newborn, don't wait for the eighth day (Epistle 58, to Fidus)

Fidus had written to raise two scruples: that a newborn is not to be baptized within the second or third day of birth, and that the “law of ancient circumcision” should govern the timing, so that baptism should wait until the eighth day. Cyprian answers for the whole council, and the very framing shows what was not disputed — the fitness of infants for baptism — and what was: only the day:

But in respect of the case of the infants, which you say ought not to be baptized within the second or third day after their birth, and that the law of ancient circumcision should be regarded, so that you think that one who is just born should not be baptized and sanctified within the eighth day, we all thought very differently in our council. For in this course which you thought was to be taken, no one agreed; but we all rather judge that the mercy and grace of God is not to be refused to any one born of man.

— Cyprian, Epistle 58.2 (Oxford ed. lxiv), to Fidus.1 The letter opens by naming the body: “Cyprian, and others his colleagues who were present in council, in number sixty-six, to Fidus their brother, greeting.”2 This is what makes the witness distinctive on the timeline: earlier testimony (Tertullian, the Apostolic Tradition, Origen) is that of individual writers, but here a synod of the African church rules on infant baptism as a body — and rules unanimously (“no one agreed” with Fidus's delay).

The circumcision analogy answered: the eighth day was a shadow fulfilled in Christ

Fidus's argument rested on a type: the eighth-day command of circumcision (Genesis 17:12; Leviticus 12:3) should, he thought, set the pattern for baptism. Cyprian replies that the eighth-day figure was a shadow already fulfilled — Christ rose “the first day after the Sabbath” and gave the “circumcision of the spirit,” so the carnal timetable no longer binds the spiritual sign:

For in respect of the observance of the eighth day in the Jewish circumcision of the flesh, a sacrament was given beforehand in shadow and in usage; but when Christ came, it was fulfilled in truth. For because the eighth day, that is, the first day after the Sabbath, was to be that on which the Lord should rise again, and should quicken us, and give us circumcision of the spirit, the eighth day… went before in the figure; which figure ceased when by and by the truth came, and spiritual circumcision was given to us.

— Cyprian, Epistle 58.4.3 The move is notable: the circumcision–baptism parallel, which later paedobaptists would deploy to defend infant baptism, is used by Fidus to restrict its timing and answered by Cyprian as a type that has expired. Cyprian's council thus keeps the substance (spiritual circumcision is for all) while dropping the calendar (the eighth day was “a shadow… fulfilled in truth”).4

“How much rather ought we to shrink from hindering an infant” — and an early appeal to inherited defilement

The theological heart of the reply is that if forgiveness is not withheld even from grievous adult sinners, it must all the more be given to the newborn, who has no sins of his own but has “contracted the contagion of the ancient death” from Adam — one of the earliest Latin statements linking infant baptism to an inherited stain of birth:

…how much rather ought we to shrink from hindering an infant, who, being lately born, has not sinned, except in that, being born after the flesh according to Adam, he has contracted the contagion of the ancient death at its earliest birth, who approaches the more easily on this very account to the reception of the forgiveness of sins — that to him are remitted, not his own sins, but the sins of another.

— Cyprian, Epistle 58.5.5 Cyprian concludes that “no one ought to be hindered from baptism and from the grace of God” — a rule to be observed for all, but “even more” for infants and the newly born.6 The rationale (remission of “the sins of another,” the “contagion” from Adam) sits alongside Origen's “stains of birth” as a pre-Augustinian anticipation of the Western doctrine of original sin, here made the ground of baptizing the newborn without delay.

Notes

  • The letter's number varies by edition. It is Epistle 58 in the Ante-Nicene Fathers arrangement and Epistle 64 (lxiv) in the older Oxford edition; the popular catalogs cite it as “Letter 64, To Fidus.” The recipient and subject are constant across all of them.7
  • What is (and isn't) in dispute. The council does not defend infant baptism against anyone who denied it; Fidus himself assumed infants are baptized and asked only about the day. That the sole live question in the African church of c. 253 was when to baptize a newborn — not whether — is itself strong evidence of how settled the practice had become, a generation after Origen and roughly fifty years after Tertullian had argued (unsuccessfully, it turns out) for delay.
  • The circumcision parallel, two ways. Here the Old-Testament circumcision analogy is raised to limit baptism (wait for the eighth day) and answered as a fulfilled type. The same proof-texts (Genesis 17; Colossians 2:11–12) are marshaled by later paedobaptists in the opposite direction — as positive warrant for baptizing infants as circumcision's covenant successor. Cyprian's use is thus a useful control: the parallel was in play by the mid-third century, but as a question of timing, not yet as a covenant-membership argument for the practice.
