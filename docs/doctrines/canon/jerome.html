Jerome on the New Testament Canon

Dates: c. 394 (De viris illustribus 392–393; the Vulgate Gospels, to Damasus, 383; Letter 53 to Paulinus c. 394; Letter 129 to Dardanus c. 414; d. 420) · Wikipedia
Summary: Jerome — the age's foremost biblical scholar and the translator whose Vulgate carried the Latin New Testament for a thousand years — is the pivotal Western witness who does two things at once. He transmits the twenty-seven-book New Testament: revising the four Gospels for Pope Damasus and setting them in the order Matthew, Mark, Luke, John, and running out the whole New Testament book by book in his letters. And he preserves the memory of which books were disputed: the Western doubt that Hebrews is Paul's (Tertullian ascribing it to Barnabas), the doubts over 2 Peter, James, and the two shorter epistles of John (assigned to "John the presbyter"), and the Eastern hesitancy over the Apocalypse. Yet in the end he receives them all. He is the scholar who knows every dispute and still ratifies the full canon — and who nowhere cites a Roman or Nicene conciliar canon to do it.

Claims & evidence

The four Gospels, revised and set in order — Matthew, Mark, Luke, John (Vulgate preface to Damasus, 383)

I therefore promise in this short Preface the four Gospels only, which are to be taken in the following order, Matthew, Mark, Luke, John, as they have been revised by a comparison of the Greek manuscripts. Only early ones have been used.

— Prefaces to the Vulgate Version of the New Testament, "The Four Gospels," addressed to Pope Damasus, a.d. 383, trans. W. H. Fremantle (NPNF 2.6), historicalchristian.faith.1 Commissioned by Damasus — "the command is given by you who are the supreme bishop" — Jerome's aim was to correct the corrupted Old Latin against the Greek, "to revise the old Latin, and not to make a new version." Two things about the fourfold Gospel he takes as settled: the number is four, and he affirms the Hebrew original of Matthew, "the first to commit to writing the Gospel of Christ." The Gospel core is not in dispute; his labour is textual, going "back to the fountainhead."

Hebrews doubted in the West — many deny it is Paul's (De viris illustribus 5)

The epistle which is called the Epistle to the Hebrews is not considered his, on account of its difference from the others in style and language, but it is reckoned, either according to Tertullian to be the work of Barnabas, or according to others, to be by Luke the Evangelist or Clement afterwards bishop of the church at Rome … Some read one also to the Laodiceans but it is rejected by everyone.

— Lives of Illustrious Men (De viris illustribus) 5, on Paul, trans. E. C. Richardson (NPNF 2.3), historicalchristian.faith (also at New Advent).2 Here Jerome records the standing Western doubt over Hebrews: after listing Paul's letters "to the Corinthians two, to the Galatians one" and the rest, he sets Hebrews apart, noting that many deny it is Paul's on stylistic grounds and ascribe it instead to Barnabas (Tertullian), Luke, or Clement of Rome. This is the same doubt the African council of Carthage would still register a few years later, setting "one epistle … to the Hebrews" a half-step apart from Paul's "thirteen." Jerome preserves the disagreement without resolving it against the book — and later, to Dardanus, he closes the question in Hebrews' favour (below).

2 Peter and James disputed — the antilegomena among the Catholic Epistles (De viris illustribus 1, 2)

He wrote two epistles which are called Catholic, the second of which, on account of its difference from the first in style, is considered by many not to be by him.

— Lives of Illustrious Men 1, on Simon Peter, historicalchristian.faith (New Advent). And of James:

James … wrote a single epistle, which is reckoned among the seven Catholic Epistles and even this is claimed by some to have been published by some one else under his name, and gradually, as time went on, to have gained authority.

— Lives of Illustrious Men 2, on James the brother of the Lord, historicalchristian.faith.3 Jerome catalogues the disputes book by book: 2 Peter doubted for its unlikeness in style to 1 Peter, James suspected of being pseudonymous and "gradually … to have gained authority." These are exactly the books Eusebius had ranked among the antilegomena — disputed yet received by many — and Jerome, the Latin heir to that tradition, records the same soft edge while keeping each book in his working New Testament.

The two shorter Johannine epistles and "John the presbyter" (De viris illustribus 9)

He wrote also one Epistle which begins as follows "That which was from the beginning, that which we have heard, that which we have seen with our eyes and our hands handled concerning the word of life" which is esteemed of by all men who are interested in the church or in learning. The other two … are said to be the work of John the presbyter to the memory of whom another sepulchre is shown at Ephesus to the present day.

— Lives of Illustrious Men 9, on the Apostle John, historicalchristian.faith (New Advent).4 The first epistle of John is received "by all"; the two shorter ones (2 and 3 John) some assign to a separate figure, "John the presbyter," to whom a second tomb was shown at Ephesus — the authorship question that runs back through Papias and Eusebius. Jerome reports the doubt and, in the same chapter, credits the Apostle with the Apocalypse he "wrote … on which Justin Martyr and Irenaeus afterwards wrote commentaries." Once more he transmits the disputed books while noting exactly where the dispute lies.

The whole New Testament surveyed — Gospels, Paul "to seven churches," the Catholic Epistles, the Apocalypse (Letter 53 to Paulinus, c. 394)

The New Testament I will briefly deal with. Matthew, Mark, Luke and John are the Lord's team of four, the true cherubim or store of knowledge. … The apostle Paul writes to seven churches (for the eighth epistle—that to the Hebrews—is not generally counted in with the others). He instructs Timothy and Titus; he intercedes with Philemon for his runaway slave. … The apostles James, Peter, John, and Jude, have published seven epistles at once spiritual and to the point … The apocalypse of John has as many mysteries as words.

— Letter 53, to Paulinus of Nola, §9, trans. W. H. Fremantle (NPNF 2.6), historicalchristian.faith (also at New Advent).5 Surveying the whole Bible book by book for Paulinus, Jerome runs the New Testament complete: the four Gospels as the fourfold cherubim (the Irenaean-Ezekielian image), the thirteen letters of Paul "to seven churches," Acts, the seven Catholic Epistles of James, Peter, John, and Jude, and the Apocalypse "of John." He counts every book in — while still flagging the one lingering Western reservation, that Hebrews "is not generally counted in with the others."

Yet he receives them all — Hebrews and the Apocalypse (Letter 129 to Dardanus, c. 414)

It must be said by us, however, that this letter which is entitled to the Hebrews, was not only received by the churches of the Orient but by all the earlier writers of ecclesiastical Greek, as being the one received by Paul the Apostle, even though most people believe that it belongs either to Barnabas or to Clement … But even if the custom of the Latins does not include it among the canonical Scriptures, neither indeed do the Greek churches receive the Apocalypse of John with the same freedom; yet we receive them both, following not the custom of this time, but the authority of the ancient writers.

— Letter 129, to Dardanus, §3 (also at New Advent), historicalchristian.faith.6 This is Jerome's own resolution of the two great disputes he had catalogued. The Latins doubt Hebrews; the Greeks doubt Revelation; each side receives the book the other questions. Jerome's verdict is to take both — "yet we receive them both" — on the authority of "the ancient writers who frequently use both testimonies … as if they were canonical and ecclesiastical." Here the scholar who knows every antilegomenon ratifies the full twenty-seven-book canon, resting his judgment on ancient reception, not on any council's decree.

Notes

  • Records the disputes, but cites no conciliar canon. Jerome settles the New Testament's doubtful books by weighing reception and antiquity — never by appeal to a Roman or Nicene list. He was Damasus's secretary at Rome in 382 and the age's foremost authority on Scripture, yet nowhere mentions a canon promulgated by that Roman synod. This silence is load-bearing in the timeline's Council of Rome (382) argument (assessment: weak): the very writer the so-called "Damasine list" depends on knew of no such decree.
  • His Old-Testament side lives elsewhere. This page treats Jerome's New-Testament witness. His far more famous canon-work — the "Hebrew verity," his ranking of Judith, Tobit, and the Maccabees below the canon "among the Apocryphal writings," and the passage read into the Nicene-canon dispute — sits on the Old Testament canon timeline. See in particular the argument Did Jerome's Preface to Judith show the Council of Nicaea counted Judith among the Scriptures? (assessment: weak): the same hand that here receives the disputed New Testament books there rules the Old-Testament deuterocanon out of the canon, which he could not do had he thought a council had decreed it in.
  • The Latin heir to Eusebius's sorting. Every dispute Jerome records — Hebrews, 2 Peter, James, 2–3 John, the Apocalypse — is one Eusebius had already sorted among the antilegomena a century earlier (see Eusebius of Caesarea). Jerome carries that Greek scholarship into the Latin West and, like Athanasius before him and Innocent I just after, ends by receiving the full twenty-seven — the reception the timeline shows hardening on both sides of the Mediterranean at the turn of the fifth century.
  • Transmission, not creation. Jerome's Vulgate did more than any list to fix the Western New Testament, simply by giving it one authoritative text in a settled order. Yet he frames the work as correction of a received collection, not selection of one — "to revise the old Latin, and not to make a new version" — the same pattern of gradual reception the timeline traces throughout.
