Tertullian on the Biblical Canon

Dates: c. 207 (Against Marcion IV and The Prescription Against Heretics, Carthage; d. c. 220) · Wikipedia
Summary: Writing in Carthage between Irenaeus and Origen, Tertullian fills the gap with the same picture Irenaeus had drawn: a fixed fourfold Gospel, authored by apostles (Matthew, John) and apostolic men (Mark “the interpreter of Peter,” Luke the companion of Paul), which he defends against Marcion’s single mutilated Luke; and a criterion of authenticity — the “authentic writings” of the apostles read aloud in the apostolic churches. His edges, though, are still soft in both directions: he hardens the boundary by throwing the Shepherd of Hermas out among the “apocryphal and false” writings that Irenaeus had cited as near-Scripture, yet he still defends the book of Enoch as Scripture and ascribes Hebrews to Barnabas — both faces of a canon taking shape but not yet closed.

Claims & evidence

The fourfold Gospel — apostles and apostolic men, against Marcion’s single mutilated Luke

We lay it down as our first position, that the evangelical Testament has apostles for its authors, to whom was assigned by the Lord Himself this office of publishing the gospel. Since, however, there are apostolic men also, they are yet not alone, but appear with apostles and after apostles … Of the apostles, therefore, John and Matthew first instil faith into us; whilst of apostolic men, Luke and Mark renew it afterwards … Now, of the authors whom we possess, Marcion seems to have singled out Luke for his mutilating process.

— Against Marcion IV.2, historicalchristian.faith (also at New Advent).1 Tertullian answers Marcion — who used one edited Gospel of Luke and “ascribes no author to his Gospel” — with the same authorship scheme as Irenaeus: the Gospels come from apostles (Matthew, John) and from “apostolic men” who wrote with apostolic authority (Luke, Mark). A few chapters on he names the link explicitly, and pins the count at four:

The same authority of the apostolic churches will afford evidence to the other Gospels also, which we possess equally through their means, and according to their usage — I mean the Gospels of John and Matthew — whilst that which Mark published may be affirmed to be Peter’s whose interpreter Mark was. For even Luke’s form of the Gospel men usually ascribe to Paul.

— Against Marcion IV.5, historicalchristian.faith (also at New Advent).1 Mark is “the interpreter” of Peter and Luke the follower of Paul — so all four rest on apostolic authority, and Marcion’s solitary, unattributed, cut-down Gospel is convicted by its lateness against the older, fuller four. This is the Irenaean Tetramorph (weighed on the wiki as an argument below) attested a generation on, in the West.

The authentic apostolic writings, read in the apostolic churches, as the criterion of authenticity

Come now, you who would indulge a better curiosity, if you would apply it to the business of your salvation, run over the apostolic churches, in which the very thrones of the apostles are still pre-eminent in their places, in which their own authentic writings are read, uttering the voice and representing the face of each of them severally. Achaia is very near you, (in which) you find Corinth … Since you are able to cross to Asia, you get Ephesus. Since, moreover, you are close upon Italy, you have Rome, from which there comes even into our own hands the very authority (of apostles themselves).

— The Prescription Against Heretics 36, historicalchristian.faith (also at New Advent).2 Against heretics who edit or invent scriptures, Tertullian points not to a list but to a test of provenance: go to the churches the apostles founded — Corinth, Philippi, Thessalonica, Ephesus, Rome — where their “authentic writings” (authenticae litterae) are still read publicly. The genuine texts are the ones handed down and read in the churches of apostolic foundation; a writing that surfaces only among the heretics, or in a mutilated form, fails the test. It is reception-and-provenance as the mark of Scripture, the criterion the whole timeline runs on.

Rejects the Shepherd of Hermas as apocryphal — hardening what Irenaeus had cited as Scripture

But I would yield my ground to you, if the scripture of “the Shepherd,” which is the only one which favours adulterers, had deserved to find a place in the Divine canon; if it had not been habitually judged by every council of Churches (even of your own) among apocryphal and false (writings); itself adulterous, and hence a patroness of its comrades …

— On Modesty (De pudicitia) 10, historicalchristian.faith (also at New Advent).3 Where Irenaeus a generation earlier had quoted the Shepherd of Hermas as “Scripture,” Tertullian — now the rigorist — casts it out: it never earned “a place in the Divine canon” and has been “judged by every council of Churches … among apocryphal and false” writings. He returns to the book ten chapters later, still branding it “that apocryphal ‘Shepherd’ of adulterers.” The antilegomena boundary is being drawn tighter: a middle-ring text once read as near-Scripture is pushed outside the line.

Ascribes the Epistle to the Hebrews to Barnabas

For there is extant withal an Epistle to the Hebrews under the name of Barnabas — a man sufficiently accredited by God, as being one whom Paul has stationed next to himself in the uninterrupted observance of abstinence … And, of course, the Epistle of Barnabas is more generally received among the Churches than that apocryphal “Shepherd” of adulterers.

— On Modesty (De pudicitia) 20, historicalchristian.faith (also at New Advent).3 Tertullian is the earliest witness to a named theory of Hebrews’ authorship: he cites it as Scripture (quoting Heb. 6:4–8 for his rigorist case against a second repentance) but assigns it not to Paul but to Barnabas — “an Epistle to the Hebrews under the name of Barnabas.” The book is inside the boundary; whose it is remains unsettled — the same lingering Western doubt over Hebrews’ authorship that the later councils would carry, and that Athanasius’s “fourteen Epistles of Paul” folds away.

Defends the book of Enoch as Scripture — a soft edge on the far side

I am aware that the Scripture of Enoch, which has assigned this order (of action) to angels, is not received by some, because it is not admitted into the Jewish canon either … But since Enoch in the same Scripture has preached likewise concerning the Lord, nothing at all must be rejected by us which pertains to us; and we read that “every Scripture suitable for edification is divinely inspired.” … To these considerations is added the fact that Enoch possesses a testimony in the Apostle Jude.

— On the Apparel of Women (De cultu feminarum) I.3, historicalchristian.faith (also at New Advent).4 Knowing full well that many rejected 1 Enoch — not least because “it is not admitted into the Jewish canon” — Tertullian nonetheless defends it as inspired Scripture, arguing that it foretells Christ and that the Epistle of Jude quotes it (Jude 14–15). So the same author who tightens the boundary against Hermas keeps Enoch and an ambiguously-authored Hebrews inside it: the canon of c. 207 has a firmly closed fourfold-Gospel core and still-fuzzy edges, exactly the picture the timeline’s later witnesses harden. Whether that early esteem amounts to 1 Enoch having been received as Scripture is weighed off-timeline under the Nephilim — was 1 Enoch received as Scripture? (assessment: weak), of which Tertullian is the strongest patristic witness “for” — and, on the Old-Testament-canon side, whether Jude’s quoting it as prophecy shows the canon was still open in the apostolic age (assessment: contested).

Notes

  • He fills the gap between Irenaeus (180) and Origen (248). Tertullian carries the Irenaean fourfold Gospel forward in the Latin West — four Gospels from apostles and apostolic men, the same “Mark = interpreter of Peter, Luke = companion of Paul” scheme — a generation before Origen would enumerate the whole New Testament from within. This is the settled core the wiki weighs at Was the fourfold Gospel already closed by c. 180? (assessment: sound).
  • Both faces of one boundary. His canon is not yet closed at the edges: he rejects the Shepherd of Hermas, defends Enoch, and ascribes Hebrews to Barnabas. That mix — a hardening center, still-open edges — is the pattern the wiki records across the whole timeline; see the argument the New Testament settled gradually, contested at its edges (assessment: sound).
  • “Authentic writings.” The Latin authenticae litterae of Prescription 36 has been read variously — as the apostles’ autographs, the Greek originals, or the full unmutilated copies as against the heretics’ garbled ones; the last sense best fits the polemic against Marcion’s edited Luke. See B. M. Metzger, The Canon of the New Testament (1987), on Tertullian’s canon.
