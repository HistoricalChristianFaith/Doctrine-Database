The Council of Laodicea on the Biblical Canon

Dates: c. 363–364 (a regional synod of Phrygia Pacatiana in Asia Minor) · Wikipedia · his Old Testament canon page
Summary: A regional Eastern synod rules that only "the Canonical Books… of the New and Old Testament" may be read in church (canon 59), and — in the much-disputed canon 60 — appends a book-list whose New Testament is the present twenty-seven minus the Apocalypse: four Gospels, Acts, seven Catholic Epistles, and fourteen of Paul, stopping just short of Revelation. It is the sharpest conciliar instance of the Eastern hesitancy over the Apocalypse that Cyril of Jerusalem shows a generation earlier — a whole synod, not one catechist, drawing the New Testament at twenty-six books.

Claims & evidence

Canon 59 — only "the Canonical Books" read in church

Let no private psalms nor any uncanonical books be read in the church, but only the Canonical Books of the New and Old Testament.

— Council of Laodicea, canon 59, trans. H. R. Percival (NPNF 2.14), CCEL.1 The fifty-ninth canon draws the boundary — the same "read in church" test the African councils would frame positively thirty years later — but names no books. The catalogue is supplied by canon 60.

Canon 60 — the New Testament, the Apocalypse omitted

And these are the books of the New Testament: Four Gospels, according to Matthew, Mark, Luke and John; The Acts of the Apostles; Seven Catholic Epistles, to wit, one of James, two of Peter, three of John, one of Jude; Fourteen Epistles of Paul, one to the Romans, two to the Corinthians, one to the Galatians, one to the Ephesians, one to the Philippians, one to the Colossians, two to the Thessalonians, one to the Hebrews, two to Timothy, one to Titus, and one to Philemon.

— Council of Laodicea, canon 60 (New Testament list), trans. H. R. Percival (NPNF 2.14), CCEL.2 The list runs four Gospels, Acts, the seven Catholic Epistles, and "fourteen Epistles of Paul" (Hebrews folded into the Pauline corpus, as Cyril, Origen, and Athanasius also count it) — twenty-six of the twenty-seven books later fixed as the New Testament, closing without the Revelation of John. Where Athanasius four years on would seal his catalogue "and besides, the Revelation of John," Laodicea stops short of it — the one book the Greek churches longest doubted, on the exclusion side by an entire synod's reckoning.

The doubtful genuineness of canon 60 — ⚠

Canon 60 is not securely part of the synod's acts. Percival prints it under the caption "[N.B.—This Canon is of most questionable genuineness]," and the book-list is missing from a range of early canon-collections of Laodicea (the Latin versions of Dionysius Exiguus and John of Antioch); Westcott judged "the external evidence… decidedly against the authenticity" of the enumeration, which many hold was appended later to spell out the "canonical books" canon 59 leaves unnamed.3 The witness therefore stands on the received text of canon 60: even bracketed as a possible later gloss, it is an early list — whether of c. 363 or a somewhat later hand — that closes the New Testament without the Apocalypse, and canon 59's own "only the Canonical Books" boundary is not in doubt.

Notes

  • Eastern hesitancy over the Apocalypse. Laodicea is the conciliar counterpart to Cyril of Jerusalem's individual list (c. 350), which likewise runs four Gospels, Acts, seven Catholic Epistles, and fourteen of Paul, "conspicuously stopping short of the Apocalypse." The Greek churches' long doubt over Revelation — visible also in the shorter New Testament of the Apostolic Canons and later Eastern lists — is here written into a synod's canon; Athanasius (367) would settle it the other way.
  • The same witness, two doctrines. This page treats Laodicea's New-Testament list; the parallel Old Testament canon page treats canon 60's Old Testament — the Hebrew twenty-two, the deuterocanon absent but Baruch and the Epistle of Jeremiah kept under "Jeremiah." Both rest on the one disputed canon 60.
  • Ratified en bloc, centuries later. The Council in Trullo (692), canon 2, confirmed the canons of Laodicea alongside those of Carthage, Athanasius, and the eighty-five Apostolic Canons — lists that differ on both the Old-Testament deuterocanon and the closing of the New — receiving the earlier councils "in block" rather than adjudicating between their rival catalogues.
