Origen of Alexandria on the Biblical Canon

Dates: c. 248 (Commentary on Matthew Book X; the Homilies on Luke somewhat earlier, c. 233–244; d. c. 253) · Wikipedia · back to timeline
Summary: Origen is the earliest witness to name the apostolic-pseudonym and infancy gospels — "the Gospel according to Thomas," "according to Matthias," "the Book of James" (the Protoevangelium) — and he names them precisely to set them outside the four. In the Homilies on Luke he marks the many gospels as mere "attempts," with "only the four" approved by the church and written "by the Holy Spirit"; in the Commentary on Matthew he can adduce a tradition from "the Book of James" for the brethren-of-the-Lord without thereby treating the book as Scripture. His point is one of authority and reception, not necessarily of formal heresy: these are books the church has not approved, not (in these passages) books he stops to anathematize.

Claims & evidence

The four "approved" by the church; the rest — Thomas, Matthias, and the others — mere "attempts"

For Matthew did not "attempt," but wrote by the Holy Spirit, as did Mark and John, and similarly Luke. However, those who wrote the Gospel according to the Egyptians and the Gospel according to the Twelve "attempted" to write. And already Basilides also dared to write his Gospel according to Basilides. "Many, therefore, have taken in hand" — for there is also the Gospel according to Thomas and the Gospel according to Matthias and several others. These are attempts; the church of God has approved only the four.

— Homilies on Luke 1 (on Luke 1:1, "Many have taken in hand"), trans. Lienhard (FOTC 94), historicalchristian.faith.1 Reading Luke's "many have taken in hand to draw up a narrative" as a quiet criticism, Origen sorts the gospels into two classes: the four the church has "approved," written by the evangelists "by the Holy Spirit," and the "many" others — Thomas and Matthias among them — that were merely "attempted." This is a boundary drawn by reception: the named gospels are excluded not by a decree but by the church's having approved only the four.

A tradition from "the Book of James" adduced — but not as Scripture

But some say, basing it on a tradition in the Gospel according to Peter, as it is entitled, or The Book of James, that the brethren of Jesus were sons of Joseph by a former wife, whom he married before Mary. Now those who say so wish to preserve the honour of Mary in virginity to the end …

— Commentary on Matthew X.17 (on Matt 13:55, "Is not this the carpenter's son?"), ANF 9, newadvent.org.2 The "Book of James" is the Protoevangelium of James; the "Gospel according to Peter" is the apocryphal gospel of that name. Origen reports the tradition they carry — that the Lord's "brethren" were Joseph's sons by an earlier marriage — and even finds it congenial to Mary's perpetual virginity, yet he frames it carefully as what "some say, basing it on a tradition" in those books, not as the teaching of Scripture. The infancy/apocryphal text is cited for a tradition without being granted the standing of the canonical Gospels.

Notes

  • Non-authority, not (here) heresy. Origen's register in both passages is reception and approval, not anathema: the four are what the church has "approved" and what was written "by the Holy Spirit," and the rest are "attempts" or carriers of a "tradition." He is not, in these texts, stopping to brand Thomas, Matthias, or the Book of James as heretical forgeries — that sharper language comes later, with Eusebius's "forgeries of heretics" (Church History 3.25) and the Western condemnations. (A scholarly caution attaches to pressing Origen too hard the other way, too: the strongest "from which the dogmas of the church are not derived" phrasing sometimes quoted for him survives only in Jerome's Latin rendering of the Luke homily, so the non-authority point should rest on the secure text — "the church of God has approved only the four" — rather than on the Latin clause.)
  • An undescribed title names nothing. Origen's bare list — "the Gospel according to Thomas … and several others" — gives no contents, so it cannot by itself decide which "Gospel of Thomas" he meant: the Gnostic sayings gospel or the Infancy Gospel. This is why his notice, like Eusebius's catalogue-name, is among the few loose enough to admit the infancy text, but proves nothing about it on its own: see the crux Which "Gospel of Thomas" did the Fathers condemn?
  • Exclusion by reception, not suppression. Origen names these books and declines to receive them; he does not call for their destruction — the same gradual non-reception that runs through the whole record, against the popular "banned and burned" framing weighed at Were the infancy gospels suppressed?
