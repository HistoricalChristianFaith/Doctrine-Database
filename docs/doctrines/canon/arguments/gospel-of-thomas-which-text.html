Which "Gospel of Thomas" did the Fathers condemn — the sayings gospel or the Infancy Gospel?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — the equation that the named-"Gospel of Thomas" condemnations of the Fathers (Hippolytus, Origen, Eusebius, Cyril of Jerusalem) and the Gelasian rejection-list are evidence against the Infancy Gospel of Thomas does not hold. Where the title is named and the text identifiable, the target is the Gnostic sayings gospel (Hippolytus quotes its saying 4) or a Manichaean "Gospel of Thomas" (Cyril); Eusebius brackets it among Gnostic apostolic-pseudonym forgeries; Origen merely lists the title without describing the book. The Infancy Gospel of Thomas — the boy-Jesus miracle narrative — was excluded too, but on partly different evidence: Irenaeus's report of the boy-Jesus alphabet tale (which does not use the title "Gospel of Thomas"), and the Gelasian Decree's own separate entries for an infancy book. This argument takes up a habitual conflation: that the early condemnations of a "Gospel of Thomas" — by Hippolytus, Origen, Eusebius, and Cyril of Jerusalem, and in the Gelasian rejection-list — were aimed at the Infancy Gospel of Thomas, the narrative of the miracle-working boy Jesus. They were not, where the text can be told apart: the named condemnations target the Gnostic sayings gospel (or a Manichaean book of the same title).

The claim

"Gospel of Thomas" is one title borne by two unrelated works. The first is the Gnostic sayings gospel — a collection of 114 secret sayings of Jesus, known from a Coptic codex recovered at Nag Hammadi in 1945 and from earlier Greek fragments at Oxyrhynchus. The second is the Infancy Gospel of Thomas — a narrative of the childhood miracles of Jesus between the ages of five and twelve, the boy who fashions living sparrows from clay and strikes dead the child who jostles him. Because the Fathers had only the bare title, modern popular and reference accounts routinely gather the patristic condemnations of "the Gospel of Thomas" — Irenaeus, Hippolytus, Origen, Eusebius, Cyril of Jerusalem, the Gelasian Decree — into one list and read them as the rejection of the infancy text.1 The interpretation in view is the equation: that the famous early "Gospel of Thomas" rejections are evidence against the Infancy Gospel of Thomas. If the equation holds, the infancy text was being condemned by name from the early third century. If it fails, the infancy text was excluded on a different and largely separate trail of evidence, and the "Gospel of Thomas" condemnations belong to another book.

Who advances it

The conflation is rarely a thesis anyone argues; it is an assumption built into how the sources are presented. Apologetic and reference surveys list "the non-canonical gospels attributed to Thomas" and run together Hippolytus, Origen, Eusebius, Cyril, and Gelasius as witnesses against a single "Gospel of Thomas," leaving the reader to suppose one book is in view; the same slippage lets the infancy text inherit the weight of the better-known sayings-gospel condemnations.2 Careful treatments flag the hazard expressly. The standard encyclopaedic note on the Infancy Gospel warns that the references "by Hippolytus of Rome and Origen of Alexandria to a 'Gospel of Thomas'" are of uncertain target — "it is unclear whether those letters refer to the Infancy Gospel or the Gospel of Thomas, a sayings gospel" — and judges it "barely possible but highly unlikely" that Cyril meant the infancy text.3 So the equation lives at the level of the survey and the headline, not the monograph.

The evidence for

What makes the conflation tempting. The ingredients are real. The Infancy Gospel of Thomas is genuinely ancient — its boy-Jesus material is attested already by Irenaeus around 180 — and genuinely excluded, so a reader meeting a string of "Gospel of Thomas" condemnations naturally files them against the text whose name is best known to a modern audience. Two of the patristic notices are, on their face, undescribed: Origen simply lists "the Gospel according to Thomas" among non-approved gospels without saying what is in it, and Eusebius names "the Gospel of Thomas" in a catalogue of rejected books without quoting a line. Where the source itself does not tell us which Thomas it means, the infancy text is an available candidate.4 And the boundary did, in the end, exclude the infancy gospel — so the conclusion the equation reaches (the infancy text was rejected) is true, even if the route is wrong.

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The Infancy Gospel of Thomas really was outside the canon, really is early, and really was condemned — by Irenaeus's report of its boy-Jesus story and on the later Western rejection-lists. The two-text problem is also genuine: the bare title "Gospel of Thomas" cannot, by itself, tell the sayings gospel from the infancy narrative, and a couple of the patristic notices give us no more than the title. To that extent caution, not the equation, is what the evidence licenses.

Why the equation fails, witness by witness.

  1. Hippolytus quotes the sayings gospel — decisively not the infancy text. In the Refutation of All Heresies 5.7 Hippolytus reports that the Gnostic Naassenes "hand down an explicit passage, occurring in the Gospel inscribed according to Thomas," and quotes it: "He who seeks me, will find me in children from seven years old; for there concealed, I shall in the fourteenth age be made manifest." That is a version of saying 4 of the Nag Hammadi sayings gospel. It has nothing to do with the boy Jesus of the infancy narrative; the one early Father who quotes a line of "the Gospel of Thomas" is quoting the sayings gospel.5

  2. Cyril names a Manichaean "Gospel of Thomas," not the infancy book. Cyril of Jerusalem condemns the title twice. In Catechetical Lecture 4.36 he warns that "the Manichaeans also wrote a Gospel according to Thomas, which being tinctured with the fragrance of the evangelic title corrupts the souls of the simple sort"; in 6.31, after naming Mani's three disciples — "Thomas, and Baddas, and Hermas" — he tells his hearers, "Let none read the Gospel according to Thomas: for it is the work not of one of the twelve Apostles, but of one of the three wicked disciples of Manes." Cyril's "Gospel of Thomas" is a book he ties to Manichaeism and to a disciple named Thomas — the sayings gospel (which the Manichaeans used) or a lost Manichaean Thomas, not the childhood-miracle narrative.6

  3. Eusebius brackets it among the Gnostic apostolic-pseudonym forgeries. In Church History 3.25 Eusebius lists "the Gospels of Peter, of Thomas, of Matthias" together as books "cited by the heretics under the name of the apostles," forgeries that "no one belonging to the succession of ecclesiastical writers deemed worthy of mention." The company he keeps it in — Peter, Matthias, the heretics' apostolic pseudepigrapha — is that of the Gnostic sayings-type gospels, not the popular infancy legend.7

  4. Origen only lists the title — he does not describe the infancy text. In the first homily on Luke Origen sets "the Gospel according to Thomas and the Gospel according to Matthias and several others" apart from the four "the church of God has approved." This is the one notice the cautious encyclopaedias call genuinely ambiguous; but ambiguity is not identification. Origen gives no childhood miracle, no boy Jesus, nothing that points to the infancy narrative rather than the sayings gospel he and his Alexandrian predecessors otherwise knew. An undescribed title cannot carry the equation.8

  5. The witness who does attest the infancy material never uses the title "Gospel of Thomas." The earliest reaction to boy-Jesus material is Irenaeus's (c. 180), who files among the Marcosians' "unspeakable number of apocryphal and spurious writings" the "false and wicked story" that "our Lord, when He was a boy learning His letters, on the teacher saying to Him … 'Pronounce Alpha,' replied … 'Alpha.' But when, again, the teacher bade Him say, 'Beta,' the Lord replied, 'Do thou first tell me what Alpha is …'" That is the alphabet episode of the Infancy Gospel of Thomas — but Irenaeus calls it neither "Thomas" nor a "gospel," and may know it as a floating tale rather than a fixed book (the same saying travels in the Epistle of the Apostles). The infancy text's exclusion thus rests on its own trail of evidence, distinct from the named-"Thomas" condemnations.9

  6. The Gelasian rejection-list itself counts the Thomas gospel and the infancy book as two entries. The very catalogue most often cited to prove "the Gospel of Thomas" was condemned distinguishes the two texts in its own line-items: it rejects "the Gospel in the name of Thomas which the Manichaeans use" — flagged, like Cyril's, as Manichaean — and lists separately "the book on the infancy of the saviour" and "the book of the nativity of the saviour and of Mary or the midwife" (the Protoevangelium material). The compilers who drew up the list plainly did not equate the Manichaean "Gospel of Thomas" with the infancy book; they shunned both, under different names.10

Assessment

Weak. The equation — that the early named-"Gospel of Thomas" condemnations are evidence against the Infancy Gospel of Thomas — does not hold on its best case. Every notice in which the text is identifiable points the other way: Hippolytus quotes the sayings gospel's saying 4; Cyril names a Manichaean "Gospel of Thomas" and ties it to a disciple of Mani; Eusebius brackets it among the Gnostic apostolic-pseudonym forgeries; and the Gelasian list, far from equating the two, registers the Manichaean Thomas gospel and the infancy book as separate entries. The only notices loose enough to admit the infancy text — Origen's bare list and Eusebius's catalogue-name — are merely undescribed, and silence identifies nothing. Meanwhile the one early witness who plainly attests the infancy material, Irenaeus, does not call it "Thomas" or even a book. The true and modest residue is that the Infancy Gospel of Thomas was excluded as well — but on its own, partly different evidence, and not by the famous "Gospel of Thomas" rejections, which were aimed at another book and should not be made to do double duty. (Whether that exclusion amounted to active suppression — banning and burning — is weighed separately; it did not.)
