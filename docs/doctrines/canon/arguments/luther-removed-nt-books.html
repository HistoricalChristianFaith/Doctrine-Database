Did Martin Luther remove four New Testament books — Hebrews, James, Jude, and Revelation?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — for the "took out… put back" framing. The popular Catholic-apologetic claim is that Martin Luther, having relocated the Old-Testament deuterocanon, also removed four books from the New Testament — Hebrews, James, Jude, and Revelation — and that these were "later put back" by other Protestants. As a description of what Luther did it does not hold on its best case: it confuses demotion-and-reordering with excision, and its "put back" half is simply false. Luther never deleted the four. In his 1522 German New Testament he translated and printed all twenty-seven books, moved these four to the end, and left them unnumbered in the table of contents under prefaces doubting their apostolicity — a "canon within the canon," kept in the Bible but ranked below the rest. The four then stood in his own New Testament and in every Lutheran and Protestant Bible since; nothing was ever taken out, so nothing was put back. What is sound underneath the slogan is a narrower point: Luther genuinely doubted these four and demoted them in rank and order on theological grounds — a real change in how a printed New Testament presented its books — but that is demotion, not removal · back to timeline · Luther's own act is documented on the Martin Luther witness page

See also: the Old-Testament twin of the same trope — did Martin Luther remove the deuterocanonical books from the Bible? (assessment: weak). The two are the same popularizer's claim about Luther applied to the two Testaments; both over-read "demoted and relocated" as "deleted." (Note one real difference the slogan blurs: Luther did gather the Old-Testament Apocrypha into a separate section between the Testaments, but the four disputed New-Testament books he left inside the New Testament, merely last and unnumbered.)

An argument page weighs a single sub-claim adduced in support of a main interpretation — a proof-text, a parallel, a historical thesis — and renders an adversarial assessment of the interpretation it serves. Unlike a person-detail page it sits across the timeline rather than at one date. Here the sub-claim is a historical thesis about a single agent and act: that Martin Luther removed four books from the New Testament. The separate question of why the early church had long disputed these very books — the antilegomena — is the timeline's own soft-edges theme; this page takes only the headline "removed / took out versus demoted-and-reordered" question.

The claim

The trope rides in on the back of its Old-Testament twin: Luther, the story runs, edited the Bible to suit his theology — pulling the deuterocanon out of the Old Testament and four books out of the New. Jimmy Akin's widely reprinted "Defending the Deuterocanonicals" states the New-Testament half plainly:

Luther also took out four New Testament books — Hebrews, James, Jude, and Revelation — and put them in an appendix without page numbers as well. These were later put back into the New Testament by other Protestants.

— Jimmy Akin, "Defending the Deuterocanonicals."1 The operative words are took out, appendix, and put back. In this sharp popular form the claim is not that Luther reordered the books or doubted them but that he excised them — that four books were in the New Testament, Luther took them out, and someone else had to restore them.

The evidence for

There is a real change underneath the slogan, and it was Luther's doing. A flat denial ("Luther changed nothing") would be as false as the slogan. He did three things to these four books that genuinely altered how a New Testament presented itself.

  1. He reordered them and dropped them from the numbered list. In the 1522 September Testament the other twenty-three books are listed in the table of contents and numbered 1–23; Hebrews, James, Jude, and Revelation are set below them, after a gap, without numbers, and moved to the end of the volume — a visible second tier.2

  2. He prefaced each with a doubt about its apostolicity. The Hebrews preface marks the four off as books that "from ancient times had a different reputation"; James he called, in the 1522 Preface to the New Testament, "really an epistle of straw… for it has nothing of the nature of the gospel about it," and in its own preface "not… the writing of an apostle"; Jude "an extract or copy of St. Peter's second epistle"; Revelation, in 1522, "neither apostolic nor prophetic."3

  3. His criterion was theological, and the demotion was doctrinally driven. Luther ranked books by their apostolicity and by whether they "preach Christ" — "whatever does not teach Christ is not yet apostolic." James fell on its apparent works-justification (James 2); Hebrews he doubted partly on its passages denying a second repentance (Heb. 6, 10). So the charge that the demotion was bound up with doctrine is, on this much, fair: Luther's own stated reasons are theological.3

So a real act stands behind the trope: Luther doubted these four and demoted them in rank and order. But that is a claim about a book's standing and place, not about taking it out of the Bible — and it is precisely the point the slogan's "took out… put back" language overshoots.

Adversarial assessment

What holds. Two things. First, Luther really did doubt the apostolicity of Hebrews, James, Jude, and Revelation — explicitly, in print, on theological grounds. Second, he really did demote and reorder them: moved to the end, unnumbered, prefaced as second-tier. The soft form of the Catholic point — "Luther downgraded four New Testament books and set them apart" — is true, and is the substance the witness page documents.

What is overstated. The slogan slides from that true, modest point to a false, dramatic one — that Luther excised the four and others had to put them back. Three facts rule the dramatic form out.

  1. Luther kept all four in his New Testament. The 1522 September Testament contains Hebrews, James, Jude, and Revelation — translated, printed, bound, and prefaced. A book set last in a volume, under a preface explaining why, has been reordered and ranked, not removed. Luther himself frames the judgment as advice, not deletion: of James he writes that he "would not thereby prevent anyone from including or extolling him as he pleases."4

  2. "Put back by other Protestants" is false — they were never out. Because the four were in Luther's own New Testament from 1522, and in every Lutheran and Protestant Bible after it, there was nothing to restore. The claim imagines a removal-and-restoration that never happened. (Akin's parallel "appendix without page numbers" also overshoots: Luther left these four inside the New Testament — last and unnumbered — whereas the genuine separate section between the Testaments was reserved for the Old-Testament Apocrypha, a distinct case.)2

  3. Luther softened the very judgments the slogan freezes. The "epistle of straw" line was deleted from the revised Preface to the New Testament and is absent from his later Bibles; and in 1530 he replaced his cold Revelation note with a long, favorable new preface, dropping the "neither apostolic nor prophetic" verdict. A man pruning the canon does not write a fresh preface drawing a doubted book back toward the center. The doctrinal reading that demoted the four is the same reading that led Luther to keep them, soften his words, and rehabilitate Revelation.5

What the dispute really turns on. Strip the "removed… put back" framing away and the genuine question is not whether Luther took four books out of the New Testament — he did not — but whether his doubts about them were idiosyncratic theology-engineering or the revival of an old and well-grounded uncertainty. And here the evidence cuts against the trope's insinuation while granting its occasion: the four were exactly the books the early church had long disputed — Eusebius's antilegomena (James, Jude, 2 Peter, 2–3 John), Hebrews doubted in the West, the Apocalypse doubted longest in the East — and Luther's contemporaries Erasmus (1516) and Cardinal Cajetan questioned the same four. Luther reopened a question the canon's soft edges had left genuinely open; he did not invent doubts to excise inconvenient texts, and in the end he excised nothing. The bare "Luther removed four New Testament books" slogan collapses a real argument over rank and apostolicity into a false picture of a sixteenth-century excision — exactly as its Old-Testament twin, "Luther removed the deuterocanon," over-reads in the same way.

Assessment

Weak — for the "Luther took out four New Testament books… later put back by other Protestants" framing. As a description of what happened the slogan does not hold: it confuses demoting and reordering books with excising them, and its "put back" half is false. Luther's 1522 German New Testament contains Hebrews, James, Jude, and Revelation — translated, printed, prefaced, and merely set last and unnumbered — so the four were doubted and demoted, not taken out; they then stood in his own New Testament and in every Lutheran and Protestant Bible since, so nothing was ever removed and nothing put back. Luther even softened the judgments the slogan freezes: the "epistle of straw" line was dropped from his later editions, and in 1530 he gave Revelation a new, favorable preface. What survives, and is sound, is the narrower and quite different claim — that Luther doubted these four books' apostolicity and demoted them in rank and order, on theological grounds, reviving the ancient antilegomena doubt — but that is demotion, not removal. Flag on the proponent's framing (Jimmy Akin's "took out… put them in an appendix… later put back") the slide from "doubted and demoted" (true) to "removed and restored" (false), and the conflation of the within-New-Testament relocation with the separate Old-Testament Apocrypha section. The argument is the New-Testament twin of the "Luther removed the deuterocanon" trope, and over-reads in the identical way.
