Was the fourfold Gospel already closed by c. 180? — Irenaeus's Tetramorph

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: sound — as history. By c. 180 Irenaeus defends the Gospels as exactly four, "neither more nor fewer," matched to the four zones of the world, the four winds, and the four cherubic faces (the "Tetramorph," Against Heresies 3.11.8). Two claims must be kept apart. The historical datum — that the fourfold Gospel was a fixed, closed collection by the late second century, a century before the rest of the New Testament settled — is sound: it is the mainstream of canon scholarship, and Irenaeus is its transmitter, not its inventor, the four-Gospel collection already attested before him. But Irenaeus's argument for the number four — the cosmic symbolism of zones, winds, and living creatures — is rhetorical rationalization of a fact already received, not the evidence that established it; taken as proof of why there must be four it is weak. The datum does not rest on the argument, so the symbolism's weakness leaves the historical claim standing · back to timeline · advanced by the modern study of the canon (Stanton, Hengel, Hill, Skeat)

An argument page weighs a single sub-claim adduced in support of a main interpretation — a proof-text, a parallel, a historical thesis — and renders an adversarial assessment of the interpretation it serves. Unlike a person-detail page it sits across the timeline rather than at one date. This argument is the complement of its sibling "settled gradually, contested at its edges": where that one shows the canon's edges stayed soft for centuries, this one shows its core — the four Gospels — was effectively closed early. Every factual claim is sourced; the proponent's own words are quoted verbatim.

The claim

It is not possible that the Gospels can be either more or fewer in number than they are. For, since there are four zones of the world in which we live, and four principal winds, while the Church is scattered throughout all the world, and the "pillar and ground" of the Church is the Gospel and the spirit of life; it is fitting that she should have four pillars, breathing out immortality on every side, and vivifying men afresh. From which fact, it is evident that the Word, the Artificer of all, He that sitteth upon the cherubim, and contains all things, He who was manifested to men, has given us the Gospel under four aspects, but bound together by one Spirit.

— Irenaeus, Against Heresies 3.11.8, ANF 1, historicalchristian.faith (also at New Advent).1 Irenaeus goes on to match the four to the four faces of the cherubim (lion, calf, man, eagle) and to "four principal covenants given to the human race," concluding "the living creatures are quadriform, and the Gospel is quadriform." Two distinct claims are folded into the one passage. The first is historical: there are four Gospels, a closed collection — the rival gospels excluded by the very count. The second is an argument for that number: it is fitting that there be four, because the world has four zones and four winds and the cherubim four faces. The argument is whether either claim holds, and they must be weighed separately.

Who advances it

The reading that the fourfold Gospel was fixed early — that Irenaeus is reporting a settled fact, not legislating a new one — is the mainstream of modern canon scholarship. Graham Stanton states it flatly: "By the time Irenaeus wrote … the fourfold Gospel was very well established. Irenaeus is not defending an innovation, but explaining why, unlike the heretics, the church has four gospels, no more, no less." Martin Hengel argues the fourfold Gospel "was already presupposed rather than occasioned by Marcion," its decision "made at an early stage"; Charles Hill (Who Chose the Gospels?) concludes Irenaeus "was no innovator but was merely transmitting an established tradition that preceded him"; and Michael Kruger notes that "Irenaeus does not write like a person advocating the scriptural status of these books for the first time."2 The interpretation this serves is the doctrine's positive backbone on the inclusion side: that the core of the New Testament — the four Gospels above all — was recognized very early and as a closed set, so that the long, fuzzy contest recorded across the rest of the timeline was always at the edges, never over the center.

The evidence for

1. The four-Gospel collection is attested before Irenaeus. Irenaeus c. 180 is the first to argue for the number four, but he is not the first to have the four. A generation earlier Justin Martyr (c. 155) already cites the apostles' "memoirs … which are called Gospels," read in the Sunday assembly as Scripture; his pupil Tatian wove precisely four Gospels — Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John — into a single harmony, the Diatessaron (c. 170), which presupposes the four as the texts worth harmonizing. The Epistula Apostolorum, a Christian writing usually dated to the 140s, names the four evangelists together and no others. And the physical record agrees: the early Gospel codices that survive (the four-Gospel P45; the John-and-Luke P75) show the Gospels already being gathered as a fixed group rather than circulating singly. The fourfold collection was in place decades before Irenaeus rose to defend it.3

2. Irenaeus grounds the four in apostolic origin, not in the symbolism. When Irenaeus actually identifies the Gospels, he names them by their authors and their apostolic descent — "Matthew … among the Hebrews," Mark "the disciple and interpreter of Peter," Luke "the companion of Paul," John "during his residence at Ephesus" (Against Heresies 3.1.1) — the same apostolic-origin criterion that orders the whole later canon. The collection he defends is identified historically; the four-winds passage is the rhetorical capstone he sets on top of it, not the load it rests on.4

3. The closed count does real work against the heretics. Irenaeus's point in pressing "neither more nor fewer" is polemical and concrete: the Valentinians and other Gnostics either added gospels (the Gospel of Truth, the Gospel of Judas) or pared them down (Marcion's single, edited Luke). That he can wield "exactly four" as a settled standard against named opponents is itself evidence that the four were already the recognized measure in the churches he writes for — one does not refute heretics by appeal to a rule one is in the act of inventing.2

Adversarial assessment

The argument for the number four is weak — and Irenaeus's contemporaries felt its weakness too. The chain "four zones, four winds, four cherubic faces, therefore four Gospels" is an a priori fitness-argument, not evidence: it would "prove" four Gospels in any world that happened to have four winds, and it presupposes exactly the conclusion it decorates. Read as a demonstration of why there must be four, it does not hold. T. C. Skeat showed, moreover, that the cherubic-creatures material Irenaeus deploys was not his own coinage but drawn from an earlier source, which is why his assignment of creatures to evangelists sits awkwardly with the later, more familiar scheme — the symbolism is borrowed ornament on a fact, not the reasoning that produced it.5 So if the argument is read as "does Irenaeus prove there are four Gospels?", the answer is no.

But the historical datum does not depend on the argument, and the datum is sound. The fourfold Gospel was a closed collection by c. 180 whether or not the four-winds reasoning is any good, because that fact is established by independent evidence — Justin's "memoirs," Tatian's harmony of the four, the Epistula Apostolorum, the early Gospel codices, and the polemical use of the count against heretics — none of which rests on cosmic symbolism. Irenaeus is a witness to a settled fourfold Gospel, and his bad argument for the number no more unsettles the fact than a weak proof of a true theorem makes the theorem false. The two questions the page keeps apart answer differently: the proponent's stated argument for four is weak, but the interpretation it serves — the four-Gospel canon was fixed early — is sound on its best, independent evidence.

What must not be overstated. "Fixed early" means the collection of four was closed and recognized, not that every detail of reception was complete: the relative order of the four still varied, the long endings and pericopae were not yet uniform, and Gospel-harmonies like the Diatessaron kept currency in some churches for generations. The claim is the modest and the secure one — that by the late second century the church received four Gospels as a bounded set, no more and no fewer — not that the Gospel text or its use was frozen. And the early closure of the Gospel core is the precise complement of, not a rival to, the gradual reception of the canon's edges: core fixed early, edges soft for centuries.

Assessment

Sound — as a historical claim. That the fourfold Gospel was a fixed, closed collection by c. 180 is mainstream canon history (Stanton, Hengel, Hill, Kruger), attested independently of Irenaeus by Justin, Tatian's Diatessaron, the Epistula Apostolorum, and the early Gospel codices; Irenaeus transmits the fact rather than inventing it. His argument for the number four — the four zones, winds, and cherubic faces — is by contrast a symbolic fitness-argument, weak as proof and (as Skeat showed) borrowed from an earlier source; but because the datum rests on independent evidence and not on the symbolism, the argument's weakness does not touch it. Keep the two apart and both verdicts stand: the proponent's reasoning for why four is weak, the interpretation that the four-Gospel canon was settled early is sound — the firm center against which the doctrine's soft-edges thesis is measured.
