The Council of Rome (382) and the "Damasine list" — did Damasus promulgate the canon?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — the popular claim that "the canon was fixed at the Council of Rome in 382 under Damasus" rests entirely on the so-called Damasine list, which survives only embedded in the Decretum Gelasianum — a compilation that critical scholarship (von Dobschütz, Burkitt) dates to the sixth century and that this site dates c. 500. There was a real Roman synod under Damasus in 382, and a respectable minority (Arevalo, Turner, Jurgens) holds its canon-chapters authentic; but the 382 attribution is an eighteenth-century conjecture unsupported by any fourth-century witness, and Jerome — present at Rome, foremost on the canon — nowhere mentions such a decree. The narrower claim, that a Damasan-era Rome circulated a list reflecting the canon as the West then received it, is plausible; the "fixed at Rome in 382" framing is weak · advanced via the Council of Rome witness

See also: the sibling arguments on mis-dated and mis-attributed conciliar canon-actions — the scholarly case for a Nicene canon-action (Jerome's Preface to Judith · the Vallicellian "Nicaea" heading on Hippo's canon 36 · Cassiodorus's Institutiones I.14), and the same document's exclusion-face on the timeline, the Decretum Gelasianum and the Greek rejection-lists.

This argument isolates the documentary basis of the widely-repeated claim that a council at Rome in 382, under Pope Damasus I, formally settled the biblical canon.

The claim

"The Council of Rome of 382 is a Christian synod convened in Rome in the year 382, under the leadership of Pope Damasus I. It is widely held that this council initially set a Biblical canon in a decree (that is, the Decretum Gelasianum, or 'Gelasian Decree') which is identical to the list of the Council of Trent."

— the popular framing, as it stands in the encyclopaedia summary of the council.1 The canon-list ascribed to that council — the so-called Damasine list — runs out the present twenty-seven-book New Testament and the longer Septuagint Old Testament (Tobit, Judith, the Wisdom books, two books of Maccabees), and on the strong form of the claim it makes Rome in 382 the place and moment the canon was decided, a decade before Hippo and twelve centuries before Trent. The list survives in one document only: the Decretum Gelasianum de libris recipiendis et non recipiendis, whose second chapter is the canon-catalogue here at issue.2

Who advances it

The proponents. The attribution of the Decretum's canon-chapters to a Damasan council of 382 is, as a scholarly thesis, comparatively modern: it was first proposed in 1794 by Faustino Arévalo, who argued that the first three of the Decretum's five chapters were really the decrees of a Roman council held under Damasus a century before Gelasius. C. H. Turner later edited those three chapters from eighth- and ninth-century manuscripts under the title The Roman Council under Damasus, and the Catholic patrologist William A. Jurgens states the strong form: "It is now almost universally accepted that these parts one and two of the Decree of Damasus are authentic parts of the Acts of the Council of Rome of 382 A.D."3 From these the claim passes into popular apologetics, where 382 is routinely cited as the year the Church "set the canon."

The adversaries. Against the 382 attribution stands the textual-critical mainstream that follows Ernst von Dobschütz, whose 1912 critical edition concluded that the Decretum is a single work throughout — "no genuine decree or letter either of Damasus or Gelasius, but a pseudonymous literary production of the first half of the sixth century" (519–553). F. C. Burkitt, reviewing it, judged that "there can, I think, be little doubt that v. Dobschütz has made out his case." Geoffrey Hahneman presses the argument from Jerome's silence; Bruce Metzger notes that claims of a definitive 382 decree lack primary evidence.4

The evidence for

What the claim has going for it. It is not built on nothing.

  1. The council is real. A synod did meet at Rome in 382 under Damasus — convened in the aftermath of Constantinople (381) and occupied with the Antiochene/Meletian schism — and Jerome, who came to Rome that year with Paulinus of Antioch and Epiphanius, was present and stayed on as Damasus's secretary. A Damasan interest in Scripture is equally real: it was Damasus who, in these very years, commissioned Jerome's Latin revision of the Gospels, the seed of the Vulgate. A Roman list of the received books in Damasus's circle is, on its face, entirely plausible.5

  2. The list itself is early in content. Whoever first drew it, the Damasine catalogue depends on Jerome — it names Lamentations by its Hebrew title "Cinoth," a Hieronymian touch — and so reflects a late-fourth-century Latin reckoning of the books, the same twenty-seven-book New Testament Hippo would enact in 393. As a witness to the Western canon as received, its substance fits the period the strong claim assigns it.

  3. A genuine scholarly tradition defends it. Arévalo, Turner, and Jurgens are not cranks; the question of whether the canon-chapters are an authentic Damasan stratum later absorbed into the Decretum is a real, live one, and the matching of the list to the eventual Catholic canon gives the thesis its pull.

Adversarial assessment

What holds. That a Roman synod met under Damasus in 382, and that the Damasine list reflects the canon as the late-fourth-century West received it, are both fair. If the question were merely "did Damasus's Rome hold the twenty-seven books and the deuterocanon to be Scripture?", the answer is very likely yes — that is the ordinary Western reception the African councils (Hippo 393, Carthage 397/419) would ratify, that Innocent I would restate from Rome itself in 405, and that Jerome's own Vulgate would carry — deuterocanon and all — for the next thousand years. The document-dating doubt and a reception doubt must be kept apart. Demoting Rome 382 removes one alleged proof of a formal canon-decree; it does nothing to show that the Western church did not hold these books as Scripture — that reception is attested by a crowd of independent witnesses who owe nothing to the Damasine list. So the skeptical case is strong exactly where it stays a case about the document; over-read into "the church had not received these books before Trent," it collapses against Innocent, the African councils, and the Vulgate.

What fails — the leap from that to a promulgated canon-decree enacted at the council of 382.

  1. The only document is sixth-century. The Damasine list survives nowhere as an independent fourth-century text; it exists only as a chapter of the Decretum Gelasianum. And von Dobschütz showed the Decretum to be one pseudonymous work of the first half of the sixth century: its first chapter quotes a work Augustine wrote in 416, so it cannot, as a unit, be a decree of 382. Burkitt endorsed the conclusion; the rejection-lists witness on this site already dates the Decretum c. 500 on the same grounds.6

  2. The 382 attribution is an eighteenth-century conjecture. No ancient source ascribes a canon-list to the 382 synod. The attribution begins with Arévalo in 1794, who detached the first three chapters and re-titled them for a Damasan council; in some manuscript recensions Damasus and the Council of Rome are not named at all. An attribution that appears only fourteen centuries after the supposed event, by separating chapters out of a later compilation, cannot bear the weight of "the council that fixed the canon." Even the word decree is anachronistic: the papal decretal as a genre begins only after Damasus's death, with Siricius's letter to Himerius of Tarragona in February 385 — so a 382 synod issuing a canon-decretal would predate the first decretal of any kind.7

  3. Jerome's silence is decisive. This is the sharpest objection. The list depends on Jerome; Jerome was at Rome in 382 and was the Latin Church's foremost authority on the canon for the next thirty-five years — yet he nowhere mentions a Roman conciliar list of the sacred books, and in his own prefaces he argues the canon out for himself, expressly ranking Judith, Tobit, and the Maccabees below the canon. As Hahneman puts it, it "seems highly improbable that, if Jerome … had ever heard of such a pronouncement … he should nowhere have mentioned it." A canon decreed at a council he attended, drawn from a list shaped by his own work, that he then ignores and contradicts, is not credible.8

  4. The early collectors did not know it as a decree. Dionysius Exiguus, compiling the papal decretals in Rome around 500, does not include the Decretum among the acts of Gelasius — evidence that the document was attached to these early popes' names only later, with "no means … of verifying it." The same drift of canon-material onto authoritative early names — later canons sliding under Nicaea's heading — is the documented pattern behind the cognate Hippo-under-Nicaea and Nicaea-counted-Judith arguments.9

  5. "Almost universally accepted" overstates the field. Jurgens's claim of near-universal acceptance is an apologetic verdict, not the state of textual scholarship, which since von Dobschütz has overwhelmingly treated the whole Decretum as a sixth-century private compilation (Italian, or, on McKitterick's view, Frankish-Gallic). Metzger grants that Damasus may have contributed to canon-awareness while noting that a definitive 382 decree lacks primary evidence. The authenticity of the canon-chapters is, at best, genuinely contested — and even if granted, it would yield a Damasan-era Roman list, not the formal "fixing" of the canon the popular claim imagines.10

  6. Carthage in 397 acts as though no Roman canon-decree existed. The canon enacted by the African council of 397 closes by referring its list across the sea for confirmation: "let the Church beyond the sea (transmarina ecclesia) be consulted to confirm this canon." Had a Roman synod under Damasus settled the canon by decree only fifteen years before, the African bishops — in steady correspondence with Rome — would have had a decisive precedent ready to hand, and the deferential "let Rome be consulted" would make little sense. Their referring the matter overseas reads instead like a boundary still being acknowledged, not one already promulgated at Rome. The objection cuts only against the decree, of course, not the reception: Carthage's own list already contained the deuterocanon.11

Assessment

Weak. The interpretation that the canon was fixed at a Council of Rome in 382 under Damasus does not hold on its best available case. Its single documentary basis is the Damasine list, which survives only inside the Decretum Gelasianum — a sixth-century pseudonymous compilation by the critical consensus, dated c. 500 on this site — and the 382 attribution is an eighteenth-century conjecture (Arévalo) unsupported by any fourth-century witness and confounded by Jerome's silence. What the evidence will bear is the modest claim that Damasus's Rome, like the rest of the late-fourth-century West, received the twenty-seven books and the deuterocanon as Scripture — a plausible reading that the African councils would soon put into conciliar form. The strong claim that 382 was the decisive canon-promulgation is weak: a real synod, a real document, but joined only by a late and unverifiable thread. (It is a near-twin of the parallel error that puts the canon-decision at Nicaea — a settled boundary read backward onto a council that left no such act in its own records.)
