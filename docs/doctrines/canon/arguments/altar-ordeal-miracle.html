The altar-ordeal miracle — did the inspired books leap onto the altar at Nicaea while the spurious fell beneath?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: unsupported — the miraculous "book-ordeal" by which the legend has Nicaea sort the canon (the inspired books found on the altar, the spurious underneath) rests on a single source: §35 of the anonymous Synodicon Vetus, compiled around AD 900 — nearly six centuries after the council — a work crowded with details found nowhere else, recounting councils scholars doubt ever met. No contemporary witness, and nothing in the council's own acts and canons, knows of it; the contemporary record (Eusebius) has the canon still disputed after 325, which a miraculous sorting would have ended. The tale entered Western letters only when its Greek manuscript was printed in 1601 and was relayed by Voltaire, hence the modern "even sorted by a miracle" embellishment of the broader legend · back to timeline · earliest form: Synodicon Vetus §35 (c. 900)

Related arguments: the Nicene-canon legend — see also did Nicaea fix the canon? (the popular legend) and were the infancy gospels suppressed?

An argument page weighs a single sub-claim adduced in support of a main interpretation — a proof-text, a parallel, a historical thesis — and renders an adversarial assessment of the interpretation it serves. Unlike a person-detail page it sits across the timeline rather than at one date. This argument isolates the mechanism strand of the Nicaea-fixed-the-canon legend: the claim that the books of Scripture were sorted at Nicaea by a miracle — the genuine ones rising to the altar, the spurious sinking beneath. Every factual claim is sourced; the proponents' own words are quoted verbatim.

The claim

"The canonical and apocryphal books it distinguished in the following manner: in the house of God the books were placed down by the holy altar; then the council asked the Lord in prayer that the inspired works be found on top and—as in fact happened—the spurious on the bottom."

— the anonymous Synodicon Vetus, §35, on the Council of Nicaea (325), in the critical edition of John Duffy and John Parker.1 The interpretation the claim serves is the same as the broader legend's — that the contents of the Bible were determined at Nicaea — but it supplies the dramatic mechanism: not a vote or a decree but a sign from heaven, the inspired books physically separating themselves from the rest atop the altar. It is this miraculous sorting that lets a modern writer say the canon was "even sorted by a miracle" at Nicaea.

Who advances it

The source. The story is recorded in exactly one place: the Synodicon Vetus, an anonymous Byzantine epitome of 158 councils of the first nine centuries, compiled after 887 (the latest events it records) — roughly AD 900, and so nearly six hundred years after Nicaea. It survives because a manuscript was carried from the Morea (Greece) in the sixteenth century by Andreas Darmasius and bought, edited, and printed in 1601 at Strasbourg by the Lutheran theologian Johannes Pappus (1549–1610). From Pappus's edition the passage circulated and was repeatedly cited — sometimes, by a slip, as though Pappus himself were its author ("Pappus, in his Synodicon"), rather than the Greek epitomist he had merely edited.2

The downstream relayers. Through Pappus the tale reached the Enlightenment. Voltaire, in the Philosophical Dictionary ("Councils," §III, 1764), retold it — "in the supplement to the Council of Nice it is related that the fathers, being much perplexed to find out which were the authentic and which the apocryphal books… laid them all upon an altar, and the books which they were to reject fell to the ground" — naming his source as "the supplement to the Council of Nice," i.e. the Synodicon Vetus. Anglophone freethought passed it on in turn: Robert Taylor reproduced the anecdote in The Diegesis (1829), citing the Synodicon by way of Pappus, as a weapon against the canon's divine credentials.3 The modern restatement that the canon was decided at Nicaea "even sorted by a miracle" is the residue of this same chain — Synodicon → Pappus → Voltaire → the popular legend.

The evidence for

What the claim has going for it. Only this: a real and famous council did meet at Nicaea in 325, and the later Byzantine church magnified it as the archetype of an inspired synod. A book-ordeal — disputed writings laid before the altar, the verdict left to God — is a vivid and pious way to dramatize the council's authority, and it attaches naturally to a council already revered as Spirit-guided. That is the whole of the support: the story is fitting. There is no independent attestation of the event itself.

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The bare frame is historical — Nicaea met in 325 and was held to have acted under the Spirit's guidance — and a single late source does report the ordeal. The legend is not invented out of nothing; it is a pious elaboration grafted onto a real council.

What fails.

  1. One source, and a very late one. The ordeal is recorded only by the Synodicon Vetus, compiled around AD 900 — almost six centuries after the council. Because the story is found nowhere earlier, there is no way to know whether it rests on any older tradition or is the epitomist's own embellishment. A miracle of this magnitude, unrecorded by every fourth- and fifth-century writer who described Nicaea at length, surfacing first in a tenth-century digest, has every mark of legend.4

  2. The source is unreliable on its own terms. The Synodicon Vetus is studded with details attested in no other record and reports as historical a number of councils that scholars doubt ever met. A document that invents synods is no warrant for a miracle at a real one; §35 cannot bear the weight placed on it.5

  3. The contemporary record contradicts the result. If the inspired books had miraculously sorted themselves at Nicaea, the canon would have been closed in 325. Yet Eusebius of Caesarea — present at the council — writes after it with the canon still open, sorting the books into "acknowledged," "disputed," and "spurious" (Church History 3.25); and Athanasius, also present, in 367 still frames listing the books as his own labor "of necessity," handed down rather than decreed. A divine verdict on every book in 325 is incompatible with the men who were there treating the limits of Scripture as unsettled long afterward.6

  4. The council's own acts are silent. The twenty surviving canons of Nicaea and the synodal letter concern the Arian and Melitian schisms, the date of Easter, and clerical discipline — none mentions the canon of Scripture, still less a miraculous ordeal to fix it. The earliest detailed accounts of the council know nothing of it.7

  5. The transmission chain explains the legend's reach, not its truth. The story owes its modern fame to an accident of printing: a single Greek manuscript carried west by Darmasius, edited by Pappus in 1601, and from that edition relayed by Voltaire and the freethinkers to the present. The recurring citation "Pappus, in his Synodicon" misremembers a seventeenth-century Lutheran editor as the source of a ninth-century Greek tale — a chain that accounts for how the anecdote spread, while adding nothing to the question of whether it happened.8

Assessment

Unsupported. The miraculous altar-ordeal — inspired books rising to the altar, spurious ones sinking beneath — has no historical basis. It is reported by a single source compiled around AD 900, nearly six centuries after Nicaea, a work otherwise known for unique-to-itself details and for councils that never met; it is unknown to every contemporary witness and to the council's own acts; and its result, a canon closed by divine sign in 325, is flatly contradicted by Eusebius and Athanasius, both present, who go on treating the canon as open. The anecdote is best understood as a late Byzantine embellishment of an already-revered council, which an accident of sixteenth- and seventeenth-century printing (Darmasius → Pappus, 1601) carried into Western letters, where Voltaire and later freethinkers relayed it as the picturesque "miracle" form of the broader — and equally unsupported — legend that Nicaea fixed the canon.
