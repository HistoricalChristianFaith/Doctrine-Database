Were the infancy gospels suppressed — banned, voted out, gathered up and burned?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: unsupported — the popular framing that the infancy gospels and other New Testament apocrypha (the Infancy Gospel of Thomas, the Protoevangelium of James, and their kin) were suppressed — outlawed, voted out, "gathered up and burned" by church or empire — has no basis. The documented history is gradual non-reception: from the second century on the proto-orthodox never widely received these texts as apostolic, and the formal exclusion-lists, when they come (Innocent I in 405, the Decretum Gelasianum c. 500), say the books are "not to be received," not that they be destroyed. The one real decree-and-bonfire near Nicaea burned a heretic's doctrinal treatises (Arius's), not the apocryphal gospels — and even that failed; meanwhile the infancy gospels went on circulating freely for a thousand years, the Protoevangelium surviving in 140+ Greek manuscripts and a dozen languages. A milder, true claim survives — they were never received as Scripture · back to timeline · popular form: Dan Brown, The Da Vinci Code (2003)

Related arguments: the Nicene-canon legend — see also did Nicaea fix the canon? (the popular legend) and the altar-ordeal miracle

An argument page weighs a single sub-claim adduced in support of a main interpretation — a proof-text, a parallel, a historical thesis — and renders an adversarial assessment of the interpretation it serves. Unlike a person-detail page it sits across the timeline rather than at one date. This argument takes the popular notion that the gospels left out of the New Testament — and the infancy gospels in particular — were actively suppressed: banned, voted down, gathered up and burned. It is the twin, on the New Testament side, of the legend that the canon was fixed in one stroke at Nicaea. Every factual claim is sourced; the proponents' own words are quoted verbatim.

The claim

"More than eighty gospels were considered for the New Testament, and yet only a relative few were chosen for inclusion — Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John among them. … Constantine commissioned and financed a new Bible, which omitted those gospels that spoke of Christ's human traits and embellished those gospels that made him godlike. The earlier gospels were outlawed, gathered up and burned."

— Dan Brown, The Da Vinci Code (New York: Doubleday, 2003), the mouth of "Sir Leigh Teabing," ch. 55.1 The interpretation is that the writings outside the New Testament were not merely passed over but actively put down — outlawed by authority, the surviving copies seized and destroyed. In the popular telling the infancy gospels are the textbook casualties: the stories of the boy Jesus and of Mary's childhood are described as "books that didn't make it into the Bible," "banned from the Bible," removed by the same suppressing hand. The image bundles three assertions, each checkable: that a council or emperor banned these texts; that the copies were destroyed; and that this is why they are not in the Bible.

Who advances it

The bestselling modern form is Dan Brown's, where the apocryphal gospels are "outlawed, gathered up and burned" by Constantine — the same one-stroke imperial act the popular legend supposes fixed the canon at Nicaea. Beneath the novel runs a softer, pervasive register in popular religious media: the infancy gospels and their kin are routinely titled the books that were "banned from the Bible," with the implication of an authority that did the banning — as in the very headline under which the Infancy Gospel of Thomas is popularly discussed, "Why It Was Banned from the Bible."2 The scholarship under that headline tells a different story: Bart Ehrman, whose work supplies much of the popular interest in "the books that didn't make it into the Bible," is explicit that there was no bonfire and no single banning vote — the canon formed by a long, uneven process of reception — and Ehrman is among those who flatly reject Brown's account of Constantine and the gospels.3 So the suppression framing lives mostly at the popular surface; it is the headline, not the history.

The evidence for

The kernel that makes "suppression" sound historical. Each ingredient has a real counterpart. The proto-orthodox did reject these writings by name and in writing: Eusebius brackets the gospels published under apostolic names among "the fictions of heretics," Innocent I lists the pseudo-apostolic books and pronounces them "not only to be rejected but also condemned," and the Decretum Gelasianum runs out some sixty rejected apocrypha to be shunned in church.4 And there was a real decree-and-bonfire in the immediate aftermath of Nicaea: Constantine ordered the writings of the heresiarch Arius sought out and burned, on pain of death to anyone caught concealing them.5 From genuine rejection-lists and a genuine imperial book-burning, the legend extrapolates a single suppression of the apocryphal gospels — banned, seized, and burned.

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The infancy gospels really were excluded. They were judged late (mid-second century and after), non-apostolic, and — in the case of the Infancy Gospel of Thomas, with its boy Jesus who withers and kills those who cross him — theologically off; the proto-orthodox never received them as Scripture, and by the fifth and sixth centuries Western authorities were naming them on lists of books to be refused. The reading is not spun from nothing. It is spun from this.

What fails, point by point.

  1. The exclusion was non-reception, not a ban — and the formal lists say so in their own title. The sharpest exclusion document, the Decretum Gelasianum, is headed De libris recipiendis et non recipiendis — "On books to be received and not to be received." Its operative verb is recipere: these books are not to be received (read in church, owned as Scripture), not that they be hunted down and destroyed. Innocent I's verdict is the same register — the pseudo-apostolic writings are "to be rejected and condemned," i.e. refused, not burned. And these documents are late: Innocent writes in 405, the Decretum is South-Gallic/Frankish of around 500 — centuries after the texts first circulated. They record a non-reception already long settled; they do not enact a suppression.6

  2. The one real bonfire near Nicaea burned a heretic's treatises, not the apocryphal gospels — and it failed. Constantine's order to burn books was aimed at Arius's doctrinal works, the writings of a condemned heresiarch, with the death penalty for concealment — and it is the single documented imperial book-burning of the period. It says nothing of the infancy or apostolic-pseudonym gospels, which were never the object of any such edict. And even that targeted suppression did not take: Arian literature and Arianism flourished for decades after. No ancient source records the apocryphal gospels "outlawed, gathered up and burned" at or after Nicaea; the burning in the legend borrows its smoke from a real but different fire.7

  3. The manuscripts themselves refute the eradication picture. The Protoevangelium of James survives in upwards of 140 Greek manuscripts and was translated into Syriac, Coptic, Armenian, Georgian, Ethiopic, Latin, Arabic, and Slavonic — a dozen languages — its frequent copying the very measure of its popularity across the Christian world; the Infancy Gospel of Thomas circulated just as freely and remained popular into the Middle Ages, feeding medieval art and legend. A text that had been banned and gathered up for burning does not leave 140 copies and a dozen translations. The survival data is the footprint of free circulation outside the canon, which is the opposite of suppression.8

  4. The boundary was drawn slowly, from the second century on, by not-receiving — not voted in one stroke. The earliest reaction to infancy-gospel material is already a quiet non-reception: Irenaeus (c. 180) files the Marcosians' tale of the boy Jesus and his alphabet-teacher among the "unspeakable number of apocryphal and spurious writings, which they themselves have forged," without any machinery of a ban. A century and a half later Eusebius sorts the apostolic-pseudonym gospels (Peter, Thomas, Matthias) among writings that "no one belonging to the succession of ecclesiastical writers deemed worthy of mention." This is how the line was drawn — by generations of churchmen declining to read these books as Scripture — not by a council voting them out or an emperor torching them. There was no candle-lit chamber where some gospels were accepted and the rest burned.9

Assessment

Unsupported. The framing that the infancy gospels and the wider New Testament apocrypha were suppressed — banned, voted out, gathered up and burned — has no historical basis. They were excluded, but by gradual non-reception: never widely received as apostolic from the second century on, judged late and (for the Infancy Gospel of Thomas) theologically off, and named on the late Western rejection-lists in the language of books "not to be received," not destroyed. The one real decree-and-bonfire near Nicaea consumed a heretic's treatises, not the apocryphal gospels, and even it failed; and the gospels in question went on being copied and read for a thousand years, the Protoevangelium alone surviving in 140-odd Greek manuscripts. The true claim the legend distorts is smaller and quieter: these books were never received as Scripture. The "banned and burned" image is the Nicaea-fixed-the-canon legend in New-Testament-apocrypha dress — and, like its parent, it fails on every part that makes it dramatic. (A separate, narrower question — whether the patristic condemnations of a "Gospel of Thomas" even name the Infancy Gospel of Thomas, or the Gnostic sayings-gospel — is weighed on its own.)
