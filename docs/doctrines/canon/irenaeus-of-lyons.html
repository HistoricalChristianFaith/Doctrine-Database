Irenaeus of Lyons on the Biblical Canon

Dates: c. 180 (Against Heresies Book I; bishop of Lyons, d. c. 202) · Wikipedia · back to timeline
Summary: Irenaeus gives the earliest datable notice of the material that would later fill the Infancy Gospel of Thomas — the tale of the boy Jesus and his alphabet-teacher — and he gives it only to reject it. He files the boy-Jesus story among the Marcosians' "unspeakable number of apocryphal and spurious writings, which they themselves have forged," and brands it a "false and wicked story." This is the earliest exclusion of infancy-gospel matter from the rule of truth, roughly two centuries before any formal rejection-list. A caution sits on the witness, though: Irenaeus reports a single circulating story the Gnostics expound, not a named book — he never calls it "Thomas" or "a gospel" — so he attests the tradition's early rejection, not yet a fixed text.

Claims & evidence

The boy-Jesus alphabet tale, filed among forged "apocryphal and spurious writings" and called "false and wicked"

Besides the above [misrepresentations], they adduce an unspeakable number of apocryphal and spurious writings, which they themselves have forged, to bewilder the minds of foolish men, and of such as are ignorant of the Scriptures of truth. Among other things, they bring forward that false and wicked story which relates that our Lord, when He was a boy learning His letters, on the teacher saying to Him, as is usual, "Pronounce Alpha," replied [as He was bid], "Alpha." But when, again, the teacher bade Him say, "Beta," the Lord replied, "Do thou first tell me what Alpha is, and then I will tell thee what Beta is." This they expound as meaning that He alone knew the Unknown, which He revealed under its type Alpha.

— Against Heresies 1.20.1, ANF 1, historicalchristian.faith1 (also newadvent.org). The chapter heading is "The Apocryphal and Spurious Scriptures of the Marcosians." Irenaeus's complaint is twofold and exactly canon-relevant: the followers of Marcus (a) multiply writings — "an unspeakable number" of them — and (b) forge them, so that the boy-Jesus episode is named not as Scripture overlooked but as a fabrication that the Church's "Scriptures of truth" stand over against. The story he reports is the same alphabet-lesson scene that later anchors chapters 6 and 14 of the Infancy Gospel of Thomas; in Irenaeus it is already on the wrong side of the boundary.

Notes

  • A story, not yet a named book. Irenaeus attests the tradition, not a fixed "Gospel of Thomas." The same alpha/beta saying circulates independently in the Epistle of the Apostles (ch. 4), and the wording Irenaeus gives is closer to a remembered episode than a citation of a titled work; many scholars therefore take him to know the scene orally or as a loose Gnostic logion, not as the later infancy gospel in its fixed form.2 So his witness establishes that the boy-Jesus material was rejected from the start — not that a book called "Thomas" already existed to reject. This is why the famous patristic condemnations of "the Gospel of Thomas" cannot simply be read as condemnations of the infancy text: see the crux Which "Gospel of Thomas" did the Fathers condemn?
  • Exclusion, not suppression. Irenaeus's response is to name and refute the forgery, not to call for its destruction — the pattern of gradual non-reception that runs through the whole record, against the popular "banned and burned" framing weighed at Were the infancy gospels suppressed?
