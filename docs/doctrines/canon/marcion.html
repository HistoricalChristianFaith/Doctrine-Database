Marcion of Sinope on the Biblical Canon

Dates: c. 144 (his break with the church of Rome; d. c. 160) · Wikipedia
Summary: Marcion is the earliest person known to have published a fixed, closed list of Christian scriptures — a single edited Gospel (a shortened Luke, carrying no author's name) and ten edited letters of Paul, with the entire Old Testament, the other three Gospels, the Acts, and the Pastoral epistles cut away. On the classic thesis his truncated canon provoked the catholic church to define its own, broader one — so he belongs on this timeline not as a witness the church followed but as the antagonist it answered. Because none of his own writings survive, everything here is reconstructed from the men who wrote against him.

Claims & evidence

The single mutilated Gospel — a shortened Luke, with no author's name

Besides this, he mutilates the Gospel which is according to Luke, removing all that is written respecting the generation of the Lord, and setting aside a great deal of the teaching of the Lord … He likewise persuaded his disciples that he himself was more worthy of credit than are those apostles who have handed down the Gospel to us, furnishing them not with the Gospel, but merely a fragment of it.

— Irenaeus, Against Heresies 1.27.2, trans. A. Roberts and W. Rambaut, Ante-Nicene Fathers vol. 1 (also at New Advent).1 Writing c. 180, Irenaeus is the earliest surviving witness to describe Marcion's Gospel: a copy of Luke stripped of the nativity and of the passages tying Jesus to the Creator-God of the Old Testament. It is "merely a fragment" of the Gospel the churches read — and it is the only Gospel Marcion kept.

Marcion, on the other hand, you must know, ascribes no author to his Gospel, as if it could not be allowed him to affix a title to that from which it was no crime (in his eyes) to subvert the very body. … Now, of the authors whom we possess, Marcion seems to have singled out Luke for his mutilating process.

— Tertullian, Against Marcion 4.2, trans. P. Holmes, Ante-Nicene Fathers vol. 3 (also at New Advent).2 Tertullian, devoting the fourth of his five books against Marcion to the "Gospel of Pontus," fastens on a telling detail: Marcion's Gospel bears no evangelist's name. Against it Tertullian sets the churches' fourfold Gospel — "John and Matthew" the apostles, "Luke and Mark" the apostolic men (4.2) — and argues that the older text must be the true one: Marcion is the late "amender," and "an amender of that Gospel … first presented himself in Marcion alone" (4.4). He faults Marcion not for having a Gospel but for having only one, and a cut-down one at that.

Marcion expressly and openly used the knife, not the pen, since he made such an excision of the Scriptures as suited his own subject-matter.

— Tertullian, The Prescription Against Heretics 38, trans. P. Holmes, Ante-Nicene Fathers vol. 3 (also at New Advent).3 The single most quoted line about Marcion's method: where the Gnostic Valentinus (Tertullian says) at least "seems to use the entire volume" and twists its meaning, Marcion instead cut — excising whole books and passages outright to leave a Scripture that "suited his own subject-matter." It is the earliest description of a canon defined as much by deletion as by selection.

The ten-letter Apostolikon — an edited Paul, with the Pastorals cut

In like manner, too, he dismembered the Epistles of Paul, removing all that is said by the apostle respecting that God who made the world, to the effect that He is the Father of our Lord Jesus Christ, and also those passages from the prophetical writings which the apostle quotes, in order to teach us that they announced beforehand the coming of the Lord.

— Irenaeus, Against Heresies 1.27.2.1 Beside his one Gospel Marcion set a corpus of Paul — the Apostolikon — likewise pruned of every appeal to the Creator and the prophets. As later opponents attest and modern scholarship reconstructs it, this was a collection of ten letters: Galatians, 1–2 Corinthians, Romans, 1–2 Thessalonians, "Laodiceans" (his name for Ephesians), Colossians, Philippians, and Philemon. He left out the three Pastoral epistles (1–2 Timothy and Titus) — and, of course, Hebrews, Acts, the Catholic epistles, and the Revelation. Paul was for Marcion the one true apostle, the rest having (he held) corrupted the gospel with Judaism; so his New Testament was a Gospel and an Apostle, eleven books in all.4

The rejection of the Old Testament — and the catholic response

Marcion of Pontus succeeded him, and developed his doctrine. … But Jesus being derived from that father who is above the God that made the world … was manifested in the form of a man to those who were in Judaea, abolishing the prophets and the law, and all the works of that God who made the world.

— Irenaeus, Against Heresies 1.27.2.1 Marcion's canon was the scriptural edge of a theology: the God of the Old Testament, the just and world-making Creator, is not the Father of Jesus Christ, the higher God of pure grace. So the whole Hebrew Bible was cut away, and with it every Christian writing that leaned on it. This is why his list mattered for the canon: it forced the question the timeline otherwise opens after — why a fixed list at all, and which books? Tertullian answers it with the criterion of antiquity:

I say that my Gospel is the true one; Marcion, that his is. I affirm that Marcion's Gospel is adulterated; Marcion, that mine is. Now what is to settle the point for us, except it be that principle of time, which rules that the authority lies with that which shall be found to be more ancient?

— Tertullian, Against Marcion 4.4.2 On the classic thesis of Adolf von Harnack — sharpened by Hans von Campenhausen, who called Marcion "the creator of the Christian holy Scripture" — it was precisely this challenge that pushed the catholic church to fix and publish a broader canon of its own: a fourfold Gospel against Marcion's one, a full Pauline corpus (Pastorals included) against his ten, and the Old Testament retained as Christian Scripture. Later witnesses on this timeline — Justin's Gospels read aloud "as long as time permits" (c. 155), Irenaeus's argument that the Gospels are exactly four (c. 180) — are, on this reading, the church drawing the line Marcion had crossed. How decisive the provocation really was is debated (see the Notes).5

Notes

  • Every word here is from a hostile witness. Marcion left no surviving writings: his Antitheses, his edited Gospel (the Evangelikon), and his edited Paul (the Apostolikon) are known only through the churchmen who quoted them in order to refute them — chiefly Irenaeus and Tertullian, with later notices in Epiphanius and others. Reconstructions of exactly what he cut, and in what order his letters stood, therefore rest on adversarial reporting and are to that degree uncertain. What is not in doubt is the shape of the thing: a deliberately delimited two-part collection, one Gospel and ten letters, published as the Christian Scriptures to the exclusion of all others.
  • The provocation thesis is contested. Harnack and von Campenhausen made Marcion the catalyst of the New-Testament canon — the church defining its list in reaction to his. Others (notably John Barton) hold that the fourfold Gospel and a Pauline collection were already forming independently, so that Marcion accelerated and sharpened a process he did not begin; Barton's memorable reframing is that Marcion "was responsible not for the Christians adopting a New Testament, but for their retaining the Old." That the four-Gospel core was hardening on its own is the subject of the argument Was the fourfold Gospel already closed by c. 180? — Irenaeus's Tetramorph. Either way Marcion stands as the first closed Christian list on record.
  • He is the antagonist the other witnesses answer. Compare the sibling pages Justin Martyr (c. 155), for whom the Gospels already function as Scripture, and Irenaeus of Lyons (c. 180), who first fixes their number at four and who is also the source, above, for what Marcion cut. Set against Marcion's one Gospel and ten letters, their work reads as the church marking out the wider boundary. See the doctrine overview at The Biblical Canon.
