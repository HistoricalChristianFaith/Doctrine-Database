Clement of Alexandria on the Biblical Canon

Dates: c. 200 (head of the catechetical school at Alexandria; the Stromata belong to c. 198–203; d. c. 215) · Wikipedia
Summary: Origen's teacher, and the clearest witness that at Alexandria around 200 the category "Scripture" still had porous edges. Clement cites the Epistle of Barnabas, the Shepherd of Hermas, the letter of Clement of Rome, and the Preaching of Peter as authorities — and draws on gospels and traditions outside the four — yet he already knows and privileges "the four Gospels that have been handed down to us." He is the taproot of this page's fuzzy-edges thesis: a canon whose core is fixed while its boundary is still soft.

Claims & evidence

The Epistle of Barnabas cited as "the apostle Barnabas"

Rightly, therefore, the Apostle Barnabas says, "From the portion I have received I have done my diligence to send by little and little to you; that along with your faith you may also have perfect knowledge."

— Stromata 2.6, historicalchristian.faith (also at New Advent).1 Clement repeatedly quotes the Epistle of Barnabas as authoritative Scripture, prefacing the citations with apostolic titles — "the Apostle Barnabas," and again "the apostolic Barnabas (and he was one of the seventy and a fellow-worker of Paul)" — and following one long extract with the plain "So far Barnabas." The Epistle of Barnabas is one of the books later dropped from the canon (it stands, with the Shepherd, bound into the fourth-century Codex Sinaiticus); Clement treats its author as an apostle.

The Shepherd of Hermas quoted as divine revelation

Divinely, therefore, the power which spoke to Hermas by revelation said, "The visions and revelations are for those who are of double mind, who doubt in their hearts if these things are or are not."

— Stromata 1.29, historicalchristian.faith (also at New Advent).2 The adverb carries the weight: what "the power… said" to Hermas is introduced "divinely," as inspired speech. He cites the Shepherd the same way elsewhere — "For the power that appeared in the vision to Hermas said, 'Whatever may be revealed to you, shall be revealed'" (Stromata 2.1), and "the Shepherd, the angel of repentance, says to Hermas" (1.17) — treating a sub-apostolic book of the mid-second century as a vehicle of revelation.

The letter of Clement of Rome cited as "the Apostle Clement"

Moreover, in the Epistle to the Corinthians, the Apostle Clement also, drawing a picture of the Gnostic, says:

— Stromata 4.17, historicalchristian.faith (also at New Advent).3 The whole of Stromata 4.17 is given over to extracts from 1 Clement — the letter of Clement of Rome to Corinth — introduced as the words of "the Apostle Clement" and quoted as authority ("For it is written in the Epistle to the Corinthians…"). This is the very book Irenaeus a generation earlier had called "most powerful," and which fourth-century Codex Alexandrinus binds after the New Testament: an authority on the boundary, later left outside it.

The Preaching of Peter cited for the Lord's own words

Accordingly, in the Preaching of Peter, the Lord says to the disciples after the resurrection, "I have chosen you twelve disciples, judging you worthy of me," whom the Lord wished to be apostles, having judged them faithful, sending them into the world to the men on the earth, that they may know that there is one God…

— Stromata 6.6, historicalchristian.faith (also at New Advent).4 Clement quotes the Preaching (Kerygma) of Peter repeatedly and without embarrassment, drawing on it for the risen Lord's commission of the Twelve and for the argument that the one God was already dimly known to the Greeks — treating a second-century apocryphon as a report of Christ's own teaching.

Traditions and gospels beyond the four — Matthias and the Egyptians

It is said that Matthias also taught that one should fight the flesh and abuse it, never allowing it to give way to licentious pleasure, so that the soul might grow by faith and knowledge.

— Stromata 3.4, historicalchristian.faith (also at New Advent).5 Against the encratite Gnostics Clement argues from the Traditions of Matthias as a source worth citing, and elsewhere in the same book weighs a saying of the Lord found "in the Gospel according to the Egyptians" — the very books his pupil Origen would rank among the rejected "attempts." Clement uses them as evidence to be sifted, not as forgeries to be barred.

Yet "the four Gospels that have been handed down to us"

In the first place we have not got the saying in the four Gospels that have been handed down to us, but in the Gospel according to the Egyptians.

— Stromata 3.13, historicalchristian.faith (also at New Advent).6 This is the decisive line for Clement's place on the timeline. However freely he cites Barnabas, Hermas, Clement of Rome, the Preaching of Peter, and even the Egyptian gospel, he already draws a settled distinction: there are "the four Gospels that have been handed down to us," and there is everything else. He weighs the Salome saying and rejects it precisely because it is absent from those four and present only in the Gospel of the Egyptians. The fourfold-Gospel core is fixed; the outer ring of "Scripture" is still soft.

Eusebius on Clement's lost Hypotyposeis — Scripture with the disputed books left in

To sum up briefly, he has given in the Hypotyposes abridged accounts of all canonical Scripture, not omitting the disputed books,—I refer to Jude and the other Catholic epistles, and Barnabas and the so-called Apocalypse of Peter.

— Eusebius, Church History 6.14.1, historicalchristian.faith (also at New Advent).7 Clement's Hypotyposeis (Outlines) survives only in fragments, but Eusebius read it whole and reports that Clement wrote commentary-summaries on "all canonical Scripture" — explicitly including the disputed Catholic epistles and Barnabas and the Apocalypse of Peter, handled side by side with the undoubted books. In the same passage Eusebius preserves Clement's traditions that "the Epistle to the Hebrews is the work of Paul, and that it was written to the Hebrews in the Hebrew language; but that Luke translated it carefully and published it for the Greeks," and, "as to the order of the Gospels," that "the Gospels containing the genealogies… were written first," with John "last of all… composed a spiritual Gospel." Even in his fullest canon-work, Clement's list of authorities runs past the eventual boundary.

Notes

  • The taproot of the fuzzy-edges thesis. Clement is the canon timeline's plainest single witness that around 200 the category "Scripture" still had soft edges: he cites Barnabas, the Shepherd, 1 Clement, and the Preaching of Peter as authorities — all later excluded — while already privileging the handed-down four Gospels. That double face is exactly the pattern weighed at Was the New Testament canon settled gradually, contested at its edges? (assessment: sound): core fixed early, edges contested late.
  • Teacher and pupil. Clement's successor at Alexandria, Origen, inherits both halves of his position but hardens the boundary: where Clement draws freely on the Gospel of the Egyptians and the Traditions of Matthias, Origen names those same books among the mere "attempts" the church did not approve, affirming that "the church of God has approved only the four." The soft ring Clement still worked within is the ring his pupil begins to close.
  • Not a rival canon, a wider usage. Clement nowhere lists the books or sets a formal rule; his is the fluid citation-practice of a teacher who quotes whatever edifies. That is why he matters here as evidence, not as a legislator: he shows what "Scripture" could still stretch to at Alexandria a century before the first exact catalogues — the porous usage later scholarship (Metzger, Bruce) points to when it describes the canon's boundary as fixed at the center and long unsettled at the rim.
