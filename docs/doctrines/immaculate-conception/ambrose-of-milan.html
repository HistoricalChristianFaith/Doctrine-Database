Ambrose of Milan on the Immaculate Conception

Dates: c. 389 (Ambrose, bishop of Milan c. 374–397; the Marian purity texts are in his Exposition of the Gospel of Luke, completed c. 385–389, and the treatise On the Instruction of a Virgin of c. 391–392) · Wikipedia
Summary: Ambrose is the Western patristic text later Latin theology — Anselm, Scotus, and finally the 1854 definition — treats as its own anchor, for he praises Mary as a virgin “free from every stain of sin.” But the praise is of her purity by grace (per gratiam), a settled gift of holiness, not a stainless nature or conception; and his own doctrine of original sin — inherited by every child of Adam born in the ordinary way, so that being immaculate “from the beginning… is impossible for human nature” and Christ “alone is without sin” — cuts squarely against reading the later doctrine back into him. He is the textbook case of a claim-(a) text (Mary’s sinless life) later pressed into service for claim (b) (freedom from original sin at conception).

Claims & evidence

The apologists’ anchor: a virgin “free from every stain of sin”

Take me up not from Sarah but from Mary, that she may be an incorrupt virgin — but a virgin through grace, free from every stain of sin (ut incorrupta sit virgo, sed virgo per gratiam ab omni integra labe peccati).

— Exposition of the Gospel of Luke, II.56. This is the single line Latin Immaculists cite most often, and the strongest Marian-purity text in Ambrose. But read it as Ambrose wrote it: the operative words are per gratiam — Mary is kept pure by grace, a gift bestowed on her, not by a stainless nature or a conception exempt from Adam’s inheritance. The sentence sets Mary against Sarah (who bore Isaac in the ordinary way) to underline the miracle of a virgin birth free of the corruption of concupiscence; it says nothing of the instant of her own conception. It is a rich witness to claim (a) — her purity of life — that later apologetics read forward into claim (b).1

“Full of grace… which no other deserved” (Luke 1:28)

This greeting was preserved for Mary alone. For she alone is said to be full of grace, who alone attained grace which no other deserved, so that she might be filled with grace by the author.

— Exposition of the Gospel of Luke, II.9, on Luke 1:28, source. Here is the grace-language the whole later case is built on — the singular fullness of grace given to Mary, tied to Gabriel’s kecharitōmenē. But Ambrose’s own emphasis is that this grace is attained and bestowed (“filled with grace by the author”), not a native immunity; the phrase is expounded for the divine maternity and Mary’s pre-eminence, not for freedom from original sin at conception — a reading the “full of grace” argument finds the verse cannot bear as strict proof.2

Against reading (b) back into Ambrose: original sin touches all but Christ

Ambrose held a robust doctrine of inherited sin — the very premise every later objector (Bernard, Bonaventure, Aquinas) would raise against the Immaculate Conception. Three of his own statements pull hard against the later reading:

Thus, it is not immaculate from the beginning; for this is impossible for human nature: but through the grace of God and its own quality, because it no longer sins, it becomes as if it were immaculate.

— Exposition of the Gospel of Luke, I.17, on the sinlessness of Zechariah and Elizabeth. Immaculateness “from the beginning” is flatly “impossible for human nature”; holiness is a cleansing worked by grace upon a nature that begins defiled — exactly the account of purity Ambrose applies to Mary (per gratiam), and exactly what claim (b) denies.3

He is one who does not have a second; for He alone is without sin (solus enim sine peccato), alone without assistance, who says: I looked, and there was no one to help.

— On the Instruction of a Virgin, on Christ’s uniqueness, source. Christ “alone is without sin” — a formula that, taken strictly, leaves even Mary among those needing the cleansing grace Ambrose everywhere ascribes to her.4

…he did not even think it necessary to assert that he himself was exempt from the stain of original sin… Finally, so that he would also initiate the benefit of redemption from his own ancestors; lest anyone think that the stain of his origin could be an impediment to virtue.

— Exposition of the Gospel of Luke, III.35, on the sinners in Christ’s genealogy, source. Ambrose reads the “stain of original sin” (originalis peccati labem) as the common inheritance of the whole line from which Christ took flesh — transmitted by ordinary generation, healed only by the redemption Christ brings. This is the Augustinian premise in seed; it is precisely what makes the later doctors ask how Mary alone could owe the Redeemer nothing.5

Notes

  • (a) vs (b) — what Ambrose does and does not say. Ambrose lavishes on Mary the language of unstained purity, and the East and West alike later drew on him; but scholarship is explicit that he “never stretched his claim… as far as the Immaculate Conception.” His governing word is per gratiam: Mary’s freedom from every stain is a grace given, coherent with — not a denial of — the inherited stain his own theology says every child of Adam contracts. The standard reference itself, cataloguing the Latin witnesses, concedes the developed doctrine required a later theological precision “never formally or explicitly” reached in the patristic age. Ambrose is thus the miniature of this whole timeline’s thesis: a genuine (a)-text over-read as (b).6
  • The Catholic Encyclopedia also cites Ambrose’s Sermo 22 on Psalm 118 as calling Mary “incorrupt, a virgin immune through grace from every stain of sin” — the same per gratiam formula, and the same claim (a).7
  • Not to be confused with “Ambrosiaster.” The anonymous fourth-century Latin commentator conventionally called “Ambrosiaster” (“would-be Ambrose”) is a different author. His importance to this family of doctrines is his Latin of Romans 5:12 — in quo omnes peccaverunt, “in whom all sinned” — which fed Augustine’s doctrine of inherited original sin. He makes no claim about Mary’s sinlessness and is not a witness here.
  • Ambrose appears on this wiki also on Mary as the New Eve, the perpetual virginity of Mary, and Theotokos; his pupil Augustine carries the Latin doctrine of original sin forward, giving the later opposition to the Immaculate Conception its decisive premise.
