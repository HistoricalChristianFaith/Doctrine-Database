Thomas Aquinas on the Immaculate Conception

Dates: c. 1270 (Summa Theologiae III) · 1225–1274 · Wikipedia
Summary: Aquinas gives the medieval opposition to the Immaculate Conception its most influential statement. Mary, he holds, was sanctified in the womb after animation, her soul having first contracted original sin; and an immaculate conception would be “derogatory to the dignity of Christ” as universal Saviour, since then Mary alone would have owed him no redemption.

Claims & evidence

Mary contracted original sin and was sanctified after animation, cleansed before birth

the soul of the Blessed Virgin did indeed contract original sin, but was cleansed therefrom before her birth from the womb.

— Summa Theologiae III, q. 27, a. 2, ad 3, source. The sanctification, Aquinas argues in the body of the article, “cannot be understood as having taken place before animation”: sanctification is a cleansing from original sin, and only a rational creature can be the subject of sin, so before the soul is infused there is nothing to cleanse. Had Mary been sanctified before animation, “she could never have incurred the stain of original sin: and thus she would not have needed redemption and salvation which is by Christ.” It “remains, therefore, that the Blessed Virgin was sanctified after animation” — and, he adds in the previous article, in the womb before her birth.1

An immaculate conception would be “derogatory to the dignity of Christ”

If the soul of the Blessed Virgin had never incurred the stain of original sin, this would be derogatory to the dignity of Christ, by reason of His being the universal Saviour of all.

— Summa Theologiae III, q. 27, a. 2, ad 2, source. This is the heart of the objection: Christ is “the Saviour of all men” (1 Timothy 4:10), and to save is to redeem from sin either present or contracted; if Mary had never contracted the stain, she would not be among the “all” whom Christ redeemed, which Aquinas judged an intolerable exception. It is precisely this argument that Duns Scotus would answer — not by denying that Mary needed redemption, but by holding that to be preserved from contracting sin is the highest form of being redeemed.2

Notes

  • Like Bernard and Bonaventure, Aquinas affirms claim (a) — Mary’s sanctification in the womb and freedom from actual sin — while denying claim (b), the immaculate conception. Because of his towering authority, the Dominican order held to his position long after the Franciscans had embraced Scotus’s, and the two schools disputed the point down to the eve of the 1854 definition.
  • Aquinas also appears on this wiki as a witness on the perpetual virginity timeline — a distinct Marian question, which he affirmed. His denial of the Immaculate Conception concerns only claim (b), original sin at conception.
  • Aquinas’s objection and Scotus’s reply are weighed off-timeline as a pair: Does the Immaculate Conception contradict Christ’s universal redemption? (assessment: contested — near-decisive on Aquinas’s own definition of redemption, but resting on it) and Does Scotus’s “preservative redemption” dissolve the objection? (assessment: plausible).
