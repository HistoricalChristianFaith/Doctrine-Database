Proclus of Constantinople on the Immaculate Conception

Dates: c. 430 (Homily 1, On the Holy Virgin Theotokos, preached in the Great Church of Constantinople, by tradition before Nestorius; Proclus archbishop of Constantinople 434–446) · Wikipedia
Summary: Proclus is one of the two Ephesus-era Greek preachers whose lavish “spotless” Marian rhetoric is sometimes read forward toward the Immaculate Conception. His famous homily — the one that lit the Nestorian fuse — heaps on Mary the imagery of unstained purity: she is “the spotless flesh of the Virgin,” “the awesome loom of the divine economy,” “the purest fleece.” The standard reference presses one phrase further, rendering him as saying Mary “was formed without any stain.” But the register is Christological-typological praise of Mary’s bodily purity as the fit vessel of the Incarnation — claim (a) — not a claim about freedom from original sin at her conception. He belongs on this timeline as a high-water mark of the Ephesus-era purity vocabulary the Latin West would later stretch toward claim (b).

Claims & evidence

The purity imagery: “spotless flesh,” the “awesome loom,” the “purest fleece”

… the awesome loom of the divine economy … the spotless flesh of the Virgin … the purest fleece … the untarnished vessel of virginity.

— Homily 1, On the Holy Virgin Theotokos, trans. Nicholas Constas, source. The homily’s governing image is a loom: the Virgin’s flesh is the “wool” from which the Word wove the garment of his humanity, so the praise of her purity is a praise of the material of the Incarnation. Mary is “the pure one,” kept “without pollution” and “without defilement,” “the bars of virginity unbroken.” This is a rhetoric of bodily integrity and fitness to bear God — typological, not a statement about the metaphysics of her soul’s origin.1

“Formed without any stain” — the phrase pressed toward (b)

… she was formed without any stain.

— Laudatio in S. Dei Genitricis ortum I.3, as rendered in the Catholic Encyclopedia’s catalogue of witnesses to “the absolute purity of Mary.”2 This is the single line an Immaculist reading leans on. But two cautions apply. First, “without any stain” (like the Latin sine macula) is the common patristic language of purity generally, and in Proclus it sits inside a sustained praise of Mary’s bodily spotlessness as Theotokos, not a discussion of inherited guilt. Second, the phrase reaches us through the reference work rather than a primary text we can link; the homily’s dominant, verifiable imagery (the loom, the fleece, the “spotless flesh”) is typological praise, and nothing in it raises the question the Immaculate Conception answers — whether Mary contracted original sin at the instant of her own conception. Verdict: (a) / rhetorical purity, no original-sin claim.

Notes

  • (a), not (b). Proclus praises Mary’s spotlessness in the highest Ephesus-era terms, but as the fit and undefiled vessel of the Word — the theme the whole homily exists to celebrate against Nestorius. He nowhere raises, still less resolves, the later Western question of freedom from original sin at conception. He is a witness to Mary’s purity (claim (a)), read forward by later apologetics toward claim (b).
  • Proclus appears on this wiki also as a witness on the Theotokos timeline, where the same Homily 1 is treated for its role in the Nestorian controversy. His fellow Ephesus-era preacher Theodotus of Ancyra shows the same “spotless” register, with one prenatal-consecration touch that leans a step further toward (b).
  • The Greek East’s own “prepurified / all-holy” strand — from Gregory of Nazianzus’s prokathartheisa to John of Damascus — is the vocabulary that comes nearest to (b) in words; it is weighed on the prokathartheisa argument page.
