The Life of the Virgin (attributed to Maximus the Confessor) on the Immaculate Conception

Dates: attributed to the early 7th century (if the work of Maximus the Confessor, c. 580–662) — but its authorship and date are genuinely contested: the text survives only in Old Georgian, its earliest manuscript is 10th-century, and a leading study argues the Greek prototype should itself be dated no earlier than the 10th century (see Notes) · Wikipedia
Summary: The earliest complete biography of Mary, the Life of the Virgin saturates its subject with Byzantine purity-titles — “all-holy, immaculate, and most blessed Theotokos,” “ever immaculate: before birth, in birth, and after birth.” Cited by some as an early Eastern anticipation of the Immaculate Conception, it in fact tells the other way when read closely: its one stretch of explicit “conceived without the corruption of sin” reasoning is applied to Christ, not to Mary; its narrative of Mary’s own conception is the plain miracle of the barren Anne, with no claim of preservation from original sin; and its translator, Stephen Shoemaker, is at pains to warn against retrojecting later Latin Marian dogma onto it. A proto-(b) witness at most — and a doubly cautious one, given the contest over whether it is even a 7th-century text.

Claims & evidence

The cascade of purity-titles — “all-holy, immaculate, and most blessed”

…let us hymn, praise, and glorify the all-holy, immaculate, and most blessed Theotokos and ever-virgin Mary.

— Life of the Virgin, §1, trans. Stephen J. Shoemaker (Yale, 2012), p. 35; the dedication likewise praises “the story of her immaculate and blessed life.”1 The register is the Eastern Panagia (“all-holy”) and Achrantos (“undefiled”) idiom — honorific praise of Mary’s sanctity. Where the text calls her “a virgin ever immaculate: before birth, in birth, and after birth” (§21), the “ever immaculate” is a formula of her perpetual virginity, not a statement about the instant of her conception.2

The “without the corruption of sin” reasoning is about Christ, not Mary

For the fruits of other women were under the curse from the original sin of Adam and Eve, and by carnal marriage and the corruption of sin they entered into the world. But the fruit of your womb alone is blessed, for he was conceived neither by the seed of a man nor by the corruption of sin… And he committed no sin at all.

— Life of the Virgin, Annunciation section, trans. Shoemaker, p. 56.3 This is the single passage in which the Life reasons explicitly about a conception free from “the original sin of Adam and Eve” — and its subject is the virginal conception of Christ, contrasted with “the fruits of other women.” It is the opposite of the object of the 1854 dogma, which concerns the conception of Mary. When the text turns to Mary’s own conception, it simply follows the Protevangelium: “from the barren Anna was born Mary the illuminator of all” — the miracle of a sterile mother, with no assertion that Mary was preserved from inherited sin at it.

Mary “consecrated immaculately to God” — formed in holiness from the first

…I have been consecrated immaculately to God… [she] was trained from the beginning in complete holiness and purity of soul and body… no desire of passion at all had come into her heart.

— Life of the Virgin, §23, trans. Shoemaker, p. 53.4 This is the text’s strongest gesture toward an original and lifelong purity — Mary formed “from the beginning” in “complete holiness… of soul and body,” untouched by passion. Read forward it is congenial to claim (a) in its fullest form (a sinless life from the start) and adjacent to (b); but it speaks of Mary’s upbringing and disposition, not of her freedom from original guilt at the moment her soul began to exist.

Notes

  • The translator’s own caution. Shoemaker devotes an excursus to warning against reading this Greek text through later Western Marian categories: the Life’s “profoundly incarnational soteriology” predates and is “altogether foreign to” Anselm’s juridical account of the atonement, so its exalted Marian language should not be recast in the mould of the medieval Latin dogmas. His explicit target is the Coredemptrix / Mediatrix retrojection; the same caution applies, by clear extension, to reading the Life as a Byzantine prototype of the Immaculate Conception — a phrase that appears nowhere in the work.5
  • The (a)/(b) line. Like Sophronius and John of Damascus, the Life belongs to the Greek “all-holy” chain: it heaps immaculate/undefiled honorifics on Mary and forms her in holiness from the first. But it nowhere teaches her preservation from original sin at her own conception — the object of claim (b). Indeed it is weaker for (b) than either Greek father: it reserves its explicit sin-free-conception language for Christ, and narrates Mary’s conception as the plain Protevangelium miracle. Proto-(b) at most, and cautiously so.
  • Authorship and date are contested. Michel van Esbroeck, who edited the Georgian (1986), and Shoemaker, who translated it (2012), assign the work to Maximus the Confessor in the early 7th century. Against this, Phil Booth (Journal of Theological Studies, 2015) argues that “Maximus cannot be the author… and… it cannot date to the seventh century”: no Greek manuscript survives, no characteristically Maximian themes appear, and no witness attests the whole Life before the second half of the 10th century. Maximos Constas likewise dates the Greek prototype to the 10th century; Mary Cunningham defends an earlier date while conceding the attribution to Maximus is doubtful. The earliest actual manuscript (Tbilisi A-40) is 10th-century, so that is the undisputed floor; anything earlier rests on the contested attribution — which is why this witness carries less probative weight than its early-7th-century billing would suggest.6
