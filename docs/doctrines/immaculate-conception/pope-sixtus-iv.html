Pope Sixtus IV on the Immaculate Conception

Dates: 1476–1483 (Cum praeexcelsa; Grave nimis) · reigned 1471–1484 · Wikipedia
Summary: Sixtus IV, a Franciscan, did for the dispute what no theologian could: he froze it. He approved the feast of Mary’s conception (1476) — while carefully declining to define the conception as “immaculate” — and then forbade either party from branding the other heretical (1483), on the express ground that the Church had not yet decided the question.

Claims & evidence

Cum praeexcelsa (1476): approve the feast, define nothing

…we consider it just, even a duty, that all the faithful of Christ for the miraculous conception of this immaculate Virgin give praise and thanks to Almighty God…

— Cum praeexcelsa, 28 February 1476.1 The wording is precisely calibrated. Sixtus calls Mary herself “immaculate,” but attaches to the conception the adjective “miraculous” (mirifica), not “immaculate.” He approves the feast, its Mass and Office, and grants indulgences to those who attend — but he defines nothing about original sin, and leaves the theological dispute exactly where he found it.

Grave nimis (1483): silence both sides

…subjecting to the same penalty and censure those who shall presume to assert… that the glorious Virgin Mary was conceived with original sin… when it was not yet decided by the Roman Church and the Apostolic See.

— Grave nimis, 4 September 1483.2 The bull rebukes preachers who called the Immaculist opinion a mortal sin or heresy — and then forbids the Immaculists to return the charge against those who held Mary conceived in original sin. The two prohibitions rest on different grounds, and the asymmetry matters: Sixtus condemned the anti-Immaculist accusation as false (the Church herself kept the feast), but forbade the Immaculist counter-charge simply because the question had not yet been decided. It is a genuine freeze, not a covert definition — and it held the controversy in suspension until 1854.

Notes

  • Sixtus does not testify for or against claim (b); he is the referee, not a party. His refusal to let either school anathematize the other is the reason the dispute could continue for centuries as a permitted difference of opinion between Dominicans and Franciscans rather than a matter of orthodoxy.
  • It was Sixtus who fixed the Western feast on 8 December. The careful Cum praeexcelsa wording — “miraculous” conception, not “immaculate” conception — is the papacy declining, deliberately, to prejudge the point Trent would likewise leave open in 1546.
