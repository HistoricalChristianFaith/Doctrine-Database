Francis of Mayronis on the Immaculate Conception

Dates: c. 1288–c. 1328 · defended the doctrine at Paris c. 1320–21; master of theology 1323 · Catholic Encyclopedia
Summary: Francis of Mayronis (Franciscus de Mayronis, “princeps Scotistarum”), a leading early disciple of Scotus, became with Peter Auriol one of the doctrine’s “most fervent champions” in the generation after their master. Where Scotus had advanced preservative redemption tentatively — the “more probable” view, pending the Church’s judgment — Mayronis threw that caution to the wind and argued forcefully and unqualifiedly for Mary’s privilegium, her exemption from original sin. His distinctive contribution is twofold: he extended Scotus’s doctrine of Christ’s absolute predestination to Mary, and he read original sin through the categories of Roman law — an inherited legal liability from which a singular exemption could be juridically granted.

Claims & evidence

He dropped Scotus’s caution and pressed the doctrine as a certainty

Notably, Francis speculated beyond the bounds set by his doctor, throwing Duns’ caution into the wind on the question of the Immaculate Conception. Duns employed language that has been best classified as “restrained and prudent” regarding the controversial opinio theologica. Diversely, Francis gained notoriety for extolling this doctrine, arguing forcefully for Mary’s divine “privilege” or exemption from original sin.

— Christiaan Kappes, “Francis of Meyronnes and the Immaculate Conception,” in Medieval Franciscan Approaches to the Virgin Mary (Brill, 2019), pp. 196–197, source. Where Auriol had held only that the preservation “can be held without danger to faith,” Mayronis asserted it — advocacy pushed toward certainty. This is exactly why the standard reference ranks him, with Auriol, among the doctrine’s “most fervent champions”: “Following in the footsteps of their own Duns Scotus, the learned Petrus Aureolus and Franciscus de Mayronis became the most fervent champions of the doctrine, although their older teachers (St. Bonaventure included) had been opposed to it.”1

Predestination and Roman law: his own mechanisms for the exemption

Two moves are Mayronis’s own, not restatements of Scotus. First, he (with John de Bassolis) was among the first to apply Scotus’s doctrine of the absolute predestination of Christ to Mary — grounding her exemption in the same eternal decree by which the Incarnate Word was willed, so that Mary is predestined immaculate together with her Son. Second, he is credited as the first schoolman to deploy the categories of Roman civil law systematically in this exegesis, treating original sin as an inherited legal liability or imputed debt — a patrimony rather than a personal fault — on which Mary’s exemption is construed as a juridically granted privilegium. This legal-privilege framing is distinct from Scotus’s mediator-argument.1 ⚠ unverified: Mayronis’s own treatise and sermons on the Conception survive in Pedro de Alva y Astorga’s Monumenta… pro Immaculata Conceptione (Louvain, 1665) and in J. Jurić’s critical study (Studi Francescani 51, 1954), neither yet consulted directly here; the account of his arguments rests for now on modern scholarship (Kappes).

Notes

  • The Scotist who out-ran Scotus. Mayronis called himself Scotus’s follower (schola nostra) and was hailed by later Scotists as princeps Scotistarum; his epithets were Doctor Illuminatus and Doctor Acutus. On the Immaculate Conception, though, he went past his master — asserting as a certainty what Scotus had left as the more probable, and building it out with predestination and juridical arguments Scotus had not applied to Mary.
  • Dating. Modern scholarship gives c. 1288–c. 1328 (the older Catholic Encyclopedia’s “c. 1280…d. 1327” is superseded). He heard Scotus lecture at Paris c. 1304–07, defended Mary at Paris c. 1320–21, and was made master of theology in 1323 at the request of Pope John XXII.
