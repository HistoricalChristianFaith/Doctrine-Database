John of Montson and the Paris condemnation of 1387

Dates: 1387 (condemnation at Paris) · Wikipedia
Summary: John of Montson (Juan de Monzón / Johannes de Montesono), an Aragonese Dominican and Paris master of theology, taught that Mary was conceived in original sin like every other child of Adam and that the immaculist opinion was heretical. The Paris theology faculty condemned fourteen of his propositions; the Bishop of Paris excommunicated defenders of them; Montson appealed to Avignon and then fled. The Order of Preachers was expelled from the University. The sharpest maculist statement of the medieval dispute and the institutional turning point that hardened Paris into an immaculist stronghold. Denies claim (b).

Claims & evidence

The thesis: Mary conceived in original sin, and the contrary view heretical

Montson lectured at the Dominican convent of St-Jacques and obtained his mastership in theology at Paris in 1387. Taking the Dominican side of the Franciscan–Dominican quarrel, he taught that the Blessed Virgin was conceived in original sin like all Adam’s descendants — and, going further, that the opposite (immaculist) opinion was heretical. It was this second charge, not the maculist view itself, that the officially neutral faculty demanded he retract. This is the plainest denial of claim (b) put on the record by a member of the Order of Preachers; defenders of the doctrine later observed that no Dominican writer is cited as having denied Mary’s privilege in these terms before 1387.

The condemnation of the fourteen propositions

The faculty of theology condemned fourteen propositions drawn from his lectures, warned him privately and then publicly to retract, and when he refused referred the matter to Pierre d’Orgemont, Bishop of Paris, “who promulgated a decree of excommunication against all who should defend the forbidden theses.”1

The appeal, the flight, and the delegates

Montson appealed to the Avignon pope Clement VII, who summoned both him and the university’s delegates. Foreseeing defeat, and despite an order to remain at Avignon under pain of excommunication, he secretly withdrew into Aragon and thence to Sicily, transferring his allegiance to Clement’s Roman rival Urban VI. Among the university delegates who pressed the case were Pierre d’Ailly (whom Denifle judged the likely drafter of the faculty’s condemnatory letters) and the young Jean Gerson. Tellingly, they argued not on the doctrine’s truth but on the university’s right to police its own teachers:

John of Montson appealed to Pope Clement VII at Avignon, and Pierre d’Ailly, Gerson and the other university delegates, while they personally supported the doctrine of the Immaculate Conception, were content to rest their case upon the legal rights of the university to test in its own way its theological teachers.

— Encyclopædia Britannica (1911), “Gerson, John.”2

The Dominicans expelled from Paris

As a direct consequence of the affair the Order of Preachers was expelled from the University of Paris and kept out for roughly a decade and a half, readmitted only in the early fifteenth century (c. 1403 by the usual reckoning).3 The episode fixed Paris — the leading theology faculty of Latin Christendom — as an immaculist bastion, and it is the immediate root of the university’s 1497 oath requiring every member to swear to defend the Immaculate Conception.

Notes

  • The condemnation is best dated to 1387 (the year of Montson’s mastership and of the faculty’s censure), agreeing across the Catholic Encyclopedia’s biographical article, the 1911 Britannica, and modern reference works. The stray parenthetical “(John of Montesono, 1373)” in one encyclopedia article conflicts with every other source and appears to be an error.
  • The observation that no Dominican is cited denying the privilege before 1387 is a commonplace of the doctrine’s later defenders (it traces to the eighteenth-century Thomist Charles-René Billuart) and is reported here on secondary authority, not verified against a primary text.
  • The bishop’s name appears as both “Pierre d’Orgemont” and “Pierre Orgement” — the same man. The pope of the appeal is the Avignon claimant Clement VII (not Clement VIII). The “~14 years / readmitted c. 1403” span for the Dominican exclusion rests on secondary reporting and is given cautiously.
