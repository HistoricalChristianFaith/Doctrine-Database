William of Ware on the Immaculate Conception

Dates: fl. c. 1290–1305 (Oxford Sentences lectures, on Book III, dist. 3) · teacher of Duns Scotus · Catholic Encyclopedia
Summary: William of Ware (Guillelmus de Ware, “Doctor Fundatus”), an English Franciscan and the master of John Duns Scotus, is the first university theologian to argue the case for claim (b) in the schools. In his disputed question “whether the Blessed Virgin was conceived in original sin” he lays out the very argument-form his pupil would make famous: God could preserve Mary from ever contracting the stain (possibility), it was fitting that the Son make his Mother as pure as he could (congruity), and therefore he did so — preserving her, not merely cleansing her after the fact. Two cautions belong on the record: the crisp Latin slogan potuit, decuit, ergo fecit and the celebrated maxim that “in doubt the more excellent is to be attributed to Mary” are Scotus’s crystallizations, not Ware’s own words; and the direction of first influence between master and pupil is disputed. What is firmly Ware’s is the reasoning itself, set down in his own hand.

Claims & evidence

The possibility–fittingness–fact argument: Christ preserved his Mother from all impurity

Congruentia autem patet, quia, postquam Filius eius est ipsa munditia, congruum fuit, ut faceret Matrem suam ita mundam, sicut potuit, et sic non solum mundaret, sed ab omni immunditia praeservaret… et quod potuit, congruum fuit, quod fecerit; et ex hoc sequitur, quod ita fecerit, cum filius debeat matrem honorare.

— Quaestio: Utrum beata Virgo concepta fuerit in originali peccato, ed. Quaracchi (1904), pp. 6–7, source. In translation: “The congruity is clear: since her Son is purity itself, it was fitting that he should make his Mother as pure as he could — and so not merely cleanse her but preserve her from all impurity… and what he could do, it was fitting that he did, and from this it follows that he did so, since a son ought to honour his mother.” The decisive word is praeservaret: Mary is not purged of a stain she contracted (claim (a)’s framework) but kept from ever contracting it — the preservation claim (b) asserts. Ware builds it on Anselm’s De conceptu virginali (which he quotes at length): it was fitting the Virgin shine with “a purity than which, under God, none greater can be conceived.”1

Preservation, not purgation: reading the Damascene’s “purified” the immaculist way

…purgans non a peccato quod infuit, sed quod infuisset, nisi praeservata fuisset; sicut matrimonium excusat a peccato, non quod inest, sed quod inesset…

— same question, reply to the opening objection (Quaracchi ed., p. 11), source. In translation: “…purifying not from sin that was present, but from sin that would have been present had she not been preserved — as marriage excuses from a sin not present but that would be present [absent the good of marriage].” Ware turns the Greek fathers’ “purified” language (the objectors’ strongest text) inside out: a preservation can still be called a purifying, just as Christ is said to be “purified” though he had no sin. It is the exact hinge on which the whole immaculist reading of the patristic “prepurified” witnesses would turn.1

Notes

  • What is his, and what is Scotus’s. The argument-form — possibility, then congruity, then fact — is genuinely Ware’s, structured in his own words and grounded on Anselm. But the pithy four-word slogan potuit, decuit, ergo fecit is a later distillation (its nearest verbatim ancestor in the 1904 volume is actually a hostile paraphrase of the immaculist case by Peter Auriol’s opponents), and the famous conclusion — that where Scripture and the Church do not forbid it, “it seems probable that the more excellent should be attributed to Mary” — stands in Scotus’s question, not Ware’s. The Quaracchi editor notes Scotus “follows the method indicated by Ware” (ducit modum a Guarra indicatum).
  • The three questions printed together. Ware’s question, Scotus’s, and Auriol’s were edited side by side in the 1904 Quaracchi critical edition — a compact record of the early Franciscan defense, with Ware’s at pp. 1–11 and Scotus’s immediately following.
  • The direction-of-influence dispute. The traditional view — the tomb epitaph naming him Praeceptor Scoti, and the observation that words “often attributed to Scotus are already found with William of Ware” — makes Ware the teacher who handed the immaculist case to his pupil. Éphrem Longpré argued the reverse: that Ware’s earlier redaction followed the standard Franciscan maculist line and he adopted the immaculist position only after Scotus. With the two men’s Oxford redactions overlapping c. 1290–1305, the safe statement is that the argument-form is documented in Ware’s own text; who reached it first is genuinely uncertain.
