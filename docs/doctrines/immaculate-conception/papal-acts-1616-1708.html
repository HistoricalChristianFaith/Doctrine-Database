Rome protects and universalizes the Immaculate Conception — Paul V to Clement XI (1616–1708)

Dates: 1616–1708 (Paul V 1616–1617, Gregory XV 1622, Innocent XII 1693, Clement XI 1708) · Catholic Encyclopedia
Summary: A run of four disciplinary and liturgical acts across the century between Trent and the eve of the 1854 definition. None defines the Immaculate Conception; together they protect the pious opinion by silencing its opponents and universalize its feast across the whole Church. They are the institutional momentum that carried the doctrine to the brink of definition — the setting in which Alexander VII’s bull of 1661 (which falls in the middle of this arc) states the doctrine’s content. Protects and universalizes claim (b) without defining it.

Claims & evidence

Paul V — the ban on public denial (1616–1617)

The controversy that provoked Rome was Spanish: a Franciscan’s claimed vision in favour of the doctrine set off so sharp a dispute that King Philip III begged the pope for a decision. Paul V declined to define anything. In 1616 he did no more than renew the decrees of Sixtus IV and Pius V forbidding either party to charge the other with heresy. The following year he took the decisive step — in the Apostolic Constitution Sanctissimus of 12 September 1617 he forbade anyone to teach publicly that Mary was conceived in original sin:

Paul V (1617) decreed that no one should dare to teach publicly that Mary was conceived in original sin.

— The Catholic Encyclopedia, “Immaculate Conception.”1 This is the first time the public denial of the doctrine was prohibited: Sixtus IV had barred each side from calling the other heretical; Paul V now barred one side from speaking against the doctrine at all. The measure came at the instance of the Spanish crown, and the enthusiasm it released spread through the Hispanic world — a Decreto of Paul V “in favor of the Immaculate Conception” was printed as far away as Lima in 1618.2

Gregory XV — silence even in private (1622)

Paul V had silenced public teaching against the doctrine; his successor closed the remaining gap. In 1622 Gregory XV extended the prohibition to writings and sermons “even private,” again reserving the question for the Holy See:

Gregory XV (1622) imposed absolute silence (in scriptis et sermonibus etiam privatis) upon the adversaries of the doctrine until the Holy See should define the question.

— The Catholic Encyclopedia, “Immaculate Conception” (same article as note 1). In the same year he abolished the maculist evasion of calling the feast the Sanctificatio B.M.V. (the “Sanctification” of the Blessed Virgin Mary) rather than her Conceptio — a wording the Dominicans had preferred because it left open when she was sanctified. Henceforth the feast was of the Conception. Like Paul V’s act, it was sought by the Spanish crown, now under Philip IV (as note 2).

Innocent XII — raising the feast (1693)

The next two acts are liturgical rather than disciplinary — they raise the rank and reach of the feast the earlier popes had protected. In 1693 Innocent XII elevated it:

Innocent XII (1693) raised it to a double of the second class with an octave for the universal Church.

— The Catholic Encyclopedia, “Immaculate Conception” (same article as note 1). A “double of the second class with an octave” is a high grade in the old calendar, with its own eight-day observance — a mark of how far the feast had risen in dignity even before its object was defined.

Clement XI — a holy day of obligation (1708)

The last act made the feast binding on the whole Church. By the bull Commissi Nobis Divinitus of 6 December 1708, Clement XI ordered 8 December kept everywhere as a holy day of obligation:

Clement XI decreed on 6 Dec., 1708, that the feast should be a holiday of obligation throughout the entire Church.

— The Catholic Encyclopedia, “Immaculate Conception” (same article as note 1); the bull’s title, Commissi Nobis Divinitus, from the standard account of the feast.3

What they do and do not show

None of these four acts defines claim (b) — freedom from original sin at the first instant of Mary’s conception. Paul V and Gregory XV protect the pious opinion by forbidding its denial; Innocent XII and Clement XI universalize its feast. Rome is doing here exactly what Sixtus IV had begun and Alexander VII would crown in 1661: shielding and spreading the doctrine while stopping short of declaring it revealed. That last step waited for Ineffabilis Deus in 1854 — whose own text renews “the Constitutions and Decrees issued by the Roman Pontiffs, our predecessors, especially Sixtus IV, Paul V, and Gregory XV,” naming these very acts as the scaffolding on which the definition was raised.4

Notes

  • The 1617 constitution is cited by title as Sanctissimus (12 September 1617) — the name under which Ineffabilis Deus and the reference literature refer to Paul V’s act. The English rendering of its substance (“no one should dare to teach publicly…”) is the Catholic Encyclopedia’s report of what was decreed, not a translation of the Latin text, which the sources consulted do not reproduce in full.
  • Paul V’s two steps are easily conflated: 1616 renewed the no-heresy-charges truce of Sixtus IV and Pius V; the prohibition of public denial belongs to the 1617 constitution. The distinction is kept here.
  • Both Paul V (1617) and Gregory XV (1622) acted at the instance of the Spanish kings — Philip III and Philip IV respectively — a reminder that this century of Roman protection was driven in part by the fervent Immaculism of the Spanish crown.
