Bonaventure on the Immaculate Conception

Dates: c. 1255 (Commentary on the Sentences) · c. 1221–1274 · Wikipedia
Summary: Bonaventure — the “Seraphic Doctor,” minister-general of the Franciscans and, later, the order to which the doctrine’s eventual champion belonged — nevertheless taught the opposing view of his day: Mary’s soul contracted original sin and was sanctified only shortly after animation, not preserved from the stain at the instant of conception.

Claims & evidence

Mary contracted original sin and was sanctified after animation, not preserved at conception

In his commentary on the Sentences, treating whether the Blessed Virgin’s soul was sanctified before it contracted original sin, Bonaventure answers in the negative: her flesh was conceived in the ordinary way, subject to the law of transmission; her soul, once infused (animation), contracted the original stain like every descendant of Adam; and God then sanctified her in the womb, soon after animation, cleansing what had briefly been contracted.1 He presents this as the “more common” opinion (communior) among the masters and judges it the safer and more reasonable, precisely because it keeps intact Christ’s office as the universal Redeemer who owes Mary’s salvation to no one but himself.

The ground of the objection: Christ’s universal redemption

Bonaventure’s reasoning tracks the same Augustinian worry that moved Bernard and Aquinas: if Mary were conceived immaculate she would not have needed to be redeemed, and Christ would not be the Saviour of all. The debt of original sin, and therefore the need of redemption, he holds, must be common to Mary with the rest of the race — her privilege lying in how swiftly and fully grace cleansed her, not in an exemption at conception.2

Notes

  • Bonaventure affirms claim (a) — Mary’s complete freedom from actual sin and her sanctification in the womb — while denying claim (b), the immaculate conception. That a Franciscan of his stature stood with Bernard and Aquinas shows the opposition was not a partisan Dominican position but the settled thirteenth-century consensus.
  • The reversal came within his own order a generation later, when Duns Scotus reframed Mary’s redemption as preservative — answering the very “universal Redeemer” objection on which Bonaventure had rested — and the Franciscans became the doctrine’s foremost defenders.
