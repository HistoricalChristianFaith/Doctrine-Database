Peter Lombard on the Immaculate Conception

Dates: c. 1155–58 (Sentences, Book III) · c. 1096–1160 · Wikipedia
Summary: Peter Lombard is not an advocate but the frame-setter: his Sentences (c. 1155–58) became the theology textbook of the medieval schools, and the whole scholastic debate over Mary’s sanctification was argued on one of its distinctions — Book III, distinction 3. There Lombard teaches that Mary’s flesh was “subject to sin” (obnoxia peccato) like the rest of Adam’s line, and was afterward purged and sanctified by the Holy Spirit — the fullest purgation coming at Christ’s conception. That is claim (a)’s framework of sanctification after conception, not the preservation at the instant of conception that claim (b) asserts; the “preservation” category is not yet even formulable in his terms. Bonaventure, Aquinas, and Scotus would each build their own answer on this distinction.

Claims & evidence

Mary’s flesh was “subject to sin” before it was purified by the Holy Spirit

Quaeritur etiam de carne Verbi, an, priusquam conciperetur, obligata fuerit peccato, et an talis assumta fuerit a Verbo. Sane dici potest et credi oportet, iuxta Sanctorum attestationis convenientiam, ipsam prius peccato fuisse obnoxiam, sicut reliqua Virginis caro, sed Spiritus sancti operatione ita mundatam, ut ab omni peccati contagione immunis uniretur Verbo, poena tantum, non necessitate sed voluntate assumentis, remanente.

— Sentences, Book III, dist. 3, ch. 1 (“On the flesh assumed, what it was like before”), source (Quaracchi critical edition, 1916, pp. 557–558; = PL 192). In translation: “It is asked, too, concerning the flesh of the Word, whether, before it was conceived, it was bound by sin, and whether it was assumed by the Word in that condition. It can indeed be said, and ought to be believed, in agreement with the testimony of the Saints, that it was previously subject to sin (obnoxia), like the rest of the Virgin’s flesh, but was so cleansed by the operation of the Holy Spirit that, free from every contagion of sin, it was united to the Word.” The premise is that the flesh from which Christ’s body was formed had the stain and was cleansed of it — not that it was preserved from ever contracting it.1

The Holy Spirit purged Mary and freed her from the “tinder of sin” — at Christ’s conception

Mariam quoque totam Spiritus sanctus, in eam praeveniens, a peccato prorsus purgavit et a fomite peccati etiam liberavit, vel fomitem ipsum penitus evacuando, ut quibusdam placet, vel sic debilitando et extenuando, ut ei postmodum peccandi occasio nullatenus exstiterit…

— Sentences, Book III, dist. 3, ch. 1, source (Quaracchi ed., 1916, p. 558). In translation: “Mary too the Holy Spirit, coming upon her, wholly purged from sin and even freed from the tinder of sin (fomes peccati) — either by wholly evacuating the tinder itself, as some hold, or by so weakening and diminishing it that afterward no occasion of sinning existed for her at all…” Lombard reads this as the moment of the Annunciation — the “coming upon” is the Spiritus sanctus superveniet in te of Luke 1:35, which he quotes next. Two features fix his frame against claim (b): the decisive purgation is worked at the conceiving of Christ, and it is a purgation from a sin she had (and from its fomes), not a preservation from ever contracting it. That the tinder was “wholly evacuated” he reports only as what “some hold” (ut quibusdam placet).

“Before it was united to the Word” the flesh was subject to sin in Mary and in her forebears

Cum autem illa caro, cuius excellentia singularis verbis explicari non valet, antequam esset Verbo unita, obnoxia fuerit peccato in Maria et in aliis, a quibus propagatione traducta est, non immerito videri potest in Abraham peccato subiacuisse, cuius universa caro peccato subiacebat.

— Sentences, Book III, dist. 3, ch. 3, source (Quaracchi ed., 1916, pp. 559–560). In translation: “But since that flesh, whose singular excellence words cannot explain, before it was united to the Word, was subject to sin in Mary and in the others from whom by propagation it was handed down, it can not without reason be thought to have lain under sin in Abraham, whose whole flesh lay under sin.” The flesh Christ took is placed squarely within the line that transmits sin by ordinary generation — Augustine’s own account of transmission, which the later objectors would wield against the Immaculate Conception.

Mary’s freedom from actual sin — the Augustine text preserved intact (claim (a))

Quod autem sacra Virgo ex tunc ab omni peccato immunis exstiterit, Augustinus evidenter ostendit… “Excepta sancta Virgine Maria, de qua propter honorem Domini nullam prorsus, cum de peccatis agitur, haberi volo quaestionem.”

— Sentences, Book III, dist. 3, ch. 2 (“That no one here is without sin, except the Virgin”), source (Quaracchi ed., 1916, p. 559), quoting Augustine, On Nature and Grace 36 (42). Lombard carries over the classic (a)-text verbatim: Mary is set apart from the question of sins “out of honour to the Lord,” and “from then on” (ex tunc) was immune from all sin. This is her sinless life — exactly the claim early tradition affirms — and it sits beside, not in place of, his teaching that her flesh had first to be purged.

Notes

  • Why he is the keystone. From the 1220s onward every bachelor of theology in the Latin West lectured on the Sentences, and the sanctification of Mary was debated at Book III, distinction 3. Bonaventure, Aquinas, and Scotus all frame the question as a commentary on this distinction — so Lombard’s text is the shared ground the whole later dispute presupposes, whichever side a schoolman took.
  • He denies claim (b) in substance, not by argument. Unlike Bernard, who wrote expressly against the new feast, Lombard raises no polemic: the “preservation at the first instant of conception” idea is simply not yet formulable in his frame. His default is sanctification after — a flesh already under sin, purged by the Spirit — and that default is the very thing Scotus’s “preservative redemption” would later break.
  • The schoolmen’s reading. Lombard’s own words treat the flesh (caro) as obnoxia peccato and place the decisive purgation at Christ’s conception; the commentators drew from the distinction the sharper question of when Mary’s soul was sanctified relative to its animation, and the “common” scholastic answer — contraction of original sin at animation, then sanctification in the womb — is a development of this text, not a verbatim quotation of it.
