John Duns Scotus on the Immaculate Conception

Dates: c. 1300 (Ordinatio III, Oxford lectures) · c. 1266–1308 · Wikipedia
Summary: The Franciscan “Subtle Doctor” broke the scholastic deadlock. Rather than deny that Mary needed Christ’s redemption — the objection Aquinas had pressed — Scotus argued that the most perfect Mediator redeems most perfectly by preserving from sin, not merely by rescuing after it; so Mary was redeemed more excellently, being preserved from ever contracting original sin.

Claims & evidence

To prevent sin is a more perfect mediation than to reconcile after it

he who prevents lest one offend, is a more perfect mediator than he who merely reconciles after the offense.

— Ordinatio (Opus Oxoniense) III, d. 3, q. 1, source. Scotus reasons that Christ is the most perfect Mediator, and a perfect Mediator performs the most perfect possible act of mediation toward at least one person. The most perfect mediation is not to cleanse a stain already contracted but to prevent it from ever being contracted at all. So it was fitting that Christ should exercise this supreme degree of mediation toward his mother.

Mary was redeemed by being preserved, not exempted

Mary had the greatest need of Christ as Redeemer, for she would have contracted original sin by reason of her descent unless this had been prevented by the grace of a Mediator.

— Ordinatio III, d. 3, q. 1, source. This is the decisive turn. Aquinas had objected that an immaculate Mary would not have needed redemption, breaking Christ’s universal saviourhood. Scotus answers that Mary’s need of the Redeemer was in fact the greatest of any creature’s: left to her descent from Adam she too would have contracted the stain, and it was only Christ’s grace, applied in anticipation of his merits, that preserved her from it. Preservation is thus a mode of redemption — the highest — not an exemption from it. He concludes that, so long as it be “not against the authority of the Church and of Holy Scripture, it would seem probable to attribute to Mary that which is more perfect.”1

Notes

  • Scotus frames his conclusion with characteristic caution — as the more probable and more fitting view, pending the church’s judgement — not as a settled certainty. It was later generations of Franciscan “Scotists” who pressed it toward definition.
  • The slogan often attached to his case, potuit, decuit, ergo fecit (“God could do it, it was fitting, therefore he did it”), is a later Franciscan summary of the argument, not a phrase Scotus himself wrote; the underlying “could and willed” reasoning reaches back through Eadmer of Canterbury to Anselm. The step it makes — from fittingness to fact — is not demonstrative, and even sympathetic commentators call it “doubtful”; Scotus leaned on preservative redemption itself, not on this formula.
  • Whether Scotus’s praeredemptio actually dissolves Aquinas’s objection is weighed off-timeline in two paired argument pages: the universal-redemption objection (assessment: contested) and the preservative-redemption reply (assessment: plausible — a coherent mechanism, but coherence is not actuality).
