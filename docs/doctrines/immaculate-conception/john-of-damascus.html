John of Damascus on the Immaculate Conception

Dates: c. 730 (John of Damascus, monk of Mar Saba and the last of the Greek fathers, c. 675/676–749; the key text is Homily I on the Nativity of the Theotokos, one of his mature Palestinian sermons, with supporting matter from An Exposition of the Orthodox Faith) · Wikipedia
Summary: John is the high-water mark of the Greek “all-holy” (Panagia) strand. Preaching on Mary’s own nativity he sanctifies not only her life but her very conception and her parents — the “wholly unblemished seed” of Joachim, the “all-holy infant” grown in Anne’s womb, a birth in which “nature did not dare to anticipate the germ of grace.” Yet he frames this as God’s foreknowing sanctification and predestination of Mary, not as her preservation from an inherited guilt — the Latin category the Greek East construes differently. He is the most (b)-adjacent of the fathers, and precisely the figure the modern Orthodox rejection of 1854 reaches back to and reads the other way.

Claims & evidence

The very conception is holy: the “unblemished seed” of Joachim, the “all-holy infant” in Anne’s womb

O most all-blessed loins of Joachim, from which a wholly unblemished seed was sent forth! O renowned womb of Anna, in which slowly, with additions from her, an all-holy infant grew, and once it had taken shape, was born!

— Homily I on the Nativity of the Theotokos, §2, trans. Mary B. Cunningham, source.1 Where earlier witnesses praise Mary’s purity in life (claim (a)), John extends the sanctity backward to the act of her conception itself and even to her parents. This is the closest the Greek fathers come to the object of the later Roman dogma.

“Nature did not dare to anticipate the germ of grace”

Nature has been defeated by grace and stands trembling, no longer ready to take the lead.

— Homily I on the Nativity of the Theotokos, §2, trans. Cunningham, source. A standard reference paraphrases the fuller sentence — “when the Virgin Mother of God was to be born of Anne, nature did not dare to anticipate the germ of grace, but remained devoid of fruit” — and reads John as teaching an “immaculate active generation and the sanctity of the conceptio carnis”: grace, not nature, takes the lead at Mary’s conception.2

God foreknew and predestined her

The God of all things, having known in advance your worth, loved you; and because of this love, he predestined you, and “at the end of times” he brought you into being and revealed you as Theotokos…

— Homily I on the Nativity of the Theotokos, §7, trans. Cunningham, source. John’s frame is predestination and foreknowing love: God sets Mary apart from all eternity and brings her into being for her office. This is the Greek key in which the whole “all-holy” language is set — a positive election and sanctification, not the negative Augustinian category of preservation from inherited guilt.

Her flesh “pure and immaculate” (the settled formula)

…the Son of God and God in that He was made flesh and became man from her pure and immaculate flesh and blood…

— An Exposition of the Orthodox Faith, IV.14, trans. S. D. F. Salmond, source. In his dogmatic handbook the language has hardened into a fixed formula: Mary’s flesh is “pure and immaculate,” the vessel from which the incarnate Word takes his humanity. This is the vocabulary that made John the Panagia theologian par excellence — and the reason both later Roman apologists and later Orthodox critics claim him.

Notes

  • The (a)/(b) line. John goes further than any earlier father toward claim (b): he sanctifies the conception and the parents, not merely the life. But he does not teach the 1854 dogma. His categories are sanctification, predestination, and all-holiness — a positive fullness of grace — not the preservation of Mary from original guilt transmitted by generation, which is the precise object of the Roman definition. The Greek East does not hold that Adam’s guilt is inherited (only his mortality and corruption), so the question the dogma answers is one John never framed. He is the culmination of the “all-holy / predestined” development, not a proponent of the later Latin claim.
  • Claimed by both sides. Pius IX’s circle cited John’s “unblemished seed” among the patristic supports for the dogma, and Western authors drew on his “immaculate active generation”; yet the same John, read for his frame of predestination and sanctification, is the fountainhead the modern Orthodox rejection invokes against Rome. The one text bears both readings because it stands just short of the distinction 1854 would draw.
  • Elsewhere on this wiki. John appears also as a witness to Mary as the New Eve, to the Assumption / Dormition, to the Theotokos, and to the perpetual virginity — the same synthesizing hand that gathered the Marian tradition into settled Byzantine formulas.
