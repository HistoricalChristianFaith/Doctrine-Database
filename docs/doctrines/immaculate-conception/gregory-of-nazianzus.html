Gregory of Nazianzus on the Immaculate Conception

Dates: c. 380 (the Oration on the Theophany, Oration 38, preached at Constantinople c. Dec. 380–Jan. 381; the same passage recurs in Oration 45, On Easter) · c. 329–390 · Wikipedia
Summary: Gregory the Theologian is the Eastern keystone of the debate. In a single participle — that the Virgin was prokathartheisa, “prepurified” in soul and body by the Holy Spirit — he supplied the word from which every later Greek “prepurified / all-holy” witness (Sophronius, John of Damascus) descends, and which modern Catholic scholarship (Christiaan Kappes) presses as a Greek antecedent of the Immaculate Conception. But the word is genuinely double-edged: on its face it names a purification worked on Mary at the conceiving of Christ, not at her own conception, and the mainstream and Orthodox reading takes it to imply the very opposite — a womb still “subject to impurity” that needed cleansing. He is neither a plain witness to claim (b) nor cleanly against it, which is why his one word became the seed of the whole Eastern dispute; see the argument page Does Gregory Nazianzen’s prokathartheisa testify to the Immaculate Conception? (assessment: contested).

Claims & evidence

The prokathartheisa passage: the Virgin “first in body and soul… purified by the Holy Ghost”

…the Word of God Himself… came to His own Image, and took on Him flesh for the sake of our flesh, and mingled Himself with an intelligent soul for my soul’s sake, purifying like by like; and in all points except sin was made man. Conceived by the Virgin, who first in body and soul was purified by the Holy Ghost (for it was needful both that Childbearing should be honoured, and that Virginity should receive a higher honour), He came forth then as God with that which He had assumed, One Person in two Natures…

— Oration 38 (On the Theophany, or Birthday of Christ), §13, source. The Greek phrase behind “first… was purified” is tēs parthenou… prokathartheịsēs — prokathartheisa, an aorist passive participle of prokathairō, “to purify beforehand / prepurify.” Gregory repeats the sentence almost verbatim in his later Easter oration — “conceived by the Virgin, who first in body and soul was purified by the Holy Ghost” (Oration 45, On Easter, §9, source) — so it is a settled formula, not a passing phrase.1

The two readings of one word

The whole Eastern controversy turns on what that prefix pro- (“before”) modifies and what the purifying removes.

[Kappes:] The Greek Fathers — in the line of the Nazianzen until the introduction of Byzantine Thomism in the 14th century — never vacillated about the all-immaculate status of the Blessed Virgin Mary.

The Catholic reading (Christiaan Kappes) takes prokathartheisa as an already-pure nature raised to a still-higher purity — a “preparation” rather than a cleansing from defilement, on the analogy of Christ’s own baptism (purified though sinless). On this account Gregory is a proto-witness to claim (b), the Eastern antecedent of the Latin Immaculate Conception.2 The mainstream and Orthodox reading takes the plain sense: purification presupposes something to be purified, and Gregory places it at the conceiving of Christ — so the Spirit cleansed a “postlapsarian womb subject to corruption and death” at the Annunciation, which (if anything) implies Mary was not already free of the ancestral condition. On this account the same passage tells against even a preservative reading; the later Fathers who inherit the word — John of Damascus among them — expressly locate the prepurification at the Annunciation, not at Mary’s own conception.3

The translation-history datum: prokathartheisa → immaculata

When Rufinus of Aquileia rendered Gregory’s Nativity oration into Latin near the end of the fourth century, he translated the Greek prokathartheisa with the Latin immaculata — the very word that would name the later dogma. It is a telling artifact of how the West read the Greek forward: a term the East used for a purification worked at the Incarnation became, in Latin dress, the vocabulary of a spotless origin. The datum cuts both ways — evidence that the language was received as immaculist, but also a caution that the Latin already imported a reading the Greek does not compel.4

Notes

  • Why he sits apart from the Western strand. Ephrem and Ambrose are claim-(a) texts (Mary’s purity of life) later over-read as (b). Gregory is different in kind: his word bears directly on origin and nature, and it launched a distinctively Eastern trajectory of “prepurified / all-holy” vocabulary that runs to Sophronius and John of Damascus. That makes him the strongest single patristic hinge on which the Eastern case can be built — and, for the same reason, the text the Orthodox tradition reads as its clearest patristic ground for rejecting the Roman dogma. The stakes are why the phrase is contested rather than decisive either way.
  • The purification is timed to the Incarnation, not to Mary’s conception. Gregory’s clause modifies the Virgin at the moment “conceived by the Virgin…” — i.e. when she conceives Christ. Nothing in the sentence speaks of the instant her own soul began to exist, which is the precise object of the Immaculate Conception (claim (b)). Reading (b) out of it therefore requires the further philological claim that the pro- points back to a purity Mary already possessed — the contested move the argument page weighs.
  • Gregory appears on this wiki also as a witness on Theotokos, prayer to the saints, infant baptism, and the Old Testament canon. On the immaculate conception he stands c. 380, between Ephrem (c. 361) and Augustine (c. 415).
