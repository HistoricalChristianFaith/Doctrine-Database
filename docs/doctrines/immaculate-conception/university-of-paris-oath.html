The University of Paris oath on the Immaculate Conception (1497)

Dates: 1497 (Cologne 1499, Vienna 1501, and others following) · Catholic Encyclopedia
Summary: In 1497 the University of Paris decreed that no one could be admitted a member of the university without swearing to defend and assert the Immaculate Conception of Mary. The oath grew directly out of the Franciscan–Dominican disputes that the Montson affair had inflamed a century earlier, and it was imitated across Europe. It is the high-water mark of the doctrine’s institutional entrenchment as a sententia pia — enforced by statute long before Rome had defined anything. Affirms claim (b).

Claims & evidence

The oath itself

In 1497 the University of Paris decreed that henceforward no one should be admitted a member of the university, who did not swear that he would do the utmost to defend and assert the Immaculate Conception of Mary.

— The Catholic Encyclopedia, “Immaculate Conception.”1 The reference literature is explicit that the oath was “introduced by the Sorbonne in consequence of the disputes… between the Franciscans and Dominicans” — the same controversy the 1387 condemnation had brought to a head, now settled at Paris by requiring assent as a condition of every degree.2

The oath spreads across Christendom

Other universities followed within a few years — Cologne in 1499, Vienna in 1501 — and eventually a long roll of studia across Europe and the New World:

Toulouse followed the example; in Italy, Bologna and Naples; in the German Empire, Cologne, Mainz, and Vienna; in Belgium, Louvain; in England, before the Reformation, Oxford and Cambridge; in Spain, Salamanca, Toledo, Seville, and Valencia; in Portugal, Coimbra and Evora; in America, Mexico and Lima.

— The Catholic Encyclopedia, “Immaculate Conception” (same article as note 1). The Protestant historian Philip Schaff records the same fact from the other side of the confessional divide: in 1497 Paris “exacted from all candidates for degrees an oath accepting the dogma of the immaculate conception.”3

What it does and does not show

The oath is a genuine affirmation of claim (b), and a striking one: the leading theology faculties of Latin Christendom now required their members to hold and defend the Immaculate Conception. But it is an act of institutional and academic entrenchment, not of magisterial definition. It ran ahead of Rome, which at exactly this period was going the other way — Sixtus IV’s Grave nimis (1483) had just forbidden either party to brand the other heretical, precisely because the point had “not yet been decided by the Roman Church and the Apostolic See.” The universities enforced as a condition of membership what the papacy still declined to define; the two would not be reconciled until 1854.

Notes

  • The year is 1497 across the sources consulted (Catholic Encyclopedia, McClintock & Strong, Schaff); no source supports 1496.
  • The English “do the utmost to defend and assert” is the Catholic Encyclopedia’s own rendering of the oath’s substance, not a translation of the Latin formula, which the sources consulted do not reproduce. It is given here as a report of what was sworn, not as the ipsissima verba.
  • The Cologne 1499 / Vienna 1501 dates are the usual figures; Vienna’s requirement was reaffirmed and formalized in the seventeenth century. “Tübingen” is sometimes added to the list but is not named in the standard sources and is omitted here.
