Augustine of Hippo on the Immaculate Conception

Dates: c. 415 (On Nature and Grace) · c. 354–430 · Wikipedia
Summary: Augustine testifies to Mary’s sinless life (claim a), not to her immaculate conception (claim b). He deliberately brackets her from any question of actual sin “out of honour to the Lord” — yet his own doctrine that original sin passes to every child by ordinary generation became the standing objection the medieval opponents of the Immaculate Conception would press.

Claims & evidence

Mary is excepted from the discussion of actual sin — but the question is left unraised

We must except the holy Virgin Mary, concerning whom I wish to raise no question when it touches the subject of sins, out of honour to the Lord; for from Him we know what abundance of grace for overcoming sin in every particular was conferred upon her who had the merit to conceive and bear Him who undoubtedly had no sin.

— On Nature and Grace, ch. 36 (42), source. The context is decisive for reading Augustine rightly here. He is refuting Pelagius, who had drawn up a list of Old- and New-Testament saints said to have “lived without sin” — and Augustine’s whole point is that all of them, if asked, would confess with the Apostle John, “If we say that we have no sin, we deceive ourselves.” Mary alone he refuses to submit to that question, and expressly on the ground of the “grace for overcoming sin” given to the mother of the sinless Lord. The statement is therefore about actual sin conquered by grace (claim a), and says nothing about her being conceived free of original sin (claim b).1

Notes

  • Augustine is routinely cited by later apologists as an early witness “for” the Immaculate Conception. He is not: he testifies to Mary’s sinlessness in life, the near-consensual claim (a), and the immaculate conception proper (claim b) is nowhere in view.
  • The deeper irony is that Augustine supplied the medieval opposition its main weapon. His teaching that original sin is contracted by every human being through generation from Adam is exactly what Bernard, Bonaventure, and Aquinas invoked to deny that Mary could have been conceived immaculate — since, on that account, she too must have needed the redemption of Christ. Duns Scotus’s later answer was not to deny Mary’s need of redemption but to recast it as a preservative redemption.
