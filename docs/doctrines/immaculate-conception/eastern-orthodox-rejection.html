The Eastern Orthodox rejection of the Immaculate Conception

Dates: modern (representative statements, 20th century) · Wikipedia
Summary: The Eastern Orthodox Church venerates Mary as Panagia (“all-holy”) and Achrantos (“undefiled”), free of every personal sin — and yet rejects the Roman dogma of 1854. The reason is not a lower view of Mary but a different doctrine of the Fall: the East does not hold that Adam’s guilt is inherited, so there is no inherited guilt for Mary to need a special exemption from. On this view the dogma answers a question the East does not ask.

Claims & evidence

The dogma breaks the solidarity of Mary with fallen humanity

The Orthodox Church does not admit the idea that the Holy Virgin was thus exempted from the lot of the rest of fallen humanity—the idea of a ‘privilege’ which makes her into a being ransomed before the redemptive work, by virtue of the future merits of her Son… Like other human beings… the Holy Virgin was born under the law of original sin, sharing with all the same common responsibility for the Fall. But sin never could become actual in her person; the sinful heritage of the Fall had no mastery over her right will.

— Vladimir Lossky, “Panagia,” in In the Image and Likeness of God (St Vladimir’s Seminary Press, 1974), pp. 203–204.1 For Lossky the dogma severs Mary from the “uninterrupted succession of Old Testament holiness” that culminates in her; she is holy within fallen humanity, not lifted out of it in advance. Her sinlessness is real but personal — sin never became actual in her — not a preservation from an inherited guilt.

The root disagreement: no inherited guilt

Most Orthodox theologians reject the idea of ‘original guilt,’ put forward by Augustine and still accepted (albeit in a mitigated form) by the Roman Catholic Church. Men (Orthodox usually teach) automatically inherit Adam’s corruption and mortality, but not his guilt: they are only guilty in so far as by their own free choice they imitate Adam.

— Kallistos (Timothy) Ware, The Orthodox Church.2 This is the hinge. The 1854 definition presupposes the Augustinian account of an inherited original guilt, transmitted by generation, from which Mary had to be preserved. Deny that premise — as the East does, reading the ancestral consequence as mortality and corruption rather than inherited guilt — and the special exemption loses its object. Sergius Bulgakov puts the same point positively: Orthodoxy will not grant “any individual sin” in Mary, but rejects an exemption at conception that “would separate her from the human race” and leave her unable to give her Son a true humanity.

Notes

  • The rejection hardened into a settled East–West difference only after 1854. As Ware notes, “in the past individual Orthodox have made statements which… approached close to it; but since 1854… the great majority of Orthodox have rejected the doctrine.” Figures sometimes cited as earlier sympathizers — Gregory Palamas, Mark of Ephesus, Nicholas Cabasilas — are read that way in a contested (and Catholic-leaning) scholarship; what the modern Orthodox consensus rejects is specifically the Roman dogmatic formulation, guilt-exemption “in the first instant.”
  • The disagreement is a useful mirror on the whole timeline: the medieval Western dispute was possible only because Augustine’s doctrine of inherited guilt made Mary’s case a problem to be solved. Where that premise is absent, the immaculate conception is neither affirmed nor needed — and Orthodox writers regard it as a solution to a difficulty the East never had.
