The Swiss Reformers (Zwingli and Bullinger) on the Immaculate Conception of Mary

Dates: c. 1525–1566 (Huldrych Zwingli, 1484–1531, reformer of Zurich, and his successor Heinrich Bullinger, 1504–1575) · Zwingli · Bullinger
Summary: The founders of the Reformed tradition in German-speaking Switzerland are the timeline’s cleanest witnesses to claim (a) — Mary’s purity and sinlessness — held without claim (b), the immaculate conception proper. Zwingli, by common reckoning the most Marian of the major Reformers, calls Mary “the immaculate and perpetual Virgin,” praises her as bearing not “the smallest trace of a stain,” and holds that she alone conceived her Son without transmitting to him the weakness inherited from Adam; Bullinger keeps the same high Marian praise, hymning her most holy body as “the most pure bed and temple of the Holy Spirit.” Yet neither taught the immaculate conception — scholarship finds “no trace in Zwingli of the doctrine” — and the reason is structural: Zwingli recast original sin as an inherited disease or defect rather than inherited guilt, and where there is no inherited guilt there is nothing a preservation from it could do. High praise of Mary’s purity, on a doctrine of the Fall that leaves the immaculate conception with no work to perform.

Claims & evidence

Zwingli — Mary “the immaculate and perpetual Virgin” (1530)

In the confession of faith he laid before the Diet of Augsburg in 1530, Zwingli set Mary’s purity into the very grammar of the Incarnation:

I believe and understand that the Son assumed flesh, because he truly assumed of the immaculate and perpetual Virgin Mary the human nature, yea, the entire man, who consists of body and soul.

— Zwingli, Fidei ratio (A Reckoning of the Faith, 1530).1 He uses “immaculate” (immaculata) of Mary without qualification — but as a title of her sinless purity, not a definition of freedom from original sin at her own conception. Alongside it he defended her perpetual virginity as firmly as any medieval, holding that she “brought forth for us the Son of God and in childbirth and after childbirth forever remained a pure, intact Virgin.”

Zwingli — “not the smallest trace of a stain” (1525)

In his systematic Commentary on True and False Religion (1525) and his preaching, Zwingli made Mary’s exemption from sin a chief ground for praising her: she alone of all women, “since the beginning of the world,” conceived her Son without transmitting to him “the human weakness that we all bring with us from Adam,” and she bore not “the smallest trace of a stain.”2 The register is high Marian purity — claim (a) — and, as George Tavard notes in surveying these texts, Zwingli “does not relate her virginal conception of Jesus to her own immaculate conception.” The one classic Marian theme absent from his preaching is the Assumption, which he does not affirm.

Zwingli — original sin as a disease, not guilt (1526): why the doctrine has no work to do

The structural reason Zwingli praises Mary’s purity yet never reaches the immaculate conception lies in his doctrine of the Fall. In his Declaration Regarding Original Sin, Addressed to Urbanus Rhegius (15 August 1526) he recast the inherited condition of Adam’s line as a defect of nature, not a personal guilt:

I have said that the original contamination of man is a disease, not a sin, because sin implies guilt, and guilt comes from a transgression or trespass on the part of one who designedly perpetrates a deed.

— Zwingli, Declaration Regarding Original Sin (1526).3 He illustrated the point with hereditary ailments — original sin is a lasting defect “as when stammering, blindness, or gout is hereditary in a family” — and concluded that “on account of such a thing no one is thought the worse or the more vicious, for things which come from nature cannot be put down as crimes or guilt.” This is precisely why the immaculate conception cannot arise for him: the Roman doctrine is the teaching that Mary was preserved from inherited guilt by a special grace — but on Zwingli’s account there is no inherited guilt to be preserved from, only a common defect that becomes sin proper solely in actual transgression. The doctrine answers a question his theology never asks.

Zwingli — the scholarly verdict: no immaculate conception, no assumption

The consensus of historians of the Reformation is that Zwingli’s warm Marian language stops short of both later Marian dogmas. Gottfried Locher states flatly that “there is no trace in Zwingli of the doctrine of the ‘immaculate conception’”; another standard assessment finds that “constantly as he insisted upon the certainty of the perpetual virginity of the mother of God, he accepted neither her immaculate conception nor her assumption.”4 Locher adds the theological logic in a phrase: for Zwingli “the holiness of Mary is derived from that of Christ, and not the other way round” — her purity is a function of her Son’s, not an endowment secured at her own conception.

Bullinger — the same high Marian praise, carried on into the Reformed church

Zwingli’s successor at Zurich, Heinrich Bullinger, preserved this Mariology of praise. He taught that Mary is properly called Mother of God and that she remained ever-virgin, and he went further than Zwingli in affirming her bodily assumption — on the biblical model of Elijah taken up in the chariot of fire — in language of great purity:

We believe that the Virgin Mary, Begetter of God, the most pure bed and temple of the Holy Spirit, that is, her most holy body, was carried to heaven by angels.

— Bullinger, sermon on the Virgin.5 His confessional writing keeps the same note: the Second Helvetic Confession (1566), the most widely adopted of the Reformed confessions and largely Bullinger’s own work, confesses that Christ was born of Mary “ever a virgin” who “most chastely conceived” him by the Holy Spirit, and the earlier First Helvetic (Basel) Confession (1536) that he took flesh “from the undefiled Virgin Mary.” As with Zwingli, this is honorific purity — claim (a) — with no doctrine of preservation from original sin at her conception: Bullinger stands within the same Reformed account of the Fall, and no immaculate conception follows from it.

Notes

  • The cleanest (a)-without-(b) witnesses on the timeline. The summary page turns on the distinction between (a) Mary’s sinless purity in life and (b) her freedom from original sin at conception. Many earlier witnesses (Ephrem, Ambrose, Proclus) heap purity-praise on Mary that later apologetics presses toward (b); the Swiss Reformers make the gap explicit — they lavish (a)-language on her and hold a doctrine of the Fall on which (b) is simply unavailable. They are the sharpest illustration the timeline offers that high Marian devotion does not entail the immaculate conception.
  • The same structural reason as the Christian East. Zwingli’s move — original sin as inherited corruption/defect rather than inherited guilt — parallels the Eastern account on which the Orthodox rejection of the 1854 dogma rests: with no inherited guilt to be preserved from, the immaculate conception answers a question the theology never poses. Zwingli reaches a Western Protestant version of the same conclusion the Greek East reached from its own doctrine of the Fall.
  • A caution on the most-quoted lines. Two of the pithiest Zwingli sayings on Mary — that he esteemed “the ever chaste, immaculate Virgin Mary” and that “it was fitting that such a holy Son should have a holy Mother” — circulate mostly through Catholic apologetic anthologies, where they are traced to a mid-twentieth-century mariological collection (E. Stakemeier, 1962) rather than to a located passage of Zwingli’s own works.6 They are consistent with everything Zwingli wrote, but the load-bearing evidence here is the directly attributable material above, not these floating phrases.
  • Chronology on this timeline. The Swiss witness sits between Cajetan’s maculist treatise (1515) and Luther’s mixed testimony (1527–1540), the two other early-sixteenth-century voices the Council of Trent would step around when it exempted Mary from its decree on original sin (1546). Where Luther grazes claim (b) and then retreats, the Swiss Reformers never approach it at all — a difference of doctrine, not of devotion.
