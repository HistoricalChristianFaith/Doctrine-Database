Bernard of Clairvaux on the Immaculate Conception

Dates: c. 1140 (Letter 174) · 1090–1153 · Wikipedia
Summary: Bernard, the most influential churchman of his age and a fervent devotee of Mary, is the first great voice to reject the Immaculate Conception. Writing to rebuke the canons of Lyons for adopting a new Feast of the Conception, he argues that Mary was sanctified in the womb after conception — making her birth holy, not her conception itself.

Claims & evidence

Sanctification could not precede conception, nor lie in the conception itself, but came after — in the womb

in order to be holy it is necessary to exist, and a person does not exist before being conceived… she received sanctification when existing in the womb after conception, which, by excluding sin, made her birth holy, but not her conception.

— Letter 174, to the Canons of Lyons, on the Conception of S. Mary, source (Samuel J. Eales trans., where the letter is numbered LXV). Bernard’s reasoning has two prongs. First, holiness presupposes a subject: before Mary was conceived she did not exist, so she could not then be made holy. Second, the conception itself — the act of ordinary generation — he takes to be bound up with the concupiscence through which original sin is transmitted, so it could not be the moment of her sanctification either. What remains is that God sanctified her in the womb, after conception, as Scripture says of Jeremiah, “sanctified before he came forth from the womb,” and of John the Baptist, who leapt in his mother’s womb at the presence of the Lord.

The new feast lacks warrant and honours Mary falsely

Bernard presses that the church of Lyons had introduced the feast without the authority of Rome and without reason, and that Mary needs no honour built on error: to celebrate her conception as holy is, he holds, to sanctify what was not holy and to give the Queen of Heaven a “false honour” she does not desire. His objection is thus not to devotion to Mary — of which he was a foremost promoter — but to grounding it on claim (b), which he judged untrue.1

Notes

  • Bernard concedes and indeed insists on claim (a) — Mary’s freedom from actual sin and her sanctification before birth — while denying claim (b), the immaculate conception. His letter fixes the terms the scholastics would argue for the next two centuries.
  • Later Immaculist theologians tried to soften Bernard’s stance or read him as objecting only to the manner of the feast; but his argument is directed at the substance — that sanctification followed conception — and the Franciscans answered it not by reinterpreting Bernard but by Scotus’s new account of a preservative redemption.
