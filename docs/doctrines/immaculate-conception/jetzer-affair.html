The Jetzer affair (Bern, 1507–1509)

Dates: 1507–1509 (executions 31 May 1509) · Wikipedia
Summary: In the Dominican convent at Bern, apparitions of the Virgin Mary were staged for a lay brother, Hans Jetzer, in which “Mary” declared she had been conceived in original sin — scripted propaganda for the maculist (Dominican) side against the Franciscan immaculist cause. The affair was exposed; after trials that turned on torture-extracted confessions, four Dominicans were burned at Bern on 31 May 1509. The most notorious scandal of the whole Dominican–Franciscan war over the doctrine — and one whose guilt verdict modern scholarship treats as genuinely contested. Handled here with care: the executions are certain; who was truly culpable is not.

Claims & evidence

The verifiable core

Hans Jetzer (c. 1483–c. 1514), a tailor’s assistant, entered the Dominican convent at Bern as a lay brother in 1507. From early that year he was subjected to a series of staged supernatural manifestations — a ghost, then apparitions of the Virgin Mary (with St Barbara), false stigmata, and a weeping image of the Virgin with painted “bloody tears.” The scripted “revelation” inverted the immaculist position: the Bern Dominicans held, with their order, that Mary “like all humans except Jesus Christ, was born into original sin,” and the staged Virgin was made to say she was distressed “at being said to have been born without sin, when that honor belonged to her son alone.”1 The purpose was to discredit the Franciscan cause of the Immaculate Conception and vindicate the Dominican view by an apparent word from heaven.

The scheme was discovered, and the matter went to trial. There were, in effect, three proceedings between 1507 and 1509, culminating in a retrial authorized by Pope Julius II; on 23 May 1509 the four accused friars were defrocked and handed to the secular arm. On 31 May 1509 the prior Johann Vatter, the subprior Franz Ueltschi, the lector Stephan Boltzhurst, and the steward Heinrich Steinegger were burned at the Schwellenmatte in Bern.2 Jetzer himself was acquitted of the friars’ crimes, banished, and escaped from prison; he is thought to have died around 1514.

Why the guilt verdict is contested

This wiki does not retell the scandal in its old confessional-polemic form — four Dominicans coldly convicted of fraud and justly burned — because that is precisely the reading modern scholarship has called into question. The verdict rested in significant part on confessions extracted under torture: Jetzer was tortured in February 1508 and only then accused his superiors, and the friars too were tortured. Nineteenth-century revisionism reopened the case: Nikolaus Paulus (1897) argued the four were victims of a “judicial murder” with Jetzer as the manipulator, and Rudolf Steck (1904), publishing all three trial records, likewise judged it a judicial murder (though he laid the blame on Rome rather than Bern). More recent scholarship — Kathrin Utz Tremp’s Warum Maria blutige Tränen weinte (2022) — leans the other way from Paulus, arguing that the illiterate Jetzer was more likely the dupe than the mastermind (the theological script drew on learned Mariological sources he could not have read), and reading his 1509 acquittal as the judgment of contemporaries who had the full evidence before them.3

What is secure, then, is the outward course of events: a Bern Dominican convent, staged Marian phenomena tied directly to the maculist–immaculist quarrel, trials driven by torture, and four friars burned on 31 May 1509. What remains genuinely disputed is culpability — how much was deliberate fraud by the friars, and how much a torture-driven, confessionally charged miscarriage of justice. The affair belongs on this timeline not as a doctrinal witness but as a measure of how bitter the institutional war over the Immaculate Conception had grown by the eve of the Reformation.

Notes

  • The executions on 31 May 1509 and the four friars’ names are well attested across the modern accounts; the trial phasing (a first examination of Jetzer in the winter of 1507–08, a main trial in the summer of 1508 before the bishops of Lausanne and Sion, and a 1509 retrial) is likewise standard.
  • Jetzer’s escape (25 July 1509) and death (c. 1514) rest on secondary reporting and are given cautiously.
  • The affair is often narrated in a sensational, anti-Dominican key going back to Reformation polemic and later Voltaire; that colouring is deliberately avoided here in favour of the contested-culpability framing that current scholarship supports.
