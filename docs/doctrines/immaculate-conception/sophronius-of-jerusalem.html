Sophronius of Jerusalem on the Immaculate Conception

Dates: c. 634 (Sophronius of Jerusalem, monk and then Patriarch of Jerusalem, c. 560–638; the key texts are his Oration II, On the Annunciation of the Theotokos, c. 635, and his Synodical Letter of 634, later read and approved at the Sixth Ecumenical Council, Constantinople III, 680–681) · Wikipedia
Summary: Sophronius carries Gregory Nazianzen’s prokathartheisa (“prepurified”) forward and gives it its most exalted pre-Damascene voice: he greets Mary as “the Immaculate,” made “purer” by the descending Spirit, filled with a grace granted to her alone, surpassing every saint and every rank of angels. Modern Catholic scholarship reads him as the clearest Greek antecedent of claim (b) — a purity Mary already possessed, only heightened at the Annunciation; the Orthodox reading takes the same “prepurified” language as a sanctifying purification worked at the Annunciation on a nature still “subject to impurity.” A proto-(b) witness at most: he stands, with the Life of the Virgin and John of Damascus, in the Greek “all-holy” chain that comes closest to the dogma without ever defining freedom from inherited guilt.

Claims & evidence

“All else is inferior when compared with the grace you have been given”

Hail, full of grace, the Lord is with you. What joy could surpass this, O Virgin Mother? What grace can excel that which God has granted to you alone?… Before the miracle we witness in you, all else pales; all else is inferior when compared with the grace you have been given.

— Oration II, On the Annunciation, §§21–22, 26 (PG 87.3, cols. 3242, 3250), in the Roman Office of Readings translation.1 Sophronius reads Gabriel’s kecharitōmenē as a grace unique to Mary, setting her above the whole of creation — “you have surpassed the hosts of angels; you have overshadowed the dazzling splendour of the archangels.”2 It is superlative praise of Mary’s fullness of grace; like the Luke 1:28 proof-text itself, it fixes that fullness at the Annunciation and specifies neither its content (original sin is not named) nor a terminus at her own conception.

“None has been purified in advance as you have been”

Many saints appeared before you, but none was filled with grace as you. No one has been purified in advance as you have been.

— Oration II, On the Annunciation, §25 (PG 87.3, cols. 3247–3248).3 The Greek is a long litany of parallel clauses — “none has been graced (kecharitōtai) as you… none magnified as you… none prepurified (prokekathartai) as you…” — of which the received English quotes two; Migne’s facing Latin renders the key clause “nemo, sicut tu, purificante gratia praeoccupatus est” (“none, like you, has been forestalled by purifying grace”). The load-bearing word is prokekathartai, the perfect passive of prokathairō (“to purify beforehand”) — the very prokathartheisa root, here in the completed-past tense. This is the sharpest pre-Damascene statement of a prior purity: not that Mary was cleansed at the Annunciation of some defilement, but that she alone “has been purified in advance” — the language on which the immaculist reading leans, though (as with Gregory’s word) the passage fixes no terminus for that purifying, whether her own conception or the Annunciation itself. Migne’s own nineteenth-century editor already pressed this clause for the immaculate reading, glossing it as Mary’s “most singular preservation from original infection.”

“The Spirit… descends upon you, the Immaculate, to make you purer”

The Holy Spirit descends upon you, who are the Immaculate, to make you purer and give you the virtue of fertility.

— Homily on the Annunciation, in Georges Gharib, Testi mariani del primo millennio, vol. 2 (Rome, 1989), p. 139.4 This is the doctrinally load-bearing sentence for the (a)/(b) crux. The Spirit’s coming does not make Mary immaculate; it descends on her “who are the Immaculate” and adds purity to a purity already there. Read that way, the Annunciation purification presupposes an antecedent all-holiness — the shape (though not yet the Latin content) of claim (b). But “the Immaculate” here renders a standard Greek epithet (Achrantos, “undefiled”), a title of Mary’s sanctity, not a statement about the instant of her conception.

The “prepurified” virgin — language that reached an ecumenical council

Sophronius’s Synodical Letter of 634 confesses that the Word made his dwelling “in the womb of the spotless (achrantou) virgin and Theotokos Mary, after she was prepurified (prokathartheisēs) in body and soul… and from her holy and blameless (amōmou) flesh.” That letter was read into the acts and approved at the Sixth Ecumenical Council (Constantinople III, 680–681), giving Gregory Nazianzen’s coinage a conciliar footing.5 The word is exactly as double-edged as it is in Gregory: it can be pressed for a purity Mary already had (Catholic reading) or read in its plain sense as a cleansing that presupposes a nature needing to be cleansed before it could bear God (Orthodox reading). The same conciliar text is quoted by the Christian East to make the opposite point — that Mary’s flesh, before the Spirit’s act, still shared the ancestral condition.

Notes

  • The (a)/(b) line. Sophronius goes further than any earlier Greek father in the direction of claim (b): “the Immaculate,” “purified in advance,” a grace “granted to you alone.” But he nowhere teaches the object of the 1854 dogma — Mary’s preservation from original sin at the instant of her own conception. His purification language is fixed at the Annunciation, when she conceives Christ; and the Greek East, which does not hold Adam’s guilt to be inherited, frames Mary’s holiness as positive sanctification and all-holiness, not the negative Augustinian category of preservation-from-guilt. He is a proto-(b) witness: closest in vocabulary, short of the definition.
  • “Holier than John the Baptist” — a reading, not a quotation. Christiaan Kappes presses Sophronius’s homily further still: building John the Baptist up as the pinnacle of the prophets (sanctified in the womb, the one who “alone prophesied in utero”) only to subordinate him to Mary, Sophronius is taken to imply she was holier than even the in-utero-sanctified Baptist — already immaculate when “purified” at the Annunciation. This is Kappes’s interpretive reconstruction of the sermon’s logic, not a verbatim sentence of Sophronius; it belongs to the immaculist case, weighed on the argument page below.6
  • Weighed as an argument. Whether Sophronius’s “prepurified” language (with Gregory’s) testifies to the Immaculate Conception is adjudicated on its own argument page (assessment: contested) — strong by philology and reception, answerable by the plain grammar and the timing.
