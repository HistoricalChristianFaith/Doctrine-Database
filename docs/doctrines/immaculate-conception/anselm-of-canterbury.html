Anselm of Canterbury on the Immaculate Conception

Dates: c. 1098 (Cur Deus Homo) · c. 1033–1109 · Wikipedia
Summary: Anselm stands on the hinge of the whole question. He denies the immaculate conception in the later technical sense — Mary, he holds, was conceived in original sin and cleansed only before Christ’s birth — yet he supplied the two materials the doctrine would afterwards be built from: the argument from “fittingness” (decuit) and an account of original sin as objectively transmitted, apart from personal fault.

Claims & evidence

Mary was conceived in sin and cleansed before Christ’s birth

the virgin, from whom that man was taken of whom we are speaking, was of the number of those who were cleansed from their sins before his birth.

— Cur Deus Homo (Why God Became Man) II.16, trans. Sidney Norton Deane, source. The passage is a dialogue: Anselm’s interlocutor Boso raises the difficulty that “the virgin herself… was conceived in iniquity, and in sin did her mother bear her” (echoing Psalm 51:5), and Anselm answers not by denying it but by locating Mary’s purification after her conception and before the Lord’s nativity. Mary, for Anselm, was cleansed from original sin; she was not preserved from ever contracting it. This is exactly the position Bernard, Bonaventure, and Aquinas would hold after him — claim (a), not claim (b).1

The raw materials of the doctrine: fittingness, and a “purity than which none greater”

It was fitting that that Virgin should shine with a purity than which, under God, none greater can be conceived.

— De conceptu virginali et de originali peccato (On the Virgin Conception and Original Sin), ch. 18 (Latin: “Decens erat ut ea puritate, qua maior sub Deo nequit intelligi, virgo illa niteret”), trans. Joseph M. Colleran; edition. The phrasing deliberately mirrors Anselm’s own famous formula for God in the Proslogion — “that than which nothing greater can be conceived” — now applied to Mary’s purity. Here is the argument from fittingness (decuit) that the Immaculists would seize on; joined to Anselm’s teaching that original sin is transmitted objectively, apart from the personal fault of the parents, it furnishes both premises the later case would need. Anselm himself did not draw the conclusion — but his disciple Eadmer did, and two centuries on Duns Scotus would raise the same fittingness argument into the “preservative redemption” that carried the day.2

Notes

  • Two works are easily confused here, and the confusion runs back to the older reference books. The “purity than which none greater” line is from Anselm’s genuine De conceptu virginali (ch. 18), not from Cur Deus Homo. And Anselm’s De conceptu virginali is a different work from the Tractatus de conceptione sanctae Mariae, which was long printed among Anselm’s works but is now assigned to Eadmer. Modern critical scholarship (F. S. Schmitt’s edition) keeps De conceptu virginali as authentically Anselm’s.
  • Anselm affirms claim (a) (Mary’s purity, her cleansing before Christ’s birth) while denying claim (b) (preservation from original sin at her own conception). His importance to the doctrine is not as a witness for it but as its unwitting quartermaster: he handed on the tools of the argument to those who would reach the conclusion he declined.
