Does Scotus’s “preservative redemption” dissolve the objection?

Related arguments: universal redemption — see also Does the Immaculate Conception contradict Christ’s universal redemption?

Type: theological-mechanism argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: plausible — as a mechanism, Scotus’s praeredemptio (“preservative” or “pre-” redemption) coherently answers the universal-redemption objection on its own terms and is arguably the doctrine’s strongest single move — but its coherence is not proof that it obtained. The step from “God could preserve her thus” to “he did” is not demonstrative, and the whole construction rests on a Franciscan account of grace and of order-of-nature-vs-time a Thomist may decline. Rate the mechanism plausible; keep separate whether it is true.

The claim

Scotus met Aquinas’s objection not by denying that Mary needed redemption but by redefining what the most perfect redemption is:

he who prevents lest one offend, is a more perfect mediator than he who merely reconciles after the offense.

— John Duns Scotus, Ordinatio III, d. 3, q. 1.1 And so far from standing outside Christ’s saving work, Mary needed it most: “Mary had the greatest need of Christ as Redeemer, for she would have contracted original sin by reason of her descent unless this had been prevented by the grace of a Mediator.”2 The privilege is thus owed to Christ’s redemption, not an exemption from it — a “pre-redemption” (praeredemptio) in which the merits of the not-yet-accomplished Passion are applied by a God outside time to the first instant of Mary’s existence, preserving her from a stain she would otherwise have contracted.

Who advances it

The move is Scotus’s (c. 1300), lecturing at Oxford, though the raw material is older: Eadmer’s “Potuit… et voluit; si igitur voluit, fecit” and the fittingness argument he took from Anselm. Once formulated, preservative redemption prevailed: it carried the schools, was declared “pious and consonant to the Catholic faith” at the (schismatic) Council of Basel (1439), and supplied the frame for the definition itself — Pius IX’s Ineffabilis Deus grants the privilege “in view of the merits of Jesus Christ, the Saviour of the human race.”3

The evidence for

The mechanism is internally consistent and does the work asked of it. Its engine is the logic of the perfect Mediator: a mediator who prevents an offence altogether shows greater mercy and effects a more complete deliverance than one who merely cures it afterwards, so the most perfect Mediator would redeem someone in this most perfect, preservative way — and the fitting recipient is his own mother. Two supports make it hang together. First, the collapse of the temporal objection: Bernard had urged that Mary could not be sanctified before she existed, but Scotus showed the priority of the sanctified state over the sin is one of nature (naturae), not of time (temporis) — in the same instant she is a daughter of Adam liable to the stain and, by grace, preserved from ever contracting it. Second, the proleptic application of merit: God, outside time, can apply the merits of the Cross to a moment chronologically before it, so Mary is redeemed by the Passion though preserved at her conception. Together these turn Aquinas’s objection inside out: Mary is not the one human being who owed Christ nothing but the one who owes him most.4

Adversarial assessment

What holds. As a way of reconciling the privilege with Christ’s universal saviourhood, the mechanism works: it is coherent, it removes the derogation Aquinas feared, and its central intuition — that preventing a fall is a greater deliverance than repairing one — is genuinely attractive and hard to dismiss. For an omnipotent God who is not bound by time, the possibility of a preservative redemption applying the Passion’s merits proleptically is very difficult to rule out. It is, deservedly, the strongest single move in the doctrine’s defence and the one the 1854 definition leaned on.

What is overstated. Coherence of the mechanism is not evidence that it obtained. The inference that completes the case — the Anselmian/Eadmerian potuit, decuit, ergo fecit (“God could, it was fitting, therefore he did”) — is not demonstrative: fittingness is not necessity, and even sympathetic commentators call the leap from decuit to fecit “somewhat dangerous and doubtful.” Scotus himself deployed it only “by way of illustration,” resting the real weight on preservative redemption rather than on the formula. That completing inference — the general form, distinct from this mechanism — is weighed on its own page, Does “potuit, decuit, ergo fecit” prove the Immaculate Conception? (assessment: weak as a proof, plausible as an illuminative mode).5 And the machinery is not neutral: the nature-not-time priority and the proleptic economy of merit trade on a specific Franciscan/Scotist account of grace. A Thomist can grant the whole mechanism as possible without granting that it happened — possibility is not actuality (this wiki’s own caution: that God could act here does not establish that he did). The mechanism shows the doctrine is not incoherent; it does not show it is true.

Assessment

Plausible. As a mechanism — a way the privilege could be reconciled with a universal Redeemer — preservative redemption is coherent, not ruled out, and arguably the doctrine’s strongest single move: it dissolves the Thomist objection on its own terms and became the frame of the 1854 definition. But that a mechanism is possible does not make it actual. The step from “God could preserve her thus” to “he did” is a fittingness inference, not a proof — flagged as “doubtful” even by friends of the doctrine — and the construction presupposes a Scotist account of grace and of order-of-nature-vs-time that a Thomist may decline while conceding the bare possibility. So the mechanism earns plausible, not sound: it answers the objection without settling the fact.
