Does Gregory Nazianzen’s prokathartheisa testify to the Immaculate Conception?

Type: patristic-testimony argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — as a claim that Gregory of Nazianzus’s word prokathartheisa (“prepurified,” Oration 38.13) is a Greek patristic antecedent of Mary’s freedom from original sin. The reading is genuinely disputed and strong by one discipline’s lights, weak by another’s. In its favour: the prefix pro- (“before”) can be pressed for a purity Mary already possessed, later “prepared” rather than cleansed of defilement (as Christ is “purified” in baptism though sinless); the word founds a distinctively Eastern “prepurified / all-holy” vocabulary running to John of Damascus; and Rufinus, translating Gregory into Latin, rendered it immaculata. Against it: on the plain grammar the participle names a purification worked on Mary at the conceiving of Christ — not at her own conception, which is the precise object of the dogma — and purification presupposes something to purify, so the mainstream and Orthodox reading takes the same text to attest a fallen womb “subject to impurity,” telling against even a preservative immaculacy. Neither camp’s reading is compelled by the word alone, which is exactly why one participle became the seed of the entire Eastern debate · advanced, in its immaculist form, by Christiaan Kappes.

See also the New-Testament proof-texts pressed the same way: Does “full of grace” (Luke 1:28) prove the Immaculate Conception? and, on the sister timeline, Does Genesis 3:15 make Mary the serpent-crusher? (the Vulgate ipsa) — each a Marian conclusion resting on how one word is read forward.

The claim

…in all points except sin was made man. Conceived by the Virgin, who first in body and soul was purified by the Holy Ghost (prokathartheisēs) — for it was needful both that Childbearing should be honoured, and that Virginity should receive a higher honour…

— Gregory of Nazianzus, Oration 38 (On the Theophany), §13, repeated verbatim at Oration 45 (On Easter), §9.1 The Greek is προκαθαρθείσης, aorist passive participle of προκαθαίρω, “to purify beforehand.” The argument presses the prefix: if Mary is purified beforehand, the purity is prior to the act it prepares her for — and (so the claim runs) prior all the way to her origin, so that Gregory testifies, in seed, to the Immaculate Conception. This single word is treated as the fountainhead of the Greek tradition of Mary’s “prepurified” and “all-holy” (Panagia) status.

Who advances it

The immaculist reading is put most fully by Christiaan Kappes, who argues that “the Greek Fathers — in the line of the Nazianzen until the introduction of Byzantine Thomism in the 14th century — never vacillated about the all-immaculate status of the Blessed Virgin Mary.” On his account prokathartheisa is not the removal of a defilement but a heightening of an already-pure nature, met head-on to the standard objection that purification implies impurity: even Christ, sinless, is “purified” in the Jordan, so the verb can mean special preparation, not cleansing from stain.2 The reading has a real philological hook and a genuine reception-history behind it; it is not idle apologetics, which is why the question is weighed here rather than dismissed.

The evidence for

Three things can be granted. First, the prefix is not nothing: pro- does mark priority, and Gregory chose the compound over the simple kathairō, so a reading that locates some anteriority in Mary’s purity is lexically available. Second, the Christ-baptism analogy is fair on its own terms: Scripture and the Fathers do use purification language of a sinless subject (Gregory himself says a few lines later that Christ was “purified” in Jordan “though He had no need of purification”), so “purified” need not entail antecedent guilt.3 Third, the reception is real: Gregory’s word seeds a Greek vocabulary of Mary’s “prepurified” and “all-immaculate” holiness, and when Rufinus of Aquileia carried the Nativity oration into Latin (c. 399–400) he rendered prokathartheisa by immaculata — the very word of the later dogma. That the Latin West received the phrase as immaculist is a datum in the reading’s favour.4

Adversarial assessment

What holds. Gregory testifies, richly, to Mary’s unique sanctity and to a purifying work of the Spirit upon her — a genuine and early Eastern datum of her holiness. And the reading is not refuted merely by the objection “purification implies impurity”: the Christ-baptism parallel shows the verb can bear a non-cleansing sense. So far the immaculist has a foothold no honest reading removes.

What is contested, and why it does not settle.

(1) The purification is timed to the Incarnation, not to Mary’s conception. This is the decisive structural point. Gregory’s clause modifies the Virgin at the moment “conceived by the Virgin…” — i.e. when she conceives Christ. The dogma of 1854 concerns the instant Mary’s own soul began to exist. Even granting the fullest immaculist force to pro-, the word reaches back only to “before the conceiving of Christ,” not demonstrably to “before Mary’s own conceiving.” The later Greek Fathers who inherit the term make this explicit: John of Damascus locates the prepurification at the Annunciation (“the sanctifying power of the Spirit… cleansed her and made her holy”), not at Anne’s conceiving of Mary. To get from Gregory’s word to claim (b) one must supply the very thing in dispute.5

(2) On the plain reading the passage tells against a prior immaculacy. The mainstream and Orthodox construal takes purification in its ordinary sense — a cleansing of a womb “postlapsarian… subject to corruption and death” — and reads Gregory as saying the Spirit fitted Mary’s fallen nature for bearing God. On that reading Mary’s flesh, before the Spirit’s act, still shared the ancestral condition; and a nature that must be cleansed to bear Christ is precisely a nature not already free of that condition. Fathers such as Ephrem and Proclus even compare the pre-purified womb to that of “unclean” creatures — meaning mortal, corruptible nature, not moral sin. So the same text that Kappes reads as proto-immaculist, the East reads as its clearest patristic refutation of the Roman dogma.6

(3) The Christ-baptism analogy is a double edge. It rescues “purified” from entailing guilt — but it does not deliver a preserved-from-origin purity either. Christ’s baptismal “purification” is a public sanctifying act at a moment in time, not a statement that He was immaculately conceived; by the analogy’s own logic, Gregory’s clause likewise fixes a sanctifying act at the Incarnation, leaving the instant of Mary’s conception untouched. The parallel neutralises the crude objection without securing the immaculist conclusion.

(4) The Rufinus datum cuts both ways. That Rufinus glossed prokathartheisa as immaculata shows the Latin ear received the phrase as immaculist; it does not show the Greek meant it. A translator’s word-choice, made a generation later in another theological climate, is evidence of reception, not of Gregory’s intent — and the same Latin term would later carry a load (freedom from original sin at conception) the Greek participle nowhere specifies. The artifact is real but proves less than it is asked to prove.

Assessment

Contested. The interpretation is genuinely disputed, and the dispute tracks a real ambiguity in the word rather than a mistake by either side. Read with the weight modern Catholic scholarship gives it — the prefix pro-, the Christ-baptism sense of “purify,” the Greek “all-holy” reception, the Latin immaculata — prokathartheisa is a coherent Eastern antecedent to the Immaculate Conception, and the strongest single patristic hook the case possesses. Read with the weight the grammar and the mainstream/Orthodox tradition give it, the participle names a sanctifying purification worked at the conceiving of Christ on a nature that shared the ancestral condition — the wrong moment and the wrong direction for the dogma, indeed the East’s own proof-text against it. What decides between them is not lexicography alone but a prior theology of the Fall and of Mary’s nature; the word underdetermines the verdict, which is why it seeded a debate rather than closing one. Two things should be kept apart: that Gregory attests Mary’s singular, Spirit-wrought holiness is sound; that his word attests her freedom from original sin from her own conception is a further reading the participle will not compel — strong by the measure of philology and reception, answerable by the measure of the plain grammar and the timing.
