Does the Eastern “all-holy” vocabulary (Panagia) testify to the Immaculate Conception?

Related arguments: the Eastern all-holy vocabulary — see also Does Gregory Nazianzen’s prokathartheisa testify to the Immaculate Conception?

Type: liturgical-testimony argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — as a lex orandi claim that the East’s settled Marian titles (Panagia, “All-Holy”; achrantos, “undefiled”; panamōmos, “all-blameless”) and the hymns of the Feast of Mary’s Conception already contain the Immaculate Conception, the reading is genuinely disputed and strong by one measure, weak by another. In its favour: centuries of “all-immaculate / spotless” predication of Mary, some of it hung on the very day of her conceiving, is exactly the kind of settled worship the axiom lex orandi, lex credendi treats as belief made explicit — the doctrine, on this reading, is the East’s own prayer. Against it: superlative purity language is honorific, not technical — it names Mary’s supreme sanctity without specifying freedom from original sin at the first instant of her conception, and it fits equally the womb-sanctification and the lifelong-cooperation readings the Greek Fathers who coined it actually held; the standard reference itself grants the older feast “is not identical in… object” with the modern dogma; and the very tradition that produced the vocabulary explicitly rejects the Roman definition. So the words resonate with the doctrine without establishing it · pressed in its lex orandi form by Christiaan Kappes, Laurent Cleenewerck, and popular Catholic apologetics; answered by Lossky, Kallistos Ware, and John Sanidopoulos.

The claim

This Day, O Faithful, from saintly parents begins to take being the Spotless Lamb, the Most Pure Tabernacle, Mary… The Unique All-Immaculate is today made manifest to the Just by the Angel.

— hymns from the Byzantine Feast of the Conception of St Anne (9 December).1 The argument runs from these and from the fixed titles that saturate Eastern prayer. Mary is Panagia (“All-Holy”), achrantos (“undefiled”), panamōmos (“all-blameless”); the daily Axion Estin hymns her “ever-blessed and most-pure”; the Small Compline greets her “O spotless, undefiled, incorrupt, immaculate, pure Virgin.” The move is methodological: by lex orandi, lex credendi — “the law of prayer is the law of belief” — what the Church prays discloses what she believes; and the East prays Mary “all-immaculate” from her conception. So (the claim runs) the East’s worship already carries the content the West defined in 1854, and modern Orthodox theology contradicts its own hymnbook.2

Who advances it

The lex orandi case is put by the convert-apologetic literature (OnePeterFive; Laurent Cleenewerck, who concludes that “Orthodox Hymnography presents Mary as Truly Panagia… entirely free from sin and stain (‘Immaculate’) from the point of Her Conception”) and, at the scholarly end, by Christiaan Kappes, who reads the whole Greek “prepurified / all-holy” strand — from the Nazianzen’s prokathartheisa to John of Damascus — as an unbroken confession of Mary’s “all-immaculate status.” The reading has a genuine liturgical corpus behind it and is not idle apologetics, which is why it is weighed here rather than dismissed.3

The evidence for

Three things can be granted. First, the corpus is real and old: the superlative titles are not a stray phrase but the standard register of Byzantine Marian devotion, formalised in the daily office centuries before the Western scholastic dispute. Second, the lex orandi axiom is a serious principle, not a debating trick — it is exactly how the Church has argued elsewhere (the Theotokos title, the Marian feasts) that a settled liturgical predicate encodes a settled belief, and worship that calls Mary “all-immaculate” is at least prima facie evidence of a belief in her all-immaculacy. Third, and most pointedly, some of the hymnody hangs the immaculacy on the conceiving itself: the Feast of the Conception of St Anne calls the child who “begins to take being” the “Spotless Lamb” and “Most Pure Tabernacle,” so the “spotless” predicate is fixed not to Mary’s later life but to the very moment the dogma is about. That is a real hook, and closer to the dogma’s object than the bare honorific titles reach.4

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The vocabulary testifies, richly and early, to Mary’s supreme, singular holiness — that she is “all-holy,” set above every saint and every rank of angels, and (in the East’s own confession) free of all personal sin. On that much the two traditions do not differ, and the lex orandi reading is right that the worship encodes a real Marian maximalism the West later drew on. So far the case has a foothold no honest reading removes.

What is contested, and why it does not settle.

(1) Honorific superlatives underdetermine the technical claim. This is the decisive point. “All-holy,” “spotless,” “undefiled,” “all-blameless” are doxological maxima — they say Mary is as pure as a creature can be. The dogma of 1854 is a narrower and more particular thing: freedom from original sin — the inherited privation of sanctifying grace — at the first instant of her conception. A title can predicate total purity of Mary and leave entirely open when and how she came to be so; the honorific does not carry the technical content. Fr. Lev Gillet makes the concession from the Orthodox side without embarrassment: “Orthodox writers multiplied expressions such as ‘all holy’, ‘all pure’, ‘immaculate’. This does not always mean that these writers believed in the Immaculate Conception.”5

(2) The same titles fit the readings the Fathers who used them actually held. The Greek writers who called Mary Panagia mostly also held that she was purified in her mother’s womb (as John the Baptist and Jeremiah were), or made supremely holy by a lifelong free cooperation with grace — both of which produce a Mary who is “all-holy” and neither of which is the immaculate conception. The predicate is thus consistent with the very positions it is being marshalled against: John of Damascus, the high-water mark of the “all-holy” strand, frames Mary’s holiness as foreknowing sanctification and predestination, not preservation from an inherited guilt. To get from the title to claim (b) one must already read it through the Western category of original guilt — the thing in dispute.6

(3) The feast’s object is not the dogma’s object. The strongest sub-case — the Conception-feast hymns that tie immaculacy to the day of conceiving — runs into the concession the standard Catholic reference makes itself: the older Eastern feast of Mary’s conception and the modern feast of the Immaculate Conception “are not identical in their object.” The East kept the day of her conception and hymned the holiness of the child then conceived; it did not thereby define freedom from original sin at that instant. Liturgical poetry that calls the conceived child “spotless” is praising the sanctity of the person, on the plain miracle of barren Anne’s conceiving — not legislating the technical claim about the state of her soul in its first moment.7

(4) The tradition that coined the words rejects the dogma. The heaviest weight against a straight lex orandi inference is that the very Church whose prayer this is declines the conclusion. If the hymns entailed the Roman doctrine, the East that sings them would have to hold it; instead its theologians — Lossky, Ware, Sanidopoulos — read the same titles as naming Mary’s sanctity as outcome, “her lifelong cooperation with God’s grace, not a miraculous exemption from the effects of the Fall,” and take the vocabulary to presuppose a Mary who shares the common lot of fallen humanity. That a whole tradition prays these words and denies the dogma is itself strong evidence that the words do not entail it — a lex orandi argument cannot prove to a Church a doctrine that Church has always understood its own prayer not to teach.8

Assessment

Contested. The interpretation is genuinely disputed, and the dispute tracks a real feature of the evidence rather than a mistake by either side. Read with the weight the lex orandi case gives it — a vast, ancient corpus of “all-immaculate” predication, some of it fixed to the conceiving itself — the Eastern vocabulary is a coherent liturgical antecedent to the Immaculate Conception, and the fullest reservoir of “proto-(b)” material the case possesses. Read with the weight the grammar of praise and the mainstream/Orthodox tradition give it, the titles are honorific maxima that name Mary’s supreme sanctity without fixing its origin, fit equally the womb-sanctification and lifelong-cooperation readings, and are prayed to this day by a Church that denies the dogma they are said to contain. What decides between the two is not the wordbook but a prior theology of the Fall and of Mary’s nature — the same fault line that runs under the Eastern-anthropology objection; the vocabulary underdetermines the verdict. Two things should be kept apart: that the East’s prayer attests Mary’s unique, all-holy sanctity is sound; that it attests her freedom from original sin from the first instant of her own conception is a further reading the honorific will not compel — strong by the measure of lex orandi and reception, answerable by the measure of what a superlative title actually specifies and by the dissent of the tradition that sings it. It resonates with the doctrine; it does not establish it.
