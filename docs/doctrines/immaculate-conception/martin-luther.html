Martin Luther on the Immaculate Conception of Mary

Dates: c. 1527–1540 (the Festival Postil sermon On the Day of the Conception of Mary the Mother of God, 1527, and the Disputation on the Divinity and Humanity of Christ, 1540; Martin Luther, 1483–1546, Augustinian friar and Reformer) · Wikipedia
Summary: Luther is the timeline’s one early-Reformation voice on Mary’s conception — and a genuinely mixed witness whose view moved across thirteen years. In a 1527 sermon he entertained, as “a sweet and pious belief,” that Mary’s soul was infused without original sin, purified “in the very infusion of her soul” — a soul-infusion version of claim (b), but pinned to ensoulment (his “second conception”) rather than to bodily conception, and offered only as a pious opinion, not an article of faith. The passage was then dropped from every reprint after 1527. By 1540 he located the purification not at Mary’s conception at all but at Christ’s — “in conception the flesh and blood of Mary were entirely purged, so that nothing of sin remained” — and in sermons of 1539–40 he taught that Mary, like every believer, was conceived and born in sin and made free from it by faith. Early Luther grazes claim (b); mature Luther retreats to a Christ-centred purification and beyond, so that few scholars read him as holding the 1854 dogma across his life.

Claims & evidence

The 1527 sermon — purification “in the very infusion of her soul”

Preaching on the feast of Mary’s conception, Luther set out — cautiously, as a “sweet and pious belief” rather than a settled doctrine — a soul-infusion account of her purity from the first instant she lived:

It is a sweet and pious belief that the infusion of Mary’s soul was effected without original sin; so that in the very infusion of her soul she was also purified from original sin and adorned with God’s gifts, receiving a pure soul infused by God; thus from the first moment she began to live she was free from all sin.

— On the Day of the Conception of Mary the Mother of God (Am tage der Empfengknus Marie der mutter Gottes), in the Festival Postil compiled by Stephan Roth, 1527.1 The mechanism is Luther’s distinction between two “conceptions”: a first conception of the body, drawn by ordinary generation from her parents, and a second conception — the infusion of the soul by God — at which the purifying grace falls. Locating the exemption at ensoulment, not at the body’s conception, makes this a version of claim (b) built on the medieval delayed-animation embryology the scholastics argued within: it grazes the doctrine 1854 would define while stopping short of “the first instant of her conception” in the dogma’s sense. And Luther expressly declined to bind it, adding (in the Erlangen printing) that “everyone may think what he wants, as long as he makes no article of faith about it.”

The deletion — the passage dropped after 1527

The 1527 statement is the fullest Luther ever gave, and it did not survive in his corpus. It appears in the 1527 Festival Postil (Roth’s compilation of Luther’s preaching) but was removed from the 1528 and all later editions printed in Luther’s lifetime; the Erlangen edition marks the end of the passage with a note that from that point “to the end” the words are “only found in the edition of the year 1527.” The deletion is documented across the confessional divide — by the Catholic historians Hartmann Grisar and Nicholas Paulus, the Lutheran Eric Gritsch, and Ewald Plass (What Luther Says).2 Two cautions follow from this and belong on the record: the Festival Postil is Roth’s redaction of sermons, not a treatise Luther penned, so the exact wording carries an editor’s hand; and the excision — whoever made it — means the doctrine was not one Luther chose to keep before his readers.

The 1540 Disputation — purification at Christ’s conception, not Mary’s

Thirteen years on, in the Disputation on the Divinity and Humanity of Christ (27–28 February 1540), Luther again speaks of Mary’s flesh being “purged” — but now the purification is worked at the conception of Christ, to secure the sinlessness of the flesh the Son took, and says nothing of Mary’s own conception:

Every man who is not a divine Person, as is Christ, has concupiscence, but the man Christ has none, because he is a divine Person, and in conception the flesh and blood of Mary were entirely purged, so that nothing of sin remained. Therefore Isaiah says rightly, “There was no guile found in his mouth”; otherwise, every seed except for Mary’s was corrupted.

— Disputation on the Divinity and Humanity of Christ, thesis X (response), trans. Christopher B. Brown.3 Read in context the “conception” here is the Incarnation: the Holy Spirit purges Mary’s flesh and blood at the conceiving of the Son so that Christ’s humanity comes without concupiscence — the same Christ-centred purification found in Peter Lombard and the pre-Scotist schools, not a preservation of Mary at her own conception. Catholic readers press the phrase toward Mary’s immaculacy; the plain sense, and the Reformed reading, take it as a purifying of the flesh Christ assumed, leaving Mary otherwise a daughter of Adam.

The later retreat — Mary “conceived and born in sin… free by faith”

The mature Luther went further still, treating Mary as every believer is treated. In sermons of 1539 and 1540 he taught that she was conceived and born in sin from sinful parents like the rest of Adam’s line, and became blessed and free from sin by faith — the position Julius Köstlin long ago identified as Luther’s settled teaching.4 This is not claim (b) at all, nor even a distinctive claim (a) about a unique preservation; it is Luther’s general doctrine of justification applied to Mary, and it is where his development came to rest.

Notes

  • A mixed (a)/(b) witness, read carefully. The summary timeline turns on the distinction between (a) Mary’s sinless life and (b) her freedom from original sin at conception. Luther is the rare witness who moves across it: the 1527 sermon touches (b) at ensoulment; the 1540 Disputation retreats to a Christ-centred purification of her flesh; the 1539–40 sermons deny even a unique exemption, making Mary sinless only as believers are, by faith. He should not be filed simply “for” the doctrine.
  • The text-critical caveat. The strongest quote (1527) circulates most in Catholic apologetics, and its transmission is genuinely disputed: it stands in an editor’s postil, was cut from every reprint after 1527, and its ending is flagged in the Erlangen edition as peculiar to that first printing. The wiki reports it with that history attached rather than as Luther’s fixed view.
  • Chronology on this timeline. Luther’s witness sits between Cajetan’s maculist treatise (1515) and the Council of Trent’s exemption of Mary from its original-sin decree (1546) — the one Reformation-era voice on the question the Council would step around.
