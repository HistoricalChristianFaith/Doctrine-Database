Durandus of Saint-Pourçain on the Immaculate Conception

Dates: c. 1275–1334 · Dominican; bishop of Le Puy, then Meaux · Wikipedia
Summary: Durandus of Saint-Pourçain (Durand de Saint-Pourçain), a Dominican famous for thinking against the grain of his own order — the “Doctor Resolutissimus,” repeatedly censured for departing from Aquinas — is the one early-fourteenth-century Dominican who leaned toward affirming claim (b). Against the maculist consensus of his brethren he held that Mary was conceived without original sin, reasoning that the union of her soul with her body and the gift of sanctifying grace happened at one and the same instant, so that no interval remained in which she could have contracted the stain. Notably he reached this by his own route — from Augustine, Anselm, and Richard of St-Victor, not from Scotus, whom he does not cite.

Claims & evidence

He held that the Virgin was conceived without original sin — ⚠ reported on secondary authority

Modern scholarship reads Durandus’s Sentences commentary (Book III, dist. 3, q. 1) as arguing that Mary “was conceived without original sin, since the union of soul and body and the bestowal of sanctifying grace took place at one and the same moment.” Bogusław Kochaniewicz, surveying the Dominican record, singles him out:

…it is the first testimony of Dominican theological reflection, which is part of the current of supporters of the Immaculate Conception… it cannot be maintained that all Dominicans were against the Immaculate Conception of Mary.

— B. Kochaniewicz, “The Stance of the Dominicans Towards the Immaculate Conception…,” Scripta Theologica 56 (2024), p. 573, source.1 The wording of Durandus’s own text has not been checked here against the 1571 Venice printing on which the account rests, so the attribution is carried as an open lead rather than a verified quotation.

His supports and authorities were pre-Scotist

The reasons Durandus adduces, on this reading, are the incorruption of Mary’s body foreshadowed by her sinless origin, the fittingness of a “most perfect” mediation for the mother of the Redeemer, and above all the simultaneity of ensoulment and grace — a mechanism that dispenses with the “instant of contracted guilt” the maculists insisted on. What is striking, Kochaniewicz emphasises, is that he “did not quote Duns Scotus. Instead, he relied on the texts of Augustine, Anselm and Richard of St. Victor,” even as his answer to the redemption objection “coincides with the teachings of Duns Scotus.” A Dominican arriving at the Franciscan conclusion by a Dominican road.1

Notes

  • The exception that proves the rule. Durandus is the outlier against a hardening consensus. His order’s official line ran the other way, and its enforcers were formidable: Herveus Natalis (Hervé Nédellec), Master General of the Dominicans 1318–1323, “was not only against the Immaculate Conception, but also polemicized with the position of [its] supporters,” arguing from the universality of original sin and of Christ’s redemption; John of Naples (Giovanni Regina di Napoli, d. c. 1336) belonged to the same Thomist camp — indeed he helped draft the list of Durandus’s own deviations from Aquinas that the order censured. Durandus’s pro-Immaculist leaning is thus doubly against the current: against the Marian consensus of his brethren, and out of the same independent streak that got him disciplined.1
  • A caution on the source. Kochaniewicz’s abstract describes the Dominican position as “negative and homogeneous,” while his treatment of Durandus argues the fourteenth-century picture was not homogeneous and makes Durandus the exception — an emphasis worth registering. The underlying primary reference is Durandus, Super IV Sententiarum, Lib. III, d. 3, q. 1 (Venice, 1571), fol. 217r–v, not consulted directly here.
