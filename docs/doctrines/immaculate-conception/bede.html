The Venerable Bede on “full of grace” (Luke 1:28) and the Immaculate Conception

Dates: c. 735 (the timeline slot; the Northumbrian monk of Wearmouth–Jarrow, c. 672/673 – 735, called “the Venerable” and the father of English history — his Commentary on Luke (In Lucae evangelium expositio), dedicated to Bishop Acca of Hexham, was written c. 709–716, and his Homilies on the Gospels somewhat later) · Wikipedia
Summary: Bede expounds Gabriel’s gratia plena (“full of grace,” Luke 1:28) as richly as anyone in the early West — but he grounds the fullness in the divine maternity and in Mary’s virginity, not in any preservation from original sin at her conception. She is “rightly… called full of grace, because she obviously attained the grace that no other had merited, that she would conceive and bear the author of grace himself.” He draws no immaculate conception from the greeting; in his Annunciation homily he even locates her sanctification “afterwards,” through the human nature Christ took from her — i.e. through the Incarnation, not before it. He is thus the decisive internal datum weighed on the kecharitōmenē proof-text argument (assessment: weak): the witness who reads “full of grace” most fully, and does not find the dogma in it.

Claims & evidence

“Full of grace” = fitted to bear the Author of grace (Luke 1:28)

Commenting on the salutation, Bede fixes the meaning of gratia plena not on Mary’s conception but on what she is chosen for — to conceive and bear the incarnate Word, the source of grace himself:

And the angel entered to her and said, Hail, full of grace, the Lord is with you: blessed are you among women. It is rightly that she is called full of grace, because she obviously attained the grace that no other had merited, that she would conceive and bear the author of grace himself.

— Commentary on Luke (In Lucae evangelium expositio), Book I, on Luke 1:28.1 The grammar of Bede’s gloss is worth marking: the grace “no other had merited” is defined by its purpose clause — that she would conceive and bear the author of grace himself. The fullness is the fullness of one appointed to the divine maternity; it is not, in Bede, a freedom from the stain of original sin dated to the first instant of her existence. On the next verse he adds that the angel, having called her full of grace, “further establishes that grace and explains it more abundantly” — and the explanation Bede supplies is again the conception and bearing of the Lord, never a preservation from sin.2

The fullness unfolded — virginity, maternity, and a sanctification “afterwards” (Homilies 1.3)

Bede expounds the same greeting at length in his Annunciation homily. Three times he repeats “truly she was full of grace,” and each time he cashes the phrase out in a concrete privilege — her primacy in vowed virginity, her worthiness to see and hear an angel, and above all her bearing of Christ — not in an immaculate conception:

Truly she was full of grace, to whom it was granted by divine gift, that she might be the first among women to offer to God the most glorious gift of virginity. Therefore, she rightly deserved to enjoy at once the sight and speech of an angel, who strove to imitate the angelic life. Truly she was full of grace, to whom it was granted to bear Jesus Christ Himself, through whom grace and truth came into being, and afterwards, through the human nature, consecrated all fullness of divinity. Truly also blessed among women was she who, without example of female condition, rejoiced with the honor of motherhood along with the beauty of virginity, and who, as befitted a virgin mother, bore God the Son.

— Homilies on the Gospels 1.3 (on the Annunciation, Luke 1:26–38).3 The telling clause is the last of the second sentence: the grace by which the fullness of divinity is “consecrated” in her comes afterwards (afterwards, through the human nature) — that is, through the Incarnation she serves, when the Word takes flesh from her. Bede orders the sanctifying grace to the moment Christ is conceived in her, not to the moment she was conceived. (The point comes through even more sharply in the Cistercian rendering: the Lord “first raised [her] up from earthly to heavenly desires… and afterwards sanctified [her], by means of his human nature, with all the fullness of his divinity.”)4

Notes

  • Why he sits on this timeline. Bede is adduced here not as a proponent of the Immaculate Conception — he never teaches it — but as the internal counter-datum on the doctrine’s central New-Testament proof-text. The kecharitōmenē argument presses Luke 1:28 (“full of grace”) as proof that Mary was preserved from original sin from conception; the decisive objection is that the fathers who expound the phrase most lavishly draw no such conclusion from it. Bede is that objection in its clearest early form: the richest Latin reading of gratia plena before the schools, tied wholly to the divine maternity.
  • Not a denial of the dogma either. Bede is no more an opponent of the Immaculate Conception than a proponent — the question is simply not before him. Writing c. 709–716, four centuries before the controversy took shape, he neither affirms nor rules out a preservative grace at Mary’s conception; he expounds the greeting in the terms available to him, and those terms are maternity and virginity. What his page establishes is negative and exact: the proof-text, read by its most eloquent early expositor, does not carry the later inference.
  • Company on the argument page. He stands with Bonaventure, the other witness weighed on the kecharitōmenē page — who lavishes still more praise on gratia plena yet, in his Sentences commentary, held that Mary’s soul contracted original sin and was sanctified only shortly after animation. In both doctors the fullest exposition of “full of grace” coexists with an absence (Bede) or an explicit denial (Bonaventure) of the immaculate conception — the pattern the argument turns on.
  • Text and transmission. Bede’s Luke commentary, woven largely from Ambrose, Augustine, Gregory the Great, and Jerome, became a staple of Carolingian and medieval exegesis and fed the Glossa Ordinaria; his Annunciation homily circulated as a fixed lection. Through both, this maternity-centred reading of “full of grace” reached the very schoolmen who would later dispute the Immaculate Conception — and it gave them no warrant in Luke 1:28 for it.
