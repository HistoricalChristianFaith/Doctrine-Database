John Capreolus on the Immaculate Conception

Dates: c. 1380–1444 · Dominican; Defensiones Bk III written c. 1426–32 · Wikipedia
Summary: John Capreolus (Jean Cabrol), hailed as the princeps thomistarum, the “Prince of Thomists,” devoted his life’s one work — the Defensiones theologiae Divi Thomae Aquinatis — to defending Aquinas against his fourteenth-century critics, chief among them the immaculists Scotus and Peter Auriol. On Mary’s conception he holds the Thomist line without softening it: she was conceived in original sin and sanctified only after her soul was infused — claim (a), not (b). He is the maculist restatement between Scotus’s breakthrough and the institutional acts of the fifteenth century.

Claims & evidence

He concludes that the Blessed Virgin was conceived in original sin

…beata Maria fuit concepta in peccato originali. Hanc conclusionem tenet beatus Thomas…

— Capreolus, Defensiones theologiae Divi Thomae Aquinatis, Bk III, dist. 3, q. 1 (“the Blessed Mary was conceived in original sin; this conclusion St Thomas holds”), Paban–Pègues edition (Tours, 1900–08), vol. 5, p. 26.1 He sets it out as the single conclusion (unica conclusio) of the article and then marshals Aquinas’s reasoning behind it, adding that this is why “blessed Bernard wrote to the people of Lyons that her conception was not to be celebrated” — the same Bernard letter that opens the Western denial of the doctrine.

He answers the immaculists point by point — defending Aquinas against Scotus and Auriol

The Defensiones is structured as a running rebuttal: for each distinction of the Sentences Capreolus states Aquinas’s teaching, then the objections of Scotus, Auriol, Durandus and others, then his replies. On Mary’s sanctification (Bk III, d. 3) he takes the immaculist arguments — including the fittingness reasoning that God could and therefore did preserve her — and turns them back on the ground the maculists never surrendered: the universality of original sin and of Christ’s redemption, from which an exemption at conception would remove Mary. It is the objection of Aquinas carried forward a century and a half, now aimed squarely at the Scotist school that had answered it.1

Notes

  • On the text. The Latin above is transcribed from the Paban–Pègues critical edition as digitized on archive.org; the page’s scanned text is imperfect, so the exact orthography should be confirmed against a clean image of p. 26. The substance — Capreolus’s single maculist conclusion at Bk III, d. 3, q. 1, credited to Aquinas — is secure and matches Aquinas, Summa III, q. 27, a. 2.
  • Where he sits. Capreolus (d. 1444) writes on the eve of the Council of Basel (1439), whose schismatic session would declare for the doctrine he here denies. The century between Scotus and Basel is often told as the immaculists’ steady advance; Capreolus is the reminder that the Thomist denial was restated with full force in the same period, keeping the question a live Dominican–Franciscan dispute rather than a settled one.
