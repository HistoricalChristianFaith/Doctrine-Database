The Council of Basel on the Immaculate Conception

Dates: 17 September 1439 (Session 36) · Wikipedia
Summary: The Council of Basel declared the immaculate conception a pious doctrine, conformable to the faith, and forbade preaching against it — the first conciliar body to do so. But by September 1439 Basel had broken with Pope Eugene IV and lapsed into schism; its later sessions are not reckoned ecumenical, and this decree bound no one. The question stayed open for another four centuries.

Claims & evidence

Basel declares the doctrine pious and conformable to the faith

This doctrine asserting that the glorious Virgin Mary, Mother of God, through the special preventing and operating grace of the divine Being, was never actually subjected to original sin, but was always exempt from original and actual sin, holy and immaculate, we define and declare as pious, and consonant to the Catholic faith, to right reason, and to Sacred Scripture… that it is not lawful for anyone to preach or to teach anything to the contrary.

— Council of Basel, Session 36 (17 September 1439), a standard English rendering.1 On its face this is a strong endorsement of claim (b) — preservation from original sin by a “preventing” grace, the very idea Scotus had made defensible a century and a half earlier. It reads as the doctrine’s first official victory.

Why it did not settle anything: Basel was in schism

The force of the decree collapses on its provenance. By 1437 Eugene IV had transferred the council to Ferrara (and thence to Florence); the rump that stayed at Basel defied him, and on 25 June 1439 — less than three months before this session — it purported to depose the reigning pope as a heretic, later electing the antipope Felix V. Its sessions from this period are, by common consent, not sessions of an ecumenical council: “all agree that the sessions held at Basel after the 25th session… cannot be regarded as sessions of an ecumenical council,” and the standard reference calls the post-transfer body a “schismatical conventicle.”2 A schismatic assembly cannot bind the Church, and Rome did not treat the decree as settling the matter: Sixtus IV would insist in 1483 that “up to this time there has been no decision made by the Roman Church and the Apostolic See.”

Notes

  • Basel marks the point at which the Immaculist side won an institutional hearing, even though it won it from a body that had put itself outside the Church’s authority. The decree is genuinely pro-(b), and it fed the momentum that carried to 1854; but it is not, and was not then treated as, a binding definition.
  • The English wording above is well attested but the original Latin of Session 36 was not confirmed here against a primary conciliar edition; for the Latin see Mansi, Sacrorum Conciliorum… Collectio, or Denzinger, Enchiridion Symbolorum.
