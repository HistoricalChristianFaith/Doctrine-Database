Theodotus of Ancyra on the Immaculate Conception

Dates: d. c. 446 (Theodotus, bishop of Ancyra; a quasi-official theologian and anti-Nestorian preacher at the Council of Ephesus, 431; the Marian-purity text is his Oration on the Mother of God) · Wikipedia
Summary: Theodotus is the second Ephesus-era Greek witness whose “spotless” Marian rhetoric is pressed toward the Immaculate Conception. His Oration on the Mother of God praises Mary as “a virgin innocent, without spot, void of culpability, holy in body and in soul… untaught the ills of Eve.” This is Eve-typology purity — claim (a) — but the same passage adds a striking touch that leans a step further: Mary, “when not yet born, was consecrated to God.” That is prenatal sanctification (on the model of Jeremiah and John the Baptist), a proto-(b) note — yet it is sanctification in the womb, not preservation from inherited guilt at the instant of conception, which is the precise object of the later dogma. He belongs on this timeline as the Greek father who comes closest to (b) in this era’s idiom while still stopping short of it.

Claims & evidence

“A virgin innocent, without spot… untaught the ills of Eve”

A virgin innocent, without spot, void of culpability, holy in body and in soul, a lily springing among thorns, untaught the ills of Eve, nor was there any communion in her of light with darkness, and, when not yet born, she was consecrated to God.

— Oration on the Mother of God (§11), as quoted in the Catholic Encyclopedia’s catalogue of witnesses to “the absolute purity of Mary.”1 Read as a whole the sentence is Eve-typology: Mary is the new and undefiled woman set against the old, “a lily springing among thorns,” “untaught the ills of Eve” — praise of her purity of life and nature (claim (a)) against the backdrop of the Fall, not a technical claim about original sin at her conception. It is of a piece with his elsewhere greeting her as “the immaculate Mother of holiness.”2

The proto-(b) touch: “when not yet born, she was consecrated to God”

The clause that reaches furthest is the last: Mary, when not yet born, was consecrated to God. This is a genuine step beyond the mere praise of a sinless life — it locates a divine setting-apart before birth. But its model is the biblical sanctification in the womb of Jeremiah (“before I formed thee in the belly I knew thee… I sanctified thee,” Jer. 1:5) and John the Baptist (Luke 1:15) — a consecration of the already-conceived child, not a preservation of the soul from original sin at the first instant of its existence. That distinction is the whole hinge of the later doctrine: Bernard would grant Mary exactly this womb-sanctification (“like Jeremiah and John the Baptist”) while denying the immaculate conception proper, precisely because holiness presupposes existence. Theodotus supplies the proto-(b) vocabulary; he does not draw, and his idiom does not require, the conclusion 1854 would draw.

Notes

  • (a) with a proto-(b) touch. Theodotus’s dominant register is Eve-typology purity (claim (a)); his one prenatal-consecration clause leans toward (b) but names a sanctification in the womb, not preservation at conception. He is the fittest Ephesus-era Greek exemplar of how close this vocabulary came to the later doctrine while still stopping short of it — a distinction the timeline keeps explicit.
  • The quoted Oration reaches us through the standard reference (the Catholic Encyclopedia, which quotes it; also catalogued in Michael O’Carroll, Theotokos, s.v. “Theodotus of Ancyra”); the Greek is in Migne, Patrologia Graeca 77.
  • His fellow Ephesus-era preacher Proclus of Constantinople shows the same “spotless” rhetoric without the prenatal-consecration touch. The Greek “prepurified / all-holy” strand that comes nearest to (b) — from Gregory of Nazianzus to John of Damascus — is weighed on the prokathartheisa argument page. Theodotus is also a witness on the Theotokos timeline as an anti-Nestorian at Ephesus.
