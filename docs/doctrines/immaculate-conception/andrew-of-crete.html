The Eastern Feast of Mary’s Conception — Andrew of Crete and Germanus of Constantinople

Dates: 7th–8th century (Andrew of Crete, c. 660–740; Germanus of Constantinople, c. 634–740) · Wikipedia
Summary: Before there was a doctrine of the immaculate conception there was a feast of Mary’s conception — the conception of Mary by her mother Anne, kept in the Christian East from the seventh century. Its two earliest voices, Andrew of Crete and Germanus of Constantinople, hymn Mary as all-pure and undefiled; but the feast celebrates the event of her conception, not the later dogmatic claim that she was preserved from original sin at it.

Claims & evidence

The feast of the Conception of St Anne (the conception of Mary), 7th century East

The feast keeping the conception of Mary by Anne originated in the monasteries of Palestine no later than the end of the seventh century and spread from the cloister to the cathedral churches, fixed on 9 December in the Eastern calendars (the West would later move it to 8 December). The oldest surviving witness to it is the liturgical Canon composed by Andrew of Crete while a monk at the monastery of St Sabas near Jerusalem, in the second half of the seventh century. That the feast was not yet universal even a generation later is clear from John of Euboea (c. 750), who says it “was not yet known to all the faithful.”1 The decisive point for this timeline is one the standard reference itself draws: “The older feast of the Conception of Mary… and the modern feast of the Immaculate Conception are not identical in their object.” The East kept the day of Mary’s conception; it did not thereby define her freedom from original sin.

Andrew and Germanus praise Mary as all-pure and undefiled

O undefiled, untainted, uncorrupted, most pure, chaste Virgin… who didst unite the Word of God to mankind through thy most glorious birth-giving.

— Andrew of Crete, Homily on the Nativity of the Theotokos.2 Germanus of Constantinople hymns her in the same key, calling her “wholly immaculate” and praising one “having no affinity with sin whatever.” This is exalted purity-language, and it is genuine; but it is the diffuse Eastern idiom of Mary the Panagia (“all-holy”) and Achrantos (“undefiled”), a praise of her holiness that does not turn on the Western question of original guilt and its transmission by generation. It witnesses claim (a); it neither raises nor settles claim (b).

Notes

  • The feast reached England early: it was kept at Canterbury by about 1030, among the Anglo-Saxon Marian devotions, until Lanfranc suppressed it in his post-Conquest reordering of the calendar (c. 1070). It was revived in the twelfth century — by Eadmer and by Anselm the Younger (nephew of St Anselm) at Bury St Edmunds around 1120 — and it was in that English revival that the feast of the conception first began to carry a claim about Mary’s sinlessness, not merely her conception.
  • The verbatim English lines above circulate through devotional anthologies and the standard scholarly conduits (Hilda Graef’s Mary: A History of Doctrine and Devotion; Michael O’Carroll’s Theotokos); the Greek originals are in the Patrologia Graeca (Andrew, PG 97; Germanus, PG 98). They should be read as praise-poetry, not as dogmatic definition.
