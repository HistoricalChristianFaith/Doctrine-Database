Ramon Llull on the Immaculate Conception

Dates: c. 1232–1315/16 · the Marian argument in the Disputatio eremitae et Raimundi, Paris, autumn 1298 · Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy
Summary: Ramon Llull (Raymond Lull), the Majorcan lay Franciscan and philosopher, argued for Mary’s freedom from original sin at Paris in the autumn of 1298 — roughly contemporary with, and by some reckonings just before, Scotus. Reasoning in the demonstrative style of his “Art,” he held that God and sin cannot coexist in the one subject who was to bear the incarnate Son, and so Mary did not contract the stain. His stated mechanism is his own and pre-Scotist — a sanctification of the very matter from which she was formed, not the “preservative redemption in view of Christ’s merits” that Scotus would frame. A popular claim that Llull was the first to coin the phrase “Immaculate Conception” is an apologetic commonplace and should not be asserted: the standard reference literature does not credit him, and the expression is nowhere fixed in the liturgical books before 1854.

Claims & evidence

God and sin cannot be united in one subject — so Mary did not contract original sin

In the Disputatio eremitae et Raimundi super aliquibus dubiis quaestionibus Sententiarum (a hermit-and-Ramon dialogue on Peter Lombard’s Sentences, written at Paris in the autumn of 1298), question 96, Llull argues — in the rendering of the Marian historian Luigi Gambero — that “the Son of God could not become incarnate in a mother who was stained by sin in any way: God and sin cannot be united in the one and same object,” so that “the Blessed Virgin Mary did not contract original sin; rather she was sanctified in the instant in which the seed from which she was formed was detached from her parents.” He adds a parallel between Christ’s and Mary’s conceptions (they should share a “like nature”), and an argument that the second creation — the Redemption begun in Christ and Mary — had, like the first, to open under the sign of total innocence.1 Provenance: the wording follows Gambero’s English translation of question 96; the underlying Latin (in the Mainz Opera Omnia and the 2024 critical edition) is not reproduced verbatim here, so this is a faithful scholarly rendering, not a quotation checked against the primary text.

Notes

  • His mechanism is not Scotus’s. Llull affirms the conclusion of claim (b) — Mary did not contract original sin — firmly, and argues it by “necessary reasons” rather than proof-texts. But his stated mechanism, a sanctification of the seminal matter at the moment it is “detached from her parents,” is a sanctification-of-the-material model of his own, distinct from and pre-dating the Scotist preservative redemption applied “in view of the merits of Jesus Christ.” Keep the two apart: he reaches the same conclusion by a different road.
  • The “first to use the phrase” claim — genuinely disputed, so state it with care. Gambero writes flatly that Llull “is the first author to use the expression ‘Immaculate Conception’ to designate the Virgin’s exemption from original sin,” and Franciscan and devotional sources repeat it. But the older reference literature does not credit him: the Catholic Encyclopedia never names him in that connection and describes the term as evolving gradually out of the feast of Mary’s Conception, noting that Immaculata Conceptio “is nowhere found in the liturgical books” before the 1854 definition. He is safely called one of the earliest to argue and publicly teach the doctrine at Paris; whether he was the first to fix the very phrase is contested, not settled.
  • Legend to set aside. The stories that Llull met Duns Scotus in 1297, that this won him the name Doctor Illuminatus, and that he directly shaped Scotus’s immaculism are pious embellishment; sober biography (the Stanford Encyclopedia) records the 1298–99 Paris residence without them. What stands is the chronology: his 1298 argument precedes Scotus’s Paris defense.
