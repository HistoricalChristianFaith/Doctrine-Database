Pope Pius IX and the definition of the Immaculate Conception

Dates: 2 February 1849 (Ubi primum, the consultation of the bishops) · 8 December 1854 (Ineffabilis Deus, the definition) · reigned 1846–1878 (1792–1878) · Wikipedia
Summary: Pius IX first polled the whole episcopate on the Immaculate Conception (the 1849 encyclical Ubi primum), and then, on the strength of a near-unanimous reply, closed the centuries-long dispute with the bull Ineffabilis Deus — defining the doctrine as a dogma to be held by all the faithful, and adopting, in substance, the “preserved from the first instant” view Duns Scotus had made defensible against the scholastic objection.

The consultation of the episcopate — Ubi primum (1849)

Before defining anything, Pius IX asked the whole Church what it believed. Writing from exile at Gaeta — he had fled Rome in the revolution of 1848 — he issued the encyclical Ubi primum on 2 February 1849, putting two questions to every bishop: how great was the devotion of their clergy and people toward the Immaculate Conception and their desire for its definition, and what the bishops themselves, in their own judgment, thought and desired.

We eagerly desire, furthermore, that, as soon as possible, you apprise Us concerning the devotion which animates your clergy and your people regarding the Immaculate Conception of the Blessed Virgin and how ardently glows the desire that this doctrine be defined by the Apostolic See. And especially, Venerable Brethren, We wish to know what you yourselves, in your wise judgment, think and desire on this matter.

— Ubi primum, given at Gaeta, 2 February 1849.1 The occasion, the encyclical explains, was that already under Gregory XVI “there was in the entire Catholic world a most ardent and wondrous revival of the desire” that the Mother of God “be finally declared by a solemn definition of the Church to have been conceived without the stain of original sin.”

The replies made the definition all but a formality. Of the roughly six hundred bishops who answered, some 536 favoured defining the doctrine; only a handful — four or five, among them the Archbishop of Paris — were opposed outright, and about three dozen, mostly from Protestant countries, judged only that the moment was inopportune, not that the doctrine was false.2 Pius IX would rehearse this near-unanimous consultation in Ineffabilis Deus itself as evidence of the mind of the Church. The method is as notable as the result: a formal canvass of the entire episcopate as the deliberate run-up to the definition — the run-up that the late-development question weighs, doctrine made explicit by first consulting the bishops and the faithful before Rome spoke.

Claims & evidence

The dogmatic definition — preserved from all stain of original sin from the first instant

We declare, pronounce, and define that the doctrine which holds that the most Blessed Virgin Mary, in the first instant of her conception, by a singular grace and privilege granted by Almighty God, in view of the merits of Jesus Christ, the Savior of the human race, was preserved free from all stain of original sin, is a doctrine revealed by God and therefore to be believed firmly and constantly by all the faithful.

— Ineffabilis Deus, 8 December 1854, source. Every load-bearing element of the developed doctrine is here: the moment (“the first instant of her conception”); the mode (“preserved,” not cleansed after contracting — Scotus’s preservative redemption); and the answer to the old objection (“in view of the merits of Jesus Christ, the Savior of the human race” — so Mary is redeemed by Christ, not exempted from his saviourhood). This is the immaculate conception proper, claim (b), defined at last.

The scriptural warrant leans on the Genesis 3:15 “woman”

Among the grounds the bull adduces is the ancient reading of Genesis 3:15 — the enmity God sets between the serpent and “the woman” and her seed — applied to Mary as the woman wholly at odds with the serpent and untouched by his power. That typology grows out of the patristic New-Eve reading of Mary, whose development is traced on its own timeline; Ineffabilis Deus gathers it, together with the tradition’s praises of Mary as “all-holy” and “immaculate,” into the case for the definition.3 One caution on how far the text can be pressed: the older Latin reading “she shall crush thy head” (Vulgate ipsa conteret) that made Mary herself the serpent-slayer rests on an interpretive Latin pronoun, not on the Hebrew or the Greek — the crusher is “her seed” (Christ), and the woman’s share is to stand at enmity with the serpent. That distinction is weighed on the argument page “Does Genesis 3:15 make Mary the serpent-crusher?”

The bull’s other scriptural anchor is the angelic salutation — Mary “proclaimed full of grace” (Luke 1:28), which the Fathers, it says, took to show “that the Mother of God is the seat of all divine graces and is adorned with all gifts of the Holy Spirit.” Notably the bull adduces the greeting for a plenitude of grace, not as a direct proof of preservation from original sin — the same restraint the older Catholic Encyclopedia makes explicit when it calls the word kecharitōmenē “an illustration, not a proof of the dogma.” How much the perfect-passive participle will actually bear — and why the witnesses who expound “full of grace” most richly still drew no immaculate conception from it — is weighed on the argument page “Does ‘full of grace’ (Luke 1:28) prove the Immaculate Conception?”

Notes

  • The definition was long prepared. Earlier landmarks include the Council of Basel’s (schismatic, non-binding) declaration of 1439; the bulls of Sixtus IV approving the feast and forbidding either party to brand the other heretical (1477, 1483); and the Council of Trent’s express exemption of Mary from its decree on original sin (Session V, 1546). Pius IX’s bull settled what those had left open.
  • This is the terminus of the timeline’s claim (b): not a witness reporting a belief but the act that made it dogma. Its companion Marian definition, the Assumption, would follow under Pius XII in 1950.
