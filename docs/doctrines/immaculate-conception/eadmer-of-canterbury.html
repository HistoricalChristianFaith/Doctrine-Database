Eadmer of Canterbury on the Immaculate Conception

Dates: c. 1125 (Tractatus de conceptione sanctae Mariae) · c. 1060–c. 1126 · Wikipedia
Summary: Eadmer — monk of Canterbury, secretary and biographer of Anselm — wrote the first sustained theological case for the immaculate conception. Taking his master’s fittingness argument and pressing it from Mary’s purification all the way to her preservation, he framed the reasoning — “God could do it; he willed it; therefore he did it” — that the later tradition would compress into the famous potuit, decuit, ergo fecit.

Claims & evidence

“He could and he willed it; therefore he did it”

Potuit plane et voluit; si igitur voluit, fecit.

— Tractatus de conceptione sanctae Mariae (“He clearly could and he willed it; if therefore he willed it, he did it”).1 Against the objection that God could not, or would not, keep Mary free of the sin every child of Adam contracts, Eadmer answers with divine omnipotence and fittingness together: it was possible, it was fitting, and God willed it — so it was done. He presses the point with a homely image: as a chestnut is “conceived and grown amid spines which do it no harm,” so God could form the body of his mother untouched amid the thorns of sin. This is the argument later summed up two centuries on as potuit, decuit, ergo fecit — a remarkable anticipation of the Scotist formula, in a monk of the 1120s.

The first real case for claim (b)

Where Anselm had located Mary’s cleansing after her conception, Eadmer moves it to the conception itself: Mary was preserved, not merely purified. It is the first developed defence of claim (b) — a full three centuries before the dispute would be settled, and against what would become the majority scholastic view. His advocacy was bound up with the revival in England of the suppressed Anglo-Saxon feast of Mary’s conception, and it spread the more widely because the treatise was long taken to be Anselm’s own.2

Notes

  • The four-word tag potuit, decuit, ergo fecit (“he could, it was fitting, therefore he did”) is the later Franciscan/Scotist crystallization, not Eadmer’s own phrase. Eadmer wrote “Potuit plane et voluit; si igitur voluit, fecit” — the same logic, the omnipotence-and-fittingness argument, in his own words. Whether that inference-form proves the doctrine (as against merely illuminating it) is weighed off-timeline on its own page, Does “potuit, decuit, ergo fecit” prove the Immaculate Conception? (assessment: weak as a proof, plausible as an illuminative mode).
  • The Tractatus de conceptione sanctae Mariae was for centuries printed among Anselm’s works and attributed to him; modern scholarship establishes Eadmer as its author. It is a distinct work from Anselm’s genuine De conceptu virginali, with which older reference books sometimes confuse it.
