Peter Auriol on the Immaculate Conception

Dates: c. 1280–1322 · Franciscan lector at Toulouse; the Tractatus de conceptione completed 20 December 1314 · Catholic Encyclopedia
Summary: Peter Auriol (Petrus Aureoli), a Franciscan lector at Toulouse, gave claim (b) its first dedicated monograph. He defended the Immaculate Conception in a public disputation at Toulouse in 1314, completed his Tractatus de conceptione beatae Mariae Virginis on 20 December 1314, and answered his attackers the next year in the Repercussorium. His case runs in the Franciscan order — God could preserve Mary, it was supremely fitting, therefore it may be held that he did — but framed with deliberate caution: he claims only that the preservation “can be held without danger to faith,” pending the Church’s judgment. His own distinctive contribution lies elsewhere: the question of the debitum peccati, whether Mary was ever under any debt or obligation to contract original sin at all.

Claims & evidence

The six-part plan: God could preserve Mary, it was most fitting, and it may be held that he did

…in tertio quoque probabitur, quod Deus de potentia absoluta Virginem, ne originale contraheret, potuit praeservare; in quarto vero evidentissime ostenditur, quod de summa decentia Mariam potuit praeservare; in quinto vero, quod absque periculo fidei et erroris potest teneri, quod Deus eam praeservavit de facto; nec una pars nec alia est de necessitate fidei, donec per ecclesiam determinatum fuerit, quid sit tenendum.

— Tractatus de conceptione beatae Mariae Virginis (1314), ed. Quaracchi (1904), p. 23, source. In translation: “…in the third [part] it will be proved that God, by his absolute power, was able to preserve the Virgin from contracting original sin; in the fourth it is shown most evidently that with the highest fittingness he was able to preserve Mary; in the fifth, that it can be held without danger to faith or error that God did in fact preserve her — though neither side is of the necessity of faith until the Church shall have determined what is to be held.” The structure is Scotist (possibility → fittingness → fact), but the fifth conclusion is markedly cautious: not that Mary was preserved, but that it may be safely held that she was. The treatise takes its text from Proverbs 8:24 (“…et ego iam concepta eram,” “and I was already conceived”).1

The debitum peccati: whether Mary was ever under any obligation to contract the stain

Auriol’s genuinely original move is not the preservation-argument itself — that he shares with Ware and Scotus — but his analysis of the debitum peccati, the “debt” of original sin. Against the reigning thirteenth-century consensus (that Mary, as a daughter of Adam, actually contracted the stain because “in Adam all sinned,” Romans 5:12), he pressed the question whether she was ever under the necessity or obligation of contracting it — treating original sin as an inherited legal liability from which a singular exemption could be granted, and pushing toward what later theology would systematize as the nullum debitum position. This juridical angle on original sin, not a restatement of Scotus’s mediator-argument, is his lasting place in the doctrine’s history.2

Notes

  • The first monographic defense. Where Ware and Scotus argued the point within a single disputed question, Auriol devoted a whole treatise to it (the Tractatus) and then a second polemic (the Repercussorium editum contra adversarium innocentiae Matris Dei, “a rebuttal against the adversary of the innocence of the Mother of God”) to answering a Dominican critic. His question was printed alongside Ware’s and Scotus’s in the 1904 Quaracchi critical edition.
  • Cautious, not triumphal. Unlike his fellow champion Francis of Mayronis, who pressed the thesis as a certainty, Auriol’s fifth conclusion is a probabilist claim — the preservation “can be held without danger to faith” — and he expressly leaves the matter open “until the Church shall have determined” it, more than five centuries before it did.
  • The 20 December 1314 date. The completion date is reported from the treatise’s colophon by its editors; it, the 1314 Toulouse disputation, and the early-1315 Repercussorium are attested by the standard reference works and the modern scholarship on Auriol’s manuscripts.
