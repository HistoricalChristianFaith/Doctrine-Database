Cardinal Cajetan on the Immaculate Conception

Dates: c. 1469–1534 · treatise to Leo X, Rome, 1515 · Wikipedia
Summary: Thomas de Vio, called Cajetan (Gaetano) after his birthplace, was the most powerful Thomist of the age — Master General of the Dominicans (1508), the great commentator on Aquinas’s Summa, cardinal (1517), and the legate who examined Luther at Augsburg (1518). In 1515, while the Fifth Lateran Council sat, he addressed a treatise against the Immaculate Conception to Pope Leo X, carrying the maculist line of Aquinas forward into the sixteenth century: Mary was cleansed from original sin after her conception, not preserved from it at the first instant. He affirmed her sinless life and her sanctification in the womb (claim (a)) while denying the immaculate conception proper (claim (b)) — and the treatise provoked a storm of intra-Dominican rebuttal.

Claims & evidence

He framed the question as: did God preserve Mary, or cleanse her?

It is proposed that you decide in the Lateran Council whether God preserved the most blessed Virgin Mary from original sin, or cleansed her from it.

— Cajetan, Treatise on the Conception of the Blessed Virgin Mary, to Leo X (Rome, 1515), ch. 1, modern English translation.1 The whole treatise turns on this fork — praeservata vs. mundata — the exact distinction the doctrine would later be defined on. Cajetan lines up “an innumerable multitude of holy doctors and saints” for the cleansing view and ranks it the “probable” one; the preservation view he leaves at best barely admissible.

The strong preservation view he judged contrary to the faith — it breaks Christ’s universal redemption

If Christ died for all, all died: but according to this position, Not all died, because the blessed Virgin did not die the death of sin: therefore Christ did not die for all: which is manifest heresy.

— Treatise, ch. 3.1 This is the universal-redemption objection in its sharpest sixteenth-century form — the same ground on which Bernard, Bonaventure, and Aquinas had denied the doctrine. Cajetan draws a careful distinction of two senses of “preserved.” The strong sense — that Mary was “totally preserved… exempt from all things pertaining to said sin,” her very flesh never infected and so under no debt (debitum) of contracting it — he brands “contrary to the Catholic faith… a manifest error in faith,” since it would place Mary outside those for whom Christ died. The weak sense — that she was preserved “from the stain of original sin alone” by infused grace, her flesh still infected and still under the debt — he allows is “not contrary to Sacred Scripture,” but no more than tolerable, and not what he takes to be true.

He rejected the Council of Basel’s pro-Immaculist decree as the act of a schism

…that congregation of Basel at that time was not a synod, nor a council, but was schismatic: as being cut off from the head of the Church Eugene IV… Hence from this nothing but the authority of the synagogue of Satan can be brought forward.

— Treatise, ch. 5.1 The Immaculists leaned on Basel’s session 36 (1439), which had declared the doctrine “pious, and consonant… with the Catholic faith.” Cajetan answers that Basel had already deposed Pope Eugene IV in session 34 and gone into schism, so its later definition binds no one — the same judgment the Council of Basel entry records on this wiki. He affirms in the same breath the sanctification of Mary in her mother’s womb as the “probable and pious” belief of the Church — claim (a), not (b).

His verdict: the cleansing view is “probable,” the preservation view only “tolerable”

…neither part of the proposed question in the sense in which it is turned into doubt, is necessarily to be believed, and that one (namely saying, that the blessed Virgin was cleansed from original sin) is probable, but the other namely saying, that the blessed Virgin was preserved from the stain of original sin, is tolerable…

— Treatise, concluding chapter; subscribed “Rome, in the year of salvation 1515.”1 The grading is the whole point: Cajetan submits both readings to the Pope’s judgment, but leaves no doubt which he holds — the Thomist cleansing-after-conception, ranked probable; the immaculist preservation, at best tolerable. He would not live to see it: Rome defined the opposite in 1854.

Notes

  • The storm it provoked. Cajetan’s treatise touched off a running controversy within his own order. The Dominican Bartolomeo Spina answered it directly with a Tractatus contra opusculum Caietani de conceptione Beatae Virginis (Venice, 1535); more remarkably, the Dominican Ambrosius Catharinus (Lancelotto Politi) broke ranks to defend the Immaculate Conception against his own confrère across 1532–51 — the episode traced in Patrick Preston’s study of the dispute.2
  • A softening, for all that. Cajetan holds the maculist conclusion firmly — the cleansing view is the one “to be believed” — yet one modern survey reads his treatise as a “turning point” in the Franciscan–Dominican debate: in distinguishing his two senses of “preserved” he is, on this account, the first Dominican to admit the properly-framed preservation (from the stain alone, by a redemptio praeservativa) as merely tolerable rather than heretical — conceding to Scotus’s mechanism the bare space the order’s earlier enforcers had denied it, “although he remained faithful to the traditional position of the Dominicans.”2
  • (a) vs. (b). Cajetan is a textbook case of the distinction this timeline turns on: he honoured Mary’s sinlessness and her sanctification in the womb (claim (a)) as warmly as any Immaculist, while denying that she was preserved from original sin at her conception (claim (b)). The same man appears on this wiki as a witness on the Old Testament canon timeline, where his Hieronymian caution about the deuterocanonical books likewise set him against the coming Tridentine consensus.
  • On the text. The Latin original is the Tractatus de conceptione Beatae Mariae Virginis ad Leonem X, printed among Cajetan’s Opuscula omnia (Lyons, 1575); the standard scholarly English is R. C. Jenkins’s 1858 rendering, The Judgement of Thomas de Vio Cajetan against the Immaculate Conception. The English quoted above is from a freely available modern translation; its exact wording has not been collated against the critical Latin, and is marked accordingly in the sources.
