The Council of Trent on the Immaculate Conception

Dates: 17 June 1546 (Session 5, Decree Concerning Original Sin) · Wikipedia
Summary: When Trent defined the doctrine of original sin against the Reformers, it faced the standing question of Mary. Its answer was to step around it: the council declared that it did not intend to include Mary in the decree, and ordered Sixtus IV’s constitutions kept. This is an exemption — Mary left outside the scope of the original-sin decree — not a positive definition of the immaculate conception.

Claims & evidence

The Marian exemption clause

This same holy Synod doth nevertheless declare, that it is not its intention to include in this decree, where original sin is treated of, the blessed and immaculate Virgin Mary, the mother of God; but that the constitutions of Pope Sixtus IV., of happy memory, are to be observed, under the pains contained in the said constitutions, which it renews.

— Council of Trent, Session 5 (17 June 1546), Decree Concerning Original Sin, trans. J. Waterworth (1848), source.1 The clause does two things, both short of a definition. It removes Mary from the reach of the decree that declares original sin universal — so that Trent’s “all have sinned” is not read as prejudging her case — and it defers to the existing constitutions of Sixtus IV, which had frozen the dispute rather than settling it. Trent leaves the controversy exactly where Sixtus left it: a permitted difference, not a matter defined. The positive definition of claim (b) would come only with Pius IX in 1854.

Notes

  • The clause is often cited as if Trent had endorsed the immaculate conception. It did not: it declined to condemn it, by carving Mary out of the original-sin decree, and it renewed Sixtus IV’s ban on either side anathematizing the other. An exemption from a decree is not an affirmation of a doctrine.
  • This is the same council that appears elsewhere on this wiki — on the real presence, the ministerial priesthood, prayer to the saints, and the canon — but those are later sessions; the Marian exemption belongs to the fifth session of 1546.
