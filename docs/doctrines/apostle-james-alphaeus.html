James, son of Alphaeus

James the son of Alphaeus is the most shadowy of the Twelve. He heads the third group of four in every list of the apostles — and the New Testament says nothing else about him at all: no saying, no deed, no mission, no death. Almost everything the church later “knew” of him was borrowed from another man. For there were, in the first Christian generation, at least three Jameses: James son of Zebedee, John’s brother, beheaded by Herod Agrippa (he has his own page); James son of Alphaeus, this silent apostle; and James the brother of the Lord — James the Just, first leader of the church at Jerusalem, martyred there about the year 62. The great question that shaped this apostle’s tradition is whether the second and third of these are one man or two. The Latin West, following Jerome, fused them, and so lent the empty apostle the whole biography of James the Just — his bishopric, his sanctity, his death at the temple. The Greek East kept them apart, and left the son of Alphaeus almost as blank as the Gospels found him. This page is not a life of James so much as a history of what was remembered and claimed about him, set out earliest source first: the bare Gospel notices, the apostle-lists that first grafted James the Just’s death onto him, Jerome’s fusion that made the graft doctrine in the West, the medieval legend that completed it, and the modern scholarship that pulls the three Jameses apart again. This is a biographical tradition-timeline: its “witnesses” are the datable sources that report on James, and the one great contested question — was he the brother of the Lord? — is weighed on its own argument page below rather than settled by assertion. Timeline

The New Testament (c. 30–90)

The New Testament names James the son of Alphaeus four times — in the lists of Matthew, Mark, Luke, and Acts — and nowhere else. In each list he stands ninth, heading the third group of four, so that his place among the Twelve is fixed while his person stays blank: not a word of anything he said, did, went, or suffered. The Gospels do name a second obscure James, “James the less” (or “the little,” ho mikros), son of a Mary who watched the crucifixion — and the later tradition would take this “James the less” to be the son of Alphaeus. Distinct from both in the New Testament’s own telling is James the brother of the Lord (Galatians 1:19), who led the Jerusalem church and whom the Gospels group among the unbelieving “brethren,” set apart from the Twelve. Whether these are two Jameses or one is the hinge of everything that follows.1

The apostle-lists (uncertain date, mutually dependent)

In the apostle-list literature — Pseudo-Hippolytus On the Twelve Apostles, Pseudo-Dorotheus, the Byzantine and Latin catalogues — the silent apostle first grows a death, and the death is not his own. Pseudo-Hippolytus reports that “James the son of Alphaeus, when preaching in Jerusalem, was stoned to death by the Jews, and was buried there beside the temple” — which is transparently the martyrdom of James the Just, stoned at Jerusalem and buried by the sanctuary in the account Hegesippus gives. A rival strand of the same literature keeps him distinct and sends him instead to Egypt, crucified at Ostrakine. That the lists cannot agree whether he died at Jerusalem (as the brother of the Lord) or in Egypt (as a separate apostle) is itself the tell: his “biography” is being assembled after the fact, partly by borrowing from another man.2

Jerome fuses the two Jameses (c. 383)

What the lists did loosely, Jerome made doctrine. In his reply to Helvidius (c. 383) he argued that the “brethren of the Lord” were Jesus’ maternal cousins, sons of “Mary the wife of Alphaeus” the Virgin’s sister — and so identified James the Lord’s brother with “Mary’s” son, the apostle James son of Alphaeus. He restated it firmly a decade later: James “called the brother of the Lord, surnamed the Just… the son of Mary sister of the mother of our Lord… after our Lord’s passion at once ordained by the apostles bishop of Jerusalem.” The completing equation — that the father Alphaeus was one man with the “Clopas” of John 19:25 — Jerome did not himself make; it was supplied by later Greek fathers (Chrysostom, Theodoret) and became the standard machinery of the fused reading. This is the same Jerome, and the same argument, weighed at length on the perpetual-virginity timeline; here its effect is to give the apostle James the life, office, and death of James the Just.3

The Golden Legend (c. 1260)

Jacobus de Voragine’s Golden Legend gives the fusion its complete medieval form. Its life of “St James the Less” simply stacks the identifications: he “was also called James the son of Alpheus”; “called also the brother of our Lord, because he resembled much well our Lord in body, in visage”; “called James the Just for his right great holiness”; “he sang in Jerusalem the first mass that ever was sung therein, and he was first bishop of Jerusalem.” His death is the death of James the Just as Hegesippus told it — cast down from a height, stoned, and finally struck on the head with a fuller’s club — which is why the club became the apostle’s emblem in Western art. It is late hagiography, a millennium and more after the events, and every biographical detail in it belongs by origin to the other James.4

Modern scholarship

Critical study pulls the three Jameses apart again. James the son of Alphaeus, stripped of what was borrowed from James the Just, is left almost exactly as blank as the Gospel lists found him — a name, a father’s name, and a fixed rank among the Twelve. His alleged martyrdoms cancel out: the Jerusalem stoning belongs to James the Just, and the Egyptian crucifixion at Ostrakine is a late and isolated notice. The equation of Alphaeus with Clopas, on which the whole fusion turns, is philologically doubtful — the two names render distinct Aramaic originals — and the Gospel data set the Lord’s brethren apart from the Twelve. The Eastern churches never fused them and to this day keep James son of Alphaeus and James the brother of the Lord as separate saints. What can safely be said is only this: a James son of Alphaeus was numbered among the Twelve; everything more is either silence or borrowed from another man.5

Arguments

Was James son of Alphaeus the same man as James the brother of the Lord? (assessment: weak)

The identification is the single question on which this apostle’s whole tradition turns: only through it does the silent son of Alphaeus acquire the bishopric, the sanctity, and the martyrdom of James the Just. The fused reading, made doctrine by Jerome and standard in the Latin West, requires that the “brethren of the Lord” include apostles — yet the Gospels set the brethren apart from the Twelve and even among the unbelievers before the resurrection, the completing equation Alphaeus = Clopas is philologically strained, and the East never made the fusion at all. Held for centuries in the West but resting on identifications that do not hold on their own evidence. Weak — on its best available case the two Jameses are better kept distinct.6
