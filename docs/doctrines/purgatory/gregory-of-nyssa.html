Gregory of Nyssa on purgatory and prayer for the dead

Dates: c. 382 · Wikipedia · back to timeline
Summary: Gregory of Nyssa, the Cappadocian, gives the fullest Greek-patristic statement of a post-mortem purifying fire. In On the Soul and the Resurrection (his dialogue with his dying sister Macrina, c. 380) and the Great Catechism (c. 385) he teaches that the soul still "welded" to evil at death must pass through a "purgatorial fire" that refines it as a furnace refines gold from dross — what is not cleansed "here" by repentance and baptism is purged "hereafter" by fire. It is the clearest Eastern witness to the fire strand; but Gregory frames it within his doctrine of universal restoration (apokatastasis): the fire is medicinal and finite, restoring every soul — even, he says, the author of evil — and aims at healing, not at satisfying a debt of punishment. So it grounds the purgative reading while diverging sharply from the later Latin satisfactory purgatory.

Claims & evidence

The soul welded to evil is refined like gold in a "purgatorial fire"

Just as those who refine gold from the dross which it contains not only get this base alloy to melt in the fire, but are obliged to melt the pure gold along with the alloy, and then while this last is being consumed the gold remains, so, while evil is being consumed in the purgatorial fire, the soul that is welded to this evil must inevitably be in the fire too, until the spurious material alloy is consumed and annihilated by this fire.

— On the Soul and the Resurrection (De Anima et Resurrectione), source. The context is the soul's purification after death. Earlier in the same dialogue Gregory had set out the alternative plainly — "whether by forethought here, or by purgation hereafter, our soul becomes free" of its attachment to material passions — and here he supplies the controlling image: the refiner's fire that consumes the dross even as it passes through the gold. The soul that has grown into evil cannot be freed of it painlessly; God "drags that which belongs to Him from the ruins of the irrational and material," and the pain of that separation is the fire. This is among the most explicit patristic statements of a cleansing fire after death — the very reading the later Latin doctrine of purgatory would build on.

What is not washed by "the mystic water" must be purified by fire

… just as the furnace is the proper thing for gold alloyed with dross,—in order that, the vice which has been mixed up in them being melted away after long succeeding ages, their nature may be restored pure again to God. Since, then, there is a cleansing virtue in fire and water, they who by the mystic water have washed away the defilement of their sin have no further need of the other form of purification, while they who have not been admitted to that form of purgation must needs be purified by fire.

— The Great Catechism (Oratio Catechetica) 35, source. Here Gregory makes the purgative fire an explicit alternative to baptism: there are "two cleansings," water and fire, and "those whose weaknesses have become inveterate, and to whom no purgation of their defilement has been applied, no mystic water … must needs be purified by fire." The purification takes time — "after long succeeding ages" — and ends in the soul being "restored pure again to God." The same gold-from-dross image recurs (and again at ch. 26, of the redemption itself, "as when some worthless material has been mixed with gold, and the gold-refiners burn up the foreign and refuse part in the consuming fire"), marking it as Gregory's settled figure for purgation.

Notes

  • Gregory is the strongest Greek-patristic witness to the fire strand of purgatory, alongside the Latin line of Cyprian, Lactantius, and Ambrosiaster; the proof-text reading of 1 Corinthians 3:13–15 on which that strand rests is weighed on the cleansing-fire crux. His witness cuts against the usual claim that the purgative fire is a purely Latin development.
  • The decisive difference from the later doctrine is the frame, not the fire. Gregory's purgation is medicinal and universal — part of his apokatastasis, the restoration of all things: the fire heals, does not punish, and finally restores every soul ("after long succeeding ages"), in the Great Catechism even "the author of evil himself." It is not the Latin satisfactory purgatory that discharges a temporal debt of punishment for the elect alone (cf. Aquinas) but a therapeutic process of nature. Care is needed not to enlist him as a witness to the developed Western doctrine without that caveat.
  • Gregory locates the purgation after the resurrection and before the final judgment (a "necessary process of nature"), not in a sharply-defined intermediate place between death and glory — closer to Lactantius's eschatological fire in timing than to Gregory the Great's settled intermediate purgatory.
