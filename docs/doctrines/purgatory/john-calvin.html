John Calvin on purgatory and prayer for the dead

Dates: c. 1559 · Wikipedia
Summary: John Calvin (1509–1564), the Geneva reformer, rejects purgatory root and branch. In the definitive 1559 Institutes (III.5.6) he treats the doctrine not as a tolerable error but as an assault on the gospel itself: because purgatory makes the souls of the dead pay a satisfaction for sin after death, it transfers the expiation of sin away from the blood of Christ — and so, Calvin says, it "makes void the cross of Christ" and "overthrows our faith." For Calvin the whole machinery of post-mortem cleansing, indulgences, and prayer for the dead presupposes that Christ's atonement was incomplete; that presupposition is the heresy he attacks. He likewise discards the medieval limbus patrum (II.16.9) as "nothing but a fable." And he exposes the doctrine's one Scriptural footing as canon-dependent: when his opponents adduce the lone proof-text — Judas Maccabeus's sin offering for the dead (2 Maccabees 12:43–45) — Calvin refuses even to answer it on its merits "lest I should seem to include that work among the canonical books" (III.5.8). The case for prayer for the dead, on Calvin's own handling, stands or falls with the canonicity of the deuterocanon — which he denies.

Claims & evidence

Purgatory is a satisfaction for sin paid after death — and so undercuts the blood of Christ

What is this purgatory but the satisfaction for sin paid after death by the souls of the dead?

— Institutes of the Christian Religion, III.5.6 ("Of the Modes of Supplementing Satisfaction — viz. Indulgences and Purgatory"), ccel.org. This is Calvin's functional definition of the doctrine he is refuting: purgatory is a post-mortem payment, a satisfaction rendered by the dead for the residue of their sins. For Calvin that is precisely its fatal flaw — expiation is sought somewhere other than in Christ's blood.

Purgatory is a deadly device of Satan that nullifies the cross of Christ

We are bound, therefore, to raise our voice to its highest pitch, and cry aloud that purgatory is a deadly device of Satan; that it makes void the cross of Christ; that it offers intolerable insult to the divine mercy; that it undermines and overthrows our faith.

— Institutes of the Christian Religion, III.5.6, ccel.org. This is the rhetorical climax of Calvin's case. Note the logic: he condemns purgatory not chiefly as unscriptural geography but as a soteriological error — it "makes void the cross" because a satisfaction paid after death implies the cross did not satisfy in full. (Older English renderings give "a deadly device of Satan" / "makes void the cross of Christ"; the substance is the charge that purgatory nullifies the atonement and overthrows faith.)

It was devised without warrant from the word of God

It was devised by prying audacious rashness, that credit was procured for it by fictitious revelations, the wiles of Satan, and that certain passages of Scripture were ignorantly wrested to its support.

— Institutes of the Christian Religion, III.5.6, ccel.org. Having denied purgatory's theological legitimacy, Calvin denies its evidential basis: no command of God established it, the alleged proofs are wrested Scriptures, and its credit rests on invented apparitions.

The limbus patrum is a fable

Though this fable has the countenance of great authors, and is now also seriously defended by many as truth, it is nothing but a fable.

— Institutes of the Christian Religion, II.16.9 ("Absurd exposition concerning the Limbus Patrum. This fable refuted"), ccel.org. The same impulse that rejects purgatory rejects the rest of the medieval afterlife geography: the limbus patrum, the supposed subterranean holding-place of the Fathers' souls, Calvin discards as having no scriptural footing, however weighty the authorities behind it.

The lone proof-text counts as Scripture only if the deuterocanon does

To the passage which they produce from the history of the Maccabees (1 Macc. 12:43), I will not deign to reply, lest I should seem to include that work among the canonical books.

— Institutes of the Christian Religion, III.5.8, ccel.org. This is the hinge that ties the purgatory dispute to the canon question. The one passage of Scripture that narrates and approves an offering for the dead is 2 Maccabees 12:43–45 — Judas Maccabeus's sin offering for his fallen soldiers (Calvin's source cites it "1 Macc. 12:43," but his own back-reference to the book's closing apology at "2 Macc. 15:38" shows it is Second Maccabees, where the sin offering stands). Calvin declines to engage it at all, because to argue from it would concede that the books of the Maccabees belong in the Bible. So his rejection of prayer for the dead is, at bottom, a rejection of the book: the practice has a Scriptural warrant if and only if the deuterocanon is canonical — the very question weighed across the Old-Testament-canon timeline and, for this exact proof-text, on the 2 Maccabees 12:43–45 argument page (assessment: contested).

The book disqualifies itself — its author begs pardon for his words

As if the author himself did not sufficiently show what degree of deference is to be paid him, when in the end he asks pardon for any thing less properly expressed. He who confesses that his writings stand in need of pardon, certainly proclaims that they are not oracles of the Holy Spirit.

— Institutes of the Christian Religion, III.5.8, ccel.org. Calvin's reason for excluding 2 Maccabees is not arbitrary: he turns the book's own colophon against it. The writer of 2 Maccabees signs off asking the reader's indulgence — "if I have done well… it is that which I desired: but if slenderly and meanly, it is that which I could attain unto" (2 Macc 15:38) — and a man who pleads for pardon for his prose, Calvin reasons, thereby disclaims that his words are the dictation of the Holy Spirit. He marshals Jerome ("of no authority in establishing doctrine") and the ancient De Expositione Symboli to the same end. The deed of Judas, even granted, proves no purgatory: "the piety of Judas is commended for no other reason than for having a firm hope of the final resurrection," and "the act, indeed, was not free from superstition and misguided zeal."

No syllable in the canonical Scriptures warrants praying for the dead

Now, since the whole Law and Gospel do not contain one syllable which countenances the right of praying for the dead, it is a profanation of prayer to go one step farther than God enjoins.

— Institutes of the Christian Religion, III.5.10, ccel.org. This is the positive form of the same point. With 2 Maccabees set aside as non-canonical, Calvin holds that the protocanonical "Law and Gospel" nowhere command or model prayer for the dead — the patriarchal and biblical burial narratives, he notes (III.5.10), record "mourning and sepulchral rites, but not one word… of prayers." The doctrine therefore has, for Calvin, no Scriptural standing once its single supporting text is removed from the canon: the canon question is not a side issue but the decisive one.

Notes

  • Calvin's argument is the sharpest form of the Reformation objection that drives the cleansing-fire argument: if Christ's satisfaction is complete, there can be no remaining satisfactory punishment for the dead to pay. The patristic and medieval "purging fire" survives, on this reading, only as a metaphor or as eschatological judgment — never as a debt the soul discharges after death.
  • Calvin's refusal to engage 2 Maccabees 12 "lest I should seem to include that work among the canonical books" (III.5.8) makes the purgatory dispute a function of the canon dispute: prayer for the dead has an explicit Scriptural warrant only if the deuterocanon is Scripture.
