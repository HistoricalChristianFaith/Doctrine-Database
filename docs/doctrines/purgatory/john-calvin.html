John Calvin on purgatory and prayer for the dead

Dates: c. 1559 · Wikipedia · back to timeline
Summary: John Calvin (1509–1564), the Geneva reformer, rejects purgatory root and branch. In the definitive 1559 Institutes (III.5.6) he treats the doctrine not as a tolerable error but as an assault on the gospel itself: because purgatory makes the souls of the dead pay a satisfaction for sin after death, it transfers the expiation of sin away from the blood of Christ — and so, Calvin says, it "makes void the cross of Christ" and "overthrows our faith." For Calvin the whole machinery of post-mortem cleansing, indulgences, and prayer for the dead presupposes that Christ's atonement was incomplete; that presupposition is the heresy he attacks. He likewise discards the medieval limbus patrum (II.16.9) as "nothing but a fable."

Claims & evidence

Purgatory is a satisfaction for sin paid after death — and so undercuts the blood of Christ

What is this purgatory but the satisfaction for sin paid after death by the souls of the dead?

— Institutes of the Christian Religion, III.5.6 ("Of the Modes of Supplementing Satisfaction — viz. Indulgences and Purgatory"), ccel.org. This is Calvin's functional definition of the doctrine he is refuting: purgatory is a post-mortem payment, a satisfaction rendered by the dead for the residue of their sins. For Calvin that is precisely its fatal flaw — expiation is sought somewhere other than in Christ's blood.

Purgatory is a deadly device of Satan that nullifies the cross of Christ

We are bound, therefore, to raise our voice to its highest pitch, and cry aloud that purgatory is a deadly device of Satan; that it makes void the cross of Christ; that it offers intolerable insult to the divine mercy; that it undermines and overthrows our faith.

— Institutes of the Christian Religion, III.5.6, ccel.org. This is the rhetorical climax of Calvin's case. Note the logic: he condemns purgatory not chiefly as unscriptural geography but as a soteriological error — it "makes void the cross" because a satisfaction paid after death implies the cross did not satisfy in full. (Older English renderings give "a deadly device of Satan" / "makes void the cross of Christ"; the substance is the charge that purgatory nullifies the atonement and overthrows faith.)

It was devised without warrant from the word of God

It was devised by prying audacious rashness, that credit was procured for it by fictitious revelations, the wiles of Satan, and that certain passages of Scripture were ignorantly wrested to its support.

— Institutes of the Christian Religion, III.5.6, ccel.org. Having denied purgatory's theological legitimacy, Calvin denies its evidential basis: no command of God established it, the alleged proofs are wrested Scriptures, and its credit rests on invented apparitions.

The limbus patrum is a fable

Though this fable has the countenance of great authors, and is now also seriously defended by many as truth, it is nothing but a fable.

— Institutes of the Christian Religion, II.16.9 ("Absurd exposition concerning the Limbus Patrum. This fable refuted"), ccel.org. The same impulse that rejects purgatory rejects the rest of the medieval afterlife geography: the limbus patrum, the supposed subterranean holding-place of the Fathers' souls, Calvin discards as having no scriptural footing, however weighty the authorities behind it.

Notes

  • Calvin's argument is the sharpest form of the Reformation objection that drives the cleansing-fire crux: if Christ's satisfaction is complete, there can be no remaining satisfactory punishment for the dead to pay. The patristic and medieval "purging fire" survives, on this reading, only as a metaphor or as eschatological judgment — never as a debt the soul discharges after death.
  • This page treats Calvin's denial of purgatory and prayer for the dead. His parallel work on the conscious survival of the soul (against Anabaptist soul-sleep, in Psychopannychia) and his rejection of the limbus patrum are set out on his intermediate-state detail page; the two pages share the limbus patrum "fable" quotation.
  • Calvin frames purgatory primarily as a soteriological error (it voids the cross), where the Westminster Confession a century later frames its denial geographically (only two places for departed souls). The two approaches converge on the same rejection; see the purgatory timeline.
