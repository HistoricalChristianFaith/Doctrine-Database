Aerius of Sebaste on purgatory and prayer for the dead

Dates: c. 355 · Wikipedia
Summary: The lone ancient figure recorded as rejecting prayer and offering for the dead. A presbyter of Sebaste in Pontus, Aerius held that naming the departed in the liturgy was pointless and even harmful — if the prayers of the living could benefit the dead, no one would need to live holily — an objection Epiphanius catalogued as the seventy-fifth heresy and answered, and which Augustine repeated. His moral-hazard argument structurally anticipates the Reformation's case against the Mass for the dead.

Claims & evidence

Prayer and offering for the dead are useless — and breed moral laxity

Why do you mention the names of the dead after their deaths (i.e., in the liturgy)? … If the prayer of the people here has benefited the people there, no one should practice piety or perform good works! He should get some friends any way he wants, either by bribery or by asking friends on his death bed, and they should pray that he may not suffer in the next life, or be held to account for his heinous sins.

— Aerius, as quoted by Epiphanius, Panarion 75 (Against the Aerians), §3.5 (Frank Williams trans.), archive.org. This is a moral-hazard argument: vicarious post-mortem help, Aerius reasons, makes personal holiness dispensable, since one could simply arrange for prayers and offerings instead of repenting. Epiphanius records that Aerius rejected prayer for the dead along with the appointed fasts and the keeping of Easter, treating the established custom as no part of the apostolic faith.

The Church's reply — and what Aerius's dissent really shows

… the church has received it, and there was a world-wide agreement before Aerius and his Aerians.

— Epiphanius, Panarion 75 §6.4, archive.org. Epiphanius answers that naming the departed confesses that they "live with the Lord" and that the prayer for them is "useful … even if it does not force back the whole of guilty charges laid to them" (§8). He treats Aerius not as raising a live question but as disturbing a settled, apostolic practice — which is why the dissent is catalogued among the heresies. Augustine later lists the Aerians on the same ground (De Haeresibus 53: Aerius "said that the sacrifice ought not to be offered for the dead"), but, as the heresiologists note, he "merely epitomises Epiphanius" — so the whole ancient record of a denial of the practice traces to this single witness.1

Notes

  • Aerius is the timeline's one dissenting voice: every other ancient witness assumes or defends the practice of prayer, alms, and the Eucharist for the dead. His objection is principled rather than frivolous — and it is structurally the same worry the Reformers press against the Mass for the dead and purgatory (that vicarious post-mortem aid undercuts the urgency of repentance). Whether his dissent shows the practice was genuinely contested in antiquity, and whether (as is sometimes said) Arius too denied it, is weighed on the prayer-for-the-dead-uncontested argument (assessment: sound — Aerius is the one securely-attested denier, and the De Fide's supposed second denier "Arius" is not borne out: the De Fide affirms the practice; the Arian tie is to Aerius's own theology, Epiphanius calling him "a thoroughgoing Arian," Panarion 75.1.3).
  • Aerius's own words survive only through Epiphanius's hostile report; no work of his is extant. The quotation above is given as Epiphanius reproduces it. His floruit is fixed by his break with Eustathius, bishop of Sebaste (made bishop c. 355), under whom Aerius had been a presbyter and a candidate for the same see.
  • Contrast the defense on the very next timeline entry — Epiphanius of Salamis (c. 375) — and the broad practice that brackets him, from Cyril of Jerusalem and the Liturgy of Saint Mark before to Ambrose and the Eastern liturgies after, on the purgatory timeline.
