Perpetua on purgatory and prayer for the dead

Dates: c. 203 · Wikipedia · back to timeline
Summary: The Passion of Perpetua and Felicity records the prison diary of Vibia Perpetua, a young North African catechumen martyred at Carthage c. 203. In it she recounts praying for her dead brother Dinocrates — who had died at seven of a disfiguring facial cancer — and seeing him in two successive visions: first in a "gloomy place," parched and unable to reach water, then, after her continued prayer, "with a clean body well clad," refreshed, the wound healed to a scar, drinking freely. She concludes that "he was translated from the place of punishment." It is the earliest detailed Christian narrative of a Christian praying for a Christian dead and the prayer availing — the classic patristic case adduced for prayer for the dead and for an interim state from which the departed can be helped. It supplies the practice and the conviction that it reaches the dead; it is not yet a worked-out doctrine of a purging fire.

Claims & evidence

Perpetua prays for her dead brother and sees him suffering in a gloomy place

After a few days, whilst we were all praying, on a sudden, in the middle of our prayer, there came to me a word, and I named Dinocrates; and I was amazed that that name had never come into my mind until then, and I was grieved as I remembered his misfortune. And I felt myself immediately to be worthy, and to be called on to ask on his behalf. And for him I began earnestly to make supplication, and to cry with groaning to the Lord. … I saw Dinocrates going out from a gloomy place, where also there were several others, and he was parched and very thirsty, with a filthy countenance and pallid colour, and the wound on his face which he had when he died. … And moreover, in the same place where Dinocrates was, there was a pool full of water, having its brink higher than was the stature of the boy; and Dinocrates raised himself up as if to drink. And I was grieved that, although that pool held water, still, on account of the height to its brink, he could not drink. And I was aroused, and knew that my brother was in suffering. But I trusted that my prayer would bring help to his suffering; and I prayed for him every day … groaning and weeping that he might be granted to me.

— The Passion of the Holy Martyrs Perpetua and Felicitas 2.3 (ANF translation), newadvent.org. Dinocrates "had been my brother after the flesh, seven years of age, who died miserably with disease." Perpetua takes for granted that she may intercede for him and that the prayer of the living can reach a soul already dead — the practice presupposed, not argued.

After her continued prayer, she sees him refreshed and "translated from the place of punishment"

Then, on the day on which we remained in fetters, this was shown to me. I saw that that place which I had formerly observed to be in gloom was now bright; and Dinocrates, with a clean body well clad, was finding refreshment. And where there had been a wound, I saw a scar; and that pool which I had before seen, I saw now with its margin lowered even to the boy's navel. And one drew water from the pool incessantly, and upon its brink was a goblet filled with water; and Dinocrates drew near and began to drink from it, and the goblet did not fail. And when he was satisfied, he went away from the water to play joyously, after the manner of children, and I awoke. Then I understood that he was translated from the place of punishment.

— The Passion of the Holy Martyrs Perpetua and Felicitas 2.4 (ANF translation), newadvent.org. The change of state — from thirst, filth, and an unreachable pool to refreshment, a healed scar, and water freely drunk — is, in the narrative's own logic, the fruit of Perpetua's prayer. This is the structural heart of the early prayer-for-the-dead case: an interim condition that the prayer of the living visibly improves.

Notes

  • The Passion is a near-contemporary martyr-act, its visionary chapters presented as Perpetua's own first-person diary; the redactor is often supposed to be (or to stand close to) Tertullian, her fellow North African of the same years. Whatever the editing, it gives precious evidence that by c. 203 a Carthaginian Christian could pray for a dead family member as a matter of course and expect to be heard.
  • What it does and does not establish. Dinocrates is the witness's own dead brother, and the help Perpetua wins for him is real but the mechanism unstated — there is no fire here, no satisfaction for sin, only a passage from a place of suffering and thirst to refreshment in answer to prayer. Like Tertullian's "refreshment for him meanwhile," it stands on the strong, prayer-for-the-dead side of the purgatory question and leaves the contested purging-fire side untouched. (It is sometimes pressed further: that Dinocrates was unbaptized and so the vision proves post-mortem aid even for the unregenerate — but the text does not say he was unbaptized, and that reading is an inference, not Perpetua's claim.)
  • The motif of refreshment (refrigerium) for the departed in the interim before the resurrection is the same language the early liturgies and Tertullian use; see Cyril of Jerusalem a century and a half later making the petition at the altar. See also the purgatory timeline.
