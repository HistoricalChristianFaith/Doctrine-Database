2 Maccabees on purgatory and prayer for the dead

Dates: c. 124 BC · Wikipedia
Summary: 2 Maccabees 12:43–45 is the earliest explicit warrant that the living can aid the dead. Finding idolatrous tokens on his fallen soldiers, Judas Maccabeus collects silver and sends a sin offering to Jerusalem on their behalf, reasoning that prayer for the dead would be "superfluous and vain" but for the resurrection, and that the offering makes "a reconciliation for the dead, that they might be delivered from sin." It is the seed-text of the later prayers-for-the-dead / purgatory line.

Claims & evidence

A sin offering is sent to Jerusalem for the slain

And when he had made a gathering throughout the company to the sum of two thousand drachms of silver, he sent it to Jerusalem to offer a sin offering, doing therein very well and honestly, in that he was mindful of the resurrection:

— 2 Maccabees 12:43 (KJV/Apocrypha), source. After idolatrous amulets are found under the coats of the dead (vv. 39–40), Judas does not write them off: he gathers silver and sends it to the temple as an offering on their behalf — an act assuming the condition of the dead is not yet fixed beyond all help.

Prayer for the dead would be "superfluous and vain" but for the resurrection

For if he had not hoped that they that were slain should have risen again, it had been superfluous and vain to pray for the dead.

— 2 Maccabees 12:44 (KJV/Apocrypha), source. The author grounds the practice in the resurrection hope: it would be "vain to pray for the dead" if the dead simply ceased to be. Intercession for them is intelligible only because they live and will rise.

A reconciliation is made for the dead, that they might be delivered from sin

And also in that he perceived that there was great favour laid up for those that died godly, it was an holy and good thought. Whereupon he made a reconciliation for the dead, that they might be delivered from sin.

— 2 Maccabees 12:45 (KJV/Apocrypha), source. The dead "that died godly" have "great favour laid up" for them, and the offering is made "that they might be delivered from sin." This is the text the Latin West would later marshal for a post-mortem cleansing and the suffrages of the living.

Notes

  • This is the earliest explicit witness that the dead can be helped by the prayers, alms, and sacrifice of the living — older than any developed theory of purgatory. It establishes the practice (intercession + offering for the dead) on which the later doctrine is built; it does not yet describe a cleansing fire, which is weighed separately on the cleansing-fire argument.
  • Its dogmatic weight turns on canonicity: 2 Maccabees is deuterocanonical (received in the Catholic and Orthodox canons, classed apocryphal by Jews and Protestants), so the Reformers could grant the passage while denying it doctrinal force. Whether the text truly teaches prayer and an atoning offering for the dead, and how much weight it can bear when its canonicity is the disputed point, is weighed adversarially on the 2 Maccabees 12:43–45 argument (assessment: contested).
