Ambrosiaster on purgatory and prayer for the dead

Dates: c. 380 · Wikipedia · back to timeline
Summary: The anonymous fourth-century Latin commentator known as Ambrosiaster reads 1 Corinthians 3:15 ("he himself shall be saved; yet so as by fire") as the believer who survives judgment but only by being purified through fire — an early purgative-fire reading of the classic proof-text.

Claims & evidence

1 Corinthians 3:15 — "He will be saved only by being purified through fire"

To suffer loss is to endure reproof. For what person, when subjected to punishment, does not lose something thereby? Yet the person himself may be saved. His living soul will not perish in the same way that his erroneous ideas will. Even so, however, he may suffer punishments of fire. He will be saved only by being purified through fire.

— Commentary on Paul's Epistles (on 1 Corinthians 3:15), source. Commenting on Paul's "yet so as by fire," Ambrosiaster takes the "loss" and the "fire" as real punishment that nonetheless stops short of destroying the believer: the person's "living soul will not perish" as his bad teaching does, but "he may suffer punishments of fire" and "will be saved only by being purified through fire." Salvation is conditioned on a purgative passage through fire — an early Latin reading of 1 Corinthians 3:15 in a purgatorial direction. (On the preceding verse, 1 Cor 3:13, he glosses: "In the fire, bad teaching will become clear to everyone, though for the moment it is deceiving some.")

Notes

  • "Ambrosiaster" is the conventional name for the anonymous author of this Latin Pauline commentary, long mistaken for Ambrose. The work belongs to the late fourth century (c. 380s).
  • This is one of the load-bearing patristic readings behind the cleansing-fire crux — whether 1 Corinthians 3:15 teaches a purgative fire. It can be set beside Caesarius of Arles, who later restricts what such fire can purge, on the purgatory timeline.
  • Note the open question of whether Ambrosiaster's "purified through fire" denotes a post-mortem purgation specifically or the fire of the final judgment; his comment does not by itself settle the timing.
