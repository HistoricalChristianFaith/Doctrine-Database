The Epitaph of Abercius on purgatory and prayer for the dead

Dates: c. 190 · Wikipedia · back to timeline
Summary: Abercius, bishop of Hieropolis in Phrygia (later second century), composed his own funerary epitaph while still living and had it carved on his tomb. After describing his travels and, in veiled mystery-cult language, the Eucharist (the "fish" caught by a "holy virgin," eaten "with wine … mingled with bread"), he closes with a request: that every fellow-believer who understands the inscription pray for Abercius. It is among the earliest epigraphic witnesses to prayer for the dead — a Christian asking, in stone, that the Church intercede for him after his death — and one of the oldest surviving monuments to mention the Eucharist. The stone (now in the Vatican Museum) is securely dated: the epitaph of one Alexander, found nearby and dated 216, already quotes it, so Abercius's text is no later than the very end of the second century.1

Claims & evidence

Abercius asks, on his own tombstone, that every believer pray for him

These things I, Abercius, having been a witness [thereof] told to be written here. Verily I was passing through my seventy-second year. He that discerneth these things, [namely] every fellow-believer, let him pray for Abercius.

— Epitaph of Abercius, closing lines (the standard restored translation, after the Catholic Encyclopedia / Quasten), earlychristianwritings.com; cf. Catholic Encyclopedia (1913).2 The petition is the point: Abercius does not merely commemorate himself, he solicits the prayers of the believers who read his stone. Because he raised the monument "while living" (the inscription's opening) for a request to take effect after his death, it is a deliberate appeal for prayer for the dead — Abercius, dead, aided by the prayer of the living Church, exactly the conviction the timeline traces.

The Eucharistic context: the "fish" eaten with bread and wine

Faith everywhere led me forward, and everywhere provided as my food a fish of exceeding great size, and perfect, which a holy virgin drew with her hands from a fountain and this it ever gives to its friends to eat, it having wine of great virtue, and giving it mingled with bread.

— Epitaph of Abercius, earlychristianwritings.com. The "fish" (ΙΧΘΥΣ, the standard early cipher for Christ) caught by a "holy virgin" and eaten with "wine … mingled with bread" is veiled Eucharistic language, which is why the epitaph is prized as the oldest dated monument to name the Eucharist. It matters here because Abercius's request for prayer stands in an explicitly liturgical and sacramental frame: the same Church that feeds on the Eucharistic "fish" is the Church he asks to pray for him — the practice of intercession for the dead and the Eucharistic life of the community are bound together on one stone, anticipating the later coupling of the offering and prayer for the departed in Cyril of Jerusalem and Augustine.1

Notes

  • The witness is to the practice (prayer for the dead), not to a doctrine of purgatory: there is no cleansing fire and no satisfaction for sin, only Abercius's confidence that the prayers of fellow Christians will avail him after death. It stands on the strong, near-universal "prayer-for-the-dead" leg of the purgatory question, weighed on the prayer-for-the-dead-uncontested argument; the contested "purging fire" leg it leaves untouched (the cleansing-fire argument).
  • Its weight is epigraphic and devotional, and unusually solid for that: unlike a literary text whose date can be argued, this is a physically surviving monument with a terminus ante quem — the dated (216) epitaph of Alexander quotes it — so the request for prayer is firmly second-century. As a self-composed, public funerary inscription it shows that asking the Church to pray for one's own soul was an ordinary, unremarkable Christian expectation by c. 190, not a later innovation.
  • The mystery-cult idiom is deliberate: Abercius wrote in code ("he that discerneth these things … every fellow-believer") so that only the initiated would grasp the Eucharistic and ecclesial references — itself evidence of a self-conscious Christian sacramental community in late-second-century Phrygia.
  • Chronologically it slots between the Acts of Paul and Thecla (c. 160), the earliest Christian narrative of prayer for the dead, and Tertullian (c. 211), the earliest Latin witness to the practice as settled custom — Abercius supplying, in between, the earliest epigraphic witness.
