Lactantius on purgatory and prayer for the dead

Dates: c. 310 · Wikipedia
Summary: Lactantius, the African rhetorician writing his Divine Institutes during the Great Persecution (c. 303–311), describes a discriminating fire of judgment that one and the same divine flame inflicts on all: it scorches and burns those "whose sins shall exceed either in weight or in number," but is not so much as felt by "they whom full justice and maturity of virtue has imbued," who carry "something of God in themselves which repels … the violence of the flame." It is a probative, sin-proportioned fire — a forerunner of the later purgative-fire reading — though set at the Last Judgment, not in an intermediate state.

Claims & evidence

One divine fire tries the righteous, burning the sin-laden but unfelt by the perfected

The same divine fire, therefore, with one and the same force and power, will both burn the wicked and will form them again, and will replace as much as it shall consume of their bodies, and will supply itself with eternal nourishment: which the poets transferred to the vulture of Tityus. Thus, without any wasting of bodies, which regain their substance, it will only burn and affect them with a sense of pain. But when He shall have judged the righteous, He will also try them with fire. Then they whose sins shall exceed either in weight or in number, shall be scorched by the fire and burnt: but they whom full justice and maturity of virtue has imbued will not perceive that fire; for they have something of God in themselves which repels and rejects the violence of the flame. So great is the force of innocence, that the flame shrinks from it without doing harm; which has received from God this power, that it burns the wicked, and is under the command of the righteous.

— The Divine Institutes VII.21 (Of the Torments and Punishments of Souls), source. Lactantius is reasoning about how immortal souls can suffer at the hand of God. He describes a single "divine fire," different in nature from earthly fire, that at the judgment is applied to all who are raised. Crucially the fire is not uniform in effect but discriminating: it scorches those whose sins are many or weighty, while those perfected in virtue "will not perceive that fire" at all, because innocence carries "something of God" that the flame shrinks from. The notion of a single judicial fire that purges in proportion to the sin it finds — sparing the pure, burning the rest until the dross is gone — is an ancestor of the Latin purgative-fire reading of 1 Corinthians 3:13–15.

Souls are not judged at once, but detained until the judgment

Nor, however, let any one imagine that souls are immediately judged after death. For all are detained in one and a common place of confinement, until the arrival of the time in which the great Judge shall make an investigation of their deserts.

— The Divine Institutes VII.21, source. This is the caveat that keeps Lactantius honest evidence rather than overstated proof: he locates the probative fire at the general judgment, not in an interval between death and glory, and holds all souls meanwhile in "one and a common place of confinement." His fire purifies the righteous as they are judged, not in a distinct intermediate purgatory — so he witnesses to the discriminating-fire idea on which purgatory would later be built, while the timing remains eschatological.

Notes

  • Lactantius's contribution to the purgatory line is the shape of the fire, not its place: one fire, applied to all, that purges in proportion to the sin it meets and leaves the perfected untouched. Compare Cyprian a generation earlier ("cleansed and long purged by fire") and the developed Latin reading of 1 Corinthians 3:15 in Ambrosiaster; the proof-text reading is weighed on the cleansing-fire argument.
  • The key difference from the later doctrine: Lactantius sets the trial-by-fire at the Last Judgment and detains souls meanwhile in a common holding-place — not in a separate intermediate purgatory running between death and glory. Care is needed not to read the sixth-century settled doctrine (cf. Gregory the Great) back into his eschatological fire.
  • The same Divine Institutes is a witness on a different doctrine: Lactantius's account of the fallen angels and their demon offspring (II.15) appears on the Nephilim timeline.
