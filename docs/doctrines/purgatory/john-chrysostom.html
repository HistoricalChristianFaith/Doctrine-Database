John Chrysostom on purgatory and prayer for the dead

Dates: c. 392 · Wikipedia · back to timeline
Summary: Chrysostom (c. 347–407) holds the two halves apart cleanly. He commends prayer, alms, and the Eucharistic offering for the dead, urging that "some refreshment may thereby ensue to them" — the apostolic custom of remembering the departed "in the dreadful Mysteries." Yet he reads 1 Corinthians 3:13–15 as the fire of judgment, not a purging cleansing: "he shall be saved" he glosses as a dark hint that the man "shall remain forever in punishment." So Chrysostom is for prayer for the dead and against the later Latin satisfactory fire.

Claims & evidence

(a) Prayer and offering for the dead are apostolic and bring them refreshment

…nor do we in vain make mention of the departed in the course of the divine mysteries, and approach God in their behalf, beseeching the Lamb Who is before us, Who taketh away the sin of the world;—not in vain, but that some refreshment may thereby ensue to them. Not in vain cloth he that standeth by the altar cry out when the tremendous mysteries are celebrated, "For all that have fallen asleep in Christ, and for those who perform commemorations in their behalf."

— Homilies on First Corinthians 41 §8, historicalchristian.faith. Chrysostom urges the living to aid the dead "not by tears, but by prayers and supplications and alms and offerings," insisting the commemoration at the altar is "not in vain… but that some refreshment may thereby ensue to them." Prayer for the departed is, for him, settled apostolic practice with real benefit.

(b) But 1 Corinthians 3:15 is the fire of judgment, not a purging cleansing

And so here in saying, "he shall be saved," he hath but darkly hinted at the intensity of the penalty: as if he had said, "But himself shall remain forever in punishment."

— Homily on First Corinthians 9, historicalchristian.faith. Where the Latin tradition would read "saved, yet so as by fire" as a soul cleansed and brought through, Chrysostom reads "saved" as bitter understatement: the fire is the penalty of the day of judgment, and "shall be saved" hints "darkly" that the man "shall remain forever in punishment." He spells out the two-fold loss explicitly: "lo, here is one punishment… lo, again, here is a second. And his meaning is, 'He himself shall not perish in the same way as his works… but he shall abide' "— abide, that is, in the fire. The verse is for him eschatological judgment, not an intermediate purgation.

Notes

  • Chrysostom is the clearest patristic case for keeping the two claims apart: a firm yes to prayer, alms, and oblation for the dead, and a firm no to reading 1 Cor 3 as a satisfactory purging fire. This is the standard Greek pattern — the East prays for the departed (cf. Cyril of Jerusalem) without adopting the Latin doctrine of purgatory.
  • His reading of 1 Cor 3:13–15 is the load-bearing Greek witness against the purgative interpretation, weighed on the cleansing-fire argument: the same proof-text the West treats as a purging fire, Chrysostom turns into a fire of final judgment.
  • For his fuller account of the soul's lot immediately after death — conscious, escorted by angels, fixed in Abraham's bosom or in torment across an impassable gulf — see his intermediate-state page. See also the purgatory timeline.
