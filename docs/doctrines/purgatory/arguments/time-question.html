Is purgatory instantaneous or durational?

Type: supporting argument / crux (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — the magisterium leaves the duration of purgatory genuinely open (CCC 1030–1032 fixes neither a place nor a span of time), and the non-temporal reading is held at the highest level: Ratzinger (Benedict XVI) calls the transforming encounter one that "cannot be quantified by the measurements of earthly time," and the popular "days and years" reckoning rests on a misreading of indulgence terms — equivalents of ancient canonical penance, not time deducted from purgatory, whose numbers were abolished in 1967. But the durational picture carries the longer tradition (Gregory the Great's souls released after an appointed time, the medieval imagination, Aquinas) and the very logic of suffrages — Masses and prayers offered for the dead across our time — presupposes the soul persists in purgatory through some duration; strong by magisterial-theological lights, weak against the durational tradition, with no settled answer · back to timeline · advanced by Ratzinger (Benedict XVI), CCC 1030–1032, and James Akin (EWTN)

The claim

The popular image of purgatory is durational: a soul spends so many days or years there, and the prayers, Masses, and indulgences of the living shorten the sentence. The claim weighed here is the counter-thesis pressed by the modern magisterium and its apologists — that purgatory is not a span of earthly time at all, but a transition or "moment" of transformation that "may take no time," so that the durational picture, and the arithmetic of "days off purgatory" that goes with it, is a misunderstanding the Church never taught.

The transforming "moment" of this encounter cannot be quantified by the measurements of earthly time. It is, indeed, not eternal but a transition, and yet trying to qualify it as of "short" or "long" duration on the basis of temporal measurements derived from physics would be naive and unproductive.

— Joseph Ratzinger (Benedict XVI), Eschatology: Death and Eternal Life.1 The thesis has three legs: (1) the magisterium nowhere defines purgatory as a place or a duration; (2) the highest-level theology treats the purification as non-temporal — a passing-over "where we are burned ere we are transformed," measured not in clock-time but in an "existential time" of the soul; and (3) the popular "days and years" attached to indulgences never meant time deducted from purgatory in the first place, but equivalences to the ancient discipline of canonical penance — a confusion the 1967 reform abolished by dropping the numbers entirely.2

Who advances it

The reading is held at the summit of Catholic theology. Joseph Ratzinger — Cardinal, then Pope Benedict XVI — devotes the purgatory section of his Eschatology to exactly this point, grounding the doctrine in 1 Corinthians 3:10–15 and insisting that the "duration" of the purifying encounter is not a quantity of physical time but lies "in the unsoundable depths of existence." The Catechism of the Catholic Church (1030–1032) defines purgatory only as "the final purification of the elect," fixing neither a place nor a length of time, and distinguishing it from "the punishment of the damned"; the popular apologetic — James Akin's widely circulated EWTN essay How to Explain Purgatory to Protestants — makes the temporal openness an explicit point, noting that "the Church has never said that purgatory involves the same kind of time as we experience here on earth, or even time at all," and reaching for the medieval category of aeviternity (a mode between earthly time and God's eternity) to describe it.3

The evidence for

The magisterium really does leave it open. Neither the conciliar definitions nor the Catechism commits the Church to a place called purgatory or to a measurable duration; the dogma is the fact of a final purification of the imperfect elect and the benefit to them of the Church's suffrages, not a clock. On the duration question the Catholic tradition has therefore always had room for the non-temporal reading, and at the highest level — Ratzinger as cardinal and as pope — that reading is affirmed without censure.

The "days and years" never meant time off purgatory. The strongest popular evidence for a durational purgatory — the medieval indulgences of "40 days," "seven years," and the like — turns out not to support it. Those figures were equivalences to periods of the early Church's canonical penance: an indulgence "of 40 days" remitted temporal punishment to the degree that 40 days of public penance would have, not 40 days of purgatorial fire. Pope Paul VI's apostolic constitution Indulgentiarum Doctrina (1967) abolished "the former determination of days and years" precisely to clear away the misreading, substituting a measure keyed to the disposition of the person performing the work. So the arithmetic that seems to presuppose a durational purgatory was, on the Church's own account, never a measurement of purgatorial time at all.2

The non-temporal reading is internally coherent. If purgatory is the soul's transforming encounter with the holiness of Christ at the threshold of glory, there is no antecedent reason its "duration" should be a quantity of the physical time that runs in this world — a world the separated soul has left. Ratzinger's "existential time" and the scholastic "aeviternity" are attempts to name a real conceptual gap: the categories of earthly clock-time may simply not apply to a disembodied soul, in which case asking whether purgatory is "instantaneous or durational" in our sense may be a malformed question.3

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The narrow, defensible claim is solid: the magisterium does not bind the faithful to a durational, clock-measured purgatory; the indulgence numbers were canonical-penance equivalents and were officially retired; and the non-temporal reading is coherent and authoritatively held. Anyone who treats "purgatory must take so many years" as Catholic dogma is mistaken, and the apologists are right to say so.

What is overstated.

  1. The durational picture is not a folk error but the longer tradition. It is not the case that the Church "never taught" anything durational. Gregory the Great's Dialogues — the text that turned the conjecture into teaching — narrates souls held and then released after an appointed term of suffrage (the priest Justus freed after thirty days of Masses), and the whole medieval imagination, canonized in art and devotion, is frankly temporal. Aquinas treats the punishment of purgatory as having both intensity and duration, lessened by the suffrages of the living. The non-temporal reading is a modern refinement, legitimate but recent; it cannot be projected back as "what the Church always meant."4
  2. The logic of suffrages presupposes duration relative to us. The oldest and strongest strand of the whole doctrine — prayer, alms, and the Mass for the dead — assumes that the soul is still being aided after the offering is made, i.e. that it persists in its state of purification across the time in which the living pray. A strictly instantaneous purgatory, completed at the moment of death, would leave nothing for next year's anniversary Mass to do. So even the non-temporal theologians must grant a duration relative to the world's time, however much they deny an inner clock; "instantaneous" in the unqualified sense sits awkwardly with the practice that anchors the doctrine.
  3. Even the non-temporal reading keeps a before-and-after. Ratzinger's own language — a "transition," a "passing-over," a being "burned ere we are transformed" — is sequential: purification precedes transformation. That is not the timeless simultaneity of God's eternity but an ordered process, which is most of what the durational party means by "takes time." The dispute thus narrows to whether that process maps onto physical clock-time, on which the magisterium is silent — not to whether purgatory is a pure instant, which the tradition's own process-language resists.

Assessment

Contested. The question is one the magisterium deliberately leaves open, and both answers have real support. By magisterial and high-theological lights the non-durational reading is strong: the Catechism fixes neither place nor span, Ratzinger holds the purifying encounter "cannot be quantified by the measurements of earthly time," and the indulgence "days and years" — the popular evidence for a durational purgatory — were canonical-penance equivalents, not purgatorial time, and were abolished as numbers in 1967. By the lights of the older tradition the durational reading is at least as strong: Gregory's released-after-a-term souls, the medieval imagination, and Aquinas's intensity-and- duration of purgatorial punishment are frankly temporal, and the suffrage practice that is the doctrine's strongest leg presupposes that the soul persists in purgatory across the time in which the living pray for it. There is no clear winner, and the disagreement is partly verbal: stripped to essentials, the non-temporal party affirms an ordered process of purification (a before-and-after, a "transition") while denying it runs on a physical clock, and the durational party affirms exactly that ordered process while picturing it in clock-time the magisterium never canonized. What the evidence does settle is the narrower negative: the rigidly durational, "so-many-years-off" purgatory of popular imagination is not Catholic dogma, and was never what the indulgence numbers measured. What it does not settle — and what the Church declines to settle — is whether the purification is, in itself, an instant or a span.
