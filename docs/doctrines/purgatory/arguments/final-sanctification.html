The "final sanctification" syllogism for purgatory

Type: supporting argument / crux (off-timeline) · Assessment: plausible — the syllogism (nothing unclean enters heaven; we are not yet perfectly holy at death; ∴ a purification must come between death and glory) is valid, and its conclusion — a final perfecting of the soul — is near-consensus, affirmed even by the Reformed as glorification. But it establishes only the genus (some final purification), not the species (the historic Catholic purgatory of penal satisfaction over time); the contested content is carried in by the name, not by the premises, and the proponent's own concession that it "may take no time" collapses the conclusion into the glorification Protestants already hold · back to timeline · advanced by James Akin (EWTN / Catholic Answers)

Related cruxes: the temporal nature of purgatory — see also is purgatory instantaneous or durational?, the explicit treatment of the time question this syllogism turns on (its "may take no time" concession).

An argument page weighs a single sub-claim adduced in support of a main interpretation — a proof-text, a parallel, a piece of reasoning — and renders an adversarial assessment of the interpretation it serves. Unlike a person-detail page it sits across the timeline rather than at one date. Every factual claim is sourced; the proponent's own words are quoted verbatim.

The claim

Purgatory is thus the final rush of our sanctification. It is our transition into glory.

— James Akin, How to Explain Purgatory to Protestants, jimmyakin.com.1 The argument weighed here is not a proof-text or a patristic citation but a piece of reasoning — a syllogism offered as establishing purgatory from premises a Protestant already grants:

  1. P1. Nothing unclean enters heaven — "there shall in no wise enter into it any thing that defileth" (Rev 21:27); "holiness, without which no man shall see the Lord" (Heb 12:14).
  2. P2. We are not yet perfectly holy when we die — "if we say that we have no sin, we deceive ourselves" (1 John 1:8); our sanctification is not complete in this life.
  3. ∴ Between death and glory there must come a purification. That purification, by definition, is purgatory — "the final sanctification," "the final rush of our sanctification."2

The argument's whole strategy is to be admissible on Protestant terms. It does not lean on 2 Maccabees, on the cleansing fire of 1 Corinthians 3, or on the practice of prayer for the dead — the strands carried by the timeline and weighed on the cleansing-fire crux. It claims, rather, that purgatory follows from principles the Reformation itself holds.

Who advances it

The fullest popular statement is by James (Jimmy) Akin, the Catholic apologist, in the widely-circulated essay How to Explain Purgatory to Protestants (EWTN library; Catholic Answers). Akin's case is deliberately minimal and steelmanned for its audience. He grants that "every historic Protestant will admit that our sinning in this life does not continue into heaven," and that they "will be quite insistent that although our sanctification is not complete in this life, it will be completed (instantaneously, they say) as soon as this life is over" — and he turns precisely that admission into the argument.3 He concedes the points that most provoke Protestant resistance: purgatory "may simply be an instantaneous 'in the twinkling of an eye' transformation"; it is "not a middle state between heaven and hell" ("everyone who goes to purgatory goes to heaven"); and far from impugning the cross, "it is because of Christ's sufferings that we are given the final sanctification of purgatory in the first place."1 The reading has weightier backing in the same minimal key: Cardinal Ratzinger (Benedict XVI), in his Eschatology, describes purgatory not as "some kind of supra-worldly concentration camp" but as "the inwardly necessary process of transformation" — a fiery encounter with Christ — and CCC 1030–1032 defines it as a final purification without fixing it as a place or a duration.4

The evidence for

The syllogism's strength is that, on its minimal reading, it is genuinely valid and its premises are genuinely shared. P1 is plain Scripture and uncontested across the traditions: the unclean do not enter the presence of God. P2 is the Augustinian-Reformation commonplace that no believer is sinlessly perfected at the moment of death. The conclusion follows: if the not-yet-perfect must become perfect to enter glory, then a final perfecting must occur at the threshold. This is why Akin can say Protestants "in general believe in purgatory, they just don't call it that" — for the Reformed do affirm exactly such a final perfecting. The Westminster Confession has the souls of the righteous, "being then made perfect in holiness," received at death into the highest heavens; the Shorter Catechism's "made perfect in holiness, and do immediately pass into glory" is the same instantaneous transition Akin describes.5 To that extent the argument lands: a final sanctification between death and glory is not a Roman peculiarity but a near-consensus of the Western tradition.

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The valid core — that the imperfect must be made perfect to enter glory, so a final purification must occur — is sound and broadly shared. Akin's framing is honest about its own modesty: by conceding that purgatory may be instantaneous, may not be a place, and is itself a gift of Christ's sacrifice, he removes the features Protestants reject and rests the case on what they affirm. As a demonstration that some final perfecting is required, the syllogism succeeds.

What is overstated.

  1. It establishes the genus, not the species. The conclusion the premises actually support is "a final purification must occur" — not "the doctrine of purgatory" in its historic Catholic content: a state in which the soul undergoes penal satisfaction for the temporal debt of forgiven sin, over a duration, aided by indulgences and the Masses of the living. None of that satisfaction apparatus is in P1 or P2; it is carried in by the name "purgatory," which is then attached to a conclusion that by itself reaches only "glorification." The move from "must be purified" to "must be purified by satisfactory suffering over time" is a separate claim, resting on the separate (and contested) cleansing-fire and satisfaction case — not on this syllogism.
  2. The concession that it "may take no time" collapses it into glorification. If the final purification is instantaneous — "in the twinkling of an eye" (1 Cor 15:52) — and monergistic (God's act in receiving the soul, not the soul's own expiation), then it simply is what the Reformed call glorification, and the distinctively purgatorial content has vanished. The argument is therefore strong exactly to the degree it claims little, and reaches the historic doctrine only to the degree it claims more than the premises grant.
  3. P2 secures a transition, not a process. That we are imperfect up to death does not entail an intermediate state with its own duration in which we are purged; the perfecting can be the boundary event itself — God making the soul "perfect in holiness" in the act of glorifying it. So even granting both premises, nothing in the syllogism requires the durational, satisfactory purgatory; it requires only a final perfecting, whose mode (instantaneous gift vs. temporal expiation) the argument leaves entirely open.

Assessment

Plausible. The interpretation the syllogism serves — that there is a final post-mortem sanctification of the not-yet-perfected faithful — is coherent, well-grounded, and in its minimal form a near-consensus of the Western tradition (the Reformed hold it as instantaneous glorification at death). As a piece of reasoning the argument is valid and its premises are shared, and the proponent states it with unusual honesty, conceding the very features that would make it objectionable. But it falls short of its advertised target. It establishes that a final purification must occur; it does not establish purgatory in the sense that divides the churches — a state of penal satisfaction for the temporal debt of sin, over time, on which indulgences and the Masses of the living operate. That distinctively Catholic content is supplied by the name and by the separate cleansing-fire / satisfaction case, not by the syllogism, whose own concession that the purification "may take no time" collapses its conclusion into the glorification Protestants already affirm. The argument is thus sound for the genus and unproven for the species: it wins the word "purgatory" only by emptying it of the content in dispute. What to flag is not Akin's reasoning, which is careful, but the slide it invites — from "there must be a final sanctification," which all grant, to "therefore the doctrine of purgatory," which does not follow from these premises alone.
