Why doesn't the pope free every soul from purgatory?

Type: internal-coherence objection / crux (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — the objection is a false dilemma. It assumes the pope holds over the dead the same jurisdictional release-power he holds over the living, so that not emptying purgatory could only mean his power is secretly limited or his charity is withheld. But the Church has always denied that premise: she has no jurisdiction over the dead, and an indulgence reaches a soul in purgatory only per modum suffragii — by way of suffrage, an offering of the treasury's satisfaction that God accepts according to His own justice and the soul's disposition, with no guaranteed or commandable effect. The "limited power" horn is therefore not a hidden embarrassment but the express teaching, and charity is not impugned, because the pope cannot empty purgatory by fiat — he is an intercessor for the dead, not a judge over them. The objection

Indulgences are the practical face of purgatory: a remission of the temporal punishment still due for sins already forgiven, drawn by the Church from the treasury of Christ's and the saints' merits, and applicable (in the developed doctrine) even to the souls being purged after death. The objection weighed here is not historical but one of internal coherence, a challenge often put to Catholics — and fielded by Catholic apologists in those very terms ("a logical problem for purgatory").1 It runs as a dilemma:

  1. A plenary indulgence remits the whole of the temporal punishment due for a soul's sins.
  2. An indulgence can be applied to a soul in purgatory by another's act — a living person performing the prescribed work, the Church granting the indulgence — so a soul's release does not depend on what that soul itself does.
  3. The pope holds the power of the keys and the disposal of the treasury of merits, and so can grant plenary indulgences at will.
  4. Perfect charity wills the greatest good of others; the release of a suffering soul into the vision of God is a very great good.

So (the objection concludes) charity, joined to the power, would seem to require the pope to grant a plenary indulgence to every soul and empty purgatory at a stroke. Since purgatory plainly persists, one of two things must follow: either his power over the souls is not what premises 1–3 claim (and indulgences for the dead are less than they advertise), or he could free them and does not — withholding charity. Either horn embarrasses the doctrine.

Who raises it

It is a popular rather than a scholarly objection — the version queued for this page comes from an online (r/Catholicism) discussion thread asking, bluntly, "why does the pope not free all souls in purgatory."1 But it is a standing challenge with a long pedigree: it is the same coherence worry behind the Reformers' attack on the indulgence trade, and a near-relation of Aerius's ancient objection that vicarious post-mortem help makes personal holiness dispensable (see the prayer-for-the-dead-uncontested crux). Catholic Answers has met it head-on under the title "A Logical Problem for Purgatory?" Because the objection is anonymous and popular, there is no proponent's overstatement to flag on a detail page.1

The Catholic reply

1. The pope has no jurisdiction over the dead; indulgences reach them only per modum suffragii. This is the decisive answer, and it concedes the dilemma's first horn outright — the power is limited, and that limit is the express teaching, not a hidden flaw. The power of the keys, the juridical "binding and loosing," reaches only the living — the wayfarers still subject to the Church's authority. Over the dead the Church has no jurisdiction; so an indulgence "applied" to a soul in purgatory is not a juridical absolution of that soul but an offering of the treasury's satisfaction to God, who alone disposes of the dead. The Catholic Encyclopedia states it plainly:

The Church in granting an indulgence to the living exercises her jurisdiction; over the dead she has no jurisdiction and therefore makes the indulgence available for them by way of suffrage (per modum suffragii) — i.e. she petitions God to accept these works of satisfaction and in consideration thereof to mitigate or shorten the sufferings of the souls in Purgatory.2

Robert Bellarmine fixed the distinction: "indulgences avail as suffrage, because they avail not after the fashion of a juridical absolution"; so "the pope does not absolve the soul in purgatory from the punishment due his sin, but offers to God from the treasure of the Church whatever may be necessary for the cancelling of this punishment."2 Aquinas had already drawn the line in the Summa: because "the dead can do none of those things for which indulgences are granted," indulgences "cannot avail them directly," but only secondarily; and a living person who performs an indulgenced work cannot of himself transfer its fruit to a chosen soul, for "it is not in his power to apply to a particular person the intention of the Church" — the application rests with God, the benefit to the dead resting "not on God's justice, but on His goodness" (the act of impetration).3 The pope, in short, was never claimed to hold over the dead a switch he could throw; he holds it over the living, and for the dead he can only pray — offer the treasury and ask. That is why he cannot "empty purgatory": not for want of charity, but because release by command is simply not a power the Church claims over the departed.

2. The offering carries no guarantee, and turns on disposition. Because an indulgence for the dead is a petition rather than a juridical act, its application depends on God's acceptance, and there is no certainty it is applied to any given soul. The apologist Jimmy Akin grants the point and only qualifies its tone: "since the departed are not subject to the jurisdiction of the earthly Church, we cannot have certain knowledge that indulgences are applied to them in particular cases, but we may have reasonable confidence that God hears and responds affirmatively." Indulgences moreover work ex opere operantis — their efficacy is "dependent on the subjective disposition of the one performing the indulgenced act" — so they are not an impersonal lever to be pulled for the whole of purgatory at once.4

3. The treasury is not dispensed arbitrarily, and God's acceptance keeps the order of His justice. The Church does not scatter the treasury at will: "there must be a sufficient reason for granting the indulgence, and this reason must be something pertaining to the glory of God and the utility of the Church" — not merely the relief of the souls. The same encyclopedia brands the contrary picture a caricature: "least of all is an indulgence the purchase of a pardon which secures the buyer's salvation or releases the soul of another from Purgatory. The absurdity of such notions must be obvious to anyone who forms a correct idea of what the Catholic Church really teaches on this subject."5 Charity, then, is not impugned: the Church does will the souls' good and prays for them universally — the whole logic of suffrages, of the Mass offered for the dead, is exactly that — but the pope is their intercessor, not their judge.

The objection's strongest form

The dilemma can be sharpened past the easy version. Grant suffrage-not-jurisdiction. The treasury offered is the superabundant, in principle inexhaustible satisfaction of Christ; the pope can offer it as suffrage; and God, by hypothesis, accepts suffrages for the dead. Then the limiting factor cannot be the size of what is offered — an infinite fund offered in maximal measure for every soul should suffice to discharge every finite debt. So the limit must lie in God's will: He requires the souls to undergo the satisfaction. But now a second dilemma bites — if the satisfaction is genuinely required, indulgences ought not shorten it; if it is not, the infinite treasury ought to end it at once. The Catholic answer is that God accepts suffrages in a measure that preserves the order of His justice and tracks the soul's own disposition and union with the Church's merit — efficacious, but neither automatic nor unbounded. That reply is coherent, but it is worth conceding what it costs: it locates the limit in the inscrutable divine economy rather than in anything demonstrable, and Catholic theologians themselves divide over whether God accepts the satisfaction out of strict justice or out of mercy.6 That is an internal question about the mechanism of suffrages, however — not an admission that the doctrine contradicts itself.

Adversarial assessment

What the objection gets right. It correctly fastens on a real feature of the system — that the pope's power over the dead is limited — and it correctly senses that vicarious post-mortem release raises a genuine question of coherence and of charity, the same intuition (that letting one person's act spare another cheapens personal holiness) that animated Aerius in the fourth century and the Reformers in the sixteenth. It is also right to reject the "release-valve" picture of indulgences — the notion that a grant simply springs a soul on demand — which the Church's own sources call absurd.

Where it fails. Its force depends entirely on a premise Catholicism denies: that the pope holds over the dead the jurisdictional release-power he holds over the living. Strip that premise out — as the doctrine has from the start — and the disjunction is no longer exhaustive. There is a third, and actual, option the dilemma never names: the pope's power over the dead is real but suffragatory, an offering and a prayer rather than a sentence; so he can neither empty purgatory by fiat nor be charged with withholding what he was never able to give. The "limited power" horn, which the objection treats as a damning concession, is simply the teaching — stated, not smuggled. And the charity horn collapses with it: one cannot fail in charity by not doing what lies outside one's power.

The objection bites hardest, and only, against a purely satisfactory construal of purgatory — purgatory as a fixed quantum of penal debt that a large enough external payment could clear. The further the magisterium has moved from that construal toward sanctification — purgatory as the soul's own transforming encounter with God, "not a span of earthly time at all" (Ratzinger; CCC 1030–1032; see the time-question and final-sanctification cruxes) — the less the dilemma can even be stated: a soul's own purifying transformation is not the kind of thing a third party could finish from outside, any more than another's act can do one's growing-up for one. On the satisfaction model the objection raises a sharp question; on the sanctification model it becomes close to a category error.6

Assessment

Weak. As a refutation of the indulgence-and-purgatory doctrine the objection does not hold, because it is built on a false dilemma. It assumes the pope could release the dead by the same jurisdictional act with which he absolves the living, and so reads his not emptying purgatory as either a covert limit on his power or a failure of charity. But the limit is neither covert nor a failure: the Church has always taught that she has no jurisdiction over the dead, and that indulgences avail them only per modum suffragii — an offering of the treasury's satisfaction that God accepts in His own measure, with no guarantee and no compulsion. The pope is the souls' intercessor, not their judge; he cannot empty purgatory by command for the same reason a man cannot vote on another's behalf — the act is not his to perform. The dilemma's disjunction is therefore not exhaustive, and its sting is lost the moment the suffrage doctrine is stated. What survives is not a contradiction but a sincere and answerable question, sharpest against the older satisfaction model of purgatory and progressively dissolved by the modern shift toward sanctification.
