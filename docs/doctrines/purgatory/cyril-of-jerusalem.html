Cyril of Jerusalem on purgatory and prayer for the dead

Dates: c. 350 · Wikipedia · back to timeline
Summary: Cyril (c. 313–386), bishop of Jerusalem, gives the classic Eastern rationale for prayer for the dead. In his Mystagogic on the Eucharist he describes the Church commemorating the departed over the consecrated gifts, "believing that it will be a very great benefit to the souls for whom the supplication is put up." He defends the practice with the parable of a king petitioned on behalf of offenders: in offering Christ for sinners who have fallen asleep, the Church entreats God's mercy for them.

Claims & evidence

The Eucharistic supplication is "a very great benefit to the souls for whom" it is offered

Then we commemorate also those who have fallen asleep before us, first Patriarchs, Prophets, Apostles, Martyrs, that at their prayers and intercessions God would receive our petition. Then on behalf also of the Holy Fathers and Bishops who have fallen asleep before us, and in a word of all who in past years have fallen asleep among us, believing that it will be a very great benefit to the souls, for whom the supplication is put up, while that holy and most awful sacrifice is set forth.

— Catechetical Lecture 23 (Mystagogic 5) §9, newadvent.org. Over "that holy and most awful sacrifice" the deacon names the departed; Cyril states plainly that the Church does so "believing that it will be a very great benefit to the souls" remembered. The dead are aided by the Church's intercession at the altar.

As a king is petitioned for offenders, so the Church offers Christ for the sinful dead

For if a king were to banish certain who had given him offence, and then those who belong to them should weave a crown and offer it to him on behalf of those under punishment, would he not grant a remission of their penalties? In the same way we, when we offer to Him our supplications for those who have fallen asleep, though they be sinners, weave no crown, but offer up Christ sacrificed for our sins, propitiating our merciful God for them as well as for ourselves.

— Catechetical Lecture 23 (Mystagogic 5) §10, newadvent.org. Cyril anticipates the objection — "what is a soul profited… if it be commemorated in the prayer?" — and answers with the king-and-crown illustration: the Eucharistic offering of Christ "propitiat[es] our merciful God" for the departed sinner. This is intercession with real efficacy for the dead, grounded in the one sacrifice rather than in any second purging of the soul.

Notes

  • Cyril is a prime Eastern witness for the prayer-for-the-dead practice and for the conviction that it benefits the departed — the strong half of the purgatory question. He does not, however, ground that benefit in a purgatorial fire; the soul is helped by the Church's offering of Christ, not by serving out a satisfactory cleansing. The distinct fire-claim is weighed on the cleansing-fire argument.
  • His account remains an Eastern one: the dead are "refreshed" and "propitiated" for, but the developed Latin theory of a temporal punishment discharged in purgatory is not in view. So Cyril supports prayer for the dead while standing apart from the later Western mechanism — a pattern shared with the East generally (cf. John Chrysostom).
