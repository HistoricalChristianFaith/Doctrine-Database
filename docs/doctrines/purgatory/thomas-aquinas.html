Thomas Aquinas on purgatory and prayer for the dead

Dates: c. 1274 · Wikipedia
Summary: Thomas Aquinas (c. 1225–1274), the Dominican master of scholastic theology, turns the patristic seed into a fully systematized doctrine. The "day of the Lord" of 1 Corinthians 3, taken as the day of death, is revealed "in the fire of purgatory, by which the elect will be cleansed." In the Summa Theologiae purgatory is assigned its own abode among the destinations of separated souls: those who die in grace but still owe satisfaction for sin are detained there and "punished ere they receive their reward." The picture is bounded by his account of Christ's descent, which emptied the limbus patrum — so since Christ the fully-cleansed go straight to God and those still owing satisfaction pass through purgatory.

Claims & evidence

The day of death is revealed "in the fire of purgatory, by which the elect will be cleansed"

But the day of the Lord which occurs at death will be revealed in the fire of purgatory, by which the elect will be cleansed, if any require cleansing: Job (23:10) can be interpreted as referring to this fire: "When he has tried me, I shall come forth as god."

— Commentary on 1 Corinthians, ch. 3 (§164), historicalchristian.faith. Reading 1 Corinthians 3:13 ("the day … shall be revealed with fire"), Aquinas distinguishes three "days of the Lord" — the general judgment, the particular judgment at death, and the trials of this life — and assigns to the second the purgatorial fire that cleanses the elect. Where Augustine had only floated such a fire and Gregory affirmed it for slight sins, Aquinas slots it into a precise scheme: it is the fire of the particular judgment at death, cleansing those of the elect whose venial debts remain.

Those who die in grace with venial sins are assigned an abode — purgatory

those who die in grace with venial sins an abode is assigned where they are punished ere they receive their reward, which abode is purgatory

— Summa Theologiae, Supplement, Appendix I ("On Purgatory"), newadvent.org. Purgatory is now one of the mapped destinations of separated souls, defined as the place "where souls are detained from receiving their reward at once on account of the sins they have committed." It is not a third destiny but a temporary station on the way to heaven: the soul is saved, but the remaining debt of temporal punishment is discharged before it enters the vision of God. This is the high-scholastic systematization — the seed of Augustine and Gregory built into a satisfaction-discharging doctrine with its own place on the map.

The holy Fathers were detained until Christ's descent emptied the limbus patrum

the holy Fathers were detained in hell for the reason, that, owing to our first parent's sin, the approach to the life of glory was not opened.

— Summa Theologiae III, q. 52, a. 5, newadvent.org. The Old-Testament righteous, free of personal guilt but barred from the vision of God by the inherited debt of original sin, waited in the limbus patrum — the "Abraham's bosom" of Luke 16 — until Christ "when … He descended into hell … delivered the holy Fathers from thence" (same article). Aquinas adds that Christ's soul "descended only into that part of hell wherein the just were detained" (III, q. 52, a. 2). This frames purgatory's boundary: the limbus patrum is a closed chapter of the past, so since Christ no one waits there — the fully cleansed go straight to God, those still owing satisfaction to purgatory.

Notes

  • From seed to system. Aquinas's purgatory is the scholastic crystallization of what Augustine floated and Gregory affirmed: a purgatorial fire, now located at the particular judgment, that cleanses the elect and discharges the temporal debt of sin already forgiven as to guilt. The distance between the cautious patristic seed and this developed, satisfaction-based doctrine is weighed on the cleansing-fire argument — the corporeal, quantified fire rests on a theology of merit and satisfaction the patristic witnesses did not spell out.
  • The limbus patrum is, for Aquinas, a feature of the past — it explains where the Old-Testament saints waited and what Christ's descent accomplished — whereas purgatory is the ongoing station for the saved who still owe satisfaction. The two must not be confused: one is emptied and closed, the other remains in use.
  • Aquinas's wider scheme distinguishes five abodes for souls after death — heaven, hell, purgatory, the limbo of the Fathers (now emptied), and the limbo of children. For that full map and his account of the particular judgment and immediate beatific vision, see his detail page on the intermediate state.
