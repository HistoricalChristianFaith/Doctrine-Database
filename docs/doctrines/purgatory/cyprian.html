Cyprian of Carthage on purgatory and prayer for the dead

Dates: c. 253 · Wikipedia · back to timeline
Summary: Cyprian, defending the Church's practice of restoring penitents to communion, draws a sharp contrast between the martyr who is "crowned" at once and the ordinary believer who must be "cleansed and long purged by fire" — an early Latin seed of post-mortem purgation. His letters also presuppose the routine offering of the Eucharistic sacrifice for the departed.

Claims & evidence

The ordinary believer is "cleansed and long purged by fire," unlike the martyr crowned at once

It is one thing to stand for pardon, another thing to attain to glory: it is one thing, when cast into prison, not to go out thence until one has paid the uttermost farthing; another thing at once to receive the wages of faith and courage. It is one thing, tortured by long suffering for sins, to be cleansed and long purged by fire; another to have purged all sins by suffering. It is one thing, in fine, to be in suspense till the sentence of God at the day of judgment; another to be at once crowned by the Lord.

— Epistle 51 (To Antonianus), §20, source. Arguing that pardon granted to the penitent does not cheapen the martyr's crown, Cyprian distinguishes several post-mortem outcomes by their cost. The believer who is restored but not yet glorified is "tortured by long suffering for sins, to be cleansed and long purged by fire" and held "in suspense till the sentence of God at the day of judgment"; the martyr, by contrast, has "purged all sins by suffering" and is "at once crowned by the Lord." The image of a cleansing fire to which the imperfectly purified are subject before glory is read by later Latin tradition as an anticipation of purgatory.

The Eucharistic sacrifice is normally offered for the departed

if any one should do this, no offering should be made for him, nor any sacrifice be celebrated for his repose. For he does not deserve to be named at the altar of God in the prayer of the priests, who has wished to call away the priests and ministers from the altar.

— Epistle 65 (To the Clergy and People of Furni), §2, source. Geminius Victor had named a presbyter as guardian in his will, against the Church's rule. As a penalty Cyprian directs that "no offering should be made for him, nor any sacrifice be celebrated for his repose," and that he not be "named at the altar of God in the prayer of the priests." The penalty assumes the norm: the offering of the sacrifice and the naming of the dead "in the prayer of the priests" was the ordinary, expected practice — which is precisely what makes its withholding a punishment.

Notes

  • Cyprian's "cleansed and long purged by fire" is among the earliest Latin witnesses to a notion of post-mortem purgation; whether it amounts to purgatory in the developed sense is debated. His "uttermost farthing" is the penny of Matthew 5:25–26, read (as in Tertullian) for a post-mortem reckoning — weighed on its own argument page (assessment: weak; here the farthing is an illustration of degrees of post-mortem cost rather than an independent exegesis), one of the Scriptural proof-texts — while his cleansing fire feeds the cleansing-fire argument, both on that proof-text and on the reading of 1 Corinthians 3:15 as purgative fire.
  • Epistle 65 is a witness to prayer and oblation for the dead by negative example — Cyprian withholds the sacrifice as a punishment, presupposing it as the Church's standing practice. Compare Epiphanius of Salamis on the purgatory timeline, who later defends the same practice against Aerius.
  • Note that Cyprian's purgative imagery is tied to penitential discipline and the day of judgment, not yet to a clearly defined intermediate state; care is needed not to read the sixth-century settled doctrine back into the third century.
