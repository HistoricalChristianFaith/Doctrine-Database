Augustine of Hippo on purgatory and prayer for the dead

Dates: c. 420 · Wikipedia · back to timeline
Summary: Augustine (354–430), bishop of Hippo, lays the Latin seed of purgatory but lays it cautiously. Reading 1 Corinthians 3, he allows that some believers may pass through a "purgatorial fire" after this life — yet flags it expressly as a thing that "may be inquired into, and either ascertained or left doubtful," conjecture rather than settled doctrine. What he holds firmly is the older, broader practice: the dead are aided by the prayers, alms, and the Eucharistic sacrifice of their living friends — but only those who in life "earned such merit, that services of this kind can help them."

Claims & evidence

A purgatorial fire after this life is "not impossible" — but may be "left doubtful"

And it is not impossible that something of the same kind may take place even after this life. It is a matter that may be inquired into, and either ascertained or left doubtful, whether some believers shall pass through a kind of purgatorial fire, and in proportion as they have loved with more or less devotion the goods that perish, be less or more quickly delivered from it.

— The Enchiridion, ch. 69 ("It is Not Impossible that Some Believers May Pass Through a Purgatorial Fire in the Future Life"), historicalchristian.faith. Augustine reads 1 Corinthians 3:11–15 (the man "saved, yet so as by fire") and reasons from the "fire" of adversity already at work in this life, then extends it past death — but only for those who will inherit the kingdom, and never as the "saved by fire" license for the unrepentant that he rejects in ch. 67–68. The hedging is the point: the fire "may be ascertained or left doubtful." This is the cautious germ from which the later Western doctrine would grow.

The dead are aided by the prayers, alms, and Eucharistic sacrifice of the living

Nor can it be denied that the souls of the dead are benefited by the piety of their living friends, who offer the sacrifice of the Mediator, or give alms in the church on their behalf. But these services are of advantage only to those who during their lives have earned such merit, that services of this kind can help them.

— The Enchiridion, ch. 110 ("The Benefit to the Souls of the Dead from the Sacraments and Alms of Their Living Friends"), historicalchristian.faith. The offerings, he adds, are "thank-offerings for the very good," "propitiatory offerings for the not very bad," and merely "a species of consolation to the living" for the very bad — their benefit being "either in obtaining a full remission of sins, or at least in making the condemnation more tolerable." This is the doctrinal seed of prayers for the dead, but tightly bounded: efficacy depends on merit already fixed in this life, not earned afresh after death.

Some are saved through fire — the man whose work is burned, who loved earthly things "carnally"

For although it might be said that even those persons would be saved by fire of whom the apostle says, "But he himself shall be saved, yet so as by fire," and that therefore that fire is to be esteemed of more efficacy than anything which a man can suffer in this life; yet we have found many evil things, which though men endure they do not on that account come to the kingdom of God.

— The Enchiridion, ch. 68, historicalchristian.faith. In the chapters surrounding his "purgatorial fire" conjecture Augustine works carefully through 1 Corinthians 3, distinguishing the believer who has built "wood, hay, stubble" — saved "yet so as by fire" because he loved lawful earthly goods too much — from those whose loss is simple condemnation. The whole treatment is exploratory and qualified, never a finished doctrine of a quantified post-mortem satisfaction.

Notes

  • Tentative, not settled. Augustine's contribution to purgatory is a question, not a dogma. He repeatedly marks the post-mortem fire as something that "may be left doubtful" (Enchiridion 69), offering it as conjecture from 1 Corinthians 3. The corporeal, satisfaction-discharging fire of the later scholastics outruns what Augustine actually committed to — a distance weighed on the cleansing-fire crux.
  • What Augustine holds without hedging is the practice, not the theory: the dead are helped by the living's prayer, alms, and the Eucharistic sacrifice (Enchiridion 110; cf. On Care to Be Had for the Dead, where "supplications for the spirits of the dead" truly reach those who "while living in the body merited that such things should profit them"). Prayer for the dead is far older and far wider than any cleansing fire — see the cleansing-fire crux, which keeps the two strands apart.
  • Gregory the Great later turns Augustine's "may be left doubtful" into a positive teaching, and Aquinas systematizes it into a mapped doctrine of a purgatorial fire that discharges the temporal debt of sin.
  • For Augustine's wider account of the soul between death and resurrection — the "hidden retreats," Abraham's bosom, Tartarus — see his detail page on the intermediate state.
