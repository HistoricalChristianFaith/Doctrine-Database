The Acts of Paul and Thecla on purgatory and prayer for the dead

Dates: c. 160 · Wikipedia
Summary: The apocryphal Acts of Paul and Thecla (part of the Acts of Paul, composed in Asia Minor in the later second century) gives the earliest Christian narrative of prayer for the dead. Queen Tryphaena's deceased daughter Falconilla appears to her in a dream asking that the holy stranger Thecla pray for her, "that I may be transferred to the place of the just"; Tryphaena begs Thecla to do so, and Thecla prays aloud that Falconilla "may live for ever." The episode is legendary, not doctrinal teaching, but it is precious evidence that intercession for the departed — and the assumption that it could reach and benefit them — was already a natural, intelligible piece of popular Christian piety by c. 160, contemporary with or earlier than Tertullian's report of the practice as settled custom.

Claims & evidence

The dead Falconilla asks, in a dream, that Thecla pray her into "the place of the just"

For her daughter Falconilla had died, and said to her in a dream: Mother, you shall have this stranger Thecla in my place, in order that she may pray concerning me, and that I may be transferred to the place of the just.

— Acts of Paul and Thecla 28 (Roberts-Donaldson / ANF translation), newadvent.org. The departed Falconilla is imagined as not yet at her final lot: she pleads, through the dream, to be "transferred to the place of the just," and names the prayer of a living holy person as the means. The whole logic presupposes that the condition of the dead is open to the prayers of the living — the same assumption that underlies the practice Tertullian reports a generation later.

Tryphaena asks Thecla to pray for her dead child

My second child Thecla, come and pray for my child, that she may live for ever; for this I saw in my sleep.

— Acts of Paul and Thecla 29, newadvent.org. Tryphaena, who has taken Thecla in as a "second child," turns the dream into a request: she asks Thecla to intercede so that the dead Falconilla "may live for ever." That the request is made without any sense of novelty or impropriety is the point — prayer for the dead is treated as an obvious recourse for a grieving mother.

Thecla prays aloud for Falconilla's eternal life

God most high, grant to this woman according to her wish, that her daughter Falconilla may live for ever.

— Acts of Paul and Thecla 29, newadvent.org. Thecla complies with a direct supplication to God for the eternal life of the departed Falconilla. This is the earliest Christian text to depict, in narrative, a believer praying by name for a specific dead person and asking God to better that person's lot after death.

Notes

  • The witness is to the practice (prayer for the dead), not to a doctrine of purgatory: there is no cleansing fire, no satisfaction for sin, only the conviction that the living's prayer can aid the departed and that the dead are not yet beyond such help. It stands on the strong, near-universal "prayer-for-the-dead" leg of the purgatory question, weighed on the prayer-for-the-dead-uncontested argument; the contested "purging fire" leg it leaves untouched (the cleansing-fire argument).
  • Its weight is devotional and popular, not magisterial. The Acts of Paul is apocryphal and legendary; Tertullian reports (De Baptismo 17) that a presbyter in Asia composed it "out of love for Paul" and was removed from office for it. So it does not teach the practice on authority — but precisely because it is a popular tale assuming prayer for the dead as ordinary piety, it shows the practice was already taken for granted in the Christian imagination of the later second century, independent of any later theory.
  • Falconilla is a pagan who died unbaptized, which makes the episode broader than the developed doctrine: it is prayer for a heathen dead, of the kind later Western theology would not sanction (purgatory being for the baptized faithful). The text reflects a pre-doctrinal moment in which the boundaries of efficacious intercession for the dead were not yet drawn — its value is as evidence of the practice's antiquity, not as a model the later Church endorsed in every particular.
  • Chronologically it slots just after 2 Maccabees (the Second-Temple seed) and beside Tertullian, the two earliest witnesses to the same conviction in, respectively, Jewish and Latin-Christian settings. See also the purgatory timeline.
