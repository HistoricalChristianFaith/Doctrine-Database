Leporius on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 426 (Leporius, a Gallic monk and priest, fl. early 5th century. Having taught Pelagian and proto-Nestorian views — that Christ endured His human life and Passion by the power of His manhood alone, without the help of His deity — he was expelled from Gaul, made his way to Africa, and there was set right by Aurelius of Carthage and Augustine of Hippo. His recantation, the Libellus emendationis or Document of Amendment, was drawn up in Africa c. 426 and sent back to the Gallic bishops Proculus of Marseille and Cillenius) · Wikipedia
Summary: Leporius is not arguing the brethren-question, nor even the perpetual virginity as such — his subject is the union of the two natures in Christ. But in the confession of faith that anchors his retraction he names the Lord’s mother “the ever-virgin Mary” (Latin semper virgo), using the title not as a thesis to be defended but as a fixed clause of the creed. That incidental, creedal use — in a Western document composed under Augustine’s eye and subscribed as the price of readmission to the Church — shows that by the 420s semper virgo was settled confession in the Latin West, the phrase a man reciting his orthodoxy would use without a second thought.

Claims & evidence

“The ever-virgin Mary” as a creedal clause in a retraction

The Libellus emendationis is a formal recantation: Leporius withdraws his errors about the person of Christ and sets down, in their place, the orthodox confession. Rehearsing the two births of the one Son — eternal from the Father, temporal from Mary — he writes:

We confess, therefore, that our Lord and God, Jesus Christ, the only Son of God, born of the Father before the ages, and in times most recent, made man of the Holy Spirit and the ever-virgin Mary…

— Leporius, Document of Amendment (Libellus emendationis) 3 (c. 426).1 The clause tracks the incarnational formula of the creed (“incarnate by the Holy Spirit of the Virgin Mary”), but Leporius sharpens “Virgin” to “ever-virgin” — the perpetual-virginity title carried into the confession as a matter of course.

Why an incidental title carries weight

A witness who argues for a doctrine tells us it was contested; a witness who simply assumes it tells us it was established. Leporius does the latter, and the setting makes the point stronger still. This is not a Mariological treatise where the writer might load his language for effect, but a disciplinary document in which every word was weighed — drafted under the supervision of Aurelius and Augustine and offered as proof of a corrected faith. That such a text reaches for semper virgo as the natural style for the Lord’s mother shows the phrase was, by the 420s, the settled confession of the Latin Church, not a party slogan. It sits a generation after Helvidius had forced the question at Rome (c. 383) and beside Augustine’s own mature affirmations — the controversy is over, and the ever-virgin title has passed into creed.

What it settles — and what it leaves open

Because Leporius affirms only the title and no account of the Lord’s brethren, his witness bears directly on the perpetual virginity itself but says nothing to decide among the three theories of the brethren. Like Athanasius, Didymus, and Chromatius before him, he attests that Mary was and remained ever-virgin while leaving the Epiphanian/Hieronymian question untouched. On the one point he does touch he is unambiguous; on the disputed genealogy he is silent.

Notes

  • Not a Marian treatise. The Libellus is a Christological recantation. Its concern is the inseparable union of Christ’s deity and humanity against the errors Leporius had held; the Marian title is a fixed clause within the confession, which is what makes it useful as evidence of settled usage rather than contested claim.
  • The Latin title. The confession renders the Lord’s mother semper virgo (“ever-virgin”) — the Latin equivalent of the Greek aēiparthenos that Athanasius and the Greek fathers had already made a standard style for Mary.
  • Text and date. The Libellus emendationis is printed in Migne, Patrologia Latina 31, cols. 1221–1230, and critically edited by R. Demeulenaere in Corpus Christianorum, Series Latina 64 (Turnhout: Brepols, 1985). Composed in Africa c. 426 during Leporius’ reconciliation under Aurelius and Augustine, it was sent to the Gallic bishops Proculus of Marseille and Cillenius; John Cassian later praised the retraction in his On the Incarnation.
