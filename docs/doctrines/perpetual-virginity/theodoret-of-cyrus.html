Theodoret of Cyrus on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 440 (his Interpretation of the Fourteen Epistles of St Paul, the Antiochene exegete’s complete Pauline commentary, written at Cyrrhus in the decade before the Council of Chalcedon; Theodoret c. 393 – c. 458/466, bishop of Cyrrhus) · Wikipedia
Summary: Theodoret, expounding Galatians 1:19, gives the Hieronymian reading its clearest Eastern statement and does so by naming and rejecting the rival Epiphanian account: James “the brother of the Lord” was not so by nature, and was not, “as some suppose, the son of Joseph by a previous marriage,” but the son of Clopas and the Lord’s cousin, his mother being “the sister of the Lord’s mother.” He backs it with the same cross-argument the Epiphanians used for their own view — that the dying Christ gave Mary to John, which he would not have done had she a son of her own in James — turning it to Hieronymian ends. Lightfoot files him last in his column C, “Sons of the Virgin’s Sister.”

Claims & evidence

James the cousin, not Joseph’s son by a former wife

Where earlier Eastern witnesses had made the “brethren” Joseph’s sons by a first marriage, Theodoret, commenting on Paul’s “James the Lord’s brother” (Galatians 1:19), sets that solution aside by name and puts the cousins-theory in its place:

He was called “the brother of the Lord” but was not so by nature. For he was not, as some suppose, the son of Joseph by a previous marriage but the son of Clopas and cousin of the Lord. For his mother was the sister of the Lord’s mother. … He was thought by others to be the Lord’s brother, both because their mothers had the same names and because the families shared one house.

— Theodoret, Commentary on Galatians 1:19.1 Two moves are worth marking. First, the explicit repudiation: “he was not, as some suppose, the son of Joseph by a previous marriage” is a direct rejection of the Epiphanian reading — the very reading a Hilary or an Epiphanius had defended — not merely a silence about it. Second, the positive identification is Jerome’s in substance: James is the son of Clopas, the Lord’s cousin, born of “the sister of the Lord’s mother” — the “Mary of Clopas” of John 19:25, on whom the whole Hieronymian construction rests. The apparent brotherhood Theodoret explains as a double accident of names and household: the two Marys bore the same name and the two families lived under one roof, so a cousin passed for a brother.

The cross-argument turned to Hieronymian ends

Theodoret then reaches for the argument from the cross — the same proof the Epiphanians had pressed for their reading (so Hilary: Mary would never have been given to John had she sons of her own) — and bends it to serve the cousins-theory instead:

For the sacred story of the Gospels tells us that the Blessed Virgin had no other son. For seeing her by the cross, the Lord gave her to the most divine John, but he would not have committed her to another if the blessed James, a man possessed of extreme virtue, had been her son.

— Theodoret, Commentary on Galatians 1:19.1 The logic is the standard one — had Mary borne James, the dying Christ would have entrusted her to him, not to John (John 19:26–27) — but the conclusion it now secures is not just that Mary bore no later children (the perpetual virginity as such) but that James, though styled “the Just” and every inch a son’s equal in virtue, was not her son at all. So the same text that the Epiphanians used to lodge the brethren in Joseph’s first family, Theodoret uses to lodge them in Mary’s sister’s.

Notes

  • Relation to the three theories. Theodoret is Hieronymian — the brethren are the Lord’s cousins, sons of Clopas and of the Virgin’s sister, and Mary bore no other child — and he is among the earliest Greek fathers to hold it plainly and by choice, rejecting the Epiphanian alternative he plainly knew. He thus marks the point at which Jerome’s Latin hypothesis had become a settled Eastern option, a generation after Chrysostom had first grafted it (hesitantly, and perhaps by an interpolator’s hand) onto the older Antiochene scheme.
  • “As some suppose.” The phrase is decisive for placing him. A father merely ignorant of the Epiphanian view (as Lightfoot judged Hilary and the Ambrosiaster to be) can be read as a Hieronymian only by inference; Theodoret names the Epiphanian solution in order to deny it, so his Hieronymianism is deliberate, not accidental.
  • Dating. Theodoret’s complete commentary on the fourteen Pauline epistles is usually placed in the 430s–440s, before the Christological storms of 448–451 consumed his last years; it is slotted here at c. 440, after Pelagius (c. 406), as the last of the patristic-era witnesses and the last name in Lightfoot’s column C (the timeline’s one later witness, Theophylact of Ohrid, is a medieval Byzantine coda, c. 1075).2 (Theodoret’s wider corpus is anchored later, c. 458; on this timeline he is dated by the Pauline commentary.)
