The Protevangelium of James on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 150 (an anonymous infancy narrative, falsely ascribed to James the Lord’s brother; usually dated to the middle or second half of the second century, and the earliest surviving text to give an account of Mary’s parentage, childhood, and betrothal) · Wikipedia
Summary: The Protevangelium is the fountainhead of the Epiphanian reading of “the brethren of the Lord.” It portrays Joseph as an elderly widower who already has sons of his own before he is ever betrothed to Mary, and it guards Mary’s virginity so jealously that a midwife is made to verify it after the birth. Its historical value is not as a record of fact — it is a legendary romance, “purely fictitious” in J. B. Lightfoot’s judgment — but as the earliest datable witness that the belief in Mary’s perpetual virginity, and the specific device of explaining the “brethren” as Joseph’s children by a former marriage, were already circulating by the mid-second century.

Claims & evidence

Joseph is an old man with sons of his own when he is betrothed to Mary (§9)

When the lot falls on Joseph to take the Virgin into his keeping, he protests that he is too old and already a father — the single detail on which the whole Epiphanian theory is built:

And the priest said to Joseph, Thou hast been chosen by lot to take into thy keeping the virgin of the Lord. But Joseph refused, saying: I have children, and I am an old man, and she is a young girl. I am afraid lest I become a laughing-stock to the sons of Israel.

— The Protevangelium of James §9, trans. Alexander Walker (Ante-Nicene Fathers, vol. 8).1 On this reading Joseph is Mary’s guardian, not in the ordinary sense her husband; the “brethren of the Lord” are his sons by an earlier wife, older step-brothers of Jesus and no blood-kin of Mary — and Mary need never have borne another child. Every later Epiphanian writer, down to Epiphanius himself, works from this premise.

The narrative gives Joseph sons who travel with the family, and never a daughter (§17–18)

The detail is carried through consistently. On the road to Bethlehem for the enrolment, Joseph reckons his sons among those to be registered, and it is a son who leads the ass:

And Joseph said: I shall enrol my sons, but what shall I do with this maiden? How shall I enrol her? As my wife? I am ashamed. As my daughter then? But all the sons of Israel know that she is not my daughter… And he saddled the ass, and set her upon it; and his son led it, and Joseph followed.

— The Protevangelium of James §17.2 At the cave, Joseph “found a cave there, and led her into it; and leaving his two sons beside her, he went out to seek a midwife.”3 Lightfoot notes that the work gives Joseph sons but names no daughters — the sisters of the Gospels are not yet accounted for; the later infancy gospels that depend on this one begin to supply Joseph’s daughters and even his first wife by name.

A midwife and Salome test the virginity after the birth — the seed of the virginitas in partu (§§19–20)

The narrative guards Mary’s virginity not only in the conception but through the birth itself. Joseph fetches a Hebrew midwife, who reaches the cave to find it filled with a great light and the newborn already at the breast; coming out, she meets a second woman, Salome, and tells her the marvel — “a virgin has brought forth — a thing which her nature admits not of.” Salome demands a bodily test, in words plainly modelled on doubting Thomas (John 20:24–29):

Then said Salome: As the Lord my God liveth, unless I thrust in my finger, and search the parts, I will not believe that a virgin has brought forth. And the midwife went in, and said to Mary: Show thyself; for no small controversy has arisen about thee. And Salome put in her finger, and cried out, and said: Woe is me for mine iniquity and mine unbelief, because I have tempted the living God; and, behold, my hand is dropping off as if burned with fire.

— The Protevangelium of James §§19–20.4 Salome’s hand is restored when, at an angel’s word, she takes up the infant — the sign healed by touching the very body whose virgin birth she had doubted. The episode is the earliest narrative vehicle of the virginitas in partu: the belief that Mary’s virginity was preserved through the act of childbirth, her body left physically intact by the delivery. Richard Bauckham observes that in the Protevangelium the stepbrother account of the “brethren” is bound up with two further beliefs found nowhere in the New Testament — the Davidic descent of Mary (10:1) and this miraculous birth (19:2–3), “whereby Mary’s virginal condition was unimpaired by childbearing” — three notions that all serve to glorify Mary’s virginity, though none of them originates with this book. The in partu strand is thus distinct from, but interwoven with, the brethren-question this page otherwise turns on.5

The distinction matters because two centuries later the virginitas in partu became the precise point of a controversy of its own. Where the Helvidian party denied the virginity after the birth (the “brethren” as Mary’s later sons), the monk Jovinian granted the virginal conception but denied the virginity in the birth — “She was a virgin when she conceived, but not a virgin when she brought forth” — and Ambrose, drafting the Council of Milan’s reply to Pope Siricius, met him with the shut “gate” of Ezekiel 44: “for as a virgin she both conceived and brought forth.” The Salome scene of the Protevangelium is where that later dogmatic claim — which the tradition would come to mark off as the in partu virginity, distinct from the ante partum (the conception) and the post partum (no later children) — first takes narrative form.

The colophon presents “James” the writer as already grown at Herod’s death (§25)

The book closes by claiming James the Lord’s brother as its author, writing as an adult in the aftermath of Herod’s death:

And I James that wrote this history in Jerusalem, a commotion having arisen when Herod died, withdrew myself to the wilderness until the commotion in Jerusalem ceased, glorifying the Lord God, who had given me the gift and the wisdom to write this history.

— The Protevangelium of James §25.6 The claim is part of the fiction — the work is pseudonymous and far too late to be James’s — but it is telling for the theory it serves: it takes for granted that “James the Lord’s brother” was old enough to be writing as a grown man around the time of Jesus’ infancy, i.e. an older step-brother rather than a younger child of Mary. The internal logic of the Epiphanian reading is already fully formed.

Notes

  • What this witness does and does not establish. The Protevangelium is not a reliable historical source — it is a devotional legend, and Lightfoot calls it “a purely fictitious… narrative.” It cannot prove that Joseph was an aged widower. What it does establish, and establishes better than any other document, is the antiquity of the belief: by roughly the middle of the second century there were Christians who already held Mary to be perpetually virgin and who explained the Lord’s brethren as Joseph’s sons by a former marriage. That is why it stands at the head of the Epiphanian tradition rather than in the middle of it.7
  • Who is Salome? She steps into the story at 19:3 with no introduction — not even “a woman named Salome,” simply “Salome” — which implies the writer expected his readers to know her. Bauckham argues she is Salome the daughter of Joseph, one of the sisters of Jesus (whom the same family tradition, and later Epiphanius, name Mary and Salome), naturally supposed to have travelled with her father and brothers to Bethlehem — a better fit than Morton Smith’s guess that she is Salome the disciple of Jesus (Mark 15:40), who would hardly be loitering outside a cave on the Bethlehem road. Her unexplained appearance troubled later copyists and authors, who harmonized it by making her the midwife or a second one: the Latin Gospel of Pseudo-Matthew makes her a second midwife beside “Zelomi,” and two Coptic narratives drop the doubting-and-healing and make the one midwife Salome a lifelong follower of Jesus — folding Salome the midwife together with Salome the disciple, which Bauckham marks “a late development in the tradition,” not the original. The onomastic case that the sister-names Mary and Salome preserve genuine early memory is weighed on its own argument page.5
  • It seeds a whole family of later texts. Lightfoot traces the same Joseph-the-widower motif, with growing embellishment (Joseph’s daughters and first wife named, the sons individualized), through the Gospel of Pseudo-Matthew, the Gospel of the Nativity of Mary, the History of Joseph the Carpenter, the Gospel of Thomas (the infancy gospel), and the Arabic Gospel of the Infancy — all downstream of the Protevangelium. The Epiphanian view thus has broad but genealogically dependent early attestation: a cluster of apocrypha copying one another, not a spread of independent witnesses (Lightfoot, note 5).
  • Relation to the three theories. This is the earliest literary anchor of the Epiphanian reading, which preserves Mary’s perpetual virginity while leaving Joseph a father of an earlier family. It is silent about Jerome’s later cousins hypothesis (the Hieronymian view), which had not yet been devised, and it stands directly opposed to the Helvidian reading that the brethren were Mary’s own later-born children. Whether the Protevangelium’s testimony reflects genuine early tradition or pious invention is exactly the point in dispute between the schools.
