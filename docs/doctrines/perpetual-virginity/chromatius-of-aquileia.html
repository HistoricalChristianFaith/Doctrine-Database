Chromatius of Aquileia on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 407 (bishop of Aquileia c. 388–407; his Tractates on Matthew preached during his episcopate) · Wikipedia
Summary: Chromatius, bishop of Aquileia and a friend of Jerome, Rufinus, Ambrose, and John Chrysostom, addresses the perpetual virginity not through the “brethren of the Lord” but at the “until” of Matthew 1:25. Preaching on the verse, he flatly rejects the inference that “he knew her not till she had borne her firstborn son” means Joseph knew Mary afterward, and defends her continuing virginity by Old-Testament type. He is thus a clear Latin witness against the Helvidian denial — but his one direct statement settles only that Mary bore no other children, not who the brethren were, so he cannot be sorted into the Epiphanian or Hieronymian camps.

Claims & evidence

Mary bore no children after Jesus: the “until” of Matthew 1:25 is no bar

The heart of Chromatius’ testimony is his tractate on Matthew 1:25. He takes up by name the very inference the Helvidians drew from the verse — that “he knew her not till she had borne her firstborn son” implies a knowing afterward — and denies it outright, grounding Mary’s perpetual virginity in the fittingness of her office and in a chain of Old-Testament figures who kept continence after an encounter with God:

But concerning what the Evangelist said, “And he did not know her till she had borne her firstborn son,” not a few careless people insist on asking whether after the Lord’s birth the holy mother Mary had relations with Joseph. But this is not admissible on the grounds of either faith or truth. Far be it indeed that after the sacrament of so great a mystery and after the birth of the sublime Lord, one should believe that the Virgin Mary was intimate with a man. Remember that Miriam the prophetess of the Old Testament (the sister of Moses and Aaron) remained a virgin unsullied by man, having beheld the light of heavenly signs after the plagues of Egypt and the parting of the Red Sea and the Lord’s glory going in advance and seen in a pillar of fire and clouds. It is not plausible therefore that the Mary of the Gospel, a virgin bearing God, who beheld God’s glory not in a cloud but was worthy of carrying him in her virginal womb, had relations with a man. Noah, who was made worthy to converse with God, declared that he would abstain from the conjugal need. Moses, after hearing God calling him from the bush, abstained from conjugal relations. Now are we to believe that Joseph, the man who always did what was right, had relations with holy Mary after the birth of the Lord?

— Tractate on Matthew 3.1 (on Matthew 1:25), trans. Thomas P. Scheck, Chromatius of Aquileia: Sermons and Tractates on Matthew (Ancient Christian Writers 75; 2018).1 Notice what the argument does and does not reach. It answers the “until” (and, in passing, the “firstborn”) proof-text — the classic Helvidian foothold — and secures a virginity that outlasts the birth; it says nothing whatever about the “brethren of the Lord.” His case is typological, not grammatical: he does not parse the Greek heōs hou but urges the sheer unfittingness that the woman who carried God should afterward have known a man, laying beside her Miriam, Noah, and Moses as figures of continence after a divine encounter.

Corroboration at Matthew 1:20: the angel warns Joseph “lest he think otherwise about Mary’s virginity”

The same conviction surfaces earlier in the tractate on the angel’s dream-message to Joseph (Matthew 1:20), where Chromatius reads the revelation as guarding Mary’s virginity from suspicion:

St. Joseph is made aware of the heavenly mystery, lest he think otherwise about Mary’s virginity. He is also made aware of this that he might exclude the evil of suspicion and receive the good of the mystery… so he might acknowledge the integrity of his fiancée and the virgin birth.

— Tractate on Matthew 2.3–4 (on Matthew 1:20), trans. Scheck, ACW 75.2 This bears on the virginity in and around the birth — the integrity of Mary and the reality of the virgin conception — and coheres with the fuller statement at 1:25 that the virginity continued after it.

Notes

  • Relation to the three theories. Chromatius is a witness for the perpetual virginity itself and against the Helvidian denial, but he is not a witness for any one of the two virginity-preserving brethren-solutions. His surviving tractates cover Matthew’s infancy and early chapters and never reach the “brethren” passages (Matthew 12:46–50; 13:55–56), so he nowhere says whether the brethren were Joseph’s sons by a former wife (Epiphanian) or the Lord’s cousins (Hieronymian). He resembles Victorinus of Pettau in leaving the three-camp question open — but for the opposite reason: Victorinus’ own words on the brethren do not survive, whereas Chromatius’ words survive and simply address a different question, the “until” inference rather than the brethren’s identity.
  • The “until”/“firstborn” proof-text. Chromatius is one of the patristic voices behind the reading defended on the argument page “Does the two-word Greek ‘until’ of Matthew 1:25 (heōs hou) imply Mary bore other children?” (assessment: weak). Where his Eastern contemporary John Chrysostom answers the inference from the grammar — “till” is often used in Scripture “without reference to limited times” — Chromatius answers it from fittingness and Old-Testament type; the two are independent Latin and Greek witnesses, of the same generation, that the Matthean “till” carries no implication of relations afterward. He touches the “firstborn” term only in passing (quoting the phrase as he found it) and does not, like Jerome, argue from the word’s legal sense; the fuller weighing of “firstborn” is at “Do the infancy phrases imply siblings?”
  • Dating and transmission. Chromatius was bishop of Aquileia from about 388 until his death around 407, a friend and correspondent of Jerome and Rufinus (until their quarrel), of Ambrose, and of John Chrysostom, whose cause he took up during the controversy over his deposition. His Tractates on Matthew were preached during his episcopate and so are here slotted near its end, at c. 407, beside Pelagius and Chrysostom. The tractates were long transmitted under Chrysostom’s name and were only re-attributed to Chromatius by R. Étaix and J. Lemarié in the 1960s (their critical edition, Corpus Christianorum Series Latina 9A, appeared in 1974); the first complete English translation is Thomas P. Scheck’s, in Ancient Christian Writers vol. 75 (2018).
