Epiphanius of Salamis on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 377 (Epiphanius, bishop of Constantia — the ancient Salamis — in Cyprus from 367, and one of the most learned churchmen of his age. His anchor here is the elaborate pastoral letter he wrote against the Arabian Antidicomarianites and later folded, whole, into Book III of his catalogue of heresies, the Panarion (“Medicine Chest”), as Panarion 78; the work was finished about 377. He died in 403) · Wikipedia
Summary: Epiphanius is the father whose name the whole reading bears — the Epiphanian view, that the “brethren of the Lord” were sons of Joseph by a former marriage, older step-brothers of Jesus and no blood-kin of the Virgin. He did not invent it; it runs back through Origen and the second-century infancy gospels. But he gave it its fullest and most circumstantial fourth-century statement, in the long refutation of the Antidicomarianites that became Panarion 78, and J. B. Lightfoot named the position after him for that reason. There Epiphanius sets out Joseph’s age and his six children by the first wife, argues the brethren-title from their being the elder household, and defends Mary’s unbroken virginity — down to the curious argument that the mother of the Lion of Judah, like a lioness, could bear but a single offspring. Discussing the question again and again and at great length, he betrays no knowledge whatever of the rival Hieronymian cousins-theory — strong evidence that Jerome’s reading did not yet exist.

Claims & evidence

Joseph’s six children by a former wife

The heart of the Epiphanian account is a household reconstructed in detail. In Panarion 78 Epiphanius names Joseph’s children one by one, gives Joseph’s age at each turn, and makes the brethren the fruit of that first marriage — so that the Virgin, taken by an aged widower, bore none of them:

Joseph begot James when he was somewhere around forty years old. After him he had a son named Joses—then Simeon after him, then Judah, and two daughters, one named Mary and one, Salome; and his wife died. (2) And many years later, as a widower of over eighty, he took Mary.

— Epiphanius, Panarion 78 (Against Antidicomarians) 8.1–2.1 The great age is not decoration but argument: “an old man of over eighty did not take a virgin as a sexual partner; she was committed to his protection,” Epiphanius insists, so that Joseph was Mary’s husband in law and guardianship only.2 He grounds the whole picture in what he calls “the tradition of the Jews,” and it coincides at every point with the second-century infancy gospels — the aged widower, the prior children, the merely legal marriage — on which the Antidicomarianites’ own error, he says, could have been avoided had they “consulted histories.”

Why they are called the Lord’s brethren

Having fixed whose sons they were, Epiphanius answers the obvious objection — that Scripture flatly calls them “his brethren” (Matthew 12:47; 13:55). They are so called, he replies, precisely because they were the elder children of the same household, sharing Joseph’s roof and reputed fatherhood, not because Mary bore them:

…let us by all means learn that it is speaking of James and the other sons of Joseph, and not of sons of Mary whom she never had… The scripture calls them brothers to confound [our opponents], and names James, Joses, Simeon, Judah, Salome and Mary, so that they will learn whose son James is and by which mother, and understand who is the elder.

— Epiphanius, Panarion 78.9.5–6.3 The same reasoning runs through his treatment of Joseph as the Lord’s reputed father: since “the incarnation took place without a man’s seed,” Joseph is called father as James is called brother — by the standing of the household, not by generation. James, “in comparison with the [years of] the Lord’s incarnation… was the elder,” the firstborn of Joseph’s first wife.

The Virgin’s unbroken virginity — and the lioness

The purpose of the whole construction is to defend what the Antidicomarianites denied: that Mary remained ever-virgin, bearing Christ and no other child. Epiphanius challenges his opponents to “show me that Mary bore children after the Savior’s birth,” and, running out of ordinary arguments, reaches for a memorable one drawn from the natural historians — the belief (older than him, in Herodotus) that a lioness bears only once in her life. If Christ is the Lion of Judah, then his mother is a lioness, and a lioness does not conceive twice:

[If the Lord is a lion], then, I should call the mother who bore him a lioness; how can any lion be born if the mother is not to be called a lioness? But a lioness does not conceive a second time. Therefore Mary never conceives again; the holy Virgin cannot have had marital relations.

— Epiphanius, Panarion 78.12.6–7.4 The argument is a curiosity — it rests on a piece of ancient zoology no one now credits — but it shows how far Epiphanius would go to bar any second child. His refutation of the Antidicomarianites is, for all that, one of the fullest and most influential statements of the Epiphanian solution the fourth century produced; his positive teaching on the sect he opposed is set out on the Antidicomarianites detail page.

The name-giver — and a witness that Jerome’s theory did not yet exist

J. B. Lightfoot gathered Epiphanius’ account and made it the type-specimen of the reading he christened “Epiphanian”:

Epiphanius came forward as the champion of the same cause against the Antidicomarianites… His account coincides with that of the apocryphal gospels. Joseph, he states, was eighty years old or more when the Virgin was espoused to him; by his former wife he had six children, four sons and two daughters, the names of the daughters were Mary and Salome… his sons, St. James especially, were called the Lord’s brethren because they were brought up with Jesus; the mother of the Lord remained for ever a virgin; as the lioness is said to exhaust her fertility in the production of a single offspring… so she who bore the Lion of Judah could not in the nature of things become a mother a second time.

— Lightfoot, “The Brethren of the Lord,” §21.5 Lightfoot presses the negative point as hard as the positive one. Epiphanius “treats of the subject several times and at great length,” yet “never once alludes to the Hieronymian account” — and a man so jealous for Joseph’s honour, and so lavish in praise of celibacy, would surely have “hailed with delight” Jerome’s solution, which saved the virginity of Joseph as well as of Mary, had he ever heard of it. Together with Origen and the “two Hilaries” (Hilary of Poitiers and the Ambrosiaster), Epiphanius is one of the writers whose silence shows the cousins-theory unknown before Jerome devised it.6

Notes

  • He named the view; he did not originate it. The reading is far older than Epiphanius — it is the burden of the Protevangelium of James and the Gospel of Peter, and is stated outright by Clement, Origen, and Eusebius. Epiphanius’ distinction is to have given it the most detailed and combative fourth-century defence, which is why Lightfoot attached his name to it. His account rests, by his own admission, on “the tradition of the Jews” and the infancy apocrypha rather than on the Gospels alone.
  • Dependence on Hegesippus. Lightfoot notes that Epiphanius, like Eusebius, drew his information about the Lord’s kindred “mainly from Hegesippus,” the second-century Palestinian collector of such traditions — so that the agreement of the two “materially strengthens the position” that the earliest Palestinian evidence leans Epiphanian rather than Hieronymian.
  • Where he stands among the three theories. In the conspectus that closes Lightfoot’s dissertation, Epiphanius is filed under column B, “sons of Joseph by a former wife,” after Gregory of Nyssa and before Ambrose — the Epiphanian column that runs from the Gospel of Peter through Origen, Eusebius, Hilary, and the Ambrosiaster.
  • Two sects, opposite errors. The same pastoral concern that produced Panarion 78 against the Antidicomarianites (who degraded Mary by denying her virginity) produced Panarion 79 against the Collyridians (who deified her, offering her cakes as to a goddess). Epiphanius steers between the two extremes — honouring the Virgin without worshipping her.
