Huldrych Zwingli on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 1522 (Eine Predigt von der ewig reinen Magd Maria — “A Sermon on the Ever-Virgin Maid Mary” — preached at Zürich 17 September 1522, printed 1524; Huldrych [Ulrich] Zwingli, 1484 – 1531, reformer of Zürich) · Wikipedia
Summary: The earliest voice of the Reformation to speak for the perpetual virginity is not Wittenberg’s but Zürich’s. Preaching on the Virgin Mary in September 1522 — the year before Luther’s That Jesus Christ Was Born a Jew — Huldrych Zwingli confesses Mary ever-virgin in the fullest terms: that “this pure virgin… remained, in the birth and after it, a pure and unsullied virgin, for eternity.” His concern is the virginity itself, not the identity of the “brethren of the Lord,” which the sermon never takes up — so he cannot be filed by camp; but that the reformer of Zürich held the doctrine as firmly as any Father, alongside Luther and Calvin, is the striking point of his witness.

Claims & evidence

Mary “a pure and unsullied virgin, for eternity”

Zwingli devoted a whole sermon to the mother of Christ, and in it professes the perpetual virginity without qualification. He first disclaims ever having taught anything to the contrary, then confesses the doctrine on the authority of the gospel:

I have never thought, still less taught, or declared publicly, anything concerning the subject of the ever Virgin Mary, Mother of our salvation, which could be considered dishonorable, impious, unworthy or evil… I believe with all my heart according to the word of holy gospel that this pure virgin bore for us the Son of God and that she remained, in the birth and after it, a pure and unsullied virgin, for eternity.

— Eine Predigt von der ewig reinen Magd Maria (Zürich, 1522).1 The confession is triple: Mary was a virgin in conceiving, a virgin “in the birth” (the virginitas in partu), and a virgin “after it” — the last being the perpetual virginity proper, the point denied by Helvidius and defended across this timeline. Zwingli grounds it not on Marian speculation but “according to the word of holy gospel,” the same appeal to Scripture by which he elsewhere pared away devotions he judged unwarranted — making his retention of this belief the more telling.

Notes

  • Camp: unfiled. The sermon defends the perpetual virginity itself but never addresses who the “brethren of the Lord” were, so Zwingli cannot be classed Epiphanian (Joseph’s sons by a former wife) or Hieronymian (cousins) — unlike Luther and Calvin, who elsewhere come down for the cousin-reading. His witness bears on the virginity, not the brethren-question.
  • The earliest Reformer for the doctrine. Zwingli’s 1522 sermon predates Luther’s 1523 defence and Calvin’s mid-century exposition — so he is the first of the magisterial Reformers on record for the perpetual virginity, further evidence that the belief was not a Roman peculiarity the Reformation swept away but one its founders largely kept.
  • What he does not claim. The sermon is a witness to Mary ever-virgin; it is not evidence for the later Marian dogmas (the immaculate conception, the assumption), and Zwingli’s wider theology cut sharply against invoking or venerating the saints. Read on its own terms, it establishes only that he held Mary a perpetual virgin and said so as plainly as any Father.
