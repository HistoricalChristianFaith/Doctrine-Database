Tertullian on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 210 (the North-African advocate flourished c. 197–220; the works that bear on the brethren cluster in that maturity — Against Marcion iv and On the Flesh of Christ c. 206–212, the Montanist On Monogamy a little later, c. 213–217) · Wikipedia
Summary: Tertullian is the earliest patristic writer usually reckoned a Helvidian — that is, the earliest father whose words imply that the “brethren of the Lord” were the sons of Joseph and Mary, born after Jesus. He never states this as a thesis; it falls out of his anti-docetic polemic. Pressing the reality of Christ’s human birth against Marcion and Apelles, he insists that the mother and brethren who “stood without” were as truly the Lord’s own as any man’s kin are his — the brethren His brothers in the same sense in which Mary was His mother. And where he does speak of Mary’s virginity, he affirms it only before the birth: she conceived a virgin but, he says plainly, “was a wife when she brought forth her son,” her womb opened as in any marriage. His authority was afterwards claimed by Helvidius himself, and Jerome, unable to deny it, could only answer that Tertullian was “not a member of the Church.” J. B. Lightfoot judged it “highly probable” — though, he cautioned, not absolutely certain — that Tertullian held the Helvidian view.

Claims & evidence

The brethren are the Lord’s brothers as truly as Mary is His mother

Marcion and Apelles seized on Christ’s words — “Who is my mother, and who are my brethren?” (Matthew 12:48) — as proof that He was never really born, having (they said) no true mother or brethren at all. Tertullian answers that the very announcement presupposes their existence, and that Christ did not deny His kin but only ranked faith above them. The argument turns entirely on their being real blood-relations: in Against Marcion he writes,

We, for our part, say in reply, first, that it could not possibly have been told Him that His mother and His brethren stood without, desiring to see Him, if He had had no mother and no brethren… If, therefore, He made them “His mother and His brethren” who were not so, how could He deny them these relationships who really had them? Surely only on the condition of their deserts, and not by any disavowal of His near relatives.

— Tertullian, Against Marcion iv.19.1 He makes the same case in On the Flesh of Christ, where he notes that the crowd knew the family perfectly well — “His supposed father, Joseph the carpenter, and His mother Mary, and His brethren, and His sisters, were very well known to them” — and insists that “nobody would have told Him that His mother and brethren were standing outside, if he were not certain both that He had a mother and brethren.”2 Tertullian never says in so many words that the brethren were Mary’s own children; but, as Lightfoot observed, “his argument seems to imply that the Lord’s brethren were His brothers in the same sense in which Mary was His mother” — the natural, plain-sense reading, and the one the Epiphanian and Hieronymian theories were later devised to avoid.3

Mary was a virgin at conception but a wife at the birth — virginity before, not perpetual

Where Tertullian speaks directly of Mary’s virginity, he limits it to the conception and expressly denies that it survived the birth. Against heretics who played on the paradox “a virgin and yet not a virgin,” he draws the line himself:

She who bare (really) bare; and although she was a virgin when she conceived, she was a wife when she brought forth her son. Now, as a wife, she was under the very law of “opening the womb”… Indeed she ought rather to be called not a virgin than a virgin, becoming a mother at a leap, as it were, before she was a wife.

— Tertullian, On the Flesh of Christ 23.4 The point is unmistakable: Mary’s womb was “opened” in the ordinary way of childbirth — a denial of virginity in partu, and, taken with the passages above, of any perpetual virginity. He says as much again in the Montanist tract On Monogamy, praising Mary as the model of the once-married: “it was a virgin, about to marry once for all after her delivery, who gave birth to Christ, in order that each title of sanctity might be fulfilled in Christ’s parentage, by means of a mother who was both virgin, and wife of one husband.”5 Mary is a virgin before the delivery and a true wife — a univira, wife of one husband — after it. Her honour, for Tertullian, lies not in unbroken virginity but in her having married only once.

Claimed by Helvidius; conceded by Jerome

The reception confirms the reading. When Helvidius argued at Rome (c. 383) that Mary bore other children, he appealed to Tertullian as a predecessor. Jerome, replying, did not dispute the appeal — he could not — but sought to blunt it: “de Tertulliano nihil amplius dico quam ecclesiae hominem non fuisse” (“concerning Tertullian I say nothing more than that he was not a man of the Church”).6 The concession is telling. Everywhere else in his reply to Helvidius, Jerome names authority after authority for Mary’s virginity — Ignatius, Polycarp, Irenaeus, Justin; but Tertullian he can only wave away by impugning his churchmanship, not his testimony. Lightfoot draws the conclusion: “It is therefore highly probable that he held the Helvidian view. Such an admission from one who was so strenuous an advocate of asceticism is worthy of notice.”3

Notes

  • “Probable,” not certain. Lightfoot is careful not to overstate. Tertullian nowhere says the brethren were Mary’s sons; the Helvidian conclusion is an inference from his anti-Marcionite argument (adv. Marc. iv.19, de Carn. Christ. 7), while his explicit statements concern Mary’s virginity only (de Carn. Christ. 23, de Monog. 8). Lightfoot grants that the usual assumption — that Tertullian held the brethren to be sons of Joseph and Mary — “though probable, is not absolutely certain,” since Jerome’s dispute with Helvidius turned on the virginity of the mother rather than on the precise relationship of the brethren. What is certain is the narrower point that no one contests: Tertullian denied Mary a virginity that outlasted the birth.3
  • Why an ascetic reads this way. Tertullian was, if anything, more rigorous about virginity and continence than the churchmen who exalted Mary’s perpetual virginity. His stake here is Christological, not Mariological: against Marcion’s Docetism he needed Christ’s birth, mother, and brethren to be fully real, and against the paradox-mongers he needed Mary’s childbearing to be a genuine, womb-opening birth. The perpetual virginity, still taking shape in his day, was simply not yet the fixed point it would later become; Tertullian could let it go without any sense of impiety.
  • Position in the debate. Tertullian heads Lightfoot’s conspectus of the first theory — the “sons of Joseph and Mary” column — ahead of Helvidius, Bonosus, and the Antidicomarianites.3 He is thus the earliest named witness on the Helvidian side, standing against the Protevangelium’s Epiphanian solution that circulated in the same century. His testimony does not by itself decide the exegetical question — it shows that a learned Latin father c. 210 read “brethren” in its plain sense and did not hold Mary ever-virgin.
