Peter Chrysologus on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 440 (Peter Chrysologus, c. 380–c. 450, a native of Imola in Emilia who served as deacon there and became bishop of Ravenna — then the imperial residence of the Western court — c. 433. Famed as a preacher, he was surnamed Chrysologus, “golden-worded,” the Latin counterpart of the Greek Chrysostomos; a collection of some 170 short sermons survives, from which this witness is drawn. Sermon 117 is undated in itself but belongs to his episcopate, conventionally placed in the 440s) · Wikipedia
Summary: Preaching at Ravenna on the two Adams, Peter Chrysologus states the perpetual virginity in its full threefold reach — Mary a virgin in conceiving, in bearing, and in remaining so afterward — and expressly contrasts her childbearing with that of ordinary women, treating it as a sign of God’s power rather than a work of nature. His “the Virgin conceives, the Virgin brings forth, and she remains a virgin” is the same threefold formula Augustine had fixed in the Latin liturgical memory (virgo concepit, virgo peperit, virgo permansit) and that Leo the Great would carry into the Christological definitions of mid-century. Like Leporius and Chromatius, he attests the perpetual virginity itself while leaving the identity of the Lord’s brethren untouched.

Claims & evidence

“The Virgin conceives, the Virgin brings forth her child, and she remains a virgin”

Sermon 117, headed “The First Adam, and the Last Adam, Born of a Virgin,” expounds Paul’s contrast of the two Adams (1 Corinthians 15:45–49): the first man was “molded from the cheapest earth,” the last “came forth from the Virgin’s precious womb.” Turning to that womb, Chrysologus rejects any levelling of Mary’s childbearing with the ordinary kind and states her virginity through all three moments:

Where are they who think that the Virgin’s conception and giving birth to her child are to be likened to those of other women?… The Virgin conceives, the Virgin brings forth her child, and she remains a virgin.

— Peter Chrysologus, Sermon 117 (c. 440).1 The three verbs — conceives, brings forth, remains — carry the doctrine at its fullest reach: virginity ante partum (in conceiving), in partu (in giving birth), and post partum (remaining so). The middle term is the strong one: the point of the contrast with “other women” is that Mary’s giving birth, unlike theirs, left her virginity intact — a miraculous in partu virginity, not merely abstinence before and after.

A sign of God’s power, not a work of nature

Chrysologus frames the virgin birth not as a natural event to be explained but as a divine sign that breaks the order of nature. The contrast that runs through the sermon — the first Adam from lowly earth, the last from the Virgin’s womb; earth changed into flesh, flesh raised up to God — makes the manner of Christ’s birth a token of who He is. That the Virgin should conceive, bear, and yet remain a virgin is, on his account, “no common occurrence, but a sign… God’s power, for He is the cause, and not nature.”2 This wiki does not discount a claim merely because it requires a miracle; Chrysologus states the miracle openly and rests it on divine power, which is the coherent shape of the in partu claim rather than an embarrassment to it.

What it settles — and what it leaves open

Chrysologus affirms the perpetual virginity but offers no account of the “brethren of the Lord.” His witness therefore bears directly on the virginity itself — and, more than most, on the disputed in partu and post partum virginity — while saying nothing to decide between the Epiphanian (sons of Joseph by a former marriage) and Hieronymian (cousins) readings of the brethren. Like Athanasius, Didymus, Chromatius, and Leporius, he attests that Mary was and remained ever-virgin and leaves the genealogy of the brethren untouched.

Notes

  • “Golden-worded.” The epithet Chrysologus was applied to Peter as the Latin match to John Chrysostom’s Chrysostomos; his surviving sermons are short, rhetorically dense homilies preached at Ravenna. He was declared a Doctor of the Church in 1729.
  • The threefold formula. “Virgin in conceiving, in bearing, and remaining” is the standard Western triad — virginitas ante partum, in partu, post partum. Augustine’s Christmas sermons had crystallized it as virgo concepit, virgo peperit, virgo permansit (Sermon 186), and Leo the Great’s Tome and Christmas sermons pressed the same reading in the years around Chalcedon (451). Chrysologus, preaching in the imperial capital of the West in these same decades, is a witness to the formula as common Latin confession.
  • Text and edition. The English is George E. Ganss’ translation in Saint Peter Chrysologus: Selected Sermons; Saint Valerian: Homilies (Fathers of the Church 17; Catholic University of America Press, 1953), pp. 199–202. The Latin is in Migne, Patrologia Latina 52, cols. 520–521; a modern critical edition of the sermons is A. Olivar, Sancti Petri Chrysologi Collectio sermonum (Corpus Christianorum, Series Latina 24, 24A, 24B; Brepols, 1975–1982).
