Augustine of Hippo on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 394 → later (his earliest engagement, the Commentary on Galatians, was written c. 394 while he was still a presbyter; the mature statements come in the anti-Manichaean Reply to Faustus, c. 398–400, and the Tractates on John, c. 406–420; Augustine was bishop of Hippo 395–430) · Wikipedia
Summary: Augustine is the weightiest Latin advocate of the Hieronymian reading — the brethren of the Lord are kinsmen, “all the blood relations of Mary,” not her later-born children. In his early Commentary on Galatians (c. 394) he still offered the Hieronymian and Epiphanian accounts as alternatives; but from his mature works on he holds Jerome’s cousins-theory without wavering, laying down as a settled rule of Scripture that “brother” runs out to any degree of kinship. Because Augustine’s authority carried nearly everything before it in the Latin West, his adoption of Jerome’s hypothesis did more than any other single witness to make it the standard Western reading.

Claims & evidence

“The kinsmen of Mary, of whatever degree, are the brethren of the Lord”

Expounding John 2:12 (“He went down to Capernaum, He, and His mother, and His brethren”), Augustine pauses on the very objection the deniers pressed — “Whence had the Lord brethren? For surely Mary did not give birth a second time?” — and answers it with a flat denial and a rule of scriptural usage. Scripture, he says, calls men “brethren” not only when they share a mother or a father but when they are kin of any degree; so the brethren are Mary’s relations, not her sons:

Then, whence the brethren? The kinsmen of Mary, of whatever degree, are the brethren of the Lord. How do we prove this? From Scripture itself. Lot is called “Abraham’s brother;” he was his brother’s son… Read the Scripture, and thou wilt find that uncle and sister’s son are called brothers. When thou hast known this rule, thou wilt find that all the blood relations of Mary are the brethren of Christ.

— Tractates on the Gospel of John, Tractate X.2 (on John 2:12).1 The virginity itself he guards in the same breath: “surely Mary did not give birth a second time? Far from it! With her begins the dignity of virgins.”1 This is the Hieronymian position exactly — the brethren are cousins/kinsmen, and Mary bore only Christ.

“Not children of the Virgin Mary, but all the blood relations of the Lord”

Answering the Manichaean Faustus, Augustine again invokes the same rule of Scripture — that “among the ancients, it was customary to call cousins brothers and sisters” (with Tobit and the Lot example) — and applies it directly to the Gospel brethren:

And, by the same use of the word, those called in the Gospel the Lord’s brothers are certainly not children of the Virgin Mary, but all the blood relations of the Lord.

— Reply to Faustus the Manichaean xxii.35.2 The two passages agree word for word in substance: the brethren are “blood relations,” and the qualification “certainly not children of the Virgin Mary” rules out the Helvidian reading as decisively as the wide sense of “brother” rules in the Hieronymian one.

From an early “either/or” to a settled cousins-theory

Augustine did not start there. In his early Commentary on Galatians (Expositio ad Galatas) — his only complete formal commentary on a book of Scripture, written c. 394 while he was still a presbyter — he pauses on “James the Lord’s brother” (Galatians 1:19) and leaves the brethren’s identity deliberately open between the two non-Helvidian accounts:

Alium autem Apostolorum non vidi, nisi Iacobum fratrem Domini. Iacobus Domini frater vel ex filiis Ioseph de alia uxore vel ex cognatione Mariae matris eius debet intellegi.

— “But other of the apostles I saw none, save James the Lord’s brother. James the Lord’s brother is to be understood either as [one] of the sons of Joseph by another wife, or as of the kindred of Mary his mother.” Expositio ad Galatas, on Galatians 1:19.3 The first limb is the Epiphanian reading (the brethren are Joseph’s children by a former marriage); the second is the kinsman reading Jerome had already sharpened into the cousins-theory. At this stage Augustine endorses neither. J. B. Lightfoot reads the passage the same way — in the Galatians commentary “he offers the alternative of the Hieronymian and Epiphanian accounts,” but “in his later works he consistently maintains the view put forward by Jerome in the treatise against Helvidius,” citing the two passages above along with a second Johannine tractate (xxviii), the Exposition of Psalm 127, and the Questions on Matthew.4 The mature Hieronymian statements are quoted above from Augustine’s own words.

The weight that carried the West

Jerome had devised the cousins-theory in 383; it was Augustine’s adoption of it that made it stick. “Jerome’s hypothesis found a much more weighty advocate in St. Augustine… Thus supported, it won its way to general acceptance in the Latin Church,” Lightfoot writes; “and the Western Services recognize only one James besides the son of Zebedee” — that is, the Latin service-books came to identify the Lord’s brother James with James the son of Alphaeus, exactly as the Hieronymian reading requires.4 In his conspectus of the authorities Lightfoot accordingly files Augustine in column C, “Sons of the Virgin’s Sister” — the Hieronymian column — between Jerome and Theodoret.4

Notes

  • Relation to the three theories. Augustine is Hieronymian — the brethren are kinsmen/cousins, “all the blood relations of Mary,” and Mary bore only Christ — and he holds it firmly, laying it down as a rule of scriptural language rather than (like Ambrose) leaving the identification deliberately open. He does not, however, reproduce the further refinement that would later complete the theory — the identification of Clopas with Alphaeus, which makes James the Lord’s brother the same man as James son of Alphaeus. Lightfoot observes that this step “does not anywhere appear” in Augustine, and enters the tradition only later, with Chrysostom and Theodoret.
  • The distinctive Augustinian note: Mary’s vow. Where the argument for the virginity most often runs through the identity of the brethren, Augustine also grounds it in Mary’s own settled intent — that her words to Gabriel, “How shall this be, seeing I know not a man?” (Luke 1:34), imply a resolve of perpetual virginity already formed — words she “would not say, unless she had before vowed herself unto God as a virgin.”5 This became a standard Latin proof and reinforced the reading that she had, and would have, no other children.
  • Dating and slotting. Augustine is placed on the timeline at c. 394, the date of his earliest engagement with the question (the Commentary on Galatians), so that his early even-handedness and his later firm Hieronymianism can be read against Jerome’s tract of 383 and Ambrose’s reticent treatise of 392. The verified quotations themselves are from later works — the Reply to Faustus (c. 398–400) and the Tractates on John (c. 406–420).
