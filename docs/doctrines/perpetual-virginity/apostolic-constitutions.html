The Apostolic Constitutions on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 380 (the Constitutions of the Holy Apostles, a large Church order compiled in Syria in the later fourth century out of older material — the third-century Didascalia Apostolorum, the Didache, and other strata — and pseudonymously issued in the name of the twelve apostles through Clement of Rome. J. B. Lightfoot judged that “the main part… may perhaps be regarded as a work of the third century, though they received considerable additions in later ages”) · Wikipedia
Summary: The Apostolic Constitutions repeatedly and deliberately keep two men named James apart: James the son of Alphaeus, one of the Twelve, and James the brother of the Lord, bishop of Jerusalem. Because the second is counted in addition to the Twelve — a supernumerary apostle like Paul, so that the roster runs to fourteen — the work bears directly on the “James not one of the Twelve” sub-question. That distinction tells squarely against the Hieronymian reading, which identifies the Lord’s brother with James the son of Alphaeus and so folds him into the Twelve; it leaves the Epiphanian and Helvidian readings, which both keep him distinct, untouched.

Claims & evidence

James the Lord’s brother is listed after the Twelve — a fourteenth apostle beside Paul

Closing the sixth book, the compiler has the apostles subscribe the whole work by name. The list runs through the Twelve — including “James the son of Alphaeus” — and then, past Matthias, adds two more: James the Lord’s brother and Paul.

On whose account also we, who are now assembled in one place,—Peter and Andrew; James and John, sons of Zebedee; Philip and Bartholomew; Thomas and Matthew; James the son of Alphaeus, and Lebbaeus who is surnamed Thaddaeus; and Simon the Canaanite, and Matthias, who instead of Judas was numbered with us; and James the brother of the Lord and bishop of Jerusalem, and Paul the teacher of the Gentiles, the chosen vessel, having all met together, have written to you this Catholic doctrine.

— Constitutions of the Holy Apostles vi.14.1 James the son of Alphaeus stands among the Twelve; James “the brother of the Lord and bishop of Jerusalem” is named separately at the end, and Paul — “the chosen vessel,” the standing type of the apostle-outside-the-Twelve — is set beside him. The two are supernumeraries, giving fourteen names in all.

The Twelve, then Paul, then James the Lord’s brother — the same reckoning in Book II

The point is not a slip of the closing formula; the same threefold reckoning governs the body of the work. Enumerating the messengers God sent in each age, Book II ends with the Christian apostolate:

…those after His passion by us, the twelve apostles, and Paul the chosen vessel. We therefore, who have been vouchsafed the favour of being the witnesses of His appearance, together with James the brother of our Lord, and the other seventy-two disciples, and his seven deacons, have heard from the mouth of…

— Constitutions of the Holy Apostles ii.55.2 Here “the twelve apostles, and Paul” are the preachers; “James the brother of our Lord” is joined to them alongside the seventy-two disciples and the seven deacons — ranked with the wider circle, not counted inside the Twelve.

James the brother heads the bishops, distinct from the Twelve who ordain

The enumeration of the bishops ordained by the apostles opens with James — not as one of the ordaining Twelve, but as the first man they set over Jerusalem, whose successors were his blood-kin:

Now concerning those bishops which have been ordained in our lifetime, we let you know that they are these:—James the bishop of Jerusalem, the brother of our Lord; upon whose death the second was Simeon the son of Cleopas; after whom the third was Judas the son of James.

— Constitutions of the Holy Apostles vii.46.3 The nineteenth-century editor of the standard English text noted the force of exactly this feature: “An incidental proof of the early origin of this compilation is furnished by the clear distinction it makes between James the son of Alphaeus and James the brother of our Lord. The theory of Jerome, which identifies them, was later.”3 And in the eighth book the ordination-canons are delivered by “we, the twelve apostles of the Lord, who are now together… there being present with us Paul the chosen vessel, our fellow-apostle, and James the bishop” — once more the Twelve, then Paul, then James, three ranks and not one.4

What the distinction settles — and what it leaves open

Lightfoot placed the work in his catena for just this reason: the Apostolic Constitutions, he wrote, “distinguish James the Lord’s brother from James the son of Alphaeus, making him, like St. Paul, a supernumerary apostle, and thus counting fourteen in all.”5 The consequence is one-sided. Jerome’s Hieronymian solution rests on identifying the Lord’s brother James with James the son of Alphaeus, so making the “brethren” the Lord’s cousins and members of the Twelve; a fourth-century Church order that flatly separates the two men — and, as its editor observed, does so before Jerome’s theory existed — is a witness against that identification. It does not, however, decide between the two readings that keep James outside the Twelve: both the Epiphanian (the brethren are Joseph’s sons by a former wife) and the Helvidian (they are Mary’s own later children) are equally consistent with a James the Lord’s brother who is no apostle of the Twelve. On this narrow but real point the Constitutions line up with the older tradition — Hegesippus, the Protevangelium, Clement, Origen — against the reading Jerome would introduce a generation later.

Notes

  • Two Jameses within one book. The same eighth book that names “James the bishop” also gives, under its own heading, “The Constitution of James the Son of Alphaeus” (“And I James, the son of Alphaeus, make a constitution in regard to confessors…”), and, in a variant reading preserved in some manuscripts, records that “James, the brother of the Lord, has been killed with stones by the Jews in Jerusalem” — the martyred bishop, distinct from the still-speaking son of Alphaeus. The compiler is not confusing the names; he is holding them apart on purpose.6
  • Date and weight. The Constitutions are a late, pseudonymous compilation, not an apostolic document; their value here is as evidence for what the fourth-century Syrian church took for granted, not for anything the apostles themselves signed. But that is exactly why the distinction counts: it is assumed, not argued — the compiler simply reckons James the Lord’s brother a fourteenth apostle beside Paul, as though no one would think to identify him with James of the Twelve. That the assumption still held around 380 shows how late Jerome’s contrary identification (c. 383) really was.
  • Relation to the three theories. Like Hegesippus, the Apostolic Constitutions weigh against the Hieronymian cousins-reading without settling the contest between the Epiphanian and Helvidian views. The perpetual virginity itself is neither affirmed nor denied by the passages above; what they establish is only that the Lord’s brother James was not, in this tradition, one of the Twelve.
