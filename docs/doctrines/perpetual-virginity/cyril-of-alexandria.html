Cyril of Alexandria on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 428 (his Commentary on the Gospel of John and the Glaphyra on Genesis, both from the great exegetical output that preceded the outbreak of the Nestorian controversy in 428; Cyril c. 376 – 444, patriarch of Alexandria) · Wikipedia
Summary: Cyril, expounding John 7:3–5, reads the “brethren of the Lord” as sons of Joseph and not of the Virgin — the Epiphanian view stated in his own voice. The unbelieving brethren treat Jesus as one of themselves, “in truth begotten of their father Joseph,” and Cyril reads Jeremiah’s “the house of thy father” as guarding the point “lest they too should be supposed to have been of the blessed Virgin, rather than of His father Joseph alone.” J. B. Lightfoot cites him (from the Glaphyra on Genesis) as evidence that, apart from the temporary Hieronymian defection of the late Chrysostom and of Theodoret, the Epiphanian reading “maintained its ground in the East,” and files him in column B, “Sons of Joseph by a former wife.”

Claims & evidence

The brethren “of His father Joseph alone,” not of the Virgin

Commenting on the scene where the “brethren” urge Jesus to leave Galilee and display His works in Judaea (John 7:3–5), Cyril explains their unbelief by the low estimate they still had of Him: not yet knowing the indwelling Word, they reckoned Jesus a mere kinsman like themselves, “begotten of their father Joseph”:

The reputed brethren of the Saviour not yet recognizing God the Word indwelling in His Holy Flesh, nor knowing at the time when they are saying these things, that He was made Man, have still petty conceptions of Him and think far too little of the grace and excellence that is in Him, seeing nothing more than the rest, deluded by the common opinions of Him, thinking that He too was in truth begotten of their father Joseph, and not seeing the hidden provision of the Mystery.

— Cyril of Alexandria, Commentary on the Gospel of John, Book IV (on John 7:3–5).1 Reaching the prophetic type in Jeremiah — “both thy brethren and the house of thy father, they too despised thee” — Cyril presses the added phrase “the house of thy father” to fix whose sons the brethren were, and states the Epiphanian reading outright:

Very watchfully did the Prophet, having named His brethren, profitably add, The house of Thy father, lest they too should be supposed to have been of the blessed Virgin, rather than of His father Joseph alone.

— Cyril of Alexandria, Commentary on the Gospel of John, Book IV (on John 7:5).1 The distinction is exactly the Epiphanian one: the brethren belong to “His father Joseph alone” and are no children of the Virgin — a brotherhood that is only reputed (Cyril calls them the “reputed brethren” and elsewhere speaks of Christ’s “generally supposed relationship after the flesh”), built on the reputed fatherhood of Joseph, exactly as Eusebius and Epiphanius had framed it. The Virgin is nowhere allowed to be their mother; the perpetual virginity is preserved by lodging the brethren in Joseph’s household, not Mary’s womb.

“The Epiphanian view maintained its ground in the East”

Cyril is J. B. Lightfoot’s proof that the Eastern defection to Jerome’s cousins-theory was a passing episode. Having traced how the late Chrysostom (on Galatians 1:19) and then Theodoret took up the Hieronymian reading, Lightfoot marks them as exceptions and turns to Cyril:

But with these exceptions the Epiphanian view maintained its ground in the East. It is found again in Cyril of Alexandria for instance (Glaphyr. in Gen. lib. vii. p. 221), and seems to have been held by later Greek writers almost, if not quite, universally.

— J. B. Lightfoot, The Brethren of the Lord.2 The passage Lightfoot cites is in the seventh book of the Glaphyra on Genesis, Cyril’s typological reading of the patriarch Joseph; that particular text is reported here at second hand from Lightfoot rather than quoted firsthand, so on its own it would stand ⚠ unverified. But the reading it attests is Cyril’s own beyond doubt, for he states the same Epiphanian view plainly in the firsthand Commentary on John above. Lightfoot accordingly files Cyril in the “Sons of Joseph by a former wife” column of his conspectus (column B), among the Eastern authorities after Chrysostom and before the later Greek service-books.

Notes

  • Relation to the three theories. Cyril is Epiphanian — the brethren are Joseph’s sons, not Mary’s, and their brotherhood to Jesus rests on Joseph’s reputed fatherhood, so Mary bore no child but the Lord. He betrays no use of the Hieronymian cousins-theory in these passages; where Theodoret, a decade or so later and in the same Antiochene-influenced East, would name the Epiphanian solution in order to reject it, Cyril simply holds it.
  • Why he matters on the timeline. Coming after the Eastern “transition” that Chrysostom embodies and roughly contemporary with Theodoret’s open Hieronymianism, Cyril shows the two Antiochene Hieronymians to have been the exception, not the rule: the older Alexandrian and Palestinian reading — running back through Origen, Eusebius, and Epiphanius — was still the settled Eastern view in Alexandria’s chief see, and (on Lightfoot’s reckoning) stayed so among “later Greek writers almost… universally.”
  • Dating. Cyril’s Commentary on John and the Glaphyra belong to the large body of exegesis he produced before the Nestorian controversy erupted in 428 and absorbed his last sixteen years; the page is anchored at c. 428, after Pelagius (c. 406) and before Theodoret (c. 440). (Cyril’s wider life runs to his death in 444; on this timeline he is dated by the exegetical works that carry the claim.)
