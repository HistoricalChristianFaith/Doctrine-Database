Jerome on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 383 (the date of the treatise; Jerome, c. 347–420, was then a young man at Rome under Pope Damasus, and wrote The Perpetual Virginity of Blessed Mary, Against Helvidius in reply to Helvidius the same year) · Wikipedia
Summary: Jerome is the eponym of the Hieronymian reading of the brethren — that the “brethren of the Lord” were His maternal cousins, sons of “Mary of Clopas” the Virgin’s sister, so that “brother” means kinsman and the perpetual virginity of Mary and Joseph alike is preserved. He struck out the theory in c. 383 in his reply to Helvidius, who had argued the brethren were the true sons of Joseph and Mary; unwilling even to grant Helvidius the Epiphanian solution (that they were Joseph’s sons by a former wife), Jerome devised a third reading that left both Mary and Joseph ever-virgin. He built it on the wide use of “brother” in Scripture and on identifying the “Mary the mother of James and Joses” of the passion narratives with the “Mary of Clopas” whom John sets beside the Virgin at the cross and calls her sister. J. B. Lightfoot, who named the view after him, judged it the latest-born of the three theories — devised for the occasion, resting on Scripture alone with no father cited before Jerome, and held by Jerome himself with a grip that loosened over his later works. The reading nonetheless carried the Latin West through Augustine and the Western service-books.

Claims & evidence

The cousins-theory — brethren as sons of “Mary of Clopas,” the Virgin’s sister

The heart of Jerome’s new hypothesis is an identification of the several Marys named at the cross and tomb. John 19:25 sets beside the Virgin “his mother’s sister, Mary the wife of Clopas”; the Synoptic passion-narratives name “Mary the mother of James the less and of Joses.” Jerome takes these to be one woman — the Virgin’s sister — and so makes her sons James and Joses the Lord’s cousins, not His brothers:

The only conclusion is that the Mary who is described as the mother of James the less was the wife of Alphaeus and sister of Mary the Lord’s mother, the one who is called by John the Evangelist “Mary of Clopas,” whether after her father, or kindred, or for some other reason.

— Jerome, Against Helvidius §15.1 From this the whole reading follows, which he states plainly:

Now here we have the explanation of what I am endeavouring to show, how it is that the sons of Mary, the sister of our Lord’s mother, who though not formerly believers afterwards did believe, can be called brethren of the Lord.

— Jerome, Against Helvidius §16.1 Jerome does not, however, press the pivot of his own theory to certainty: he grants he cannot say why the woman is called “of Clopas,” and declines to insist on whether “Mary the wife of Clopas and Mary the mother of James and Joses were different women, provided it is clearly understood that Mary the mother of James and Joses was not the same person as the Lord’s mother.” That hesitation over the very hinge of the reading is one Lightfoot would later fasten on (see Notes).

“Four kinds of brethren” — the philological argument

To license “brother” = “cousin,” Jerome lays out the range of the word in Scripture, distinguishing four senses (in his Latin: natura, gente, cognatione, affectu):

In Holy Scripture there are four kinds of brethren—by nature, race, kindred, love. Instances of brethren by nature are Esau and Jacob, the twelve patriarchs, Andrew and Peter, James and John.

— Jerome, Against Helvidius §16.1 The brethren of the Lord, he argues, belong to the third class, kinship — as Scripture calls a nephew a “brother”:

Certainly Lot was not Abraham’s brother, but the son of Abraham’s brother Aram.

— Jerome, Against Helvidius §16 (citing Genesis 13:8; he adds Jacob called “brother” of his uncle Laban, Genesis 29:15).1 The four-sense scheme, and the Lot and Laban parallels, became the standard exegetical machinery of the Hieronymian reading ever after. The philological point is real but does less than Jerome makes it do: the parallels license “brother” = “kinsman” only in cases of a single, already-known relationship named loosely in direct speech, not the habitual third-party designation of a whole group the cousin-reading requires — and the Greek had a precise word for cousin (anepsios) which the tradition uses of the family’s one actual cousin. That the lexical case leaves the cousin-reading barely possible but gives it no positive weight is weighed on the argument page “Does ‘brother’ (adelphos) mean ‘cousin’?” (assessment: weak).

The virginity of Joseph too

Where the Epiphanian solution kept Joseph a widower and father of an earlier family, Jerome’s reading spares Joseph any prior children at all, and he presses the ascetic logic to its end — the virginity not of Mary only but of Joseph:

You say that Mary did not continue a virgin: I claim still more, that Joseph himself on account of Mary was a virgin, so that from a virgin wedlock a virgin son was born.

— Jerome, Against Helvidius §21.2 This was, as Lightfoot notes, the boast of the theory — it “asserted the virginity not of Mary only but of Joseph also” — and the very feature that ought to have commended it to earlier ascetic writers, had any of them known it.

No father before him — the appeal that names no one

Helvidius had claimed the older authority of Tertullian and Victorinus of Pettau. Jerome, unable to produce witnesses for his own cousins-reading, answers not by naming supporters of it but by disqualifying Tertullian and gesturing at a list of fathers who had defended the virginity in general:

Of Tertullian I say no more than that he did not belong to the Church.

Might I not array against you the whole series of ancient writers? Ignatius, Polycarp, Irenaeus, Justin Martyr, and many other apostolic and eloquent men, who against Ebion, Theodotus of Byzantium, and Valentinus, held these same views, and wrote volumes replete with wisdom.

— Jerome, Against Helvidius §19.3 But these named men had defended Mary’s virginity, not the cousins-identification of the brethren; for that reading Jerome quotes none of them, offering it, as Lightfoot observed, “as a critical deduction from the statements of Scripture.” This silence — that Jerome, so eager elsewhere to pile up authorities, could name not one for his own account of the relationship — is the linchpin of the case that the theory was new with him.

Later restatements — firm, then loosening

Nine years on, Jerome restated the reading without hedging, in his catalogue of Christian writers:

James, who is called the brother of the Lord, surnamed the Just, the son of Joseph by another wife, as some think, but, as appears to me, the son of Mary sister of the mother of our Lord of whom John makes mention in his book, after our Lord’s passion at once ordained by the apostles bishop of Jerusalem…

— Jerome, Lives of Illustrious Men 2 (392).4 Yet in between, expounding the very verse (Galatians 1:19) on which the debate turned, he had declined to re-argue the point and spoke only vaguely of it:

I remember that while I was in Rome, I published a book on the perpetual virginity of Saint Mary under the urging of the brothers. In this matter, I had to argue for a long time about those who are called the brothers of the Lord. Therefore, we should be satisfied with whatever we have written. Now let it suffice that, because of his outstanding character and incomparable faith and wisdom, he was called the Lord’s brother, not in an ordinary sense.

— Jerome, Commentary on Galatians 1:19 (c. 387).5 Lightfoot read the works in chronological order as a curve — vague in 387, firm again in 392 and in the Commentary on Matthew (398), but by the Letter to Hedibia (c. 406–7) treating the keystone identification with plain indifference — and wondered whether Jerome, long resident in Bethlehem and steeped in the Palestinian tradition, had at last relaxed his hold on his own hypothesis (see Notes).

Notes

  • The theory Jerome actually stated, versus the completed theory. Jerome himself advanced only that the brethren were sons of “Mary of Clopas” the Virgin’s sister. Two further identifications usually reckoned part of the “Hieronymian” view — that Clopas is Alphaeus (making the brethren the apostles James and Jude “of Alphaeus”/“of James” among the Twelve) — Jerome nowhere advances; Lightfoot found them first in Chrysostom and Theodoret, who grafted them onto Jerome’s original. Jerome even “confesses plainly that he does not know why Mary is called” of Clopas. The strongest form of the reading is thus not Jerome’s own but a later, fuller construction.6
  • Lightfoot’s adverse verdict. J. B. Lightfoot — whose 1865 dissertation named all three views — gave the Hieronymian reading its most influential modern assessment, and rejected it. Two counts weigh heaviest: that “Jerome claims no traditional support for his theory… he cannot, or at least does not, name a single writer,” so that “no decisive instance can be produced of a writer holding Jerome’s view, before it was propounded by Jerome himself”; and that “Jerome does not hold his theory staunchly and consistently.” On Lightfoot’s reading of the sources, writers who discuss the question before Jerome — Origen, the “two Hilaries,” Epiphanius — betray no knowledge of the cousins-reading, which one so jealous for Joseph’s honour as Epiphanius “would have hailed with delight… had he known of it.” Lightfoot files Jerome in column C of his conspectus, “Sons of the Virgin’s Sister” (with Pelagius, Augustine, and the Western service-books), and concludes the theory must “lead to its rejection.”6
  • The Hedibia wobble. Lightfoot reports that in the late Letter to Hedibia (c. 406–7) Jerome, enumerating the Marys of the Gospels, names “Mary of Cleophas the maternal aunt of the Lord and Mary the mother of James and Joses as distinct persons,” adding that “others contend” the mother of James and Joses was His aunt — so that the identification “which was the keystone of his own theory” he now names “with such indifference.” The point here rests on Lightfoot’s reading of that letter, cited to Lightfoot rather than quoted from Jerome’s primary text.6
  • Dating. The treatise against Helvidius is dated “about a.d. 383” by its standard editors and by Jerome’s own reply; Lightfoot puts it “in the year 382 (or 383).” The timeline slots Jerome at c. 383, immediately after Helvidius, whose treatise it answers.
  • Relation to the three theories. Jerome is the Hieronymian namesake. His reading stands against both the Helvidian denial (Helvidius, Tertullian, the Antidicomarianites) and the Epiphanian solution he refused to borrow — the “sons of Joseph by a former wife” of Origen, Eusebius, Hilary, Ambrosiaster, and Epiphanius, whom he taunted with “following the ravings of the apocryphal writings.”
