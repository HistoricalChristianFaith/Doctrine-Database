Origen on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 248 (the Alexandrian, then Caesarean, master lived c. 185–253/4; his Commentary on Matthew, from which the decisive passage comes, belongs to the last years at Caesarea, c. 246–248) · Wikipedia
Summary: Origen declares himself very distinctly for the Epiphanian reading — that the “brethren of the Lord” were sons of Joseph by a former wife, married before Mary, so that they are no children of Mary and her perpetual virginity stands. He attaches the view to two named second-century texts, the Gospel according to Peter and the Book of James (the Protevangelium), and grounds it in a theological fitness of his own: as Jesus was the first-fruit of chastity among men, so Mary was the first-fruit of virginity among women. He is the earliest father to name those source-texts as the pedigree of the reading, and the fullest early exponent of it. J. B. Lightfoot took Origen’s testimony as doubly weighty: it shows both that he himself held the Epiphanian view and that he was wholly unaware of the Hieronymian — the cousins-theory Jerome would devise a century and a half later — since the only alternative Origen even entertains is the outright denial of the perpetual virginity.

Claims & evidence

The brethren were Joseph’s sons by a former wife — on the Gospel of Peter and the Book of James

Commenting on “Is not this the carpenter’s son? Is not his mother called Mary? and his brethren James, and Joseph, and Simon, and Judas?” (Matthew 13:55), Origen sets out the tradition and endorses it in his own voice:

Some say, basing it on a tradition in the Gospel according to Peter, as it is entitled, or The Book of James, that the brethren of Jesus were sons of Joseph by a former wife, whom he married before Mary. Those who say so wish to preserve the honour of Mary in virginity to the end, so that that body of hers which was appointed to minister to the Word… might not know intercourse with a man after that the Holy Ghost came into her. And I think it in harmony with reason that Jesus was the first-fruit among men of the purity which consists in chastity, and Mary among women; for it were not pious to ascribe to any other than to her the first-fruit of virginity.

— Origen, Commentary on Matthew, Book X.17 (on Matthew 13:55).1 The reading is unambiguously Epiphanian: the brethren are Joseph’s children by an earlier marriage, step-relations of Jesus, and Mary bore no child but her Son. The closing clause — “I think it in harmony with reason” — is Origen speaking for himself, not merely reporting others; Lightfoot notes that a reader who overlooks it (as J. H. Mill did) misses “Origen’s real meaning” and mistakes his own settled judgement for a bare report of “a minority… founded on apocryphal traditions.”2

“The Lord’s brother” can also mark character, not only kinship

Elsewhere Origen glosses the same title in a looser register. Explaining why Paul calls James “the Lord’s brother” (Galatians 1:19), he says Paul so names him “not so much on account of his kinsmanship or their companionship together, as on account of his character and language.”2 Lightfoot observes that this is “not inconsistent with the explicit statement already referred to”: the Epiphanian reading already makes the brotherhood a household relation rather than a blood-tie to Mary, so remarking that the title also fits James’s holiness only reinforces that “brother” here is not the plain uterine sense the Helvidians pressed.

Notes

  • The earliest named pedigree of the Epiphanian view. Origen is the first witness to attach the reading to specific texts — the Gospel according to Peter and the Book of James (the Protevangelium of James). Because the Gospel of Peter’s own words on the brethren do not survive, its testimony on this point reaches us only through Origen; his report is the sole warrant for seating that gospel on the timeline at all (see the summary’s entry for it). His firsthand endorsement, by contrast, is preserved in full in his own Commentary on Matthew.
  • He had never heard of the cousins-theory. Lightfoot draws a second, larger conclusion from the passage: it “shows not only that Origen himself favoured the Epiphanian view… but that he was wholly unaware of the Hieronymian, the only alternative which presented itself being the denial of the perpetual virginity.” A father who canvasses the question this fully, yet knows only two options — brethren as Joseph’s earlier sons, or as Mary’s later children — is strong evidence that the third reading, the maternal cousins of “Mary of Clopas,” did not exist before Jerome devised it c. 383. Lightfoot lists the same silence in the two Hilaries and Epiphanius as part of the case that the Hieronymian solution is the latest-born of the three.2
  • Position in the debate. Origen stands fourth in Lightfoot’s roll of the “sons of Joseph by a former wife” authorities — after the Gospel of Peter, the Protevangelium, and Clement, and ahead of Eusebius, Gregory of Nyssa, Epiphanius, and the Eastern churches.2 As Clement’s successor at Alexandria he carries the Alexandrian tradition of the reading into the mid-third century and gives it, for the first time, both a documentary pedigree and a reasoned theological motive.
  • What it does and does not show. Origen’s note establishes that the Epiphanian reading was held, argued, and traced to earlier texts by the most learned biblical scholar of the third-century East. It does not settle the exegetical question of who the brethren were: Origen’s own supports are a tradition he takes from apocryphal gospels and an argument from theological fitness (the “first-fruit of virginity”), not a demonstration from the Gospel text itself — the same limitation that attends the reading in Clement and the infancy apocrypha.
