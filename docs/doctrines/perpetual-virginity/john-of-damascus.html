John of Damascus on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 749 (the timeline slot; his systematic Exact Exposition of the Orthodox Faith was compiled c. 743, near the end of a life spent c. 675/676 – 749 as a monk of Mar Saba near Jerusalem; called the last of the Greek Fathers) · Wikipedia
Summary: By the eighth century the perpetual virginity is settled dogma, and John of Damascus enters it into his summa of the Eastern faith. In the chapter of the Exact Exposition of the Orthodox Faith on the Mother of God he calls her the “ever-virgin” (aeiparthenos) and states the doctrine at its widest reach — virgin in the conception, virgin in the birth, and virgin ever after, “having never at any time up till death consorted with a man.” He rebuts the two objections from Matthew 1:25 exactly as the fathers before him: “first-born” does not imply later-born, and “till” marks a limit without denying what lies beyond it — the very reading of the phrase weighed on this timeline’s “heōs hou” argument. His concern is the virginity itself; he does not take up the identity of the “brethren,” so he cannot be filed under any of the three camps.

Claims & evidence

Virgin in the birth as in the conception

John treats the virginity as unbroken across the whole event of the Nativity — the seal kept intact through the birth, as through the conception:

But just as He who was conceived kept her who conceived still virgin, in like manner also He who was born preserved her virginity intact, only passing through her and keeping her closed. The conception, indeed, was through the sense of hearing, but the birth through the usual path by which children come, although some tell tales of His birth through the side of the Mother of God. For it was not impossible for Him to have come by this gate, without injuring her seal in anyway.

— An Exposition of the Orthodox Faith, Book IV, ch. 14.1 He guards, that is, not only the virginity before the birth (the virginal conception, which no party denies) but the virginity in and after it — the point on which the whole question of later children turns.

“First-born” and “till” do not imply other children (Matthew 1:25)

Having affirmed that she “remains even after the birth still virgin, having never at any time up till death consorted with a man,” John meets the objection raised from the text of Matthew 1:25 — “and knew her not till she had brought forth her first-born Son” — on both of its words:

yet note that he who is first-begotten is first-born even if he is only-begotten. For the word “first-born” means that he was born first but does not at all suggest the birth of others. And the word “till” signifies the limit of the appointed time but does not exclude the time thereafter. For the Lord says, And lo, I am with you always, even unto the end of the world, not meaning thereby that He will be separated from us after the completion of the age. The divine apostle, indeed, says, And so shall we ever be with the Lord, meaning after the general resurrection.

— An Exposition of the Orthodox Faith, Book IV, ch. 14.1 The two-part answer is the standard patristic one, already made by Jerome against Helvidius: that a “first-born” is simply one who opens the womb, whether or not others follow; and that Scripture’s “till” (Greek heōs) states a term without asserting a change after it — illustrated, as Jerome had illustrated it, from the risen Christ’s “lo, I am with you always, even unto the end of the world” (Matthew 28:20), which no one reads as a parting after that day. He closes the chapter with the argument from fitness: she “who had borne God and from experience of the subsequent events had come to know the miracle” could not thereafter “receive the embrace of a man. God forbid!”1

Notes

  • Relation to the three theories. John cannot be filed with the Epiphanians, the Hieronymians, or the Helvidians, because the chapter simply does not raise the brethren-question: he defends the perpetual virginity without saying whose children the “brethren of the Lord” were. What he witnesses is the doctrine in its eighth-century, settled form — and, being a systematic epitome of the received Eastern faith rather than one controversialist’s opinion, his statement carries the weight of the whole tradition behind it.
  • The Matthew 28:20 parallel and the heōs hou objection. John’s handling of “till” is the classic rebuttal that later grammatical critics (notably Eric Svendsen) have pressed on: the counter-examples he and Jerome cite — Matthew 28:20 among them — render the one-word heōs (or heōs + noun), whereas Matthew 1:25 has the two-word heōs hou, whose usage tends (though not without exceptions) to imply a reversal after the term. The distinction, and how far it dents the classic answer John gives, is weighed on the argument page “Does ‘heōs hou’ in Matthew 1:25 imply Mary had later children?” (assessment: weak). John’s value here is as an eighth-century witness that the “till”/“first-born” rebuttal was the standard Eastern reading of the verse long before the modern debate.
  • “Ever-virgin” as a title. John repeatedly styles Mary aeiparthenos (“ever-virgin”) and Theotokos (“Mother of God”) — the fixed liturgical vocabulary of the Greek Church by his day — so that the perpetual virginity is presented not as a thesis to be proved but as a datum of the faith, defended only against the objections a catechumen might raise from the Gospel text.
