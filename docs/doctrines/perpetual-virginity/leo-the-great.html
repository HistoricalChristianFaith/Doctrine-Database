Leo the Great on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 450 (Leo I, “the Great,” c. 400–461, a Roman deacon who became bishop of Rome in 440 and held the see until his death. The most consequential pope of the fifth century, he is best known for the dogmatic Tome (Letter 28, 449) read at the Council of Chalcedon in 451, which fixed the Western formula of the two natures in the one person of Christ; a large body of his Christmas, Epiphany, and Passion sermons survives. The Christmas sermon quoted here belongs to his episcopate and is conventionally placed in the 440s) · Wikipedia
Summary: Preaching at Rome on the Feast of the Nativity, Leo the Great states the perpetual virginity in its full threefold reach — “a Virgin conceived, a Virgin bare, and a Virgin she remained” — and rests it, characteristically, on the Christology: because the One born was God as well as man, His birth kept “the primal virginity of the Mother” intact. His is the confession Augustine had fixed in the Latin memory (virgo concepit, virgo peperit, virgo permansit), carried in the years around Chalcedon (451) into the Christological mainstream of the West. Like Peter Chrysologus, Leporius, Athanasius, and Chromatius, he attests the perpetual virginity itself while leaving the identity of the Lord’s brethren untouched.

Claims & evidence

“A Virgin conceived, a Virgin bare, and a Virgin she remained”

Explaining what he calls the “new character of the birth of Christ,” Leo sets Christ’s origin apart from ordinary human generation: the Holy Spirit came upon Mary and the power of the Most High overshadowed her (Luke 1:35), so that “the origin is different but the nature like: not by intercourse with man but by the power of God was it brought about.” On that ground he states Mary’s virginity through all three moments:

The origin is different but the nature like: not by intercourse with man but by the power of God was it brought about: for a Virgin conceived, a Virgin bare, and a Virgin she remained.

— Leo the Great, Sermon 22 (On the Feast of the Nativity, II), §2.1 The three verbs carry the doctrine at its fullest reach: virginity ante partum (in conceiving), in partu (in bearing), and post partum (remaining so). Leo immediately points the reader away from the biology of the birth and toward its author — “Consider here not the condition of her that bare but the will of Him that was born” — the same move Peter Chrysologus makes when he refuses to liken Mary’s childbearing to that of “other women.”

The virginity grounded in the Christology

Leo does not treat Mary’s intact virginity as an isolated marvel but as a consequence of who her Son is. Because He came “to do away with, not to endure, our pollutions,” it “behoved Him to be born by a new order, who brought to men’s bodies the new gift of unsullied purity”; and so “the uncorrupt nature of Him that was born had to guard the primal virginity of the Mother, and the infused power of the Divine Spirit had to preserve in spotlessness and holiness” her womb.2 The virginity is thus the pattern of the Incarnation itself: the purity of the Son requires and preserves the virginity of the Mother. This is the same instinct that governs Leo’s dogmatic Tome, where Christ is “born of the Holy Spirit and the Virgin Mary” with “the reality of His mother’s virginity” unimpaired — the reading that, carried to Chalcedon in 451, made the ever-virgin birth part of the West’s settled Christological confession.3

What it settles — and what it leaves open

Leo affirms the perpetual virginity — and, with weight on the disputed in partu and post partum virginity — but offers no account of the “brethren of the Lord.” His witness therefore bears directly on the virginity itself while saying nothing to decide between the Epiphanian (sons of Joseph by a former marriage) and Hieronymian (cousins) readings of the brethren. Like Peter Chrysologus, Athanasius, Didymus, Chromatius, and Leporius, he attests that Mary was and remained ever-virgin and leaves the genealogy of the brethren untouched.

Notes

  • The pope of Chalcedon. Leo’s dogmatic authority in the fifth century was immense: his Tome (449) was acclaimed at the Council of Chalcedon (451) with the cry “Peter has spoken through Leo,” and it fixed the Western two-natures Christology within which the ever-virgin birth is stated. His preaching on the Nativity is thus not incidental piety but part of the doctrinal settlement of the mid-fifth century.
  • The threefold formula. “Virgin in conceiving, in bearing, and remaining” is the standard Western triad — virginitas ante partum, in partu, post partum. Augustine’s Christmas sermons had crystallized it as virgo concepit, virgo peperit, virgo permansit (Sermon 186); Leo and his contemporary Peter Chrysologus, preaching in the two chief pulpits of the Latin West, are witnesses to the formula as common Latin confession in the decades around Chalcedon.
  • Text and edition. The English is the translation of Charles Lett Feltoe in the Nicene and Post-Nicene Fathers, 2nd series, vol. 12 (1895). The Latin sermons (Tractatus septem et nonaginta) are critically edited by A. Chavasse in the Corpus Christianorum, Series Latina 138, 138A (Brepols, 1973).
