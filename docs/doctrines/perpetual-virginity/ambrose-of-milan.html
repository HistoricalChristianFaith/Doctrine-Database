Ambrose of Milan on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 392 (On the Instruction of the Virgin and the Perpetual Virginity of Mary, the treatise De Institutione Virginis, addressed to Eusebius of Bologna; Ambrose was bishop of Milan c. 374–397) · Wikipedia
Summary: Ambrose devotes a whole treatise to defending Mary’s perpetual virginity against the deniers of his day, and when he reaches the “brethren of the Lord” he takes the Epiphanian line — the term “brothers” is a wide one, and they “could be from Joseph, not from Mary.” But he pointedly refuses to press the identification or to argue out which relationship is meant: it is enough for his purpose that “brother” runs wider than sons of one mother. J. B. Lightfoot read that reticence as deliberate — Ambrose had heard of Jerome’s newly-minted cousins-theory but would not adopt it, and, valuing a united front against a common enemy, declined to divide the champions of the virginity over details. Lightfoot filed him in the “sons of Joseph by a former wife” column of his conspectus, next after Epiphanius.

Claims & evidence

A whole treatise against the deniers of the perpetual virginity

Ambrose’s De Institutione Virginis is a book of instruction for consecrated virgins, but its long central section (chapters V–VIII) turns aside to refute those who had begun to deny that Mary remained a virgin after Christ’s birth. He treats the denial as a scandal forced into the open:

And yet, even though everyone is called to imitate the holy virginity of Mary, there were some who denied that she remained a virgin. We have long preferred to remain silent about this great sacrilege, but because the matter has been brought into the open in such a way that even the Bishop himself was accused of her fall, we believe it should not be left unpunished.

— On the Instruction of the Virgin and the Perpetual Virginity of Mary, ch. V.1 He then works through the stock objections one by one — Mary called “woman” (John 2:4), “before they came together” (Matthew 1:18), “knew her not till” (Matthew 1:25), Joseph taking his “wife” — and answers each in defence of the ever-virginity, capping the argument with the figure that would become a Marian commonplace: Mary is the “closed gate” of Ezekiel 44:1–3, through which the Lord passed while “the locks of virginity were not opened… it shall not be opened by Joseph.”1

“Brothers” is a wide term — sons of Joseph, not of Mary — but he declines to press it

Reaching the objection from the “brethren of the Lord,” Ambrose neither concedes they were Mary’s sons nor labours to prove whose sons they were. He shows instead that “brother” in Scripture reaches beyond common parentage, and lets the Epiphanian answer stand as the natural one without insisting on it:

Moreover, the Lord himself teaches that brothers may be called the fellowship of the family and the people, when he says: I will tell your name to my brothers; in the midst of the Church I will praise you (Psalm 21:23). Paul also says: I wished myself to be an anathema for the sake of my brothers (Romans 9:3). However, the brothers could be from Joseph, not from Mary. Indeed, if someone were to pursue this further, they would find it. We did not consider it necessary to pursue this, since it is clear that the name of brotherhood is common to many.

— On the Instruction of the Virgin and the Perpetual Virginity of Mary, ch. VI.1 Two things are plain here. First, the reading Ambrose reaches for is the Epiphanian one — the brethren are Joseph’s sons, kin to Jesus through Joseph’s household, no children of Mary. He goes so far as to say that one who pursues the question “will find” this to be so — which J. B. Lightfoot rendered, from the Latin, “he will find this to be the true account.”2 Second, and just as deliberately, he will go no further: it is “of no importance to decide what particular relationship is implied,” and enough for his purpose that “brother” is used in an extended sense. He asserts the virginity uncompromisingly, but leaves the precise standing of the brethren an open door.

Lightfoot’s inference: heard of Jerome’s tract, would not adopt the cousin theory

Ambrose wrote in 392, nine years after Jerome had launched the rival Hieronymian hypothesis (the brethren as maternal cousins, sons of Mary of Clopas). From Ambrose’s careful obscurity Lightfoot drew a threefold inference — that Ambrose “had heard of, though possibly not read, Jerome’s tract”; that had he read it, “he did not feel inclined to abandon the view with which he was familiar” — the Epiphanian — “in favour of the novel hypothesis put forward by Jerome”; and that, “seeing the importance of coöperation against a common enemy, he was anxious not to raise dissensions among the champions of the perpetual virginity by the discussion of details”:

In a passage which is perhaps intentionally obscure he speaks to this effect: ‘The term brothers has a wide application… Doubtless they might be called brothers as sons of Joseph, not of Mary. And if any one will go into the question carefully, he will find this to be the true account. For myself I do not intend to enter upon this question: it is of no importance to decide what particular relationship is implied; it is sufficient for my purpose that the term “brethren” is used in an extended sense.’

— Lightfoot’s paraphrase and reading of the passage.2 So Ambrose belongs with the Epiphanian witnesses, but as a reluctant, irenic one: he holds the view, will not fight over it, and treats the united defence of the virginity itself as the thing that matters. Lightfoot accordingly set him in his conspectus of the “sons of Joseph by a former wife” authorities, in the same column as Gregory of Nyssa and Epiphanius, standing last.2

Notes

  • Relation to the three theories. Ambrose is Epiphanian — the brethren are sons of Joseph, not of Mary — but he holds the reading loosely, declining to argue the identification or to rule out other senses of “brother.” He shows no sign of adopting Jerome’s cousins-theory, and Lightfoot took his refusal to “enter upon this question” as a quiet unwillingness to embrace the novelty. His concern is the perpetual virginity itself, which he defends without reserve; the standing of the brethren he leaves deliberately open.
  • The occasion — the Bonosus affair. The “bishop… accused of her fall” whom Ambrose mentions points to the contemporary controversy over Bonosus of Sardica, who had taught that Mary bore other children to Joseph. A synod (associated with the Council of Capua, 391) referred the case for judgment to the bishops of Macedonia and to Ambrose, whose treatise belongs to the same years and the same dispute — the setting that explains why a book of instruction for virgins pauses to refute the deniers of the virginity at such length.
  • Dating. Lightfoot dates the work to 392; it is commonly placed c. 391–393. It is here slotted at c. 392, after Jerome’s Against Helvidius (c. 383), so that Ambrose’s reticence can be read against the cousins-theory already in circulation.
