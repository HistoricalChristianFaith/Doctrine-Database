The Lateran Council of 649 on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: 649 (a Roman synod of some 105 bishops convened at the Basilica of St John Lateran under Pope Martin I, chiefly to condemn Monothelitism — the doctrine of a single will in Christ — and the imperial documents that had promoted it. Its twenty canons include the third, on the Mother of God, quoted here. Not an ecumenical council, but a papal synod whose faith was afterward received in the East at the Third Council of Constantinople, 680–681) · Wikipedia
Summary: The Western counterpart to Constantinople II (553): where the Eastern council named Mary aeiparthenos in a single clause, the Lateran canon spells the triple virginity out — before, in, and after the birth — and puts denial under anathema. It settles the virginity itself and, like the councils and Fathers around it, leaves the identity of the Lord’s brethren untouched.

Claims & evidence

“Her virginity remaining indestructible even after His birth”

The synod’s third canon confesses Mary ever virgin and states the doctrine through all three moments — conceiving without seed, bearing incorruptibly, and remaining a virgin after the birth — condemning any who will not so confess:

If anyone does not properly and truly confess in accord with the holy Fathers, that the holy Mother of God and ever Virgin and immaculate Mary in the earliest of the ages conceived of the Holy Spirit without seed, namely, God the Word Himself specifically and truly, who was born of God the Father before all ages, and that she incorruptibly bore [Him], her virginity remaining indestructible even after His birth, let him be condemned.

— Lateran Council (649), Canon 3 (Denzinger no. 256; later editions DS 503).1 The canon’s three clauses are the standard Western triad — virginity ante partum (“conceived… without seed”), in partu (“incorruptibly bore”), and post partum (“remaining indestructible even after His birth”) — the same threefold formula Augustine had crystallized as virgo concepit, virgo peperit, virgo permansit and Leo the Great had carried into the Christology of Chalcedon. Here it is not preached but legislated: to deny it is to fall under the council’s anathema, and confessing it a condition of communion with the Roman see.

What it settles — and what it leaves open

The canon confesses the perpetual virginity in its fullest reach, laying weight on the disputed in partu (“incorruptibly bore”) and post partum (“even after His birth”) virginity. It offers no account of the “brethren of the Lord,” so it says nothing to decide between the Epiphanian and Hieronymian readings. Like Constantinople II, Leo the Great, and John of Damascus, it attests the virginity itself and leaves the genealogy of the brethren untouched — and so cannot be filed by camp.

Notes

  • “Immaculate” here is not the later dogma. The canon calls Mary “immaculate” (Greek achrantos, unstained) as a title of her purity; this is not the defined dogma of the Immaculate Conception, which was not proclaimed until 1854 (Pius IX, Ineffabilis Deus). The sole subject of the canon is her virginity.
  • A local synod with more than local weight. The Lateran Council of 649 was not an ecumenical council, but it was presided over and sanctioned by Pope Martin I, who proposed its faith as a term of communion and put its denial under anathema; the Third Council of Constantinople (680–681) afterward received the faith it formulated. Its date — the seventh century, more than a millennium before Trent — is the Western answer to the charge that the perpetual virginity is a “late invention,” weighed on this timeline’s “late invention” argument (assessment: weak).
  • Text and edition. The English is the translation of Roy J. Deferrari in Henry Denzinger, The Sources of Catholic Dogma (Enchiridion Symbolorum), no. 256 (St Louis: B. Herder, 1957); the same canon is numbered DS 503 in the later Denzinger–Schönmetzer editions. The Greek text of the canon, of equal authority with the Latin, has aphthoros (“without corruption”) for the birth.
