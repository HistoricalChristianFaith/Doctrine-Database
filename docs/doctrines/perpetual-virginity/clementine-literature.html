The Clementine Literature on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 220 (the Pseudo-Clementine romance — the Greek Clementine Homilies and the Latin Recognitions, both descended from a lost Jewish-Christian “Basic Writing” of the earlier third century that drew in turn on second-century Ebionite sources: the Kerygmata Petrou, and — underlying Recognitions i.27–71 — the Ascents of James. The prefixed Epistle of Clement to James belongs to the same cycle. The extant Homilies and Rufinus’s Latin Recognitions are fourth-century redactions, but the material is older: J. B. Lightfoot dated the Homilies “not late in the second century” and the Ascents of James “very early”) · Wikipedia
Summary: The Pseudo-Clementine writings are the literature of a Jewish-Christian (Ebionite) Christianity that made James the Lord’s brother its supreme figure — “bishop of bishops” ruling Jerusalem, the touchstone against which all preaching must be measured — and set him apart from, and in some respects above, the Twelve. Most sharply, the Recognitions (following the older Ascents of James) has “James the son of Alphaeus” dispute among the Twelve while “James, the chief of the bishops,” the Lord’s brother, presides over them and receives their report: the two men who bear the name James are held plainly distinct. Like Hegesippus and the Apostolic Constitutions, that distinction bears on the narrow “James not one of the Twelve” sub-question — it tells against the Hieronymian reading, which identifies the Lord’s brother with James the son of Alphaeus, while leaving the Epiphanian and Helvidian readings untouched. The value is only that one distinction: these Ebionite circles denied the virgin birth outright, so the Clementines are in no sense a witness for Mary’s perpetual virginity — their weight here is confined to keeping the two Jameses apart.

Claims & evidence

James the Lord’s brother is “bishop of bishops” who rules Jerusalem

The letter that stands at the head of the Homilies, the Epistle of Clement to James, opens by addressing James not as one apostle among others but as the ruler over all the churches:

Clement to James, the lord, and the bishop of bishops, who rules Jerusalem, the holy church of the Hebrews, and the churches everywhere excellently founded by the providence of God, with the elders and deacons, and the rest of the brethren, peace be always.

— Epistle of Clement to James 1.1 James is styled “the lord… and the bishop of bishops” — not “apostle” — and the whole cycle gives him this marked supremacy over the wider Church, a rank the Twelve do not share.

James, “called the brother of my Lord,” is the standard by which all teaching is tested

In the eleventh Homily, Peter warns the churches to trust no teacher who cannot show that his preaching agrees with James’s — the head of the Jerusalem church made the measure of orthodoxy:

…remember to shun apostle or teacher or prophet who does not first accurately compare his preaching with that of James, who was called the brother of my Lord, and to whom was entrusted to administer the church of the Hebrews in Jerusalem,—and that even though he come to you with witnesses.

— Clementine Homilies xi.35.2 James is “called the brother of my Lord” — the same reputed-brotherhood phrasing Hegesippus used — and stands over “apostle or teacher or prophet” alike, once more set above rather than within the apostolic college. Lightfoot noted that the expression itself “has been variously interpreted as favouring all three hypotheses… and is indecisive in itself”; the weight of the passage is in the supremacy it assigns James, not in the phrase.5

The Recognitions keeps the two Jameses apart — one of the Twelve, one over them

What the Homilies imply the Recognitions states outright. In its account of a seven-day disputation in the Temple — a section widely held to rest on the early Ebionite Ascents of James — the twelve apostles each speak “at the request of James,” and among those twelve disputants is James the son of Alphaeus:

After him James the son of Alphaeus gave an address to the people, with the view of showing that we are not to believe on Jesus on the ground that the prophets foretold concerning Him, but rather that we are to believe the prophets, that they were really prophets…

— Recognitions i.59.3 Presiding over the whole conference, and distinct from that disputant, is another James — the Lord’s brother, whom the Twelve report to and whom the high priest addresses as head of the Church:

…when we twelve apostles… had come together… each one of us, at the request of James, stated briefly, in the hearing of the people, what we had done in every place.

…the chief of the priests asked of James, the chief of the bishops, that the discourse concerning Christ should not be drawn but from the Scriptures.

— Recognitions i.44, i.68.4 The scene therefore holds two men named James in view at once: one is numbered with the Twelve and disputes as their colleague; the other, “the chief of the bishops,” stands over the Twelve and receives their account. The Lord’s brother is not the son of Alphaeus.

What the distinction settles — and what it does not

Lightfoot set the Clementine literature in his catena for exactly this feature. Of the Homilies and the prefixed epistle he observed that James is “styled not Apostle, but Bishop of Bishops, and seems to be distinguished from and in some respects exalted above the Twelve”; and of the Recognitions, resting on the Ascents of James, that “the distinction thus implied in the Homilies is explicitly stated… while James the son of Alphaeus is distinctly mentioned among the Twelve as one of the disputants (i.59).”5 The consequence is the same one-sided result the other early Palestinian and Syrian witnesses give: Jerome’s Hieronymian reading depends on identifying the Lord’s brother James with James the son of Alphaeus, and a text that keeps the two men side by side and apart is a witness against that identification. It decides nothing between the Epiphanian and Helvidian readings, which both leave James outside the Twelve — and, given the Ebionite denial of the virgin birth, the Clementines lend no support to the perpetual virginity itself. On the single point of the two Jameses they line up with Hegesippus and, later, the Apostolic Constitutions against the reading Jerome would introduce toward the end of the fourth century.

Notes

  • The Ascents of James and the dating. The extant Homilies (Greek) and Recognitions (surviving whole only in Rufinus’s Latin) are fourth-century works, but both descend from a common third-century “Basic Writing” that itself incorporated older Jewish-Christian sources. The Temple-disputation of Recognitions i.27–71, from which the two-Jameses distinction comes, is commonly identified with the Ascents of James (Anabathmoi Iakobou), an Ebionite writing Lightfoot called “very early” and modern scholars place in the later second century. So the witness here reflects a second-century Jewish-Christian tradition even though its containers are later.
  • Ebionite provenance — not a virgin-birth witness. These are the writings of Ebionite Christianity, which generally held Jesus to be the natural son of Joseph and Mary and denied the virgin birth. The Clementines are therefore emphatically not proponents of Mary’s perpetual virginity; they are cited here for one thing only — that they keep James the Lord’s brother distinct from James the son of Alphaeus — which tells against the Hieronymian identification regardless of what their authors thought about Mary.
  • Relation to the three theories. Like Hegesippus and the Apostolic Constitutions, the Clementine literature weighs against the Hieronymian cousins-reading without settling the contest between the Epiphanian and Helvidian views. What it establishes is only that, in this early Jewish-Christian tradition, the Lord’s brother James was not one of the Twelve.
