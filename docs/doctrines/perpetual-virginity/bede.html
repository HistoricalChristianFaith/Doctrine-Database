The Venerable Bede on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 735 (the timeline slot; the Northumbrian monk of Wearmouth–Jarrow, c. 672/673 – 735, called “the Venerable” and the father of English history — his Commentary on Luke (In Lucae evangelium expositio), dedicated to Bishop Acca of Hexham, was written earlier in his career, c. 709–716) · Wikipedia
Summary: Commenting on Mary’s reply to Gabriel — “How shall this be, seeing I know not a man?” (Luke 1:34) — Bede reads the question as her reverent disclosure of a settled purpose of virginity: she had “decided to lead a virginal life,” and asks not for a sign, as Zachariah did, but only after the manner of obedience. He is a firsthand Latin proponent of the vow-of-virginity reading of Luke 1:34, transmitting to the medieval West the reading Augustine had made the Latin locus classicus.

Claims & evidence

Mary’s question discloses a settled purpose of virginity (Luke 1:34)

Expounding the Annunciation, Bede takes Mary’s perplexity not as ignorance of how children are conceived but as the reverent unveiling of a resolve already formed — to remain a virgin:

But Mary said to the angel: How will this be, since I do not know a man? She reverently expressed the purpose of her mind, that is, that she had decided to lead a virginal life. Because she was the first among women to devote herself to such great virtue, she rightfully deserved, by unique merit, to excel in blessedness above other women. How, she said, will this be? She did not say: How will I know this; but, How will this be, she said, since I do not know a man. She inquired about the order of obedience to which she should submit, not asking for a sign to believe. For it did not befit the virgin chosen to bear God to exist in doubtful mistrust but in cautious prudence, since man could not easily know the mystery that was hidden in God through the ages. Therefore, because she had read, Behold a virgin shall conceive, and bear a son, but had not read how it could happen, rightfully believing in what she had read, she asked the angel about what she did not find in the prophet (Isaiah VII).

— Commentary on Luke (In Lucae evangelium expositio), Book I, on Luke 1:34.1 The Latin is unmistakable on the point: Propositum suae mentis reverenter exposuit, vitam videlicet virginalem se ducere decrevisse — “She reverently explained the purpose of her mind, namely that she had decided to lead a virginal life.”2 Bede does not argue the perpetual virginity here so much as presuppose it: Mary’s virginity is a propositum, a standing purpose, which her betrothal was never meant to end.

Her question is prudence, not distrust — unlike Zachariah’s

The heart of Bede’s reading is a contrast the fathers drew between the two Annunciation scenes of Luke 1. Zachariah, told he would father the Baptist, asked Whereby shall I know this? (Luke 1:18) and was struck dumb for unbelief; Mary asks How shall this be? Bede insists the difference is decisive — the one demands a sign, the other only inquires after the way it will come to pass:

She did not say: How will I know this; but, How will this be, she said, since I do not know a man. She inquired about the order of obedience to which she should submit, not asking for a sign to believe.

— Commentary on Luke, on Luke 1:34.1 Having read Isaiah’s a virgin shall conceive (Isaiah 7:14) but nowhere read how a virgin could conceive, she “rightfully believing in what she had read, asked the angel about what she did not find in the prophet.” For Bede the question therefore presupposes, rather than doubts, her virginity: it is the puzzle of a woman who does not expect to “know a man,” wondering by what order the promised conception will be worked.

Notes

  • A proponent of the vow-of-virginity reading. Bede’s gloss is a firsthand instance of the argument weighed on this timeline as the vow-of-virginity argument (assessment: contested) — that Mary’s question at Luke 1:34 makes sense only if she had already resolved to remain a virgin. He gives the modest form of the claim (a settled purpose or resolve of virginity, propositum…vitam virginalem se ducere decrevisse), which is the defensible steelman of that argument, rather than the strong juridical form (a formal, already-sworn vow) that the same page finds anachronistic. He stands in the line the argument traces — firsthand in Gregory of Nyssa and Augustine, quoted by Aquinas as the sed contra of the article he devotes to it, and the common opinion of the medieval schools.
  • Relation to the three theories. Like John of Damascus, Bede here defends the virginity itself without taking up the identity of the “brethren of the Lord,” so on this passage he cannot be filed with the Epiphanians, Hieronymians, or Helvidians. What he witnesses is that by the early eighth century the perpetual virginity — and the vow-reading of Luke 1:34 that undergirds it — was settled teaching in the Latin West as in the Greek East.
  • Text and transmission. Bede’s Luke commentary, largely woven from Ambrose, Augustine, Gregory the Great, and Jerome, became a staple of Carolingian and medieval exegesis and fed the Glossa Ordinaria; through it this reading of Luke 1:34 reached the schools that would, from the thirteenth century, make the vow their common opinion.
