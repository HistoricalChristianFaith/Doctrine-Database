Victorinus of Pettau on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 300 (Victorinus, bishop of Poetovio in Pannonia — Pettau, the modern Ptuj — the earliest Latin commentator on Scripture, martyred under Diocletian c. 303–304) · Wikipedia
Summary: Victorinus is an uncertain witness — the one figure in Lightfoot’s catena whom both sides of the later Roman controversy tried to claim. Helvidius quoted him for the view that the brethren were Mary’s own sons; Jerome denied that reading and counterclaimed him for the kinship sense. His own comment on the brethren does not survive, and his two extant works honour Mary’s virginity in the conception while saying nothing of who the brethren were. What he actually held on the disputed point cannot now be recovered.

Claims & evidence

Both Helvidius and Jerome claimed him — the primary record is Jerome’s rebuttal

When Helvidius argued at Rome (c. 383) that the Lord’s brethren were Mary’s later-born children, he closed by producing two older authorities for his side: Tertullian and Victorinus of Pettau. Jerome, replying, could not simply ignore the citation; he conceded Tertullian (only to disqualify him as “not of the Church”) but contested Victorinus outright, insisting the bishop had meant “brother” in the looser sense of kinship:

Feeling himself to be a smatterer, he there produces Tertullian as a witness and quotes the words of Victorinus bishop of Petavium. Of Tertullian I say no more than that he did not belong to the Church. But as regards Victorinus, I assert what has already been proved from the Gospel—that he spoke of the brethren of the Lord not as being sons of Mary, but brethren in the sense I have explained, that is to say, brethren in point of kinship not by nature.

— Jerome, Against Helvidius 19 (§17 in Lightfoot’s citation).1 This much is firm and firsthand: Helvidius enlisted Victorinus for the Helvidian reading, and Jerome, unable to leave the appeal unanswered, denied it and pulled Victorinus onto his own side. What the passage does not give is Victorinus’s actual words — Jerome quotes them (“the words of Victorinus”) but does not reproduce them, and both disputants are plainly reading their own conclusion into a text neither cares to lay out in full.

His own comment on the brethren does not survive — ⚠ unverified

The book Helvidius and Jerome were quarrelling over is lost, and Victorinus’s surviving works never touch the identity of the brethren. Both do reverence Mary’s virginity in the conception — the Commentary on the Apocalypse says the Son “has at this time taken a beginning from the Virgin,” and On the Creation of the World that “the Holy Spirit overflowed the Virgin Mary” — but neither says a word about whether she bore other children, or whose sons the “brethren” were.

…He was, because with the Father He made all things, and has at this time taken a beginning from the Virgin; He is to come, because assuredly He will come to judgment.

— Victorinus, Commentary on the Apocalypse of the Blessed John (on Rev. 1:8).2 Because his statement on the brethren is preserved only at second hand — through the rival reports of Helvidius and Jerome, who read it in opposite senses — his own position on the disputed point remains ⚠ unverified. He affirmed the virginal conception; on the perpetual virginity, and on the brethren, the surviving evidence is silent.

Why the witness is uncertain

That two opponents could each honestly claim the same man is itself the clue to what he wrote. J. B. Lightfoot drew the natural inference:

Victorinus Petavionensis (about 300) was claimed by Helvidius as a witness in his own favour. Jerome denied this and put in a counterclaim. It may perhaps be inferred from this circumstance that Victorinus did little more than repeat the statements of the evangelists respecting the Lord’s brethren.

— J. B. Lightfoot, “The Brethren of the Lord,” §12.3 If Victorinus merely called them “the brethren of the Lord,” naming them as the Gospels do without deciding whose sons they were, then his bare words would fit the Helvidian, Epiphanian, and Hieronymian readings alike — which is exactly why Helvidius and Jerome could both fasten on him. Lightfoot accordingly declines to enlist him for any camp: in his conspectus of the early authorities Victorinus of Pettau stands under the single heading Uncertain.3

Notes

  • Not the later Victorinus. This is Victorinus of Pettau (Poetovio), the martyr-bishop and exegete of c. 300 — not Marius Victorinus the Philosopher (c. 360), the Roman rhetorician and convert whose commentary on Galatians is a clear and separate witness: taking the “but” of Galatians 1:19 as opposition rather than exception, he states flatly that James the Lord’s brother was not one of the Twelve — “Cum autem fratrem dixit, apostolum negavit” (“when he called him a brother, he denied he was an apostle”). Lightfoot files the Philosopher, unlike the bishop, among the definite “James not one of the Twelve” witnesses.4
  • Relation to the three theories. Victorinus decides nothing among them. Unlike Hegesippus, the Clementine literature, and the Apostolic Constitutions — each of which at least tells against the Hieronymian reading — the lost Victorinus is claimed by both a Helvidian and Jerome and confirmable for neither. He is on the timeline as an honest datum of the record, a name both parties wanted and neither could securely hold, not as evidence for any one solution.
  • What Jerome’s move shows. Even taken at face value, Jerome’s counterclaim cuts against his own larger thesis less than it seems: he reads Victorinus for “kinship, not nature,” but that phrase equally suits the Epiphanian step-brother sense, and Jerome nowhere reports that Victorinus identified the brethren as the Virgin’s sister’s sons — the distinctive Hieronymian claim. That Jerome had to argue for his reading of Victorinus “from the Gospel,” rather than quote the bishop saying it, is of a piece with Lightfoot’s finding that the cousins-theory can be shown in no writer before Jerome devised it.
