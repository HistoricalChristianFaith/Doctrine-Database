Basil of Caesarea (Basil the Great) on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 377 (the homily On the Holy Generation of Christ, In sanctam Christi generationem — a Nativity sermon of his episcopate at Caesarea in Cappadocia, 370–379; lifespan c. 330–379) · Wikipedia
Summary: Basil firmly holds Mary’s perpetual virginity — yet he is the timeline’s frankest witness that a father could hold it while conceding it was not a required article of faith. Expounding “he knew her not till she had brought forth” (Matthew 1:25), he grants that the words might be read as Helvidius read them, and says outright that a contrary conclusion “would not have affected the teaching of our religion at all,” since Mary’s virginity was necessary only up to the service of the Incarnation. He nonetheless affirms the ever-virginity, resting it not on dogmatic necessity but on the consensus of the faithful: “since the lovers of Christ do not allow themselves to hear that the Mother of God (Theotokos) ceased at a given moment to be a virgin, we consider their testimony to be sufficient.” As to who the brethren were, Basil does not say in so many words; J. B. Lightfoot files him among the witnesses for the perpetual virginity who leave the brethren-question open, but infers from Basil’s supporting appeal to an apocryphal story — that Zacharias was slain for testifying to Mary’s virginity, a tale close to the Protevangelium — that he took the Epiphanian account which ran through those infancy gospels.

Claims & evidence

He holds the ever-virginity — but calls it no required dogma

Preaching on the Nativity, Basil comes to the hard word of Matthew 1:25 — that Joseph “knew her not till she had brought forth her firstborn son” — and, unusually for a defender of the doctrine, he concedes what the Helvidian could make of it before answering:

For ‘he did not know her’ — it says — ‘until she gave birth to a Son, her firstborn’ (Mt 1:25). But this could make one suppose that Mary, after having offered in all purity her own service in giving birth to the Lord, by virtue of the intervention of the Holy Spirit, did not subsequently refrain from normal conjugal relations. That would not have affected the teaching of our religion at all, because Mary’s virginity was necessary until the service of the Incarnation, and what happened afterward need not be investigated in order to affect the doctrine of the mystery. But since the lovers of Christ do not allow themselves to hear that the Mother of God (Theotokos) ceased at a given moment to be a virgin, we consider their testimony to be sufficient.

— On the Holy Generation of Christ, §5.1 Two things stand out. First, Basil settles the doctrine on the side of the perpetual virginity, and states the ever-virgin conviction plainly: the faithful will not endure to hear that the Theotokos ever ceased to be a virgin. Second, he grounds it on the consensus of the lovers of Christ — the piety and tradition of the Church — rather than on any exegetical necessity, having just allowed that the opposite view “would not have affected the teaching of our religion at all.” His witness is therefore of a distinctive kind: a firm holder of the doctrine who is candid that, on the bare words of Matthew, the question could have gone the other way without touching the mystery of the Incarnation.

Which reading of the brethren? Lightfoot’s inference: Epiphanian

Basil does not, in this homily, state whose sons the “brethren of the Lord” were — so of the three theories he cannot be pinned to one from his own express words, and J. B. Lightfoot files him in his conspectus simply among the witnesses to the “perpetual virginity of Mary” who leave the brethren-question open (a reading compatible with either the Epiphanian or the Hieronymian solution). But Lightfoot draws a further inference from what Basil says next:

Basil the Great († 379), while allowing that the perpetual virginity is not a necessary article of belief, yet adheres to it himself ‘since the lovers of Christ cannot endure to hear that the mother of God ever ceased to be a virgin’… As immediately afterwards he refers, in support of his view, to some apocryphal work which related that Zacharias was slain by the Jews for testifying to the virginity of the mother of Jesus (a story which closely resembles the narrative of his death in the Protevang. §§ 23, 24), it may perhaps be inferred that he accepted that account of the Lord’s brethren which ran through these apocryphal gospels.

— Lightfoot, The Brethren of the Lord, §18.2 The Protevangelium of James is the fountainhead of the Epiphanian reading — it makes Joseph an aged widower whose sons by a former wife are the “brethren” — so Basil’s reliance on the same body of infancy tradition points, in Lightfoot’s judgment, to the Epiphanian solution rather than to Jerome’s later cousins-theory. This last step is Lightfoot’s inference, not Basil’s statement; the Zacharias detail on which it rests is reported by Lightfoot from the homily rather than quoted here from Basil’s own words, and Basil’s brethren-view accordingly remains, on the strict evidence, ⚠ unverified.

What Basil’s testimony settles

Basil adds to the timeline a witness of unusual candor: writing in the East in the 370s, a full generation before Jerome’s controversy at Rome, he holds the perpetual virginity firmly and yet declines to make it turn on the grammar of Matthew 1:25, resting it instead on the mind of the Church. He shows that the doctrine could be affirmed as a matter of received piety even by a father who granted that Scripture alone did not compel it — a datum that cuts both ways in the later debate, telling for the antiquity and strength of the belief while conceding the exegetical looseness of its chief proof-text. And so far as his brethren-view can be read at all, it points (with the earlier Alexandrian and Palestinian witnesses) to the Epiphanian account, not to the cousins-theory Jerome would devise in the 380s.

Notes

  • Which Basil, which work. This is Basil of Caesarea “the Great” (c. 330–379), the Cappadocian bishop and brother of Gregory of Nyssa — not Basil of Seleucia. The homily On the Holy Generation of Christ (Greek In sanctam Christi generationem) is his Nativity sermon; the crucial passage is edited at Patrologia Graeca 31, col. 1468. Its authenticity has occasionally been questioned but is generally accepted.
  • Relation to the three theories. Basil is firmly for the perpetual virginity (against the Helvidian denial), but does not expressly decide between the Epiphanian (sons of Joseph by a former wife) and Hieronymian (maternal cousins) accounts of the brethren. Lightfoot classes him under “perpetual virginity of Mary” generally, while inferring an Epiphanian leaning from his use of the infancy-gospel tradition.
  • “Not a necessary article.” Basil’s concession — that a denial of Mary’s virginity after the birth “would not have affected the teaching of our religion at all” — is his own, and later Mariologists (e.g. Luigi Gambero) read it as making the consensus of the faithful, not exegetical necessity, the ground on which he nonetheless holds the doctrine an indispensable requirement of ecclesial faith.
