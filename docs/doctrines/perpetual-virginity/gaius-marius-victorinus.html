Marius Victorinus the Philosopher on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 360 (Gaius Marius Victorinus, the celebrated African rhetorician who taught at Rome and, in old age, converted to Christianity — his conversion the famous prize Augustine recalls in the Confessions. His commentaries on the Pauline epistles, the earliest Latin exposition of St Paul to survive, were written after his baptism and, it seems, after Julian’s edict of 362 drove Christians from the chairs of rhetoric; J. B. Lightfoot dates his witness on the brethren “about 360.” Not to be confused with the earlier Victorinus of Pettau, the martyr-bishop) · Wikipedia
Summary: Commenting on Galatians 1:19 — “other of the apostles saw I none, save James the Lord’s brother” — Victorinus reads Paul’s “save” (Greek ei mē) not as an exception that folds James into the apostles but as an opposition that shuts him out of them, and states flatly that James the Lord’s brother was not an apostle: “when he called him brother, he denied him to be an apostle.” That places him squarely among the “James not one of the Twelve” witnesses, and so against the Hieronymian reading, which identifies the Lord’s brother with James the son of Alphaeus and folds him into the Twelve. As Lightfoot noted, Victorinus does not himself decide between the Epiphanian and Helvidian readings, which both keep James distinct from the Twelve.

Claims & evidence

Paul’s “save James” denies James is an apostle

Victorinus is the first Latin commentator on Galatians, and he takes the grammar of 1:19 to be deliberate and pointed. Paul writes that on his first visit to Jerusalem he saw none of the other apostles — and then names James, the Lord’s brother, precisely as one outside that number. The reading turns on the little phrase “save” / “except” (nisi, Greek ei mē): Victorinus construes it adversatively — not “no apostle except James (who is therefore an apostle),” but “no apostle; James, however” — so that the sentence excludes James from the apostolic college even as it records that Paul saw him:

Alium autem apostolorum neminem vidi, nisi Jacobum fratrem Domini. Magna doctrina etiam hoc adjunxit magnoque ingenio: primo quod ita dixit, apostolorum neminem alium vidi: nam Jacobum apostolum Symmachiani faciunt quasi duodecimum… Ergo hic Paulus negavit Jacobum apostolum, dicendo alium autem apostolorum neminem vidi quod neminem apostolorum alium vidisse se dixit, nisi Jacobum. Et causa, quare Jacobum, adjecta est fratrem Domini qui frater est habitus secundum carnem. Cum autem fratrem dixit, apostolum negavit. Videndus autem hic fuerat honorifice.

— Marius Victorinus, Commentary on Galatians, Book I, on 1:19.1 In English: “But other of the apostles saw I none, save James the Lord’s brother. With great learning, and with great skill, he added this too: first, in that he said thus, ‘I saw none other of the apostles’ — for the Symmachians make James an apostle, as it were a twelfth… Therefore Paul here denied that James was an apostle, by saying ‘but other of the apostles saw I none,’ in that he said he had seen no other of the apostles save James. And the reason why [he names] James — ‘the brother of the Lord’ — is added, who was reckoned his brother according to the flesh. But when he called him brother, he denied [him to be] an apostle. Yet he had to be visited here out of honour.” The last line supplies the point of the visit: Paul called on James not because James ranked among the apostles but out of the honour owed the Lord’s brother and bishop of Jerusalem.

The foil: the Symmachian (Ebionite) count that makes James “the twelfth”

Victorinus states his reading against a named opponent. The people who do reckon James an apostle, he says, are the Symmachiani — a Jewish-Christian sect (taking their name from the translator Symmachus, and commonly reckoned a branch of the Ebionites) who “make James an apostle, as it were a twelfth,” and who “join to our Lord Jesus Christ the observance of Judaism.” He adds that though they confess Jesus Christ, they teach “that he is Adam himself, and is the universal soul, and other such blasphemies” — the charge of heresy attaching to the Symmachiani and their Christology, not to James.2 The detail is telling. The same Jewish-Christian current that the Clementine literature represents — which exalted James the Lord’s brother, “the bishop of bishops,” over the Twelve — is here glimpsed from the Latin side, in a form that folds James into the apostolic number as a twelfth. Victorinus rejects the elevation outright: for him James is the Lord’s brother in the flesh and no apostle at all.

What the reading settles — and what it leaves open

J. B. Lightfoot set Victorinus the Philosopher in his catena for exactly this feature: he “takes ei mē in Galatians 1:19 as expressing not exception but opposition, and distinctly states that James was not an Apostle: ‘Cum autem fratrem dixit, apostolum negavit.’”3 In Lightfoot’s conspectus of authorities Victorinus stands under the heading “‘Brethren’ in a strict sense — James the Just not one of the Twelve,” beside Hegesippus, the Clementine writings, the Ascents of James, the Apostolic Constitutions, and (probably) Cyril of Jerusalem.3 The consequence is one-sided, as with the others in that group. Keeping James the Lord’s brother outside the Twelve tells against Jerome’s Hieronymian reading, which rests on identifying him with James the son of Alphaeus — but it does not choose between the two readings that agree he was no apostle of the Twelve: the Epiphanian (the brethren are Joseph’s sons by a former wife) and the Helvidian (they are Mary’s own later children). Because Victorinus argues from Paul’s grammar rather than from the family history, his comment neither affirms nor denies the perpetual virginity as such; what it establishes is the narrower point that the Lord’s brother was, for this Latin exegete of c. 360, not one of the Twelve.

Notes

  • Grammar, not genealogy. Victorinus’ interest is the syntax of Galatians 1:19, and modern grammarians still debate the very point he pressed: whether ṁteron…ouk…ei mē makes James one of the apostles or sets him over against them. His verdict — that Paul “denied James to be an apostle” — is thus a fourth-century witness to the adversative reading of the phrase, independent of the family-relationship theories that dominate the rest of the timeline.
  • Two Victorini. This Marius Victorinus, the rhetorician-convert (c. 360), is a distinct person from Victorinus of Pettau (Poetovio, martyred c. 304), the earliest Latin biblical commentator, whom both Helvidius and Jerome tried to claim and whom Lightfoot could only file under “Uncertain.” Lightfoot himself is at pains to keep the two apart (§§12, 16). Here the position is definite: Marius Victorinus reads Galatians 1:19 as denying James’s apostleship.
  • Relation to the three theories. Like Hegesippus, the Clementine literature, and the Apostolic Constitutions, Marius Victorinus weighs against the Hieronymian cousins-reading without settling the contest between the Epiphanian and Helvidian views.
