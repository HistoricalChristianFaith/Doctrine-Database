Theophylact of Ohrid on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 1075 (his Gospel and Pauline commentaries, largely epitomized from Chrysostom, written during his archiepiscopate at Ohrid; Theophylact c. 1050 – after 1107, Byzantine archbishop of Ohrid) · Wikipedia
Summary: Theophylact, a Byzantine exegete writing six centuries after the patristic debate had settled, offers a late hybrid of the two virginity-preserving readings. Commenting on both Matthew 13:55 and Galatians 1:19 he fuses the Epiphanian and Hieronymian views by the Mosaic law of the levirate: Clopas died childless, and his brother Joseph “raised up seed” to him by the widow, so the brethren were Joseph’s natural sons (and thus the Lord’s reputed brothers) and Clopas’s legal sons (and thus the Lord’s cousins) at once — Joseph the natural father, Clopas the legal. Either way they are no children of the Virgin, so the perpetual virginity stands; but the harmonization itself, J. B. Lightfoot notes, won little favour.

Claims & evidence

James both reputed brother and cousin — the levirate fusion (Galatians 1:19)

Expounding Paul’s “James the Lord’s brother” (Galatians 1:19), Theophylact denies a fleshly brotherhood and then explains James’s descent from Clopas by the levirate — Joseph begetting children to his dead brother by the widow:

And indeed, if he had wanted to distinguish him, he would have called him the son of Cleopas. For he was not a brother of the Lord according to the flesh, but was only considered so. How then was he the son of Cleopas, listen: Cleopas and Joseph were brothers; when Cleopas died childless, Joseph raised up seed for him and begot him and his other brothers, and Mary, whom, although she was the daughter of Cleopas, the Gospel calls the sister of the Mother of the Lord, since Joseph maintained toward the Most Holy Virgin the care of a father rather than the affection of a husband.

— Commentary on Galatians, on Galatians 1:19.1 The move is a deliberate splice. From the Hieronymian side he keeps “Mary of Clopas” as the Virgin’s reputed sister and James as a son of Clopas; from the Epiphanian side he keeps Joseph as the man who actually begat the brethren, so that they are the Lord’s brothers only as sharing Joseph’s house and reputed fatherhood. The levirate law (Deuteronomy 25:5–10) is the hinge that lets both be true: the child a dead brother’s heir in law, the living brother’s son in fact. Note too the last clause — Joseph “maintained toward the Most Holy Virgin the care of a father rather than the affection of a husband” — guarding Mary’s virginity in the very sentence that peoples Joseph’s household with children.

The brethren as Joseph’s children by Clopas’s widow (Matthew 13:55)

The same construction appears in his comment on the Nazareth pericope, where the townsfolk name the Lord’s “brethren” and “sisters” (Matthew 13:55–56):

The Lord had brothers and sisters, the children of Joseph which he begat by the wife of his brother Cleopas. For when Cleopas died childless, Joseph took his wife in accordance with the law and had six children by her, four boys and two girls, Mary, who was called the daughter of Cleopas, in accordance with the law, and Salome.

— Commentary on Matthew, on Matthew 13:55.2 Here the mechanism is spelled out plainly: Clopas “died childless,” Joseph “took his wife in accordance with the law” — the levirate marriage — and by her “had six children.” The daughter Mary is “called the daughter of Cleopas, in accordance with the law” (i.e. reckoned to the legal, deceased father) though begotten by Joseph. This is exactly the double parentage J. B. Lightfoot summarized from Theophylact: “James… was the Lord’s reputed brother as the son of Joseph and the Lord’s cousin as the son of Clopas; the one was his natural, and the other his legal father.”3

Notes

  • Relation to the three theories. Theophylact belongs to neither of the ancient camps cleanly: his reading is a fusion of the two that preserve the virginity. With the Epiphanians he makes the brethren Joseph’s children, not Mary’s (so the perpetual virginity is safe on the Epiphanian ground); with the Hieronymians he ties them to Clopas and “Mary of Clopas,” the Virgin’s reputed sister, making them the Lord’s cousins. The levirate is the bridge that spares him choosing. It is the same instinct that, centuries earlier, moved Chrysostom and Theodoret to borrow across the camps — but pressed to a formal harmonization neither of them attempted.
  • Reception — the fusion won little favour. Lightfoot files the theory among the shifts he judged untenable and records its fate: “This novel suggestion however found but little favour, and the Eastern Churches continued to distinguish between James the Lord’s brother and James the son of Alphaeus. The Greek, Syrian, and Coptic Calendars assign a separate day to each.”3 The East, that is, kept the old distinction the levirate scheme would have collapsed.
  • A tension within his own corpus. The harmonization does not sit easily beside the older Epiphanian material Theophylact elsewhere reproduces: his preface to the four Gospels gives the plain “former wife” account — Joseph “had seven children by his previous wife—four sons, and three daughters” — with no levirate at all, and a different count (seven, not the six of the Matthew comment).4 The levirate fusion looks to be his own overlay on an inherited Epiphanian base, not a settled single view.
  • A chronological wrinkle. The scheme presumes Joseph outliving his brother Clopas; yet John 19:25 places “Mary the wife of Clopas” still living at the cross, and Joseph is nowhere in the Gospel ministry, presumed dead. Fitting the levirate marriage into the Gospel chronology is not straightforward — one of the reasons, no doubt, the harmonization gained no lasting foothold.
  • The fixed point. Whatever the mechanism, Theophylact never makes the brethren children of the Virgin. That is the constant across all three ancient readings and this medieval hybrid alike: the perpetual virginity is the datum every theory is built to protect.
