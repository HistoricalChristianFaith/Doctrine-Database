Cyril of Jerusalem on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 350 (Cyril, bishop of Jerusalem from about 350 and one of the great fourth-century catechists; his Catechetical Lectures, delivered to baptismal candidates at Jerusalem during Lent, are usually dated to about 350, near the start of his ministry. He died in 386, the year his source-corpus records as his default anchor; the lectures themselves belong a generation earlier) · Wikipedia
Summary: Cyril nowhere discusses the “brethren of the Lord” as a problem, and states no theory of who they were. But at two points he handles James the Lord’s brother in a way that keeps him apart from the Twelve. Rehearsing the resurrection appearances of 1 Corinthians 15 as a ladder of successive witnesses — Cephas, then the Twelve, then the five hundred, then James, then Paul — he names “James, His own brother, and first Bishop of this diocese” as a step beyond the Twelve (Catech. xiv.21); and elsewhere he couples “the Apostles” and “James the bishop of this Church” as distinct parties (Catech. iv.28). J. B. Lightfoot filed Cyril, with a query, among the witnesses for “James the Just not one of the Twelve” — a reading that tells against the Hieronymian theory (which folds the Lord’s brother into the Twelve as James son of Alphaeus) while leaving the Epiphanian and Helvidian readings, which both keep him distinct, untouched. Because the point is drawn by inference from Cyril’s language rather than stated by him outright, Lightfoot cautioned that “too much stress must not be laid on it,” and his witness on the disputed question remains ⚠ a guarded inference.

Claims & evidence

The resurrection ladder: James named after the Twelve (Catech. xiv.21)

Preaching on the Resurrection in his fourteenth catechesis, Cyril answers the Manichees’ denial that Christ rose in the body by marshalling Paul’s roll of eyewitnesses from 1 Corinthians 15:5–8. He treats the list not as a flat enumeration but as a mounting series — each appearance a further step in the weight of testimony, so that if one witness be doubted there are twelve, and if the twelve, then five hundred:

And He was seen of Cephas, then of the twelve; (for if thou believe not the one witness, thou hast twelve witnesses;) then He was seen of above five hundred brethren at once; (if they disbelieve the twelve, let them admit the five hundred;) after that He was seen of James, His own brother, and first Bishop of this diocese. Seeing then that such a Bishop originally saw Christ Jesus when risen, do not thou, his disciple, disbelieve him. But thou sayest that His brother James was a partial witness; afterwards He was seen also of me Paul, His enemy.

— Cyril of Jerusalem, Catechetical Lectures xiv.21.1 The force for the brethren question lies in the sequence: “the twelve” are one link, “James, His own brother” a later and separate one, exactly as Paul’s Greek sets “then to the twelve… after that to James” in distinct clauses. Cyril calls this James the Lord’s “own brother” (ton idion adelphon) and “first Bishop of this diocese” — the James of Jerusalem, not James son of Alphaeus who stood among the Twelve. As Lightfoot put it, Cyril’s “language implies that each appearance was a step in advance of the testimony afforded by the former… It may be gathered thence that he distinguished this James from the Twelve.”2

“The Apostles, and James the bishop of this Church” (Catech. iv.28)

The same distinction surfaces in the fourth lecture, where Cyril warns against eating things offered to idols and grounds the caution in apostolic precedent — the decree of Acts 15. There he names, as two parties who took earnest care of the matter, “the Apostles” on the one hand and “James the bishop of this Church” on the other:

Guard thy soul safely, lest at any time thou eat of things offered to idols: for concerning meats of this kind, not only I at this time, but ere now Apostles also, and James the bishop of this Church, have had earnest care: and the Apostles and Elders write a Catholic epistle to all the Gentiles, that they should abstain first from things offered to idols…

— Cyril of Jerusalem, Catechetical Lectures iv.28.1 The Greek Lightfoot quotes — kai tois apostolois kai Iakōbō tō tautēs tēs ekklēsias episkopō (“both to the Apostles and to James the bishop of this Church”) — joins the two by a coupling “and… and,” which most naturally sets James alongside the Apostles rather than inside their number. Lightfoot judged that here too “Cyril seems to make the same distinction,” though he added at once that “here again the inference is doubtful,” since James the bishop of Jerusalem might be singled out simply as the local authority whether or not he was reckoned an Apostle.2

What the reading settles — and what it leaves open

J. B. Lightfoot set Cyril of Jerusalem in his conspectus under the heading “‘Brethren’ in a strict sense. James the Just not one of the Twelve,” beside Hegesippus, the Clementine writings, the Ascents of James, the Apostolic Constitutions, and Victorinus the Philosopher — but alone among that group he is marked with a query, “Cyril of Jerusalem (?),” because his witness rests on inference, not statement.2 The consequence, where it holds, is the same one-sided one as with the rest of that group. Keeping James the Lord’s brother outside the Twelve tells against the Hieronymian reading, which rests on identifying him with James son of Alphaeus; but it does not decide between the two readings that agree he was no apostle of the Twelve — the Epiphanian (the brethren are Joseph’s sons by a former wife) and the Helvidian (they are Mary’s own later children). Cyril himself says nothing of whose sons the brethren were, and so his testimony bears only on the narrower point, and only by inference: that for this mid-fourth-century bishop of Jerusalem, James “the Lord’s own brother” seems to have stood distinct from the Twelve.

Notes

  • Inference, not statement. Unlike Marius Victorinus, who argues expressly from Galatians 1:19 that “when he called him brother, he denied him to be an apostle,” Cyril never poses the question. Both his relevant passages are about something else — the evidence for the Resurrection, and abstaining from idol-meats — and the distinction of James from the Twelve falls out of his phrasing. Lightfoot accordingly weighs him with explicit caution: “As this… is only an inference from his language, and not a direct statement of his own, too much stress must not be laid on it.”
  • Dating the lectures. The Catechetical Lectures are commonly assigned to about 350, near the beginning of Cyril’s episcopate; he died in 386. The related Mystagogical Catecheses (whose Cyrilline authorship is disputed, some assigning them to his successor John) are a separate matter and are not in view here.
  • Relation to the three theories. Like Hegesippus, the Clementine literature, Marius Victorinus, and the Apostolic Constitutions, Cyril — so far as the inference holds — weighs against the Hieronymian cousins-reading without settling the contest between the Epiphanian and Helvidian views.
