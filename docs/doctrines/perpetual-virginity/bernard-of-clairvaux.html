Bernard of Clairvaux on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 1153 (the timeline slot; Bernard, 1090/91 – 20 August 1153, the Cistercian abbot of Clairvaux, called Doctor Mellifluus and later a Doctor of the Church — his four homilies In laudibus Virginis Matris, on the Gospel words Missus est Gabriel (“the ‘Missus est’ homilies”), were among his earliest works, written c. 1120–1125) · Wikipedia
Summary: In the fourth of his homilies on the Annunciation, Bernard reads Mary’s reply to Gabriel — “How shall this be done, because I know not man?” (Luke 1:34) — as the question of a woman already bound by a vow of virginity. What sets him apart from a bare vow-proponent is that he dramatizes the very tension the vow creates: put in the mouth of a betrothed woman just told she will bear a son, the question voices her fear that God’s wonder might require her to break the vow, and her resolve to obey either way. He thus answers the standing objection — how could a betrothed woman have vowed virginity? — from inside the vow reading.

Claims & evidence

Mary’s question presupposes a vow to “know no man” (Luke 1:34)

Bernard takes Mary’s perplexity not as doubt of the fact but as inquiry into the manner — and behind the inquiry he sets a vow already sworn. He imagines her interior reasoning at the angel’s word:

She doubts not the fact, but only inquires about the manner of its accomplishment. She says not “Will it be done?” but “How will this be done?” … As if she would say: “Since my Lord knows, and my conscience bears me witness, that His handmaid has made a vow to know no man, by what law shall it please Him to work this wonder? If I must break my vow that I may bring forth such a Son, I rejoice on account of the Son, but I grieve because of my vow. Nevertheless, His will be done. If, however, as a Virgin I may bring forth this Son … then I shall know that He hath had regard to the humility of His handmaid.”

— Homilies in Praise of the Virgin Mother (on Missus est), Homily IV.1 The reasoning only works if Mary is already under a standing obligation of virginity: were she merely a betrothed woman expecting an ordinary marriage, the promise of a son would be no puzzle at all. For Bernard the puzzle is precisely a conflict of goods — the promised Son on one side, the vow on the other — which is intelligible only where the vow is real and prior.

The betrothal objection answered from inside the vow reading

The perennial objection to the vow reading is that a woman betrothed to Joseph could not coherently have vowed never to know a man. Bernard does not evade the difficulty; he makes it the very drama of the scene. His imagined Mary reckons openly with the possibility that the miracle might cost her the vow — “If I must break my vow … I rejoice on account of the Son, but I grieve because of my vow” — and submits regardless: “Nevertheless, His will be done.” The relief of the passage is that the miracle turns out to spare the vow: God works the conception virginally, so that she brings forth the Son as a Virgin and her resolve is honoured, not annulled.1 The tension is thus resolved not by denying the vow but by the virgin birth itself — the divine wonder that lets a vowed virgin become a mother without ceasing to be a virgin.

Notes

  • A proponent of the vow-of-virginity reading. Bernard’s meditation is a firsthand instance of the argument weighed on this timeline as the vow-of-virginity argument (assessment: contested) — that Mary’s question at Luke 1:34 makes sense only if she had already resolved never to “know” a man. He stands in the line the argument traces — firsthand in Gregory of Nyssa and Augustine, echoed by Bede, and quoted by Aquinas (a near-contemporary) as the sed contra of the article he devotes to it.
  • His distinctive contribution. Where earlier witnesses simply assert the vow, Bernard alone stages the objection it raises — that a betrothed woman’s vow of virginity would seem to bar the very motherhood now promised — and answers it dramatically, from within Mary’s own supposed reasoning. His “if I must break my vow…” concedes the difficulty and then dissolves it in the virgin birth. This is the strong, juridical form of the vow (a formal vow already sworn) rather than the modest propositum that the argument page finds the more defensible steelman; on that page the juridical form is judged the weaker, chiefly because there was no institution of vowed celibacy in Second-Temple Judaism, so a betrothed woman’s sworn vow is anachronistic. Bernard’s dramatization is homiletic and devotional, not an argument that the vow was legally possible in first-century Galilee.
  • Relation to the three theories. Like Bede and John of Damascus, Bernard here defends and celebrates the virginity itself without taking up the identity of the “brethren of the Lord,” so on this passage he cannot be filed with the Epiphanians, Hieronymians, or Helvidians. What he witnesses is that by the twelfth century the perpetual virginity — and the vow-reading of Luke 1:34 that undergirds it — was so settled in the Latin West that a preacher could build an extended dramatic meditation on it and expect his monks to take the vow for granted.
  • The homilies. Bernard himself wished these four sermons called De laudibus Virginis Matris (“In Praise of the Virgin Mother”), though they are universally known by their opening lemma, Missus est (“[The angel Gabriel] was sent,” Luke 1:26). Written early in his career, they are the seed-bed of the fervent Marian devotion for which the “mellifluous doctor” became famous.
