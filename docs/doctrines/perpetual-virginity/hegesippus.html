Hegesippus on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 175 (a Palestinian Hebrew Christian who compiled five books of Memoirs — Hypomnēmata — of the apostolic tradition; he travelled to Rome under Anicetus and remained into the episcopate of Eleutherus, c. 174–189, which fixes his literary activity around 165–180. J. B. Lightfoot dated him “about 160”) · Wikipedia
Summary: Hegesippus is the earliest Palestinian writer to collect traditions about the Lord’s kindred, and his testimony is a linchpin of the modern debate over the brethren. He carefully distinguishes two relationships: James and Jude are always styled the Lord’s brother, while Symeon, the son of Clopas who succeeded James as bishop of Jerusalem, is the Lord’s cousin — Clopas being (Hegesippus says) the brother of Joseph. This separation of brother from cousin tells against the Hieronymian reading, which makes the brethren themselves cousins; and by calling Jude one who was “said to be” the Lord’s brother “according to the flesh,” Hegesippus was read by Lightfoot as leaning Epiphanian. His words survive only as quoted by Eusebius, who drew heavily on him for these traditions.

Claims & evidence

Symeon, son of Clopas, is the Lord’s cousin, and Clopas the brother of Joseph

Recounting the succession at Jerusalem after James the Just was martyred, Hegesippus names Symeon — not as another “brother of the Lord,” but as his cousin, the son of Joseph’s brother Clopas. Eusebius quotes him:

And after James the Just had suffered martyrdom, as the Lord had also on the same account, Symeon, the son of the Lord’s uncle, Clopas, was appointed the next bishop. All proposed him as second bishop because he was a cousin of the Lord.

— Hegesippus, in Eusebius, Church History iv.22.4.1 Eusebius makes the same point twice more from Hegesippus. Reporting the succession he writes that Symeon “was a cousin, as they say, of the Saviour. For Hegesippus records that Clopas was a brother of Joseph” (H.E. iii.11);2 and narrating Symeon’s martyrdom he again cites Hegesippus on “Symeon, son of Clopas, an uncle of the Lord” (H.E. iii.32).3 So the cousinship runs through the father’s side: Clopas and Joseph are brothers, and Symeon is therefore the Lord’s true blood-cousin.

James and Jude, by contrast, are always the Lord’s brother

Where Hegesippus speaks of James and Jude, he uses a different word entirely — not cousin but brother. Of James he writes:

James, the brother of the Lord, succeeded to the government of the Church in conjunction with the apostles. He has been called the Just by all from the time of our Saviour to the present day; for there were many that bore the name of James.

— Hegesippus, in Eusebius, Church History ii.23.4.4 And of Jude, describing the grandsons hauled before Domitian, he writes: “Of the family of the Lord there were still living the grandchildren of Jude, who is said to have been the Lord’s brother according to the flesh” (H.E. iii.20).5 The same asymmetry shows in his bishop-list, which opens “the first, then, was James, the so-called brother of the Lord; the second, Symeon” — James the brother, Symeon left unqualified.1 Thus, as Lightfoot observed, “in Hegesippus Symeon is never once called the Lord’s brother, while James is always so designated,” and Jude too, when he appears, is styled the brother.6

The distinction tells against the Hieronymian view — and, Lightfoot argued, favours the Epiphanian

The force of the evidence is this. Jerome’s cousins hypothesis (the Hieronymian view) makes the “brethren of the Lord” the sons of “Mary of Clopas” — that is, cousins — and so identifies the brethren with the very family of Clopas to which Symeon belonged. But Hegesippus, a Palestinian who made it his business to gather these family traditions, does not treat brother and cousin as one relationship: he reserves “brother of the Lord” for James and Jude and calls Symeon son of Clopas the Lord’s cousin. Had he thought the brethren themselves were sons of Clopas, the distinction would collapse. Lightfoot therefore judged the testimony “distinctly opposed to the Hieronymian view.”6

Modern defenders of the cousin-theory (Josef Blinzler, John McHugh) meet this by re-parsing the one Greek word at the end of the appointment-clause. Where Eusebius quotes Hegesippus that Symeon was “put forward… being a cousin of the Lord, the second” (onta anepsion tou kyriou deuteron, H.E. iv.22.4), they take deuteron with anepsion — “a second (i.e. another) cousin of the Lord” — implying that James, just named, was also a cousin; the Loeb translation renders it that way. But the more probable reading takes deuteron with an understood episkopon (“the second [bishop]”): “bishop” is the word named in the same sentence (kathistatai episkopos), the whole passage is about episcopal succession, and Eusebius uses the identical ellipsis at H.E. iii.22 — where he numbers Ignatius “the second bishop of Antioch” and Symeon “the second” of Jerusalem while still calling James “the brother of our Saviour.” On that reading, as Richard Bauckham argues, Hegesippus’ evidence “must be held to count against the Hieronymian view.” The grammar is weighed adversarially on the argument page, “Does Hegesippus tell against the cousin-theory — or can ‘dēuteron’ (HE 4.22.4) mean ‘a second cousin’?” (assessment: sound).8

Between the two remaining readings, Lightfoot took Hegesippus to lean Epiphanian rather than Helvidian, resting on the phrase applied to Jude — one “said to be the Lord’s brother according to the flesh.” The word “said to be” (or “called,” Greek legomenou), he argued, “seems… to point to the Epiphanian rather than the Helvidian view, the brotherhood of these brethren, like the fatherhood of Joseph, being reputed but not real.”6 This last inference is softer than the first and is contested: the qualifier “according to the flesh” can just as naturally be read as affirming a real bodily kinship (the Helvidian point), and “said to be” may reflect only the reputed fatherhood of Joseph without settling how the brethren were related to Mary. The hard result — that Hegesippus keeps brother and cousin distinct, against the Hieronymian identification — does not depend on that reading.

Notes

  • Preserved only through Eusebius. Hegesippus’ five books of Memoirs are lost; every sentence above survives only because Eusebius quoted it in the Church History. The fragments are, however, given as Hegesippus’ own words, and Eusebius names him as his source each time, so they carry the weight of a firsthand second-century Palestinian witness rather than a later report. Both Eusebius and Epiphanius drew their account of the Lord’s brethren largely from him, which (as Lightfoot notes) strengthens the position.6
  • “Never once called the brother.” The editor of the standard English Eusebius, A. C. McGiffert, makes the same observation in his note on H.E. iii.11: “It is noticeable that Hegesippus nowhere calls him [Symeon] the ‘brother of the Lord,’ though he does give James that title in Bk. II. chap. 23.” He adds that there is “no reason for doubting” Hegesippus’ account that Symeon was son of Clopas.7
  • Relation to the three theories. Hegesippus is the earliest historical (as opposed to legendary) witness worked here on the brethren question, and his weight falls against the Hieronymian cousins-theory and, on Lightfoot’s reading, toward the Epiphanian. He does not, of course, prove the perpetual virginity: a real cousin (Symeon) and reputed brothers (James, Jude) are equally compatible with the Epiphanian solution, in which the brethren are Joseph’s sons by a former wife. What he shows is that the earliest Palestinian tradition already distinguished the Lord’s cousin from the Lord’s brethren — a distinction Jerome would later have to overturn.
