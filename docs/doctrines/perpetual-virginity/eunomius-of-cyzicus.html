Eunomius of Cyzicus on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 360 (Eunomius, c. 325–c. 393, was consecrated bishop of Cyzicus about 360 and soon deposed; the “c. 360” anchors his episcopate and the height of his influence, not the report of his Mariology, which reaches us only through a commentary written a generation later) · Wikipedia
Summary: Eunomius of Cyzicus was the ablest and most uncompromising leader of the Anomoeans — the “Neo-Arian” party that held the Son unlike (anomoios) the Father in essence. On the perpetual virginity he is a dissenting witness: he is reported to have taught the plain Helvidian reading — that after Jesus’ birth Joseph “knew” Mary and she bore further children. The report is secondhand and hostile in tenor: it comes from the anonymous, Arian-leaning Incomplete Commentary on Matthew (Opus imperfectum, c. 400), which names the view only to reject it, and nothing on Mary survives from Eunomius’s own hand. His teaching on the point is therefore marked ⚠ unverified. Even so it gives the “against” camp a named fourth-century head in the Greek East, beside the Roman Helvidius and the Arabian Antidicomarianites.

Claims & evidence

A Helvidian denial — Mary bore children to Joseph after Jesus — ⚠ unverified

The attribution rests on a single passage. Expounding “he knew her not until she had given birth to a son” (Matthew 1:25), the author of the Opus imperfectum pauses over the inference some drew from “until,” and traces it to Eunomius:

Therefore he said, “But he knew her not until she had given birth to a son.” He knew who she was after his birth. From this statement, some people, following Eunomius, think that until Mary gave birth Joseph did not have carnal relations with her, but afterwards he knew her and she gave birth to children.

— Incomplete Commentary on Matthew (Opus imperfectum), Homily 1, on Matthew 1:25.1 The position described — Joseph abstained only until the birth, then knew Mary, who bore children — is exactly the Helvidian denial of the perpetual virginity, resting on the same reading of Matthew 1:25’s “until” that Helvidius pressed at Rome about the same time. The commentator reports it in order to refute it: he takes “firstborn” in the sense “whom no sibling followed,” and holds that Joseph, once he knew who Mary was, would not have touched her. So the notice is not a friendly summary but an opponent’s report — and because it is the only witness to Eunomius’ view, and no primary from Eunomius survives, the claim is marked ⚠ unverified.

Who Eunomius was — and why nothing firsthand survives on Mary

Eunomius (c. 325–c. 393), a Cappadocian by birth and pupil and secretary of Aetius, became the systematic theologian of the Anomoeans, the radical Arians who taught that the Son is “unlike” the Father in essence. Consecrated bishop of Cyzicus about 360, he was soon accused and deposed, and spent the rest of his life leading his sect from exile; his followers were known as Eunomians.2 The great Cappadocian fathers answered him at length — Basil’s Against Eunomius and Gregory of Nyssa’s enormous refutation of the same name — but that whole controversy turned on the doctrine of God, the generation of the Son, and the knowability of the divine essence, not on Mary. Of Eunomius’ own writings the Apology survives entire, the Apology for the Apology only in the fragments Gregory quotes, and a later Confession of Faith; none of them treats the virginity of Mary post partum.2 The Helvidian teaching is thus attached to his name only by the Opus imperfectum, a source a generation after his death and unsympathetic to it — enough to log him as a dissenting head, not enough to verify the view in his own words.

Notes

  • Secondhand and hostile attestation. Everything here rests on one passage in a commentary that names the view to reject it. On the wiki’s rule that a claim resting only on a later or unsympathetic report is marked ⚠ unverified, Eunomius’ Mariology is so marked; what is verified is that the Opus imperfectum ascribes the Helvidian reading of Matthew 1:25 to “those following Eunomius.”
  • Where he stands among the readings. Eunomius is a Helvidian witness — the brethren are Mary’s own later-born children, so she was not ever-virgin. He is the counterpart, in the Greek East, to the Roman Helvidius, the Arabian Antidicomarianites, and the Latin Bonosus and Jovinian, against the great Epiphanian and Hieronymian chorus that fills the rest of this timeline. Whether his Arian Christology drove the Mariology — a Son “unlike” the Father needing no perpetually-virgin mother — the sources do not say; the connection is often assumed but is not stated in the one notice we have.
  • The proof-text. The reading turns entirely on Matthew 1:25’s “knew her not until” — weighed, in its ancient and its modern (heōs hou) forms, on the argument page “Does the two-word Greek ‘until’ of Matthew 1:25 imply Mary bore other children?” and its companion “Do ‘till she brought forth’ and ‘firstborn son’ imply Mary bore other children?”
