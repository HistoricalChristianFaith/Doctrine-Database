Thomas Aquinas on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 1270 (the Summa Theologiae, Third Part, q. 28, written c. 1271–1273; the Lectura super Matthaeum reported c. 1269–1270; Thomas Aquinas, 1225 – 1274, Dominican friar and doctor of the Latin schools) · Wikipedia
Summary: Two centuries after Theophylact, the perpetual virginity is no longer contested in the West but formalized into scholastic argument. In the Summa Theologiae Aquinas devotes a whole question (III, q. 28) to Mary’s virginity and, in its third article, condemns the Helvidian denial outright — “without any hesitation we must abhor the error of Helvidius” — and marshals four reasons for it: it derogates from Christ’s perfection as the Only-begotten, insults the Holy Ghost whose “shrine” was the virginal womb, dishonours Mary’s dignity, and imputes gross presumption to Joseph. On the identity of the “brethren” he is firmly Hieronymian: following Jerome he makes them the Lord’s cousins, not Joseph’s sons by a former wife, and holds that Joseph too “remained a virgin” — so his witness preserves the virginity of Mary and of Joseph alike.

Claims & evidence

Four reasons against Helvidius (Summa III, q. 28, a. 3)

The article asks “whether Christ’s Mother remained a virgin after His birth,” and Aquinas answers with a flat repudiation of Helvidius, structured as four arguments — from Christ, from the Holy Ghost, from Mary, and from Joseph:

Without any hesitation we must abhor the error of Helvidius, who dared to assert that Christ’s Mother, after His Birth, was carnally known by Joseph, and bore other children. For, in the first place, this is derogatory to Christ’s perfection: for as He is in His Godhead the Only-Begotten of the Father, being thus His Son in every respect perfect, so it was becoming that He should be the Only-begotten son of His Mother, as being her perfect offspring.

Secondly, this error is an insult to the Holy Ghost, whose “shrine” was the virginal womb… wherein He had formed the flesh of Christ: wherefore it was unbecoming that it should be desecrated by intercourse with man.

Thirdly, this is derogatory to the dignity and holiness of God’s Mother: for thus she would seem to be most ungrateful, were she not content with such a Son; and were she, of her own accord, by carnal intercourse to forfeit that virginity which had been miraculously preserved in her.

Fourthly, it would be tantamount to an imputation of extreme presumption in Joseph, to assume that he attempted to violate her whom by the angel’s revelation he knew to have conceived by the Holy Ghost.

We must therefore simply assert that the Mother of God, as she was a virgin in conceiving Him and a virgin in giving Him birth, so she remained a virgin ever afterwards.

— Summa Theologiae, Third Part, q. 28, a. 3.1 The four heads are the older patristic instincts distilled into an ordered scholastic proof: Christ’s only-begottenness (the argument John of Damascus had drawn from “firstborn”), the sanctity of the womb-as-temple, Mary’s dignity, and Joseph’s reverence — the last two turning the denial into a moral affront rather than merely an exegetical mistake.

The “till” and “firstborn” of Matthew 1:25

Within the same article Aquinas disposes of the two stock proof-texts urged against the doctrine, exactly as Jerome had. To “knew her not till she had brought forth” he answers, with Jerome, that the Scriptural “until” sometimes marks a fixed term and sometimes an indefinite one:

Jerome, however, grants that this is to be understood of knowledge by intercourse; but he observes that “before” or “until” has a twofold sense in Scripture. For sometimes it indicates a fixed time… On the other hand, it sometimes indicates an indefinite time, as in Psalm 122:2: “Our eyes are unto the Lord our God, until He have mercy on us”; from which it is not to be gathered that our eyes are turned from God as soon as His mercy has been obtained… the evangelist says that the Mother of God was not known by her husband until she gave birth, that we may be given to understand that still less did he know her afterwards.

— Summa Theologiae, Third Part, q. 28, a. 3.2 His Commentary on Matthew presses the same point at greater length and sharpens Jerome’s a fortiori against Helvidius (“Elvidius”): “if… a warning in sleep was strong enough to keep him from knowing Mary, how much more the knowledge of the angels and the adoration of the shepherds and the Magi?” — and reads “firstborn” as Jerome did, of “all those whom others did not precede,” not of a first among later siblings.3 This is the reading of the phrase weighed on this timeline’s “heōs hou” argument (assessment: weak) — the same grammatical answer Chrysostom, Chromatius, and John of Damascus gave, now set out as a formal scholastic respondeo.

The brethren as cousins — the Hieronymian reading

On the identity of the “brethren of the Lord,” Aquinas follows Jerome against the Epiphanian “former wife” account, and does so explicitly. Still in q. 28, a. 3 he weighs the two options and adopts the cousins-reading:

Some, as Jerome says on Matthew 12:49–50, “suppose that the brethren of the Lord were Joseph’s sons by another wife. But we understand the brethren of the Lord to be not sons of Joseph, but cousins of the Saviour, the sons of Mary, His Mother’s sister.” For “Scripture speaks of brethren in four senses”… Wherefore the brethren of the Lord are so called, not by birth, as being born of the same mother; but by relationship, as being blood-relations of His. But Joseph, as Jerome says, is rather to be believed to have remained a virgin, “since he is not said to have had another wife,” and “a holy man does not live otherwise than chastely.”

— Summa Theologiae, Third Part, q. 28, a. 3.4 His Commentary on Matthew repeats the judgment on the Nazareth pericope in his own voice: of “his brethren James and Joseph, Simon and Jude,” he writes, “Helvidius understood these to be sons of Mary. But this is false; rather they were his cousins. Or they are called brethren because they were of the kindred of Joseph” — citing Abraham’s calling Lot, his nephew, “brother” (Genesis 13:8) for the loose kinship-sense of the word.5

Notes

  • Camp: Hieronymian. By making the brethren cousins (not step-brothers) and holding that Joseph “remained a virgin” too, Aquinas takes the full Hieronymian position — the reading that preserves the virginity of Mary and of Joseph alike, standard in the Latin West since Jerome and Augustine. He notes the rival Epiphanian “former wife” account fairly (“Some… suppose…”) but sets it aside.
  • Where he fits the timeline. Aquinas is the scholastic West’s representative, the heir of the Latin tradition running from Hilary, Ambrosiaster, and Jerome through the medieval schools. Where Theophylact a century and a half earlier had tried to fuse the two virginity-preserving readings, Aquinas simply chooses one — the Hieronymian — and states it as settled doctrine, no longer a live controversy but a thesis to be defended by ordered argument.
  • The four-reasons form. The argument against Helvidius bears on the perpetual virginity as a whole, not on any one proof-text: it does not turn on the grammar of “until” or on who the brethren were, but on the fittingness (convenientia) of a virginity outlasting the birth, argued from the perfection of Christ, the sanctity of the Spirit’s dwelling, and the honour due to Mary and Joseph. It is a theological, not merely exegetical, defence — the mode in which the medieval West held the doctrine.
