The Antidicomarianites on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 370 (an obscure sect of the Arabian churches in the latter half of the fourth century, active before Epiphanius wrote his pastoral letter against them — incorporated as Panarion (Haereses) 78 into his great catalogue of heresies, itself finished about 377. No writing of the sect survives; the group is known only from Epiphanius, who coined the mocking name — “opponents of Mary” — and from later notices dependent on him) · Wikipedia
Summary: The Antidicomarianites were a fourth-century Arabian sect that held the plain Helvidian reading of the brethren — that after Jesus’ birth Mary bore other children to her husband Joseph, so that she was not ever-virgin. They are the one party, as against individual writers, that the timeline can name for the denial of the perpetual virginity in the East. But they left no book: everything we know of them comes from Epiphanius of Salamis, who wrote against them and gave them their derisive name, so the sect’s own voice survives only in the report of its opponent — and their own words are, accordingly, ⚠ unverified. J. B. Lightfoot files them in his conspectus under the “sons of Joseph and Mary” heading, beside Tertullian and Helvidius; a reaction the other way, in the same region, produced the Collyridians, who paid divine honours to the Virgin.

Claims & evidence

A Helvidian party — Mary bore children to Joseph after Jesus

The sect’s tenet is stated only by the man refuting it. In the letter that became Panarion 78, Epiphanius reports the claim he means to answer — that the Lord’s mother did not remain a virgin but lived as Joseph’s wife after the Nativity:

For I have heard from someone that certain persons are venturing to say that she had marital relations after the Savior’s birth. And I am not surprised. The ignorance of persons who do not know the sacred scriptures well and have not consulted histories, always turns them to one thing after another, and distracts anyone who wants to track down something about the truth out of his own head. To begin with, when the Virgin was entrusted to Joseph — lots having compelled her to take this step — she was not entrusted to him for marriage, since he was a widower. He was called her husband because of the Law, but it plainly follows from the Jewish tradition that the Virgin was not entrusted to him for matrimony.

— Epiphanius, Panarion 78.7.1 (on Matthew 13:55 // Mark 6:3).1 The “certain persons” are the Antidicomarianites, and their position — “she had marital relations after the Savior’s birth” — is exactly the Helvidian denial of the perpetual virginity. Epiphanius’ own answer is the Epiphanian solution the sect took its name from opposing: Joseph was already a widower with children when the Virgin was “entrusted” to him, so that he was her husband only “because of the Law” and the brethren were his sons by the former marriage. Because the sect wrote nothing that survives, this hostile summary is all the evidence there is, and the Antidicomarianites’ teaching in their own words remains ⚠ unverified.

Known only through Epiphanius — and the Collyridian reaction

J. B. Lightfoot’s notice gathers what little is recoverable, and pairs the sect with its mirror-image opposite in the same Arabian churches:

The Antidicomarianites, an obscure Arabian sect in the latter half of the fourth century, maintained that the Lord’s mother bore children to her husband Joseph. These opinions seem to have produced a reaction, or to have been themselves reactionary, for we read about the same time of a sect called Collyridians, likewise in Arabia, who going to the opposite extreme paid divine honours to the Virgin.

— J. B. Lightfoot, “The Brethren of the Lord,” §20.2 Epiphanius came forward against them, Lightfoot adds, “not very long before Jerome wrote in defence of the perpetual virginity… against the Helvidians at Rome,” denouncing them in the “elaborate pastoral letter” he later folded into the Panarion; and it is telling that, discussing the question at length there, Epiphanius “never once alludes to the Hieronymian account,” the cousins-theory Jerome had not yet devised.2

Where the sect stands among the three theories

In the conspectus that closes his dissertation, Lightfoot sorts every witness under the reading it supports, and sets the Antidicomarianites with the deniers of the perpetual virginity — the Helvidian column:

A. Sons of Joseph and Mary: Tertullian; Helvidius; Bonosus; Jovinianus (?); Antidicomarianites.

— Lightfoot, conspectus of authorities.3 Augustine, cataloguing heresies a generation later, treated the Antidicomarianites (he spells it Antidicomaritae) as simply the same party as Jerome’s Helvidians: “They held the same tenets, it is true,” Lightfoot allows — but he cautions that “there seems to have been otherwise no connexion between the two,” and that “considerations of time and place alike resist this identification.” The Roman Helvidius and the Arabian sect reached the same conclusion independently; they were not one movement.3

Notes

  • Hostile, second-hand attestation. The sect produced no surviving text; every particular — its region, its date, even its name — comes from Epiphanius, who wrote to refute it. The very label “Antidicomarianites” (“opponents of Mary”) is, as Lightfoot notes, one of the “terms of ridicule invented by their enemies.” On the wiki’s rule that a claim resting only on a later or hostile report is marked ⚠ unverified, the sect’s teaching in its own voice is so marked; what is verified is that Epiphanius reports and combats exactly this Helvidian position.
  • Relation to the three theories. The Antidicomarianites are a Helvidian witness — they make the brethren sons of Joseph and Mary and so deny the perpetual virginity outright. They are the counterpart, on the timeline, to the great Epiphanian chorus that runs through Origen, Eusebius, and Hilary: the same “brother = later child of Mary” reading Tertullian is reckoned to have held. Epiphanius’ refutation of them is one of the fullest statements of the Epiphanian solution in the fourth century.
  • Not the Collyridians. The same passage names a contrary Arabian sect, the Collyridians (Panarion 79), women who offered cakes (kollyrides) to Mary as to a goddess. Epiphanius attacks both — the one for degrading the Virgin, the other for deifying her — steering between the two extremes; the Collyridians bear on the honours paid to Mary, not on the brethren question, and are noted here only because Lightfoot pairs the two as reaction and counter-reaction.
