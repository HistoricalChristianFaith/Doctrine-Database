Ephraem the Syrian on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 363 (Ephraem — Ephrem the Syrian, c. 306–373 — deacon, hymnographer, and biblical commentator, the foremost of the Syriac fathers; a native of Nisibis, he removed to Edessa after the city was ceded to Persia in 363 and there produced his major exegetical work, including the Commentary on the Acts of the Apostles drawn on here. The commentary cannot be dated closely within his Edessene decade [363–373]; it is anchored to c. 363 for the timeline) · Wikipedia
Summary: Ephraem holds the Epiphanian account of the brethren — they are sons of Joseph by a former marriage, no children of Mary — and so witnesses the perpetual virginity by the stepbrother reading. What sets him apart from the timeline’s other Epiphanians is a second move: in his Commentary on Acts (on the apostle-roll of Acts 1:13) he folds two of the brethren into the Twelve, identifying the apostle “Judas of James” with Jude the Lord’s brother (author of the Epistle of Jude, and Matthew and Mark’s Lebbaeus/Thaddaeus) and the apostle Simon the Zealot with Simon the Lord’s brother — while keeping both distinct from the apostle Judas Thomas. He thus supplies a genuine fourth-century Syrian witness that cuts against the “brethren distinct from the Twelve” corollary maintained by Hegesippus, the Clementines, Marius Victorinus, Cyril of Jerusalem, and the Apostolic Constitutions — though on the Epiphanian basis (Joseph’s sons), not the Hieronymian (cousins), and only for Jude and Simon, not James.

Claims & evidence

The brethren are Joseph’s sons and brothers of the Lord (Acts 1:13)

Ephraem’s Commentary on the Acts of the Apostles does not survive in the original Syriac; it is preserved in an Armenian version, from which J. Rendel Harris printed extracts (with an English rendering) in his 1894 Four Lectures on the Western Text, and which F. C. Conybeare later translated in full into Latin for the Beginnings of Christianity. Commenting on the list of the Twelve gathered in the upper room (Acts 1:13), Ephraem stops on Simon and Jude and identifies each as a son of Joseph and a brother of the Lord:

But Simeon (Shmawon) the Zealot is by Matthew and Mark called Simon the Cananaean. Perhaps in the Hebrew tongue he is called Zealot. And it is averred by many that he is son of Joseph father of the Lord, and brother of the Lord. Moreover Judas (brother) of Jacob, was brother of the same Simon and son of Joseph, who also was brother of the Lord. This one wrote the Catholic epistle which in his name is called the epistle of Judas, in which at the beginning out of humility instead of calling himself brother of the Lord, he writes brother of Jacob. And hence it is clear that he is the same whom Matthew and Mark call Lebaeus and Thadaeus, so that they and Luke do not respectively name different persons, but only one and the same person by different names.

— Ephraem the Syrian, Commentary on Acts, on Acts 1:13 (Armenian), trans. J. Rendel Harris, Four Lectures on the Western Text of the New Testament (1894), pp. 37–38.1 Two things stand together in this passage. First, the brethren are “son of Joseph father of the Lord, and brother of the Lord” — the Epiphanian account, in which “brother of the Lord” rests on Joseph’s (reputed) fatherhood and implies no blood-tie to Mary, so that the perpetual virginity stands. Ephraem never suggests these are Mary’s own later children; and the reasoning he gives for Jude’s self-styling — that Jude, out of humility, wrote “brother of James” rather than claim the nearer title “brother of the Lord” — is the very argument Clement of Alexandria had used to the same Epiphanian end. Second, and distinctively, the two brethren are here apostles: Simon the Zealot and “Judas of James” are both members of the Twelve, and Ephraem takes the trouble to harmonize the apostle-lists so that Luke’s “Judas of James,” Matthew’s Lebbaeus, and Mark’s Thaddaeus are “one and the same person.”

“Judas of James” read as Jude the brother of James — and kept distinct from Judas Thomas

Ephraem’s harmonization turns on reading the ambiguous apostolic name “Judas of James” (Ioudas Iakobou, Luke 6:16; Acts 1:13) as “Judas the brother of James” — the self-designation of the author of the Epistle of Jude (Jude 1) — rather than “Judas the son of James.” Having made that identification, he continues past the passage above to distinguish this apostle from a different Thaddaeus (one of the Seventy, associated with the Abgar legend) and to keep him separate again from the apostle Judas Thomas, whom the East Syrian church cherished under that name:

And no wonder if in Hebrew there was a plenty of double names and multiple names, whence the ambiguity in question of the Evangelists as to Thadaeus and Judas is one of name only, not of persons. For of the first set chosen by Christ not one perished, but only Judas the traitor. It is certain then that the other Thadaeus who was with Abgar was of the Seventy… For Thadaeus, one of the Seventy, died in Armenia in the region Artazon; but Judas of Jacob who in Matthew and Mark is Thadaeus, one of the Twelve, died in Ormi in Armenia. Thus the agreement of the Evangelists as to the names of the Apostles is confirmed.

— Ephraem the Syrian, Commentary on Acts, on Acts 1:13–17 (continuing).1 Richard Bauckham summarizes the whole: Ephraem here “identifies the apostle Judas of James (understood as Judas the brother of James) with Judas the Lord’s brother, but neither with Thomas… He also identifies the apostle Simon the Zealot with Simon the Lord’s brother.”2 The last point matters for a separate reading weighed elsewhere on this doctrine: the modern claim that Jude the Lord’s brother was the apostle Thomas, Jesus’ physical twin. The tradition that most treasured the name “Judas Thomas” — Ephraem’s own — does not read Judas Thomas as one of the brethren at all; Ephraem places Jude the Lord’s brother among the Twelve as “Judas of James”/Thaddaeus and keeps Judas Thomas a different man, so the name and the twin-identification travel separately (see the argument page “Was Jude the apostle Thomas, Jesus’ twin?”).

What Ephraem’s testimony settles — and where it complicates the timeline

On the doctrine itself, Ephraem is a firm Epiphanian witness: the brethren are Joseph’s sons, Mary bore only Jesus, and the perpetual virginity is untouched. But his placing of Jude and Simon inside the Twelve puts him at odds with the sub-tradition, well represented on this timeline, that the Lord’s brethren stood apart from the Twelve — the reading urged from Hegesippus, the Clementine literature, Marius Victorinus, Cyril of Jerusalem, and the Apostolic Constitutions, and the corollary of the appearance-to-James argument. It is worth being exact about how far the tension runs:

  • It is a real counter-datum, honestly weighed. A respected fourth-century Syrian father did read two of the four named brethren as apostles of the Twelve. That the “brethren distinct from the Twelve” reading was not universal in the fourth century is a fact the timeline should register rather than smooth over.
  • But it is narrower than the Hieronymian folding. Ephraem does not fold in James the Lord’s brother — the figure the “James the Just not one of the Twelve” witnesses are concerned to keep separate, and the one the Hieronymian reading must identify with James son of Alphaeus. His Jude and Simon are still “brothers of the Lord” and sons of Joseph, not cousins; he is an Epiphanian who happens to reckon two of the stepbrothers among the apostles, not a proto-Hieronymian.
  • And its load-bearing move is the less-probable one. The identification rests on reading “Judas of James” as “brother” rather than “son” of James — the reading J. B. Lightfoot judged the less natural (the earliest versions, the Peshito Syriac and the Thebaic, supply “son”), and one that must then place the apostle so named — called Lebbaeus, Thaddaeus, “Judas of James,” and “Judas not Iscariot,” but never “the Lord’s brother” — against the grain of the Gospels, where the brethren are set apart from and at points opposed to the Twelve (John 7:5; Acts 1:14). Ephraem’s antiquity shows the identification is not merely a “later graft” of Jerome’s completers, but its being ancient does not make it the better reading of the ellipsis. (The philology is weighed on the argument page “Do the identifications that complete Jerome’s cousin-theory hold?”)

So Ephraem complicates the timeline’s “brethren apart from the Twelve” strand without overturning it: he confirms the Epiphanian core the pro-virginity case rests on, and he adds a dissenting fourth-century voice on the narrower apostleship question that is worth weighing, but that dissent turns on the weaker of the two readings of “Judas of James” and does not reach James at all.

Notes

  • Text and transmission. The Syriac original of Ephraem’s Acts commentary is lost. It survives in an Armenian translation (edited by N. Akinian, Vienna, 1921); Harris’s 1894 Four Lectures printed excerpts in English in an appendix devoted to recovering Ephraem’s Acts text, and F. C. Conybeare gave a full Latin translation in The Beginnings of Christianity, Part I, vol. III (1926), pp. 373–453. The quotations here follow Harris’s English of the Armenian.
  • “Averred by many.” Ephraem introduces the Simon identification with “it is averred by many,” reporting a current view rather than coining one, and states the Jude identification in his own voice as its consequence (“Moreover Judas… was brother of the same Simon… brother of the Lord”). Both rest on the Epiphanian premise that the brethren are Joseph’s sons.
  • Relation to the three theories. Ephraem belongs with the “sons of Joseph by a former wife” authorities (Clement, Origen, Eusebius, Hilary, Epiphanius) on the doctrine, but stands with none of them on the apostleship of the brethren: uniquely among the Epiphanians here he reckons Jude and Simon members of the Twelve. J. B. Lightfoot, whose 1865 conspectus organizes the other witnesses on this timeline, did not use Ephraem’s Acts commentary (Harris first put the Armenian text within reach in 1894).
