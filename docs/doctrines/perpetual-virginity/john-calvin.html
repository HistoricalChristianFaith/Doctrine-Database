John Calvin on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 1562 (Sermon on Matthew 1:22–25, pub. 1562; treated also in the Harmony of the Evangelists; John Calvin, 1509 – 1564, the Genevan Reformer) · Wikipedia
Summary: A second magisterial Reformer for the doctrine, and a more cautious one than Luther. Expounding Matthew 1:25 — both in his Harmony of the Evangelists and in a Geneva sermon on Matthew 1:22–25 — Calvin does not so much demonstrate the perpetual virginity as deny that the text decides the question against it. The two stock Helvidian inferences — that “first-born” implies later-born children and that Joseph “knew her not till” she gave birth implies he knew her after — he rules groundless: the “first-born” marks only that Jesus was born of a virgin, and the “till” is “limited to that very time,” the evangelist reporting nothing whatever of what followed. From the pulpit he is sharper, calling the suggestion that Mary “had other children… folly.” His concern in these Matthean texts is the inference from the verses, not the identity of the “brethren”; but his commentary on John 7:3 glosses the Lord’s “brethren” as reaching to cousins and kin, inclining him — more cautiously than Luther — toward the cousin (Hieronymian) reading. The striking point remains that yet another leading Reformer held Mary ever-virgin.

Claims & evidence

“No just and well-grounded inference” — the Harmony on Matthew 1:25

In the Harmony of the Evangelists Calvin names Helvidius’ construction of the verse and sets it aside — not by proving the perpetual virginity from the text, but by denying that the text speaks to the question at all. “First-born” establishes the virgin birth and nothing more; the “till” fixes only the time before the birth:

And knew her not. This passage afforded the pretext for great disturbances, which were introduced into the Church, at a former period, by Helvidius. The inference he drew from it was, that Mary remained a virgin no longer than till her first birth, and that afterwards she had other children by her husband. Jerome, on the other hand, earnestly and copiously defended Mary’s perpetual virginity. Let us rest satisfied with this, that no just and well-grounded inference can be drawn from these words of the Evangelist, as to what took place after the birth of Christ. He is called first-born; but it is for the sole purpose of informing us that he was born of a virgin. It is said that Joseph knew her not till she had brought forth her first-born son: but this is limited to that very time. What took place afterwards, the historian does not inform us… Certainly, no man will ever raise a question on this subject, except from curiosity; and no man will obstinately keep up the argument, except from an extreme fondness for disputation.

— Harmony of the Evangelists, on Matthew 1:25.1 The nuance is worth marking: Calvin does not, in these words, assert the perpetual virginity as Aquinas or Jerome do. He says the Evangelist leaves the later question open and reproves those who force it either way from this verse — siding, in effect, against the Helvidian who reads later children out of the text, while declining to read the perpetual virginity into it.

“What folly this is!” — the 1562 sermon on Matthew 1:22–25

Preaching on the same passage, Calvin is blunter than the commentary. He rebukes those who take Matthew 1:25 to mean Mary bore other children, and again explains “first-born” as a term of precedence that implies no second child:

There have been certain folk who have wished to suggest… that the Virgin Mary had other children than the Son of God, and that Joseph then dwelt with her later; but what folly this is! For the gospel writer did not wish to record what happened afterwards; he simply wished to make clear Joseph’s obedience… And besides this Our Lord Jesus Christ is called the first-born. This is not because there was a second or a third, but because the gospel writer is paying regard to the precedence. Scripture speaks thus of naming the first-born whether or not there was any question of the second.

— Sermon on Matthew 1:22–25 (1562).2 The two arguments are the ones the Fathers deployed and the ones weighed on this timeline’s “heōs hou” argument (assessment: weak): that the Scriptural “till” states a terminus without implying a change after it, and that “first-born” simply denotes the opening of the womb, not the first of several. (Calvin’s fuller case for the neutral “until” — drawn from Matthew 18:34 and 2 Peter 1:19 — is treated on that argument page, where he is cited among those who read the two-word heōs hou non-terminatively.)

“Unfounded and altogether absurd” — the Harmony on Luke 1:34 (against the vow)

The same caution cuts the other way when Calvin turns to Luke 1:34 — Mary’s “How shall this be, seeing I know not a man?” Where the medieval doctors read her question as the trace of a vow of perpetual virginity (the reading stated firsthand by Augustine and Gregory of Nyssa and adopted by Aquinas), Calvin rejects the inference outright — not because he doubts the virginity, but because a standing vow of continence would make the betrothal itself an act of bad faith and rests on an institution he says did not exist:

The conjecture which some have drawn from these words, that she had formed a vow of perpetual virginity, is unfounded and altogether absurd. She would, in that case, have committed treachery by allowing herself to be united to a husband, and would have poured contempt on the holy covenant of marriage… Besides, it is an idle and unfounded supposition that a monastic life existed among the Jews.

He supplies his own account of why Mary asks at all — not a hidden vow but sheer wonder at what has been announced:

the greatness or rather majesty of the subject made so powerful an impression on the virgin, that all her senses were bound and locked up in astonishment… this question was not so contrary to faith, because it arose rather from admiration than from distrust.

— Harmony of the Evangelists, on Luke 1:34.3 The tension is only apparent: Calvin holds Mary ever-virgin (above) yet denies that the fact can be proved from Luke 1:34 or that any vow lies behind her words. He is thus the sharpest early-modern opponent of the vow reading — and, as one who grants the doctrine while rejecting this route to it, its most pointed witness. His objection is weighed on this timeline’s vow-of-virginity argument (assessment: contested), where his historical point — that Second-Temple Judaism knew no institution of vowed lifelong celibacy for women — is judged to bite the strong, juridical form of the claim while leaving the modest form (a settled resolve of virginity) standing.

“Brethren” = cousins and kin — the commentary on John 7:3

On one point Calvin does touch the brethren-question his Matthean expositions leave aside. Commenting on “his brethren” at John 7:3, he takes the word in its extended Hebrew sense:

Under the word brethren the Hebrews include all cousins and other relations, whatever may be the degree of affinity.

— Commentary on the Gospel of John, on John 7:3.4 The note endorses precisely the lexical premise of the cousin-meaning of adelphos (assessment: weak) — that “brother” in biblical usage ranges over cousins and kin — and, applied as it is to the Lord’s own “brethren,” it inclines Calvin toward the Hieronymian reading. He states it as a bare gloss, not the outright verdict of Luther’s “‘brothers’ really mean ‘cousins’”; but the lean is real, and it is more than his Matthean texts commit him to.

Notes

  • Camp: leans Hieronymian. Calvin’s Matthean texts defend the virginity itself — against the Helvidian inference — without saying who the “brethren of the Lord” were. His commentary on John 7:3, however, glosses the Lord’s “brethren” as including “all cousins and other relations,” which inclines him to the cousin (Hieronymian) reading rather than the Epiphanian (Joseph’s sons by a former wife) — though more cautiously than Luther, who commits to it outright.
  • The cautious stance. Calvin is more guarded than Luther or Aquinas. Where Luther calls the Helvidian reading “babble… without justification” and Aquinas “abhor[s] the error of Helvidius,” Calvin’s hinge is that the text does not decide the question — “no just and well-grounded inference can be drawn… as to what took place after the birth of Christ.” He treats a settled dogmatic answer as a matter of “curiosity” and “disputation,” even while, from the pulpit, dismissing the contrary suggestion as “folly.” Read on their own terms his words establish that he rejected the Helvidian denial and personally held Mary ever-virgin, without resting that belief on Matthew 1:25.
  • A Reformer for the doctrine. Calvin joins Luther and Huldrych Zwingli among the magisterial Reformers who retained the perpetual virginity — further evidence that the belief was not a Roman peculiarity the Reformation discarded but one its founders largely kept.
