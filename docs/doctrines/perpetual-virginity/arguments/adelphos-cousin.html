Does “brother” (adelphos) mean “cousin” in “the brethren of the Lord”?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — the Greek adelphos can bear an extended kinship sense, but that mere possibility gives the cousin-reading no positive weight: cousins are nowhere else habitually and exclusively called someone’s “brothers” by third parties, and the precise word for cousin (anepsios) both existed and was used of the actual cousin in this very family · advanced by Jerome

The claim

In Holy Scripture there are four kinds of brethren—by nature, race, kindred, love… Certainly Lot was not Abraham’s brother, but the son of Abraham’s brother Aram.

— Jerome, Against Helvidius §16 (citing Genesis 13:8; he adds Jacob called “brother” of his uncle Laban, Genesis 29:15).1 The Hieronymian reading of the “brethren of the Lord” (Matthew 13:55; Mark 6:3; John 7:5; Acts 1:14; 1 Corinthians 9:5; Galatians 1:19) stands or falls on this semantics: the brethren can be maternal cousins, sons of “Mary of Clopas,” only if the Greek word rendered “brother” (adelphos) will bear the sense “kinsman” when others apply it to them. The narrow question weighed here is not who the brethren were, but whether the word alone can carry the cousin-reading.

Who advances it

Jerome put the argument into circulation in c. 383 (Against Helvidius), laying out the “four kinds of brethren” (in his Latin, natura, gente, cognatione, affectu) and the Old-Testament parallels of Abraham and Lot, Laban and Jacob, to license “brother” = “cousin”; the four-sense scheme became the standard machinery of the Hieronymian reading ever after. Modern defenders press the same lexical point, and even a critic of the cousin-theory such as Richard Bauckham — who himself holds the Epiphanian view — concedes the underlying semantics: adelphos “could sometimes be used more loosely of other close kinship relationships.”2 The reading also drew the magisterial Reformers: Martin Luther, in his Sermons on the Gospel of St. John (1537–39), came down for it outright — “‘brothers’ really mean ‘cousins’ here, for Holy Writ and the Jews always call cousins brothers” — and John Calvin, glossing “his brethren” at John 7:3, endorsed the same lexical breadth: “Under the word brethren the Hebrews include all cousins and other relations, whatever may be the degree of affinity.”3

The evidence for

The lexical premise is genuinely grounded. Behind the Greek of the Gospels lies a Semitic idiom in which one word (Hebrew ’āh, Aramaic ’ahā) covers a wide arc of kinship — brother, half-brother, nephew, kinsman — and the Greek Old Testament carries that breadth into adelphos. When Abraham says to his nephew Lot, “Let there be no strife… for we are brethren” (Genesis 13:8), and again when Lot the nephew is called Abraham’s “brother” at his rescue (Genesis 14:14, 16), the Septuagint writes adelphos; so too when Laban addresses his sister’s son Jacob, “Because you are my brother, should you therefore serve me for nought?” (Genesis 29:15).1 The word demonstrably can, in biblical Greek, denote a nephew or collateral kinsman rather than a full sibling. Bauckham’s concession that “there is good evidence that it could sometimes be used more loosely of other close kinship relationships” states the point from the side least friendly to the cousin-theory.2 The range runs wider still, reaching past blood-kin altogether to covenant partners and friends: David laments the slain Jonathan, “I am distressed for thee, my brother Jonathan” (2 Samuel 1:26); Hiram of Tyre answers his treaty-ally Solomon, “What cities are these which thou hast given me, my brother?” (1 Kings 9:13); and Amos arraigns Tyre for forgetting “the brotherly covenant” with Edom (Amos 1:9), where the Septuagint’s “covenant of brethren” (diathēkē adelphōn) names a treaty between whole nations.4 On the lexicon alone, then, the cousin-reading is not excluded: “brother of the Lord” need not mean a uterine son of Mary.

Adversarial assessment

What holds. That adelphos has an extended kinship range in the Greek Old Testament is not in dispute; the objection to the cousin-reading cannot be that the word is incapable of the sense. The Hieronymian reading is not refuted by the bare meaning of the word, and Jerome’s Lot and Laban parallels are real data.

What is overstated or imprecise. Possibility is not probability, and the argument quietly slides from “adelphos can denote a kinsman” to “adelphos does denote cousins here.” The extended instances do not match the pattern the cousin-reading needs. Every one of them is a single, already-identified relationship, named loosely — by one party in direct address (Abraham to Lot, Laban to Jacob, Hiram to Solomon), in an elegy (David over Jonathan), or as a settled covenant-term (the treaty of Tyre and Edom) — echoing a Hebrew idiom, not a case of a whole group being denominated someone’s “brothers” repeatedly, by several independent narrators and bystanders, without any qualification, one of them even singled out by an apostle as “James the Lord’s brother” (Galatians 1:19). That is exactly J. B. Lightfoot’s objection: “it is scarcely conceivable that the cousins of any one should be commonly and indeed exclusively styled his ‘brothers’ by indifferent persons; still less, that one cousin in particular should be singled out and described in this loose way, ‘James the Lord’s brother.’”5 Two further facts cut the same way. First, the precise word for cousin was available and in use: anepsios stands in the New Testament for Mark “the cousin of Barnabas” (Colossians 4:10), and in the Greek Old Testament for the daughters of Zelophehad marrying “their father’s brothers’ sons” (Numbers 36:11) — so a writer who meant cousin had the word to hand.6 Second, and decisively, the earliest Palestinian tradition distinguishes the two words within this very family: Hegesippus reserves anepsios for Symeon son of Clopas, the Lord’s actual “cousin,” while calling James and Jude His adelphos — so the source closest to the family does not blur brother and cousin but keeps them apart.6 Lightfoot adds a collateral point: the brethren are named in the Gospels “in connexion with Joseph His reputed father and Mary His mother, never once with Mary of Clopas… It would surely have been otherwise, if the latter Mary were really their mother.”5

Assessment

Weak. The lexical claim taken narrowly — that adelphos can extend to a nephew or collateral kinsman — is sound, and it does neutralize any attempt to disprove the cousin-reading from the dictionary. But as a positive argument for reading the brethren as cousins it does not hold: the attested loose uses are all of a single known relationship named in direct speech, whereas the cousin-reading needs a group habitually and exclusively styled “brothers” by indifferent parties, one singled out as “the Lord’s brother” — a usage unexampled outside such patriarchal-speech contexts. That the precise word anepsios existed, is used elsewhere for “cousin,” and is used by Hegesippus for the one man in this family who really was a cousin, turns the semantics from a support into a difficulty. The word leaves cousin barely possible; it lends the Hieronymian identification no positive weight, which must be carried, if at all, by the separate chain of identifications (Mary of Clopas = the Virgin’s sister; Alphaeus = Clopas) that the reading also requires. Jerome’s presentation — deploying the four-sense scheme as though it settled the identification — overstates what the lexicon can do, and is flagged as such on his page.
