Does John 19:25 make “Mary of Clopas” the Virgin’s sister — two sisters both named Mary?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — the maternal cousinhood at the heart of Jerome’s reading needs the received punctuation of John 19:25 to make “Mary of Clopas” the Virgin’s own sister, and that yields two sisters both named Mary — a coincidence J. B. Lightfoot judged “most improbable.” The early versions (Peshito, the Jerusalem Syriac lectionary, the Thebaic) and the couples-arrangement of the Greek all point instead to four women beside the cross, on which reading the Virgin’s sister is unnamed (plausibly the Salome of Mark 15:40) and Mary of Clopas is no sister of Mary at all · advanced by Jerome as the pivot of the cousin-theory

Related arguments: the cousin theory — see also Does “brother” (adelphos) mean “cousin”? · Do the identifications that complete Jerome’s cousin-theory hold? · Are “James the little” and Joses the Lord’s brothers? · Does the “Papias” fragment give early support? · Does Hegesippus tell against the cousin-theory?

The claim

Jerome’s Hieronymian reading makes the “brethren of the Lord” the sons of “Mary of Clopas” and identifies that Mary as the Virgin’s own sister, so that the brethren are the Lord’s maternal cousins. That sisterhood hangs on a single verse. John 19:25 names the women at the cross: “Now there stood by the cross of Jesus his mother, and his mother’s sister, Mary the wife of Cleophas, and Mary Magdalene.” Read in its received punctuation, the clause “his mother’s sister, Mary the wife of Clopas” is a single apposition — the sister is Mary of Clopas — so the verse names only three women. Combined with the further identification of “Mary of Clopas” with “Mary the mother of James the less and of Joses” (Matthew 27:56; Mark 15:40), this makes James and Joses the Virgin’s nephews. The maternal cousinhood therefore rests on the three-women reading — which yields two sisters, the Virgin and “Mary of Clopas,” both named Mary.

Who advances it

Jerome made the identification the hinge of his case against Helvidius (c. 383): “the Mary who is described as the mother of James the less was the wife of Alphaeus and sister of Mary the Lord’s mother, the one who is called by John the Evangelist ‘Mary of Clopas.’”1 Yet even Jerome held the pivot loosely: he “confesses plainly that he does not know why Mary is called” of Clopas, and in the Letter to Hedibia (c. 406) he listed “Mary of Clopas” and “the mother of James and Joses” as distinct women — loosening, as Lightfoot noted, “the keystone of his own theory.” J. B. Lightfoot, who named the reading after Jerome, isolates the sisterhood as the third of three assumptions on which the whole maternal cousinhood depends: the identification of James the son of Alphaeus with James the Little; the identification of “Mary of Clopas” with the mother of James and Joses; and “the correctness of the received punctuation of John 19:25, which makes ‘Mary of Clopas’ the Virgin’s sister. If any one of these be rejected, this cousinhood falls to the ground.”2

The evidence for

The Greek of John 19:25 sets no conjunction between “his mother’s sister” and “Mary of Clopas,” so the two phrases can be read in apposition — three women, not four — and on that reading the verse itself supplies the sisterhood Jerome needs, with no further supposition. The second link in the chain, moreover, is the one Lightfoot himself grants: of his three assumptions “the second alone can safely be pronounced more likely than not… for it avoids the unnecessary multiplication of Maries,” so that “Mary of Clopas” and “Mary the mother of James and Joses” are indeed one woman. If she is also the Virgin’s sister, her sons are the Lord’s cousins — the tidy single family that recommended the reading to the Latin West.2

Adversarial assessment

Two sisters both named Mary is most improbable. The three-women reading requires the Virgin and her sister to bear the identical name — “it is at least improbable that two sisters should have borne the same name,” a difficulty Lightfoot ranked “most improbable… far surpassing that of supposing two or even three cousins bearing the same name.” The Herodian family, sometimes offered as a parallel for such duplication, does not answer: “Herod was a family name, and it is unlikely that a humble Jewish household should have copied a practice which must lead to so much confusion.”2

The versions read four women. The earliest translations resolve the ambiguity against the three-women reading. The Peshito Syriac inserts a conjunction — “His mother and His mother’s sister, and Mary of Cleophas and Mary Magdalene” — giving four women arranged in two couples, so that the Virgin’s sister is left unnamed and Mary of Clopas is not she. The Jerusalem Syriac lectionary, which cites the verse three times, twice puts a stop after “His mother’s sister,” separating those words from “Mary of Cleophas” and fixing the same four-woman sense by punctuation. And the Thebaic reads “Mary daughter of Clopas,” likewise refusing to fuse the sister with her.3

The Greek admits the four-woman arrangement. Even without the versions, the syntax is open: “the Greek at all events admits, even if it does not favour, this interpretation, for the arrangement of names in couples has a parallel in the lists of the Apostles” (e.g. Matthew 10:2–4). On the four-woman reading the two named pairs are “his mother’s sister” / “Mary of Clopas” and — the unnamed sister is naturally the “Salome” of Mark 15:40 (the “mother of Zebedee’s children” of Matthew 27:56), so that John records the very group of women the Synoptics do, without positing a second Mary in the family at all.3

“Sister” need not be a blood-sister. Finally, even were the three-women reading right, “his mother’s sister” need not name a uterine sister: it could denote a sister-in-law or wider kinswoman — the sense a defender of the cousin-reading adopts when, dropping the two-sisters assumption, he derives the cousinhood on the father’s side instead, joining Hegesippus’ report that Clopas was Joseph’s brother. The most economical modern form of the Hieronymian reading (Josef Blinzler’s) does exactly this, dispensing with the maternal-sisterhood prop altogether — conceding it is not load-bearing.4

Assessment

Weak. The maternal cousinhood Jerome asserts requires John 19:25 to make “Mary of Clopas” the Virgin’s own sister — and that requires two sisters both named Mary, which is, as Lightfoot rightly judged, most improbable. The early versions (Peshito, the Jerusalem Syriac lectionary, the Thebaic) and the couples-arrangement of the Greek all point to four women, on which reading the Virgin’s sister is unnamed (plausibly Salome) and Mary of Clopas is no sister of the Virgin. As positive support for the Hieronymian reading the punctuation-argument therefore fails; it lends the cousin-identification no weight. Like the other props of the completed theory it is one the strongest modern form of the cousin-reading (Blinzler’s) simply discards, resting the cousinhood, if at all, on the fathers’ side via Hegesippus — and Jerome’s own drift into treating the two Marys as distinct persons confirms that the identification never bore the weight later placed on it. This does not by itself refute the cousin-reading, but it removes one of the three legs Lightfoot found the maternal version standing on, and it is the shakiest of the three.
