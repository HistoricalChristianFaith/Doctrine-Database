Does the “Papias” fragment give early (pre-Jerome) support for the cousin theory?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: unsupported — the Latin catena fragment prefixed with the name “Papias,” long cited at the head of the patristic authorities as second-century backing for the Hieronymian cousin-reading, is a medieval forgery: it survives only in a Bodleian manuscript of 1302/3, its one load-bearing sentence (“Maria uxor Alphei”) is lifted almost verbatim from Jerome’s own adversus Helvidium (so it cannot pre-date Jerome), and its author is a medieval namesake — Papias the eleventh-century lexicographer of the Elementarium — not Papias of Hierapolis. It provides no pre-Jerome evidence for the cousin theory whatever · long marshaled by nineteenth-century reference works (e.g. contributors to Smith’s Dictionary of the Bible) at the head of the Hieronymian authorities, and exposed by J. B. Lightfoot

The claim

A short Latin extract, headed with the name Papias, lists the several Marys of the Gospels and makes the Lord’s brethren the sons of “Mary the wife of Clopas or Alphaeus” — the cousin-identification at the heart of the Hieronymian reading. Printed by Grabe (Spicilegium II.34) and Routh (Reliquiae Sacrae I.16) among the remains of Papias of Hierapolis (fl. c. 110), it runs:

Maria mater Domini: Maria Cleophae, sive Alphei uxor, quae fuit mater Jacobi episcopi et apostoli et Symonis et Thadei et cujusdam Joseph: Maria Salome uxor Zebedei mater Joannis evangelistae et Jacobi: Maria Magdalene: istae quatuor in Evangelio reperiuntur.

— “Mary the mother of the Lord; Mary of Cleophas, or wife of Alphaeus, who was mother of James the bishop and apostle, and of Simon and Thaddaeus, and of one Joseph; Mary Salome, wife of Zebedee, mother of John the evangelist and of James; Mary Magdalene: these four are found in the Gospel.”1 The claim built on it is that here, in a writer of the sub-apostolic age who had known the daughters of Philip and heard those who had heard the apostles, the cousin theory stands attested two centuries before Jerome first proposed it — making Jerome’s reading not a fourth-century novelty but the recovery of primitive tradition.

Who advances it

The fragment was, in Lightfoot’s words, “frequently quoted at the head of the patristic authorities, as favouring the view of Jerome” — taken up by defenders of the Hieronymian reading and reproduced in nineteenth-century reference works (contributors to Smith’s Dictionary of the Bible among them) precisely because it seemed to supply the one thing Jerome’s theory conspicuously lacked: an early witness. Grabe and Routh had printed it soberly among the genuine remains of Papias of Hierapolis, and later writers cited it as such.1 J. B. Lightfoot, cataloguing the patristic evidence for his dissertation, examined the fragment and pronounced it spurious.

The evidence for

Taken at face value the fragment would be the strongest card in the Hieronymian hand. The name of Papias is prefixed to it as a title; if genuine it would be the earliest patristic notice of the brethren’s parentage by a wide margin, older than the second-century apocrypha that take the step-brother (Epiphanian) view for granted, and it confirms Jerome’s scheme “in the most minute points” — the very equation Alphaeus = Clopas that later completers had to graft onto Jerome’s core is here already made. An explicit, apostolic-age authority for the cousin-reading would answer at a stroke the standing objection that Jerome’s theory has no witness before Jerome. That is why it was seized upon, and why its genuineness was worth defending.1

Adversarial assessment

The text post-dates Jerome and depends on him. The fragment’s decisive sentence is not independent testimony but a copy. As Lightfoot observed (following Dr. Mill), “the statement about ‘Maria uxor Alphei’ is taken from Jerome (adv. Helvid.) almost word for word… and it is purely arbitrary to reject this as spurious and accept the rest as genuine.”1 Jerome’s Against Helvidius (c. 383) is where the “Mary of Clopas = wife of Alphaeus” identification is first argued;2 a text that reproduces it verbatim is necessarily later than 383, and so cannot possibly witness to the cousin theory before Jerome — it witnesses only to Jerome.

Jerome’s own silence refutes it. Had Papias of Hierapolis really written this, Jerome could not have overlooked it. “The writings of Papias were in Jerome’s hands, and eager as he was to claim the support of authority, he could not have failed to refer to testimony which was so important and which so entirely confirms his view in the most minute points.” More: “nor is it conceivable that a passage like this, coming from so early a writer, should not have impressed itself very strongly on the ecclesiastical tradition of the early centuries, whereas in fact we discover no traces of it.”1 An apostolic-age proof for the cousin-reading that left no mark on the tradition and went unmentioned by the very father hunting for authorities is a proof that did not exist.

Wrong Papias. The manuscript itself is late — a Bodleian codex (no. 2397) “of the date 1302 or 1303”1 — and Lightfoot afterwards traced the extract to its real author: not the bishop of Hierapolis at all, but “a mediaeval namesake… Papias the author of the ‘Elementarium,’ who lived in the 11th century,” whose lexicon (the Elementarium Doctrinae Rudimentum, a standard medieval dictionary) is the source of the note.3 The whole apparatus rests on a confusion of two men separated by nine centuries, sharing only a name. The modern critical edition confirms the verdict rather than reopening it: Stephen C. Carlson’s exhaustive 2021 collation of everything ever attributed to Papias files the four-Marys note not among the fragments or testimonia but under the spurious material — “Papias of Lombardy, On the Four Maries,” an entry excerpted from the eleventh-century Elementarium and dependent on Jerome, expressly excluded from the genuine remains of Papias of Hierapolis.4

Assessment

Unsupported. As early evidence for the cousin theory the fragment collapses on every count: the manuscript is fourteenth-century, its one relevant sentence is copied almost word for word from Jerome (so it is later than the theory it is offered to attest, not earlier), the real Papias’s silence — and the tradition’s — tells decisively against it, and its author is the eleventh-century lexicographer Papias, not Papias of Hierapolis. It is not a weak witness but a spurious one; it supplies no support for the Hieronymian reading at all, and the standing objection it was meant to answer — that the cousin theory has no attestation before Jerome — stands untouched. This verdict is a source-critical one and settles only the pedigree: it removes a mistaken early prop without bearing, either way, on whether the cousin-reading is true on other grounds (weighed on the companion pages). Any page tempted to cite “Papias” for the brethren-as-cousins should cite Jerome instead — which is, in the end, all this fragment is.
