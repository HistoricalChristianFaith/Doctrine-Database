Was the cousin theory a novelty without earlier witness — one Jerome himself let go?

Type: adverse argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — the anti-Hieronymian thesis that the cousin-reading was a novum struck out by Jerome for the occasion (c. 383) against Helvidius: pleading no traditional authority, argued from Scripture alone, and held by Jerome himself with a grip that loosened toward apparent abandonment. Its historical core — that the cousin theory has no witness before Jerome — is strong and close to consensus. But its force against the reading is disputed: novelty is not disproof, the argument from silence is not decisive, and the theory’s modern defenders rebuild it on grounds independent of Jerome’s tradition-claim; while the further “Jerome abandoned it” point is J. B. Lightfoot’s plausible but unproven conjecture · the case is Lightfoot’s, marshalled for the Epiphanian/Helvidian side against the cousin theory

The claim

Of the three historic readings of “the brethren of the Lord,” the Hieronymian — that they were the Lord’s maternal cousins, sons of “Mary of Clopas” the Virgin’s sister — is the last to appear, and it appears all at once, in Jerome’s Against Helvidius (c. 383). The argument here is that this timing is telling: the reading was a novum devised for the polemical occasion, resting on Scripture alone with no earlier writer to vouch for it, and held by its own author with a grip that visibly slackened over his later works. J. B. Lightfoot, who gave the three views their names, built the case on five observations.

1. Jerome argues from Scripture and names no predecessor. Helvidius had claimed two older writers, Tertullian and Victorinus of Pettau. Jerome, Lightfoot notes, “is obliged to concede him Tertullian, whose authority he invalidates as ‘not a member of the Church,’ but denies him Victorinus”1 — and for his own account of the relationship produces not a father but a deduction: he “cannot, or at least does not, name a single writer; he simply offers it as a critical deduction from the statements of Scripture.”1

2. The contrast with the virginity is telling. For the perpetual virginity itself — the ground his theory shares with the Epiphanian — Jerome is at no loss for authorities: “Ignatius, Polycarp, Irenaeus, Justin, and many other ‘eloquent apostolic men’ occur to him at once.”1 He marshals them in his own text — “Might I not array against you the whole series of ancient writers? Ignatius, Polycarp, Irenaeus, Justin Martyr, and many other apostolic and eloquent men”2 — so that his silence on the cousin-relationship, where he could otherwise pile up names, is “all the more telling.”

3. No earlier instance can be produced. Lightfoot’s survey of the fathers before Jerome yields none who read the brethren as cousins: “No decisive instance can be produced of a writer holding Jerome’s view, before it was propounded by Jerome himself.”1

4. Jerome holds the pivot loosely from the first. The hinge of the whole reading is the identification of “Mary of Clopas” with “Mary the mother of James and Joses” — and Jerome will not press even that. He says outright that he does not care to fight hard for it: “contentiosum funem non traho” (“I do not pull the rope of contention”), leaving it open “whether Mary the wife of Clopas and Mary the mother of James and Joses were different women, provided it is clearly understood that Mary the mother of James and Joses was not the same person as the Lord’s mother.”3 The pivot of his theory he thus advances as subordinate to his real point, the virginity, and declines to defend to the hilt.

5. By 406–7 he appears to let it go. In the late Letter to Hedibia, enumerating the Marys of the Gospels, Jerome lists Mary of Cleophas (the Lord’s maternal aunt) and Mary the mother of James and Joses as distinct persons, adding “although others contend that the mother of James and Joses was His aunt” — assigning to unnamed others the very equation that had been “the keystone of his own theory.”4 Lightfoot, reading the works in order, wondered whether Jerome, long resident in Bethlehem and steeped in the Palestinian tradition, “first relaxed his hold of and finally relinquished his own hypothesis.”1

Who advances it

The novelty of the cousin theory is registered first by the pre-Jerome witnesses themselves — who write as though the reading did not exist — but as a marshalled argument it is J. B. Lightfoot’s, in his 1865 dissertation “The Brethren of the Lord,” the modern locus classicus of the whole question. Lightfoot files Jerome in column C of his conspectus, “Sons of the Virgin’s Sister,” and rests his rejection of that column on two counts: that “Jerome claims no traditional support for his theory” and that “Jerome does not hold his theory staunchly and consistently.”1 The argument serves the Epiphanian and Helvidian readings alike, which both keep the brethren clear of the Twelve and neither of which needs the cousin-identification; it is neutral between those two.

Its modern carrier to a popular audience is the Reformed apologist James White (Alpha and Omega Ministries), who presses it within a sola scriptura frame: the perpetual virginity has no warrant in the New Testament and grew instead from second-century works outside it — above all the Protoevangelium of James — and from an ascetic devaluing of marriage, so that Jerome’s cousin-reading is a late construct the Reformers were slow to disown and later Protestants rightly rejected. White treats the tradition-from-antiquity case as specious and prefers to argue the question from Scripture upward.5 His historical core coincides with Lightfoot’s; the further, rhetorical step — from “novel and unscriptural” to “therefore false” — is exactly the overreach the assessment below keeps in check, for novelty is not disproof.

The evidence for

The historical core is powerfully corroborated by the timeline that precedes Jerome. The writers who discuss the brethren’s parentage before c. 383 know only two options — the stepbrother (Epiphanian) solution and the Helvidian denial — and betray no knowledge of a third. Origen (c. 248) “declares himself very distinctly in favour of the Epiphanian view” and is “wholly unaware of the Hieronymian, the only alternative which presented itself being the denial of the perpetual virginity”; Hilary of Poitiers (c. 353), a learned Latin bishop refuting the deniers, “not only adopts the Epiphanian solution, but shows himself entirely ignorant of the Hieronymian”; the Ambrosiaster (c. 375) “entirely coincides” with him; and Epiphanius, so jealous for Joseph’s honour that he would have “hailed [the cousin-reading] with delight… had he known of it,” does not know it.1 The one text ever produced to fill the gap — a short Latin catena headed with the name “Papias” — is a medieval forgery whose load-bearing sentence is lifted from Jerome’s own Against Helvidius, so that it post-dates the theory it was offered to attest (weighed at “Does the ‘Papias’ fragment give early support?”, assessment: unsupported).

This is an argument from silence, but of a peculiarly strong kind. It is not merely that no father happens to attest the reading; it is that the two men best placed to attest it do not. Jerome himself, hunting for authorities and eager to array “the whole series of ancient writers,” can name none for the relationship — while readily naming five for the virginity. And Epiphanius, whose lifelong project was the defence of Mary’s and Joseph’s honour, and who would have prized a reading that spared Joseph a first family, shows no sign of having heard of it. When the advocates who most wanted a thing, and were best equipped to find it, come up empty, the silence carries more weight than a bare argumentum e silentio. To this Lightfoot adds the internal evidence of Jerome’s own wavering — the loosely-held pivot of §15 and the apparent retraction of the Letter to Hedibia — so that even the theory’s author, on this reading of the sources, did not finally stand by it.

A positive datum stands beside the silence: the shape of the apocrypha themselves. Where the fathers are silent, the apocryphal infancy literature is voluble — and it speaks with one voice. The whole downstream stream that elaborates Joseph’s household — the Protevangelium of James at its head, and following it the Gospel of Pseudo-Matthew, the Gospel of the Nativity of Mary, the History of Joseph the Carpenter, the infancy Gospel of Thomas, and the Arabic Gospel of the Infancy — uniformly makes Joseph an aged widower with sons of his own, the Epiphanian shape; not one of them reaches for a cousin-relationship. These narratives are, in Lightfoot’s words, “pure fabrications” wherever they step beyond the Gospels — yet “the fabrications would scarcely have taken this form, if the Hieronymian view of the Lord’s brethren had been received or even known when they were written.” The pious imagination that spun out Joseph’s first family ran wholly in the stepbrother channel, which it would have had no reason to do had the cousin-reading been available to it. And the irony doubles back on Jerome: it is precisely these sources he swept aside as deliramenta apocryphorum, the “ravings of the apocryphal writings,” when he taunted the Epiphanians for “following” them — yet the ravings he mocked testify, by the very form they take, that his own cousin-theory was not yet in the field to be chosen. Two cautions keep the point from being pushed too far. The apocrypha are genealogically dependent, copying the Protevangelium rather than attesting independently, so their unanimity is that of a single tradition, not a chorus of witnesses; and, being fabrications, they show only that the cousin-reading was unknown — not that the stepbrother account is history, a distinct question weighed at “Is the second-century stepbrother tradition genuine memory or a construct?”6

Adversarial assessment

Novelty is not falsity, and the silence is not decisive. That a reading is late-attested does not make it wrong; a true interpretation can go unremarked for centuries and be recovered by a later critic. The fathers before Jerome who discuss the brethren’s parentage at all are few, and Jerome’s own context was a reply to Helvidius, not a doxography — so his arguing from Scripture rather than from a catena of predecessors is at least partly explained by the polemical occasion, not only by his having no predecessor to cite. An argument from silence, even a strong one, establishes a probability, not a proof.

The interpretation’s truth is separable from Jerome’s tradition-claim. Two questions must be kept apart: whether Jerome’s argument had traditional backing (it did not), and whether the cousin interpretation is true (which turns on other evidence). The theory’s strongest modern defenders — Josef Blinzler, John McHugh — discard Jerome’s own weakest props (the John 19:25 punctuation, the completing identifications) and rebuild a pared-down cousin-reading on independent grounds, chiefly the patristic tradition around Hegesippus and Clopas. So the reading does not stand or fall with Jerome’s failure to name a predecessor; the novelty argument bears on Jerome’s case, and only obliquely on the interpretation, whose merits are weighed on the companion pages.7

The abandonment reads better as a conjecture than a fact. The Letter to Hedibia passage will bear a lighter construction than recantation. There Jerome is untangling the resurrection appearances and enumerating the Gospel Marys, a different exegetical task from the brethren-controversy; distinguishing the two Marys in that setting need not mean he had renounced his earlier identification, only that he was not pressing it. Lightfoot himself frames the point as a question — “Can it be that… he first relaxed his hold of and finally relinquished his own hypothesis?” — not as a finding; and the English of the Hedibia letter available here is a provisional rendering, its wording not to be pressed too hard.4 The wavering is real — Jerome plainly held the pivot loosely (§15) — but “he abandoned the theory” is an inference beyond what the texts compel.

Assessment

Contested. The argument bundles a strong claim with a weaker one, and they should be scored apart. The historical core — that the cousin theory has no witness before Jerome and was a fourth-century novelty — is well-grounded and close to consensus: the pre-Jerome timeline knows only the Epiphanian and Helvidian options, the sole alleged early prop (the “Papias” fragment) is spurious and depends on Jerome, Jerome himself, eager for authorities, could name none for the relationship, and the whole apocryphal infancy tradition — uniformly Epiphanian in shape — would “scarcely have taken this form” had the cousin-reading been known. As a matter of attestation-history the point holds. But its force against the cousin reading is genuinely disputed. Novelty is not disproof; an argument from silence is not decisive; and the reading’s modern defenders ground it independently of Jerome’s tradition-claim, so the interpretation’s truth is not settled by Jerome’s lack of predecessors — that is decided on the philological and historical merits weighed elsewhere in this group. The further “Jerome abandoned it” strand is Lightfoot’s plausible but unproven conjecture, which the Letter to Hedibia permits without requiring. So the argument is weighty by the lights of the historian of tradition, yet short of decisive as a disproof of the interpretation, and it must not be overstated into “Jerome recanted” — contested. Like the group’s other members it is neutral between the Epiphanian and Helvidian readings: it tells against the cousins, not for any one of the rivals.
