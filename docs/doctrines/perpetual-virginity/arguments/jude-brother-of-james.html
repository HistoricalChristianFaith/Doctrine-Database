Does Jude 1’s “brother of James” denote literal kinship, or a figurative brotherhood (gloss, spiritual fraternity, or “co-worker”)?

Type: proof-text / lexical argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: sound — the letter of Jude opens “Jude, the servant of Jesus Christ, and brother of James” (Jude 1). The overwhelming majority of scholars take this Judas to be the brother of Jesus named at Mark 6:3 (∥ Matthew 13:55), and Richard Bauckham argues the self-designation “brother of James” itself fixes it: James is “the only man in the primitive church who could be called simply ‘James’ without risk of ambiguity” — James the Lord’s brother, the Jerusalem leader — so Jude places himself by kinship to that James. Against this stand the minority readings that empty “brother” of literal kinship — a scribal gloss (Barns), spiritual fraternity (Plessis), or “co-worker” (Ellis) — but the natural sense of adelphos + a named individual in the genitive is family, and (Bauckham’s rebuttal) had the writer meant to mark out a Barsabbas or a Thomas, the obvious tag was the surname, not “brother of James.” So “brother of James” is most naturally a real kinship note. But like its companion literal-kinship arguments it is neutral between the Helvidian and Epiphanian readings (it fixes that Jude is kin and brother of James, not whose son he was) and does not by itself touch Mary’s virginity · argued from Richard Bauckham, with the primary text in Jude

See also the companion arguments on whether Jude’s named grandsons support a literal-kinship reading and on whether 1 Corinthians 9:5’s married-missionary “brothers of the Lord” support one — the same anchor (that “brother of the Lord” names literal kin, not a figure of speech) reached here from the self-designation of the letter of Jude. The rival Judas Thomas identification and the “Jude of James” (Luke 6:16) reading that props Jerome’s cousin-theory both turn on this same phrase.

The claim

The letter of Jude names its author only once, in its opening words:

Jude, the servant of Jesus Christ, and brother of James, to them that are sanctified by God the Father, and preserved in Jesus Christ, and called…

— Jude 1.1 The author does not call himself the brother of the Lord — only the brother of James. Who is he, and what does “brother of James” mean? Richard Bauckham — himself holding the perpetual virginity on the Epiphanian reading — catalogues the identifications that have been proposed and argues that the phrase settles the matter in favour of literal kinship.2

The majority reading: kinship to a uniquely-identifiable James. “The overwhelming majority of scholars have understood this Judas to be the brother of Jesus” — the Jude listed among the four brethren at Mark 6:3 and Matthew 13:55, and known from Hegesippus (ap. Eusebius, H.E. iii.19–20) for his grandsons. And, Bauckham argues, the identification is not merely traditional but is “strongly supported by the phrase, ‘brother of James,’ which distinguishes this Judas from others of the same name by mentioning his relationship to the only man in the primitive church who could be called simply ‘James’ without risk of ambiguity” — James the Lord’s brother, head of the Jerusalem church.2 A writer who identifies himself by an unadorned “James” can only mean the one James so famous he needs no epithet; and to name oneself that James’s brother is, on its face, to claim his family.

The readings that empty “brother” of kinship. A minority have taken the author to be someone else, and have had, in consequence, to explain “brother of James” as something other than a kinship note. Some identify him with Judas Barsabbas (Acts 15:22) and read “brother of James” accordingly — J. M. B. Barns as a later scribal gloss, P. J. du Plessis as spiritual fraternity, E. Earle Ellis as “co-worker.” Others (the East-Syrian tradition, revived by some moderns) identify him with the apostle Judas Thomas, taking “brother of James” to equate Thomas with the brother of Jesus. And Harnack and Moffatt supposed the author “an otherwise unknown Judas,” with “brother of James” a later gloss intended to give the letter authority.3

The interpretation weighed here is the reading these data are adduced to support: “brother of James” in Jude 1 is a note of literal family kinship — Jude names himself the brother of James the Lord’s brother, and so is himself the Lord’s brother — and not a figurative, spiritualized, honorific, or “co-worker” brotherhood, nor a secondary gloss.

What the argument establishes

1. “Brother of James” functions as an identifier only if “James” is unique — which points to the Lord’s brother. This is the argument’s real force. The author gives no epithet with “James” — not “James son of Zebedee,” not “James son of Alphaeus,” just “James.” A bare name can identify only where one bearer overshadows every other; and the one James who could be named without qualification in the first-century church was James the Lord’s brother, the Jerusalem leader (cf. Acts 15:13; Galatians 1:19; and the “then he was seen of James” of 1 Corinthians 15:7, the same undesigned datum). To attach oneself to that James by the word “brother” is naturally to claim kinship with him — and since that James is the brother of Jesus (Mark 6:3), Jude is drawn into the same family group (James, Joses, Jude, Simon) the Gospels name.

2. The natural sense of adelphos + a named individual is family. The general Christian use of “brother” for a fellow-believer is real, and Ellis’s narrower thesis — that adelphos in the Pauline mission could carry a semi-technical sense of “co-worker” — is not baseless as a description of the address-formulae of the letters. But the construction here is not the vocative “brethren” of a congregation; it is adelphos governing a single personal name in the genitive (“brother of James”), and that idiom, throughout the New Testament, denotes literal kinship: “Andrew, Simon Peter’s brother” (John 1:40), “James… and John his brother” (Matthew 4:21). One is the “co-worker” of a shared task or of the Lord, not the “co-worker” of a particular named man; a personal-name genitive after adelphos is the language of the family register.

3. Bauckham’s rebuttal to the alternatives: the surname would have served. The readings that dissolve the kinship all rest on identifying the author with a differently-named figure — a Barsabbas or a Thomas — and then re-explaining “brother of James.” But, Bauckham objects, “the obvious way to distinguish these figures from others of the same name would have been to use their surnames Thomas and Barsabbas, not ‘brother of James.’” A writer who was Judas Barsabbas or Judas Thomas, wanting to be known, would have written “Judas Barsabbas” or “Judas Thomas” — the ready, unambiguous label — not reached for a roundabout “brother of James.” That the writer chose kinship-to-James as his distinguishing mark is itself evidence that kinship-to-James is what identified him.4

What it does not establish

4. It is neutral between the Helvidian and Epiphanian readings. Like its companion literal-kinship arguments, this one fixes that Jude was kin — the brother of James, and so of the Lord — not whose son he was. On the Helvidian reading James and Jude are Mary’s own later sons; on the Epiphanian reading they are Joseph’s sons by a former wife, Jesus’ elder stepbrothers — and on both they are literal brothers of one another and reputed brothers of the Lord. “Brother of James” reads the same either way, so it lends the perpetual virginity no direct weight and says nothing of Mary’s childbearing. It presses only against the purely-figurative “brother”; the choice among the three literal readings, and the Hieronymian cousin-reading among them (cousins are kin too), it leaves to be settled elsewhere.

5. The identity and authorship of the letter’s Jude are a separate, disputed question — but do not weaken the kinship sense. Whether the letter was actually written by Jude the Lord’s brother, or is pseudonymous, is genuinely contested, and nothing here settles it. But the meaning of “brother of James” does not hang on that dispute: even a pseudepigrapher writing in Jude’s name intends the phrase as a claim of kinship to James the Lord’s brother — that is precisely why Harnack could suppose it added “to give the letter authority.” The gloss hypothesis, moreover, has no textual support: “brother of James” stands in the whole manuscript tradition, so treating it as a later insertion is a conjecture in the service of a theory of authorship, not a reading of the evidence. The kinship sense is what the phrase says on any account of who wrote it.

One caution runs the other way, and is honestly owed. The apostle-list phrase “Judas of James” (Ioudas Iakōbou, Luke 6:16; Acts 1:13) is elliptical, and the AV supplies “Judas the brother of James” precisely on the analogy of Jude 1 — whereas the more natural filling of the ellipsis is “son of James” (so the Peshito and the Thebaic), which is why the “Jude of James = brother” step is weighed as the less-probable reading on the Hieronymian-identifications page. That caution touches the apostle-list, not Jude 1: in the letter’s own opening the word is spelled out — adelphos Iakōbou, “brother of James” — with no ellipsis to fill, so the kinship sense is on the surface of the text and not a supplied conjecture.

Assessment

Sound. On the interpretation it is actually offered for — that “brother of James” in Jude 1 denotes literal family kinship, not a figurative, spiritual, honorific, or “co-worker” brotherhood — the reading is the mainstream one and well-grounded. The phrase is an identifier, and it identifies by relationship to the one James who needed no epithet; the idiom (adelphos + a personal name) is the ordinary New-Testament language of kinship; and the alternatives that empty it of family sense all require first identifying the author with a differently-named man and then re-reading the phrase against its grain, when the plain surname would have identified him at once. Steelmanned honestly, Ellis’s “co-worker” nuance and du Plessis’s “spiritual fraternity” are coherent as accounts of the vocative “brethren,” but they do not fit “brother of James”; and Barns’s and Harnack’s gloss has no textual footing. The kinship reading holds.

Two limits keep the finding modest for this doctrine, and both are limits on what is shown, not defects in the reading. First, it is neutral between the Helvidian and Epiphanian readings — “brother of James” fits a full brother and a stepbrother alike — so it gives the perpetual virginity no direct support and does not touch Mary’s childbearing. Second, its bite is against the purely-figurative “brother,” not against the Hieronymian cousin-reading, which keeps the kinship (as cousinhood) while disputing its degree. The honest verdict, then: a sound datum for the literal kinship of the brethren — the same anchor the grandsons of Jude and the married-missionary brothers of 1 Corinthians 9:5 supply, reached here from the letter’s own self-designation — that leaves the choice among the three literal readings, and so the perpetual virginity itself, to be settled on other grounds.
