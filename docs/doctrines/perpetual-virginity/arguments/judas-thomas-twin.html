Was Jude the Lord’s brother the apostle Thomas, Jesus’ physical twin?

Type: identity argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — the east-Syrian church of Edessa knew the apostle Thomas as “Judas Thomas” and, reading the name Thomas (Aramaic tĕ’ōmā, “twin”) as a description, two of its sectarian works (the Acts of Thomas and the Book of Thomas) present him as the twin of Jesus — whence some moderns (Koester, Layton) have made him Jesus’ physical twin, and so the same man as Jude the Lord’s brother. On its best case the identification does not hold: the twin-of-Jesus motif is confined to a narrow, late, geographically isolated tradition, serves a plainly theological purpose (Thomas as the disciple’s spiritual “double”), is coloured by the pagan Dioscuri myth, and is hard to take seriously beside the virgin-birth tradition current in the very same region; “Judas” was most likely Thomas’s own personal name, and he is never styled “Judas the brother of James” as Jude 1 styles its author · argued from Richard Bauckham, against Helmut Koester and Bentley Layton, with the primary Thomas literature

The claim

The brethren-question asks who the “brethren of the Lord” — James, Joses, Simon, and Jude (Mark 6:3) — really were. One proposed answer folds the fourth of them, Jude, into a different figure: the apostle Thomas. In the East Syrian church of Edessa and its region the apostle was known not as “Thomas” simply but as Judas Thomas — so in the title of the Gospel of Thomas, throughout the Acts of Thomas and the Book of Thomas the Contender, in the Curetonian Syriac of John 14:22, in the Abgar legend Eusebius reports (H.E. 1:13), and in Ephraem.1 Since the name “Thomas” renders the Aramaic word for twin (and the Gospel and Acts add the Greek equivalent, Didymus, to the same effect), that same tradition took the apostle to be the twin brother of Jesus — and a twin of Jesus, sharing his mother, would be a son of Mary. Read that way, “Judas Thomas” the twin is Jude the Lord’s brother, and at least one of the “brethren” was Mary’s own child, carried in the same womb as Jesus.1

The reading is not merely ancient curiosity: modern scholars have pressed it. Helmut Koester and Bentley Layton have favoured the historicity of the twinship and linked the New Testament Letter of Jude to it — Koester suggesting the letter was written in Syria under the pseudonym of Jude the brother of Jesus precisely because “Judas Thomas” was a revered authority in the region’s Gnostic circles.2 That is the interpretation weighed here: Jude the Lord’s brother was the apostle Thomas, the physical twin of Jesus.

Steelmanning the claim

Two things must be granted before the reading is weighed, so that it is not dismissed too cheaply. First, the East Syrian name is genuinely old and genuinely distinctive: no invention of the schoolmen, “Judas Thomas” is embedded across the earliest Thomas literature and even in a Syriac Gospel text, and it is a real datum that this apostle bore, or was given, the name Judas. Richard Bauckham — himself a critic of the twin-of-Jesus reading — grants that “the east Syrian tradition that his personal name was Judas is entirely plausible.”1 Second, “Thomas” really does mean “twin,” and the apostle probably was a twin of someone — that is the natural force of a surname no one would coin without cause. The question is not whether there was a Judas called “the twin,” but whether his twin was Jesus, and whether that Judas was Jude the Lord’s brother. On both points the case fails.

Why it does not hold

1. The twin-of-Jesus motif is confined, late, and local. The bare name “Judas Thomas” is itself a regional peculiarity — “only in works from this particular geographical area of the early church is the name Judas given to the apostle Thomas.” The further step, making Thomas a brother of the Lord, is narrower still: it “is known at an early date only in the east Syrian area, appearing elsewhere only in much later works under the influence of the Acts of Thomas.” And the explicit identification of him as Jesus’ twin “is confined to the Acts of Thomas and the Book of Thomas, though it may well be presupposed in the Gospel of Thomas.” A reading that must be quarried out of two sectarian works from one corner of the second- and third-century church is a slender foundation on which to overturn the plain sense of the Gospel “brethren.”1

2. Several witnesses to the name give no hint of the identification — and one severs it outright. In many of the sources that call him “Judas Thomas” there is no suggestion that he is the Lord’s brother at all. Ephraem, tellingly, distinguishes Judas Thomas from both the apostle “Judas of James” (Luke 6:16) and Jude the Lord’s brother — while identifying those two with each other (in his Commentary on Acts he makes Jude the Lord’s brother the apostle “Judas of James”/Thaddaeus, yet keeps Judas Thomas a separate man). So the very tradition that most cherished “Judas Thomas” did not read him as one of the brethren; the name and the identification travel separately.1

3. The twinship is a theological figure, not a genealogical report. Where the Acts and the Book of Thomas do make him Jesus’ twin, the point is transparently spiritual. “The identification of Thomas as the twin brother of Jesus serves a highly theological purpose in these works”: it makes him the model of Christian self-knowledge — to know oneself is to know one’s divine “double” or twin. “The real interest is in Thomas as the spiritual twin of Jesus,” his “twin and true companion” (Book of Thomas 138:1), “the apostle who knows Jesus best and most resembles him.” Even his being cast as a carpenter like Joseph reads as a metaphor for that likeness. The understanding of the two as twins is further coloured by the pagan myth of the Dioscuri (the divine twins Castor and Pollux). So Bauckham concludes that “the main impetus to this identification came from the theory of spiritual twinship with Jesus,” and that a merely spiritual reading of the name may well have generated the idea of a physical twin rather than the reverse: “It is possible that a tradition of Thomas’ physical twinship to Jesus was later exploited theologically, but it is at least equally possible that an interpretation of his name as implying his spiritual twinship with Jesus later brought with it the idea of physical twinship.”1

4. A physical twin of Jesus is barely thinkable in the tradition that produced the name. Whether the motif was ever meant physically is itself doubtful: “It is hard to imagine how the idea of a twin brother of Jesus could have been taken seriously in circles where the tradition of the virgin birth was current.” The same East Syrian milieu that told of Judas Thomas the twin also received the virgin birth (cf. the Odes of Solomon 19); and in the Acts of Thomas itself Jesus appears “in the form of Thomas” — his “identical twin” — only as one of his “many forms,” a docetic shape-shifting, not a shared birth. The twin language belongs to that Christology, not to a claim about Mary’s childbearing.1

5. “Judas” was most probably Thomas’s own name — and even a real twinship need not point to Jesus. The simplest account of the data is that Judas was the apostle’s personal name and “Thomas” (“Twin”) his distinguishing surname — as “Peter/Cephas” (Rock) served Simon, and “Barnabas” served Joseph — used to tell him apart from the several other early Judases (two among the Twelve, the Lord’s brother, Judas Barsabbas). On that reading his personal name survived where the Gospel tradition ran under his name, in East Syria, and was forgotten elsewhere. He probably was a twin — but, Bauckham notes, “considering him the twin brother of Jesus would not necessarily identify him with” Jude rather than with another of the brothers, and his actual twin “need not have been prominent in the early church and could be completely unknown to us.” (Later non-Syrian legend duly supplied him an otherwise unknown twin — a brother Eliezer, or a sister Lysia.) So “that the name Judas was actually Thomas’ name is more probable than that he was so called… as a result of his identification as a twin of Jesus and therefore with Jude the Lord’s brother.”1

6. He is never called “Judas the brother of James” — the one title that would tie him to Jude. Even inside the Thomas literature the apostle is regularly “Judas Thomas” or simply “Thomas”; only in the Acts of Thomas, and only once his identity is beyond doubt, is he called “Judas” plainly. “He is never called ‘Judas, the brother of James.’” Yet it is exactly the phrase “brother of James” that Jude 1 uses to mark off its author from the other Judases. Anyone writing pseudonymously as the Edessene “Judas Thomas” would have reached for the surname Thomas; anyone outside East Syria would not have known the apostle Thomas was ever called Judas at all. So the attempt to attach the Letter of Jude to the twin-of-Jesus tradition is, in Bauckham’s words, “most implausible.”1

Assessment

Weak. There is a real tradition here to reckon with — an old and distinctive East Syrian name, “Judas Thomas,” and a genuine ancient interpretation of it as “the twin” — so the claim is not baseless, and it is rightly weighed rather than waved off. But on its best available case the interpretation it is marshalled for — that Jude the Lord’s brother was the apostle Thomas, Jesus’ physical twin — does not hold. The twin-of-Jesus motif is confined to two sectarian works from one region, absent from (and even contradicted by) other witnesses to the same name, plainly theological in function, coloured by the Dioscuri myth, and scarcely compatible with the virgin-birth tradition of the very milieu that coined it; the more economical reading — Judas his personal name, “Thomas” his surname for a real but unknown twinship — accounts for the evidence without any of that freight; and he is never given the one title (“brother of James”) that would connect him to Jude. The claim therefore lends the Helvidian case no positive weight: it does not establish that Mary bore a twin, and it cannot bear the identification of Jude with Thomas that it needs.

Two guardrails keep the finding honest. First, the verdict is not a rejection of the supernatural or of the miraculous: nothing here turns on ruling out a marvel a priori. The twin claim fails on ordinary historical grounds — attestation, geography, dating, and the manifest theological purpose of the sources — not because a twin of Jesus would be wondrous. Second, “weak” is not “fabricated”: the name “Judas Thomas” is a real feature of East Syrian Christianity, and the apostle may indeed have been someone’s twin. What the evidence will not support is the leap from that surname to a shared birth with Jesus, and from there to an identity with Jude — the leap on which the argument’s bearing on Mary’s perpetual virginity entirely depends.
