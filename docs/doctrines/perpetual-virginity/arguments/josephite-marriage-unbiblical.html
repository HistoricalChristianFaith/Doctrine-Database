Is a never-consummated (“Josephite”) marriage unbiblical — does 1 Corinthians 7 forbid a permanently celibate Joseph and Mary?

Type: adverse argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — the objection is not the grammatical one about “until” but a theological one about the nature of marriage: that a real marriage which is never consummated is itself contrary to Scripture — Paul in 1 Corinthians 7 tells spouses not to “defraud one the other,” and Matthew’s “took unto him his wife” reads as a full marital union — so a permanently celibate (“Josephite”) marriage between Joseph and Mary is implausible and unbiblical. It does not hold as a disproof: 1 Corinthians 7:5 expressly permits abstinence “with consent,” the chapter positively exalts continence (7:7–8, 7:32–35), and the classical theology of marriage — stated at length by Augustine on this very case — holds that marriage is constituted by consent and covenant, not by intercourse, so a mutually vowed continence leaves a true marriage intact. The objection shows at most that a Josephite marriage is unusual and not the scriptural norm, not that it is unbiblical or impossible · advanced at the popular level by Mike Winger and GotQuestions.org

The claim

… their teaching is, no, they got married but they never consummated the marriage, which is considered a wonderful thing in this scripture… 1 Corinthians 7 talks about how you’re not supposed to stay celibate in marriage — that’s not how it works, this is bad for marriage. You want to stay celibate, then stay single.

— Mike Winger, “Unbiased stuff the Catholic Church teaches” (2015), at about 57:23–59:37.1 A companion form of the objection is pressed by GotQuestions.org from Matthew’s wording: “Matthew also makes a point of telling us that Joseph ‘took Mary home as his wife’… Matthew’s readers would naturally conclude that Mary became Joseph’s wife in every sense of the word.”2 The assertion, in its strongest form, is that Scripture presents sexual union as intrinsic to marriage — so a “marriage” deliberately and permanently without it is not a real marriage at all, and the perpetual virginity, which requires exactly such an arrangement, is therefore unbiblical.

Who advances it

This is a distinct objection from the grammatical dispute over “knew her not till” (Matthew 1:25), weighed separately at the infancy-language argument and the two-word heōs hou argument. Here the proof-text is not the adverb but the doctrine of marriage. At the popular level the argument is put by the Bible teacher Mike Winger, who grounds it on 1 Corinthians 7 and the good of marital union, and by the widely-used apologetics site GotQuestions.org, which presses the “took his wife ⇒ a real, consummated marriage” inference from Matthew 1:24. Both are modern popularizers restating an old Protestant objection; neither cites a scholarly authority for this particular thrust, which rests on a reading of 1 Corinthians 7 and of what the word “wife” must entail.

The interpretation weighed

The interpretation adjudicated here is the objection itself: a marriage never consummated is contrary to Scripture, so a permanently celibate Joseph–Mary marriage is unbiblical. Two supports carry it — (i) 1 Corinthians 7:3–5, that spouses owe each other conjugal “due benevolence” and must not “defraud” one another; and (ii) the sense of “wife” in Matthew 1:24, that to “take” a woman “as wife” is to enter a full marital union. The question is whether these establish that a never-consummated marriage is unbiblical or impossible, and not merely that it is not the ordinary scriptural pattern.

The case for

The objection has a real point of contact with the text, and should be put at its strongest before it is weighed. Scripture does present the one-flesh sexual union as belonging to the purpose of marriage from the first — “they shall be one flesh” (Genesis 2:24) — and Paul, writing to a church at Corinth, sets out the conjugal duty in blunt terms: “Let the husband render unto the wife due benevolence: and likewise also the wife unto the husband. The wife hath not power of her own body, but the husband… Defraud ye not one the other, except it be with consent for a time… and come together again, that Satan tempt you not for your incontinency” (1 Corinthians 7:3–5).3 On its face this makes sexual mutuality an obligation of the married state and treats prolonged abstinence as spiritually hazardous — an opening to temptation. If that duty is constitutive of marriage, then a couple who married with a settled intention never to render it would be doing something the apostle does not contemplate; and Matthew’s report that Joseph “took unto him his wife” (1:24) would, on the natural reading, describe a man entering just such an ordinary union. So the objection is not frivolous: the perpetual virginity does require a marital arrangement that Scripture nowhere holds up as a model, and the burden of showing it possible falls on its defenders.

Adversarial assessment

What holds. One observation survives: a permanently celibate marriage is not the scriptural norm. The Bible’s ordinary picture of marriage includes sexual union, and 1 Corinthians 7:3–5 does treat the conjugal due as the expected pattern and prolonged abstinence as a risk for those without the gift of continence. A defender of the perpetual virginity should concede that a Josephite marriage is an extraordinary case, unattested as a pattern elsewhere in Scripture — which is, in fact, precisely how the fathers who taught it framed it: as a singular grace, not a general model.

What is overstated. But as a disproof the argument fails at every joint.

First, 1 Corinthians 7 cuts the other way. The very verse pressed against the doctrine contains its own exception: “Defraud ye not one the other, except it be with consent” (7:5). Paul’s target is unilateral deprivation — one spouse withholding from the other — and the danger of temptation for couples who lack self-control; he expressly allows abstinence by mutual agreement, and adds that he speaks “by permission, and not of commandment” (7:6). A continence both parties freely and permanently vow is not the “defrauding” the passage forbids. And the chapter as a whole prizes celibacy: Paul wishes “that all men were even as I myself” (7:7), calls it “good” for the unmarried to remain single (7:8), and holds that “he that is unmarried careth for the things that belong to the Lord… that she may be holy both in body and in spirit” (7:32–34).3 A chapter that exalts continence as a higher freedom for the Lord is an odd text on which to rest the claim that continence within marriage is “bad” and “not how it works.”

Second, marriage is constituted by consent, not by intercourse. The objection’s hidden premise — that a “wife” who is never known carnally is not truly a wife — is exactly the premise the classical theology of marriage denies, and denies with this case in view. Augustine, treating Joseph and Mary at length in On Marriage and Concupiscence, holds that their marriage was fully real: “God forbid that the nuptial bond should be regarded as broken between those who have by mutual consent agreed to observe a perpetual abstinence… Nay, it will be only a firmer one.” Mary “is called his wife because of her first troth of betrothal, although he had had no carnal knowledge of her, nor was destined to have. The designation of wife was neither destroyed nor made untrue, where there never had been, nor was meant to be, any carnal connection.” All three goods of marriage were present without it — “there was offspring… there was faithfulness… there was the bond… Only there was no nuptial cohabitation” — so that he can ask: “Wherefore, then, may not persons remain man and wife when they cease by mutual consent from cohabitation; seeing that Joseph and Mary continued such, though they never even began to cohabit?”4 This answers GotQuestions’ “wife in every sense of the word” directly: the word “wife” in Matthew 1:24 marks the covenant Joseph entered by consent, and Matthew himself notes in the very next verse (“knew her not till she had brought forth”) that this real marriage began without consummation — so “took his wife” cannot be made to entail sexual union. The same framing is older than Augustine: Epiphanius insists Mary was “not entrusted to him for marriage… He was called her husband because of the Law,” a bond of guardianship and law rather than of the marriage-bed, and Chromatius likewise took the union as real yet celibate.5

Third, the case is sui generis, and the tradition treats it so. Paul’s counsel in 1 Corinthians 7 is pastoral guidance for ordinary believers deciding whether to marry or to abstain; it is not a law that every marriage must be consummated, and it cannot be stretched from “the norm is X” to “any exception is forbidden.” The perpetual virginity claims a unique, redemptive-historical exception — the mother of the incarnate Lord — not a pattern for Christian couples. That a permanently celibate marriage is unusual is granted; that it is unbiblical does not follow.

Fourth, the “obviously unbiblical” framing sits ill with its own Protestant heritage. The doctrine Winger calls contrary to Scripture was retained by the magisterial Reformers on scriptural grounds — Luther, Calvin, and Zwingli all professed Mary ever-virgin — which is difficult to reconcile with the claim that the plain sense of Scripture rules it out.

Assessment

Weak. As a disproof of the perpetual virginity the “a never-consummated marriage is unbiblical” objection does not hold on its best available case. Its two supports both give way: 1 Corinthians 7 forbids only unilateral deprivation and expressly permits abstinence by consent while positively commending celibacy, and the premise that consummation is what makes a marriage is denied by the classical account of marriage-by-consent (Augustine, with Epiphanius and Chromatius) worked out on this very case — where the word “wife” names the covenant, not the marriage-bed, and Matthew 1:25 itself records a real marriage begun without consummation.

What the argument legitimately shows is narrower and worth keeping: a Josephite marriage is not the scriptural norm, and the perpetual virginity does require an extraordinary marital arrangement for which Scripture offers no positive model — a fair observation that its defenders should own rather than deny. But that is a point about unusualness, not about biblical prohibition; possibility is not disproved by unusualness. The proponents’ overreach is to press “this is bad for marriage… that’s not how it works” (Winger) and “his wife in every sense of the word” (GotQuestions) as if they settled the matter — treating pastoral counsel as a universal law and consummation as constitutive of the marriage bond, both of which the argument above shows to be more than the texts will bear.
