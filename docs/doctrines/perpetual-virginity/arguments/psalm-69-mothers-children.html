Does Psalm 69:8’s “my mother’s children” prove Mary bore other children?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — the proof-text’s strength is real (Psalm 69 is among the most messianically-quoted psalms, and “mother’s children,” not “father’s children,” would — if it spoke of Christ — cut against the cousin and stepbrother readings alike), but it over-reads the genre: Psalm 69 is a Davidic lament of the suffering righteous, fulfilled in Christ only in the clauses the New Testament actually applies to him — and v. 5, “thou knowest my foolishness; and my sins are not hid from thee,” cannot be predicated of the sinless Christ at all, so the psalm cannot be read verse-by-verse as his first-person speech; the reading needs exactly that, and it adds nothing the plain “brethren” texts do not already give · advanced by Koinonia House and John MacArthur

Related arguments: the later-children question — see also Were the brethren Mary’s own children? — the natural (Helvidian) reading · Do “till she brought forth” and “firstborn son” imply Mary bore other children? · Does the two-word Greek “until” (heōs hou) of Matthew 1:25 imply Mary bore other children? · Does Jesus entrusting Mary to John (John 19:26–27) show she had no other sons?

The claim

“I am become a stranger unto my brethren, and an alien unto my mother’s children.”… This verse also teaches the virgin birth of Christ. A very strange phrase for the psalmist to use is “…and an alien unto my mother’s children”—not His father’s children because Joseph was not His blood father; His siblings were half-brothers and half-sisters.

— Koinonia House, “The Life of Christ in Psalm 69”.1 The argument runs in three steps. Where “brother” (adelphos) is lexically elastic and so open to the cousin- or stepbrother-reading (weighed at “Does adelphos mean cousin?”), Psalm 69:8 is said to be unambiguous: it names “my mother’s children.” And Psalm 69 is messianic — v. 4 (“they that hate me without a cause”) is applied to Jesus in John 15:25, and v. 9 (“the zeal of thine house hath eaten me up”) in John 2:17. So, the argument concludes, v. 8 speaks of Christ too, making the children Mary bore after him explicit and unavoidable — and “mother’s children,” not “father’s,” is the decisive word: it forecloses not only the cousin-reading but the Epiphanian stepbrother-reading, on which the brethren are Joseph’s sons by a former wife (his children, not Mary’s).

Who advances it

The reading is a staple of Reformed and evangelical anti-Marian apologetics. John MacArthur presses it in his Luke commentary, arguing that the “brothers” of Jesus cannot be cousins or stepbrothers because the psalm’s term names the Messiah’s mother’s sons;2 Chuck Missler’s Koinonia House makes the same case verse by verse, treating “mother’s children” as a remez (a deliberate hint) that at once teaches the virgin birth and names Mary’s later offspring.1 It is a proof-text of the same family as the infancy-language and two-word “until” arguments — a single verse enlisted to carry what the plain “brethren” texts are thought to leave open.

The evidence for

Psalm 69 really is heavily messianic. This is the argument’s genuine strength, and it should not be understated. After Psalm 22 it is among the most-quoted psalms in the New Testament, and its clauses are applied to Christ across the Gospels and Paul: “they hated me without a cause” (v. 4) in John 15:25; “the zeal of thine house hath eaten me up” (v. 9a) in John 2:17; “the reproaches of them that reproached thee are fallen upon me” (v. 9b) in Romans 15:3; and the gall and vinegar of v. 21 in the Passion narratives (Matthew 27:34, 48).3 A reader who grants that v. 8 sits between two verses (4 and 9) both quoted of Christ has a real prima-facie case that it too describes him.

The “mother’s” precision is pointed. If v. 8 does speak of Christ, its steelman is sharper than the bare “brethren” texts. “An alien unto my mother’s children” specifies the shared parent as the mother, and the estrangement it names is exactly what the Gospels record: “neither did his brethren believe in him” (John 7:5). Read so, the verse would tell against both rival readings at once — against the cousins (not children of his mother at all) and against the Epiphanian stepbrothers (Joseph’s sons, his father’s children, not his mother’s) — leaving only the Helvidian sons of Mary. That is the strongest form of the argument, and it is the form assessed here.

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The messianic character of Psalm 69 is not in doubt, and the observation that “mother’s children” would, if predicated of Christ, exclude the stepbrother as well as the cousin is a fair one — a Joseph’s-former-son is not Mary’s child. The verse is at least consistent with the Helvidian reading, and its estrangement-motif matches John 7:5.

Where it over-reads the genre. The argument stands or falls on treating Psalm 69 as Christ’s own first-person speech verse by verse — and that premise is false. Psalm 69 is a lament of the suffering righteous in David’s voice, fulfilled in Christ typologically and in the particular clauses the New Testament applies to him, not as a continuous first-person oracle every line of which transfers. The decisive tell is internal: v. 5 — “O God, thou knowest my foolishness; and my sins are not hid from thee” — cannot be true of the sinless Christ.4 The New Testament’s own use confirms the selective pattern: it lifts vv. 4, 9, 21 as fulfilled in Christ, but applies the imprecation of v. 25 (“let their habitation be desolate”) to Judas — Christ’s betrayer, not Christ (Acts 1:20).3 A psalm one of whose verses is fulfilled in the traitor and another of which confesses sin is plainly not a line-by-line prophecy of the Lord; to isolate v. 8 as literal-of-Christ while quietly exempting v. 5 is selective literalism — the proof-text proves too much.

Even granting v. 8 to Christ, the precision is David’s. On the typological reading the literal speaker is David, whose “mother’s children” are David’s own brothers; what the messianic fulfilment carries over is the pattern — the righteous sufferer rejected by his own kin (fulfilled in John 7:5) — not the genealogical detail of whose mother is meant. The phrase’s force as a datum about Mary is thus borrowed, not given. And the Hebrew idiom cuts the other way besides: “my mother’s son(s)” (ben immi) is a standing Semitic intensifier for one’s closest kin — the full brother as against the half-brother by another wife — Joseph calling Benjamin “his brother… his mother’s son” (Genesis 43:29), the bride’s “mother’s children” who were angry with her (Song of Solomon 1:6).5 The idiom presupposes a shared father too and simply heightens “brethren” to nearest kin; it does not encode the modern point “same mother, different father” that the argument needs to read out of it.

And it adds nothing. That Jesus had “brethren” who were estranged from him is already plain and undisputed (John 7:5); the whole question is whether they were Mary’s own sons or Joseph’s by an earlier marriage — and a poetic parallel in a Davidic lament, whose premises forbid reading it as literal Christ-speech, cannot settle it. The positive weight, if any, must still come from the plain “brethren” of the Gospels (weighed at “Were the brethren Mary’s own children?”), where it is judged the natural but unproven default — not from Psalm 69:8.

Assessment

Weak. As an interpretation the Psalm 69:8 proof-text does not hold on its best available case. Its premise — that a messianic psalm may be read as Christ’s first-person speech clause by clause — is refuted by the psalm itself (v. 5’s confession of sin; the traitor’s inheritance of v. 25 at Acts 1:20), so the inference from “Psalm 69 is messianic” to “v. 8 predicates Mary’s children of Jesus” is invalid. Even conceded, the verse yields only the estrangement the Gospels already report, while its “mother’s children” precision belongs literally to David and idiomatically means simply full-brother rather than “Mary’s son by a different father.” The argument is weak — a genre over-read — and the Helvidian reading it serves must rest, as it does, on the plain “brethren” texts, where it stands as the natural but unproven default (contested), not on this psalm.
