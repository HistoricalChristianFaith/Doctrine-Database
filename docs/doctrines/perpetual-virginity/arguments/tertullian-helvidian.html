Is Tertullian the earliest Helvidian — did he teach that Mary bore children after Jesus?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — two claims must be kept apart. That Tertullian (c. 210) denied Mary a virginity that outlasted the birth — the virginitas in partu — is secure and uncontested: he says in so many words that she “was a wife when she brought forth her son,” her womb “opened” as in any childbirth. But the further, load-bearing claim — that he positively held the Helvidian reading, that the “brethren of the Lord” were Mary’s own children born after Jesus — is an inference from his anti-Marcionite polemic, not a statement he ever makes, and it is genuinely disputed. J. B. Lightfoot judged it “highly probable”; Catholic scholars (Blinzler, McHugh) read the same texts as touching only the manner of the birth, leaving the brethren’s parentage open. Much turns on the verdict: Richard Bauckham makes the whole antiquity of the debate hinge on it — the Epiphanian reading is “the only view positively attested in Christian tradition before Tertullian, if Tertullian is correctly interpreted as holding the Helvidian view, or before the late fourth century, if he is not.” No clear winner — strong by the plain-sense reading most modern exegetes take, resisted by the harmonizers · the Helvidian reading of Tertullian given its sharpest statement by J. B. Lightfoot; the dispute flagged by Richard Bauckham

The two claims, kept apart

It is easy to run together two things Tertullian says, and the whole question turns on prising them apart. He treats the birth of Christ in two registers — the manner of Mary’s childbearing, and the identity of the brethren — and he is explicit on the first, silent (or only suggestive) on the second.

  • The manner of the birth (explicit). Against heretics who played on the paradox “a virgin and yet not a virgin,” Tertullian draws the line himself: “although she was a virgin when she conceived, she was a wife when she brought forth her son. Now, as a wife, she was under the very law of ‘opening the womb’… becoming a mother at a leap, as it were, before she was a wife.”1 This is a flat denial of the virginitas in partu — the miraculous, seal-preserving birth that the Protevangelium was already teaching in his century. On this point there is no dispute: even his critics grant that Tertullian denied a virginity surviving the birth.
  • The identity of the brethren (inferred). Pressing the reality of Christ’s human birth against Marcion and Apelles, Tertullian insists the mother and brethren who “stood without” were truly His: “if… He made them ‘His mother and His brethren’ who were not so, how could He deny them these relationships who really had them?”2 He never adds whose children the brethren were. That they were Mary’s own is a conclusion drawn from the argument, not a thing he asserts.

So the secure minimum is that Tertullian was not a witness for the developed perpetual virginity (which includes the in partu). The contested surplus is whether he was a positive witness for the specifically Helvidian account of the brethren.

The case that he is Helvidian (Lightfoot)

J. B. Lightfoot set Tertullian at the head of his conspectus of the “sons of Joseph and Mary” authorities and judged the Helvidian reading of him “highly probable.” His grounds are cumulative:

The brethren-argument implies blood-brotherhood. Tertullian’s reply to Marcion turns entirely on the brethren being real relations of the same order as the mother: his argument, Lightfoot wrote, “seems to imply that the Lord’s brethren were His brothers in the same sense in which Mary was His mother.”3 The natural, plain-sense reading of “brother” is the one his polemic leans on, and it is the reading the Epiphanian and Hieronymian theories were later devised to avoid.

He affirms what the Epiphanian device denies. The Epiphanian escape — the brethren are Joseph’s sons by a former wife — was in circulation in Tertullian’s own day, and an ascetic with his exaltation of continence had every motive to reach for it. He does not. He nowhere mentions a prior marriage of Joseph; instead he makes Mary a true wife who bore in the ordinary way and, in the Montanist tract On Monogamy, holds up her single marriage as the very model of the once-wed: it was “a virgin, about to marry once for all after her delivery, who gave birth to Christ… a mother who was both virgin, and wife of one husband.”4 A Mary who is virgin before the delivery and a genuine univira after it has no room in Tertullian’s frame for the Epiphanian stepbrothers — which points the brethren back to her.

The reception confirms it. When Helvidius argued at Rome (c. 383) that Mary bore other children he claimed Tertullian as a predecessor, and Jerome, replying, did not deny the appeal on the merits — he could only impugn Tertullian’s churchmanship: “of Tertullian I say no more than that he was not a man of the Church.”5 Everywhere else in that reply Jerome answers testimony with counter-testimony; here he has none, and waves the witness away. That silence, Lightfoot urged, is telling: “It is therefore highly probable that he held the Helvidian view. Such an admission from one who was so strenuous an advocate of asceticism is worthy of notice.”3 Bauckham notes in passing that most modern exegetes treat the Helvidian reading as “virtually self-evident” — so on this side of the ledger the reading is not eccentric but mainstream.6

The case that he is not (Blinzler, McHugh)

Against this, Josef Blinzler and John McHugh — both defending the perpetual virginity — read Tertullian’s evidence as far less decisive, and Bauckham flags their dissent as a live one.7 The dissenting case rests on the same gap between the two claims:

His explicit statements concern only the birth, not the brethren. Where Tertullian speaks directly of Mary’s virginity (On the Flesh of Christ 23; On Monogamy 8), his target is the manner of childbearing — the virginitas in partu — against heretics who used the “a virgin and not a virgin” paradox to deny a real human birth. As Lightfoot himself conceded, “the more distinct passages… relate to the virginity only.” That a woman’s womb was “opened” in bearing her first child does not, of itself, say she bore a second.

The brethren-argument needs them only real, not Mary’s. Tertullian’s anti-Marcionite point requires the brethren to be genuine kin of the flesh — but the Epiphanian reading supplies exactly that. Stepbrothers, Joseph’s sons reared in the one household, are “brethren” as truly as any blood-brother for the purposes of Tertullian’s reply to Marcion; the argument works whether they were Mary’s sons or Joseph’s by an earlier marriage. So the passage that looks Helvidian does not in fact discriminate between the Helvidian and Epiphanian readings — it tells only against Marcion’s Docetism and against the cousin-theory (which makes the brethren no household-kin at all). The specifically Helvidian conclusion is therefore read into Tertullian from the plain sense of “brother,” not read out of anything he states.

The argument from Jerome’s silence proves less than it seems. Jerome’s exchange with Helvidius turned on the virginity of the mother, not on the precise relationship of the brethren; that he could not gainsay Helvidius’ appeal to Tertullian shows Tertullian denied the perpetual virginity, which no one contests — not necessarily that Tertullian had taught the brethren were Mary’s sons. Lightfoot granted as much: the usual assumption, “though probable, is not absolutely certain.”

Adversarial assessment

What holds on both sides. The uncontested floor is firm: Tertullian denied the virginitas in partu and made Mary a true wife who bore in the ordinary way — he is no witness for the developed perpetual virginity, and cannot be enlisted as one. Above that floor, Lightfoot’s Helvidian reading is the stronger single reading, and for a real reason the dissent must absorb: Tertullian, who knew the Epiphanian device and had every ascetic motive to use it, conspicuously did not, and affirmed instead the one thing the device exists to deny — that Mary became a genuine wife. His silence about a former marriage of Joseph is not neutral; it is the silence of a man to whom that solution had not occurred or did not appeal.

What keeps it from being settled. But the dissent lands one blow the Helvidian case cannot fully parry: the brethren-argument, taken by itself, needs the brethren real, not Mary’s, and the Epiphanian reading meets that need. So the Helvidian conclusion is an inference — a very natural one, from the plain sense of “brother” joined to Tertullian’s refusal of the Epiphanian escape — but an inference, not a datum. Tertullian never wrote the sentence “the brethren were Mary’s sons,” and a determined harmonizer can read him as denying only the manner of the birth while leaving the brethren’s parentage formally open. Neither side can compel the other from the text alone.

Why it matters, and how far. The stake is not small. Bauckham makes the antiquity of the whole Epiphanian–Helvidian question hinge precisely here: if Tertullian is Helvidian, then before him the Epiphanian stepbrother reading stands as “the only view positively attested in Christian tradition”; if he is not, the earliest positive attestation of either the Helvidian denial runs down to the late fourth century (Helvidius, the Antidicomarianites). Yet the verdict cuts less than the framing suggests: whichever way Tertullian is read, he is no support for the developed perpetual virginity, and the earlier witnesses that are positively Epiphanian (the Protevangelium, the Gospel of Peter, Clement) stand regardless. What turns on the Tertullian question is the narrower matter of when the Helvidian reading first surfaces in a named writer — and there the honest answer is that it depends on a contested reading of one ambiguous father.

Assessment

Contested. Keep the two claims apart and the verdict follows. That Tertullian denied Mary a virginity outlasting the birth is secure — his own words settle it, and even his critics concede it — so he is firmly no witness for the perpetual virginity in its developed (in partu) form. That he positively taught the Helvidian reading of the brethren is the more probable reading of him but remains an inference: strong by the plain sense of “brother” and his pointed refusal of the Epiphanian device (Lightfoot’s “highly probable”), resisted by scholars who note that his explicit statements touch only the manner of the birth and that his brethren-argument needs them merely real, which the Epiphanian reading equally supplies (Blinzler, McHugh). No clear winner, and Bauckham treats the question as genuinely open — so, on the specifically Helvidian claim, contested. The wiki accordingly keeps Tertullian’s detail page and his place on the timeline while marking the Helvidian identification as probable-but-disputed rather than assured.
