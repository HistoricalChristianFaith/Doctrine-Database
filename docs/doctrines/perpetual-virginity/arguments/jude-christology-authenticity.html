Does the Epistle of Jude’s christological vocabulary — the title “Master” (despotēs), tied to the desposynoi — support the letter’s authenticity, that it was written by Jude the Lord’s brother?

Type: historical / lexical argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — Richard Bauckham argues that the four christological titles current in the circle of Jesus’ relatives — Messiah, the Lord, our Lord, and Master (despotēs) — all appear in the short letter of Jude, and that the last is a lexical fingerprint of that circle. Jude 4 calls Christ despotēs (“our only Master and Lord Jesus Christ”) — a christological usage otherwise unattested in extant early Christian literature before the later second century, the one other first-century instance (2 Peter 2:1) being dependent on Jude—and this is “only explicable” because the relatives of Jesus were known as οἱ δεσπόσυνοι, “the Master’s people” (Julius Africanus ap. Eusebius, H.E. i.7.14): ho despotēs was current for Jesus precisely in the kin-circle where the desposynoi were leaders. So the letter’s own vocabulary carries the idiom of the relatives’ circle — evidence for its authenticity. That the letter is early and Palestinian, and even tied to the kin-circle’s idiom, is well-grounded; but the payoff presupposes the authenticity it is offered for — a writer of that same circle in the next generation could share the idiom, so the vocabulary fixes the letter’s milieu more securely than its author (though the despotēs datum, having no model in extant literature, is harder to fake than generic Palestinian coloring). And even granting authenticity, the point is neutral between the Helvidian and Epiphanian readings and does not by itself touch Mary’s virginity · argued from Richard Bauckham

The claim

Like its companions from Jude’s learned exegesis and his knowledge of the Testament of Moses, this argument reaches the literal-kinship case for the brethren through the letter’s authenticity: its author names himself “brother of James” (Jude 1), so a genuine Jude who could write it is a real member of the family group the Gospels name — James, Joses, Jude, Simon (Mark 6:3). Where the exegesis argument reads authenticity off Jude’s method and the Testament argument off his knowledge of a source, this one reads it off Jude’s christological vocabulary. The case is Richard Bauckham’s — himself holding the perpetual virginity on the Epiphanian reading.

Four titles current in the relatives’ circle — and all four appear in Jude. From the reliable evidence about the relatives of Jesus, Bauckham argues, we can already recover the christological titles used in their circle, quite apart from the letter. Four were certainly current: (1) Messiah (Χριστός) — used, since they were “well-known for their claim to Davidic ancestry,” of Jesus the royal Messiah of David’s house; (2) the Lord (ὁ κύριος) — presupposed by the fact that the brothers were themselves known as “the brothers of the Lord” (1 Cor 9:5; Gal 1:19; Hegesippus); (3) our Lord — the Aramaic invocation preserved as Maranatha (“Our Lord, come!”; 1 Cor 16:22); and (4) Master (δεσπότης) — presupposed by the designation οἱ δεσπόσυνοι for the relatives (Julius Africanus ap. Eusebius, H.E. i.7.14). “All four of these christological titles — and only these four — occur in the letter of Jude.” Because despotēs and desposynoi are distinctive precisely as Greek words, the circle had “its own Greek christological terminology”: the titles in Jude are not mere translations but the actual usage of the brothers’ bilingual circle.1

The load-bearing datum: despotēs. The first three titles in Jude are unremarkable; the fourth is striking. Christological despotēs is otherwise unattested in extant early Christian literature before the later second century — up to the mid-second century the word is used “almost exclusively of God the Father” — and Jude 4 with the dependent 2 Peter 2:1 are “the only extant examples before the second half of the second century” (the next are Melito, the Martyrdom of Justin [Rec. B], and Clement of Alexandria). Yet the term desposynoi for the relatives, Bauckham argues, “is only explicable if ho despotēs was commonly used to refer to Jesus in Palestinian Jewish Christianity.” So Jude’s use of the title, “though exceptional by comparison with other extant Christian literature of the first century, probably reflects a usage current in the circles in which the relatives of Jesus were leaders” — “an impressive, but neglected indication of his letter’s origin in those Palestinian Christian circles in which the desposynoi were leaders.”2

What the title meant — the messianic Ruler, and his royal family. In Jude 4 the phrase is emphatic: ton monon despotēn kai kyrion hēmōn Iēsoun Christon, “our only Master and Lord Jesus Christ.” Bauckham revises his own earlier (1983) view that despotēs here evoked Jesus as master of the household slaves — the sense 2 Peter 2:1 gives it in taking the word over from Jude. That nuance may be secondary, but is “rather unlikely to have been the primary meaning”: the term desposynoi for the relatives “is easier to understand if despotēs described Jesus as the messianic Ruler and desposynoi therefore attributed to his relatives the dignity of a royal family.” The best rendering is “our only Sovereign and Lord,” despotēs and kyrios being alternative Greek renderings of a single Aramaic māranā’, with the same divine overtone (the emphatic “only” evoking “the special Jewish insistence on the unique lordship of God”). This dovetails with the well-attested claim of Jesus’ kin to Davidic descent (Eusebius, H.E. iii.20; iii.32).3

On this footing Bauckham draws the authenticity inference: the letter’s vocabulary is not that of the wider first-century church but that of the kin-circle, and so tells for the letter’s origin there. The interpretation weighed here is the one this datum is adduced to support: the Epistle of Jude’s distinctive christological vocabulary — above all the title despotēs, presupposing the living desposynoi usage of the relatives’ circle — shows that it was really written by Jude, the brother of James and of the Lord, and not a later pseudepigraph.4

What the argument establishes

1. The vocabulary is early, Palestinian, and specifically the kin-circle’s. This is the solid core, and it is largely independent of who physically wrote the letter. The convergence Bauckham traces is real: the four titles reconstructible for the relatives’ circle from independent evidence (Paul, Hegesippus, Julius Africanus) are exactly the four that appear in Jude, and the rarest of them — christological despotēs — is otherwise absent from first-century usage yet is presupposed by the very name (desposynoi) by which the relatives were known. That is not generic “Palestinian coloring” a forger might sprinkle on; it is a specific lexical tie to a named group, and it points where Bauckham says it does — to the bilingual circle in which the desposynoi were leaders. Whoever wrote Jude wrote from within, or in the idiom of, that circle. A late, Hellenistic, Gentile-Christian forgery is not what this vocabulary looks like; the argument rules that class of hypothesis out.

2. The despotēs datum is harder to fake than the milieu-arguments’ are. The exegetical method and the Testament of Moses a skilled imitator of the next generation could reproduce by sharing the same living culture. The christological despotēs is a harder case for the forgery hypothesis on its own terms: since the usage is unattested elsewhere in the first century and does not appear again until the later second, a pseudepigrapher had no literary model to copy — he would have had to know the circle’s spoken idiom directly, from the currency of desposynoi itself. This makes the vocabulary a genuinely weightier pointer toward an insider origin than the milieu-arguments taken alone.

What it does not establish

3. Kin-circle vocabulary fixes the circle, not the man. Even granting all the above, the argument outruns its evidence at the same joint its companions do. That the letter speaks the idiom of the desposynoi’ circle shows the kind of writer and community that produced it; it does not by itself show that the writer was Jude the Lord’s brother in particular, rather than another member or associate of that circle — who would share the same living usage — writing in the generation after Jude in the name of a revered kinsman. The point that a forger had no literary model for despotēs tells against a distant imitator, but not against a writer within the circle, for whom the idiom was native speech, not something to be copied out of books. So the vocabulary narrows the origin impressively — more tightly than the method or the source-knowledge do — without singling out the author within that origin. Bauckham’s inference is sound if the letter is authentic, and it is offered in a study that argues authenticity on other grounds (the survey of the pseudepigraphy debate; the author’s conspicuous refusal of the title “brother of the Lord”); but the vocabulary weighs toward authenticity by removing the objection that Jude is too late or too Hellenistic to be genuine, rather than carrying the author’s identity on its own. The authorship of Jude remains a genuinely disputed question in the scholarship.

4. The kin-circle facts it leans on are already secure — the fresh step is the inference. The desposynoi designation (Julius Africanus), the relatives’ Davidic-descent claims (Eusebius), and their place as “brothers of the Lord” are not new here; they are weighed already under Jude’s grandsons and the married-missionary brothers of 1 Corinthians 9:5, and are well-grounded. What this chapter adds is not those facts but the argument that Jude’s own christological terminology is a fingerprint of that circle. The strength of the argument therefore rides entirely on that inferential step — and the step, however elegant, moves from a shared vocabulary to a particular author, which is exactly where the milieu-versus-man gap reopens.

5. Even granting authenticity, it is neutral for the doctrine. Suppose the argument succeeds and the letter is Jude’s own. Like its companion authenticity arguments, it fixes only that Jude was a real brother of James and reputed brother of the Lord — a flesh-and-blood member of the family group — not whose son he was. On the Helvidian reading he is Mary’s own later son; on the Epiphanian reading he is Joseph’s son by a former wife, Jesus’ elder stepbrother; on both he is a genuine brother of James, fully capable of writing the letter. So authenticity presses against any picture of the brethren as unreal or merely figurative, but it lends the perpetual virginity no direct weight and says nothing of Mary’s childbearing.

Assessment

Contested. The argument has two parts, and they fare differently. Its foundation — that the four christological titles recoverable for the relatives’ circle are the four found in Jude, and that the rarest of them, christological despotēs, is otherwise unattested in the first century yet presupposed by the very name desposynoi — is well-grounded and locates the letter’s idiom securely in the kin-circle of Palestinian Jewish Christianity; it genuinely excludes the picture of Jude as a late, Hellenistic, Gentile forgery, and (unlike the method- and source-arguments) the despotēs datum is hard to fake, since a distant imitator had no literary model for it. But the conclusion the argument is offered for — that this establishes authorship by Jude the Lord’s brother — does not follow from the vocabulary alone: it fixes the letter’s milieu more tightly than its author, and a writer within the same circle, for whom the idiom was native speech, fits that milieu as well as the historical Jude does. Authorship of Jude is a genuinely disputed question, strong scholars ranged on both sides, and Bauckham’s inference presupposes the authenticity it is adduced to support rather than demonstrating it. Weighed honestly, the datum is positive, and somewhat weightier than its companions, but not decisive: it removes a leading objection and narrows the origin impressively without settling the identity of the author. Because the reading it serves — the letter’s authenticity — is itself unresolved on the wider evidence, with no clear winner, the honest verdict is contested.

Two limits keep the finding modest for this doctrine, and both are limits on what is shown. First, the argument bears on the letter’s authenticity, and touches Mary’s virginity only at second hand — through the reality of a Jude who could name himself “brother of James.” Second, even at full strength it is neutral between the Helvidian and Epiphanian readings, so it decides nothing between the literal readings of the brethren. What it contributes — alongside its exegetical and source-knowledge companions and the grandsons of Jude and married-missionary brothers — is that the Lord’s brethren, and the letter one of them left, belong to real first-century Palestinian history, not to a figure of speech; the choice among the readings, and so the perpetual virginity itself, is left to be settled on other grounds.
