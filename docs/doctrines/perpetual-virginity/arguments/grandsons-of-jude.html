Do the brethren’s traceable family line — Jude’s named grandsons — support reading “brother of the Lord” as literal kinship?

Type: historical-tradition argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: sound — the earliest historical witness on the Lord’s kindred, Hegesippus (c. 175, preserved by Eusebius), reports that two grandsons of Jude “the Lord’s brother according to the flesh” — named Zoker and James — were poor Galilean farmers who were examined as Davidides, released, and afterwards “ruled the churches” as relatives of the Lord, surviving until Trajan. Richard Bauckham strips off the Domitianic-persecution frame as apologetic embroidery but accepts the core as reliable: one of the four named “brethren” demonstrably married, had children, and left a family line remembered by name two generations on. That is a solid datum for reading “brother of the Lord” as a designation of literal kinship — real flesh-and-blood family, not a merely figurative or honorific title — and it dovetails with the married-missionary picture of 1 Corinthians 9:5. But it is neutral between the Helvidian and Epiphanian readings (a married Jude with descendants fits a full brother and a stepbrother alike), it tells only mildly against the Hieronymian, and it does not by itself touch Mary’s virginity · argued from Richard Bauckham, with the primary text in Eusebius

The claim

The Gospels name Jude (Judas) among the four “brethren of the Lord” (Mark 6:3 ∥ Matthew 13:55), but tell us nothing of what became of him or his family. The earliest historical tradition does. Hegesippus, a Palestinian Hebrew Christian who gathered the traditions of the Lord’s kindred around 175 and whose words survive only where Eusebius quotes them, reports that in the reign of Domitian two grandsons of Jude were still living. Eusebius sets down the story, quoting Hegesippus’ own words for its heart:

Of the family of the Lord there were still living the grandchildren of Jude, who is said to have been the Lord’s brother according to the flesh. Information was given that they belonged to the family of David, and they were brought to the Emperor Domitian by the Evocatus… And he asked them if they were descendants of David, and they confessed that they were. Then he asked them how much property they had, or how much money they owned. And both of them answered that they had only nine thousand denarii, half of which belonged to each of them… this property did not consist of silver, but of a piece of land which contained only thirty-nine acres, and from which they raised their taxes and supported themselves by their own labor. Then they showed their hands, exhibiting the hardness of their bodies and the callousness produced upon their hands by continuous toil as evidence of their own labor.

— Hegesippus, in Eusebius, Church History iii.20.1–5.1 Questioned about “Christ and his kingdom, of what sort it was and where and when it was to appear,” the two answered that it was “not a temporal nor an earthly kingdom, but a heavenly and angelic one, which would appear at the end of the world”; Domitian, “despising them as of no account, let them go.” And — the point that matters here — “when they were released they ruled the churches because they were witnesses and were also relatives of the Lord. And peace being established, they lived until the time of Trajan.”1 Eusebius takes up the same thread again at H.E. iii.32, resuming Hegesippus after the martyrdom of the Lord’s cousin Symeon: “there were also others, descended from one of the so-called brothers of the Saviour, whose name was Judas… They came, therefore, and took the lead of every church as witnesses and as relatives of the Lord.”2 Eusebius does not give the grandsons’ names, but an ancient summary of the same passage of Hegesippus does: “Hegesippus… says that one was called Zoker and the other James” — Zoker (Ζωκῆρ) being a Semitic hypocorism of Zechariah.3

The interpretation weighed here is the reading this datum is adduced to support: “brother of the Lord” names a literal family kinship — the brethren were real kin, one of whom (Jude) married and left a traceable line — and not a merely figurative, honorific, or spiritualized “brother.”

The evidence — what survives the adversarial strip-down

The story as Hegesippus tells it carries obvious embroidery, and the case must be built only on what remains once that is removed. Richard Bauckham — himself holding the perpetual virginity on the Epiphanian reading, and no friend of inflating the tradition — does exactly this strip-down, and it is worth following him because it keeps the argument honest.

Set aside as apologetic or hagiographical: the framing that the interview caused Domitian to end a persecution of the church is doubtful — the grandsons were accused as Davidides, not as Christians (the charge fits the Vespasianic hunt for the house of David, H.E. iii.12), so the connection to a Domitianic persecution “seems historically gratuitous.” The credal little speech about Christ’s heavenly kingdom “coming in glory to judge the quick and the dead” is a string of conventional early-Christian formulae, “stereotyped, almost credal,” so “we can scarcely claim to have access to their own views.” And the tidy terminus “until Trajan” serves Hegesippus’ scheme of the church’s pristine age closing with the apostolic generation.4

Accepted as reliable memory: what is left is not the frame but the family. That Jude the Lord’s brother had grandsons living in Palestine in the 90s; that they were poor working farmers — “that Zoker and James were poor farmers is no doubt accurately remembered”; that their smallholding was a specific 39 plethra (about 29 acres) valued at 9,000 denarii, “so precisely stated that perhaps this too is accurate tradition”, the inherited family property whose size was still known in the second-century Palestinian Jewish-Christian circles that preserved the story; and that they and their kin held leadership in the Galilean and Palestinian churches. This last coheres with the wider evidence for the desposynoi (“those who belong to the family of the Lord”): a recognized pattern of leadership passing within Jesus’ family — James, then his cousin Symeon at Jerusalem, then Jude’s grandsons “found presiding over the churches” — that is “unlikely to be Hegesippus’ creation,” resting on the Jewish-Christian tradition behind him.4

The reliable core is independently corroborated. Julius Africanus, drawing on a Palestinian Jewish-Christian source, described the desposynoi travelling as missionaries from their home villages of Nazareth and Kokhaba — and, Bauckham argues, “the reference is to the same missionary travels which are presupposed as well-known by Paul in 1 Corinthians 9:5,” where Paul takes it for granted that “the brothers of the Lord” travelled as married missionaries, accompanied by believing wives.5 A married Jude who left grandchildren is exactly what that datum, decades earlier and from a wholly independent quarter, would lead one to expect.

The same Palestinian tradition remembered the family not merely as kin but as self-conscious Davidides, carrying a real blood-pedigree. Gathering the three strands of his study, Bauckham concludes that the notion “that Jesus was first considered the messianic ‘Son of David’ without reference to his ancestry must now be laid to rest. We must now see the family of Jesus as Davidides, conscious, through family tradition, of the hopes of Davidic restoration which had been cherished in their line since Zerubbabel. Their insignificant social status would have made the family tradition of this potent ancestry no less tenacious or significant.”6 That bears directly on the point at issue. A transmitted family genealogy — the very pedigree Julius Africanus says the desposynoi expounded on their travels — presupposes a real blood-line; a merely figurative or honorific “brotherhood” carries no such descent. A family that knew and taught its own line from David through Zerubbabel is a family with literal kin, and the datum reinforces, from a further angle, the same literal-kinship anchor. It sits more awkwardly still with the Hieronymian tendency to dissolve the brethren into the Clopas cousin-kindred, since Hegesippus keeps the Davidic brethren and their traceable line distinct from that cousin. It remains, like the rest, neutral between the Helvidian and Epiphanian readings, and silent about Mary’s virginity.

What the argument does — and does not — establish

1. It anchors the brethren as literal, married, reproducing kin. This is the argument’s real force. Whatever the exact relationship the word “brother” encodes, the “brethren of the Lord” were not a figure of speech: at least one of them married, fathered children, and left a line his second-century descendants could name and locate on a known farm. Any reading that dissolves the brethren into a purely spiritual or honorific “brotherhood” — a fellowship-title rather than a family-tie — founders on a remembered family line. On the point it is actually adduced for, the datum holds.

2. It is neutral between the Helvidian and Epiphanian readings. Literal kinship is common ground between them. On the Helvidian reading Jude is Mary’s own son; on the Epiphanian reading he is Joseph’s son by a former wife and Jesus’ elder stepbrother — and on both he is a real member of the household who could marry and have children. Jude’s traceable family therefore cannot, by itself, decide whether the brethren were the Virgin’s children or Joseph’s earlier ones. It establishes that the brethren were literal kin, not which kinship they bore.

3. It does not by itself touch Mary’s virginity, and tells only mildly against the Hieronymian. A married stepbrother Jude with descendants is fully compatible with Mary’s perpetual virginity; equally, a married full brother would be compatible only with its denial. The datum is silent as between these, because it speaks to Jude’s marriage, not Mary’s childbearing. Against the Hieronymian cousin-theory its weight is real but limited: cousins marry and have children too, so a fertile Jude does not refute Jerome. What it does is reinforce, from the same Palestinian tradition, the picture in which the “brethren” are a remembered family group with their own descendants — kept distinct, in Hegesippus, from the Lord’s cousin Symeon son of Clopas — so that the tendency of the cousin-reading to collapse the brethren into the Clopas kindred sits awkwardly with the evidence. That is a supporting consideration for the cousin-theory arguments, not an independent refutation.

Assessment

Sound. On the interpretation it is actually offered for — that “brother of the Lord” is a designation of literal family kinship — the argument is well-grounded and unrefuted, and rests on the strongest kind of evidence this whole question affords: the earliest historical (as opposed to legendary) witness, a Palestinian who gathered the family’s own traditions, reporting a named, dated, geographically-located family line, with a critic of the tradition’s excesses (Bauckham) vouching for the core after cutting away the frame. The single fixed point — that Jude the Lord’s brother married and left grandchildren who led the Galilean churches — converges with Paul’s independent, decades-earlier assumption that the Lord’s brothers travelled as married missionaries (1 Corinthians 9:5). Literal kinship is, besides, the common reading of all three historic solutions; the datum simply confirms it against the one reading it excludes, the purely-figurative “brother.”

Two limits keep the finding modest for this doctrine, and they are limits on what is shown, not defects in the evidence. First, the datum is neutral between the Helvidian and Epiphanian readings — a married, fertile Jude fits a full brother and a stepbrother equally — so it lends the perpetual virginity no direct weight, and does not touch Mary’s virginity at all, which turns on her childbearing and not on her sons’ or stepsons’ marriages. Second, its bite against the Hieronymian cousin-theory is supporting, not decisive: cousins marry too, and the traceable family line reinforces the “brethren distinct from the cousin” picture without disproving Jerome. The honest verdict, then: a sound datum for the literal kinship of the brethren — and one more brick in the historically-real portrait of the Lord’s family that the extra-canonical tradition preserves — but one that leaves the choice among the three literal readings, and so the perpetual virginity itself, to be settled on other grounds.
