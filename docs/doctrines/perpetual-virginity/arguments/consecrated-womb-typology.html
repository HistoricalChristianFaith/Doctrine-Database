Is Mary’s perpetual virginity positively grounded in the “consecrated womb / new tomb” typology and the shut gate of Ezekiel 44:2 — not merely in ascetic devaluation of marriage?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: plausible — a coherent, ancient, genuinely non-ascetic rationale of fittingness, with a firsthand spine in Origen and a broad patristic proof-text in Ezekiel 44:2, but a congruence-argument on an accommodated text: it adorns and corroborates the doctrine rather than proving it, and bears most directly on the virginity in the birth, not the after the birth at issue in the brethren debate · framed as a positive rationale by J. B. Lightfoot, on the typology of Origen, Ambrose, Augustine, and the fathers

The claim

…it is not altogether correct to represent this belief as suggested solely by the false asceticism of the early Church which exalted virginity at the expense of married life. It appears in fact to be due quite as much to another sentiment which the fathers fantastically expressed by a comparison between the conception and the burial of our Lord. As after death His body was placed in a sepulcher ‘wherein never man before was laid,’ so it seemed fitting that the womb consecrated by His presence should not thenceforth have borne any offspring of man. It may be added also, that the Epiphanian view prevailed especially in Palestine where there was less disposition than elsewhere to depreciate married life, and prevailed too at a time when extreme ascetic views had not yet mastered the Church at large.

— J. B. Lightfoot, The Brethren of the Lord, in the discussion of the Epiphanian theory, link.1 The claim is that the doctrine has a positive ground — a typology of fitness, that the womb which bore God, like the tomb in which He lay, should be one “wherein never man before was laid” — and is not merely the by-product of an ascetic depreciation of marriage. Its standard scriptural spine is the “shut gate” of Ezekiel 44:2 (“This gate shall be shut… because the Lord, the God of Israel, hath entered in by it”), read of Mary by the fathers almost with one voice.

Who advances it

The observation that the belief rests on a positive fittingness-sentiment, and “not… solely” on ascetic prejudice, is J. B. Lightfoot’s historical judgement. The typology itself is the fathers’. The womb/tomb comparison Lightfoot points to is stated firsthand and at length by Origen (c. 248), and the “shut gate” proof-text of Ezekiel 44:2 runs in a long, near-unbroken line — Jerome, Ambrose, Augustine, Cyril of Alexandria, Theodoret, John of Damascus, and, gathering them up in the West, Thomas Aquinas.

The evidence for

The firsthand womb/tomb parallel. Lightfoot’s “comparison between the conception and the burial of our Lord” is not his own conceit; Origen draws it explicitly in Against Celsus, reasoning from the three Evangelists’ care to note that the sepulchre was new and unused:

…it became him, who was unlike other dead people… and who was, so to speak, a new dead man, to be laid in a new and clean tomb, in order that, as his birth was purer than any other (because he was born not in the way of ordinary generation but of a virgin), his burial also might have the purity symbolically indicated in his body being deposited in a sepulcher that was new… quarried and hewn out of one rock, united together in all its parts.

— Origen, Against Celsus 2.69, on the new tomb “wherein was never man yet laid” (John 19:41; Luke 23:53).2 The birth “of a virgin” and the burial in the virgin tomb are set in deliberate parallel — the very fitness Lightfoot describes, articulated firsthand two centuries before him.

The shut gate of Ezekiel 44:2. The doctrine’s positive proof-text is the closed east gate of Ezekiel’s temple vision: “This gate shall be shut, it shall not be opened, and no man shall pass through it; because the Lord, the God of Israel, hath entered in by it: therefore it shall be shut.” From the third century the fathers read the gate as Mary and the Lord who passes through it as Christ — a reading so settled it became, as one summary page notes, “a Marian commonplace.” Jerome: “Some quite emphatically understand this closed gate… as the Virgin Mary, who remains a Virgin before and after childbirth.” Ambrose: “Who is this gate, if not Mary? Is it not closed because she is a virgin?” Augustine: “What means this closed gate in the House of the Lord, except that Mary is to be ever inviolate?… a virgin before His Birth, a virgin in His Birth, and a virgin after His Birth.” Theodoret: the words “refer to the womb of the Virgin, through which no one enters and from which no one departs other than the only one who is the Lord.” John of Damascus and, in the West, Aquinas (quoting Augustine) carry it to the high Middle Ages.3

Lightfoot’s two historical caveats. He adds that the Epiphanian (stepbrother) reading — the vehicle of the perpetual virginity in its earliest centuries — “prevailed especially in Palestine where there was less disposition than elsewhere to depreciate married life,” and “at a time when extreme ascetic views had not yet mastered the Church.” Both cut against reducing the belief to a hatred of marriage: it flourished first where marriage was least disparaged, and before the ascetic tide was at its height.1

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The historical core is sound. The fathers did ground the belief in a positive fittingness-typology and not merely in an ascetic devaluation of marriage: Origen supplies the womb/tomb parallel firsthand, the shut-gate reading of Ezekiel 44:2 is a genuinely broad and ancient patristic consensus (not a late or isolated fancy), and Lightfoot’s two caveats about Palestine and about the pre-ascetic timing are well-founded. As a coherent theological rationale for the doctrine — a reason the belief is congruent and not arbitrary — the typology stands, and this wiki does not discount a reading merely for being theological or for turning on the virgin birth’s own miracle.

What is overstated or imprecise. As a ground that would establish the doctrine, it does not reach proof, on three counts.

First, it is an argument from fittingness, not necessity. Lightfoot’s own words are “it seemed fitting”; Origen’s are “purity symbolically indicated”; Jerome introduces the gate as what “some quite emphatically understand.” That the womb which bore God should be kept inviolate is a congruence; it does not demonstrate that it was. Possibility and even seemliness are not proof.

Second, Ezekiel 44:2 on its plain sense is a prophecy of a literal outer gate in Ezekiel’s visionary temple, kept shut and reserved to “the prince” (Ezekiel 44:3); the Marian application is a patristic accommodation — hence Jerome’s hedge (“some… understand”) — not the text’s literal reference. It is corroboration read with the doctrine, not an independent datum that yields it.

Third, the fathers deploy the shut gate primarily for the virginity in the birth (the in partu — that the delivery left Mary physically intact: “the manner of His birth did not break the seals of virginity,” Ambrose), which is a distinct claim from the virginity after the birth (the post partum — that she bore no other children) that the whole “brethren of the Lord” debate actually turns on. Augustine and John of Damascus do stretch the one gate to cover all three moments (before, in, and after the birth), but that fuller reach is the doctrine read into the figure, not drawn from it. So even at full stretch the proof-text lands hardest on a neighbouring claim, and touches the disputed one only by extension.

Assessment

Plausible. The positive typological rationale is coherent, ancient, and genuinely more than ascetic prejudice — Lightfoot’s historical point is sound and Origen gives it a firsthand spine — and it is not ruled out. But it is a fittingness argument resting on an accommodated proof-text, so it adorns and corroborates the doctrine rather than establishing it; the doctrine must be carried, if at all, on the historical and exegetical grounds weighed in the other arguments (the identity of the brethren). And Ezekiel 44:2 bears most directly on the virginity in the birth, not the after-the-birth question the brethren debate contests. Coherent and venerable, short of demonstrative: plausible.
