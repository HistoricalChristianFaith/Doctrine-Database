Does Mary’s reply to Gabriel — “How shall this be, seeing I know not a man?” (Luke 1:34) — imply she had taken a vow of perpetual virginity?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — the reading is ancient (Augustine, Gregory of Nyssa), coherent, and became the common opinion of the medieval doctors, and it answers the betrothal puzzle with effort (Aquinas’s conditional-then-absolute vow); but it rests on four words, faces a live rival reading (astonishment at the manner of a virginal conception, or the messianic almah), and has little support in modern critical exegesis — where even the magisterium (John Paul II) retreats from “vow” to “the will to remain a virgin.” Its strong form (a formal, absolute juridical vow already sworn) overreaches; its modest form (a settled resolve of virginity that her question presupposes) is the defensible steelman — firsthand in Augustine and Gregory of Nyssa, weighed against Calvin

Related arguments: the vow of virginity — see also Does the present tense of “I know not a man” (Luke 1:34) prove a vow?

The claim

Mary is betrothed to Joseph (Luke 1:27) and is told she will conceive and bear a son (Luke 1:31) — that is, in the ordinary course, that she will begin a family with her husband. Yet she answers with a question that treats her virginity as an obstacle:

Then said Mary unto the angel, How shall this be, seeing I know not a man?

— Luke 1:34. On the ordinary reading — a betrothed woman who will in the normal course consummate her marriage — the question is puzzling: she of all people would know how a child is conceived. The argument infers that it makes sense only if Mary never intended to “know” Joseph — that she had already resolved, or vowed, to remain a virgin even within marriage. As the Catholic Answers tract puts it, “from the Church’s earliest days, as the Fathers interpreted this Bible passage, Mary’s question was taken to mean that she had made a vow of lifelong virginity, even in marriage,” so that “her question makes sense only if there was an apparent conflict between keeping a vow of virginity and acceding to the angel’s request.”1

Who advances it

The reading is old and weighty. It is stated firsthand by Gregory of Nyssa (a festal Homily on the Nativity, c. 386) and, as the locus classicus of the Latin West, by Augustine (On Holy Virginity 4, c. 401), whose sentence — “which assuredly she would not say, unless she had before vowed herself unto God as a virgin” — was quoted verbatim by Thomas Aquinas as the sed contra of the article he devotes to the question (Summa III, q. 28, a. 4). Between and after them a long train of theologians carried it — Bede, Bernard of Clairvaux (Homilies in Praise of the Virgin Mother IV), and, as Neal Flanagan’s survey documents, John Geometra, Sophronius, Eadmer, Rupert of Deutz, Hugh of St Victor, Duns Scotus, Suárez, Maldonatus, Petavius, Calmet, and Cornely — until, from the thirteenth century, the vow was the common opinion of the schools.2 In modern popular apologetics it is pressed by the Catholic Answers tract, by Tim Staples (“this question makes no sense unless Mary had a vow of virginity”), and by Stefano Manelli, who calls Mary’s reaction “inexplicable” on ordinary grounds.1 The argument is offered as corroboration of a doctrine already held on other grounds (the identity of the “brethren”), not as its sole foundation.

The evidence for

The exegetical logic. The force of the argument is the mismatch between what Mary is told (she will bear a son) and what she asks (how, since she “knows not a man”). A merely-betrothed woman who expected in due course to live with her husband had no occasion for the question: the announcement of motherhood would carry no perplexity at all. The perplexity arises, the argument holds, only if Mary did not reckon on ever “knowing” a man — if virginity was, for her, a settled purpose and not a passing circumstance.

Firsthand in Gregory of Nyssa. The oldest surviving statement of the reading draws exactly this inference. Preaching on the Nativity, Gregory sets Mary’s resolve against the angel’s announcement:

The Angel proclaims the glad tidings of the birth; but she cleaves to virginity, judging incorruption to be preferable to the appearance of the Angel, and neither does she disbelieve the Angel nor will she relinquish her resolve… This utterance of Mary is proof… For if she had been taken in marriage by Joseph, how would she have been surprised by him who announced that she was to bear a child, since she would herself have expected to become a mother according to the law of nature?

— Gregory of Nyssa, Homily on the Nativity of Christ, on Mary’s “I have refrained from knowing any man… How shall this be, seeing I know not a man?”3 Augustine states the same at a stroke: “How, says she, shall this be, seeing I know not a man? Which assuredly she would not say, unless she had before vowed herself unto God as a virgin” (Latin: Quod profecto non diceret, nisi Deo virginem se ante vovisse).4

The answer to the betrothal puzzle. The reading’s standing objection — why would a woman who had vowed virginity enter betrothal at all? — was felt and answered by its own proponents. Aquinas distinguishes two stages, so that the vow does not collide with a real marriage:

The Mother of God is not believed to have taken an absolute vow of virginity, before being espoused to Joseph, although she desired to do so, yet yielding her own will to God’s judgment. Afterwards, however, having taken a husband, according as the custom of the time required, together with him she took a vow of virginity.

— Aquinas, Summa III, q. 28, a. 4.5 On this account the resolve was conditional at first (deferring to God and to the Law, which honoured marriage and childbearing) and made absolute only with Joseph’s consent, after the espousal — a construction Bernard of Clairvaux dramatizes when he has Mary at the Annunciation torn between joy in the Son and grief for her vow.2 That the doctrine of a Josephite (never-consummated) marriage is itself not unbiblical is weighed separately on this timeline’s “is a celibate marriage unbiblical?” argument (assessment: weak), which removes the theological obstacle a prior resolve of virginity would otherwise face.

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The reading is genuinely ancient — not a late apologetic contrivance — firsthand in Gregory of Nyssa and Augustine, adopted by Aquinas, and the common opinion of the medieval theologians. Its exegetical intuition is real: on any reading that is not messianic (see below), Mary’s question does sit awkwardly unless virginity was for her a settled purpose, and the betrothal objection has a coherent answer in the conditional-vow construction. As a congruent gloss on Luke 1:34, harmonizing her question with the doctrine held on other grounds, it stands.

What is overstated. As a proof the argument overreaches on three counts, and the strong form of the claim — a formal, juridical, already-sworn vow — is the weakest part.

First, it rests on four words. The whole inferential weight falls on “seeing I know not a man,” a single clause whose sense is contested; a doctrine cannot be established, only illustrated, from so slender a base — which is why its careful proponents adduce it as corroboration, not foundation.

First-century plausibility tells against the formal vow. John Calvin pressed the objection at its sharpest:

The conjecture which some have drawn from these words, that she had formed a vow of perpetual virginity, is unfounded and altogether absurd. She would, in that case, have committed treachery by allowing herself to be united to a husband, and would have poured contempt on the holy covenant of marriage… it is an idle and unfounded supposition that a monastic life existed among the Jews.

— Calvin, on Luke 1:34.6 Calvin’s historical point has force: Second-Temple Judaism knew no institution of vowed lifelong celibacy for women, and a betrothal entered under a standing, absolute vow not to consummate would indeed be a strange act. (His further charge — that such a vow would be sinful — is answered by the classical theology of marriage-by-consent, weighed on the Josephite-marriage argument; and Calvin himself is no denier of the perpetual virginity, only of this inference to a vow.) The anachronism bites the strong, juridical form; the modest form — a personal resolve of virginity — is not so easily dismissed, but it is also not what “vow” strictly names.

Second, there is a live rival reading, so the claim that the question “makes no sense” otherwise fails. Cardinal Cajetan (16th c.) is reckoned the first Catholic exegete to depart from the vow reading, taking Mary’s question as astonishment not at marriage-in-general but at conceiving then and there, virginally, at that very moment — a reading of the manner, not a disclosure of a vow.7 A second modern rival reads Mary as understanding the angel to name her the virgin almah of Isaiah 7:14 — “how shall this messianic maternity be, since I have no husband?” — a question about the promised virgin-birth rather than about a resolve of continence. And the philology of the phrase itself — whether the Greek present “I know not” renders a Semitic idiom of permanent condition, or states only Mary’s virginity at that moment — is a distinct question, weighed on its own; the best treatments of that grammar find it shows only that Mary was a virgin then, not that she had vowed to remain one. Once a coherent alternative is on the table, the modern popularizers’ “makes no sense unless…” and “inexplicable” are overstatements: the verse does make sense on other readings.

Third, the magisterium itself has softened the claim. In his catechesis on Mary’s virginity, John Paul II grounds the perpetual virginity in continuity of intent — “there are no reasons for thinking that the will to remain a virgin, which Mary expressed at the moment of the Annunciation (cf. Lk 1:34), was then changed” — speaking of a will or resolution to remain a virgin, not a juridical vow.8 That retreat concedes the point: the text will bear a settled intention more readily than a sworn vow. And in modern critical scholarship the vow reading has little support, the majority preferring one of the rival readings above.9

An honest tally of the tradition, finally, is narrower than the roll of names suggests. As Flanagan grants (following Campana), one cannot marshal “a whole host of Fathers” for the vow: the strongest that the patristic record yields is that no Father denies it, while those who take up Luke 1:34 read it this way — a consensus of the few who discuss it, not of the many.2

Assessment

Contested. The two forms of the claim fare differently, and the fair verdict tracks the steelman rather than the popular overstatement. The strong form — that Luke 1:34 discloses a formal, absolute vow of perpetual virginity already sworn — is weak: it is anachronistic to a Judaism with no such institution, it strains the betrothal it must explain, and even its defenders reach it only by the two-stage construction that concedes no absolute vow preceded the espousal. The modest form — that Mary’s question presupposes a settled resolve or intention of virginity — is the defensible reading: it is ancient, firsthand in Gregory of Nyssa and Augustine, coherent, and, on any non-messianic construal of the verse, arguably the most natural way to account for her perplexity; it is the form the magisterium now prefers. But it is not decisive. It rests on a single contested clause, it competes with a coherent rival (astonishment at the manner of the conception, or the messianic almah), and it wins little assent in modern critical exegesis — strong by the lights of the theological tradition, weak by those of contemporary philology, with no clear winner between them. A genuine, corroborating illustration of the doctrine that falls short of proving it: contested.
