John Chrysostom on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 398 (the anchor here is his latest word on the question, the Commentary on Galatians, finished before he left Antioch for Constantinople in 398; his Homilies on Matthew, which give the earlier Epiphanian reading, belong to the same Antiochene presbyterate, c. 390. Chrysostom c. 347–407) · Wikipedia
Summary: Chrysostom marks the East’s point of transition. J. B. Lightfoot observed that he “wrote both before and after Jerome’s treatise had become generally known, and his expositions of the New Testament mark a period of transition.” In the homilies on the earlier books he is plainly Epiphanian: expounding Matthew 1:25 he defends Mary’s perpetual virginity and explains that the “brethren” are so called only in the same reputed sense in which Joseph was “supposed to be husband of Mary” — no blood-kin of Mary at all — and elsewhere he keeps James the Lord’s brother a figure apart, one of the unbelieving brethren of John 7:5 who became the first bishop of Jerusalem. But in the Commentary on Galatians (on 1:19) he turns: he calls James “the son of Cleophas, as the Evangelist does” — grafting Jerome’s cousins-identification onto the older scheme, and appearing to complete it by taking Clopas for Alphaeus. So he stands as a witness on both sides: the Epiphanian reading early, the Hieronymian late.

Claims & evidence

Early and Epiphanian: the brethren “reputed,” Mary ever-virgin (on Matthew 1:25)

In the fifth of his Homilies on Matthew (delivered at Antioch, c. 390), Chrysostom reaches the crux — Joseph “knew her not till she had brought forth her first-born son” — and, like Hilary before him, reads it as guarding the perpetual virginity rather than denying it. The word “till,” he argues, fixes only what was true before the birth and leaves the sequel to inference; and the sequel is that Mary bore no one else:

He hath here used the word “till,” not that thou shouldest suspect that afterwards he did know her, but to inform thee that before the birth the Virgin was wholly untouched by man… that which both was seen to be a consequence of the former statement, and was acknowledged, this in its turn he leaves for thee to perceive; namely, that not even after this, she having so become a mother… could that righteous man ever have endured to know her. For if he had known her, and had kept her in the place of a wife, how is it that our Lord commits her, as unprotected, and having no one, to His disciple, and commands him to take her to his own home?

Then he faces the objection head-on — if Mary bore no other children, in what sense are the “brethren” his brethren? — and answers it in the pure Epiphanian key: the title is a reputed one, exactly parallel to the reputed fatherhood of Joseph, and no assertion of a common mother:

How then, one may say, are James and the others called His brethren? In the same kind of way as Joseph himself was supposed to be husband of Mary. For many were the veils provided, that the birth, being such as it was, might be for a time screened. Wherefore even John so called them, saying, “For neither did His brethren believe in Him.”

— Chrysostom, Homilies on the Gospel of St Matthew V.5–6 (on Matthew 1:22–25).1 He goes on to trace what became of those unbelieving brethren: James “was so admired as even to be the first to be entrusted with the bishop’s office,” the man to whom Paul reported at Jerusalem — the brethren who “made light of this Man living” being turned by the resurrection into those who “consented even to be slain for His sake.” Lightfoot took this homily as the plainest statement of Chrysostom’s earlier view: the brethren were “at one time… unbeliever[s]” with the rest, the resurrection their turning-point, and they “were called the Lord’s brethren, as Joseph himself was reputed the husband of Mary” — adding that “hitherto he betrays no knowledge of the Hieronymian account.”

The same Epiphanian reading in the homilies on John and 1 Corinthians

The reading recurs across the Antiochene homilies. Expounding John 7:5 (“For neither did his brethren believe in him”), Chrysostom makes the unbelieving kinsmen a distinct company from the Twelve and singles out what one of them became — James, kept carefully apart from the apostolic college:

Of those who said these things, one became first Bishop of Jerusalem, the blessed James, of whom Paul saith, “Other of the Apostles saw I none, save James, the Lord’s brother” (Gal. i. 19); and Judas also is said to have been a marvelous man… superiority among kindred is wont somehow to be envied by such as are not alike exalted. But who are those that they call disciples here? The crowd that followed Him, not the twelve.

— Chrysostom, Homilies on the Gospel of St John XLVIII.2 (on John 7:1–5).2 And commenting on 1 Corinthians 9:5, where Paul ranges “the brethren of the Lord” alongside “the rest of the Apostles… and Cephas,” Chrysostom reads them as a class of their own, neither identified with the Twelve nor dissolved into them:

“Have we no right to lead about a wife that is a believer, even as the rest of the Apostles, and the brethren of the Lord, and Cephas?” Observe his skilfulness. The leader of the choir stands last in his arrangement… But neither does he mention Peter alone, but all of them.

— Chrysostom, Homilies on the First Epistle to the Corinthians XXI.6 (on 1 Corinthians 9:4–5).3 None of these passages knows anything of Jerome’s cousins-theory; they belong with the Eusebian and Cappadocian mainstream that keeps the Lord’s brother distinct from the apostolic Twelve.

Late and Hieronymian: James “the son of Cleophas” (on Galatians 1:19)

The turn comes in the Commentary on Galatians. Expounding Paul’s “other of the Apostles saw I none, save James, the Lord’s brother” (Galatians 1:19), Chrysostom pauses on why Paul chose that title, and in doing so lets slip the new identification — that James could have been named instead “the son of Cleophas, as the Evangelist does”:

Had he only wished to point out whom he meant, he might have shown this by another appellation, and called him the son of Cleophas, as the Evangelist does. But as he considered that he had a share in the august titles of the Apostles, he exalts himself by honoring James; and this he does by calling him “the Lord’s brother,” although he was not by birth His brother, but only so reputed.

— Chrysostom, Commentary on the Epistle to the Galatians, on 1:19.4 The reflex “not by birth His brother, but only so reputed” is still the old Epiphanian formula; but the new thing is that Chrysostom now equates James the Lord’s brother with the “son of Cleophas” of John 19:25 — the very identification that is the keystone of Jerome’s reading. He treats James here as if he were an apostle, and by naming him “son of Cleophas” (i.e. the James “son of Alphaeus” of the apostolic lists, on the Aramaic equation Clopas = Alphaeus) appears, as Lightfoot put it, to have “accepted the hypothesis of Jerome and to have completed it by the identification of Clopas with Alphaeus.” Lightfoot noted that this stretch of the exposition “is somewhat confused, and it is difficult to resist the suspicion that it has been interpolated” — but on its face it is the first appearance of the Clopas = Alphaeus equation, which after Chrysostom passed to Theodoret (more explicit still on the same verse) and into the later Greek harmonisers.5

Notes

  • Relation to the three theories. Chrysostom is the timeline’s clearest single-author witness to the drift of Eastern opinion: Epiphanian in the Antiochene homilies (on Matthew, John, and 1 Corinthians), Hieronymian in the Commentary on Galatians. Lightfoot registered the split by entering “[Chrysostom]” — in brackets — in both columns of his closing conspectus: under B, “Sons of Joseph by a former wife,” and under C, “Sons of the Virgin’s Sister.” He is the only name so doubled. Neither reading, on either side of the shift, ever entertains the Helvidian denial: throughout, Mary is held to have borne no child but Jesus.
  • A stray Epiphanian fragment. Lightfoot flags a comment on 1 Corinthians 9:4–7 attributed to Chrysostom in Cramer’s Catena — but “not found in the Homilies” — that is more explicit still: it calls the brethren “those reputed to be his brethren… the sons of Joseph,” so styled “on account of Joseph’s betrothal to the Theotokos,” and names them James bishop of Jerusalem, Joseph, Simon, and Judah. The same note recurs almost verbatim in the Oecumenian catena and in Theophylact. Whether or not it is genuinely Chrysostom’s, it is one more Eastern voice for the Epiphanian solution; because its authorship is doubtful it is not pressed here, and rests on Lightfoot’s report.6
  • Dating. Both bodies of work belong to Chrysostom’s years as a presbyter at Antioch (386–398), before his elevation to Constantinople. The Homilies on Matthew are usually placed c. 390; the Commentary on Galatians is his latest word on the point and cannot be later than 398. He is slotted at c. 398 — after Augustine’s early Commentary on Galatians (c. 394) and before Pelagius (c. 406) — so that his completed Hieronymian identification, the point that makes him a hinge in the Eastern tradition, can be read at the place where the cousins-theory was gaining ground. (On other timelines Chrysostom is anchored c. 395 by his Antioch homilies; here the later Galatians work sets the slot.)
