The Gospel of Peter on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 150 (an apocryphal gospel esteemed by the Docetae; already in circulation when Serapion of Antioch found it at Rhossus late in the second century, so “about the middle of the second century at the latest, and probably somewhat earlier” in J. B. Lightfoot’s dating) · Wikipedia
Summary: Alongside the Protevangelium of James, the Gospel of Peter is one of the two earliest named sources of the Epiphanian reading — that the “brethren of the Lord” were sons of Joseph by a former wife, so that Mary bore no child but Jesus. But nothing of the Gospel of Peter’s own text on this point survives: the single fragment recovered in modern times is a Passion narrative that says nothing of the brethren. The attribution rests wholly on Origen, who names “the Gospel according to Peter… or the Book of James” as the tradition on which the Epiphanian party leaned. This witness is therefore mediated: Origen’s report is firsthand and quotable; the Gospel of Peter’s wording is lost.

Claims & evidence

The testimony survives only through Origen’s report (Comm. Matt. x.17)

Commenting on “Is not this the carpenter’s son? is not his mother called Mary? and his brethren…” (Matthew 13:55), Origen († c. 253) records the two texts to which the perpetual-virginity party appealed — the Gospel of Peter being the first he names:

But some say, basing it on a tradition in the Gospel according to Peter, as it is entitled, or “The Book of James,” that the brethren of Jesus were sons of Joseph by a former wife, whom he married before Mary. Now those who say so wish to preserve the honour of Mary in virginity to the end… And I think it in harmony with reason that Jesus was the first-fruit among men of the purity which consists in chastity, and Mary among women; for it were not pious to ascribe to any other than to her the first-fruit of virginity.

— Origen, Commentary on Matthew x.17, trans. John Patrick (Ante-Nicene Fathers, vol. 9).1 Two things follow. First, the “former wife” solution was, on Origen’s own showing, already anchored in named second-century texts — not an invention of the fourth-century controversialists. Second, Origen knew of only two answers to the brethren question, the Epiphanian and the denial of the perpetual virginity; Jerome’s later cousins hypothesis had not yet been conceived. ⚠ unverified — the claim that the Gospel of Peter itself contained this cannot be checked against the text of that gospel, which does not survive on the point; it rests on Origen’s report (and Lightfoot’s relaying of it).

The Gospel of Peter was an early and widely-read text

The document Origen names was no obscure curiosity. It was esteemed by the Docetae, and late in the second century Serapion, bishop of Antioch, found it in use at Rhossus in Cilicia and at first allowed it, withdrawing his sanction only when he judged some of its language to favour heresy — the episode preserved in a fragment of Serapion’s letter quoted by Eusebius.2 Serapion writes of it as long in circulation, which sets its composition around the middle of the second century at the latest, and probably somewhat earlier — contemporary with, or a little before, the Protevangelium. That is why Lightfoot places it at the head of his list of Epiphanian authorities.3

Notes

  • What survives, and what does not. The only substantial portion of the Gospel of Peter known today is the Akhmîm fragment, recovered from an Egyptian grave in 1886–87 — a Docetic-tinged account of the Passion and resurrection. It contains nothing about the infancy, about Joseph, or about the brethren of the Lord. Origen’s notice is thus our sole evidence that the gospel ever addressed the question at all, and his precise words cannot be checked against the source he cites.4
  • Named in one breath with the Protevangelium. Origen offers the two texts as alternatives (“the Gospel according to Peter… or the Book of James”), and Lightfoot treats them as the twin fountainheads of the Epiphanian reading. Because the Protevangelium’s Joseph-the-widower narrative does survive in full, it carries the evidential weight the Gospel of Peter can no longer bear; the two are best read together (see the Protevangelium detail page).
  • Relation to the three theories. This is, with the Protevangelium, the earliest datable anchor of the Epiphanian view, which preserves Mary’s perpetual virginity while leaving Joseph the father of an earlier family. It knows nothing of Jerome’s later cousins hypothesis (the Hieronymian view) and stands opposed to the Helvidian reading that the brethren were Mary’s own later-born children — the only alternative Origen himself could see. Whether so early an attestation reflects genuine memory or pious construction is exactly the point the schools dispute.
