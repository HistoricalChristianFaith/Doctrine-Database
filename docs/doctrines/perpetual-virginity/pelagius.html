Pelagius on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 406 (his Expositions of the Thirteen Epistles of St Paul, written at Rome c. 406–409, a few years after Jerome, and before the sack of Rome in 410 and the outbreak of the Pelagian controversy; Pelagius fl. c. 380–418) · Wikipedia
Summary: Pelagius, expounding Galatians 1:19 only a few years after Jerome, is the first commentator to fall in behind Jerome’s newly-minted Hieronymian reading — the “brethren of the Lord” are the Lord’s kinsmen, not Mary’s other sons. But which Pelagius said so is a textual question. His genuine note, recovered by Alexander Souter from the uninterpolated manuscripts, is terse and restrained: Mary bore no other sons; the two apostolic Jameses are sons of Alphaeus and of Zebedee, neither a son of Mary or Joseph; the brethren are so called “from kinship.” The fuller gloss that parrots Jerome’s own “four kinds of brethren” and names James a son of “Mary of Clopas, the sister of the Lord’s mother” belongs only to the interpolated Pseudo-Jerome recension — the very tampering J. B. Lightfoot suspected when he wrote that Pelagius “adopts his theory and even his language… unless his text has been tampered with here.”

Claims & evidence

The genuine note: no other sons of Mary, the brethren are kinsmen

Pelagius’ commentary on Paul long circulated under other names — most of it under Jerome’s — and Alexander Souter’s critical edition set out to recover the original text from beneath its later accretions. In that reconstructed text the whole of Pelagius’ comment on “James the Lord’s brother” (Galatians 1:19) is a single tight sentence, aimed squarely at the deniers of the perpetual virginity:

Nisi Iacobum fratrem domini. Contra eos qui dicunt beatam Mariam alios filios habuisse, quia duos Iacobos apostolos fuisse legimus, unum Alphaei et alterum Zebedaei, neminem Mariae uel Ioseph, sed fratres domini de propinquitate dicuntur.

— Pelagius, Expositio in Galatas 1:19, ed. Alexander Souter, Pelagius’s Expositions of Thirteen Epistles of St Paul, II. Text (Texts and Studies IX.2; Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 1926), p. 311.1 In English: “Except James the Lord’s brother. Against those who say that blessed Mary had other sons — for we read that there were two apostles named James, one the son of Alphaeus and the other the son of Zebedee, neither a son of Mary or of Joseph; rather, they are called the Lord’s brothers from kinship.” Two things are settled and one is left open. Settled: Mary bore no children after Jesus (“against those who say blessed Mary had other sons”), and the men called his “brethren” are kin in a wider sense — de propinquitate, “from kinship,” not from a common mother. That is the Hieronymian substance: the virginity of Mary preserved, the brethren reduced to kinsmen. Left open in the genuine note: Pelagius does not here spell out the particular kinship — he does not name Mary of Clopas, nor rehearse Jerome’s catalogue of the senses of “brother.”

The interpolated gloss: Jerome’s “four kinds of brethren” and Mary of Clopas

Two of Souter’s witnesses do supply exactly that missing apparatus — but they are the wrong two. In place of the terse sentence above, the Pseudo-Jerome recension (Souter’s siglum H) and the interpolated St Gall manuscript no. 73 (G) carry a fuller gloss that reproduces Jerome’s own argument almost word for word:

Quattuor modis fratres appellari quis dubitet? Primo, naturae; secundo, cognationis; tertio, gentis; quarto, adfectu; unde Iacobus secundum cognationem frater dicitur, quoniam de Maria Cleopae, sorore matris domini, natus esse monstratur.

— the reading of the Pseudo-Jerome (H) and St Gall (G) manuscripts, recorded in Souter’s apparatus at Galatians 1:19.1 In English: “Who could doubt that ‘brothers’ are so called in four ways? — first, by nature; second, by kinship; third, by race; fourth, by affection. Whence James is called brother by kinship, since he is shown to have been born of Mary of Clopas, the sister of the Lord’s mother.” The fourfold division of “brother” is Jerome’s signature move — “In Holy Scripture there are four kinds of brethren—by nature, race, kindred, love” (Against Helvidius §16) — and the identification of James with the son of “Mary of Clopas… sister of Mary the Lord’s mother” is the very keystone of Jerome’s cousins-theory. Souter marks this expanded version as the reading of two interpolated witnesses, not of the original: in his edition the Pseudo-Jerome manuscripts (H) and St Gall 73 (G) are filed among the “manuscripts of interpolated forms,” and their extra matter is the deposit of a later hand harmonising Pelagius with the fuller Hieronymian doctrine that had meanwhile become standard.1

Lightfoot: “adopts his theory and even his language… unless his text has been tampered with”

J. B. Lightfoot, working in 1865 before Souter’s edition existed, had the whole thing right in a single parenthetical sentence. Reviewing how quickly Jerome’s reading spread after 383, he wrote:

Pelagius, who commented on St. Paul a few years after Jerome, adopts his theory and even his language, unless his text has been tampered with here (Gal i. 19).

— J. B. Lightfoot, “The Brethren of the Lord.”2 Lightfoot could only guess at the alternative — either Pelagius truly borrowed Jerome’s language, or a copyist had inserted it. Souter’s later disentangling of the genuine Pelagius from the Pseudo-Jerome interpolations decides the guess in favour of the second horn: the “even his language” — Jerome’s four kinds of brethren, Mary of Clopas — is present only in the tampered recension, while the uninterpolated Pelagius keeps a note of his own that is Hieronymian in tendency but not in wording. Lightfoot accordingly files Pelagius under column C of his conspectus, “Sons of the Virgin’s Sister” — Jerome, Pelagius, Augustine, Chrysostom, Theodoret — the first name to follow Jerome’s into that column.2

Notes

  • Relation to the three theories. Pelagius is Hieronymian — the brethren are the Lord’s kinsmen, not Mary’s later children — and, coming within a few years of Jerome’s Against Helvidius, he is the earliest witness to adopt the new reading, ahead even of Augustine’s mature works. But the adoption is milder in his genuine text than the tradition long supposed: he denies that Mary had other sons and calls the brethren kinsmen “from kinship,” without rehearsing Jerome’s scheme or naming Mary of Clopas. Only the interpolated recension makes him a full-throated Hieronymian.
  • The text under Jerome’s name. Pelagius’ Pauline commentary is one of the most textually fraught in the Latin tradition: after his condemnation as a heretic it survived chiefly by being ascribed to orthodox authors — above all to Jerome, in the recension Souter calls “Pseudo-Jerome.” That an editor working under Jerome’s name should top up Pelagius’ comment on the brethren with Jerome’s own words on the brethren is exactly the kind of harmonising the transmission invited — and is what the H and G readings preserve.
  • Dating. Souter places the composition of the Expositions at Rome, c. 406–409, before Pelagius left the city ahead of Alaric’s sack in 410 and before the Pelagian controversy proper. It is here slotted at c. 406, after Augustine’s early Commentary on Galatians (c. 394), so that Pelagius’ place as the first follower of Jerome’s reading can be read on the timeline. (On the perpetual-virginity timeline Pelagius is dated by this commentary, not by the c. 415–418 anchor of his role in the later grace-and-original-sin dispute.)
