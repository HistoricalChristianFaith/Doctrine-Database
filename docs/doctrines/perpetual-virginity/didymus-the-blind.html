Didymus the Blind on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 386 (Didymus the Blind, c. 313–398, the blind Alexandrian exegete who from childhood mastered Scripture by ear and, appointed by Athanasius, headed the catechetical school of Alexandria for half a century, teaching Rufinus, Palladius, and — by report — Jerome. The treatise On the Trinity (De Trinitate) traditionally ascribed to him is placed in his last years, c. 381–392, conventionally c. 386 — though its attribution to Didymus is disputed; see the note below) · Wikipedia
Summary: Didymus meets the perpetual-virginity question at the same Matthean proof-text the deniers pressed — “until she brought forth her firstborn son” (Matthew 1:25) — and reads it, as the tradition did, to imply no later children. “Firstborn” and “until,” he holds, fix only that Jesus was born first and that Mary was a virgin up to the birth; they say nothing against a virginity outlasting it. Mary “married no one else,” “never became the Mother of anyone else,” and “even after childbirth… remained always and forever an immaculate virgin.” The witness is an Alexandrian exegete of the same generation as Athanasius, writing before Jerome’s reply to Helvidius made the question an open Latin controversy (c. 383).

Claims & evidence

“Firstborn” and “until” are no bar to a perpetual virginity

The passage sits in a discussion of Christological vocabulary — how the titles “first-born” and “only-begotten” are to be understood — and Didymus turns to Matthew 1:25 to illustrate the point, drawing from the same words that the Helvidians read for later children the opposite conclusion:

It helps us to understand the terms “first-born” and “only-begotten” when the Evangelist tells that Mary remained a virgin “until she brought forth her first-born son” [Matt. 1:25]; for neither did Mary, who is to be honored and praised above all others, marry anyone else, nor did she ever become the Mother of anyone else, but even after childbirth she remained always and forever an immaculate virgin.

— Didymus the Blind, On the Trinity (De Trinitate) 3.4.1 The argument is the Fathers’ standard one, made here in passing: “first-born” marks that Jesus was born first, not that others followed (one is first-born even when only-begotten), and the “until” of Matthew 1:25 fixes only what was true up to the birth, without implying any change after it. Both legs of that reading are the ones weighed on this timeline at the infancy-language argument (assessment: weak) — the “till she brought forth” of Matthew 1:25 and the “firstborn son” of Luke 2:7 — and, for the narrower modern refinement of the “until” leg, at the “heōs hou” argument (assessment: weak). Didymus thus stands beside Chrysostom, Chromatius, and later John of Damascus as a patristic voice reading the Matthean “till” and “firstborn” as no bar to a virginity that outlasts the birth — and squarely against the Helvidian denial.

What it settles — and what it leaves open

Like Athanasius and Chromatius, Didymus affirms the perpetual virginity itself without settling the identity of the “brethren of the Lord.” His concern is the meaning of “firstborn” and “until,” not the three-camp question of who the brethren were, so he cannot be filed Epiphanian or Hieronymian. On the one point he does touch — that Mary bore no child but Jesus and remained ever-virgin — he is unambiguous.

Notes

  • An Alexandrian datapoint before the Latin controversy. Whoever wrote On the Trinity, the passage is a fourth-century Greek-Alexandrian reading of Matthew 1:25 that takes the perpetual virginity for granted while refuting the “firstborn”/“until” inference — from the milieu of Clement, Origen, and Athanasius, and before Helvidius and Jerome made the brethren a matter of open dispute in the West (c. 383).
  • Disputed authorship of De Trinitate. The three-book On the Trinity survives in a single manuscript (Biblioteca Angelica, Rome) and was ascribed to Didymus by its eighteenth-century editor, G. L. Mingarelli. After the 1941 discovery of Didymus’s genuine Tura commentaries allowed a stylistic comparison, several scholars questioned the attribution — some proposing Apollinaris of Laodicea or an anonymous author — while Alasdair Heron’s 1972 study mounted the strongest defense of Didymus’s authorship. Recent treatments lean cautiously back toward Didymus without regarding the case as closed; textual critics accordingly flag the citation of Matthew 1:25 here as Didymusdub (“dubious”). Either way the passage is a genuine fourth-century Alexandrian witness; the doubt bears only on whether the specific hand is Didymus’s.2
  • The “firstborn” text of Matthew 1:25. Didymus’s citation reads “her first-born son” — the longer reading of Matthew 1:25 (later harmonized to Luke 2:7). That he uses the very word the Helvidians leaned on, yet draws the perpetual virginity from it, is why the passage recurs in New-Testament textual criticism as well as in the Marian debate.
