Athanasius of Alexandria on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 360 (Athanasius of Alexandria, c. 296–373, the great anti-Arian bishop of Alexandria and champion of the Nicene homoousion. His Four Discourses (Orations) Against the Arians are conventionally assigned to the fruitful years of his third exile among the Egyptian monks, c. 356–360 — though a minority of scholars, following Charles Kannengiesser, argue for a date around 339–343) · Wikipedia
Summary: Athanasius is not arguing the brethren-question at all — he is defending the reality of Christ’s flesh against the Arians. But in the course of that argument he calls the Lord’s mother “Mary Ever-Virgin” (Greek aēiparthenos, ἀειπάρθενος), using the title not as a thesis to be proved but as a settled, uncontroversial premise. That incidental use, from the leading theologian of mid-fourth-century Alexandria and a full generation before Helvidius raised the challenge at Rome (c. 383), is a strong datapoint for how established the perpetual-virginity title already was in the Greek East.

Claims & evidence

“Mary Ever-Virgin” as a settled premise in an anti-Arian argument

The setting is Christological, not Mariological. In Oration II Athanasius is pressing the Arians on the reality and purpose of the Incarnation: only if the Word is truly God and truly took human flesh could He deify what He assumed and bring man into the Father’s presence. He turns the point into a double challenge — those who deny the Son’s true deity may as well deny His true flesh, since both are needed for our salvation — and it is here, naming the flesh’s source, that the title falls:

And as we had not been delivered from sin and the curse, unless it had been by nature human flesh, which the Word put on… so also the man had not been deified, unless the Word who became flesh had been by nature from the Father and true and proper to Him… Therefore let those who deny that the Son is from the Father by nature and proper to His Essence, deny also that He took true human flesh of Mary Ever-Virgin; for in neither case had it been of profit to us men, whether the Word were not true and naturally Son of God, or the flesh not true which He assumed. But surely He took true flesh, though Valentinus rave; yea the Word was by nature Very God, though Ariomaniacs rave.

— Athanasius, Four Discourses Against the Arians, Discourse II, §70.1 The reality of the flesh is the thing under dispute (against the Valentinian docetists on one side and the Arians on the other); Mary’s perpetual virginity is not. The epithet “Ever-Virgin” is dropped in passing, as a fixed liturgical-doctrinal title the readers are expected to grant without argument.

Why an incidental title carries weight

A witness who argues for a doctrine tells us the doctrine was contested; a witness who simply assumes it tells us it was established. Athanasius does the latter. He never sets out to prove that Mary remained a virgin after Christ’s birth — he takes it as already belonging to her standard designation, the way “the Word” or “the Father” belong to their subjects, and uses it to anchor a point about something else entirely. Coming from the most authoritative Greek theologian of his generation, and from Alexandria (the see of Clement and Origen before him, both already witnesses to the perpetual virginity), the casual title testifies that aēiparthenos was common coin in the mid-fourth-century East — decades before Helvidius and Jerome made the question a matter of open controversy in the Latin West (c. 383).

What it settles — and what it leaves open

Because Athanasius affirms only the title and not any account of the Lord’s brethren, his witness bears directly on the perpetual virginity itself but says nothing to decide among the three theories of the brethren. He does not tell us whether he took the brethren to be Joseph’s sons by a former marriage (the Epiphanian view) or cousins (the Hieronymian); he simply presupposes that the Virgin was and remained ever-virgin. On the one point he does touch he is unambiguous, and on the disputed genealogy he is silent.

Notes

  • Not a Mariological treatise. The passage belongs to a Christological argument about the true, saving reality of Christ’s flesh, framed against Valentinian docetism (“though Valentinus rave”) and Arian subordinationism (“though Ariomaniacs rave”). The Marian title is incidental to that purpose — which is exactly what makes it valuable as evidence of settled usage rather than contested claim.
  • The Greek title. The NPNF renders aēiparthenos (ἀειπάρθενος) “Ever-Virgin” — the same compound epithet that would become a standard conciliar and liturgical style for Mary in the Greek church.
  • Date. The Orations are usually placed in Athanasius’ third exile (c. 356–360), when he withdrew to the Egyptian desert and wrote a cluster of major works; Robertson noted they seem from the language of II.1 to have been “issued at intervals.” A minority (Kannengiesser) argues for an earlier date, c. 339–343. Either way the witness sits well before the Latin controversy of the 380s.
