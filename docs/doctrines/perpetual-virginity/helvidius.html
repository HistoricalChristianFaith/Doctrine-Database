Helvidius on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 383 (a layman resident at Rome under Pope Damasus; his treatise on the brethren of the Lord appeared about 383, and Jerome’s reply, The Perpetual Virginity of Blessed Mary, Against Helvidius, followed the same year. Nothing else of his life is recorded; his own treatise is lost, and, as the standard editors note, “the only contemporary notice preserved of Helvidius is that by Jerome”) · Wikipedia
Summary: Helvidius is the eponym of the Helvidian reading of the brethren — that the “brethren of the Lord” were the true sons of Joseph and Mary, born after Jesus, so that Mary was not ever-virgin. He argued it at Rome about 383, chiefly to check the era’s exaltation of virgin over married life by the example of the Lord’s own mother, who (he held) had gone on to bear children to her husband. His treatise does not survive: it is known only where Jerome quotes it to refute it, so his own words come to us at second hand and are marked ⚠ unverified. Jerome preserves the shape of his case — the proof-texts from Matthew (“knew her not till…”; “her first-born son”) and the appeal to earlier writers, Tertullian and Victorinus of Pettau. Helvidius won enough of a following that contemporaries spoke of the Helvidians as a party; J. B. Lightfoot files him in his conspectus under “sons of Joseph and Mary,” at the head of the Helvidian column beside Tertullian and the Antidicomarianites. It was Jerome’s answer to him that, on Lightfoot’s reckoning, first launched the rival Hieronymian cousins-theory.

Claims & evidence

The brethren are sons of Joseph and Mary — Mary bore children after Jesus

Helvidius’ thesis survives only in the treatise Jerome wrote against it. Jerome opens by naming his opponent and the occasion:

I was requested by certain of the brethren not long ago to reply to a pamphlet written by one Helvidius. I have deferred doing so, not because it is a difficult matter to maintain the truth and refute an ignorant boor…

— Jerome, Against Helvidius §1.1 The pamphlet’s contention, as the treatise reports it throughout, was that the Gospel “sisters” and “brethren” of the Lord prove that the Virgin “had subsequent issue” — children born to her and Joseph after Jesus — and that therefore virginity could claim no superiority over marriage. The clearest fragment of Helvidius’ own voice that Jerome lets through is his insistence that the two titles fall on the same people:

The same names are repeated by the Evangelists in another place, and the same persons are there brethren of the Lord and sons of Mary.

— Helvidius, as quoted in Jerome, Against Helvidius §15.2 Because his treatise is lost and every line of it reaches us only through the pen of the man refuting it, Helvidius’ own words are marked ⚠ unverified; what is verified is that Jerome reports and combats exactly this Helvidian position.

The proof-texts — “knew her not till” and “first-born son”

Helvidius rested the reading on two phrases in Matthew’s infancy narrative, and Jerome answers each in turn. From Matthew 1:25 — Joseph “knew her not till she had brought forth her son” — Helvidius argued that a man who “knew her not until” a point in time must have known her afterward, and that “before they came together” (Matthew 1:18) implies they did come together in the end:

the Evangelist would not have said before they came together if they were not to come together, for no one would use the phrase “before he dined” of a man who was not going to dine.

— the argument as Jerome states it, Against Helvidius §4.3 From the second phrase — Mary “brought forth her first-born son” (Luke 2:7) — Helvidius pressed a further inference:

From this he endeavours to show that the term first-born is inapplicable except to a person who has brothers, just as he is called only begotten who is the only son of his parents.

— Jerome, Against Helvidius §10.4 Jerome’s replies became the classic rebuttals: Scripture uses “till” without implying a change after (as God says “I am with you till the end of the world”), and “first-born” is a legal term for the child that “openeth the womb,” applicable to an only child as much as to the eldest of many. But the arguments themselves are Helvidius’, and they are the stock Helvidian proof-texts ever since.

The appeal to Tertullian and Victorinus, and the attack on virginity

Helvidius did not claim to innovate: he enlisted two older Latin writers for the reading, and aimed the whole treatise at the growing cult of celibacy. The editors of the standard English edition summarize:

Helvidius maintained that the mention in the Gospels of the “sisters” and “brethren” of our Lord was proof that the Blessed Virgin had subsequent issue, and he supported his opinion by the writings of Tertullian and Victorinus. The outcome of his views was that virginity was ranked below matrimony.

— introduction to Jerome, Against Helvidius (Nicene and Post-Nicene Fathers, 2nd ser., vol. 6).5 Jerome, unable simply to dismiss the appeal, conceded Tertullian — retorting only that he was “not a member of the Church” — but denied him Victorinus of Pettau, counterclaiming that Victorinus had meant kinsmen, not sons of Mary. That Jerome had to answer the appeal at all is itself evidence for the antiquity of the Helvidian reading; and that Helvidius’ deeper target was the exaltation of virginity is why Jerome devotes the treatise’s last chapters (§§19–22) to defending the superiority of the virgin state.5

A named party — and the occasion of Jerome’s new theory

Helvidius was no isolated crank: he persuaded a following large enough to be spoken of as a sect, and like-minded voices arose elsewhere in the same years. J. B. Lightfoot gathers the notice:

Somewhere about the year 380 Helvidius, who resided in Rome, published a treatise in which he maintained that the Lord’s brethren were sons of Joseph and Mary. He seems to have succeeded in convincing a considerable number of persons, for contemporary writers speak of the Helvidians as a party. These views were moreover advocated by Bonosus, bishop of Sardica in Illyria, about the same time, and apparently also by Jovinianus a monk probably of Milan.

— J. B. Lightfoot, “The Brethren of the Lord,” §22.6 In the conspectus that closes his dissertation, Lightfoot sorts every witness under the reading it supports, and sets Helvidius among the deniers of the perpetual virginity — the Helvidian column, which lends the view its name:

A. Sons of Joseph and Mary: Tertullian; Helvidius; Bonosus; Jovinianus (?); Antidicomarianites.

— Lightfoot, conspectus of authorities.6 It was the reply to this treatise that reshaped the whole Western tradition: Jerome, unwilling to grant Helvidius even the Epiphanian solution (which would have let “brother” stand for Joseph’s sons by a former wife), struck out the Hieronymian cousins-theory instead — and Lightfoot judged that “no decisive instance can be produced of a writer holding Jerome’s view, before it was propounded by Jerome himself” in this very book.6

Notes

  • Lost treatise, hostile transmission. Helvidius wrote a book; not a sentence of it survives except as embedded in Jerome’s refutation, which quotes it only to knock it down. On the wiki’s rule that a claim resting only on a later or hostile report is marked ⚠ unverified, Helvidius’ teaching in his own voice is so marked. What is firmly attested is that Jerome answered exactly this position, preserving its proof-texts (Matthew 1:18, 1:25; Luke 2:7) and its appeal to Tertullian and Victorinus.
  • The infancy proof-texts prove less than he claimed. Helvidius did not merely note that Matthew 1:25 and Luke 2:7 are compatible with later children; he pressed them as though they demonstrated some — “knew her not till…” supposedly requiring union afterward, “firstborn” supposedly requiring later-born siblings. Both idioms are in fact neutral: “till” (heōs hou) denies a prior state without asserting its reversal, and “firstborn” is a consecration term fixed at birth (“every male that openeth the womb,” Luke 2:23). The phrases fit his reading but cannot carry it — weighed in full on the argument page, “Do ‘till she brought forth’ and ‘firstborn son’ imply Mary bore other children?” (assessment: weak).
  • Dating. Lightfoot dates the treatise itself “about the year 380” (§22); the standard editors and Jerome’s own reply place the exchange “about a.d. 383,” when both men were at Rome under Damasus. The timeline slots Helvidius at c. 383 with the reply, just after the Apostolic Constitutions and immediately before Jerome’s counter-theory.
  • Relation to the three theories. Helvidius is the Helvidian namesake — the brethren are sons of Joseph and Mary, and the perpetual virginity is denied. He is the Roman counterpart to the Arabian Antidicomarianites and the reading Tertullian is reckoned to have held; Augustine later equated the two parties, though Lightfoot cautioned that “considerations of time and place alike resist this identification.” Against him stand the whole Epiphanian chorus (Origen, Eusebius, Hilary, Epiphanius) and, newly minted in reply to him, the Hieronymian cousins-theory of Jerome.
