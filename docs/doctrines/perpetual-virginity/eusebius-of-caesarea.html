Eusebius of Caesarea on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 324 (the Church History in its final edition; Eusebius, bishop of Caesarea in Palestine and the “Father of Church History,” was present at Nicaea in 325 and died c. 340) · Wikipedia
Summary: Eusebius is the fullest patristic witness of his generation for the Epiphanian reading — that the “brethren of the Lord” are sons of Joseph and no blood-kin of Mary. He does two things that place him there. He explains that James was called the Lord’s brother precisely because he was a son of Joseph, and Joseph was the reputed father of Christ — reputed brotherhood tracking reputed fatherhood, so that James is Joseph’s son, not Mary’s (H.E. ii.1). And he consistently reckons James the Lord’s brother a supernumerary apostle like Paul, standing beside the Twelve rather than among them (H.E. i.12; vii.19), which tells against the Hieronymian reading that folds him into the Twelve as James the son of Alphaeus. J. B. Lightfoot filed him in his catena of the “sons of Joseph by a former wife” authorities, immediately after Origen.

Claims & evidence

James is “called the brother of the Lord” because Joseph was his reputed father — the Epiphanian mechanism

Opening his second book on the course the apostles took after the Ascension, Eusebius names James the Just as the first bishop of Jerusalem, and stops to explain how a man who was Joseph’s son came to be styled the Lord’s brother at all:

Then James, whom the ancients surnamed the Just on account of the excellence of his virtue, is recorded to have been the first to be made bishop of the church of Jerusalem. This James was called the brother of the Lord because he was known as a son of Joseph, and Joseph was supposed to be the father of Christ, because the Virgin, being betrothed to him, “was found with child by the Holy Ghost before they came together,” as the account of the holy Gospels shows.

— Church History ii.1.2.1 The logic is exactly the Epiphanian one. James is a son of Joseph; he is called the brother of the Lord only because Joseph was the reputed father of Christ — a reputed brotherhood built on a reputed fatherhood, with no blood-tie to Mary asserted anywhere. Lightfoot took the phrase “most naturally… to refer to the reputed brotherhood of James, as a consequence of the reputed fatherhood of Joseph, and thus to favour the Epiphanian view,” and noted that the verb “cannot… imply any doubt on the part of Eusebius about the parentage of James, for the whole drift of the passage is plainly against this.”2 The same “so-called” idiom recurs whenever Eusebius touches the relationship — James “one of the so-called brethren of the Saviour” (i.12), James “called a brother of Christ” (vii.19).

A supernumerary apostle, like Paul — beside the Twelve, not among them

Cataloguing the apostles and the wider circle of the seventy, Eusebius comes to the resurrection appearances Paul lists in 1 Corinthians 15, and uses them to mark James off as an apostle added to the Twelve, on the same footing as Paul himself:

Afterwards he says he appeared unto James, who was one of the so-called brethren of the Saviour. But, since in addition to these, there were many others who were called apostles, in imitation of the Twelve, as was Paul himself, he adds: “Afterward he appeared to all the apostles.”

— Church History i.12.4–5.3 James the Lord’s brother is thus “a supernumerary apostle like St. Paul” — an apostle in the wider sense, over and above the Twelve, not one of them.4 The reckoning holds when Eusebius describes the relic still shown at Jerusalem in his own day:

The chair of James, who first received the episcopate of the church at Jerusalem from the Saviour himself and the apostles, and who, as the divine records show, was called a brother of Christ, has been preserved until now…

— Church History vii.19.1.5 James “received the episcopate… from the Saviour himself and the apostles” — set apart from “the apostles” as their appointee, again “called” (not simply was) a brother of Christ. Because this keeps the Lord’s brother James firmly outside the Twelve, it weighs against the Hieronymian reading, which identifies him with James the son of Alphaeus — while leaving the Epiphanian and Helvidian readings, which both keep him distinct, untouched. On the parentage question itself, the ii.1 explanation carries Eusebius further than the bare distinction does: it makes James a son of Joseph, and so points to the Epiphanian answer specifically.

The explicit “former wife” — Joseph’s sons by Anna

The Church History makes James a son of Joseph but does not there name a former marriage. Two other places under Eusebius’ name supply the missing detail. His Commentary on Isaiah again distinguishes James the Lord’s brother from the Twelve, representing him as a supernumerary apostle like Paul;6 and a Syriac tract circulated as Eusebius’ On the Star spells out the “former wife” expressly, listing in the flight into Egypt “Joseph and Mary and Our Lord with them, and the five sons of Hannah (Anna) the first wife of Joseph” — the brethren as Joseph’s children by an earlier marriage. Its own words survive only through Lightfoot’s report of William Wright’s translation, and the tract’s attribution and the antiquity it claims (a source “dating a.d. 119”) are doubtful, so that testimony remains ⚠ unverified.7

What Eusebius’ testimony settles

Eusebius is the hinge witness of the early fourth century. He collected and preserved the older Palestinian tradition — it is only through his quotation that Hegesippus survives at all — and he read it as the Epiphanian tradition read it: the brethren are Joseph’s sons, James is called the Lord’s brother by a reputed and not a natural relationship, and he ranks among the apostles as a supernumerary beside Paul. Lightfoot accordingly set “Eusebius” in his conspectus of the “sons of Joseph by a former wife” authorities, next after Origen and before Hilary and Gregory of Nyssa — and observed that both Eusebius and Epiphanius “derived their information mainly from Hegesippus” and “gave this account of the Lord’s brethren,” which “materially strengthens the position” that the earliest Palestinian evidence leans Epiphanian.8 His witness thus falls a full half-century before Jerome would devise the cousins-theory (c. 383), and shows no awareness of it.

Notes

  • The Clement “two Jameses” extract he quotes. In the very same chapter (H.E. ii.1.4) Eusebius quotes Clement of Alexandria: “there were two Jameses: one called the Just, who was thrown from the pinnacle of the temple… and another who was beheaded.” The line has been claimed for the Hieronymian side (as though Clement recognised only two men of the name, identifying the Lord’s brother with James of the Twelve). Lightfoot answered that it “proves nothing”: Clement “says that there were two of the name of James, but he neither states nor implies that there were two only,” his object being simply to distinguish the Lord’s brother from the son of Zebedee.9 Either way the words are Clement’s, not Eusebius’ own; Eusebius quotes them “with apparently no suspicion that it contradicts his own opinion,” the opinion he states in his own voice at ii.1.2.
  • Relation to the three theories. The H.E. ii.1 explanation is Epiphanian in substance (James a son of Joseph, no blood-tie to Mary); the i.12 and vii.19 “supernumerary apostle” passages, like Hegesippus and the Apostolic Constitutions, cut against the Hieronymian reading without by themselves deciding between Epiphanian and Helvidian. Taken together they place Eusebius with the Epiphanian tradition. The perpetual virginity itself he everywhere assumes: Mary is “the Virgin” who conceived by the Holy Ghost, and James her step-son by Joseph, not her child.
