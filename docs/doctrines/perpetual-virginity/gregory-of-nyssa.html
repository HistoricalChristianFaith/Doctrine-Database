Gregory of Nyssa on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 382 (a festal homily; the witness is his second oration On the Resurrection of Christ [In Christi resurrectionem ii], one of the Easter sermons he preached across his later career — the individual homily cannot be dated closely). Gregory (c. 335 – c. 395), bishop of Nyssa in Cappadocia from 372, was the younger brother of Basil of Caesarea and the third of the three Cappadocian Fathers. · Wikipedia
Summary: Gregory holds the Epiphanian reading — the “brethren of the Lord” are Joseph’s sons, not Mary’s, so her perpetual virginity stands. But he joins to it an idiosyncratic and much-criticized twist: he identifies the “Mary the mother of James and Joses” of the Passion and resurrection narratives (Matthew 27:56; Mark 15:40) not with a second Mary but with the Theotokos herself, reckoned the boys’ step-mother because she had taken over the raising of Joseph’s children; and he reads the “James the little” of Mark 15:40 as marked off from “James the son of Alphaeus” — the “great” James, who was one of the Twelve — so that the Lord’s brother is neither of Mary’s body nor of the apostolic Twelve. J. B. Lightfoot filed Gregory in his catena of the “sons of Joseph by a former wife” authorities, next after Ambrosiaster and before Epiphanius, while judging the Marian identification “very untenable.” The resurrection homily on which the brethren-claims rest survives in Migne’s Patrologia Graeca (In Christi resurrectionem oratio ii, PG 46, col. 647–648), where Gregory’s own Greek bears out Lightfoot’s report; that oration is, however, of disputed authenticity — one of the resurrection sermons excluded from the modern critical edition (the Gregorii Nysseni Opera) and from S. G. Hall’s translation of the genuine Easter sermons as a dubium — so the caveat now attaches to whether the sermon is Gregory’s at all, not to whether it says what Lightfoot reported. A second and firsthand strand of Gregory’s Marian teaching survives in his Homily on the Nativity of Christ: there he reads Mary’s reply to Gabriel — “How shall this be, seeing I know not a man?” (Luke 1:34) — as proof that she had already consecrated her virginity to God, making him, with Augustine, one of the two fourth-century sources of the “vow” reading of that verse.

Claims & evidence

The brethren are Joseph’s sons — the Epiphanian reading

Gregory’s underlying position is the standard Eastern one, shared with Clement, Origen, Eusebius, and his fellow Cappadocians: the “brethren of the Lord” are the elder children of Joseph by a former marriage, older step-brothers of Jesus and no blood-kin of Mary, so that Mary bore Jesus alone and remained ever-virgin. J. B. Lightfoot records simply that Gregory “certainly adopted the Epiphanian account.”1 That much aligns him with the whole line of witnesses this timeline sets before Jerome; the interest of his testimony lies in the two peculiar moves he adds to it.

The odd twist: “Mary the mother of James and Joses” is the Theotokos herself

Where most of the tradition kept the Virgin distinct from the “other Mary” who stood by the cross and came to the tomb, Gregory collapses the two. Preaching on the resurrection, he takes “the Mary who is designated in the other Evangelists (besides St. John) the mother of James and Joses” to be “the mother of God and none else” — the Virgin Mary called “mother of James and Joses” not because she bore them but because, as Joseph’s wife, she “undertook the education of these her stepsons.”1 The move is meant to serve the Epiphanian scheme — it keeps the brethren Joseph’s and lets the Virgin be their step-mother — but it does so at the cost of an identification the rest of the tradition, and Gregory’s own century, largely resisted. Lightfoot calls it “the very untenable position” Gregory “takes up” alongside the sound Epiphanian core.1

“James the little” is not James son of Alphaeus of the Twelve

Gregory couples that identification with a second inference from the Gospel wording. Mark 15:40 names among the women “Mary the mother of James the little (or ‘the less’) and of Joses.” Gregory reads the epithet as a deliberate contrast: this James is “the little” precisely to distinguish him from James the son of Alphaeus, who was “the great” because he was numbered among the Twelve Apostles — which the Lord’s brother was not.1 This places Gregory, on the “James the Just not one of the Twelve” question, with Hegesippus, the Clementine writings, Cyril of Jerusalem, Marius Victorinus, and the Apostolic Constitutions — a further witness against the Hieronymian reading, which folds the Lord’s brother into the Twelve by identifying him with James son of Alphaeus. Gregory does the opposite: he holds the two Jameses firmly apart and keeps the Lord’s brother outside the apostolic college.

The vow reading of Luke 1:34 — Mary’s consecrated virginity

Apart from the reported resurrection homily, Gregory left a firsthand and much weightier testimony to Mary’s virginity in his festal Homily on the Nativity of Christ (In diem natalem Salvatoris, c. 386). Expounding the Annunciation, he fastens on Mary’s reply to Gabriel and takes it as evidence that her virginity was a settled resolve, consecrated to God before ever she was betrothed — not a passing circumstance a bride would expect to leave behind:

The Angel proclaims the glad tidings of the birth; but she cleaves to virginity, judging incorruption to be preferable to the appearance of the Angel, and neither does she disbelieve the Angel nor will she relinquish her resolve. “I have refrained from knowing any man,” she says: “How shall this be, seeing I know not a man?” This utterance of Mary is proof of the narrative that I have drawn from the aforementioned apocryphal work. For if she had been taken in marriage by Joseph, how would she have been surprised by him who announced that she was to bear a child, since she would herself have expected to become a mother according to the law of nature?

— Gregory of Nyssa, Homily on the Nativity of Christ, on Luke 1:34.2 The inference is explicit: a betrothed woman who expected in the ordinary course to live with her husband would have had no cause for perplexity at the promise of a child; Mary’s astonishment is intelligible only because she had never reckoned on “knowing” a man. Gregory names his own source for the tradition of the consecrated virgin — the “aforementioned apocryphal work,” the second-century Protevangelium of James, which has Mary raised in the Temple and vowed to God from childhood — and reads Luke 1:34 as the canonical confirmation of it.

This is the oldest surviving statement of the vow reading of Luke 1:34, which surfaces in the same generation in Augustine (“which assuredly she would not say, unless she had before vowed herself unto God as a virgin”) and, from the thirteenth century, became the common opinion of the medieval schools, Aquinas defending it by a two-stage vow. Whether the verse will bear the weight of a formal vow — and whether Gregory’s reliance on an apocryphon undercuts it — is weighed adversarially on the argument page “Does Luke 1:34 imply a vow of virginity?” (assessment: contested). Unlike the brethren-claims above, this testimony rests on Gregory’s own words and is verified.

Notes

  • The Marian identification and its pedigree. Gregory was not alone in equating “Mary the mother of James and Joses” with the Virgin: Chrysostom reasons the same way at Matthew 27:56, and the identification had already appeared in an infancy apocryphon, the History of Joseph the Carpenter (§4), from which Lightfoot suggests Gregory may have drawn it. The same equation was also a plank of the Helvidian hypothesis — where, making the brethren Mary’s own children, it sat more naturally — and Jerome exploited it against Helvidius (Against Helvidius 12). In Gregory’s mouth, wedded to the Epiphanian scheme, it is awkward: it asks the reader to believe the evangelists styled the Virgin “mother of James and Joses” after her step-sons rather than her own child. This is the point Lightfoot judged “very untenable” — a conjecture beyond the evidence, and the weakest link in an otherwise ordinary Epiphanian testimony — while leaving Gregory’s Epiphanian core (brethren = Joseph’s sons; the Lord’s brother not of the Twelve) untouched.3
  • Consonant with his settled devotion to the ever-virgin. The idiosyncrasy is one of exegetical mechanics, not of the doctrine itself: Gregory’s commitment to Mary’s virginity is firm and pervasive. In the companion Easter homily preserved here, On the three days between the death and resurrection of our Lord Jesus Christ, he dwells on “the unwed mother, the fatherless flesh, the painless labor, the unblemished birth,” expounding Isaiah 7:14 of a virgin birth free of the pangs of ordinary childbearing.4 His treatise On Virginity makes the ascetic case at length, and his brother Basil and their circle held the perpetual virginity as the mind of the Church.
  • Relation to the three theories. Gregory is squarely Epiphanian — the brethren are Joseph’s sons, no children of Mary — and, like the other Eastern witnesses, shows no awareness of the Hieronymian cousins-theory; his insistence that the Lord’s brother is not James son of Alphaeus of the Twelve cuts directly against it. His peculiar identification of the Theotokos with “the mother of James and Joses” is a private overreach layered on top of that Epiphanian frame, not a fourth position.
