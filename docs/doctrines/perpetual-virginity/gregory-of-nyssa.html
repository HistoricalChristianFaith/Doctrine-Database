Gregory of Nyssa on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 382 (a festal homily; the witness is his second oration On the Resurrection of Christ [In Christi resurrectionem ii], one of the Easter sermons he preached across his later career — the individual homily cannot be dated closely). Gregory (c. 335 – c. 394), bishop of Nyssa in Cappadocia from 372, was the younger brother of Basil of Caesarea and the third of the three Cappadocian Fathers. · Wikipedia
Summary: Gregory holds the Epiphanian reading — the “brethren of the Lord” are Joseph’s sons, not Mary’s, so her perpetual virginity stands. But he joins to it an idiosyncratic and much-criticized twist: he identifies the “Mary the mother of James and Joses” of the Passion and resurrection narratives (Matthew 27:56; Mark 15:40) not with a second Mary but with the Theotokos herself, reckoned the boys’ step-mother because she had taken over the raising of Joseph’s children; and he reads the “James the little” of Mark 15:40 as marked off from “James the son of Alphaeus” — the “great” James, who was one of the Twelve — so that the Lord’s brother is neither of Mary’s body nor of the apostolic Twelve. J. B. Lightfoot filed Gregory in his catena of the “sons of Joseph by a former wife” authorities, next after Ambrosiaster and before Epiphanius, while judging the Marian identification “very untenable.” The homily itself is not among the works surveyed here firsthand, so the claims below rest on Lightfoot’s report of it and are marked ⚠ unverified.

Claims & evidence

The brethren are Joseph’s sons — the Epiphanian reading

Gregory’s underlying position is the standard Eastern one, shared with Clement, Origen, Eusebius, and his fellow Cappadocians: the “brethren of the Lord” are the elder children of Joseph by a former marriage, older step-brothers of Jesus and no blood-kin of Mary, so that Mary bore Jesus alone and remained ever-virgin. J. B. Lightfoot records simply that Gregory “certainly adopted the Epiphanian account.”1 That much aligns him with the whole line of witnesses this timeline sets before Jerome; the interest of his testimony lies in the two peculiar moves he adds to it.

The odd twist: “Mary the mother of James and Joses” is the Theotokos herself

Where most of the tradition kept the Virgin distinct from the “other Mary” who stood by the cross and came to the tomb, Gregory collapses the two. Preaching on the resurrection, he takes “the Mary who is designated in the other Evangelists (besides St. John) the mother of James and Joses” to be “the mother of God and none else” — the Virgin Mary called “mother of James and Joses” not because she bore them but because, as Joseph’s wife, she “undertook the education of these her stepsons.”1 The move is meant to serve the Epiphanian scheme — it keeps the brethren Joseph’s and lets the Virgin be their step-mother — but it does so at the cost of an identification the rest of the tradition, and Gregory’s own century, largely resisted. Lightfoot calls it “the very untenable position” Gregory “takes up” alongside the sound Epiphanian core.1

“James the little” is not James son of Alphaeus of the Twelve

Gregory couples that identification with a second inference from the Gospel wording. Mark 15:40 names among the women “Mary the mother of James the little (or ‘the less’) and of Joses.” Gregory reads the epithet as a deliberate contrast: this James is “the little” precisely to distinguish him from James the son of Alphaeus, who was “the great” because he was numbered among the Twelve Apostles — which the Lord’s brother was not.1 This places Gregory, on the “James the Just not one of the Twelve” question, with Hegesippus, the Clementine writings, Cyril of Jerusalem, Marius Victorinus, and the Apostolic Constitutions — a further witness against the Hieronymian reading, which folds the Lord’s brother into the Twelve by identifying him with James son of Alphaeus. Gregory does the opposite: he holds the two Jameses firmly apart and keeps the Lord’s brother outside the apostolic college.

Notes

  • The Marian identification and its pedigree. Gregory was not alone in equating “Mary the mother of James and Joses” with the Virgin: Chrysostom reasons the same way at Matthew 27:56, and the identification had already appeared in an infancy apocryphon, the History of Joseph the Carpenter (§4), from which Lightfoot suggests Gregory may have drawn it. The same equation was also a plank of the Helvidian hypothesis — where, making the brethren Mary’s own children, it sat more naturally — and Jerome exploited it against Helvidius (Against Helvidius 12). In Gregory’s mouth, wedded to the Epiphanian scheme, it is awkward: it asks the reader to believe the evangelists styled the Virgin “mother of James and Joses” after her step-sons rather than her own child. This is the point Lightfoot judged “very untenable” — a conjecture beyond the evidence, and the weakest link in an otherwise ordinary Epiphanian testimony — while leaving Gregory’s Epiphanian core (brethren = Joseph’s sons; the Lord’s brother not of the Twelve) untouched.2
  • Consonant with his settled devotion to the ever-virgin. The idiosyncrasy is one of exegetical mechanics, not of the doctrine itself: Gregory’s commitment to Mary’s virginity is firm and pervasive. In the companion Easter homily preserved here, On the three days between the death and resurrection of our Lord Jesus Christ, he dwells on “the unwed mother, the fatherless flesh, the painless labor, the unblemished birth,” expounding Isaiah 7:14 of a virgin birth free of the pangs of ordinary childbearing.3 His treatise On Virginity makes the ascetic case at length, and his brother Basil and their circle held the perpetual virginity as the mind of the Church.
  • Relation to the three theories. Gregory is squarely Epiphanian — the brethren are Joseph’s sons, no children of Mary — and, like the other Eastern witnesses, shows no awareness of the Hieronymian cousins-theory; his insistence that the Lord’s brother is not James son of Alphaeus of the Twelve cuts directly against it. His peculiar identification of the Theotokos with “the mother of James and Joses” is a private overreach layered on top of that Epiphanian frame, not a fourth position.
