The Second Council of Constantinople on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: 553 (the fifth ecumenical council, convened at Constantinople by the emperor Justinian I and presided over by Patriarch Eutychius. Its business was the “Three Chapters” controversy and the reception of Chalcedon’s two-natures Christology; among its fourteen anathemas (the Capitula) is the confession quoted here) · Wikipedia
Summary: The point at which the perpetual virginity passes from patristic confession into the creed of an ecumenical council. Anathematizing those who deny the Word’s two nativities, the council names the mother of the second nativity “Mother of God and always a virgin” (aeiparthenos) — spoken not as the thing to be defended but as settled, shared truth. Like John of Damascus, it attests the virginity itself and says nothing to decide the identity of the Lord’s brethren.

Claims & evidence

“Mother of God and always a virgin”

The council’s second capitulum anathematizes anyone who will not confess the two nativities of the Word — the eternal generation from the Father and the temporal birth from Mary. In naming the second birth it calls Mary aeiparthenos, “always a virgin”:

If anyone shall not confess that the Word of God has two nativities, the one from all eternity of the Father, without time and without body; the other in these last days, coming down from heaven and being made flesh of the holy and glorious Mary, Mother of God and always a virgin, and born of her: let him be anathema.

— Second Council of Constantinople, The Capitula of the Council, Anathema 2.1 The doctrinal weight of the sentence is Christological — its point is the single subject of the two births, against those who would divide the Word from the man born of Mary. But the perpetual virginity is presupposed in the very title by which it names her: not merely “virgin” (of the conception) but always a virgin, the standing Greek epithet aeiparthenos. By the mid-sixth century the doctrine needs no argument in the East; it is simply part of how an ecumenical council names the Mother of God.

What it settles — and what it leaves open

The canon confesses the perpetual virginity and, by placing it in the title aeiparthenos (“ever-virgin”), reaches past the conception to the whole of Mary’s life. It offers no account of the “brethren of the Lord,” so it says nothing to decide between the Epiphanian (sons of Joseph by a former marriage) and Hieronymian (cousins) readings. Like John of Damascus, Leo the Great, and the councils around it, it attests the virginity itself and leaves the genealogy of the brethren untouched — and so cannot be filed by camp.

Notes

  • A creedal, not an incidental, affirmation. That the epithet is aeiparthenos — “ever-virgin,” not just “virgin” — is the substance: the fifth ecumenical council builds the perpetual virginity into the church’s formal confession of Christ. Its date, 553, is the answer to the charge that the doctrine is a “late invention” demanded only in the modern era; that objection is weighed on this timeline’s “late invention” argument (assessment: weak).
  • The Western counterpart. Where this Eastern council names the title in a single clause, the Latin Lateran Council of 649 a century later spells the triple virginity out (before, in, and after the birth). Pope John Paul II later paired the two — Constantinople II (553) “speaks of Mary as ‘ever virgin,’” and the Lateran Synod (649) teaches that her virginity “remained incorrupted, and even after the birth… remained intact” — as the church’s two conciliar statements of the doctrine.
  • Text and edition. The English is the translation of Henry R. Percival in the Nicene and Post-Nicene Fathers, 2nd series, vol. 14 (1900), The Seven Ecumenical Councils.
