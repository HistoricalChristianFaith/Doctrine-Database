Ambrosiaster on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 375 (the Commentary on Galatians, part of the earliest complete Latin commentary on the thirteen Pauline epistles; “Ambrosiaster” is the modern name for the anonymous Roman churchman who wrote it under Pope Damasus, c. 366–384 — long transmitted under the name of Ambrose, and identified by older writers with a “Hilary,” whence J. B. Lightfoot’s label “the Ambrosian Hilary”) · Wikipedia
Summary: Ambrosiaster reads the “James the Lord’s brother” of Galatians 1:19 as a son of Joseph, called “the brother of the Lord” in exactly the way Jesus was called “the son of Joseph” — a reputed relationship resting on Joseph’s reputed fatherhood, not a blood-tie to Mary. This is the Epiphanian reading. He drives it home with a reductio against the deniers of the perpetual virginity: if the brethren are Mary’s true sons, then Joseph must be Christ’s true father — which no Christian will grant. Like Hilary of Poitiers and Epiphanius, he sees only two options, the Epiphanian solution and the outright denial, and shows no knowledge of Jerome’s cousins-theory. Lightfoot set him in his catena of the “sons of Joseph by a former wife” authorities, immediately after Hilary of Poitiers and before Gregory of Nyssa, judging that “his testimony entirely coincides with that of his greater namesake.”

Claims & evidence

James is Joseph’s son — “brother of the Lord” as Jesus is “son of Joseph”

Coming to Paul’s words “I saw none of the other apostles, except James the Lord’s brother” (Galatians 1:19), Ambrosiaster stops to say who this James was and why he bears the title:

This James was the son of Joseph, who was therefore called the brother of the Lord, because Joseph was also called the father of Him, the Lord.

— Commentary on Galatians (on Galatians 1:19).1 The logic is a strict parallel. Jesus is called “the son of Joseph” in the Gospels — Ambrosiaster cites Mary’s “your father and I have sought you” (Luke 2:48) and Philip’s “Jesus of Nazareth, the son of Joseph” (John 1:45) — yet Joseph is only His reputed father, not His natural one. By the very same usage James and the rest are called “brethren of the Lord”: they are Joseph’s sons, kin to Jesus through Joseph’s household, not children of Mary. The title is legal and domestic, not a claim of common parentage from the Virgin.

The reductio: true brothers would make Joseph the true father

Ambrosiaster then turns the point against those who read the brethren as Mary’s own later children — the Helvidian denial of the perpetual virginity. His argument is that the two titles stand or fall together: whoever insists that “brother of the Lord” must mean a natural, blood brother is bound, by parity, to make “son of Joseph” mean a natural, begotten son — and so to make Joseph the true father of Christ, denying the virgin birth. In his own words: “For if these were his true brothers, then Joseph will be the true father; because the one who called Joseph his father also called his brothers James and the others.”1 J. B. Lightfoot translated the whole comment and quoted it in full:

The Lord is called the brother of James and the rest in the same way in which He is also designated the son of Joseph. For some in a fit of madness impiously assert and contend that these were true brothers of the Lord, being sons of Mary, allowing at the same time that Joseph, though not His true father, was so called nevertheless. For if these were His true brothers, then Joseph will be His true father; for he who called Joseph His Father also called James and the rest His brothers.

— Lightfoot’s rendering of the Latin.2 Lightfoot’s verdict: “his testimony entirely coincides with that of his greater namesake” — Hilary of Poitiers — and “he sees only the alternative of denying the perpetual virginity as Helvidius did, or accepting the solution of the Protevangelium; and he unhesitatingly adopts the latter.”2

Ignorant of the cousins-theory — another witness against its antiquity

As with Hilary of Poitiers, the negative testimony matters as much as the positive. Ambrosiaster is a learned Roman commentator writing in the mid-370s, expounding the very verse — “James the Lord’s brother” — on which Jerome would soon rest the Hieronymian reading that the brethren were maternal cousins, sons of Mary of Clopas. Yet Ambrosiaster frames the question as a straight choice between the Epiphanian solution and the Helvidian denial and never so much as mentions the cousins-hypothesis, which would have suited his purpose at least as well. Lightfoot made him one of the “two Hilaries” whose silence tells against the antiquity of Jerome’s reading: “there is not the slightest indication that the Hieronymian solution ever occurred to any individual or sect or church, until it was put forward by Jerome himself. If it had been otherwise, writers like Origen, the two Hilaries, and Epiphanius, who discuss the question, could not have failed to notice it.”2

What Ambrosiaster’s testimony settles

Ambrosiaster gives the Epiphanian reading a second independent Latin voice within a generation of Hilary of Poitiers and still before Jerome’s Against Helvidius (c. 383). His distinctive contribution is the grammatical-parallel argument — “brother of the Lord” is titular exactly as “son of Joseph” is titular — which fastens the identity of the brethren directly to the words of the Gospel rather than to the infancy apocrypha. And his silence about the cousins-theory adds one more name to the cumulative case that Jerome’s solution was a genuine novelty of the 380s. Lightfoot accordingly filed “Ambrosiaster” in his conspectus of the “sons of Joseph by a former wife” authorities, next after Hilary of Poitiers and before Gregory of Nyssa.2

Notes

  • Who is Ambrosiaster. “Ambrosiaster” (“the would-be Ambrose”) is the name scholars give to the anonymous author of a complete Latin commentary on the thirteen epistles of Paul, written at Rome under Pope Damasus. His work circulated for centuries under the name of Ambrose of Milan, and some manuscripts and older writers ascribed it to a “Hilary” — hence Lightfoot’s tag “the Ambrosian Hilary,” and his care to keep this witness distinct from Hilary of Poitiers, whom he calls the “greater namesake.” The two are separate men who happen to give the same Epiphanian answer. He is likewise not the aged rhetorician Marius Victorinus, the other early Latin commentator on Galatians 1:19.
  • Relation to the three theories. Ambrosiaster is squarely Epiphanian: the brethren are Joseph’s sons, kin to Jesus through Joseph and no children of Mary, so that Mary bears Jesus alone and remains ever-virgin. His reductio (true brothers → true father) is aimed precisely at the Helvidian denial, and he shows no awareness of the Hieronymian cousins-reading.
