John Wesley on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 1749 (A Letter to a Roman Catholic, Dublin, 18 July 1749; John Wesley, 1703 – 1791, the Anglican priest and founder of Methodism) · Wikipedia
Summary: The latest of the notable Protestants to profess the doctrine, and the one who carries the line two full centuries past the Reformers. In A Letter to a Roman Catholic — an irenic appeal written at Dublin in 1749 to cool the sectarian hatred between Protestants and Catholics in Ireland — Wesley sets out a creed of “the faith of a true Protestant,” and in it confesses that Christ was “born of the blessed Virgin Mary, who, as well after as before she brought Him forth, continued a pure and unspotted virgin.” Like Zwingli before him, his statement affirms the virginity itself and never takes up the identity of the “brethren of the Lord” (as Luther and Calvin elsewhere do), so he cannot be filed by camp; the point is simply that a leading eighteenth-century Protestant — no Roman controversialist but the founder of Methodism — still held Mary ever-virgin.

Claims & evidence

“As well after as before… a pure and unspotted virgin” — the 1749 Letter

Section 7 of the Letter is a compact confession of the person and work of Christ, cast in the idiom of the ancient creeds. Within it, professing the Incarnation, Wesley affirms Mary’s virginity after the birth as well as before — the perpetual virginity in its plain sense:

I believe that He was made man, joining the human nature with the divine in one person; being conceived by the singular operation of the Holy Ghost, and born of the blessed Virgin Mary, who, as well after as before she brought Him forth, continued a pure and unspotted virgin.

— A Letter to a Roman Catholic, §7 (Dublin, 18 July 1749).1 The phrasing — “as well after as before” — is unambiguous: it is precisely the claim that Mary’s virginity outlasted the Nativity, the point Helvidius denied and the point the whole timeline turns on. Wesley states it not as a private opinion but as an article of the common faith he is inviting his Catholic reader to recognize as shared ground.

The setting: an irenic creed of shared belief

The confession is not a piece of Marian controversy but part of an appeal for peace. Writing amid the sectarian bitterness of Ireland, Wesley lays out what “a true Protestant” believes precisely in order to show a Roman Catholic how much the two hold in common, urging that both “resolve… to help each other on in whatever we are agreed leads to the kingdom” and give over “this endless jangling about opinions.”2 That framing sharpens the witness: the perpetual virginity appears among the things Wesley reckons a Protestant may profess without controversy — not a Roman peculiarity he is conceding, but a belief he assumes as held in common.

Notes

  • Camp: unfiled. As with Zwingli, the statement defends the virginity itself and never says who the “brethren of the Lord” were, so Wesley cannot be classed Epiphanian (Joseph’s sons by a former wife) or Hieronymian (cousins) — unlike Luther and Calvin, who elsewhere come down for the cousin-reading. His witness bears on the virginity, not the brethren-question.
  • The latest of the Protestant witnesses. Wesley extends the line of magisterial-and-later Protestants who kept the doctrine — Zwingli (1522), Luther (1523), Calvin (1562) — a further two centuries, to an eighteenth-century founder of a major Protestant movement. That reach is the standing reply to the charge that the perpetual virginity was a Roman invention the Reformation swept away: it bears directly on the “late invention” charge (assessment: weak), where the doctrine’s retention across the Protestant centuries is among the counters.
  • A separate Wesley reading of “until.” On the grammatical crux, Wesley — like Calvin — elsewhere reads a Scriptural “until” non-terminatively (Matthew 18:34, the unpayable debt, as a debt never discharged), a datum noted on the “heōs hou” argument (assessment: weak).
