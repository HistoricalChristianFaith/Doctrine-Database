Hilary of Poitiers on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 353 (the Commentary on Matthew, Hilary’s earliest work — the first Latin passage-by-passage commentary on a Gospel to survive almost intact, written before his exile of 356; Hilary, bishop of Poitiers in Gaul and later “the Athanasius of the West,” died 367/368) · Wikipedia
Summary: Hilary is the earliest Latin father to state the Epiphanian reading outright — that the “brethren of the Lord” were sons of Joseph by an earlier marriage, not children of Mary. He argues it from the scene at the cross: had Mary borne other sons, Jesus would not have needed to hand her over to the care of the apostle John. And — the point J. B. Lightfoot pressed — a Latin bishop discussing the very question around 353 knows only two options, the Epiphanian solution and the outright denial of the virginity; of the Hieronymian cousins-theory he betrays no knowledge whatever, because Jerome had not yet devised it. Lightfoot filed him in his catena of the “sons of Joseph by a former wife” authorities, immediately after Eusebius.

Claims & evidence

The brethren are Joseph’s sons by a former marriage — argued from the cross

Expounding the opening of Matthew, Hilary comes to the “brothers” of Jesus and to those who make them a weapon against Mary’s virginity. He answers them from the Passion narrative — from Christ’s committing his mother to the beloved disciple:

And yet some very depraved men take from this the basis of their view that there were many brothers of our Lord as a point of tradition. If there had been sons of Mary who were not rather produced from a previous marriage of Joseph’s, Mary never would have been transferred to the apostle John as his mother at the time of the Passion, nor would the Lord have said to them both, “Woman, behold your son,” and to John, “Behold your mother,” unless perhaps he was leaving his disciple’s filial love in order to comfort her who was left behind.

— Commentary on Matthew 1.4 (on Matthew 1:18–25).1 Two things are settled here. First, Hilary takes the brethren to be “sons… produced from a previous marriage of Joseph’s” — the plain Epiphanian answer, step-brothers of Jesus and no blood-kin of Mary. Second, his argument for it is the distinctive one that recurs down the Epiphanian line: at the cross Jesus entrusts his mother to John (John 19:26–27), which would have been needless, even improper, had Mary’s own younger sons been standing by to keep her. The care of a son is bestowed on John precisely because there was no other son to bestow it. Lightfoot summarised the passage: Hilary “denounces those who ‘claim authority for their opinion… from the fact of its being recorded that our Lord had several brothers,’” adding that “if these had been sons of Mary and not rather sons of Joseph, the offspring of a former marriage, she would never at the time of the passion have been transferred to the Apostle John to be his mother.”2 The inference is forceful but not decisive, and Hilary’s “never would have” overstates it: the brethren were unbelievers and apparently absent at the cross (John 7:5), so a dying Jesus had reason to turn to the one believing intimate present, and even a defender of the perpetual virginity such as Richard Bauckham reads the entrustment as a theological act — Mary received into the community of disciples — rather than a statement of who her sons were. The argument is weighed on its own page, “Does Jesus entrusting Mary to John (John 19:26–27) show she had no other sons?” (assessment: contested).

Ignorant of the cousins-theory — a witness against its antiquity

The negative testimony matters as much as the positive. Hilary is a learned Latin bishop, writing about 353, expressly refuting those who deny Mary’s perpetual virginity — the exact controversy in which Jerome would, thirty years later, unveil the Hieronymian reading that the brethren were maternal cousins. Yet Hilary shows no sign of ever having heard of it. He frames the question as a straight choice between the Epiphanian solution and the Helvidian denial, and reaches for the former; the cousins-hypothesis, which would have served his purpose at least as well, never enters his field of view. Lightfoot drew the conclusion for the whole early tradition: “there is not the slightest indication that the Hieronymian solution ever occurred to any individual or sect or church, until it was put forward by Jerome himself. If it had been otherwise, writers like Origen, the two Hilaries, and Epiphanius, who discuss the question, could not have failed to notice it.” So Hilary “not only adopts the Epiphanian solution, but shows himself entirely ignorant of the Hieronymian.”3

What Hilary’s testimony settles

Hilary carries the Epiphanian reading into the Latin West a full generation before Jerome. He is one of the earliest Latin writers to touch the brethren question at all, and the first to answer it in his own voice; his argument from the cross gives the Epiphanian view a fresh, Scriptural leg to stand on, independent of the infancy apocrypha that had seeded it. And his silence about the cousins-theory — matched, Lightfoot noted, by the “Ambrosian Hilary” (Ambrosiaster) and by Epiphanius — is part of the cumulative case that Jerome’s solution was a genuine novelty of the 380s, not a reading the Church had always held. Lightfoot accordingly set “Hilary of Poitiers” in his conspectus of the “sons of Joseph by a former wife” authorities, next after Eusebius and before Gregory of Nyssa.3

Notes

  • Does Hilary rest the reading on the Protevangelium / infancy apocrypha? No. The stepbrother idea first surfaces around the middle of the second century in the apocryphal infancy narratives — above all the Protevangelium of James, which makes Joseph an aged widower with sons before his betrothal — and those narratives are widely taken to be its ultimate source. But Hilary himself makes no appeal to them. In the one place he states the Epiphanian view (Commentary on Matthew 1.4, quoted above) he grounds it entirely on the canonical Gospel text — the entrustment of Mary to the beloved disciple at the cross (John 19:26–27) — and cites no apocryphal infancy source. He is thus the earliest Latin writer to give the reading a fresh, Scriptural argument of its own rather than resting it on the Protevangelium.1 (That the belief may nonetheless have reached him downstream of that apocryphal stream is a separate question from what he argues from — the origin-and-antiquity of the tradition is weighed at “Is the second-century stepbrother tradition genuine historical memory or a construct?”)
  • Two Hilaries. This is Hilary of Poitiers (c. 315–367), the Gallic bishop and Trinitarian champion. He is not to be confused with the “Ambrosian Hilary” — Ambrosiaster, the anonymous Roman commentator on Paul once passed off under Ambrose’s name — whose comment on Galatians 1:19 states the same Epiphanian reading (James “the son of Joseph… called the brother of the Lord”) and, Lightfoot observed, “entirely coincides with that of his greater namesake.” Ambrosiaster is a separate witness on his own detail page.4
  • Relation to the three theories. Hilary is squarely Epiphanian: the brethren are Joseph’s sons by a former marriage, Mary bears no child but Jesus and remains ever-virgin, and Joseph — unlike the later Hieronymian portrait — is a widower who had married before. The perpetual virginity itself Hilary everywhere assumes; his commentary reads Matthew 1:25 (“knew her not till”) as leaving her virginity after the birth untouched.
