J. B. Lightfoot on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 1865 (“The Brethren of the Lord,” a dissertation appended to St Paul’s Epistle to the Galatians; Joseph Barber Lightfoot, 1828–1889, Bishop of Durham and one of the foremost Victorian New-Testament scholars) · Wikipedia
Summary: The scholar whose taxonomy organises this whole timeline is himself a witness on it. In his 1865 study Lightfoot named the three historic solutions — Epiphanian (Joseph’s sons by a former wife), Helvidian (Mary’s own later children), and Hieronymian (maternal cousins) — assembled the patristic catena, and reached his own verdict: on the Scriptural evidence the Hieronymian reading “must be abandoned,” and of the remaining two “the balance of the argument is against the Helvidian and in favour of the Epiphanian.” He is thus a definite Epiphanian witness — the brethren are step-brothers, sons of Joseph by an earlier marriage — who reads the infancy language (“till she brought forth,” “firstborn”) as neutral: it denies a prior union without asserting a later one, and consecrates the child that opens the womb whether or not others follow.

Claims & evidence

The infancy language is neutral — “till” and “firstborn” neither assert nor imply later children

Lightfoot takes up the two stock Helvidian proof-texts — Matthew’s “knew her not till she had brought forth” (Matthew 1:25) and Luke’s “firstborn son” (Luke 2:7) — and denies that either implies siblings, the context in each case suggesting an explanation “which does not decide anything one way or the other”:

St. Matthew’s expression however, ‘till she brought forth,’ as appears from the context, is intended simply to show that Jesus was not begotten in the course of nature; and thus, while it denies any previous intercourse with her husband, it neither asserts nor implies any subsequent intercourse. Again, the prominent idea conveyed by the term ‘firstborn’ to a Jew would be not the birth of other children, but the special consecration of this one… Thus ‘firstborn’ does not necessarily suggest ‘later-born,’ any more than ‘son’ suggests ‘daughter.’ The two words together describe the condition under which in obedience to the law a child was consecrated to God. The ‘firstborn son’ is in fact the Evangelist’s equivalent for the ‘male that openeth the womb.’

— “The Brethren of the Lord.”1 He grants what is fair to the other side — that had the Evangelists thought the perpetual virginity “a matter of such paramount importance as it was held to be in the fourth and following centuries, they would have avoided expressions which are at least ambiguous” — but concludes that “these expressions are not in themselves fatal to such a belief.” This is exactly the finding weighed on this timeline’s infancy-language argument (assessment: weak) — where Lightfoot is cited to the same effect alongside Richard Bauckham — and on its narrower “heōs hou” refinement (assessment: weak).

His verdict: the Epiphanian reading is best supported — the brethren are Joseph’s sons by a former wife

Unlike the Reformers on this timeline (Luther, Calvin, Zwingli), who defend the virginity without saying who the brethren were, Lightfoot files himself squarely by camp. Weighing the three solutions on the Scriptural notices, he abandons the Hieronymian (cousins) reading and prefers the Epiphanian:

Thus it would appear that, taking the scriptural notices alone, the Hieronymian account must be abandoned; while of the remaining two the balance of the argument is against the Helvidian and in favour of the Epiphanian.

— “The Brethren of the Lord.”2 The Epiphanian solution he adopts is the reading that “these brethren were in fact sons of Joseph by a former wife, before he espoused the Virgin; and that they are therefore called the Lord’s brethren only in the same way in which Joseph is called His father” — no blood-kin of Mary at all. He grounds the preference on tradition as well as Scripture: “there is not the slightest indication that the Hieronymian solution ever occurred to any individual or sect or church, until it was put forward by Jerome himself,” whereas “the Epiphanian account has the highest claims to the sanction of tradition… this solution seems especially to represent the Palestinian view.”2

Notes

  • Camp: Epiphanian. Lightfoot both holds the perpetual virginity and commits on the identity of the brethren — step-brothers, Joseph’s sons by an earlier marriage. He is the modern witness on this timeline for the Epiphanian reading; where Luther (outright) and Calvin (more cautiously) came down for the rival Hieronymian cousin-reading, Lightfoot argues against it. Zwingli and Wesley, by contrast, defend the virginity but leave the brethren-question open, and so “cannot be filed by camp.”
  • The framing scholar in his own right. Because his three-name taxonomy (Epiphanian / Helvidian / Hieronymian) organises this doctrine’s summary and is cited across its detail pages, it is easy to read Lightfoot only as the neutral adjudicator. He was not neutral: he weighed the evidence and came down for the Epiphanian reading. His conspectus of the patristic authorities — the “catena” that supplies many of this timeline’s classifications — is offered precisely to show how far that reading “can plead the prestige of tradition.”
  • A caution he concedes. Lightfoot does not pretend the case is airtight: he allows that the infancy expressions are “at least ambiguous and might be taken to imply the contrary,” and that had the perpetual virginity mattered as much to the Evangelists as to the fourth century they might have written more guardedly. His claim is the modest one this wiki also reaches — that the language does not settle the question against the doctrine — not that it proves it.
