Bonosus of Sardica and Jovinian on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 390 (two Western contemporaries of Helvidius, both condemned by synods at the close of the fourth century for impugning the perpetual virginity: Jovinian, a lapsed monk who taught at Rome, condemned by synods at Rome and at Milan about 390; Bonosus, bishop of Sardica in Illyria, condemned by the synod of Capua in 392. Neither man’s own writing survives entire — Jovinian’s book is known through the answers of Ambrose and Jerome, Bonosus only through conciliar notices) · Wikipedia (Jovinian) · Wikipedia (Bonosus)
Summary: J. B. Lightfoot names two further Western voices raised against the perpetual virginity in the same decade as Helvidius, and files both in the Helvidian column of his conspectus — but they did not hold identical positions, and Lightfoot marks the second with a query. Bonosus, bishop of Sardica, is reported to have held the plain Helvidian reading, that the Lord’s mother bore children to Joseph after Jesus; his own words do not survive, so his teaching is ⚠ unverified, known only from Lightfoot and the record of the synod of Capua (392) that condemned him. Jovinian, an anti-ascetic monk condemned at Rome and Milan about 390, is the better attested but the odder witness: his central campaign was against the exaltation of virginity over marriage — “a virgin is no better as such than a wife” — and his heterodoxy about Mary was not the Helvidian claim of later children but a denial of her virginity in the birth itself (in partu): “She was a virgin when she conceived, but not a virgin when she brought forth.” That formula, which differs from Helvidius’, is preserved firsthand in the synodal letter of the Council of Milan drafted by Ambrose, and Jovinian’s wider case was answered at length by Jerome in the two books Against Jovinianus.

Claims & evidence

Bonosus of Sardica — the Helvidian denial, condemned at Capua

Bonosus left no book, and what survives of his teaching on the brethren is only what a later hand reports. Lightfoot gathers him with Helvidius as one who advocated the same reading — that the “brethren of the Lord” were the true sons of Joseph and Mary:

Somewhere about the year 380 Helvidius, who resided in Rome, published a treatise in which he maintained that the Lord’s brethren were sons of Joseph and Mary. He seems to have succeeded in convincing a considerable number of persons, for contemporary writers speak of the Helvidians as a party. These views were moreover advocated by Bonosus, bishop of Sardica in Illyria, about the same time, and apparently also by Jovinianus a monk probably of Milan. The former was condemned by a synod assembled at Capua (AD 392), and the latter by synods held at Rome and at Milan (about AD 390).

— J. B. Lightfoot, “The Brethren of the Lord,” §22.1 Because Bonosus’ own statement of the position is lost — the record is the hostile summary of a council and the report of a later scholar — his teaching on the brethren remains, on this wiki’s rule, ⚠ unverified. What is firm is the conciliar fact: the synod of Capua in 392 took up his case and referred the sentence to Anysius of Thessalonica and the Macedonian bishops, and the see of Rome under Siricius held him a heretic on the point.

Jovinian — not later children, but virginity denied in the birth (in partu)

Jovinian’s error about Mary is often lumped with the Helvidian denial, but the men who condemned him were careful to distinguish it, and the distinction is the whole point. His claim was not that Mary bore other children afterward, but that she was not a virgin in the act of giving birth — that the birth was an ordinary parturition which opened the womb. The synod of Milan’s reply to Pope Siricius, drawn up in all probability by Ambrose himself, quotes the Jovinianist formula and answers it:

How great is the madness of their dismal barkings, that the same persons should say that Christ could not be born of a virgin, and yet assert that women, after having given birth to human pledges, remain virgins?… But from their perverse ways they are induced to say ‘She was a virgin when she conceived, but not a virgin when she brought forth.’ Could she then conceive as a virgin, and yet not be able to bring forth as a virgin, when conception always precedes, and birth follows?

— Ambrose (with Sabinus, Bassianus, and the rest), Letter 42.4, the reply of the Council of Milan to Siricius (c. 390).2 Ambrose meets it with the same Epiphanian-tradition proof-text he would deploy in his later treatise — the “gate of the sanctuary… which remains shut” of Ezekiel 44: “Is not Mary this gate…? therefore it shall be shut after birth; for as a virgin she both conceived and brought forth.” The editor of the letter observes that the council “argue in especial detail against his argument with regard to the Virgin Mary, which differs from that of Helvidius and other assailants of the” ever-virgin (ἀεὶ παρθένος) — so that Jovinian’s Mariology is a denial of the virginity in partu, a distinct moment from the post partum virginity Helvidius and Bonosus denied.

The wider campaign — “a virgin is no better than a wife”

Mary was only one front. Jovinian’s book advanced four propositions, of which the first — the levelling of virginity with marriage — was the engine of the rest. Jerome, who answered the whole in two books, sets it out in Jovinian’s own terms:

He says that “virgins, widows, and married women, who have been once passed through the layer of Christ, if they are on a par in other respects, are of equal merit.”

— Jerome, Against Jovinianus i.3.3 Jerome read the manoeuvre exactly: “his object in proclaiming the excellence of marriage was only to disparage virginity. For when the less is put upon a level with the greater, the lower profits by comparison, but the higher suffers wrong.” This anti-ascetic aim is the common thread that ties Jovinian to Helvidius and Bonosus — all three set themselves against the age’s exaltation of celibacy, and used the mother of the Lord as the test case — even though Jovinian reached the perpetual virginity by a different road, attacking the birth rather than positing later children.

Where they stand among the three theories

In the conspectus that closes his dissertation, Lightfoot sorts every witness under the reading it supports, and places both men in the “sons of Joseph and Mary” column with Tertullian, Helvidius, and the Antidicomarianites — but appends a query to Jovinian’s name:

A. Sons of Joseph and Mary: Tertullian; Helvidius; Bonosus; Jovinianus (?); Antidicomarianites.

— Lightfoot, conspectus of authorities.4 The parenthetical “(?)” is Lightfoot’s own signal that Jovinian sits uneasily under the heading: his denial of the virginity fell on the birth, not on any later childbearing, and his surviving argument (as reported by Ambrose) is not the Helvidian argument at all. Bonosus, by contrast, is filed there without a query — the reports make him a straightforward Helvidian — but only the reports survive.

Notes

  • Three moments of the virginity. The doctrine classically distinguishes Mary’s virginity before the birth (ante partum, the virginal conception), in the birth (in partu, that the birth left her physically intact), and after the birth (post partum, that she bore no other children). The Helvidian party — Helvidius, Bonosus, the Antidicomarianites — denied the post partum virginity by making the “brethren” Mary’s later sons. Jovinian denied the in partu virginity, granting the virginal conception but insisting the delivery was ordinary. The two are different errors, which is why Ambrose and Lightfoot alike keep them apart, and why Jovinian’s witness bears on the perpetual virginity from a different angle than Bonosus’. The in partu claim Jovinian denied was no innovation of Ambrose’s: its earliest narrative form is the Salome episode of the Protevangelium of James (§§19–20, c. 150), where a doubting Salome tests Mary’s virginity after the birth and her hand withers — the seed of the doctrine Ambrose defends here with the shut “gate” of Ezekiel 44.
  • Attestation is uneven. Bonosus’ view is ⚠ unverified: no firsthand text survives, only Lightfoot’s notice and the conciliar record of Capua. Jovinian’s Marian claim, by contrast, is verified at first hand — his very formula (“a virgin when she conceived, but not a virgin when she brought forth”) is quoted in Ambrose’s synodal letter — and his four propositions are preserved verbatim by Jerome. His own book is lost, but his opponents quote him closely enough that the position is not in doubt.
  • Bonosus and the “Bonosians.” Bonosus of Sardica gave his name to a later sect, the Bonosiaci, whom the sources associate both with his denial of the perpetual virginity and with an adoptionist Christology; the two charges are variously reported, and some of the later notices may conflate him with other controversialists of the same name. What the earliest record fixes is the condemnation of 392 and the association, in Lightfoot’s reading, with the Helvidian denial of the perpetual virginity.
  • Relation to the timeline. These two stand at the hinge of the controversy, between Helvidius and Jerome (both c. 383) and Ambrose’s De Institutione Virginis (c. 392): it was precisely the party gathered around Bonosus and Jovinian that Ambrose wrote his treatise to refute, and the synodal letter quoted here is the same Ambrose answering the same movement a year or two earlier. Jerome’s Against Jovinianus (393) is the Roman counterpart, as his Against Helvidius (383) had been to the earlier phase.
