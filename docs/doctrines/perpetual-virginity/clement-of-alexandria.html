Clement of Alexandria on the perpetual virginity of Mary

Dates: c. 200 (the Alexandrian teacher flourished c. 190–202, when the Paedagogus and Stromateis were composed; he died c. 215. The Hypotyposeis, from which the note on Jude survives, belongs to this maturity) · Wikipedia
Summary: Clement is the earliest father to state the Epiphanian solution outright and in his own voice — that the “brethren of the Lord” were sons of Joseph, older step-relations of Jesus, not children of Mary. Commenting on the Epistle of Jude, he explains why its author calls himself “brother of James” rather than brother of the Lord: Jude was “one of the sons of Joseph,” and out of reverence forbore to claim the nearer title. The remark survives only in a Latin abridgement of his lost Hypotyposeis made by Cassiodorus in the sixth century, but the statement about Jude’s relationship must be Clement’s own, since Cassiodorus, a Latin churchman, would have held the rival Hieronymian view and had no motive to insert it. A separate extract preserved by Eusebius — Clement’s remark that “there were two Jameses” — has sometimes been read as pointing the other way, but it neither states nor implies that there were only two, and does not contradict the plain Epiphanian note on Jude. J. B. Lightfoot judged the statement “explicit” and placed Clement second, after the Gospel of Peter and Protevangelium, in his roll of the “sons of Joseph by a former wife” authorities.

Claims & evidence

Jude was “one of the sons of Joseph” — the brethren are Joseph’s, not Mary’s

In his Adumbrationes — brief notes on the Catholic Epistles, surviving in Cassiodorus’ Latin — Clement opens his comments on Jude by explaining the epistle’s self-designation. Why does Jude, kin to the Lord, style himself only “brother of James”? Because, Clement answers, he was a son of Joseph, and modesty kept him from claiming the Lord as his brother:

Jude, who wrote the Catholic Epistle, the brother of the sons of Joseph, and very religious, whilst knowing the near relationship of the Lord, yet did not say that he himself was His brother. But what said he? “Jude, a servant of Jesus Christ,”—of Him as Lord; but “the brother of James.” For this is true; he was His brother, (the son) of Joseph.

— Clement of Alexandria, Comments on the Epistle of Jude.1 The reasoning is unmistakably Epiphanian: Jude and James are “the sons of Joseph,” brothers of the Lord in the household sense but not His mother’s children, so that Jude can rightly be called “brother of James” and only in a looser way “brother of the Lord.” Lightfoot renders the same passage “Jude… being one of the sons of Joseph and [the Lord’s] brother… nevertheless did not say he was His brother,” and calls the testimony “explicit” — Clement “puts forward the Epiphanian solution” plainly.2

The “two Jameses” extract in Eusebius does not contradict this

Against the Latin note stands a Greek fragment Eusebius drew from the sixth and seventh books of the same Hypotyposeis: Clement there says that after the resurrection the Lord gave the gnosis to James the Just, John, and Peter, and adds, “Now there are two Jameses: one called the Just, who was thrown down from the pinnacle of the temple… and another who was beheaded.”3 Because Clement counts “two” — James the Just (the Lord’s brother) and James the son of Zebedee — and omits James the son of Alphaeus, his authority has often been claimed for the Hieronymian view, which identifies the Lord’s brother James with the apostle son of Alphaeus. But the inference will not hold. As Lightfoot observes, “Clement says that there were two of the name of James, but he neither states nor implies that there were two only. His sole object was to distinguish the son of Zebedee from the Lord’s brother; and the son of Alphaeus… did not occur to his mind when he penned this sentence.” There is “nothing which contradicts the Latin extract” — and the explicit statement on Jude, that the brethren are sons of Joseph, remains Clement’s settled view.2

Notes

  • The statement is Clement’s, not Cassiodorus’. The note on Jude reaches us only through the Adumbrationes, a Latin abridgement that Cassiodorus (c. 485–585) says he made of Clement’s notes on the Catholic Epistles, “purging” what he thought unsafe. That raises the question whether the Epiphanian remark could be the translator’s. Lightfoot answers that it cannot: “the statement about the relationship of Jude must be Clement’s own and cannot have been inserted by Cassiodorus, since Cassiodorus in common with the Latin Church would naturally hold the Hieronymian hypothesis” — that is, would have made the brethren cousins, not Joseph’s sons. A Latin editor purging error would have removed such a line, not added it; its survival is a mark of fidelity to Clement.2
  • Position in the debate. Clement stands second in Lightfoot’s conspectus of the “sons of Joseph by a former wife” authorities — after the Gospel of Peter and the Protevangelium, ahead of Origen, Eusebius, Gregory of Nyssa, Epiphanius, and the Eastern churches.2 He is the first named father, as against an anonymous apocryphon, to hold it in his own voice, and he does so a decade before Tertullian — the earliest Helvidian — wrote in the Latin West. His pupil Origen would soon repeat the Epiphanian reading and attach it to the Gospel of Peter and the Book of James, so that the Alexandrian succession carries the view forward through the third century.
  • What it does and does not show. Clement’s note shows that a learned catechist of the Alexandrian church, c. 200, took the brethren to be Joseph’s sons and Mary’s perpetual virginity for granted — strong evidence for how early and how widely the Epiphanian reading was held in the East. It does not by itself settle the exegetical question of who the brethren were: Clement offers no argument from the Gospel text, only the traditional identification, which he shares with the second-century infancy apocrypha rather than derives independently.
