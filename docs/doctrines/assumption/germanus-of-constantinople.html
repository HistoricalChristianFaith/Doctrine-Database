Germanus of Constantinople on the Assumption / Dormition of Mary

Dates: c. 720 (Germanus, Patriarch of Constantinople c. 715–730; c. 634–733/740) · Wikipedia
Summary: In his homilies for the feast Germanus preaches Mary’s genuine falling-asleep in death — and then insists, as firmly as any Father, that her body knew no decay. It was a “stranger to all dissolution,” impossible for the tomb to hold; her flesh did not see corruption but was taken up incorrupt. Far from conceding any bodily dissolution, Germanus is one of the tradition’s strongest voices for her incorruption — which is why the 1950 definition would later quote him by name.

Claims & evidence

Her body knew no corruption — a stranger to dissolution

“Your virginal body is utterly holy, utterly pure, truly the dwelling-place of God, and because of this it endures and does not know earthly dissolution.”

— Germanus of Constantinople, First Homily on the Dormition (PG 98.345B), trans. Stephen J. Shoemaker, Ancient Traditions of the Virgin Mary’s Dormition and Assumption (Oxford, 2002).1 Brian Daley’s rendering of the same homily is to the same effect: her “flesh did not undergo the corruption of the tomb,” her body “changed, in its humanity, to the highest incorruptible life,” so that Germanus can declare it “impossible that you, the vessel which bore God, should be dissolved and decomposed into the dust of death.”2

Death could not hold her — the tomb found empty

Germanus preaches a real death — a true Koimēsis — but one death could not master: “he has taken you to himself in his incorruption,” he says, so that “your tomb was revealed now as empty of your flesh.”3 The Dormition is thus, for Germanus, no simple departure of the soul: the body itself, kept from decay, is carried up — the fullest form of the bodily claim, and the one the later tradition would settle on.

Notes

  • Germanus is the tradition’s clearest “no corruption” witness, not an exception to it. When Pope Pius XII defined the Assumption in Munificentissimus Deus (1950), he cited Germanus among the patristic authorities for Mary’s incorrupt body taken into heaven — so the seventh-century preacher stands directly behind the twentieth-century dogma.
  • Like Andrew of Crete, his near-contemporary, Germanus holds together the two claims the Eastern Dormition keeps in tension: that Mary truly died, and that her body was nonetheless kept from the corruption of the grave. The pairing traces back to Theoteknos of Livias a century earlier.
