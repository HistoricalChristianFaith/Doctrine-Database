Germanus of Constantinople on the Assumption / Dormition of Mary

Dates: c. 720 (Germanus, Patriarch of Constantinople c. 715–730; c. 634–733/740) · Wikipedia
Summary: In his homilies for the feast Germanus preaches Mary’s genuine falling-asleep in death — and, in the first homily, insists as firmly as any Father that her body knew no decay: it was a “stranger to all dissolution,” impossible for the tomb to hold; her flesh did not see corruption but was taken up incorrupt. His second homily, though, opens with a striking qualification — her bones had “become hard and shrivelled in decay” in the earth before being re-clothed with incorruptible flesh — so that his witness is not the seamless “no corruption at all” formula it first appears, but a two-stage account in which a real interval of decay is overtaken by restoration. Even so, the incorrupt, assumed body is the end he preaches, which is why the 1950 definition would later quote him by name.

Claims & evidence

Her body knew no corruption — a stranger to dissolution

“Your virginal body is utterly holy, utterly pure, truly the dwelling-place of God, and because of this it endures and does not know earthly dissolution.”

— Germanus of Constantinople, First Homily on the Dormition (PG 98.345B), trans. Stephen J. Shoemaker, Ancient Traditions of the Virgin Mary’s Dormition and Assumption (Oxford, 2002).1 Brian Daley’s rendering of the same homily is to the same effect: her “flesh did not undergo the corruption of the tomb,” her body “changed, in its humanity, to the highest incorruptible life,” so that Germanus can declare it “impossible that you, the vessel which bore God, should be dissolved and decomposed into the dust of death.”2

Death could not hold her — the tomb found empty

Germanus preaches a real death — a true Koimēsis — but one death could not master: “he has taken you to himself in his incorruption,” he says, so that “your tomb was revealed now as empty of your flesh.”3 The Dormition is thus, for Germanus, no simple departure of the soul: the body itself, kept from decay, is carried up — the fullest form of the bodily claim, and the one the later tradition would settle on.

The one concession — bones “shrivelled in decay” before their restoration

The seamless picture is Germanus’s first homily. His second opens with a different, and franker, image: Mary’s body is glorified not by escaping decay altogether but by a decay that is undone.

“For when her human bones were once transformed, by the mercy of God, in the depths of the earth, the immaculate body of the Mother of God again put flesh on them, though they had become hard and shrivelled in decay — indeed, they grew ‘softer than oil’ (Ps 54:22 [LXX]) in their incorruptibility, because of the resurrection of him who was born of her.”

— Germanus of Constantinople, Second Homily on the Dormition (PG 98.361), trans. Brian E. Daley, On the Dormition of Mary (St. Vladimir’s Seminary Press, 1998).4 The tension with the first homily is real, and worth stating plainly: there her flesh “did not undergo the corruption of the tomb” and neither sepulchre “was a workshop of decay”; here her bones do decay — “hard and shrivelled” in the earth — and are only afterward re-fleshed and made incorrupt. This is not doubt about the assumption itself; the body is still raised and taken up. It is a different account of how the incorruption is reached — through decay overtaken by restoration, rather than a body that never decayed at all. Among the early Greek Dormition preachers Germanus stands nearly alone in granting even this much: Andrew of Crete has her tomb “allow no corruption,” and John of Damascus asks how “corruption [could] dare to assault that body once filled with life.” So Germanus is not, after all, the tradition’s flattest “no-corruption” voice — he is the one who lets a shadow of the grave fall across the body before the glory.

Notes

  • Germanus is one of the tradition’s firmest witnesses to Mary’s incorrupt, assumed body — though, as the passage above shows, not a perfectly seamless one. When Pope Pius XII defined the Assumption in Munificentissimus Deus (1950), he cited Germanus among the patristic authorities for Mary’s incorrupt body taken into heaven — so the seventh-century preacher stands directly behind the twentieth-century dogma.
  • Like Andrew of Crete, his near-contemporary, Germanus holds together the two claims the Eastern Dormition keeps in tension: that Mary truly died, and that her body was nonetheless kept from the corruption of the grave. The pairing traces back to Theoteknos of Livias a century earlier.
