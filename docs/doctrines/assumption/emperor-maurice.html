Emperor Maurice on the Assumption / Dormition of Mary

Dates: c. 595 (Maurice, Eastern Roman emperor, reigned 582–602) · Wikipedia
Summary: Maurice belongs on this timeline not for anything he believed but for something he is said to have organized: later tradition credits him with fixing the feast of Mary’s Dormition on 15 August throughout the empire. The commemoration already existed — kept in Palestine well before his reign, on differing dates elsewhere — so his decree standardized an existing observance rather than attesting any new evidence for the doctrine. And the attribution itself rests only on much later sources.

Claims & evidence

The feast of the Dormition fixed for 15 August, empire-wide

“In the Greek Church … some kept this feast in January, with the monks of Egypt; others in August, with those of Palestine; wherefore the Emperor Maurice (d. 602), if the account of the ‘Liber Pontificalis’ (II, 508) be correct, set the feast for the Greek Empire on 15 August.”

— Catholic Encyclopedia, “Feast of the Assumption,” newadvent.org. The same act is more commonly credited to the fourteenth-century historian Nikephoros Kallistos Xanthopoulos, whose Ecclesiastical History reports that Maurice decreed the Dormition’s observance on 15 August across the empire.1 Both channels are late — the Liber Pontificalis notice and Nikephoros alike write centuries after Maurice, and the Catholic Encyclopedia itself hedges the report (“if the account … be correct”). No contemporary edict survives.

Standardization, not institution — the feast was older than the decree

What Maurice is remembered for regularizing already existed. The fifth-century Armenian Lectionary of Jerusalem places a “Memory of Mary” on 15 August at the Kathisma church on the road to Bethlehem — a Marian commemoration kept in Palestine generations before Maurice, which only gradually took on the specific sense of her Dormition and bodily assumption.2 Elsewhere the date differed — January in Egypt, other days in the West — and it is precisely that calendrical patchwork that the imperial decree is said to have resolved by imposing the Palestinian August date everywhere. His contribution is therefore administrative: he unified when and where the feast was kept, not what was known of Mary’s end.

The Egyptian side of that patchwork can be seen from the inside in the Coptic homily of Pseudo-Evodius, which does not merely keep the feast in January instead of August but preserves a two-stage reckoning: Mary’s death on 21 Tobe (mid-January) and the taking-up of her body on 16 Mesore (mid-August), seven months apart. Egypt thus kept a January Dormition and an August Assumption as two moments of one story — so what Maurice is said to have standardized was a single date over a tradition that, at least in Egypt, had already spread the commemoration across the year.

Notes

  • Maurice’s decree bears on liturgical history, not on the evidence for the doctrine. That the whole empire came to keep 15 August tells us the commemoration had become universal by c. 600; it adds nothing to the thin documentary record behind the belief itself, which still traced back through the apocryphal Transitus narratives rather than Scripture or apostolic testimony.
  • The lateness of the attestation is worth keeping in view: the earliest witness to the decree that names Maurice by name is the Liber Pontificalis, and the fullest is Nikephoros Kallistos, writing around 1320 — some seven centuries after the event.
