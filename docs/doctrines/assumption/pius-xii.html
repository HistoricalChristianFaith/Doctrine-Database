Pius XII on the Assumption / Dormition of Mary

Dates: 1 November 1950 (the apostolic constitution Munificentissimus Deus) · Eugenio Pacelli, Pope Pius XII, 1876–1958; reigned 1939–1958 · Wikipedia
Summary: Pius XII closed the long development by defining the Assumption as Catholic dogma. He declared that Mary, at the end of her earthly life, was assumed body and soul into heaven — and framed the definition with deliberate care so as not to decide whether she died first.

Claims & evidence

The dogmatic definition

“we pronounce, declare, and define it to be a divinely revealed dogma: that the Immaculate Mother of God, the ever Virgin Mary, having completed the course of her earthly life, was assumed body and soul into heavenly glory.”

— Munificentissimus Deus §44, Vatican. The constitution surveys the liturgical feast, the medieval theologians, and the “fittingness” arguments — among them the reasoning of John of Damascus — before issuing the definition; it is the capstone of the timeline traced on this page.

Whether Mary died is deliberately left open

The phrase “having completed the course of her earthly life” (expleto terrestris vitae cursu) was chosen precisely to avoid pronouncing on the ancient dispute over whether Mary died before her assumption or was taken up without death. Elsewhere the constitution speaks of her having “passed from this life” (§§5, 14)1 — language equally guarded. The definition thus affirms the bodily assumption while leaving unresolved the very question Epiphanius of Salamis had confessed no one could answer, fifteen centuries earlier.

Notes

  • The 1950 definition is the point at which the Eastern Dormition (“falling asleep,” presupposing death) and the Western Assumption converge in defined dogma — while the guarded wording keeps faith with the older Eastern instinct that she truly died, without requiring it.
  • Pius XII appears on this wiki also as a witness on the ministerial priesthood timeline (the encyclical Mediator Dei, 1947).
