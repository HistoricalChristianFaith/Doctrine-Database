Paschasius Radbertus on the Assumption / Dormition of Mary

Dates: c. 860 (the letter Cogitis me, written for the nuns of Notre-Dame at Soissons; Paschasius, abbot of Corbie, c. 785–865) · Wikipedia
Summary: Paschasius wrote the medieval West’s most influential Assumption sermon — the letter Cogitis me, which circulated for centuries under the name of Jerome and was read at the Feast of the Assumption. Its defining feature is caution: he honours Mary’s glory, but warns against the apocryphal narratives of her passing and refuses to define whether her body was raised or only her soul.

Claims & evidence

Warning against the apocryphal Transitus

“In case that apocryphal work on the passing of the virgin should have come into your hands and you accepted doubtful things as certain …”

— Cogitis me (Pseudo-Jerome, Epistle 9, to “Paula and Eustochium”), Epistolae (Columbia). Writing under Jerome’s name, Paschasius steers his readers away from the Transitus Mariae narratives — the same cycle Rome had branded apocryphal — lest they take their embroideries for fact.

The bodily assumption left undecided

“It is not known, however, how, or at what time, or by which persons her most holy body was carried away from there, or whether she was resurrected …”

— Cogitis me, Epistolae (Columbia). He notes that Mary’s tomb in the valley of Josaphat “is now shown to be empty,” and that “many of our people doubt whether she was assumed with the body or left the body behind” — and he declines to settle it, judging that “it is more fitting to hold an opinion with a pious desire than to define unadvisedly what is not known without danger.”1

Notes

  • Paschasius marks the high reserve of the Latin tradition on the eve of its long medieval growth: nine centuries after Christ, the West’s ablest advocate for the feast held the bodily assumption as pious opinion, not defined faith — the very question left open again, from the other side, in the 1950 definition’s guarded “having completed the course of her earthly life.” Where Gregory of Tours had simply retold the Transitus story as fact, Paschasius warns against it.
  • The letter’s attribution to Paschasius (rather than Jerome) rests on modern scholarship — C. Lambot, Revue Bénédictine 46 (1934); doubt about the Jerome attribution led to its removal from the Roman breviary at the Tridentine reform.
