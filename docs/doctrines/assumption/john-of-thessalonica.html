John of Thessalonica on the Assumption / Dormition of Mary

Dates: c. 615 (John, archbishop of Thessalonica, r. c. 610–649, d. c. 630; the Homily on the Dormition was composed to introduce the feast to his church) · Wikipedia
Summary: John’s homily is the earliest surviving “official” episcopal retelling of Mary’s Dormition — and it is candid about its own foundations. He wrote it to introduce a feast his city had not been keeping, and he explains why: the narratives of Mary’s end had been so corrupted by heretics that his predecessors had kept their distance. His remedy was to edit the apocrypha, cutting what he judged false and preaching only what remained. The result is a striking self-portrait of the tradition policing its own sources at the very moment it enters the liturgy.

Claims & evidence

The feast had not been kept in Thessalonica

“Practically every place under heaven celebrates every year the memory of her going to her rest, with the exception of only a few, including the region around this divinely protected city of Thessalonica.”

— John of Thessalonica, Homily on the Dormition, trans. Brian E. Daley, On the Dormition of Mary (St. Vladimir’s Seminary Press, 1998), English text online.1 Even in the early seventh century, then, the feast was not yet universal: John writes precisely to establish it where it had been absent.

Why it had lapsed — heretics had corrupted the accounts

“Although those who were present then described her end truthfully, we are told, mischievous heretics later corrupted their accounts by adding words of their own, and for this reason our ancestors distanced themselves from these accounts as not in accord with the catholic Church.”

— Homily on the Dormition, trans. Daley.2 This is a remarkable admission from a promoter of the feast: the documentary record of Mary’s Dormition was known to be contaminated, and the earlier church of Thessalonica had for that reason refused to commemorate it.

His method — purge the fabrications, preach the remainder

“We have gathered these testimonies together in love of truth and in fear of God, taking no account of fabricated stories, since they have been interpolated into the traditions by the malice of those who fabricated them.”

— Homily on the Dormition, trans. Daley.3 John claims to relay only “what truly happened,” corroborated where possible by the physical sites still shown in his day. The narratives he trims are the same Transitus Mariae cycle behind Gregory of Tours; modern study identifies the corrupt version he blames with the name “Leucius,” a byword for heterodox apocrypha.

Notes

  • John’s preface is a rare window on how the doctrine reached the liturgy: not by discovery of new evidence but by an editorial act — a bishop selecting from suspect apocrypha the portions he could vouch for and publishing them as the feast’s official story. The very defensiveness of the move testifies to how insecure the sources were felt to be.
  • Two and a half centuries later the West’s ablest advocate, Paschasius Radbertus, would make the same wary judgment from the other side — warning his readers against the apocryphal Transitus altogether rather than editing it for use.
