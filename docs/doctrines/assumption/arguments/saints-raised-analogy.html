Do the “saints raised” texts (1 Corinthians 15; Matthew 27:52–53) supply a Scriptural analogy for the Assumption?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — the modest form of the argument (that a bodily glorification ahead of the general resurrection is scripturally conceivable and fitting) is plausible; but as adduced — analogy pressed toward warrant for Mary’s Assumption — it is weak. Analogy establishes possibility, not that the event occurred; the strongest-sounding prop, Matthew 27:52–53, is cited nowhere in the 1950 definition and is itself text-critically disputed; and 1 Corinthians 15’s own ordering — “every man in his own order… afterward they that are Christ’s at his coming” (15:23) — tells against a lone bodily assumption ahead of everyone else. At most these texts show the doctrine is not incoherent, which is all its defenders claim Scripture supplies.

Related arguments: the Scriptural case — see also the woman clothed with the sun (Revelation 12)

The claim

And the graves were opened; and many bodies of the saints which slept arose, and came out of the graves after his resurrection, and went into the holy city, and appeared unto many.

— Matthew 27:52–53.

So when this corruptible shall have put on incorruption, and this mortal shall have put on immortality, then shall be brought to pass the saying that is written, Death is swallowed up in victory.

— 1 Corinthians 15:54. The argument holds that these resurrection texts — Christ “the firstfruits of them that slept” whose victory swallows up death (1 Corinthians 15), and the bodies of the saints raised at the crucifixion (Matthew 27) — supply a Scriptural analogy or precedent for a bodily glorification like Mary’s Assumption: if others were raised bodily before the general resurrection, then a bodily assumption of Mary is neither unscriptural nor without pattern.

Who advances it

The analogy is a staple of popular apologetics for the Assumption, and it is worth stating at the outset what the 1950 definition actually cites, because the two are often run together. Munificentissimus Deus does not quote Matthew 27:52–53 anywhere. Its one verbatim use of 1 Corinthians 15 is verse 54 (“Death is swallowed up in victory”) in §39, and there it is folded into the New-Eve theme built on Genesis 3:15 — Mary sharing the victory over sin and death promised in the protoevangelium — not offered as a resurrection-analogy; the broader block (1 Cor 15:21–26, 54–57, Christ the firstfruits and death the last enemy) appears only in an endnote, as the theological framework of Christ’s victory.1 So the “saints raised” analogy is largely the apologists’, not the magisterium’s — a distinction that matters, since its most vivid element, Matthew 27, carries no magisterial weight at all.

The evidence for

The modest point behind the analogy is sound enough. Scripture does record bodily glorifications ahead of the general resurrection: Enoch and Elijah are taken up bodily in the Old Testament, and Matthew 27:52–53 has “many bodies of the saints” raised at Christ’s death. And 1 Corinthians 15 establishes the principle that “this mortal shall have put on immortality” — the destiny of the redeemed body is incorruption and glory. Against that background a bodily assumption of Mary is not an alien idea but a particular, early instance of the pattern the whole New Testament announces. As a fittingness argument — showing the Assumption conceivable, coherent with the shape of redemption, not contrary to Scripture — the analogy does real work.

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The doctrine is not incoherent, and Scripture supplies a real pattern of bodily glorification into which an assumption of Mary would fit. If the argument claims only that — that the Assumption is possible and fitting — it is plausible, and it is essentially all the doctrine’s more careful defenders claim the sacred text yields.

What is overstated. The trouble is the step from analogy to warrant.

(1) Analogy establishes possibility, not occurrence. That God has raised bodies early, and could do so for Mary, shows the Assumption conceivable; it does not show that it happened. Possibility is not proof — the historical question of whether Mary was in fact assumed still turns on attestation, which the doctrine famously lacks for its first centuries.2

(2) The most vivid prop is the weakest. Matthew 27:52–53 is cited nowhere in the definition, and it is itself one of the more disputed passages in the Gospels — peculiar to Matthew, absent from the parallels, its “after his resurrection” a chronological crux, and read by many interpreters (ancient and modern) as a theological sign of Christ’s victory over death rather than a bare report of a mass bodily resurrection. A contested text cannot firm up a contested doctrine.3

(3) 1 Corinthians 15 arguably cuts the other way. Paul is at pains to fix an order: “But every man in his own order: Christ the firstfruits; afterward they that are Christ’s at his coming” (15:23). The sequence he lays down is Christ first, then the rest of the redeemed together “at his coming” — the parousia — with no individual exception carved out ahead of time. The very chapter adduced for an early assumption of Mary sets up a schema that resists one.4

(4) The Matthew 27 raising is a general-resurrection sign, not a template for a privilege. The saints raised at the crucifixion function in the text as a corporate prefigurement of the resurrection Christ won for all — the first fruits of the tombs He emptied — not as a precedent for one uniquely privileged early assumption. Read as Matthew reads it, it points to the general resurrection of the many, not to a solitary exception.

Assessment

Weak. As a proof or warrant for the Assumption the analogy does not hold: it establishes that a bodily glorification is conceivable, never that Mary’s occurred, and the texts do not cooperate as neatly as the argument needs — Matthew 27:52–53 is not the magisterium’s to begin with and is itself disputed, and 1 Corinthians 15’s ordering (“every man in his own order… at his coming”) leans against a lone early assumption rather than toward it. What survives is only the modest, plausible kernel: bodily glorification has Scriptural precedent, so the Assumption is not contrary to the shape of redemption — a fittingness point, which is exactly what the doctrine’s defenders concede is all Scripture supplies, resting the case itself on tradition. The analogy shows the doctrine possible; the timeline shows how thin the evidence is that it is true.
