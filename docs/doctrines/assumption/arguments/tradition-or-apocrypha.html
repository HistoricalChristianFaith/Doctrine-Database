Does the tradition John of Damascus invokes trace to the apostles, or into the apocrypha?

Type: objection (off-timeline) · Assessment: plausible — the apocryphal-origin reading is the more probable one: the doctrine’s honest patristic champion grounds it not on Scripture but on tradition, and the tradition’s traceable chain (Dormition homilies → Gregory of Tours → the Transitus narratives → the Liber Requiei) runs into the fifth- and sixth-century apocrypha, not through an independent apostolic memory — a conclusion the Euthymiac History interpolation in his own homily illustrates rather than refutes. It stops short of sound because an argument from a traceable chain caps rather than strictly disproves an untraced oral tradition · grounded on John of Damascus (three Dormition homilies, c. 730)

Related arguments: the apocryphal foundation — see also Are the apocryphal Transitus narratives a sound documentary foundation?

The claim

“Why do you seek in the tomb one who has been assumed to the heavenly courts?”

— John of Damascus, Second Homily on the Dormition of the Theotokos, c. 730, English translation. John preaches the bodily glory of Mary with full confidence — yet he is candid that it does not rest on Scripture, and he takes pains to supply the pedigree of the tradition it rests on instead. The question this argument weighs is where that pedigree actually leads: back to an apostolic memory handed down from the beginning, as the doctrine’s defenders claim, or into the fifth- and sixth-century apocrypha that are the belief’s earliest surviving witnesses.

Who advances it

This is the hinge of the historical case, and it turns the doctrine’s most honest champion into its own best witness. Because John of Damascus concedes the appeal is to tradition and not to Scripture, the whole weight falls on the traceability of that tradition — and the critical historians who have reconstructed it (Martin Jugie, whose survey established the late emergence; Brian E. Daley, who edited and translated the early homilies; Stephen J. Shoemaker, who mapped the narrative families) find its chain running back into the Transitus literature rather than to the apostles.1

The evidence for

Two things make the apocryphal terminus the more probable one. First, the chain is traceable, and it descends. John’s classic Eastern statement (c. 730) inherits the formula — a genuine death, an incorrupt body, a heaven-borne translation — from the Greek Dormition homilists a century before him (Theoteknos, John of Thessalonica, Andrew of Crete); in the Latin West the belief enters mainstream preaching when Gregory of Tours (c. 590) retells it “out of the Transitus”; and the Transitus Mariae cycle itself descends from the earliest recoverable narrative, the Palm-of-the-Tree-of-Life tradition behind the Ethiopic Liber Requiei.2 At no link in that chain does the trail cross an independent, pre-apocryphal apostolic report; it terminates in the fifth-/sixth-century narratives, behind which lies Epiphanius’s flat confession (c. 377) that no one knew Mary’s end.

Second, the “ancient tradition” is demonstrably retrofitted at the very point where it claims the most antiquity. Appended to John’s second homily is the Euthymiac History, which supplies the belief its oldest-looking pedigree: at the Council of Chalcedon (451), it says, Juvenal of Jerusalem told the Emperor Marcian and Empress Pulcheria that Mary’s tomb had been opened at their request and found empty, only the grave-clothes remaining.3 But that excerpt is not John’s: it is a later interpolation, inserted into the homily by a hand that names its source as “chapter 40 of the third book of the Euthymiac History,” added early enough in transmission that every manuscript carries it — and the Euthymiac History itself is a Chalcedonian compilation of roughly the sixth to eighth century, no earlier than the apocrypha it was meant to antedate.3 The mechanism by which a fifth-century belief acquires a first-century pedigree is here caught in the act: a later editor grafts an authoritative-sounding “ancient tradition” onto the homily of the doctrine’s greatest champion.

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The core is well grounded. The champion himself grounds the doctrine on tradition rather than Scripture; the traceable chain of that tradition descends into the Transitus apocrypha and stops there; and the one document that would push the pedigree back to Chalcedon and beyond, the Euthymiac History, is a later interpolation into John’s homily and a source no older than the apocrypha. On the documentary evidence, an apostolic origin for the tradition John invokes is the less-probable reading, and a fifth-century apocryphal origin the more probable one.

What is overstated or imprecise. Two limits keep this from being airtight. First, an argument from a traceable written chain establishes where the surviving documents lead; it cannot strictly disprove an oral tradition that left no earlier trace. The Church’s own warrant for the doctrine was never a documentary pedigree but a received tradition and its theological fittingness (John says as much), and on that account the absence of an apostolic paper-trail is expected rather than damning. So the inference “traceable chain ends in the apocrypha, therefore the tradition originated there” is probable, not certain. Second, the point should not be pressed into a charge of bad faith against John: the Euthymiac interpolation is the work of a later editor, not of John himself, and his own candor — grounding the belief on tradition, not Scripture — is exactly what makes him a trustworthy witness to the doctrine’s real footing.

Assessment

Plausible — and, on the documentary evidence, the more probable reading. The doctrine’s ablest Eastern champion rests it frankly on tradition rather than Scripture, and the traceable chain of that tradition runs down into the fifth-/sixth-century Transitus apocrypha, not up to the apostles; the Euthymiac History that would supply an earlier pedigree is a demonstrable later interpolation into John’s own homily and a source no older than the narratives it was meant to antedate. That makes an apostolic origin for this tradition the less-probable reading and a late-antique, apocryphal one the more probable. It is filed as plausible rather than sound for one reason: an argument from a traceable written chain caps a claim without strictly disproving an untraced oral memory, and the doctrine’s defenders rest it on tradition and fittingness, not on a documentary pedigree it never claimed to have. The overreach, if any, is the interpolator’s, not John’s; his candor is what exposes the tradition’s real footing.
