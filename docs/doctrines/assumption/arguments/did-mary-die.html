Did Mary die before she was assumed, or was she taken up without dying?

Type: sub-question (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — the bodily glorification is common ground on both readings; the death sub-question is genuinely open. The Dormition (she truly died) is the dominant and more probable reading — the whole Eastern tradition, the earliest narratives, and the ordinary magisterium all presuppose a real death — but a real immortalist minority held that Mary never died, its chief early proof-text is now redated late, and the 1950 definition went out of its way not to decide · grounded on Epiphanius of Salamis (Panarion 78, c. 377) and the homily ascribed to Timothy of Jerusalem

The question

The doctrine traced on this timeline is that Mary was taken up body and soul into heaven. That claim — the bodily glorification — is not what is in dispute here. What is disputed is a sub-question the timeline keeps gesturing at but never settles: did Mary first die? The East’s very name for the event, the Dormition (“falling asleep,” Koimēsis), presupposes that she truly died and that her soul survived death before her body was raised. But a minority strand held instead that Mary was immortal — that she never died at all but was translated alive, on the pattern of Enoch and Elijah. The earliest writer to raise Mary’s end, Epiphanius (c. 377), lays out three possibilities — that she died, that she was martyred, or that she remained alive — and refuses to choose;1 and fifteen centuries later, when Rome at last defined the Assumption, it framed the definition so as to leave that same question open. So the sub-question is live from the first witness to the last.

The two readings

Dormition — she truly died. This is the mainstream reading and the one the whole developed tradition assumes. The two oldest narrative families both have Mary really die and then be bodily raised: in the Six Books she dies and is restored to life in Paradise; the Coptic Pseudo-Evodius even dates her death and her later bodily raising to two separate days of the calendar. Every early Greek homilist on this timeline — Theoteknos, Modestus, Germanus, Andrew of Crete, John of Damascus — preaches a genuine death (a body that “did taste death,” a tomb, an incorruption preserved in the tomb) joined to a heaven-borne, undecayed body. On this reading the Assumption presupposes the same middle state between death and resurrection traced on the intermediate-state timeline: Mary dies, her soul survives, and her body is raised ahead of the general resurrection.

Immortalist — she was taken up without dying. The minority reading held that Mary, uniquely preserved, never underwent death. Its chief early witness is a homily On Simeon and Anna (In Simeonem et Annam) transmitted under the name of a Timothy of Jerusalem, which states that Mary is

“immortal to the present time through him who had his abode in her and who assumed and raised her above the higher regions.”2

Epiphanius had already kept the possibility open as one of his three, declining to rule out that Mary “remained alive.”1 Some Latin medievals kept the immortalist option live, and modern apologists point to the guarded wording of the 1950 definition as evidence that Rome itself would not close it.

The magisterial hedge

When Pius XII defined the dogma in Munificentissimus Deus (1950), he chose his words precisely so as not to decide the death. The definition declares that Mary, “having completed the course of her earthly life, was assumed body and soul into heavenly glory” — a neutral phrase that affirms the assumption while declining to state that she died first.3 The reticence was deliberate: the drafting deliberately avoided the word “death” in the operative clause because the tradition was itself divided. It is a rare case of a defined dogma leaving its own premise open. This is not the same as saying Rome is neutral: the body of the same document plainly assumes Mary died — it speaks of her, “like her only begotten Son,” having “passed from this life,” her body never subject to “the corruption of the tomb”3 — so the ordinary teaching leans firmly toward a real death, even as the solemn definition declines to bind it.

Adversarial assessment

What both sides share. Nothing in this dispute touches the bodily glorification: both the Dormition and the immortalist readings hold that Mary’s flesh was taken to heaven undecayed. The whole quarrel is over whether death intervened. So the sub-question cannot make or unmake the doctrine as such — it decides only its shape.

Why the Dormition is the stronger reading. On the documentary record the death view wins on nearly every count. It is the reading of the oldest narratives, of every early homilist, of the feast’s very name in the East, and of the ordinary magisterium down to Munificentissimus Deus itself. The immortalist strand, by contrast, is thin, and its strongest prop has weakened under scrutiny. The “Timothy of Jerusalem” homily was long cited — on Martin Jugie’s dating — as a late-fourth- or early-fifth-century witness, which would have made it the earliest datable statement of Mary’s immortality; Jugie rested that early date on internal signs (no mention of the Jerusalem tomb legend, no anti-Nestorian polemic, the use of parthenos rather than theotokos). But Bernard Capelle’s study showed the homily to be the work of an otherwise unknown author writing somewhere between the sixth and eighth centuries, and Shoemaker and the current scholarship follow the later dating.4 Redated, “Timothy” is no earlier than the mature Dormition preaching it dissents from, so the immortalist reading loses its claim to be the more primitive one. On the evidence, that Mary died is the more probable reading.

Why it stays open rather than settled. Three things keep the death from being simply established. First, the very earliest witness we have, Epiphanius, deliberately would not decide it, and listed survival without death as a live option — so the tradition begins agnostic, not committed. Second, the immortalist reading is not incoherent or refuted: it is coherent with the doctrine (an unfallen Mary spared death as she was spared corruption is a recognizable theological move), it has real if minority patristic and medieval support, and it cannot be disproved from Scripture, which is silent on Mary’s end either way. Third, and decisively for the status of the question, the church that defines the Assumption has itself declined to define the death: the 1950 definition’s guarded phrasing means that, at the highest level, the point is left open on purpose. A sub-question its own defining authority refused to close is by definition not settled.

Assessment

Contested. The bodily glorification is common ground; the death sub-question genuinely divides the tradition and has never been closed. Weighing the two readings on the evidence, the Dormition (she truly died) is the mainstream and more probable one — it is the sense of the feast’s Eastern name, of the oldest narratives, of every early homilist, and of the ordinary magisterium, while the immortalist strand is a real but minority tradition whose chief early proof-text (“Timothy of Jerusalem”) is now dated too late to establish an earlier pedigree. But it falls short of settled, and honestly so: the earliest witness (Epiphanius) refused to decide, the immortalist reading remains coherent and unrefuted, and the 1950 definition was framed precisely so as not to bind the point. The right verdict is therefore not “she died” simpliciter but “more probably she died, yet the church left it open” — a genuinely contested sub-question inside an otherwise defined doctrine, and one of the few dogmas that leaves its own premise undecided.
