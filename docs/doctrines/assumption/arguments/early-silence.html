Does the silence of the first centuries refute the Assumption’s apostolic antiquity?

Type: objection (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — the factual core (no attestation for some three centuries; the belief is a demonstrable late-antique development) is near-consensus and conceded even by Catholic historians, but the leap from that silence to a verdict against the doctrine turns on how much an argument from silence can carry and on the development-of-doctrine question · grounded on Epiphanius of Salamis (Panarion 78, c. 377)

The claim

“But if some think us mistaken, let them search the Scriptures. They will not find Mary’s death; they will not find whether she died or did not die… For my own part, I do not dare to speak, but I keep my own thoughts and I practice silence.”

— Epiphanius of Salamis, Panarion 78 (Against the Antidicomarianites), c. 377, as quoted (Carol, Mariology II, 139–40). The objection built on this witness runs: an event as extraordinary as a bodily assumption — the flesh of the Mother of God taken to heaven — would surely have left some early trace: a text, a homily, a relic, a tomb cult, a feast. Instead the first three-plus centuries are wholly silent; the New Testament says nothing of Mary’s end; and the one learned fourth-century bishop who actually asks the question confesses that no one knows and that Scripture “kept silence.” A doctrine that surfaces only in fifth- and sixth-century apocrypha and enters mainstream preaching later still cannot be what its defenders call it — an apostolic tradition handed down from the beginning.

Who advances it

The objection is the standard Protestant and historical-critical case against the Assumption’s antiquity, and it is not merely a polemical one: it is largely conceded by Catholic scholarship, which grants that “there is no positive historical data in proof of the historical fact of the Assumption” and that the doctrine rests on tradition and theological fittingness rather than early attestation.1 The critical-historical spine of the modern discussion is Stephen J. Shoemaker’s Ancient Traditions of the Virgin Mary’s Dormition and Assumption, which maps the earliest narratives and their dates.2

The evidence for

The documentary record is exactly as thin as the objection says. No canonical text records Mary’s death, burial, or translation. No second- or third-century Father — not the writers who eagerly retold every scrap of apostolic memory, not the martyr-acts, not the earliest liturgies — mentions any of it. The first extant writer to raise the question, Epiphanius (c. 377), lays out three possibilities (natural death, martyrdom, or survival without death) and settles on none, precisely because he can find no authoritative tradition to appeal to.3 The bodily-assumption belief then surfaces in the apocryphal Transitus Mariae narratives, which the Gelasian Decree branded among the books “not to be received,”4 and only from there enters mainstream preaching — Gregory of Tours in the West (c. 590), the Greek Dormition homilists in the East. Even the veneration of a specific tomb of Mary in Jerusalem does not begin until about the middle of the fifth century, with disagreement over its very location — itself a mark of how late the concrete cult is.1 On the ordinary canons of historical method, a claim first attested three centuries after the fact, in documents its own church judged spurious, is not demonstrably apostolic.

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The factual kernel is sound and near-universally granted: there is no evidence for the Assumption in the first three-plus centuries, the earliest narratives are fifth- and sixth-century apocrypha of dubious and partly heterodox origin, and the doctrine cannot be traced to the apostles by the historical record. The wiki’s house caution about the supernatural does not blunt this objection: the problem here is not a naturalistic premise (“bodies cannot rise, so this did not happen”) — which would beg the question against a miracle — but plain evidentiary absence, and absence of evidence bites regardless of whether the event is natural or miraculous. So any claim that the Assumption is a demonstrable apostolic tradition, taught from the beginning, is untenable; apostolic origin is at best a less-probable reading, and a late-fourth/fifth-century emergence fits the record far better.

What is overstated. Three qualifications keep this from being a clean win for the skeptic, and together they make it genuinely contested rather than decisive.

First, an argument from silence caps a claim; it does not disprove one. Silence shows the belief is not attested early — it cannot show the underlying event did not occur, and the doctrine’s own defenders never rested it on a documentary chain but on tradition and fittingness (John of Damascus and Paschasius Radbertus both say as much on the timeline). “Refutes apostolic antiquity” is sound if it means “refutes any demonstrable apostolic pedigree”; it overreaches if pressed to mean “disproves that Mary was assumed.”

Second, the revisionist history cuts partly the other way — and it is not one scholar’s idiosyncrasy but the majority dating. Shoemaker argues that the earliest Dormition traditions, and the fourth-century Marian cult Epiphanius was in fact combating, push the emergence of the belief earlier than the surviving manuscripts — into the fourth century, perhaps earlier — and he even reads Epiphanius’s neighbouring book (Panarion 79, against the Kollyridians) as evidence that some Christians already held Mary’s bodily immortality. Richard Bauckham independently dates the earliest narrative family, the Six Books apocryphon, “from the fourth century at the latest, but perhaps considerably earlier”; van Esbroeck and Gribomont concur in a fourth-century origin, and only a minority (Jugie, Mimouni) dates the traditions late, as post-Chalcedon.2 This does not reach the apostles — a three-century gap remains — but it complicates the flat picture of “total silence until the apocrypha” and turns Epiphanius from a clean witness against the belief into a witness that the question was already alive and contested in his day.

Third, defenders press a counter-argument from the same silence: in a church that fervently collected, enshrined, and fought over the bones of far lesser saints, no city, church, or order ever claimed to possess a bodily relic of Mary, and the empty-tomb report attributed to Juvenal of Jerusalem at Chalcedon (451) fits that vacuum.1 The counter is real but limited: it is an inference from absence no stronger than the objection it answers, the Jerusalem tomb-cult’s own late-fifth-century date and locational confusion undercut it, and the parallel of Joseph — equally relic-less, yet never claimed to have been assumed — shows that missing relics do not by themselves imply a missing body. It blunts the skeptic’s certainty without establishing the doctrine.

Assessment

Contested. The historical core is as close to settled as this subject gets, and it is conceded across the confessional divide: the Assumption leaves no trace in the first three centuries, the earliest witness (Epiphanius, c. 377) confesses ignorance, and the belief is a demonstrable late-antique development that surfaces first in apocrypha its own church judged spurious — so the doctrine cannot be shown to be of apostolic antiquity, and its apostolic origin is the less-probable reading. What is contested is the further inference the objection wants to draw. Silence establishes the absence of early attestation, not the non-occurrence of the event; the belief’s defenders rest it on tradition and fittingness, not on a documentary chain, so it is not refuted on its own terms; the revisionist dating (Shoemaker and Bauckham) pushes the belief’s emergence back toward Epiphanius’s own century (though never to the apostles); and the “no relics / empty tomb” counter draws on the same silence to the opposite end. The verdict is therefore strong by the historian’s lights and answerable by the theologian’s: decisive against a claim of proven apostolic pedigree, short of a disproof of the doctrine itself.
