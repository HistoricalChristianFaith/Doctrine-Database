Does Mary’s sinlessness (the Immaculate Conception) entail her bodily incorruption and Assumption?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — strong by the systematic theologian’s lights, weak as demonstrative or historical proof. This is the theological backbone of the doctrine — the argument that does the real work once the proof-texts are conceded to show only that a bodily assumption is conceivable: because bodily corruption is a penalty of sin and Mary was preserved from all sin (the Immaculate Conception), it was fitting — for many of the scholastics, morally necessary — that her flesh not see corruption but be assumed. Within a dogmatic framework that already grants the Immaculate Conception and the strict sin→corruption link, the inference is coherent and is what the magisterium and the great scholastics actually rely on; as a demonstration that the event occurred it does not hold — it inherits all the contestedness of the (separately defined) Immaculate Conception, and decuit ergo fecit (“it was fitting, therefore he did it”) is a piety-and-probability inference, not a proof · advanced, magisterially, by Pius XII in Munificentissimus Deus (§§4–5, 21) and, before him, by the scholastic tradition.

The claim

Now God has willed that the Blessed Virgin Mary should be exempted from this general rule. She, by an entirely unique privilege, completely overcame sin by her Immaculate Conception, and as a result she was not subject to the law of remaining in the corruption of the grave, and she did not have to wait until the end of time for the redemption of her body.

— Pope Pius XII, Munificentissimus Deus (1950), §5.1 The argument is a chain of premises. Death and the corruption of the body are the wages of sin (Romans 6:23; the ground cursed, “dust thou art, and unto dust shalt thou return,” Genesis 3:19); Mary alone among the redeemed was preserved from sin altogether, from the first instant of her conception; therefore sin’s bodily penalty — the decay of the grave — has no claim on her, and it was fitting that her body be kept incorrupt and taken up. Pius XII states the fittingness in the words the tradition had long used, quoting John of Damascus: “It was fitting that she, who had kept her virginity intact in childbirth, should keep her own body free from all corruption even after death.”2 This is not a proof-text but a deduction, and it is the one the constitution treats as decisive: the scriptural “figures” it gathers (Revelation 12, the Ark, the “saints raised”) are adduced only to show the conclusion congruous, while this is the reasoning that links the Assumption to Mary’s sinlessness and so to the whole Marian edifice.

Who advances it

The reasoning is the magisterium’s own and the scholastic tradition’s before it. In the Latin West the Assumption was argued from fittingness and incorruption from an early date — the widely circulated Pseudo-Augustine sermon De Assumptione Beatae Mariae Virginis (in fact a twelfth-century treatise, long printed among Augustine’s works) reasons that the flesh from which Christ took his own incorrupt body could not itself be left to decay, and that it would be unfitting for the Mother of God to be food for worms.3 The completing move is the “potuit, decuit, ergo fecit” schema the Franciscan school made famous for the Immaculate Conception — God could preserve her, it was fitting, therefore he did — here applied to the further privilege that follows from her sinlessness. Munificentissimus Deus gathers this tradition, joins the two privileges explicitly (§§4–5), and rests the doctrine’s theological weight on it: the New-Eve/victory-over-death argument of §§39–40 is this same fittingness inference given its scriptural dress. This page weighs the deduction itself; the prior claims it rests on — the Immaculate Conception and the sin→corruption principle — are weighed on their own ground.

The evidence for

Taken as systematic theology rather than as demonstration, the argument is strong, and this is its home discipline. Its premises are not idiosyncratic: that death and bodily corruption entered the world through sin is Pauline (Romans 5:12; 6:23) and patristic common ground, and the principle that a soul preserved from all sin is fittingly preserved from sin’s bodily wage follows naturally once it is granted. The inference-form is a legitimate and ancient mode of reasoning under revelation: because the economy of salvation is God’s free and ordered work, what supremely befits that plan is a real, if defeasible, guide to what is actual — the same convenientia by which Aquinas reasons the fittingness of the Incarnation.4 And the argument has the singular merit of unifying the Marian doctrines rather than multiplying independent miracles: the Assumption is not a free-standing marvel but the natural term of a sinlessness already confessed — the Immaculate Conception carried to its bodily conclusion. On this footing the East’s own homilists reach the incorruption without hesitation: Modestus, Germanus, John of Damascus all preach that the body which bore the incorrupt God could not itself be dissolved into dust — though even here the East is not quite of one voice on the mechanism: Germanus’s second homily reaches the same incorruption through an interval of real decay (her bones “hard and shrivelled” in the earth) undone at the resurrection, rather than a body that never decayed at all. As an exhibition of the doctrine’s coherence and fittingness, held within the framework that grants its premises, the argument holds — which is exactly why the magisterium leans on it and not on the proof-texts.

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The fittingness conclusion holds on its premises: grant the Immaculate Conception and the strict sin→corruption link, and a Mary kept from the grave’s decay coheres. Nothing below disturbs that. What fails is the stronger claim the argument is enlisted to carry — that sinlessness establishes that the Assumption occurred — and the deduction’s dependence on prior claims at least as disputed as its conclusion.

What is overstated. Three difficulties press on the stronger claim.

(1) It is a conditional argument, and inherits the contestedness of its condition. The whole inference runs through the Immaculate Conception — a doctrine defined only in 1854, which the Christian East has never dogmatized and which the medieval West itself long disputed (Aquinas denied it). An argument can be no firmer than the premise it hangs on; a conclusion drawn from the Immaculate Conception is at most as secure as the Immaculate Conception, and that is contested, not settled. Notably the Eastern tradition reaches Mary’s bodily incorruption without the Immaculate Conception at all — grounding it on Christ’s action and her divine maternity, not on a preservation from original sin — which shows the sinlessness-premise is a specifically Latin, scholastic route to the conclusion, not a necessary one.5

(2) “Fitting” is not “occurred.” The completing step — decuit, ergo fecit, from “it was fitting” to “therefore he did it” — is not a demonstration, as the schema’s own friends concede: Aquinas holds even the Incarnation “not necessary” in the strict sense but fitting, and arguments of convenientia, bearing on God’s free acts, establish propriety, not fact. A schema that licensed “could + fitting → did” would prove far more than anyone accepts. That possibility and congruity are not actuality — that God’s being able and fittingly willing to act does not establish that he did — is this wiki’s standing caution, and the inference-form is weighed in full under the Immaculate Conception’s own fittingness-inference argument (weak as a proof, plausible as an illuminative mode). The finding there transfers here: the deduction can carry a doctrine already held; it cannot lift one into place.6

(3) Death and corruption do not strictly track personal sin. The premise supposes that sinlessness of itself exempts from sin’s bodily penalty — yet the sinless Christ truly died, and the mainstream reading of this very doctrine (the Dormition) holds that Mary too really died despite her sinlessness, her body glorified by a divine act rather than by any automatic immunity of unfallen flesh. If the Immaculately Conceived Mary still underwent death, then sinlessness does not, by itself, suspend the bodily consequences of the fallen condition; her incorruption, like Christ’s (“thou wilt not… suffer thine Holy One to see corruption,” Psalm 16:10; Acts 2:31), is then a gift specially bestowed, not a strict entailment of being sinless. The link the argument treats as a law of nature is at that point itself a fittingness claim — which is precisely what was to be shown.

Assessment

Contested — genuinely disputed, and for a reason the wiki’s definition names: it is strong by one discipline’s lights and weak by another’s, with no neutral court to decide between them. As systematic theology — the reasoning that displays how the Assumption follows from and completes Mary’s sinlessness, unifying the Marian doctrines under a single fittingness rather than heaping up independent marvels — the argument is coherent, ancient, and is the ground the magisterium and the scholastics actually occupy; on that footing it is the doctrine’s strongest positive case, far stronger than any of its proof-texts. As demonstration — evidence that the event occurred — it does not hold: it is a conditional argument that inherits the whole contestedness of the Immaculate Conception it hangs on (a doctrine the East never defined and the medieval West long disputed); its completing move, decuit ergo fecit, is a probability-and-piety inference that even its authors deny is demonstrative; and its governing premise — that corruption strictly tracks personal sin — is qualified by the sinless Christ’s real death and by the tradition’s own holding that Mary died. So the honest verdict is not that the fittingness argument is empty but that it is two arguments in one: unanswerable as an exhibition of the doctrine’s congruity, and inconclusive as a proof of its truth — strong for the believer who already grants the premises, unpersuasive to anyone who does not. (The scriptural form of the same inference is weighed under the victory-over-death argument; the bare inference-form under the Immaculate Conception’s fittingness-inference argument; and whether the doctrine’s tradition traces to the apostles or into the apocrypha under the apocryphal foundation.)
