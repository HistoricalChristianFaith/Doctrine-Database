Was Mary taken up without dying? (the immortalist reading)

Related arguments: the death of Mary — see also Did Mary truly die before she was assumed? (the Dormition reading)

Type: sub-question (off-timeline) · Assessment: plausible — that Mary was translated alive, without dying, on the pattern of Enoch and Elijah, is a real but minority reading: coherent with the doctrine, held open by the earliest witness (Epiphanius, c. 377), not refuted by Scripture’s silence, and left un-foreclosed by the guarded 1950 definition. But it falls short of the dominant Dormition reading: it is thin in the record, and its chief early proof-text — the homily ascribed to Timothy of Jerusalem — is now redated too late to give it an earlier pedigree.

The question

The doctrine traced on this timeline is that Mary was taken up body and soul into heaven — the bodily glorification, which is common ground and not what this page weighs. What it weighs is a sub-question: did Mary first die, or was she taken up alive? This page states and rates the immortalist reading — that Mary, uniquely preserved, never underwent death but was translated living into glory, as Enoch “was not, for God took him” (Genesis 5:24) and Elijah went up by a whirlwind (2 Kings 2:11). The rival and dominant Dormition reading — that she truly died — is stated and rated on its own page; here the minority case is set out on its best evidence.

The case for a translation without death

The Timothy of Jerusalem homily. The chief early witness is a homily On Simeon and Anna (In Simeonem et Annam), transmitted under the name of a Timothy of Jerusalem, which states outright that Mary is

“immortal to the present time through him who had his abode in her and who assumed and raised her above the higher regions.”1

Read at face value the homily denies that Mary ever died: she is “immortal to the present time,” already “assumed and raised” alive. On the dating its defenders long assigned it — Martin Jugie’s late-fourth- or early-fifth-century placing — it would have been the earliest datable statement of Mary’s end at all, and its immortalism would then stand at the head of the tradition.

Epiphanius holds the option open. The earliest writer to raise Mary’s end at all, Epiphanius of Salamis (c. 377), lays out three possibilities — that she died, that she was martyred, or that she remained alive — and refuses to choose, declining to rule out that Mary “remained alive.”2 So survival-without-death is not a late aberration read back into the tradition; it is one of the live options the very first witness explicitly kept on the table.

The pattern of Enoch and Elijah, and a magisterial reticence. Scripture already knows the type of a living translation into glory without intervening death — Enoch and Elijah — so an unfallen Mary spared death, as she was (on the developed doctrine) spared corruption, is a recognizable theological move rather than an ad hoc one. Some Latin medievals kept the immortalist option live on just this reasoning; and modern apologists point to the guarded wording of the 1950 definition, which affirms the assumption of one “having completed the course of her earthly life” while pointedly declining to say she died, as evidence that Rome itself would not close the question.3

Adversarial assessment

What both readings share. Nothing here touches the bodily glorification: the immortalist and the Dormition readings alike hold that Mary’s flesh was taken to heaven undecayed. The dispute is only over whether death intervened, so the sub-question decides the doctrine’s shape, not its truth.

The chief prop has weakened. The immortalist reading’s strongest early support — the “Timothy of Jerusalem” homily — no longer carries the weight once placed on it. Jugie rested his early date on internal signs (no mention of the Jerusalem tomb legend, no anti-Nestorian polemic, the use of parthenos rather than theotokos). But Bernard Capelle’s study showed the homily to be the work of an otherwise unknown author writing somewhere between the sixth and eighth centuries, and Shoemaker and the current scholarship follow the later dating.4 Redated, “Timothy” is no earlier than the mature Dormition preaching it dissents from, so the immortalist reading loses its claim to be the more primitive one — it is a minority dissent from within the developed tradition, not its original voice.

Why “plausible” and not “weak.” Losing its bid for antiquity does not refute the reading; three things keep it coherent and unruled-out. First, the earliest witness we have, Epiphanius, deliberately would not decide against it and listed survival without death as a live option — the tradition begins agnostic, not committed to death. Second, the reading is coherent with the doctrine: an unfallen Mary spared death as she was spared corruption is a recognizable move, it has real if minority patristic and medieval support, and it cannot be disproved from Scripture, which is silent on Mary’s end either way. Third, the church that defined the Assumption declined to define the death: the 1950 definition’s guarded phrasing leaves the point open on purpose, at the highest level. A reading its own defining authority refused to foreclose is not weak — it is a coherent minority position — even though, against the whole weight of the narratives, homilists, and ordinary magisterium, it remains the less probable answer and short of majority support.

Assessment

Plausible. That Mary was taken up without dying is coherent and not ruled out — held open by the earliest witness (Epiphanius), patterned on Enoch and Elijah, unrefuted by Scripture’s silence, and left un-foreclosed by the deliberately guarded 1950 definition. But it is a minority reading and the less probable one: it is thin in the record next to the Dormition, and its chief early proof-text (“Timothy of Jerusalem”) is now dated too late — Capelle’s sixth-to-eighth-century placing against Jugie’s fourth/fifth — to give it the antiquity that would let it head the tradition. It survives as a coherent option the defining church chose not to close, not as the reading the evidence favors.
