Modestus of Jerusalem on the Assumption / Dormition of Mary

Dates: c. 630 (the Encomium on the Dormition is ascribed to Modestus, patriarch of Jerusalem 632–634; its authenticity is disputed — see Dating & authenticity below — so the year is approximate) · Modestus of Jerusalem
Summary: Under the name of Modestus — patriarch of the very see that hosted the Dormition tradition — stands what is often called the oldest surviving Greek homily for the feast. It preaches, in the same breath, a real death and a body kept from decay and taken up to God: Mary “did not experience corruption in that life-sheltering body of hers, even in the tomb.” The attribution, however, is contested, and the surviving text may be a generation or two later than the historical Modestus.

Claims & evidence

Her body did not see corruption, even in the tomb

“O most blessed dormition of the glorious Mother of God, of her who always remained a virgin, even after giving birth, and who did not experience corruption in that life-sheltering body of hers, even in the tomb! She is preserved [from it] by Christ the Savior, who came forth from her and who is mighty to do all things.”

— Encomium on the Dormition of Our Most Holy Lady, Mary, Mother of God and Ever-Virgin §7, trans. Brian E. Daley, On the Dormition of Mary: Early Patristic Homilies (St. Vladimir’s Seminary Press, 1998), English text online.1 The homily assumes a genuine death and a tomb — against any notion that Mary never died — and joins to it the claim the later Greek homilists would make standard: that the body laid there saw no decay.

Christ invested her with “an incorruptibility touching all her corporeal frame”

“For Christ our God, who put on living and intelligent flesh, which he took from the ever-virgin and the Holy Spirit, has called her to himself and invested her with an incorruptibility touching all her corporeal frame; he has glorified her beyond all measure of glory, so that she, his holy Mother, might share his inheritance.”

— Encomium on the Dormition §5, trans. Daley.2 The incorruptibility is explicitly bodily — it “touch[es] all her corporeal frame” — and it is Christ’s act of “calling her to himself,” not merely the soul’s survival, that the homilist celebrates.

The ark of sanctification is “brought home… to himself”

“When [Mary] had completed her life’s voyage happily… The one who gave the Law on Sinai, and who administered it from Sion, our God, summoned his ark of sanctification to be brought home from Sion to himself, just as David, her ancestor, had said of her in a psalm, ‘Go up, O Lord, to the place of your rest, you and your ark of sanctification’ (Ps 131:8). She is not carried like Moses’ ark of old, drawn by oxen, but she is escorted and surrounded by an army, heaven’s holy angels.”

— Encomium on the Dormition §4, trans. Daley.3 The Ark typology — Mary as the true “ark of sanctification” summoned by God to his rest — is the homily’s figure for the taking-up, escorted not by oxen but by the angelic host. It is imagery for a bodily translation, in the vocabulary of Psalm 131:8, not an argument from Scripture that one occurred.

Dating & authenticity

The homily is transmitted under the name of Modestus, who administered the see of Jerusalem after the Persian sack of 614 and was its patriarch c. 632–634. If genuinely his, it is the earliest datable Jerusalem homily for the feast — a witness to the bodily-glorification claim from the tradition’s own home — and belongs around c. 630.

The attribution is, however, seriously contested. Martin Jugie argued the piece is pseudepigraphic (hence the author is often called “pseudo-Modestus”), and the strongest internal evidence is doctrinal: the text speaks in terms of Christ’s two natural wills, the dyothelite formula defined only at the Third Council of Constantinople (680–681) — some fifty years after Modestus’s death. On that reading the surviving text cannot be the historical Modestus’s, and dates later in the seventh century.4 Either way the homily remains a seventh-century Jerusalem witness to a confident, detailed affirmation of the bodily Dormition; only its precise date, and whether it is the oldest such homily, turn on the authenticity question.

Notes

  • Modestus’s formula matches, almost word for word, the pairing already set out by Theoteknos of Livias (c. 600): a real death joined to an incorrupt body taken up to heaven. The two Palestinian homilists show the mature Dormition doctrine circulating in its birthplace by the seventh century.
  • The Jerusalem provenance matters to the doctrine’s history: it is from this see that John of Damascus (c. 730) would later relay the Euthymiac History’s story of Mary’s empty tomb, opened at Chalcedon (451). Whether that Jerusalem tradition reaches back to an apostolic memory or into the fifth-/sixth-century apocrypha is weighed in the argument Tradition or apocrypha?
