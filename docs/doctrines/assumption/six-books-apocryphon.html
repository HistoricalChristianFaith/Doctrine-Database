The Six Books Dormition apocryphon

Dates: c. 400 — an anonymous Syriac narrative of Mary’s death and bodily assumption (“The Departure of my Lady Mary from this World”), surviving in manuscripts of the fifth and sixth centuries but, on the dominant scholarly dating, composed in the fourth (its date is disputed: see below) · e-clavis: Six Books Dormition of the Virgin
Summary: The Six Books is one of the two oldest families of Dormition narrative (alongside the Palm-of-the-Tree-of-Life tradition behind the Liber Requiei), and the earliest datable text to tell the bodily assumption as a full story: Mary truly dies, her soul departs, and her body is borne by the twelve apostles in a chariot of light to the Paradise of Eden, where Christ raises it to life again and crowns her in glory. It is the apocryphal stratum the later homilists and Gregory of Tours drew on — and, if the early datings are right, it carries datable Dormition belief back toward the century of Epiphanius.

Claims & evidence

Mary dies, and her body is carried by the apostles to the Paradise of Eden

“And straightway the soul of the blessed one departed from her, and He sent it to the mansions of the Father’s house. … And our Lord commanded them to place the blessed one in a chariot of light; and the twelve apostles bore it as it went to the Paradise of Eden.”

— The Departure of my Lady Mary from this World (Book Fourth), trans. William Wright, Journal of Sacred Literature 7 (April 1865), p. 152. The narrative presupposes a real death — the soul genuinely “departed” — and then a bodily translation: it is her body (“it”) that the apostles bear in the chariot of light to paradise. This is the Dormition pattern (a true falling-asleep) joined to the Assumption (the body taken up), told as a single completed event some two centuries before Gregory of Tours states the same in the West.

In the Paradise of Eden Christ raises her body and crowns her in glory

“When the blessed one was placed in the Paradise of Eden and was crowned with this great glory … our Lord Jesus came to his mother into the Paradise of Eden. … And when our Lord came to my Lady Mary, he called to her and said: ‘Mary, rise.’ And straightway she was restored to life and worshipped Him.”

— The Departure of my Lady Mary (Book Fifth), trans. Wright, Journal of Sacred Literature 7 (April 1865), p. 156. The body is not left to the grave: it is reunited with the soul, called back to life by Christ, and glorified in paradise. From here the text carries Mary on a long apocalyptic tour of the heavens — the “cosmic tour” whose archaic eschatology is central to the dating argument below.

The disputed date — and why it matters

The Six Books is the single most important counterweight to the reading of the Assumption as a purely late-antique invention, because its date is contested and the dominant case pushes it early. The surviving Syriac manuscripts run to the fifth and sixth centuries (the oldest a late-fifth-century palimpsest edited by Agnes Smith Lewis), but the composition is widely placed earlier. Stephen Shoemaker judges that the “age of manuscripts secures a date of the early fifth century at the absolute latest, but a number of features identify a likely origin by the second half of the fourth century if not even earlier.”1 Richard Bauckham, analysing the concluding apocalyptic “cosmic tour,” reaches the same neighbourhood: though the apocryphon “in its present form probably dates from the fifth century,” the tour’s archaic picture of the dead — awaiting judgment in an intermediate state, with paradise and hell prepared but not yet inhabited — leads him to conclude it “dates from the fourth century at the latest, but perhaps considerably earlier.”2 Van Esbroeck, Gribomont, and others concur in a fourth-century (or earlier) origin.

The dating is genuinely disputed, not settled: a minority — Martin Jugie and, in detail, Simon Mimouni — dates the tradition late, holding that it presupposes the Council of Chalcedon (451); Mimouni assigns even the oldest manuscript only to the second half of the sixth century.3 The debate, then, is not “early Shoemaker vs. late Bauckham” — the two are allies on an early date — but an early-dating majority (Bauckham, Shoemaker, van Esbroeck, Gribomont) against a late-dating minority (Jugie, Mimouni).

If the early datings hold, the payoff is large. Shoemaker argues that Epiphanius’s own polemic against the Kollyridians — women offering bread in Mary’s honour (Panarion 78–79, c. 377) — presupposes the Six Books, which is “the sole source from the ancient church to mandate regular liturgical offerings of bread to the Virgin,” set within its Dormition account; that would put the narrative in circulation in Palestine already by the middle of the fourth century, in Epiphanius’s own generation.4 Datable Dormition belief would then stand not two centuries after Epiphanius’s “no one knows” but alongside it — and Epiphanius, who elsewhere writes that Mary “was assumed and has not seen death” (Panarion 79.5.2), turns from a clean witness against the belief into a witness that the question was already alive and contested in his day.

Notes

  • The early date establishes the circulation of the traditions, not a fixed dogma: even the strongest case places a narrative in the fourth century, whereas Epiphanius’s guarded Letter to Arabia still confesses that Scripture “kept silence” on Mary’s end. Possibility of early circulation is not proof of an apostolic tradition, and a three-century gap back to the apostles remains. The Six Books thus bears on how early the belief is datable, not on whether it is apostolic — see the argument on the silence of the first centuries.
  • Belonging to the same apocryphal Transitus Mariae cycle later listed among the books “not to be received” in the Gelasian Decree, the Six Books is a chief exhibit in the argument over whether the apocryphal narratives are a sound documentary foundation. Notably, though, its milieu is less heterodox than the oldest Palm-tradition layer: Shoemaker finds in the Six Books an already “advanced Marian piety” in a broadly orthodox, monotheist setting, where the Liber Requiei carries gnostic-tinged vocabulary and a demiurgic myth.
  • The text’s bodily assumption is the pattern the East’s Dormition homilists and Gregory of Tours (c. 590) would inherit; the belief passes from this apocryphal narrative into mainstream episcopal preaching over the following two centuries.
