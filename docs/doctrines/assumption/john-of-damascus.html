John of Damascus on the Assumption / Dormition of Mary

Dates: c. 730 (the three homilies on the Dormition, preached at the church of Gethsemane; John, monk of Mar Saba near Jerusalem, c. 675/676–749, called the last of the Greek Fathers) · Wikipedia
Summary: John of Damascus gives the Dormition its classic Eastern statement. He proclaims the bodily glorification of Mary without reserve — her tomb could not hold her, her flesh was kept from corruption — yet he is candid that the account rests on tradition, not on Scripture, and he takes pains to relay the pedigree of that tradition through the Euthymiac History.

Claims & evidence

Mary was assumed; the tomb could not hold her

“Why do you seek in the tomb one who has been assumed to the heavenly courts?”

— Second Homily on the Dormition of the Theotokos, English translation. John argues from fittingness that she who bore the source of life should not be left to decay: “It was fitting that the body of her, who preserved her virginity unsullied in her motherhood, should be kept from corruption even after death.”1 The Dormition is preached with full confidence — but as a mystery received, not as a text expounded.

The warrant is tradition — the Euthymiac History and Chalcedon

John rests the belief on a tradition he is careful to trace. The Euthymiac History, an excerpt appended to his second homily and circulated with it in every manuscript, reports that at the Council of Chalcedon (451) the Emperor Marcian and Empress Pulcheria, wishing to enshrine Mary’s body in a church at Constantinople, asked Juvenal of Jerusalem for her relics; Juvenal answered that when Mary fell asleep the apostles were gathered, her body was laid in Gethsemane, and after three days the tomb was opened — at the late-arriving apostle’s request — and found empty, only the grave-clothes remaining, whence they knew her body had been taken up.2 The appeal is telling: the doctrine’s classic advocate grounds it not in the sacred text but in a chain of tradition he must explicitly vouch for.

Notes

  • The Dormition presupposes that Mary truly died — and so a soul surviving death, the middle state traced on the intermediate-state timeline. The Assumption is then the claim that Mary does not await the general resurrection like everyone else but is raised and glorified ahead of it.
  • John of Damascus appears on this wiki also as a witness for the perpetual virginity of Mary, where he calls her aeiparthenos (“ever-virgin”).
