Andrew of Crete on the Assumption / Dormition of Mary

Dates: c. 725 (Andrew, archbishop of Crete, c. 660–740; three homilies on the Dormition) · Wikipedia
Summary: Andrew’s three Dormition homilies give the Eastern feast its most developed early statement. He treats Mary’s death as a real death — but a Koimēsis, a sleep unto resurrection — and holds that her flesh, like her virginity, was preserved from corruption: her tomb was found empty and her body transported to heaven. With him the bodily assumption is settled Eastern preaching a few years before John of Damascus gives it its classic form.

Claims & evidence

Her body was taken up; the tomb allowed no corruption

“The [Lord’s] birth escaped corruption, nor did her [Mary’s] tomb allow corruption, which does not touch what is holy.”

— Andrew of Crete, Second Homily on the Dormition, trans. Brian E. Daley, On the Dormition of Mary (St. Vladimir’s Seminary Press, 1998), English text online.1 Andrew reasons from the empty tomb, as the Eastern tradition regularly does: the grave-clothes remain but the body is gone, because “what had been entombed there escaped destruction.”

Christ carries his mother out of the world

“Today, Jesus transports the Queen of the human race outside of her earthly dwelling …”

— Second Homily on the Dormition, trans. Daley.2 The verb is deliberate: not that Mary rises by her own power but that she is carried up — an assumption, a being-taken, in line with the older Palestinian language. Andrew parallels her passing to Christ’s own: as the Virgin’s womb was not corrupted in childbearing, so her flesh did not perish in death.

Notes

  • Andrew keeps the two Eastern claims in balance: Mary truly died (his homilies dwell on her death as a genuine Koimēsis, a separation of soul and body that calls forth the hope of resurrection), yet her body was not left to decay. This is the same pairing found in Theoteknos of Livias and Germanus of Constantinople — real death joined to incorruption — and it is what distinguishes the Dormition from a mere translation like Elijah’s.
  • Andrew, Germanus, and John of Damascus — the three great eighth-century Dormition homilists — all affirm Mary’s real death. The 1950 definition would deliberately decline to settle that very point, leaving open whether Mary died before she was assumed (see Pius XII).
