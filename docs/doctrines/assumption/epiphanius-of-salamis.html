Epiphanius of Salamis on the Assumption / Dormition of Mary

Dates: c. 377 (Epiphanius, bishop of Constantia — ancient Salamis — in Cyprus, one of the most learned churchmen of his age; his witness here is the treatise against the Arabian Antidicomarianites folded whole into Book III of his Panarion as chapter 78, finished about 377; he died in 403) · Wikipedia
Summary: Epiphanius is the earliest writer known to raise the question of how Mary’s earthly life ended, and he answers it with deliberate agnosticism: Scripture is silent, the possibilities are several, and no one knows. He neither affirms nor denies a bodily assumption — he refuses to decide, and grounds that refusal in the silence of the record.

Claims & evidence

Scripture does not record whether Mary died

“But if some think us mistaken, let them search the Scriptures. They will not find Mary’s death; they will not find whether she died or did not die.”

— Panarion 78 (Against the Antidicomarianites), c. 377, as quoted (Carol, Mariology II, 139–40). Epiphanius challenges his opponents to comb the sacred text for any account of Mary’s end and insists they will find none — the whole force of his argument being that the church of his day possessed no authoritative record of what became of her.

Three possibilities, and a chosen silence

“For my own part, I do not dare to speak, but I keep my own thoughts and I practice silence.”

— Panarion 78.23, as quoted (Carol, Mariology II, 139–40). In the surrounding passage Epiphanius canvasses three fates for the Virgin without endorsing any: that she died and was honourably buried; that she was martyred (reading the “sword” of Luke 2:35 literally); or that she was left alive, “for God is not incapable of doing whatever he wills.” Having set them side by side he declines to choose — a reticence he ties to the absence of any scriptural or traditional warrant, adding that even the apostle John, who took Mary into his care, nowhere records that he carried her with him or what befell her.

Notes

  • Epiphanius is often cited on both sides of the modern debate. His “no one knows” is the strongest early evidence that no fixed Assumption tradition yet existed; but his openness to Mary having “remained alive” is sometimes read as an early hint of the belief. The safer reading is the one his own words state: he is undecided, and says so because the record gave him nothing to decide with. Stephen Shoemaker notes that his indecision itself suggests some difference of opinion had already arisen among Christians — but no authoritative tradition Epiphanius could appeal to. Whether that silence refutes the doctrine’s apostolic antiquity is weighed on its own page: Does the silence of the first centuries refute the Assumption’s apostolic antiquity? (assessed contested).
  • The same treatise, Panarion 78, is Epiphanius’s great defence of Mary’s perpetual virginity against the Antidicomarianites — so the earliest witness to the silence about her end is also a chief witness for her ever-virginity. He appears on this wiki as a witness on several other timelines as well.
