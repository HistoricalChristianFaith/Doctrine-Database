Gregory of Tours on the Assumption / Dormition of Mary

Dates: c. 590 (Gregory, bishop of Tours in Gaul, c. 538–594; the account stands in his Glory of the Martyrs, De gloria martyrum I.4, written 580–594) · Wikipedia
Summary: Gregory gives the earliest plain narrative affirmation of the bodily assumption in the Latin West. He retells, as received fact, the story that had come to him through the apocryphal Transitus tradition: at the end of Mary’s life the apostles gathered, the Lord received her soul, and her body was carried in a cloud to paradise and there rejoined to her soul.

Claims & evidence

Mary’s body was taken up to paradise and rejoined to her soul

“The Lord … commanded the body of Mary be taken in a cloud into paradise; where now, rejoined to the soul, Mary dwells with the chosen ones.”

— Glory of the Martyrs 4, as translated online (cf. R. Van Dam, trans., Glory of the Martyrs, Liverpool University Press). In the fuller account Gregory describes the apostles miraculously assembled at Mary’s house as her earthly course closed; the Lord Jesus coming with his angels and giving her soul to the archangel Michael; the apostles laying her body in a tomb and keeping watch; and the Lord returning to bear the body away. It is the first Western text to state the bodily assumption as a completed event.

Notes

  • Gregory’s source is not Scripture or any named apostolic testimony but the Transitus Mariae (“Passing of Mary”) narratives — the same apocryphal cycle later listed among the books “not to be received” in the Gelasian Decree. His witness therefore marks the point at which the belief passes from apocryphal narrative into mainstream episcopal teaching, two centuries after Epiphanius could say only that no one knew Mary’s end.
  • Gregory’s account reads as an epitome of the “Bethlehem” family of Transitus narratives, whose fullest Latin representative is Pseudo-Melito’s Transitus Mariae (c. 500) — the likeliest channel by which the story reached Western preaching. Notably, that Latin text opens by disowning an earlier Transitus it brands heretical, so the tradition Gregory here relays as settled fact is one its own documents were busy correcting.
  • Later Latin advocacy is markedly more guarded than Gregory: Paschasius Radbertus (c. 860) would warn against those very apocrypha and decline to affirm the bodily assumption as certain.
