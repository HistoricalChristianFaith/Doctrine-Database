Theoteknos of Livias on the Assumption / Dormition of Mary

Dates: c. 600 (Theoteknos, bishop of Livias in Palestine, east of the Jordan; his episcopate is placed between 550 and 650, and the homily with it) · e-Clavis
Summary: Theoteknos preached one of the two oldest surviving Greek homilies for the feast, and it marks a decisive shift in vocabulary and confidence. Where his contemporaries speak of Mary’s Koimēsis (“falling asleep”), Theoteknos calls it her Análēpsis — her “taking up,” the strong word — and affirms outright that her body, though it truly died, was kept from corruption and lifted to heaven with her soul.

Claims & evidence

Mary was “taken up into heaven” — body with soul

“she who had become wider than the heavens and higher than the cherubim was to receive the palm of reward to which she had been called, and to be taken up into heaven.”

— Theoteknos of Livias, Encomium on the Assumption of the Holy Mother of God §1, trans. Brian E. Daley, On the Dormition of Mary: Early Patristic Homilies (St. Vladimir’s Seminary Press, 1998), English text online.1 His very title uses the “taking-up” word: this is the earliest surviving homily to frame the feast as an Assumption rather than merely a Dormition, and to press the bodily claim so plainly.

Her body tasted death, yet was kept incorrupt

“even though the God-bearing body of that holy one did taste death, it was not corrupted; for it was kept incorrupt and free of decay, and it was lifted up to heaven with her pure and spotless soul by the holy archangels and powers.”

— Encomium on the Assumption §4, trans. Daley.2 The formula is exact and much imitated after him: a real death (against those who would have Mary never die), joined to a body preserved from decay and raised. It is this precise pairing — death and incorruption — that the later Greek homilists would take up and make standard.

Notes

  • Theoteknos stands only two centuries after Epiphanius of Salamis, who in c. 377 could say only that Scripture is silent and “no one knows” how Mary’s life ended. The distance between them measures the doctrine’s development: an open question in the fourth century has become a confident, detailed affirmation in the seventh — carried not by any newly discovered apostolic memory but by the growth of the feast and the apocryphal narratives that fed it.
  • The bishopric of Livias lay in the old territory of Peraea, east of the Jordan — squarely within the Palestinian heartland where the 15 August Marian feast is first attested (see Emperor Maurice). Theoteknos writes from within the tradition’s birthplace.
