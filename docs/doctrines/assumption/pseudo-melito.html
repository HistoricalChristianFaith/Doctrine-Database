Pseudo-Melito, Transitus Mariae (the Latin Passing of Mary)

Dates: c. 500 (Latin; commonly placed in the late fifth or sixth century) — the Transitus Mariae falsely ascribed to Melito, bishop of Sardis (who in fact died c. 180), so cited as “Pseudo-Melito”; the “B” recension is the most widely copied of the Latin narratives and was edited critically by Monika Haibach-Reinisch (1962) · e-clavis: Assumption of the Virgin, by Pseudo-Melito
Summary: Pseudo-Melito’s Transitus is the most influential Latin narrative of Mary’s bodily assumption and the likeliest conduit by which the story reached Western preaching — the same “Bethlehem”-family account that Gregory of Tours epitomizes a generation or so later. Its unusual prologue is what makes it a witness worth pausing over: it presents itself openly as an orthodox correction of an earlier Transitus it brands heretical — the work of one “Leucius” — so heterodox that its account of Mary’s assumption “would be impious not only to read … in the church of God, but even to hear.”

Claims & evidence

The prologue: a self-declared purge of a heretical Transitus

“Melito, servant of Christ, bishop of the church of Sardis, to the venerable brethren in the Lord appointed at Laodicea, in peace greeting. I remember that I have often written of one Leucius, who, having along with ourselves associated with the apostles, turned aside through alienated feelings and a rash soul from the path of rectitude, and inserted very many things in his books about the acts of the apostles. … Nor did he think this to be enough; but he even vitiated, by his impious writing, the assumption of the blessed ever-virgin Mary, the mother of God, to such a degree that it would be impious not only to read it in the church of God, but even to hear it.”

— The Passing of Mary (Second Latin Form / Pseudo-Melito), prologue, trans. Ante-Nicene Fathers, vol. VIII (cf. M. R. James, The Apocryphal New Testament, Oxford, 1924, pp. 209–16).1 The prologue then promises a clean text — “those things which we heard from the apostle John” — set down “believing, not the strange dogmas hatched by heretics, but the Father in the Son, the Son in the Father.” It is one of the frankest admissions in the tradition that the narrative of Mary’s end was known, from within, to have grown up in doctrinally compromised hands — here a Latin counterpart to the Greek disavowal John of Thessalonica would voice about the same corpus. (The “Leucius” framing is itself a literary device: the pseudonymous author, writing centuries after the real Melito, backdates his correction to the apostolic age, so its historical value lies in the concession, not in the attribution.)

The narrative: Mary’s body raised from the tomb and borne to paradise

“Arise, my beloved and my nearest relation; thou who hast not put on corruption by intercourse with man, suffer not destruction of the body in the sepulchre. And immediately Mary rose from the tomb, and blessed the Lord … the angels along with Him, carrying the blessed Mary into the paradise of God.”

— The Passing of Mary (Second Latin Form / Pseudo-Melito), §§16–17, trans. Ante-Nicene Fathers, vol. VIII.2 As in the Syriac Six Books, the pattern is Dormition joined to Assumption: a real death and burial, then the soul restored to an incorrupt body which is carried bodily into paradise. This is the completed-event story Gregory of Tours would tell as received fact in the Latin West.

Notes

  • Date and recension. The text is pseudonymous — ascribed to Melito of Sardis (d. c. 180) but composed centuries later, generally between the fifth and seventh centuries, with the late fifth century the earliest commonly proposed. The dominant “B” recension (surviving in scores of manuscripts) was given its critical edition by Monika Haibach-Reinisch, Ein neuer ‘Transitus Mariae’ des Pseudo-Melito (Rome, 1962); an older Latin form had been printed by Tischendorf.3
  • The conduit to the West. Pseudo-Melito’s Transitus is the fullest Latin representative of the “Bethlehem” narrative family, and Gregory of Tours’s account in the Glory of the Martyrs (c. 590) is best read as an epitome of just this kind of Latin Transitus — so this text (or its immediate source) is the likeliest channel by which the bodily-assumption story passed from apocryphal narrative into mainstream Western preaching and, in time, the Roman liturgy.
  • Squarely within what Rome branded spurious. A Transitus “Passing of holy Mary” is precisely what the Gelasian Decree lists among the books “not to be received” — the same condemnation Paschasius Radbertus would invoke against “that apocryphal work on the passing of the virgin.” That Pseudo-Melito himself opens by disowning a heretical predecessor makes this text a chief exhibit in the argument over whether the apocryphal Transitus narratives are a sound documentary foundation.
