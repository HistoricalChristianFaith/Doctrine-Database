Pseudo-Evodius of Rome on the Assumption / Dormition of Mary

Dates: c. 590 (the Coptic Dormition homily attributed to Evodius of Rome; sixth century, dating disputed) · Wikipedia
Summary: The Coptic homily On the Falling Asleep of Mary, pseudonymously ascribed to Evodius “the archbishop of the great city Rome … the second after Peter,” is the fullest surviving witness to the distinctive Egyptian two-stage pattern: Mary dies on the 21st of Tobe (mid-January) and her body is raised and taken up seven months later, on the 16th of Mesore (mid-August). It corroborates with a primary text the calendrical split the Emperor Maurice page reports — “some kept this feast in January, with the monks of Egypt; others in August, with those of Palestine” — by showing that Egypt in fact kept both moments as one narrative sequence.

Claims & evidence

Mary’s death, dated to 21 Tobe: her soul leaps into her Son’s bosom

“An instruction which our holy and in every wise honourable father Abba Evodius, the archbishop of the great city Rome, who was the second after Peter the apostle, delivered. Now he delivered it concerning the Lady of us all, the holy God-bearer Mary; manifesting the matter to us, that she ended her holy life on the twenty-first of the month Tobi according to the Egyptians.”

“It came to pass when she saw him with her eyes, her soul leaped from her body into the bosom of her beloved Son in the place where He was sitting … And it came to pass, when He had hold of the soul of His virgin mother — for it was white as snow — He saluted it, and wrapped it in garments of fine linen, and gave it to Michael the holy archangel, who bare it on his wings of light, until He appointed the place for her holy body.”

— The Falling Asleep of Mary. Instruction by Evodius (Bohairic), §§XII, in Forbes Robinson, Coptic Apocryphal Gospels (Cambridge, 1896), pp. 45, 60. The homily presupposes a real death — the soul departs and is received by Christ — and fixes it firmly to the Egyptian calendar (21 Tobe), the January date that would become the Coptic feast of the Dormition.

A seven-month interval: the body is hidden until the 16th of Mesore

“behold a great voice came from heaven like the sound of a trumpet, saying, Go ye everyone to his place until the seventh month. For behold I have hardened the heart of the chief priests and of all the Jews, that they may not perceive nor know this place any more, nor seek the body of My virgin mother until I take it up to the heavens with Me. And ye all the disciples, and the virgins also that have tarried with you, come ye all hither on the sixteenth of the month Mesore, that ye may see My virgin mother when I take her to the heavens with Me, her soul being in her body, living as it was on earth with you.”

— ibid. §XVII, pp. 63–64. This deferral is the hinge of the two-stage tradition: the falling-asleep and the bodily assumption are separated by a seven-month gap, and each is anchored to its own feast-day — 21 Tobe for the death, 16 Mesore for the taking-up.

The assumption on 16 Mesore: a chariot of light, the body raised and reunited with the soul

“Straightway we looked, and saw a great chariot of light. It came and stayed in our midst, Cherubim drawing it, the holy Virgin Mary sitting upon it, and shining ten thousand times more than the sun and the moon … And the Lord called into the tomb, and raised the body of His virgin mother, and put her soul into her body again; and we saw it living in the body even as it was with us formerly, wearing the flesh. And our Saviour stretched out His hand, and set her on the chariot with Him … Behold ye see her face to face, raised by Me from the dead.”

— ibid. §XVIII, pp. 64–65. The bodily claim is explicit and unhedged: the corpse is raised, re-ensouled, seen “wearing the flesh,” and carried to heaven. The homily closes by naming both dates together: “This is the end of the life of the Lady of us all, the holy God-bearer Mary, on the twenty-first of Tobi and her assumption on the sixteenth of Mesore.”1

Notes

  • Pseudonymous. The historical Evodius was remembered as bishop of Antioch, the successor of Peter there — not of Rome. The homily’s claim that he was “archbishop of the great city Rome … the second after Peter” and an eyewitness of Mary’s end (“I saw all these things come to pass”) is a literary device; the work is a late-antique Coptic composition, not a first-century memoir. It belongs to a family of pseudonymous Egyptian Dormition homilies (others go under the names of Cyril of Jerusalem, Theophilus of Alexandria, and Evodius) that Shoemaker groups as the distinct Coptic Transitus tradition.2
  • Dating. The pseudonymous corpus is generally placed in the later sixth century, in non-Chalcedonian Egypt — a literary activity often located in the patriarchate of Damian of Alexandria (578–607). Shoemaker dates the earliest (Sahidic) form of the Evodius homily to before 567; the Bohairic recension translated by Robinson, which is the one that separates the two feasts across 21 Tobe and 16 Mesore, is later still. The exact anchor is therefore uncertain, and the c. 590 placed on the timeline is a central estimate, not a fixed date.2
  • The two feasts, reconciled. The Emperor Maurice page cites the report that “some kept this feast in January, with the monks of Egypt; others in August, with those of Palestine.” This homily shows the Egyptian side of that split from the inside: Egypt did not simply keep the feast in January instead of August — it kept a January Dormition and an August Assumption as two commemorations of one story. What Maurice is said to have standardized was one date (the Palestinian 15 August) over a patchwork that included this Egyptian two-stage reckoning.
  • Documentary foundation. Like the Latin Pseudo-Melito and the Syriac Six Books, this is an apocryphal narrative, not an apostolic report, however firstperson its framing — a further datum for the argument over the Transitus narratives as a documentary foundation. Its value here is as an early, regionally distinct witness to how confidently the bodily assumption was already being preached in sixth-century Egypt.
