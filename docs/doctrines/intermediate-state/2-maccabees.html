2 Maccabees on the Intermediate State

Dates: c. 124 BC · Wikipedia · back to timeline
Summary: 2 Maccabees 12:39–45 is the earliest explicit Jewish witness that the living can aid the dead. Finding idolatrous tokens on his fallen soldiers, Judas Maccabeus takes up a collection and sends a sin offering to Jerusalem for them, reasoning from the resurrection that to "pray for the dead" is "an holy and good thought" — making "a reconciliation for the dead, that they might be delivered from sin." It is the seed of the later prayers-for-the-dead / purgatory line.

Claims & evidence

A sin offering is sent to Jerusalem for the slain

And when he had made a gathering throughout the company to the sum of two thousand drachms of silver, he sent it to Jerusalem to offer a sin offering, doing therein very well and honestly, in that he was mindful of the resurrection:

— 2 Maccabees 12:43 (KJV/Apocrypha), Wikisource. After idolatrous amulets are found under the coats of the dead (vv. 39–40), Judas does not write them off: he gathers silver and sends it to the temple as an offering on their behalf — an act assuming that the condition of the dead is not yet fixed beyond all help.

Prayer for the dead presupposes the resurrection

For if he had not hoped that they that were slain should have risen again, it had been superfluous and vain to pray for the dead.

— 2 Maccabees 12:44 (KJV/Apocrypha), Wikisource. The author grounds the practice in the resurrection hope: it would be "vain to pray for the dead" if the dead simply ceased to be. Intercession for them only makes sense because they live and will rise — an intermediate state in which their case is still open.

A reconciliation is made for the dead, that they might be delivered from sin

And also in that he perceived that there was great favour laid up for those that died godly, it was an holy and good thought. Whereupon he made a reconciliation for the dead, that they might be delivered from sin.

— 2 Maccabees 12:45 (KJV/Apocrypha), Wikisource. The dead "that died godly" have "great favour laid up" for them, and the offering is "to make a reconciliation for the dead, that they might be delivered from sin." This is the text the Latin West would later marshal for a post-mortem cleansing and the suffrages of the living.

Notes

  • This is the earliest explicit witness that the dead can be helped by the prayers, alms, and sacrifice of the living — older than any developed theory of purgatory. Whether it warrants a distinct cleansing fire is weighed on the purgatorial-fire argument page; its dogmatic weight turns on canonicity, since 2 Maccabees is deuterocanonical (received in the Catholic and Orthodox canons, classed apocryphal by Jews and Protestants).
  • It also presupposes, alongside 1 Enoch 22 and Wisdom 3, a Second Temple background in which the dead consciously await the resurrection rather than passing at once to a fixed and final state.
