Justin Martyr on the Intermediate State

Dates: c. 165 · Wikipedia
Summary: Justin Martyr (c. 100–165), philosopher and apologist, holds that the soul is not by nature immortal but survives death by God's will: at death the souls of the pious go to a better place and those of the unjust to a worse, both awaiting the resurrection and judgment. He treats the notion that souls go straight to heaven at death (with no resurrection) as a mark of heretics who are not to be reckoned Christians.

Claims & evidence

The souls of the pious and the unjust remain in distinct places, awaiting the judgment

But I do not say, indeed, that all souls die; for that were truly a piece of good fortune to the evil. What then? The souls of the pious remain in a better place, while those of the unjust and wicked are in a worse, waiting for the time of judgment. Thus some which have appeared worthy of God never die; but others are punished so long as God wills them to exist and to be punished.

— Dialogue with Trypho, ch. 5, historicalchristian.faith. In the chapter headed "The Soul is Not in Its Own Nature Immortal," Justin (relaying the old man who converted him) denies the Platonic doctrine that the soul is unbegotten and inherently immortal: the soul lives only as a "partaker of life" because God wills it. On this footing the dead do not lapse into nothing, nor do they pass at once to their final reward; the pious are kept "in a better place" and the wicked "in a worse," both waiting for the time of judgment — a conscious, divided intermediate state short of the resurrection.

To say souls go to heaven at death (and deny the resurrection) is the mark of a heretic, not a Christian

For if you have fallen in with some who are called Christians, but who do not admit this [truth], and venture to blaspheme the God of Abraham, and the God of Isaac, and the God of Jacob; who say there is no resurrection of the dead, and that their souls, when they die, are taken to heaven; do not imagine that they are Christians, even as one, if he would rightly consider it, would not admit that the Sadducees, or similar sects of Genistae, Meristae, Galilaeans, Hellenists, Pharisees, Baptists, are Jews … But I and others, who are right-minded Christians on all points, are assured that there will be a resurrection of the dead, and a thousand years in Jerusalem, which will then be built, adorned, and enlarged, [as] the prophets Ezekiel and Isaiah and others declare.

— Dialogue with Trypho, ch. 80, historicalchristian.faith. Justin couples two errors as a single heresy: denying the resurrection and holding that souls are "taken to heaven" immediately at death. Against this he ranks himself with the "right-minded Christians" who expect a bodily resurrection (and, here, a millennial reign in Jerusalem). The polemic shows that, for Justin, the intermediate state is precisely an interim: the saved are not yet in heaven but wait, in a better place, for the resurrection to come.

Notes

  • Justin's view is the early "soul sleep is not it, but neither is immediate heaven" position: the dead are conscious and sorted (pious vs. unjust), yet still awaiting the resurrection and judgment — an explicit two-place, interim geography rather than a final one.
  • His framing is distinctive in branding the "souls-to-heaven-at-death" view as a heresy that disqualifies its holders from the name "Christian." This places him sharply against any reading that collapses the intermediate state into the final state.
  • The denial of the soul's natural immortality (ch. 5–6: the soul lives only as God wills, as a "partaker of life") is the metaphysical ground of his account: survival after death is a gift of God's will, not an inherent property of the soul.
  • Justin's expectation of "a thousand years in Jerusalem" (ch. 80) ties his intermediate-state doctrine to his chiliasm: the righteous dead wait for the resurrection that inaugurates the kingdom on earth.
  • In the same Dialogue (ch. 72) Justin lists, among Scriptures he says the Jews "cut out," a one-line descent oracle he ascribes to Jeremiah — "The Lord God remembered His dead people of Israel … and He descended to preach to them His own salvation." His general charge that the Jewish text was being revised has a real basis (Aquila's contemporaneous literal revision was displacing the Septuagint; Justin himself misread such a revision as malice at Isa 7:14), but the specific excision claim does not hold: the line survives in no text of either prophet, not even the pre-Christian Greek, and his excision formula also tags a passage that never left the canon (Jer 11:19) and a clause ("from the wood," Ps 96:10) that is in fact a Christian addition rather than a Jewish deletion. Weighed on the excised-descent-prophecy argument (assessment: weak).
