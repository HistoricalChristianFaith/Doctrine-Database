Eusebius of Caesarea on the Intermediate State

Dates: c. 324 · Wikipedia · back to timeline
Summary: Eusebius of Caesarea, in the Church History, preserves a compact descent confession: Christ went down to Hades, burst its ancient bars, and raised the dead — "he descended alone, but rose with many." It is a fourth-century witness to the harrowing of Hades within an otherwise narrative report.

Claims & evidence

Christ descended into Hades, burst its eternal bars, and raised the dead — "descended alone, but rose with many"

and concerning his new preaching, and his abasement and humiliation, and how he humbled himself, and died and debased his divinity and was crucified, and descended into Hades, and burst the bars which from eternity had not been broken, and raised the dead; for he descended alone, but rose with many, and thus ascended to his Father.

— Church History (Historia Ecclesiastica) I.13.19, historicalchristian.faith. The line sits within Eusebius's report of the Edessa/Abgar correspondence: it is the preaching the apostle Thaddeus promises to deliver before the citizens of Edessa, summarizing the gospel from the incarnation to the ascension. The "descended alone, but rose with many" formula classically expresses the freeing of the captive dead — Christ enters the place of the dead by himself, but leads its prisoners out with him when he rises.

Notes

  • This is a brief credal-style descent statement, not a developed treatment of Hades or of the soul's interim condition. Eusebius is quoting (or paraphrasing) a missionary discourse, not arguing a doctrine of the intermediate state in his own voice; the value of the witness is that the harrowing of Hades is rehearsed as a settled, summary article of the gospel in the early fourth century.
  • The triad — descent into Hades, the breaking of its "bars which from eternity had not been broken," and the raising of the dead — assumes the place of the dead is a real locale holding captives that Christ liberates. See the descent to the dead argument page for the wider adjudication of this reading.
  • "He descended alone, but rose with many" compactly states the harrowing motif also developed at length by earlier witnesses (e.g. Irenaeus, who reads Christ's descent as preaching to the prophets and patriarchs below). Eusebius's contribution is not novelty but the attestation that this descent-and-deliverance language was a fixed part of the Church's preaching as he records it.
