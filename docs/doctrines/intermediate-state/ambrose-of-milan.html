Ambrose of Milan on the Intermediate State

Dates: c. 380 · Wikipedia · back to timeline
Summary: The early Latin Easter office hymn Aurora Lucis Rutilat ("Heaven with rosy morn is glowing"), traditionally ascribed to Ambrose, sings the harrowing of hell: the risen Christ despoils the realm of the dead — "Hell is writhing in despair" — and "leads his saints to life and light," putting the descent-and-liberation into the Church's worship. (The ascription to Ambrose himself is traditional, not certain — see Notes.)

Claims & evidence

The risen Christ harrows hell and leads his saints out into life and light

Heaven with rosy morn is glowing,
Songs of triumph fill the air,
Strains of praise from earth are flowing,
Hell is writhing in despair.

Earth's great king, in glory springing,
From the deep sepulchral night,
While loud anthems round are ringing,
Leads His saints to life and light.

— Aurora Lucis Rutilat ("Heaven with rosy morn is glowing"), tr. John Williams, hymnary.org. The hymn frames Easter as a contest of realms: as heaven and earth break into triumph, the underworld below recoils — "Hell is writhing in despair." The risen "Earth's great king" rises "from the deep sepulchral night" not alone but as a deliverer, leading "His saints" — the righteous dead who waited below — out "to life and light." This is the descent-and-liberation (the harrowing of hell) cast as liturgy: the soul's interim sojourn among the dead, and Christ's breaking of that captivity, sung in the Church's Easter worship.

Notes

  • Attribution caveat (important): the ascription of Aurora Lucis Rutilat to Ambrose is traditional, not certain. It is an anonymous early-medieval Latin office hymn long associated with the Ambrosian / early Milanese tradition, but not securely Ambrose's own composition. We slot it under Ambrose at c. 380 by that traditional ascription, with this caveat noted; the witness is to the early Latin liturgical tradition more than to a documented authorial position of Ambrose.
  • This is a worship / hymn witness, not a treatise — it shows the descent and the liberation of the dead already lodged in the Church's Easter liturgy, sung rather than argued.
  • The English here is a 19th-century translation (John Williams, from Ancient Hymns of the Holy Church, Hartford, 1845); the underlying Latin is the older text. See also descent to the dead.
