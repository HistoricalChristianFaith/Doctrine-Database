The "excised" descent-prophecy — authentic Scripture cut by the Jews, or an apocryphal logion?

Type: supporting argument / crux (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — as a witness to the descent the logion is genuine and early (Justin and Irenaeus both cite it as prophetic Scripture before AD 200), but the specific reading that earns it a place here — that it is an authentic canonical passage the Jews deliberately cut out of Jeremiah (or Isaiah) — does not hold: it is absent from every surviving Hebrew and Greek text of both prophets, Irenaeus's own attribution floats between Isaiah, Jeremiah and no prophet at all, and Justin's "the Jews excised it" is a charge he levels by formula at passages that in fact survive. The descent doctrine it was adduced for stands on other ground · back to timeline · advanced by Justin Martyr (Dialogue with Trypho 72, the excision charge) and Irenaeus (who quotes the logion five times as prophetic Scripture) — weighed against the text-critical mainstream, which classes it an apocryphal testimonium

An argument page weighs a single sub-claim adduced in support of a main interpretation — a proof-text, a parallel, a historical thesis — and renders an adversarial assessment of the interpretation it serves. Unlike a person-detail page it sits across the timeline rather than at one date. Every factual claim is sourced; the proponent's own words are quoted verbatim.

This crux concerns the authenticity of a single proof-text — a one-line prophecy of Christ's descent to the dead that the second-century Fathers quote as Scripture but that no biblical manuscript contains — and is kept distinct from the descent-to-the-dead crux, which weighs the doctrine itself. The descent can be true (and is independently grounded) whether or not this particular logion is genuine Scripture; the narrow question here is only whether the patristic claim that the Jews excised an authentic prophecy can be sustained.

The claim

Cataloguing passages he says Jewish teachers have removed from the Scriptures to hide their witness to Christ, Justin adds the descent logion to the list:

And again, from the sayings of the same Jeremiah these have been cut out: "The Lord God remembered His dead people of Israel who lay in the graves; and He descended to preach to them His own salvation."

— Justin Martyr, Dialogue with Trypho, ch. 72 (titled, in the manuscripts, "Passages Have Been Removed by the Jews from Esdras and Jeremiah").1 The argument the passage is adduced for is the descensus: here is a prophet, on Justin's reckoning, foretelling in so many words that the Lord would "descend to preach … His own salvation" to "His dead people … in the graves." Were the text genuine, it would be the Old Testament's most explicit anticipation of Christ's descent to the human dead — far plainer than the disputed Eph 4:9 ("the lower parts of the earth") or Ps 16:10 on which the doctrine otherwise leans.

Who advances it

The charge of deliberate excision is Justin's; the logion's standing as prophetic Scripture is underwritten above all by Irenaeus, who quotes it no fewer than five times across two works as the utterance of a named prophet, treating it as a settled testimony to the descent. Through Irenaeus it became a fixture of the early descent proof-base, and it is still occasionally produced — in popular and retrieval-minded surveys of the "lost texts" of the harrowing — as evidence that the descent was prophesied in the Hebrew Scriptures and suppressed. Against this stands the text-critical and patristic mainstream, which finds the passage in no biblical manuscript and reads it instead as a free-floating Christian testimonium mistakenly ascribed to the prophets.

The witnesses

The logion is unusually well attested for an extra-canonical line — and that very spread of citation is what exposes its problem. Within a single author, Irenaeus, it is fathered on different prophets:

  1. To Isaiah — "And that it was not a mere man who died for us, Isaiah says: 'And the holy Lord remembered His dead Israel, who had slept in the land of sepulture; and He came down to preach His salvation to them, that He might save them'" (Against Heresies III.20.4).2
  2. To Jeremiah — "As Jeremiah declares, 'The holy Lord remembered His dead Israel, who slept in the land of sepulture; and He descended to them to make known to them His salvation, that they might be saved'" (AH IV.22.1) — agreeing with Justin's ascription.3
  3. To no prophet in particular — woven anonymously into catalogues of messianic testimonies: Christ "remembered His own dead ones who had formerly fallen asleep, and came down to them that He might deliver them" (AH IV.33.1), and again, "Others … said, 'The holy Lord remembered His own dead ones who slept in the dust, and came down to them to raise them up, that He might save them'" (AH IV.33.12).4
  4. Back to Jeremiah — "And in Jeremiah He thus declares His death and descent into hell, saying: 'And the Lord the Holy One of Israel, remembered his dead, which aforetime fell asleep in the dust of the earth; and he went down unto them, to bring the tidings of his salvation, to deliver them'" (Demonstration of the Apostolic Preaching 78).4

No two quotations agree word-for-word, and the prophet named is Isaiah once, Jeremiah twice, and left blank twice — exactly the profile of a text recalled from memory or a proof-text anthology, not transcribed from a scroll.

The case against authenticity

  1. It is in no surviving text of either prophet. The logion appears nowhere in the Masoretic Jeremiah or Isaiah, nowhere in the Septuagint of either, and in no other version — the editors of the Ante-Nicene Fathers flag it at the point of citation as simply "wanting in our Scriptures … cited by Iren., iii. 20, under the name of Isaiah, and in iv. 22 under that of Jeremiah."5 A line genuinely struck from the canon by second-century Jewish teachers, yet still extant (Justin says) "in some copies … in the synagogues" in his own day, ought to have left some textual trace; none survives.
  2. The floating attribution tells against a fixed source. That Irenaeus assigns the same sentence to Isaiah, to Jeremiah, and to no one — and never quite identically — is hard to reconcile with his reading it off a biblical manuscript, and easy to explain if he and Justin both drew it from a circulating collection of messianic proof-texts (a testimonia book) in which such logia travelled without secure provenance. (The well-worn suggestion that a Latin copyist simply miswrote "Isaiah" for "Jeremiah" at III.20.4 rescues one of the five citations at best, and not the anonymous ones.)6
  3. Justin's "the Jews cut it out" is a formula, not a finding. In the very same breath Justin charges the Jews with excising a "from the wood" clause from Ps 96:10, and a sentence from "Esdras" about the passover; in the adjacent chapter he repeats the accusation. Tellingly, the other "excised" Jeremiah saying he lists in ch. 72 — "I was like a lamb that is brought to the slaughter … let us lay wood on His bread" — corresponds to Jeremiah 11:19, which has not dropped out of our text at all but stands in MT and LXX alike. Justin's excision charge is thus a recurring polemical topos applied to passages that demonstrably survive, which badly undercuts its evidential weight for a passage that does not.7

What can be said for it

The logion is not a late forgery: it is attested independently of Justin by Irenaeus, in Gaul, within a generation, and was evidently regarded as authoritative prophecy across the second-century church — so something was in circulation early and widely. It is possible the saying derives from a now-lost Jewish apocryphon, or is a midrashic expansion of a real prophetic kernel (Isaiah's "thy dead shall live," 26:19; Hosea 13:14; Ezek 37) crystallised into a descent oracle. On the most generous reading, the Fathers were not inventing but transmitting an inherited text they sincerely took for Scripture. But none of this revives the specific claim under assessment. "An inherited extra-canonical logion the Fathers mistook for prophecy" is precisely the apocryphal-testimonium verdict; it is not "an authentic passage the Jews excised from Jeremiah/Isaiah," which remains unevidenced.

Assessment

Weak. The interpretation that secures this passage a place in the descent proof-base — that it is genuine canonical prophecy, deliberately suppressed by Jewish scribes — does not hold on its best available case. The logion is absent from every Hebrew and Greek witness to both prophets to which it is variously ascribed; Irenaeus's own attribution wanders between Isaiah, Jeremiah and nothing, the signature of a free-floating testimonium rather than a scriptural text; and Justin's excision charge is a formulaic accusation he also lays against passages (Jer 11:19) that never left the canon. The honest description is the scholarly one: an early, widely trusted, but apocryphal descent logion — an agraphon that entered the patristic stock of messianic proof-texts and was sincerely but wrongly filed under the prophets. Two things follow, and they pull apart. As a witness to belief, the logion is valuable and authentic: it shows how vivid and how scriptural the descent to the dead already felt by AD 160. As a proof from prophecy, it fails, and its loss costs the descent-to-the-dead crux nothing it cannot supply elsewhere — that doctrine never rested on this verse, but on Acts 2:27/31, the broad patristic descensus, and the (themselves debated) Eph 4:9 and Ps 16:10. The right verdict is therefore not that the Fathers lied, but that they trusted a text that was not Scripture; the descent stands, the "excision" does not.
