The two-compartment Hades (Tartarus vs. Paradise)

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — the structured intermediate Hades is the early-patristic and Eastern mainstream and fits a plain reading of Luke 16, but reading that parable as cosmography is disputed and the specific "chasm between Tartarus and Paradise" welds the human gulf of Luke 16 to the angelic Tartarus of 2 Peter (the Reformed denial of any third place weighs on the proponent's consistency, not the reading's truth) · advanced by Doug Wilson

The claim

There was a chasm between Tartarus on the one side, and Paradise on the other. … The lowest pit of Hades there was called Tartarus, and that is where the angels were locked up—the ones that Jesus preached to.

— Doug Wilson, The Nephilim, Hades, and Other Oddments, dougwils.com. The asserted reading: the underworld (Hades / Sheol) is a single structured place divided by a chasm into Tartarus — the lowest pit, the prison of the rebel angels (2 Pet 2:4) — and Paradise / Abraham's Bosom — the rest of the righteous dead (Luke 16:22–26), where the penitent thief joined Christ "that day" (Luke 23:43).

Who advances it

In its modern popular form the structured Hades is pressed by Doug Wilson (and, in overlapping ways, by Fr. Stephen De Young's Second-Temple recovery on the Nephilim side). But the reading is not a modern novelty: it is the ancient and Eastern mainstream, with roots in Second Temple Judaism. Wilson is reviving an older cosmology — one his own Reformed confession had deliberately set aside (see below).

The evidence for

The case for a structured intermediate Hades is genuinely strong, and older than any modern proponent:1

  1. Luke 16:19–31 depicts exactly two conscious post-mortem states — "Abraham's bosom" and a place of "torments" — within the realm of the dead ("in hell [Hades] he lift up his eyes"), separated by "a great gulf fixed" that none can cross. On its face this is a divided underworld.
  2. 2 Peter 2:4 places the sinning angels in Tartarus (tartarōsas), "chains of darkness, to be reserved unto judgment" — a distinct holding-place for the rebel powers, named with a word the New Testament shares with Greek and Second Temple cosmology.
  3. 1 Enoch 22 already maps a structured Sheol — the dead gathered into "hollow places" and separated, the righteous by "the bright spring of water," the sinners apart, "till the day of their judgement" — the Second Temple matrix the New Testament writers inherited.
  4. Patristic consensus. Tertullian (De Anima 55) makes Hades a subterranean region holding all souls with Abraham's bosom as an interim refreshment; Hippolytus (Against Plato) describes one Hades in which the righteous are led "toward the right … Abraham's bosom" and the unrighteous "toward the left," with "a deep and vast abyss" between. A divided, conscious intermediate Hades was close to the universal early reading and remains the Eastern view.

Adversarial assessment

What holds. That the dead are conscious, that two destinies are already separated, and that Scripture pictures a structured realm of the dead — all of this is well-grounded in Luke 16, in 2 Peter 2:4's Tartarus, in the 1 Enoch background, and in the early Fathers. As a description of the pre-Christ arrangement of the righteous dead, the structured Hades is close to the patristic-medieval consensus.

What is overstated or contestable.

  1. Genre. Luke 16:19–31 is a parable — but the blunt form of this objection ("it is only a parable, so it settles nothing about the afterlife") is weaker than it looks, and the structured reading largely answers it. Jesus' parables draw their force from furniture the hearers already take to be real (a king, a vineyard, a wedding feast), and the punchline here — an irreversibility sealed by "a great gulf fixed" — works only if the separation it pictures is real; the parable plainly presupposes the received Second-Temple map of the dead (conscious, divided into two destinies; cf. 1 Enoch 22) rather than inventing it. The uniquely named Lazarus was read from antiquity as a signal that a real case, not a bare fiction, is in view: Tertullian asks "why the name of Lazarus in this narrative, if the circumstance is not in (the category of) a real occurrence?" and treats the scene as fixed cosmographical fact — Hades "is not in any case opened for (the escape of) any soul," as the Lord "firmly established … in the person of Abraham." He does not merely assert the real-occurrence reading; he builds doctrine on it, drawing the two-compartment structure of Hades straight from the parable's two figures — "All souls … are shut up within Hades … there are already experienced there punishments and consolations; and there you have a poor man and a rich" (De Anima 58) — and insists that even if the scene "is to be regarded as imaginary, it will still be a testimony to truth and reality" (De Anima 7). On his reading the parable's furniture witnesses to a real structure of the dead either way, which is precisely the genre re-weigh: a divided, conscious Hades does not stand or fall on whether Luke 16 is classed as parable.2 So the genre point does not defeat a structured, conscious intermediate state. What the parable will not on its own license is the further architectural detail Wilson loads onto it — "the lowest pit," the coordinates of Tartarus relative to Paradise, the welding of the angelic Tartarus to the human gulf — which the texts must be harmonized to yield (point 3 below), not the parable's own furniture.
  2. The present tense. One might object that a currently two-compartment Hades is undercut by the very descent the structured reading invokes: Abraham's bosom / the limbus patrum is a pre-Christ holding-place, and at the descent Christ "delivered the holy Fathers from thence" (Aquinas, ST III q. 52 a. 5; cf. "led captivity captive," Eph 4:8), after which the righteous go to him directly — "to day shalt thou be with me in paradise," "present with the Lord." But this empties only one room. The place of torment (Luke 16's rich man — the wicked human dead awaiting resurrection) and Tartarus (the angels "reserved unto judgment," 2 Pet 2:4 — an ongoing reservation) both persist, so a structured, multi-compartment Hades survives the descent; only the righteous compartment's tenancy changed. And Wilson's own wording is already past tense — "there was a chasm between Tartarus on the one side, and Paradise on the other" — reading most naturally as a snapshot of the cosmography at the descent (every room occupied, Christ present), not a claim about the present hour; elsewhere he affirms the emptying outright ("the part of Hades that contained the saints of God has been emptied out"), which places him inside the descent tradition, not against it. So the present tense is best taken as a flag on the proponent's wording — an untempered present where the descent tradition would qualify it — not as a defeater of the structured interpretation.3
  3. A fused synthesis. "A chasm between Tartarus and Paradise" welds together two texts that do not themselves connect: Luke 16's gulf runs between the righteous dead and the wicked dead, whereas 2 Peter 2:4's Tartarus holds the angels. Scripture nowhere places the angelic Tartarus and the human Paradise on opposite banks of one chasm; that equation is an interpretive harmonization, not a stated datum. What can be said in mitigation — and it narrows the charge — is that the co-location of an angelic prison with the compartmented human dead is not Wilson's invention but inherited Second-Temple furniture. 1 Enoch's own underworld tour runs without a break from the rebel host to the dead: Enoch is shown the seven transgressing stars bound in fire — "this place is the prison of the angels, and here they will be imprisoned for ever" (ch. 21) — and "thence I went to another place" (ch. 22), the deep "hollow places" where "the spirits of the souls of the dead" assemble and are "separated," the righteous marked off by "the bright spring of water." Angelic prison and divided human dead thus already sit within one connected cosmography in the Jewish source the New Testament writers drew on, so the bare fact that the two holding-places adjoin is not a Greek import read back into the text. And the Fathers received exactly this layered, vertical geography as a Christian picture, not merely a Greek one: Tertullian maps Hades as a stratified subterranean realm — "a vast deep space in the interior of the earth," whose hidden recess for the faithful is "superimposed on the abysmal depths which lie still lower down" (De Anima 55) — and Augustine, reading 2 Peter 2:4, has the rebel angels "dragged … down by infernal ropes to Tartarus … kept in custody for judgment" beneath, while Abraham's bosom is a "hidden resting-place" above with a gulf fixed between (City of God 21.23). One realm, a deeper pit below, the righteous region above: the integrated vertical relation — Hades the general realm, its abysmal floor the prison of the rebel powers — is patristic furniture, not a Greek graft. What stays Wilson's own welding is narrower than the flat "fusion" label implies: not the adjacency of an angelic prison to the human dead, but the equation of Luke 16's specific human gulf with 2 Peter's specific Tartarus — the two named proof-texts do not cross-reference each other, even where the wider geography each presupposes is one and the same.4

The Reformed denial — a confessional choice, not a strike against the reading

Wilson's own confessional tradition rejects a structured intermediate Hades outright. Calvin calls the limbus patrum "nothing but a fable" (Institutes II.16.9); the Westminster Confession (XXXII) recognizes heaven and hell only — "besides these two places … the Scripture acknowledgeth none" — reading "paradise" and "Abraham's bosom" as heaven itself and the descent as Christ's victory rather than the emptying of a holding-pen.5 But this does not weigh against the interpretation the way the three cautions above do. By the rule this page adjudicates under — rate the reading, not the proponent's tradition — a late-17th-century confessional choice is evidence about one tradition's settled position, not evidence about the reading's truth; and the reading the confession set aside is the older and long-dominant one (the patristic consensus surveyed above). The flattening also carries its own exegetical cost: to admit no third place, Westminster must construe "Abraham's bosom" and "paradise" as the final heaven, though Luke 16's rich man is explicitly "in hell [Hades] he lift up his eyes," conscious and before the resurrection, with Abraham and Lazarus visible across the gulf within that same realm of the dead — exactly the structured intermediate Hades the confession denies. So the Reformed denial belongs on the proponent-consistency ledger — Wilson reviving a cosmology his own confession had deliberately abandoned, a tension flagged on his detail page — not among the exegetical reasons the interpretation is contested.

The Hellenization objection (Wilson ↔ Perriman)

A standing objection to the structured Hades — pressed against Wilson directly — is that the whole two-compartment underworld is a Hellenistic intrusion: Greek cosmology (the Aeneid's Hades split into Tartarus for the wicked and Elysium for the righteous) dressed in Jewish names, not a biblical datum. Wilson met the charge head-on in a follow-up, Hell and Hellenism, and Andrew Perriman answered him — an exchange that sharpens, rather than settles, the argument.6

Wilson's defense. He grants the Greek pedigree of the vocabulary but denies it is disqualifying: "the label 'Hellenism' is not enough to condemn it," and "if Hebraic thought forms were the 'be all and end all' … then why did God switch to Greek for the New Testament?" Peter himself names Tartarus (2 Pet 2:4) without flinching; and on the parable, "there is … nothing which allows us to take these expressions as fancy metaphors for 'the grave.'" The point is fair as far as it goes — Greek vocabulary alone does not convict a reading of importing Greek metaphysics.

Perriman's rebuttal. The weight, he answers, is not on the vocabulary but on the specific equations Wilson builds from it. (i) Wilson "blithely asserts" that the Jewish name for Elysium was Abraham's bosom or Paradise — "where is the evidence for that?" (ii) "Even in 1 Enoch the 'paradise of righteousness' is on earth (32:3), not in Hades"; Jesus' "paradise" to the thief reaches for Old-Testament promise (the garden, the tree of life), not a Greek compartment of the underworld. (iii) "'Tartarus' in 2 Peter 2:4 is certainly a Hellenistic sojourner in the conceptual world of Judaism … only the fallen angels are kept here, and there is no reference to torment" — so Tartarus cannot double as the rich man's place of torment, which is precisely the fusion flagged above. (iv) When the Septuagint rendered Sheol by Hades, the translators were carrying Jewish ideas into the common tongue, not grafting Greek concepts onto the olive tree.7

Chambers of souls across Second-Temple Judaism. The structured holding-place for the dead is not peculiar to 1 Enoch: the same furniture recurs across the Jewish apocalypses written around the turn of the era, which weighs against the charge that a compartmentalized intermediate state is a Greek intrusion. 4 Ezra (2 Esdras) repeatedly houses the dead in promptuaria — "chambers," "secret places," "habitations" — and explicitly separates the two destinies: at the resurrection "the secret places shall deliver those souls that were committed unto them" (7:32), but in the interim the wicked "shall not enter into habitations, but shall wander and be in torments forthwith, ever grieving and sad" (7:80), while the righteous enjoy "the rest which, being gathered in their chambers, they now enjoy with great quietness, guarded by angels" (7:95). 2 Baruch calls the same repositories the "treasuries of souls": Baruch prays that "Sheol be sealed so that from this time forward it may not receive the dead, and … the treasuries of souls restore those which are enclosed in them" (21:23), and at the Messiah's coming "the treasuries will be opened in which is preserved the number of the souls of the righteous, and they shall come forth," while "the souls of the wicked … shall waste away the more" (30:2–4). Two further Jewish texts, then, independently picture a structured intermediate state in which souls are held in compartments and the righteous are already marked off from the wicked — the very shape Wilson reads in Luke 16, set out in a wholly Jewish idiom with no Greek borrowing. The honest qualification matches the rabbinic parallel below: 4 Ezra and 2 Baruch are both post-70 CE (c. 100 CE), roughly contemporary with the later New Testament rather than demonstrably earlier, so they witness to the breadth of the Second-Temple Jewish milieu, not to a pre-Christian pedigree (that weight rests on 1 Enoch's Book of the Watchers). What they add is multiplicity: the compartmentalized soul-store is broad Jewish furniture attested across several independent witnesses, not a Hellenistic graft.8

A native-Jewish "great gulf." The hinge — inherited Jewish furniture or imported Greek cosmology — is further lightened by a strand of rabbinic tradition that pictures the two destinies just as Luke 16 does: divided, fixed close together, and visible to one another across the gap. Reading "God also hath set the one over against the other" (Eccl 7:14), Kohelet (Ecclesiastes) Rabbah 7:14 glosses "this as well as that" as "Gehinnom and the Garden of Eden": "How much is between them? A handbreadth (tefach). Rabbi Yoḥanan said: A wall. The Rabbis say: They are adjacent, so they can peek at one another." That is the very motif of the parable's "great gulf fixed" across which Abraham and the rich man see and speak — a fixed separation between two destinies, narrow enough that each side beholds the other — set out in a wholly Jewish, non-Hellenistic idiom. The corroboration is real but must be weighed honestly: Kohelet Rabbah is a late compilation (c. 6th–8th century), redacted well after the New Testament, so it cannot show the picture is pre-Christian; the named tradents (R. Yoḥanan, 3rd c.; R. Aḥa, to whom the handbreadth view is assigned, 4th c.) are earlier than the redaction but later than Luke. It tells, then, as a parallel, not a proof — evidence that a divided afterlife with sight across a fixed gulf is at home in rabbinic Judaism on its own resources, not a motif the rabbis took over from Greece.9

A caveat on "Josephus." One Jewish-attributed witness is sometimes pressed into service here and has to be set aside honestly. A description of exactly this structured Hades — souls held in one subterranean realm, the righteous led "toward the right" to "the bosom of Abraham," the unrighteous dragged left toward the fire, and "a chaos deep and large … fixed between them" that neither side can cross — circulated for centuries under Josephus' name as the Discourse to the Greeks concerning Hades, printed among his works in Whiston's standard translation. Taken at face value it would seem to clinch the Jewish side of the hinge: a first-century Jew picturing Luke 16's chasm-divided Hades. But the attribution is spurious. The piece is the same fragment already weighed on this page under its proper author — Hippolytus's Against Plato, on the Cause of the Universe, whose "deep and vast abyss … so that neither can any of the righteous … pass it, nor any of the unrighteous dare to cross it" is that very passage in a different translation — and the Josephan ascription, carried from Photius (Bibliotheca cod. 48) into the early-modern editions, is now generally rejected; the standard catalogues file the text under Hippolytus. So it cannot be counted a second time, least of all as independent Jewish evidence: it is the Christian patristic witness already entered above ("the evidence for," point 4), not a Second-Temple one. What it does show is minor and double-edged — that a structured, chasm-divided Hades read naturally enough as a Jew's voice for it to circulate under Josephus' name — but that reception rests on a misjudgment and adds no probative weight to the question whether the cosmology is inherited Jewish or imported Greek.10

Where it leaves the argument. The objection lands hardest exactly where the structured reading is already weakest — the unevidenced Elysium = Paradise equation and the Tartarus/Paradise fusion — and barely touches the part that is strong: a divided, conscious Sheol is already in 1 Enoch 22, a Jewish text, before any Greek borrowing; the compartmentalized soul-store recurs across the turn-of-the-era Jewish apocalypses (the promptuaria / "chambers" of 4 Ezra 7, the "treasuries of souls" of 2 Baruch 21; 30); and the same divided picture — two destinies a hand's-breadth apart, each visible across the gap — recurs natively in later rabbinic tradition (above). So the debate does not overturn the structure; it relocates the question to a single hinge — is the two-compartment architecture inherited Second-Temple Jewish cosmology (1 Enoch) that the New Testament writers simply used, or imported Greek cosmology read back into the parable? That is a real and unresolved divide, which is why the assessment stays contested.

Assessment

Contested. A structured intermediate Hades — conscious, divided, awaiting resurrection — is the oldest reading, fits a plain construal of Luke 16, and carries firm early-patristic and Eastern support; as the pre-Christ condition of the righteous dead it is well-grounded. The "it is only a parable" objection does not by itself sink it: the named Lazarus and the presupposed Second-Temple map answer the charge of over-literalism (so Tertullian, who read the scene as a real occurrence), so a structured, conscious intermediate state survives the genre point. But the structure is not settled, a wording caveat attaches, and a further caveat narrows what the parable will bear. The specific "chasm between Tartarus and Paradise" fuses the human gulf of Luke 16 with the angelic Tartarus of 2 Peter, a synthesis those two texts do not make — though the charge narrows to that welding alone: the broader layered geography it presupposes (a deeper pit of the rebel powers beneath the realm of the human dead) is inherited Second-Temple and patristic furniture, the continuous itinerary of 1 Enoch 21→22 and the stratified Hades of Tertullian and Augustine, not Wilson's invention. The present-tense two-compartment claim is a subtler matter than a defeater: the descent empties only the righteous compartment, while the place of torment and the angelic Tartarus persist, so a structured Hades survives the descent and the present tense flags the proponent's untempered wording rather than the interpretation (Wilson himself affirms the emptying, and his own verb — "there was a chasm" — is already past tense). And even a parable read as true description will not on its own license the detailed cosmography ("the lowest pit," the coordinates) Wilson builds — that rests on the harmonization, not the parable. The interpretation is strong by one discipline's lights (early-patristic reception, the Second Temple background, the surface of Luke 16) and weak by another's (the limits of what a parable's furniture will license, and the unresolved question whether the two-compartment architecture is inherited Second-Temple cosmology or a Greek import) — a genuine, long-standing divide with no clear winner. The Reformed-confessional denial of any third place does not enter that ledger as a strike against the reading: by the rule that an argument is rated on the interpretation rather than the proponent's tradition, it is a later tradition's choice, weighed against the proponent's own consistency (Wilson reviving what his confession had set aside) and itself exegetically costly (it must read Luke 16's Hades scene as the final heaven). The point to flag on the proponent's page is not the structured Hades as such but the fusion ("a chasm between Tartarus and Paradise") and the unqualified present tense, which a structured-Hades proponent in the descent tradition would himself temper.
