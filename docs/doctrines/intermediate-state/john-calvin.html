John Calvin on the Intermediate State

Dates: c. 1542 · Wikipedia
Summary: John Calvin (1509–1564), the Geneva reformer, makes two moves on the intermediate state that pull in opposite directions from the medieval map. In his early treatise Psychopannychia (written 1534, published 1542) he refutes the "soul-sleep" of some Anabaptists: the soul is a substance that, after the body dies, still lives — conscious, sensing, understanding — the believing dead resting with Christ and the wicked already suffering. But Calvin equally rejects the scholastic geography of the intermediate state: the limbus patrum he calls a "fable," and the speculative mapping of the soul's "abode" he treats as idle curiosity. The result is a stripped-down doctrine — conscious survival with Christ at death, but no limbo and no purgatory.

Claims & evidence

The soul survives death, conscious and sensing — it does not sleep

the soul, after the death of the body, still survives, endued with sense and intellect

— Psychopannychia, monergism.com. This is the thesis the whole treatise defends against the Anabaptist "sleepers." Calvin maintains the soul is "a substance, and after the death of the body truly lives, being endued both with sense and understanding" — so the title Psychopannychia (the all-night "wake" of the soul) marks his denial that the soul lies dormant between death and resurrection.

The structured intermediate abode is idle curiosity

it is foolish and rash to inquire into hidden things, farther than God permits us to know

— Institutes of the Christian Religion, III.25.6, ccel.org. While Calvin insists the soul lives and is held in joy or torment after death, he refuses to map its "abode" in detail, warning that to press beyond what Scripture reveals about the intermediate condition is presumption rather than piety. The believing dead are with Christ; the precise topography is not ours to chart.

The limbus patrum is a fable

Though this fable has the countenance of great authors, and is now also seriously defended by many as truth, it is nothing but a fable.

— Institutes of the Christian Religion, II.16.9 ("Absurd exposition concerning the Limbus Patrum. This fable refuted"), ccel.org. Calvin grants that the descent into hell "was of no little importance to the accomplishment of redemption," but rejects the reading that makes it a journey to a "subterraneous cavern" where the Fathers' souls were shut up awaiting release. The medieval limbus patrum — the very compartment Aquinas built into his scheme — he discards as having no scriptural footing, however weighty the authorities behind it.

Notes

  • Calvin's two emphases must be held together: against the Anabaptists, the soul does not sleep but consciously lives with Christ; against Rome, there is no limbus patrum and no purgatory to house or cleanse it. He keeps the patristic conviction of conscious survival while cutting away the scholastic apparatus that Aquinas had erected around it.
  • Psychopannychia was one of Calvin's earliest works (composed 1534; published 1542). The position it defends — conscious survival, no soul-sleep, no compartments — is carried forward and made confessional by the Westminster Confession a century later.
  • Calvin's reinterpretation of "He descended into hell" (Institutes II.16) as Christ's spiritual bearing of divine judgment, rather than a literal trip to the limbus patrum, is the counterpart of his rejection of the compartmental geography: with no abode of the Fathers to visit, the article must mean something other than a descent to release them.
