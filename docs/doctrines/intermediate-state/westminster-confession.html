The Westminster Confession on the Intermediate State

Dates: 1646 · Wikipedia · back to timeline
Summary: The Westminster Confession of Faith (completed 1646 by the Westminster Assembly) is the mature confessional statement of Reformed teaching on the soul after death. Chapter XXXII, "Of the State of Men after Death, and of the Resurrection of the Dead," makes three claims in one paragraph: the soul neither dies nor sleeps but at death immediately returns to God; the righteous are received into the highest heavens and the wicked are cast into hell; and — pointedly — "besides these two places … the Scripture acknowledgeth none." This is the settled Reformed denial of any intermediate compartment: no Abraham's bosom as a waiting-room third place, no limbo, no purgatory. There are two destinies only, entered at once.

Claims & evidence

The soul neither dies nor sleeps; it returns to God immediately

The bodies of men, after death, return to dust, and see corruption; but their souls (which neither die nor sleep), having an immortal subsistence, immediately return to God who gave them.

— Westminster Confession of Faith, XXXII.1, creeds.net. Against soul-sleep on the one hand and any delay on the other, the Confession affirms that the soul, immortal in subsistence, passes to God the moment the body dies — carrying forward the conscious-survival doctrine that Calvin had defended in Psychopannychia.

The righteous to the highest heavens, the wicked to hell — at once

The souls of the righteous, being then made perfect in holiness, are received into the highest heavens, where they behold the face of God in light and glory, waiting for the full redemption of their bodies; and the souls of the wicked are cast into hell, where they remain in torments and utter darkness, reserved to the judgment of the great day.

— Westminster Confession of Faith, XXXII.1, creeds.net. The righteous behold God's face now, while still "waiting for the full redemption of their bodies" at the resurrection — so the intermediate state is genuinely intermediate, yet already glorious. There is no purgatorial cleansing first: the righteous are "then made perfect in holiness" at death itself.

Besides these two places, Scripture acknowledges none

Besides these two places for souls separated from their bodies, the Scripture acknowledgeth none.

— Westminster Confession of Faith, XXXII.1, creeds.net. This single clause is the Reformed rejection of the whole medieval geography in one stroke: no limbus patrum, no limbo of children, no purgatory, no third "Abraham's bosom" holding-place — only heaven and hell, entered immediately at death.

Notes

  • The Confession completes the Reformed trajectory: it keeps the patristic and Calvinian insistence that the soul is conscious after death (it "neither die[s] nor sleep[s]"), and it formalises Calvin's rejection of the scholastic compartments into a flat denial — "besides these two places … the Scripture acknowledgeth none."
  • This is the direct confessional counter to Aquinas's five-abode map: where Aquinas distinguished heaven, hell, purgatory, the limbus patrum, and the limbo of children, Westminster recognises two places only.
  • The clause "waiting for the full redemption of their bodies" preserves the intermediate state as a real but not final condition — the blessed already behold God, yet the resurrection of the body is still to come, the subject of the chapter's remaining paragraphs.
