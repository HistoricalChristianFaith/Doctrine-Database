Aphrahat the Persian Sage on the Intermediate State

Dates: c. 345 · Wikipedia · back to timeline
Summary: Aphrahat (c. 270–345), the earliest Syriac Father, teaches a genuine sleep of the dead: from death until the resurrection the dead "sleep this slumber without knowing good from evil," so that "as yet no one has received his reward" — neither the righteous their kingdom nor the wicked their torment. This is the clearest early positive witness to psychopannychism (soul-sleep). He qualifies it, in the Syriac manner, by a distinct heavenly "spirit" that returns to the Lord and keeps watch for the resurrection of its body.

Claims & evidence

Death is a sleep — Scripture's own word for it

For such death is a sleep, as David said, I lay down and slept, and awoke. Again Isaiah said, They that sleep in the dust shall awake. And our Lord said concerning the daughter of the chief of the synagogue, The damsel is not dead, but sleeping a slumber. And concerning Lazarus, He said to His disciples:—Our friend Lazarus has fallen asleep; but I go to waken him. And the Apostle said:—We shall all sleep, but we shall not all be changed. And again he said:—Concerning those that sleep, be ye not grieved.

— Demonstration VIII, Of the Resurrection of the Dead, §18, historicalchristian.faith. Aphrahat builds the doctrine on the Bible's pervasive "sleep" language for the dead (Ps 13:3; Isa 26:19; Mark 5:39; John 11:11; 1 Cor 15:51; 1 Thess 4:13) and reads it not as a euphemism but as a real description of the dead's condition.1

The sleeping dead are unconscious — "without knowing good from evil" — until the Judge comes

But our faith thus teaches, that when men fall asleep, they sleep this slumber without knowing good from evil. And the righteous look not forward to their promises, nor do the wicked look forward to their sentence of punishment, until the Judge come and separate those whose place is at His right hand from those whose place is at His left.

— Demonstration VIII, §20, historicalchristian.faith. This is the load-bearing sentence: the dead are not awake to reward or punishment but lie in an unknowing slumber until the general judgment. Just before it (§19) Aphrahat pictures the dead as a sleeping servant who dreams pleasantly or fearfully of his coming wages — but the dream is a figure for the quality of their rest, and §20 states the literal teaching: they sleep "without knowing good from evil."2

As yet no one has received his reward; even the wise virgins are "sleeping up to the present time"

From all these things, understand thou, my beloved, as it has been made certain for thee, that as yet no one has received his reward. For the righteous have not inherited the kingdom, nor have the wicked gone into torment. … And those virgins that are waiting the bridegroom are sleeping up to the present time, and are awaiting the cry when they will awake.

— Demonstration VIII, §22, historicalchristian.faith. Reward and torment are alike deferred to the resurrection: the harvesters have not been paid, the King has not yet returned, and the wise and foolish virgins alike are still asleep. The intermediate state, for Aphrahat, is precisely an interval of waiting, not a place of present recompense.3

The qualification: a heavenly "spirit" goes to the Lord until the resurrection

But recollect, my beloved … that the spirit which the righteous receive, according to its heavenly nature, goes to our Lord until the time of the Resurrection, when it shall come to put on the body in which it dwelt. And at every time it has the memory of this in the presence of God, and looks eagerly for the Resurrection of that body in which it dwelt.

— Demonstration VIII, §23, historicalchristian.faith. Pressed with Paul's "absent from the body … present with the Lord" (2 Cor 5:8), Aphrahat does not abandon soul-sleep but distinguishes: the man sleeps in the dust, while the heavenly spirit the righteous were given returns to God and waits there for the resurrection of the body it once animated. The wicked, lacking that Spirit, "are buried after the manner of animals." This characteristically Syriac move keeps the body-and-soul asleep while a divine endowment abides with God.4

Notes

  • Aphrahat is the timeline's one positive soul-sleep witness — the view that elsewhere appears only as a target: the error Justin half-condemns (souls taken to heaven at once, denying the resurrection — a different error, but adjacent), the "soul-sleep of some Anabaptists" Calvin wrote Psychopannychia against, and the "neither die nor sleep" the Westminster Confession denies.
  • His soul-sleep is not the soul-death of later conditionalists: the heavenly spirit consciously waits with God (§23). The sleep is predicated of the buried person, who knows neither good nor evil until the resurrection — a real unconsciousness, but bounded by the spirit's vigil. Whether Scripture's "sleep" language teaches this is weighed on the soul-sleep argument page (assessment: weak).
  • The whole demonstration is anti-Greek in temper: like Justin, Aphrahat denies the soul an inherent immortality and stakes everything on the bodily resurrection — "in the day of the Resurrection thy body shall arise in its entirety" (§25). Soul-sleep is the corollary of making resurrection, not a disembodied heaven, the believer's hope.
  • Date: the Demonstrations were composed 337–345; Aphrahat is the earliest named Syriac author and predates his compatriot Ephrem, in whom a similar "sleep of the dead" recurs.
