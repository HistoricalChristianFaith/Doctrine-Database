Gregory of Nyssa on the Intermediate State

Dates: c. 395 · Wikipedia · back to timeline
Summary: Gregory of Nyssa (c. 335 – c. 395), Cappadocian bishop and brother of Basil the Great and Macrina, sets out his teaching on the soul after death chiefly in the dialogue On the Soul and the Resurrection, a deathbed conversation with his dying sister Macrina ("the Teacher"). He holds that the soul is "simple and uncompounded" and therefore survives the dissolution of the body, persisting as a conscious, intelligent essence that retains memory and recognizes its own — it does not lapse into sleep or non-existence while it awaits the resurrection. Reading the parable of Lazarus and the rich man, he treats "Abraham's bosom" not as a physical place but as a symbol of the good state of the soul at rest, the harbour toward which the virtuous sail when they depart this life.

Claims & evidence

"Abraham's bosom" is a symbol of the soul's good state at rest

"It seems to me that Scripture uses the 'bosom of Abraham,' in which the patient sufferer finds rest, as a symbol of the good state of the soul. This patriarch was the first person recorded to have chosen the hope of things to come in preference to the enjoyment of the moment. Deprived of everything he had in the beginning of his life, living among strangers, he searched for a future prosperity through present affliction. We use the word bosom when referring figuratively to a part of the outline of the sea. It seems to me that Scripture uses the word bosom as a symbol of the immeasurable goals toward which those who sail virtuously through life will come to when having departed from life. They anchor their souls in this good bosom as in a quiet harbor."

— On the Soul and the Resurrection, historicalchristian.faith. Commenting on Luke 16:22, Gregory reads "Abraham's bosom" figuratively: as a bay or gulf of the sea is a "bosom," so the resting place of the righteous names not a location but the soul's secure condition — the "quiet harbor" in which those who have lived virtuously anchor after death. The repose of the righteous in the intermediate state is thus a state of the soul, with Abraham the pattern of one who preferred the hope of things to come.

The soul survives the body's dissolution, conscious and recognizing its own

she remains after dissolution in those very atoms in which she first grew up, and, like a guardian placed over private property, does not abandon them when they are mingled with their kindred atoms, and by the subtle ubiquity of her intelligence makes no mistake about them

— On the Soul and the Resurrection, historicalchristian.faith. In the dialogue with the dying Macrina, Gregory defends the soul as a "simple and uncompounded" intellectual essence that does not perish when the composite body breaks up. It persists with its faculties intact — keeping watch over the scattered elements of its own body so that nothing is lost against the resurrection, and (as Macrina adds a little later) "she always remembers her own as it was when compact in bodily form, and after dissolution she never makes any mistake about it." The soul between death and resurrection is therefore awake, intelligent, and self-identical, not asleep or annihilated.

Notes

  • The framing dialogue is itself a witness to the doctrine: Gregory presents the teaching as Macrina's, delivered on her own deathbed, so that the conscious survival of the soul is argued precisely at the moment of dying.
  • Gregory's reading of "Abraham's bosom" as a state rather than a place stands beside the more spatial, two-compartment picture of John Chrysostom (c. 407): both affirm conscious rest for the righteous after death, but Gregory leans allegorical where Chrysostom leans topographical (angels carrying the soul, an impassable gulf).
  • The same dialogue takes up the Lazarus narrative again to argue that the soul, though stripped of the bodily organs of sense, still perceives and remembers in the intermediate state — Gregory's answer to the objection that a disembodied soul could not "see" or "feel" as the parable depicts.
