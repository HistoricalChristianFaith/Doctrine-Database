The New Testament on the Intermediate State

Dates: c. AD 60–90 · Wikipedia
Summary: The New Testament supplies the data the later geography is built on: a conscious post-mortem existence, two destinies separated by an impassable gulf (Luke 16), and the believer "with Christ" / "in paradise" immediately at death (Luke 23; 2 Cor 5; Phil 1). All quotations are KJV.

Claims & evidence

The dead are conscious: Lazarus in Abraham's bosom, the rich man in torment

And it came to pass, that the beggar died, and was carried by the angels into Abraham's bosom: the rich man also died, and was buried; And in hell he lift up his eyes, being in torments, and seeth Abraham afar off, and Lazarus in his bosom.

— Luke 16:22–23 (KJV), historicalchristian.faith. Both men are conscious after death and before the resurrection: Lazarus comforted in "Abraham's bosom," the rich man "in hell" (Gk. Hades) "in torments." This is the locus classicus for the two-compartment intermediate state.

A great, impassable gulf is fixed between the two destinies

And beside all this, between us and you there is a great gulf fixed: so that they which would pass from hence to you cannot; neither can they pass to us, that would come from thence.

— Luke 16:26 (KJV), historicalchristian.faith. The two divisions of the dead are permanently separated by a "great gulf fixed" — the impassable barrier that the structured-Hades tradition (already in 1 Enoch 22) places between the righteous and the sinners.

The believer is with Christ in paradise the same day he dies

And Jesus said unto him, Verily I say unto thee, To day shalt thou be with me in paradise.

— Luke 23:43 (KJV), historicalchristian.faith. Christ promises the penitent thief immediate ("to day") presence with him in "paradise" — naming the blessed compartment of the intermediate state and dating entry into it to the moment of death.

To be absent from the body is to be present with the Lord

We are confident, I say, and willing rather to be absent from the body, and to be present with the Lord.

— 2 Corinthians 5:8 (KJV), historicalchristian.faith. Paul treats death (absence "from the body") as immediate presence "with the Lord," not unconsciousness or extinction — a conscious intermediate state for the believer.

To depart and be with Christ is far better

For I am in a strait betwixt two, having a desire to depart, and to be with Christ; which is far better.

— Philippians 1:23 (KJV), historicalchristian.faith. Paul expects that to "depart" (die) is at once "to be with Christ," and that this state is "far better" than continued earthly life — again a conscious, blessed condition before the resurrection.

Notes

  • The NT does not lay out a systematic map of Hades; it gives the data points — conscious survival, two separated destinies, the believer "with Christ" / "in paradise" at death — that the later patristic geography (Abraham's bosom, place of torment, the gulf, Tartarus, the descent) organizes into a system.
  • The two-compartment structure and impassable gulf of Luke 16 closely echo the divided chambers of 1 Enoch 22, the earliest detailed structured-intermediate-state text.
