Martin Luther on the Intermediate State (the Descent into Hell)

Dates: c. 1533 (the Torgau sermon) · Wikipedia · back to timeline
Summary: Martin Luther (1483–1546), the Wittenberg reformer, gave the descent into hell a reading distinct from both the medieval Catholic one he broke with and from Calvin's later one. In a celebrated Easter sermon preached in the castle at Torgau in 1533, he taught a real, victorious descent: the whole Christ — God and man — descended after his burial and destroyed hell and the devil's power, delivering believers from it. But Luther refused to speculate about the manner of it (when, how, in body or soul): he pressed Christians to "stick to the words" in simple faith and leave the mechanics for the next world. This is the reading the Lutheran Formula of Concord (1577) later made binding.

Claims & evidence

A victorious descent: Christ destroyed hell and broke the devil's power

hell has been torn apart for us through Christ, and that the devil's kingdom and power have been destroyed, which is why Christ died, was buried, and descended, so that they should no longer harm us or subdue us

— Third Sermon for Easter Day, Torgau, 1533 (tr. Paul A. Rydecki from the Weimar edition, WA 37), godwithuslc.org. For Luther the descent is not a journey to a holding-cell of waiting patriarchs (the limbus patrum he had already rejected) but an act of conquest: Christ went down to hell to wreck it. The point of the article is its benefit — that hell and the devil can no longer hold the believer.

The point is deliverance, not a tour of the underworld

He descended into hell, in order that He might also deliver us out from there who should have lain captive therein

— Third Sermon for Easter Day, Torgau, 1533, godwithuslc.org. Luther reads the descent soteriologically: Christ entered death and the grave, and then hell, precisely "that He might bring His own out from there." The accent falls on the believer's rescue rather than on the deliverance of the Old-Testament saints that the medieval scheme had foregrounded.

Stick to the words; do not pry into the manner

Whoever wishes to keep from veering off the straight path, let him only stick to the words and imagine them for himself in a simple way, as best he can.

— Third Sermon for Easter Day, Torgau, 1533, godwithuslc.org. Luther deliberately leaves the disputed questions — whether the descent happened before or after death, in body or soul, as suffering or as triumph — unanswered, treating them as beyond what the article requires. It is this self-limiting "Christian simplicity" that the Formula of Concord singles out and adopts.

Notes

  • Luther's descent is a third Reformation-era reading, distinct from the two the timeline already carries. Against the Catholic reading (Christ descends to empty the limbus patrum of the OT righteous — so Aquinas) Luther makes the descent a victory over hell, not a prison-release; against Calvin's later reading (the "descent" is Christ's soul bearing the torments of the damned during the hours on the cross — Institutes II.16) Luther keeps a real, bodily, post-burial descent placed between burial and resurrection.
  • The Torgau sermon was the third of Luther's Easter-Day sermons that year (1533). Its lasting importance is that the Lutheran confessional tradition treated it as the authoritative statement of the article: the Formula of Concord (Epitome and Solid Declaration, Art. IX) explicitly directs the churches to "Dr. Luther … in his sermon, delivered in the castle at Torgau in the year 1533."
  • Where this bears on the wider debate: Luther's "victorious descent" is one of the live options weighed on the descent-clause argument (should the creed's "he descended into hell" be retained, and what does it mean?) and the descent-to-the-dead argument (what the descent accomplished).
