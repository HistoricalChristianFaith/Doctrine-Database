N. T. Wright on the Intermediate State

Dates: c. 2008 · Wikipedia
Summary: N. T. Wright (b. 1948), New Testament scholar and former Bishop of Durham, is a modern reframer. In Surprised by Hope (2007) he keeps the tradition's conscious intermediate state — "paradise," a "blissful garden" of rest where the Christian dead are in "restful happiness," consciously with Christ — but insists this interim is not the Christian hope. The real hope is bodily resurrection in God's renewed creation: not merely "life after death" but "life after life after death." His target is the popular reduction of the gospel to "going to heaven when you die."

Claims & evidence

Paradise is the intermediate rest, not the final destination

Paradise is, rather, the blissful garden where God's people rest prior to the resurrection.

— Surprised by Hope (SPCK / HarperOne, 2007), p. 41, archive.org. Commenting on Luke 23:43, Wright argues that the paradise Jesus promises the thief "clearly cannot be their ultimate destination" — the next chapter's empty tomb is — but is the temporary, restful place where God's people await the resurrection. He restates this at p. 150: paradise is "not a final destination but the blissful garden, the parkland of rest and tranquility, where the dead are refreshed as they await the dawn of the new day."

The intermediate state is conscious "restful happiness" with Christ

All the Christian departed are in substantially the same state, that of restful happiness. … they are held firmly within the conscious love of God and the conscious presence of Jesus Christ while they await that day.

— Surprised by Hope, p. 172, archive.org. Against soul-sleep on one side and a structured purgatory on the other, Wright lands on a single, undifferentiated interim: the dead are conscious, at rest, and "with Christ" (Phil 1:23) — but not yet raised. He explicitly rejects purgatory and any further sorting of the saved, aligning the interim's shape with the Reformed denial of a third place even as he keeps its conscious rest.

The real hope is "life after life after death" — bodily resurrection

Resurrection [is] a way of talking about a new bodily life after whatever state of existence one might enter immediately upon death. It [is], in other words, life after life after death.

— Surprised by Hope, p. 151, archive.org. This is Wright's signature move: "life after death" (the conscious interim) is real but penultimate; the Christian hope proper is "life after life after death" — the resurrection of the body into God's new creation. He warns that "as long as we see salvation in terms of going to heaven when we die," the Church mislocates its mission; the resurrection, not the intermediate state, is the gospel's horizon.

Notes

  • Wright sits with the tradition's overwhelming majority on conscious survival — against the soul-sleep minority — while, on the structure question, siding with the Reformed against compartments and purgatory: one interim, undifferentiated, conscious.
  • His contribution is one of emphasis rather than novelty: he relativizes the intermediate state (the long preoccupation of this timeline) by subordinating it to the bodily resurrection. In effect he reads the whole "where do souls go" debate as secondary to "what is our final hope?"
  • His reading of "paradise" as a temporary garden of rest echoes the early Fathers' Abraham's-bosom rest (Gregory of Nyssa's "good state of the soul," Bede's "rest of the blessed") — but he strips it of the descent-emptied limbus and the structured Hades, keeping only the conscious wait.
