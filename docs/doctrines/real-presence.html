The Real Presence of Christ in the Eucharist

When the Church gathers to "break bread," what is received? Real Presence is the belief that, in the Eucharist, the consecrated bread and wine are — not merely symbolize — the body and blood of Christ, so that the communicant truly partakes of the Lord himself. The conviction is among the oldest in Christian worship and, for most of the Church's history, went without serious challenge; the disputes that fill this page are mostly about how Christ is present, and only in the last five centuries about whether the presence is real at all.

The interpretations fall along a spectrum, which this timeline will trace as it grows:

  • Symbolic / memorialist — the bread and wine are signs that represent Christ's body and blood and call the Supper to mind; the presence is in the believing remembrance, not in the elements (Huldrych Zwingli, and much of the later Reformed and Baptist tradition).
  • Spiritual / "pneumatic" presence — Christ is truly received, but spiritually, by the faithful, the Holy Spirit lifting the communicant to feed on the body and blood; the elements remain bread and wine (John Calvin).
  • Sacramental union — Christ's body and blood are present "in, with, and under" the bread and wine, which remain bread and wine (the Lutheran reading; popularly, if imprecisely, called "consubstantiation").
  • The realist patristic line, defined as transubstantiation — the substance of the bread and wine is changed into the body and blood of Christ, the outward appearances ("accidents") alone remaining; the realist language of the Fathers, given a scholastic account by the medieval West and dogmatically defined by the Council of Trent (1551).

This page presents the witnesses and the views; the arguments below weigh the disputed readings of the texts on which the doctrine rests.

The Scriptural texts

The doctrine rests on four bodies of New Testament text:

  • The Bread of Life discourse — "Except ye eat the flesh of the Son of man, and drink his blood, ye have no life in you … For my flesh is meat indeed, and my blood is drink indeed" (John 6:53–58), with the qualifying word "It is the spirit that quickeneth; the flesh profiteth nothing" (John 6:63).1
  • The Words of Institution — "Take, eat; this is my body … this is my blood of the new testament" (Matthew 26:26–28; Mark 14:22–24; Luke 22:19–20, which adds "this do in remembrance of me").2
  • Paul's "communion of the body and blood" — "The cup of blessing which we bless, is it not the communion of the blood of Christ?" (1 Corinthians 10:16) — and his warning that to eat or drink "unworthily" is to be "guilty of the body and blood of the Lord," failing to discern "the Lord's body" (1 Corinthians 11:23–29).3

Whether this language is meant literally or figuratively is the central interpretive question; it is weighed on its own page in the Arguments below.

Timeline

Ignatius of Antioch (c. 108)

Writing on his way to martyrdom, Ignatius is the earliest witness after the apostles. He marks out the Docetists precisely by their refusal of the Eucharist: they "abstain from the Eucharist and from prayer, because they confess not the Eucharist to be the flesh of our Saviour Jesus Christ, which suffered for our sins, and which the Father, of His goodness, raised up again" (Smyrnaeans 7).4 Longing for martyrdom, he writes that he desires "the bread of God, the heavenly bread, the bread of life, which is the flesh of Jesus Christ … and … the drink of God, namely His blood" (Romans 7).5 And he calls the one bread the Church breaks together "the medicine of immortality, and the antidote which prevents us from dying" (Ephesians 20).6

Arguments

The cross-cutting disputes adduced for and against the doctrine, each weighed on its own page.

Is the Real Presence taught by the New Testament, or is the language symbolic? (assessment: contested)

The literal-vs-figurative reading of John 6 and the Words of Institution — the hinge of the whole doctrine. The realist reading presses the escalating, scandalizing concreteness of John 6:53–58 and Paul's "guilty of the body and blood"; the symbolic reading presses Jesus' habitual figurative idiom ("I am the door," "the true vine"), the qualifier "the flesh profiteth nothing" (John 6:63), and "this do in remembrance of me." Genuinely disputed, and strong or weak by the lights of different disciplines.7
