The Real Presence of Christ in the Eucharist

When the Church gathers to "break bread," what is received? Real Presence is the belief that, in the Eucharist, the consecrated bread and wine are — not merely symbolize — the body and blood of Christ, so that the communicant truly partakes of the Lord himself. The conviction is among the oldest in Christian worship and, for most of the Church's history, went without serious challenge; the disputes that fill this page are mostly about how Christ is present, and only in the last five centuries about whether the presence is real at all.

The interpretations fall along a spectrum, which this timeline will trace as it grows:

  • Symbolic / memorialist — the bread and wine are signs that represent Christ's body and blood and call the Supper to mind; the presence is in the believing remembrance, not in the elements (Huldrych Zwingli, and much of the later Reformed and Baptist tradition).
  • Spiritual / "pneumatic" presence — Christ is truly received, but spiritually, by the faithful, the Holy Spirit lifting the communicant to feed on the body and blood; the elements remain bread and wine (John Calvin).
  • Sacramental union — Christ's body and blood are present "in, with, and under" the bread and wine, which remain bread and wine (the Lutheran reading; popularly, if imprecisely, called "consubstantiation").
  • The realist patristic line, defined as transubstantiation — the substance of the bread and wine is changed into the body and blood of Christ, the outward appearances ("accidents") alone remaining; the realist language of the Fathers, given a scholastic account by the medieval West and dogmatically defined by the Council of Trent (1551).

This page presents the witnesses and the views; the arguments below weigh the disputed readings of the texts on which the doctrine rests.

Related doctrines: The Eucharist and the ministry — see also the ministerial priesthood

The Scriptural texts

The doctrine rests on four bodies of New Testament text:

  • The Bread of Life discourse — "Except ye eat the flesh of the Son of man, and drink his blood, ye have no life in you … For my flesh is meat indeed, and my blood is drink indeed" (John 6:53–58), with the qualifying word "It is the spirit that quickeneth; the flesh profiteth nothing" (John 6:63).1
  • The Words of Institution — "Take, eat; this is my body … this is my blood of the new testament" (Matthew 26:26–28; Mark 14:22–24; Luke 22:19–20, which adds "this do in remembrance of me").2
  • Paul's "communion of the body and blood" — "The cup of blessing which we bless, is it not the communion of the blood of Christ?" (1 Corinthians 10:16) — and his warning that to eat or drink "unworthily" is to be "guilty of the body and blood of the Lord," failing to discern "the Lord's body" (1 Corinthians 11:23–29).3

Whether this language is meant literally or figuratively is the central interpretive question; it is weighed on its own page in the Arguments below.

Timeline

The witnesses are listed earliest first. This timeline is in its early stages and will be filled out as the doctrine's development across the centuries is traced.

Ignatius of Antioch (c. 108)

Writing on his way to martyrdom, Ignatius is the earliest witness after the apostles. He marks out the Docetists precisely by their refusal of the Eucharist: they "abstain from the Eucharist and from prayer, because they confess not the Eucharist to be the flesh of our Saviour Jesus Christ, which suffered for our sins, and which the Father, of His goodness, raised up again" (Smyrnaeans 7).4 Longing for martyrdom, he writes that he desires "the bread of God, the heavenly bread, the bread of life, which is the flesh of Jesus Christ … and … the drink of God, namely His blood" (Romans 7).5 And he calls the one bread the Church breaks together "the medicine of immortality, and the antidote which prevents us from dying" (Ephesians 20).6

Justin Martyr (c. 155)

Defending Christian worship to the emperor, Justin gives the earliest surviving explicit statement that the consecrated elements are not ordinary food. Of the food "called among us the Eucharist" he writes that "not as common bread and common drink do we receive these; but in like manner as Jesus Christ our Saviour, having been made flesh by the Word of God, had both flesh and blood for our salvation, so likewise… the food which is blessed by the prayer of His word… is the flesh and blood of that Jesus who was made flesh" (First Apology 66).7 The Incarnation is his warrant: as the Word truly took flesh, the food consecrated by his word truly is that flesh and blood. In the Dialogue with Trypho he calls "the Eucharist of the bread and the cup" the "pure sacrifice" foretold by Malachi, offered "in every place" among the Gentiles (Dialogue 41, 117)8 — the sacrificial thread weighed on the Malachi 1:11 "pure offering" argument on the ministerial-priesthood side of this cluster.

Irenaeus of Lyons (c. 180–189)

Against the Gnostics, who scorned the flesh, Irenaeus makes the Eucharist an argument for the goodness and resurrection of the body — and in doing so becomes the first father to be explicit about a change in the elements. The bread "which is produced from the earth, when it receives the invocation of God, is no longer common bread, but the Eucharist, consisting of two realities, earthly and heavenly; so also our bodies, when they receive the Eucharist, are no longer corruptible, having the hope of the resurrection" (Against Heresies IV.18.5).9 When "the mingled cup and the manufactured bread receives the Word of God," he writes, "the Eucharist of the blood and the body of Christ is made," from which "the substance of our flesh is increased and supported" — so the flesh, "nourished from the body and blood of the Lord," cannot be incapable of eternal life (Against Heresies V.2.2–3).10

Clement of Alexandria (c. 198)

In The Instructor (Paedagogus), Clement treats the eucharistic cup as efficacious, not merely commemorative. "The blood of the Lord is twofold," he writes, "and to drink the blood of Jesus, is to become partaker of the Lord's immortality."11 The mingled cup of water and the Word "is called Eucharist, renowned and glorious grace; and they who by faith partake of it are sanctified both in body and soul."12 Clement writes as an allegorist — wine, water, blood, and Word are layered symbols for him — yet the efficacy he ascribes to the Eucharist (immortality, and the sanctification of the whole person, body as well as soul) is realist in force.

Tertullian (c. 211)

In North Africa, Tertullian witnesses to the church's handling of the consecrated elements. Listing the settled customs that rest on tradition rather than explicit Scripture — the baptismal renunciations, offerings for the dead, the sign of the cross — he records that the faithful receive the Eucharist "from the hand of none but the presidents" and "feel pained should any wine or bread, even though our own, be cast upon the ground" (De Corona 3). The grief at a fallen fragment is reverence owed not to ordinary food but to a holy thing — the practical corollary of a real presence, and the earliest witness here to how the elements were treated as Christ's body.13

Origen (c. 248)

Answering the pagan Celsus — who urged thank-offerings to the demons set over the things of this world — Origen sets against it the Christian's thanksgiving to the Creator: "we also eat the bread presented to us; and this bread becomes by prayer a sacred body, which sanctifies those who sincerely partake of it" (Against Celsus VIII.33).14 Origen is the boldest allegorist of the early fathers and elsewhere spiritualizes the eating of Christ's flesh; yet here the bread becomes — and becomes it by prayer, a consecratory act — "a sacred body" that sanctifies the worthy communicant: efficacy, not bare remembrance.

Cyprian of Carthage (c. 253)

The first Latin witness here, Cyprian argues the church's realism from a disciplinary quarrel: writing against the "Aquarians," who used water alone in the cup, he insists the mingled cup is the blood of Christ. Because "that was wine which He called His blood," it follows that "the blood of Christ is not offered if there be no wine in the cup, nor the Lord's sacrifice celebrated with a legitimate consecration unless our oblation and sacrifice respond to His passion" (Epistle 62.9).15 The Eucharist is for him a true sacrifice — "the Lord's passion is the sacrifice which we offer" — which the ordained priest re-presents: he "offers a true and full sacrifice… when he proceeds to offer it according to what he sees Christ Himself to have offered" (Epistle 62.14, 17).16

Cyril of Jerusalem (c. 350)

Explaining the sacraments to the newly baptized, Cyril gives the patristic realism its first sustained catechetical form. In the lecture On the Body and Blood of Christ he tells them to "consider… the Bread and the Wine not as bare elements, for they are, according to the Lord's declaration, the Body and Blood of Christ… Judge not the matter from the taste, but from faith be fully assured" (Mystagogic 4.6).17 Walking them through the liturgy, he locates the change at the invocation of the Spirit over the gifts, "that He may make the Bread the Body of Christ, and the Wine the Blood of Christ; for whatsoever the Holy Ghost has touched, is surely sanctified and changed" (Mystagogic 5.7) — an early witness to a consecratory epiclesis.18 Cyril pairs this realism with the vocabulary of "figure" and "antitype," showing that in the Greek fathers such language named the sign through which the reality is given, not a merely symbolic substitute.

Gregory of Nyssa (c. 383)

In The Great Catechism Gregory gives the Greek fathers' strongest change-of-substance statement. The Body Christ took was maintained by bread and yet "transmuted to the dignity of Godhead" by the indwelling Word; "rightly, then, do we believe that now also the bread which is consecrated by the Word of God is changed into the Body of God the Word" — and changed "at once… by means of the Word," not by being eaten (ch. 37).19 He coins a verb for it — God "transelements" (metastoicheiōsis) "the natural quality of these visible things to that immortal thing" — the sharpest patristic term for a re-constitution of what the elements are, which later Western writers read as anticipating transubstantiation.

Ambrose of Milan (c. 390)

In his mystagogical sermons Ambrose gives the Latin West its sharpest early statement of a conversion of the elements: "this is not what nature made, but what the blessing consecrated… because by blessing nature itself is changed" (On the Mysteries 9.50).20 He fixes the moment and cause of the change on the words of institution: "Before the blessing of the heavenly words another nature is spoken of, after the consecration the Body is signified… Before it is consecrated, it is bread; but when the words of Christ have been spoken, it becomes the body of Christ" (On the Mysteries 9.54; On the Sacraments 4).21 It is the seed of the medieval Western account of consecration that Trent would define as transubstantiation.

John Chrysostom (c. 392)

The Antiochene preacher Chrysostom reaches for the most concrete language of any of these witnesses. Standing the reader before the altar in his Treatise on the Priesthood, he marvels that "He who sitteth on high with the Father is at that hour held in the hands of all, and gives Himself to those who are willing to embrace and grasp Him" (3.4).22 Preaching on Paul's "communion of the blood," he identifies the contents of the cup with the blood of the cross — "this which is in the cup is that which flowed from His side" — and asks, "what is the bread? The Body of Christ" (Homilies on First Corinthians 24).23 His realism is homiletic, in the service of awe and of worthy reception, but it is unmistakably a real, not a token, presence.

Augustine of Hippo (c. 412)

No father is claimed so evenly by both sides of the later dispute. To the newly baptized Augustine says plainly that "the bread is the body of Christ, the cup is the blood of Christ," bidding them "be what you see, and receive what you are" (Sermon 272); and expounding "fall down before His footstool" he draws the sharpest realist corollary before the medievals — the flesh given to eat is the very flesh born of Mary, so that "no one eateth that flesh, unless he hath first worshipped … we sin in not worshipping" (Expositions on the Psalms 98.9).24 Yet the same Augustine makes John 6's "eat my flesh" his textbook example of a figure — a command that, taken literally, "seems to enjoin a crime … it is therefore a figure" (On Christian Doctrine 3.16) — reads the eating as incorporation into the body of Christ, the Church ("O sign of unity … let him draw nigh, believe, be incorporated, that he may be quickened"), and tells his hearers "believe, and thou hast eaten already" (Tractates on John 25.12).25 The reconciling key is his doctrine of the sacrament — a sign in which "one thing is seen, another understood," that truly bears the reality it signifies: sacramentum and res held together, which is why realist and figurative readers alike can quote him against each other.

Cyril of Alexandria (c. 440)

Against a sect who held that the consecrated Eucharist lost its power if kept and consumed on a later day, Cyril insists the presence does not lapse with time: "Christ is not altered, nor is his holy body changed, but the power of the consecration and his life-giving grace is perpetual in it" (Letter to Calosyrius). It is the realism of Irenaeus carried to its next question — not only that the bread becomes the body, but that it remains so when the assembly disperses — the premise of eucharistic reservation.26

Paschasius Radbertus (c. 831)

After a long silence in the surviving record, the Carolingian West produced the first full-length Latin treatise on the Eucharist, and with it the sharpest pre-scholastic statement of the Real Presence. The abbot of Corbie, Paschasius, presses the realist tradition to a strict identity: what is received on the altar is "the true body of Christ, which was crucified and buried," made present by the priest's consecration "with the word of Christ through the Spirit" (De Corpore et Sanguine Domini, c. 831). The eucharistic flesh, he teaches, is the very flesh born of the Virgin — the Incarnation the pattern of the daily miracle. It is the seed of the medieval Western doctrine, though without the term or the Aristotelian analysis that Trent would call transubstantiation.27

Ratramnus of Corbie (c. 843)

At the same abbey and the same king's request, Ratramnus answered his confrere with the great medieval counter-witness. The elements are Christ's body and blood in figura, "in a figure" — the sacrament of the body, distinguished from the historical body born of Mary — received spiritually by faith; and yet they are "not mere empty figures, or naked signs." His "figure" is the Augustinian sign that truly bears its reality, not a bare token; his treatise — revived in the eleventh century and later prized by the Reformers — is the standing alternative to Paschasius's identity-realism. That two monks of one house could disagree so sharply shows the ninth-century West had no settled scholastic account to appeal to.28

Berengar of Tours (c. 1070)

Master of the school of Tours, Berengar revived Ratramnus's line and denied a material change: after the consecration the bread and wine remain, and are the "sign" or "sacrament" of Christ's body — "the reality of the sign" received by faith, not the flesh physically present on the altar. Condemned at a string of synods, he was forced in 1059 to swear an oath that the faithful "crush" Christ's body "with their teeth" — a formula so grossly physical that later theology quietly retired it — and in 1079 a milder one introducing "substantial conversion." The reaction to his denial produced the very language that would harden into the defined dogma.29

The Fourth Lateran Council (1215)

The council convoked by Innocent III gave the verb its first dogmatic, conciliar use. Its opening confession of faith professes that Christ's body and blood are "truly contained in the sacrament of the altar under the forms of bread and wine, the bread being changed (transsubstantiatio) by divine power into the body, and the wine into the blood" — and, in the same sentence, reserves the confecting to "the priest who has been duly ordained." It is the point at which the medieval West made the word official, a century before Trent, binding the Real Presence and the ministerial priesthood in a single dogmatic clause.30

Thomas Aquinas (c. 1274)

Aquinas supplies the account of how that Lateran IV had named but left unexplained. In the Summa Theologiae (III, q. 75) he holds that the substance of the bread and wine does not remain; that "the whole substance of the bread is changed into the whole substance of Christ's body," a conversion so unlike any natural change that it must be "called by a name of its own, 'transubstantiation'"; and that the sensible accidents (appearances) remain. His Aristotelian exposition is the philosophical machinery Trent presupposes — and the reason the "medieval-innovation" thesis is half right about the philosophy and wrong about the ancient faith it formalizes.31

Huldrych Zwingli (c. 1525)

The Reformation breaks the long realist consensus, and Zwingli of Zürich is the fountainhead of the memorialist reading. The "is" of "This is my body," he held, means signifies: the bread and wine are signs that represent Christ's body and call his once-for-all sacrifice to mind — "those things are called the body and blood of Christ which are the symbols of the true body." Christ's true body ascended and sits at the Father's right hand, so it is received "by the contemplation of faith," not "masticated with our mouth and teeth." He made "the flesh profiteth nothing" (John 6:63) the linchpin of his case, and at the Marburg Colloquy (1529) he and Luther agreed on fourteen of fifteen articles but split irreconcilably on this one.32

Martin Luther (c. 1529)

Luther broke with Rome over much, but not over the reality of the presence. Against Zwingli he insisted the consecrated bread and wine are the true body and blood of Christ, present "in and under" the elements, on the plain force of the words of institution — "distinct, clear… words, which certainly are no trope." At Marburg he chalked Hoc est corpus meum on the table and refused the Swiss the hand of fellowship. His reading is sacramental union, not transubstantiation: he left the substances of bread and wine standing — "I do not argue whether the wine remains wine or not… let it be with the wine as God wills" — grounding the presence in the Word rather than in a change of substance.33

John Calvin (c. 1541)

Calvin of Geneva took the middle road between Zwingli's memorial and Luther's bodily presence. Christ is truly received in the Supper, he taught, but spiritually: the Holy Spirit lifts the believer up to feed on the body and blood of the ascended Christ in heaven, so the elements remain bread and wine yet are no bare signs but efficacious instruments — the Supper conveys its grace "not by presenting a vain or empty sign, but by there exerting an efficacy of the Spirit." Against Zwingli the sign "truly exhibits" what it signifies; against Rome and Luther there is no local, carnal presence in the elements; and of the mode he confessed he "rather feel[s] than understand[s] it." His spiritual-presence doctrine became the standard Reformed teaching.34

The Council of Trent (1551)

Answering the Reformation, Trent's thirteenth session defines the realist line as transubstantiation — "a conversion… of the whole substance of the bread into the substance of the body of Christ" — and draws out its worship corollary: because Christ "the true God" is wholly present, "all the faithful of Christ may… render in veneration the worship of latria… to this most holy sacrament," reserving it, venerating it, and bearing it in procession, on pain of anathema to any who would call such adoration idolatry (Session 13, chs. 4–6 and Canon 6). It is the dogmatic terminus of the reverence-and-reservation thread that runs from Tertullian and Cyril.35

Arguments

The cross-cutting disputes adduced for and against the doctrine, each weighed on its own page.

Related arguments — the New Testament words

Whether the Real Presence is taught by Scripture turns on how a handful of New Testament texts are read. These two arguments weigh that question — the first the whole hinge, the second the single verse most often pressed against the realist reading.

Is the Real Presence taught by the New Testament, or is the language symbolic? (assessment: contested)

The literal-vs-figurative reading of John 6 and the Words of Institution — the hinge of the whole doctrine. The realist reading presses the escalating, scandalizing concreteness of John 6:53–58 and Paul's "guilty of the body and blood"; the symbolic reading presses Jesus' habitual figurative idiom ("I am the door," "the true vine"), the qualifier "the flesh profiteth nothing" (John 6:63), and "this do in remembrance of me." Genuinely disputed, and strong or weak by the lights of different disciplines.36

Does "the flesh profiteth nothing" (John 6:63) refute the realist reading? (assessment: weak)

The verse Huldrych Zwingli made the linchpin of the memorialist case. But on the near-unanimous patristic reading — Chrysostom, Cyril of Alexandria, Augustine, Theophylact — "the flesh" names a carnal manner of understanding (or the flesh considered apart from the Spirit), not Christ's eucharistic flesh; the verse bars a crude Capernaite literalism but does not touch the reality of the presence. Weak as a refutation of the doctrine, sound only as that modest caution.37

Related arguments — the patristic witness

Where the arguments above turn on the New Testament words, these two turn on the ancient Church: was the realist reading really the mind of the Fathers, and is the later, precise doctrine continuous with them? The first tests the strongest alleged patristic counter-witness; the second tests the definition itself.

Augustine's sign and figure: was he a symbolic witness against the Real Presence? (assessment: contested)

No father is claimed so evenly by both sides. Augustine's "figure" rule, his sacramentum/res distinction, and "believe, and thou hast eaten" give the memorialist a first-rank patristic voice; yet the same Augustine says the bread is the body and that the eucharistic flesh must be worshipped. Neither pole can enlist him flatly — both strands are really there, which is itself evidence that the texts do not settle the question by grammar alone.38

Is transubstantiation a medieval innovation, or the patristic realism made precise? (assessment: contested)

The substance/accidents apparatus and the word transubstantiatio are demonstrably medieval — coined in the eleventh century, dogmatized at the Fourth Lateran Council (1215), and elaborated by Aquinas — yet the realist change of the elements they formalize is asserted by Gregory of Nyssa and Ambrose centuries earlier. The thesis is right about the philosophy and weak about the faith it formalizes; which of the two "transubstantiation" names is the whole dispute.39

Was the earliest Eucharist a psychedelic sacrament continuous with the pagan Mysteries? (assessment: weak)

The Muraresku thesis — that the Supper began not as a christological rite but as a drugged visionary wine inherited from the Eleusinian Mysteries and later suppressed by the Church. The narrow premise (that some ancient pagan rites may have used psychoactives — the ergot reading of the Eleusinian kykeon, one real ergot find in an Iberian shrine) is a live minority possibility; but the load-bearing claim it is marshaled for — that the Christian Eucharist was one such rite — has no direct archaeochemical support, and its one Christian-adjacent text (Irenaeus on the Gnostic Marcus reddening a cup) is a hostile polemic against a fringe sect, not the apostolic Supper; the thesis is rejected by specialists in both Greek mystery religion and biomolecular archaeology, and reads a pharmacology into sources that frame the rite by the body, the blood, and the Incarnation. Weak: a conjecture built well beyond the evidence, with the narrow pagan sub-claim the more defensible remainder.40
