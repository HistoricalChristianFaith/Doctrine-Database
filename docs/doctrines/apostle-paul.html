Paul

Paul is the apostle we know best and the one whose end the New Testament leaves half-told. Almost half of Acts follows him, and thirteen letters carry his name — so his origin (a Pharisee of Tarsus, a Roman citizen, “a Hebrew of Hebrews” who had persecuted the church before the risen Christ arrested him on the Damascus road), his three missionary journeys, the Jerusalem council, and his arrest and voyage under guard to Rome are documented as no other apostle’s life is. But Acts stops with Paul in his own hired house at Rome, preaching “no man forbidding him,” and says nothing of how he died — so for once the tradition that grew up around an apostle is filling not a total silence but a single, load-bearing gap: what happened after Acts 28. The oldest strand answers quickly and soberly — within a generation Clement of Rome writes that Paul, “having taught righteousness to the whole world,” reached “the extreme limit of the west” and “suffered martyrdom under the prefects” — and a broad, early, independent line of witnesses (Ignatius, Dionysius of Corinth, the Roman presbyter Gaius pointing to the “trophy” on the Ostian way, Tertullian, Eusebius, Jerome) agrees that he was executed at Rome under Nero, and beheaded, as a citizen’s death by the sword rather than the cross. Around that firm core the tradition also embellishes: the intended Spanish mission (Romans 15) becomes, in Clement, the Muratorian canon, and Jerome, an accomplished journey to the far West and a release-and-second-imprisonment behind the Pastoral epistles; and the second-century Acts of Paul supplies the famous head-to-toe portrait — “small in size, bald-headed, bandy-legged… with eyebrows meeting, rather long-nosed” — along with a romance of talking lions and self-baptizing beasts. This page sets the witnesses out earliest source first — the New Testament, Clement of Rome, the Acts of Paul, Tertullian, Eusebius, Jerome, and the modern scholarship that grades what is left — and weighs the two live questions (did he really reach Spain? and how firm is the beheading-under-Nero?) on their own argument pages below rather than settling them by assertion. This is a biographical tradition-timeline: its “witnesses” are the datable sources that report on him. For the shared ladder of sources and how far to trust each rung, see the apostles hub.

Timeline

The New Testament (c. 34–67)

No apostle is drawn in the New Testament so fully. Saul of Tarsus in Cilicia — a Roman citizen by birth, of the tribe of Benjamin, “a Hebrew of Hebrews,” a Pharisee schooled in Jerusalem “at the feet of Gamaliel,” a tent-maker by trade — first appears consenting to the stoning of Stephen and “making havock of the church.” On the road to Damascus the risen Christ struck him down and made a persecutor into “a chosen vessel” and apostle to the Gentiles. Three missionary journeys carried him across Asia Minor and Greece; the Jerusalem council (Acts 15; Galatians 2) settled that his Gentile converts need not be circumcised; and a riot at the Temple ended in his arrest, his appeal “unto Caesar” as a citizen, and a storm-wrecked voyage to Rome, where Acts leaves him under house-arrest preaching “with all confidence, no man forbidding him.” He was not one of the Twelve — he came to the risen Christ last of all, “as of one born out of due time” — yet by his own account he “laboured more abundantly than they all,” and he wrote to Rome of a further plan the book of Acts never shows fulfilled: to preach in Spain, at the western edge of the world.1

Clement of Rome (c. 96)

The first writer after the New Testament to speak of Paul’s end is Clement, bishop of Rome, writing to Corinth perhaps thirty years after the event, in the city where it happened. Setting Peter and Paul before his readers as “the greatest and most righteous pillars” martyred in “our own generation,” Clement writes that Paul, after “seven times” imprisoned, exiled, and stoned, “taught righteousness to the whole world, and came to the extreme limit of the west, and suffered martyrdom under the prefects.” The notice is short but weighty: a Roman Christian, within living memory, attests Paul’s martyrdom at Rome, and — on the natural reading of “the extreme limit of the west” from the vantage of Rome — a mission that reached Spain. Both the Spanish journey and the fact of the Roman death are weighed on the argument pages below.2

The Acts of Paul and Acts of Paul and Thecla (c. 160)

Where the sober record thins, romance fills in. The second-century Acts of Paul — whose most famous episode is the tale of Thecla, the noble virgin of Iconium who follows Paul, survives fire and beasts, and baptizes herself — is orthodox in aim but legendary in substance; Tertullian reports that its author, a presbyter in Asia, was deposed for composing it “from love of Paul.” It is prized for one thing above all: the earliest physical portrait of the apostle, “a man small in size, bald-headed, bandy-legged, well-built, with eyebrows meeting, rather long-nosed, full of grace — for sometimes he seemed like a man, and sometimes he had the countenance of an angel.” Its closing Martyrdom has Paul beheaded at Rome under Nero, milk spurting from the severed neck — the martyrdom-fact the older sources carry, dressed now in miracle. It is devotion and legend, not evidence for the life; but its portrait fixed how the West would picture Paul for a thousand years.3

Tertullian (c. 200)

Writing from Carthage, Tertullian points inquirers to the churches the apostles founded, and to Rome above all — “how happy is its church, on which apostles poured forth all their doctrine along with their blood! where Peter endures a passion like his Lord’s, where Paul wins his crown in a death like John’s.” The comparison is exact and deliberate: John here is John the Baptist, beheaded — so Tertullian, our earliest explicit witness to the manner of death, has Paul executed by the sword, the end Roman law reserved for a citizen, as against Peter’s cross. In the Scorpiace he is blunter still — “that Paul is beheaded… a birth suited to Roman citizenship” — and names Nero as the emperor who first “stained with blood the rising faith” at Rome. The tradition has now hardened into its lasting form: Paul beheaded, Peter crucified, both at Rome, both under Nero.4

Eusebius (c. 325)

Eusebius gathers the threads and adds the physical monuments. Following Acts, he notes that Paul was “sent bound to Rome” under Festus and spent two years in open custody; but he then records the tradition — behind the Pastoral epistles — that Paul was acquitted at that first trial, “sent again upon the ministry of preaching,” and only on a second visit to Rome “suffered martyrdom under Nero.” For the death itself he cites hard evidence: Paul “was beheaded in Rome itself,” and the Roman presbyter Gaius (c. 200) could already point to the apostles’ graves — “I can show the trophies of the apostles: for if you will go to the Vatican or to the Ostian way, you will find the trophies of those who laid the foundations of this church” — while Dionysius of Corinth attests that Peter and Paul “suffered martyrdom at the same time.” Quoting Origen, Eusebius sums up the mission: “Paul… preached the Gospel of Christ from Jerusalem to Illyricum, and afterwards suffered martyrdom in Rome under Nero.”5

Jerome (c. 393)

In his catalogue of Christian authors, Jerome opens the entry on Paul with a biography and closes it with the fullest early statement of the martyrdom. He dates the death precisely — “in the fourteenth year of Nero,” the same day as Peter — and names the place of burial: Paul “was beheaded at Rome for Christ’s sake and was buried in the Ostian way,” where the basilica of San Paolo fuori le Mura stands over the traditional tomb. Jerome also states the release-and-second-imprisonment openly — Paul was “dismissed by Nero, that the gospel of Christ might be preached also in the West” — and reads 2 Timothy’s “I was delivered out of the mouth of the lion” as Paul’s own word on Nero. With Jerome the tradition reaches its classic shape, and the Western cult of the two apostles’ Roman tombs is fixed.6

Modern scholarship

Critical study leaves the core of the tradition standing and trims its extensions. That Paul was executed at Rome under Nero, in the mid-60s, is among the best-attested facts of apostolic history — multiply and independently witnessed (Clement, Ignatius, Dionysius of Corinth, Gaius, Tertullian, Eusebius, Jerome) within a century and a half, with no rival account of a different death anywhere — so that surveys of the apostles’ fates grade Paul’s martyrdom, with Peter’s and James’s, at the very top of the scale. That the death was by beheading is early (Tertullian) and coheres with his Roman citizenship, though it is one step less firm than the fact of martyrdom. The Spanish mission and the release between two Roman imprisonments are genuinely disputed: they rest on real early testimony (Clement, the Muratorian canon) and make the best sense of the Pastoral epistles, but Acts ends without them and no Spanish church preserves a memory of Paul’s founding. The romance of the Acts of Paul — Thecla, the baptized lion, the milk at the block — is second-century devotional fiction, valuable as the earliest picture of Paul and as evidence of his cult, not as history. The two live questions are argued below.7

Arguments

Did Paul reach Spain? (assessment: contested)

A long-standing argument holds that Paul carried out the Spanish mission he announced in Romans 15 — released after the two years Acts records, he journeyed west to Spain, returned to the East (the travels the Pastoral epistles presuppose), and was re-arrested and executed on a second imprisonment at Rome. The case has real early support: Clement of Rome, writing at Rome, places Paul at “the extreme limit of the west” — which from the capital most naturally means Spain, the Pillars of Hercules being the world’s western edge; the Muratorian canon names “the journey of Paul … to Spain” outright; the second-century Acts of Peter open with Paul sailing west; and the release-and-second-trial scheme makes the best sense of 2 Timothy. But it runs into real resistance: Acts closes with Paul still in Roman custody and breathes no word of Spain, no Spanish church anywhere claims Paul as its founder, and the several witnesses may all merely echo Paul’s own announced plan (Romans 15) rather than record that he carried it out. Strong by one discipline’s lights, weak by another’s, with no consensus resolving it. Contested.8

Was Paul beheaded at Rome under Nero? (assessment: sound)

The tradition that Paul was put to death at Rome under Nero (c. 64–67), and beheaded by the sword as befit a Roman citizen, rests on a chain of witnesses as strong as any in apostolic history: Clement of Rome within a generation, then Ignatius, Dionysius of Corinth, Gaius’s pointing to the tomb on the Ostian way, Tertullian (the first to specify the sword), Eusebius, and Jerome — broad, early, geographically independent, and unopposed by any competing account. The fact of the Roman martyrdom under Nero is as secure as the death of any apostle; the specific manner (beheading rather than the cross) is early and fits his citizenship, a half-step less certain than the fact itself; and the picturesque details — the milk from the severed neck, the three fountains where the head bounced — are late legend that does not touch the core. Sound.9
