Charles Spurgeon on Baptist Successionism

Dates: c. 1861 (1834–1892) · Wikipedia
Summary: Charles Haddon Spurgeon, the English Particular Baptist preacher, gave the clearest nineteenth-century pulpit statement of Baptist successionism — the claim that Baptists are "the original Christians," holding "an unbroken line up to the apostles themselves," who "never came from the Church of Rome, for we were never in it." He framed it in an 1861 address welcoming the Baptist churches of London to the newly opened Metropolitan Tabernacle.

Claims & evidence

Baptists are "the original Christians" with "an unbroken line up to the apostles"

We believe that the Baptists are the original Christians. We did not commence our existence at the Reformation, we were reformers before Luther or Calvin were born; we never came from the Church of Rome, for we were never in it, but we have an unbroken line up to the apostles themselves. We have always existed from the very days of Christ, and our principles, sometimes veiled and forgotten, like a river which may travel underground for a little season, have always had honest and holy adherents.

— The New Park Street Pulpit, Vol. VII (1861), Sermon No. 376, "Public Meeting of Our London Baptist Brethren" (delivered 2 April 1861), p. 225, full text at ccel.org (also at Answers in Genesis). The setting is a welcome address, not a history lecture: Spurgeon had invited the Baptist churches of London to gather at the just-completed Metropolitan Tabernacle, telling them the building belonged not to him or his congregation especially but "to all the Baptist denomination," and that he felt as if he were "rendering up the trust deeds to the proper proprietors" — those "who hold the pure primitive ancient apostolic faith." The successionist claim is the rhetorical climax of that welcome.

Notes

  • Spurgeon is a clear proponent witness for Baptist successionism — the "Trail of Blood" / Baptist-perpetuity thesis later popularized by J. M. Carroll (1931) and tied to the Landmark movement (J. R. Graves). His statement supplies the movement's most-quoted nineteenth-century endorsement from a figure who was not himself a Landmarkist.
  • The claim is stated more strongly than Spurgeon's wider corpus bears out, and is best read as celebratory pulpit rhetoric rather than a worked-out historical thesis. Spurgeon styled himself the "last of the Puritans" and lavishly esteemed the (overwhelmingly paedobaptist) Puritan divines; he nowhere defends the documentary chain of dissenting churches — Montanists, Novatianists, Donatists, Paulicians, Waldenses, Anabaptists — that strict successionism strings together. Whether he held Baptist perpetuity as a literal genealogy or affirmed only a perpetuity of Baptist principles is debated among his readers. The overstatement is flagged here per the wiki's practice; the underlying interpretation is weighed on its own page, Is there an unbroken Baptist succession from the apostles? (assessed weak).
  • Textual note: the standard reprint reads "for a little season"; a digitized copy renders "for a little time." The passage is securely located at NPSP/MTP Vol. VII, p. 225 (sermon 376) and is verified against the full sermon text.
