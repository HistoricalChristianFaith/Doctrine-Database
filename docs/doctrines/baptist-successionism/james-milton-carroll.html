James Milton Carroll on Baptist Successionism

Dates: c. 1931 (1852–1931) · Wikipedia
Summary: James Milton Carroll, a Texas Southern Baptist minister, wrote the booklet that made Baptist successionism a household idea in twentieth-century Baptist life. The Trail of Blood — five lectures gathered into a tract, completed before Carroll's death in January 1931 and published posthumously that year — charts a supposed unbroken line of "true" churches running from Christ and the apostles, through a chain of persecuted medieval dissenting groups, to the modern Baptists, bypassing both Rome and the Reformation. With its fold-out color chart it became the single most-circulated statement of the thesis (well over two million copies), and it is the work the popular phrase "the Trail of Blood" names.

Claims & evidence

Not "apostolic succession" but an unbroken succession of churches from Christ

Carroll is careful to distinguish his thesis from Roman apostolic succession: the apostolic office ended with the apostles, but the apostolic church, he holds, has existed continuously ever since.

Baptists do not believe in Apostolic Succession. The Apostolic office ceased with the death of the Apostles. It is to His churches that He promised a continual existence from the time He organized the first one during His earthly ministry until He comes again. He promised—

"I will build my church and the gates of hell shall not prevail against it." (Matt. 16:18)

— The Trail of Blood (1931), Lecture I, subtitled "Following the Christians Down Through the Centuries or The History of Baptist Churches From the Time of Christ, Their Founder, to the Present Day," full text at archive.org (scan and catalog page at archive.org/details/TheTrailOfBlood).1

The chain of dissenting groups carrying the "true church"

The accompanying chart lines up the persecuted groups Carroll identifies as Baptists-under-other-names — nicknames, he says, given them by their enemies, the line beginning with "Christians" (Acts 11:26) and running underground through the "dark ages":

All the other names in that column were given in the same manner— Montanists, Novationists, Donatists, Paulicians, Albigenses, Waldenses, etc., and Ana-Baptists. All of these will again and again be referred to as the lectures progress.

Elsewhere he adds Paterines, Cathari, Petro-Brussians, Arnoldists, and Henricians to the roll.2 The naming, on his account, settles only late: the "Ana-Baptist" label was applied to these earlier groups and then, "Very early in the sixteenth century, even prior to the origin of the Lutheran Church, the first of all the Protestant Churches," shortened to "Baptists" — so that Baptists, having never been in Rome, are on this reading neither Catholic nor Protestant.3

Notes

  • Carroll is the headline proponent and popularizer of Baptist successionism — the "Trail of Blood" / Baptist-perpetuity thesis in its hardest, most concrete form. He stands downstream of nineteenth-century Landmarkism (J. R. Graves) and Charles Spurgeon's 1861 pulpit statement of the same idea, but where Spurgeon gave celebratory rhetoric Carroll supplied a worked-out chart and a named lineage.
  • The thesis is stated far more confidently than the documents support, and the chain is its weakest link: it strings together groups whose theology Baptists themselves repudiate. The Paulicians, Bogomils, and Albigenses (Cathari) were dualists who rejected the Old Testament, the material creation, and the ordinary sacraments — not believers'-baptism congregationalists; the Montanists were ecstatic "new-prophecy" enthusiasts; the Donatists practiced infant baptism; the medieval Waldenses did not begin as opponents of paedobaptism. Bundling these into one "Baptist" succession is exactly the move mainstream historians reject, and it is flagged here per the wiki's practice; the underlying interpretation — whether any continuous Baptist genealogy or only a recurring perpetuity of Baptist principles can be shown — is weighed on its own page, Is there an unbroken Baptist succession from the apostles? (assessed weak).
  • When the booklet's sources were chased down, they did not bear the weight Carroll put on them. Rodney A. Harrison's study of Carroll's own research collection at Southwestern Baptist Theological Seminary found the cited documents largely unverifiable or misused, and the mainstream account — that modern Baptists arose out of early-seventeenth-century English Separatism (Smyth and Helwys, c. 1609; the Particular Baptists, c. 1638) — standing unmoved.4
  • Publication note: the Baptist Sunday School Board declined to publish the work; it appeared posthumously in 1931 with an introduction by Clarence Walker (pastor, Ashland Avenue Baptist Church, Lexington, KY), and the spelling of the author's name is James Milton Carroll (the import note's "carrel" is an error).
