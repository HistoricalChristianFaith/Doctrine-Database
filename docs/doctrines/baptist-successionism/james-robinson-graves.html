J. R. Graves on Baptist Successionism

Dates: c. 1855–1880 (1820–1893) · Wikipedia
Summary: James Robinson Graves, the Southern Baptist editor of the Tennessee Baptist (Nashville, later Memphis) and founder of the Landmark movement, gave Baptist successionism its first systematic statement. From the Cotton Grove Resolutions of 1851 through his introduction to Orchard's Baptist history (1855) and his mature treatise Old Landmarkism: What Is It? (1880), he taught that Christ's kingdom has stood in "unbroken continuity" since "the days of John the Baptist," carried by a succession of true (Baptist) churches — and, correspondingly, that the Catholic and Protestant bodies are late human societies, not churches of Christ. He is the most systematic proponent of the thesis Spurgeon later voiced from the pulpit and Carroll charted in The Trail of Blood.

Claims & evidence

Christ's kingdom has had "an unbroken continuity until now"

Landmark Baptists very generally believe that for the Word of the Living God to stand, and for the veracity of Jesus Christ to vindicate itself, the kingdom which He set up "in the days of John the Baptist," has had an unbroken continuity until now.

— Old Landmarkism: What Is It? (Memphis, 1880), ch. "The Seventh Mark," p. 121, full text at archive.org (chapter transcription at reformedreader.org). For Graves this continuity is not optional but a test of Christ's own truthfulness: the kingdom "has never yet been 'broken in pieces'… never for a day 'been moved,' nor ceased from the earth," so that to deny its unbroken existence "is to impeach His veracity." The claim is thus stated as a doctrinal necessity rather than a conclusion drawn from the surviving documents.

Baptist churches alone have "preserved the doctrine of the gospel in all ages"

To establish the fact in the minds of all, who will give me an impartial hearing, that Baptist churches are the churches of Christ, and that they alone hold, and have alone ever held, and preserved the doctrine of the gospel in all ages since the ascension of Christ.

— Old Landmarkism: What Is It? (1880), Introductory, p. 25, archive.org. Graves states this as the aim of the whole book. The corollary Landmark distinctive follows from it: because the Catholic and Protestant bodies "were originated and set up by men many ages after the ascension of Christ," they are "not divine — but human institutions," and their "societies are not churches of Christ" that can validly baptize.

"A succession of Baptist churches… from the days of Paul until now"

…yet it can be shown upon the most unquestionable authorities, that there has been a succession of Baptist churches in England and Wales, from the days of Paul until now, and it is an established fact that a majority of the churches planted in America, from the year 1645-1730, were organized by Welsh Baptists, and constituted upon articles of faith, brought over with them from the mother churches.

— Graves's Introductory Essay to G. H. Orchard, A Concise History of the Baptists (American ed., Nashville, 1855), p. xxi, transcription at baptisthistoryhomepage.com. It was Graves who commissioned and prefaced the American edition of Orchard's succession-history, endorsing its claim to prove "a succession of them from the earliest ages down to the present time" — the historiography that Carroll's 1931 chart would later render as a fold-out genealogy.

Notes

  • Graves is the pivotal proponent witness for Baptist successionism: he supplied the thesis its systematic doctrinal form (the "unbroken continuity" of Christ's kingdom as a corollary of Christ's veracity) and its historiographical backbone (sponsoring Orchard's succession-history), decades before J. M. Carroll charted it in The Trail of Blood (1931). Spurgeon's more famous 1861 pulpit statement is a popular echo of the thesis Graves had already put in print; the two are often conflated, but Spurgeon was not a Landmarkist and never defended the documentary chain, whereas Graves built the movement on it.
  • Graves's argument is explicitly a priori: the succession must exist because Christ promised His kingdom would stand, so its denial "impeaches His veracity." This is its structural weakness — the chain is affirmed as a matter of doctrine and then sought in history, rather than established from the records. When those records were later tracked down (by Carroll's critics, and by the Baptist historian and former proponent James Edward McGoldrick), the documentary links proved unverifiable and the groups strung together held doctrines Baptists reject. The interpretation Graves systematized is weighed on its own page, Is there an unbroken Baptist succession from the apostles? (assessed weak).
  • Timeline note: Graves is anchored at c. 1855 (his first published successionist works — the Great Iron Wheel and the Orchard introduction), with his mature systematic statement in Old Landmarkism (1880). His successionism thus predates Spurgeon's 1861 address, and he is placed accordingly as the earliest witness on this timeline.
