Is there an unbroken Baptist succession from the apostles?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — the literal apostles-to-modern chain of Baptist churches is historically unsupported; only a far milder "Baptist principles kept recurring" residue survives, and it proves nothing like a succession · advanced by J. R. Graves (its systematic originator), Charles Spurgeon, J. M. Carroll, and (in soft form) John MacArthur

Related arguments: Baptist origins — see also Did modern Baptists originate in early-seventeenth-century English Separatism?

The claim

"We believe that the Baptists are the original Christians… we did not commence our existence at the Reformation, we were Reformers before Luther and Calvin were born; we never came from the Church of Rome, for we were never in it, but we have an unbroken line up to the apostles themselves."

— Charles Spurgeon, "Public Meeting of Our London Baptist Brethren" (1861), ccel.org. In its hardest form (J. M. Carroll's The Trail of Blood, 1931) the same thesis becomes a charted, baptism-by-baptism genealogy of "true" churches running through a roll of persecuted dissenting sects — Montanists, Novatianists, Donatists, Paulicians, Bogomils, Albigenses (Cathars), Waldenses, Anabaptists — from Christ to the modern Baptists, who are therefore (it is said) neither Catholic nor Protestant.

Who advances it

The thesis runs from the nineteenth-century Landmark movement (J. R. Graves) through Carroll's widely circulated 1931 pamphlet, and survives today in two grades. The strong grade is a literal institutional/baptismal succession of Baptist churches (Landmarkism, Carroll, and the absolutist edge of Spurgeon's pulpit rhetoric). The weak grade — represented here by John MacArthur — claims only a perpetual "remnant" of true-gospel believers outside Rome, the doctrinal connective tissue rather than a documented chain of congregations. The case against both was pressed most decisively by a Baptist historian who had himself once held the theory: James Edward McGoldrick, in Baptist Successionism: A Crucial Question in Baptist History (Metuchen, NJ: Scarecrow Press / ATLA Monograph Series 32, 1994).

The evidence for

The strongest defensible core of the thesis is the weak grade. Believers'-baptism convictions, a gathered (rather than territorial) church, and resistance to a sacral, persecuting establishment do recur across the centuries — among Novatianists and Donatists who rebaptized, among the medieval Waldenses and later Anabaptists who gathered dissenting congregations outside Rome. That these impulses keep resurfacing, and that a stream of dissent from the Catholic mainstream genuinely persisted through the Middle Ages, is not in dispute.1 McGoldrick himself grants the premise this far: free-church groups in earlier ages "sometimes promoted doctrines and practices agreeable to modern Baptists."2

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The modest claim survives: Baptist-like principles recur in many ages, and dissent from Rome was continuous. A reader who means only "the convictions modern Baptists hold were never wholly absent" is on defensible ground — but that is a claim about ideas recurring, not about an unbroken line of churches, and it is far less than successionism asserts.

What is overstated or imprecise. The strong grade — the thesis as actually named and charted — fails on two counts.

The documentary chain does not exist. When Carroll's own cited sources were tracked down in the seminary library he drew on, they proved largely unverifiable; the chart asserts links the records do not supply.3 McGoldrick, after "extensive graduate study and independent investigation," concluded that the view he had once held "has not been, and cannot be, verified… surviving primary documents render the successionist view untenable."4 His verdict on the whole roster is blunt: "when judged by standards now acknowledged as baptistic, not one of them merits recognition as a Baptist church. Baptists arose in the 17th century in Holland and England. They are Protestants, heirs of the reformers."2

The links held doctrines Baptists reject — often the very doctrines that made them "heretics." The chain is assembled from groups that contradict both Baptist distinctives and one another. The Montanists and Novatianists were schismatic Catholics, not believers'-baptism free-churches (the New Prophecy was a rigorist movement over discipline within the Church, not a doctrinal or ecclesial breakaway — see doctrine or discipline? and did Tertullian ever formally leave the church?); the Donatists practiced infant baptism; the Paulicians were anti-Trinitarian with an adoptionist Christology; the Bogomils and Cathars (Albigenses) were dualists whose theology was not recognizably Christian, let alone Baptist; and the medieval Waldenses began as a reform movement within the Catholic Church (closer, McGoldrick notes, to the Franciscans), the Reformation-era Waldenses turned Reformed, and the modern ones became Methodist. A lineage cannot be "Baptist" when its links are paedobaptist, dualist, or anti-Trinitarian — the heresies were not incidental to these groups but the reason they were branded as such.1 (The specific successionist sub-claim that the medieval Waldenses — the sect most often pressed into the chain — rejected infant baptism is weighed on its own and likewise found weak.)

The mainstream account stands unrefuted: recognizably Baptist churches — a gathered church, believers' baptism, baptism by immersion — emerged out of English Separatism in the early seventeenth century (Smyth and Helwys's General Baptists, c. 1609; the Particular Baptists of the 1630s–40s), as a wing of the Reformation, not a survival from before it.5

Assessment

Weak. In the form the doctrine is named for — a literal, unbroken succession of Baptist churches from the apostles, running around Rome and before the Reformation — the interpretation does not hold: the documentary chain is unverifiable and the groups strung together held doctrines Baptists repudiate, so a Baptist who is also a historian and a former proponent (McGoldrick) judges it untenable, with the mainstream English-Separatist account of Baptist origins left in place. What survives is the far milder observation that Baptist principles recur across the centuries and that dissent from Rome was continuous — true, and worth saying, but a claim about ideas resurfacing rather than a church genealogy, and nothing like the "unbroken line up to the apostles" the proponents assert. The proponents' absolutist framing (Spurgeon's "unbroken line," Carroll's charted chain) is flagged on their pages as celebratory overreach beyond what the evidence bears; MacArthur's "remnant" is the defensible residue, but it proves far less than successionism needs.
