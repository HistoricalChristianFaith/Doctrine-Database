John MacArthur on Baptist Successionism

Dates: c. 2018 (1939–2025) · Wikipedia
Summary: John MacArthur, the American Reformed-Baptist pastor of Grace Community Church (Sun Valley, California), gave a contemporary statement of the soft form of the succession thesis: that throughout "the years of the Roman Catholic monolith" there were "always dissenters" — Waldensians, Anabaptists, Huguenots — who "really were the remnant of those who held to the true New Testament gospel." The claim is of a continuous remnant of true believers outside Rome, not of a literal institutional chain of Baptist churches.

Claims & evidence

An ever-present "remnant" of true-gospel dissenters under Rome

Throughout the years of the Roman Catholic monolith in the Western world there were always dissenters, there were always those who upheld the true faith, whether you were talking about Waldensians or Anabaptists or Huguenots or whatever it was there were always those groups who really were the remnant of those who held to the true New Testament gospel.

— interview clip "Exposing Roman Catholicism (John MacArthur)," recorded for Larry Wessel's documentary crew at a conference, at 0:45–1:07 (YouTube, published 12 Jan 2018).1 MacArthur's wider preaching frames the same history through the lens of the "sacral society" that punished dissenters: in his 2003 series Explaining the Heresy of Catholicism he notes the Donatists and "Anabaptists and others" as those who "protested" the church-state monolith, holding that "there have always been true people of God who challenged the sacral society."2

Notes

  • MacArthur is a contemporary proponent witness for the milder ("remnant") reading of the succession thesis, not for strict Baptist successionism. He claims a perpetual remnant of true-gospel believers outside Rome — the connective tissue the harder "Trail of Blood" chain (J. M. Carroll, 1931) relies on — but he does not assert a literal, baptism-by-baptism genealogy of Baptist churches from the apostles. His interest is doctrinal (salvation by grace alone, through faith alone) rather than ecclesial-genealogical: the named groups matter to him as gospel witnesses, not as a documented institutional lineage.
  • The list shares the weakness of the harder thesis: the groups MacArthur gathers as one "remnant" were doctrinally diverse and at points mutually contradictory — the Anabaptists rejected infant baptism while the medieval Waldensians retained it, and Catholic critics note that the Waldensians of Peter Waldo began as a reform movement within the Catholic Church (accepting the sacraments and a priestly hierarchy), not as proto-Baptists. So even on the soft reading, "the remnant of those who held to the true New Testament gospel" telescopes groups that would not have recognized one another as co-religionists.3 The overstatement is flagged here per the wiki's practice; the underlying interpretation is weighed on its own page as the Arguments section is built out.
  • Provenance note: the quoted span is transcribed from the interview video itself, the firsthand source. The phrasing does not appear verbatim in MacArthur's published Grace to You sermons surveyed here (the Explaining the Heresy of Catholicism series and the Catholic-Mass sermons), which carry the cognate "sacral society / dissenters" material cited above rather than this exact sentence.
