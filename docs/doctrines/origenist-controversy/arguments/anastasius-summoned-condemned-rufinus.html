Did Pope Anastasius I formally summon Rufinus to Rome and personally condemn him as a heretic?

Type: historical argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — for the strong version (a formal papal summons and a personal anathema on the man). The record shows Anastasius condemning the translation of Origen while leaving Rufinus himself “to his own conscience” and disowning any knowledge of where he lived. A milder claim — that real hostile pressure was brought to bear, and Rufinus felt bound to answer it — holds.

The claim

At the instigation of Theophilus of Alexandria, Anastasius (pope 398–402) summoned Rufinus from Aquileia to Rome to vindicate his orthodoxy; but he excused himself from a personal attendance in a written Apologia pro fide sua.

— “Rufinus, Tyrannius,” Encyclopædia Britannica (11th ed., 1911).1 In its strongest popular form the assertion has two parts: that Pope Anastasius I (399–401) issued a formal summons ordering Rufinus to appear at Rome and answer for his orthodoxy, and that he then personally condemned Rufinus as a heretic. The question here is whether either half survives the primary record — Anastasius's own letter and Rufinus's Apology — or whether the papacy of c. 400 condemned only the book and left the man untouched.

Who advances it

The strong form is the older handbook tradition. The 1911 Britannica states it flatly — Anastasius “summoned Rufinus from Aquileia to Rome” — though even there the entry at once concedes that the pope, “while he condemned Origen … left the determination of Rufinus's orthodoxy to Rufinus's own conscience.”1 The 1913 Catholic Encyclopedia is already more guarded, recording only that Rufinus “wrote his short Apology to Pope Anastasius” in connection with the controversy, with no summons and no personal condemnation.2 Against the strong reading stands the NPNF editor W. H. Fremantle, who judged that “it is improbable that Anastasius should have summoned Rufinus, as some suppose him to have done,” and that Rufinus's letter “bears no trace of being an answer to a summons or judgment of the Pontiff.”3

The evidence for

The strong claim is not invented out of nothing. Real pressure was brought to bear at Rome, and something summons-like did happen. Jerome, praising the widow Marcella's part in the campaign, reports that she “had the volumes of the Peri Archon brought in, and pointed out the alterations” the translator had made, “till at last letters were written, and that more than once, summoning the heretics to come and defend themselves, but they did not dare to come.”4 Rufinus himself plainly felt obliged to answer — he opens his Apology to Anastasius saying that “since I hear it reported that my reputation has been attacked, I have thought it right to make my position clear to your Holiness in writing,” and pleads that “it was impossible for me to do this in person” after thirty years abroad.5 And Anastasius was unmistakably hostile to what Rufinus had done: he had “formally condemned Origenism” at the earnest request of Theophilus of Alexandria, and told John of Jerusalem that a man “who gives his encouragement to vicious acts committed by another” does not “differ at all from the guilty party.”6 On these facts a reader could easily compress the episode into “summoned and condemned.”

Adversarial assessment

What holds. That Anastasius condemned Origen and the translation, that Jerome's Roman friends got up letters demanding “the heretics” appear, and that Rufinus felt bound to send a written defense — all of this is in the sources. A modest claim built only on these — Rufinus was put under real, hostile pressure from Rome and answered it in writing — is sound.

What is overstated. Both halves of the strong claim fail.

No formal papal summons. The one contemporary description of a “summons” is Jerome's, and it is telling in its detail: the letters were the fruit of Marcella's lobbying (“she was the cause of the condemnation”), they summoned “the heretics” as a class, and they issued from a partisan campaign — not a formal juridical citation of Rufinus by name to a papal tribunal.4 Anastasius's own letter is decisive against a personal summons: he tells John of Jerusalem that Rufinus “is so completely separate from all part or lot with us, that I neither know nor wish to know either what he is doing or where he is living.”6 A pope who does not know where a man lives has not summoned him. Rufinus's Apology, correspondingly, reads as an unsolicited profession of faith prompted by rumour, not as a defendant's answer to a citation — as Fremantle noted, it “bears no trace of being an answer to a summons or judgment.”3 The jurisdictional facts point the same way: Rufinus was living at Aquileia under its bishop Chromatius, and the authority of the Roman see c. 400 was “not what it afterwards became” — a binding cross-jurisdiction summons of another bishop's guest is improbable on its face.3

No personal condemnation of the man. Anastasius drew exactly the line the strong claim erases. He condemned Origen and the translation — “far be such teaching from the catholic system of the Church of Rome” — but of the translator he said only that “Rufinus … must bring his conscience to the bar of the divine majesty. It is for him to see how he can approve himself to God,” adding, “it is for him to consider where he may obtain absolution.”6 That is pointed disapproval and a refusal of communion, but it is deliberately not a personal sentence of heresy; it hands the man over to his own conscience. Even the Britannica, which supplies the “summoned” wording, grants that the pope “left the determination of Rufinus's orthodoxy to Rufinus's own conscience.”1 Rufinus was never formally declared a heretic.

A guarded correction on identity, not the argument. Some digitized editorial notes to Rufinus's Apology confuse this pope with a later namesake and misdate his reign to 498–503; the pope in view is Anastasius I (399–401), contemporary with Jerome, not Anastasius II (496–498). The mistake does not touch the substance, but it is worth flagging because it can make the whole episode look like a later, more juridically developed papacy than the one that actually acted.

Assessment

Weak — for the strong “formal summons + personal condemnation” version. The primary record supports the milder reading on every disputed point: Anastasius condemned Origen and the translation, but left the translator to his conscience and professed not even to know where he was living; and Rufinus's Apology reads as a voluntary, rumour-prompted profession of faith, not a defendant's answer to a citation. The kernel of truth the strong claim inflates is real — a hostile Roman faction did get up letters “summoning the heretics,” and Rufinus did feel compelled to defend himself in writing — so a modest claim (Anastasius was hostile; Rufinus was pressed and answered) is sound and should be kept distinct from the overstatement. What fails is the picture of a formal papal summons obeyed by proxy and a personal anathema pronounced on Rufinus himself: the pope condemned the book, not the man.
