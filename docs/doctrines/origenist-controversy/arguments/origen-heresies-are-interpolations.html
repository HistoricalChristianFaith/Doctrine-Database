Were Origen's heterodox passages heretical interpolations rather than his own teaching?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — as the sweeping thesis Rufinus made of it, that Origen's characteristic heterodoxies (the pre-existence of souls, the subordination of the Son, the restitution of all things) are foreign matter smuggled in by later heretics into an otherwise-orthodox corpus, the claim does not hold: those doctrines are woven through Origen's authenticated Greek works, the surviving Greek shows Rufinus softened his author rather than removing intrusions, and his own preface concedes the method. The thesis is not, however, absurd or baseless: ancient texts really were interpolated, Rufinus adduced genuine cases, Origen himself complained in a surviving letter of his works being falsified, and modern textual scholarship, while rejecting the wholesale claim, has vindicated Rufinus's general reliability as a translator against the opposite excess — leaving a narrow, real kernel about a few specific passages. The thesis is Rufinus's; the rebuttal Jerome's.

Related arguments: Rufinus's translation of Origen — see also Was Rufinus's doctrinal “smoothing” of Origen a defensible translation method? · Was Rufinus's draft Peri Archon interpolated before it reached Rome?

The same “the offending passages are not authentic” move was pressed in the opposite direction on the condemnation timeline: to rescue Gregory of Nyssa from his own universalism, Germanus of Constantinople argued that the restoration passages in Nyssa were Origenist forgeries — the interpolation defence turned to keep a saint orthodox rather than to keep a condemned man’s book his own. That comparandum, and what the asymmetry does and does not show, is weighed on its own page.

The claim

To license his “restoring” translation of Origen, Rufinus appended to his Latin Apology for Origen of the martyr Pamphilus a short treatise of his own, On the Falsification of the Works of Origen (the De Adulteratione). Its thesis is that a learned and self-consistent author cannot have written flat contradictions of himself in “successive sentences”; where such contradictions appear, heretics have been at work:

Whenever they found in any of the renowned writers of old days a discussion of those things which pertain to the glory of God so full and faithful that every believer could gain profit and instruction from it, they have not scrupled to infuse into their writings the poisonous taint of their own false doctrines … either by inserting things which the writers had not said or by changing by interpolation what they had said … they meant it to appear that well-known and orthodox men had held as they did.

— Rufinus, On the Falsification of the Works of Origen, link.1 The corollary, stated in Rufinus's preface to the Peri Archon, is the translator's warrant he claimed from it: “Wherever therefore I have found in his books anything contrary to the truth concerning the Trinity which he has in other places spoken of in a strictly orthodox sense, I have either omitted it as a foreign and not genuine expression or set it down in terms agreeing with the rule of faith.”2 The question here is whether that premise is true — whether Origen's heterodox passages are interlopers' insertions, so that pruning them recovers the genuine Origen, or whether they are Origen's own, so that pruning them rewrites him.

Who advances it

The thesis is Rufinus's. He published it as a supplement to the Apology for Origen that the martyr Pamphilus and Eusebius of Caesarea had composed a century earlier — but he did not take it from there: the surviving book of that Apology answers the charges by quoting Origen at length, and nowhere argues that his text has been tampered with. What Rufinus did build on is Origen's own testimony: in the De Adulteratione Rufinus quotes a letter Origen wrote “to certain intimate friends at Alexandria” complaining that an opponent had taken the notes of a public disputation and “added or struck out whatever he chose, and changed things as he thought right, and published it abroad as if it were my work.” Origen there likens his fate to the falsification of Paul's letters (the forged second-Thessalonians alarm) and to Marcion's mutilation of the Gospels.3 On that footing Rufinus argued that what the world would not believe of a heretic — that his errors were someone else's insertions — it should believe of Origen, whose own words attested the tampering.

The evidence for

Interpolation was a real and documented phenomenon. Rufinus's treatise is, in effect, a small dossier of cases, and the cases are not fictions. He instances the Clementine Recognitions, into which “the heresy of Eunomius” had been worked so that Clement seems to argue the Son was created; the Alexandrian Clement and Dionysius of Alexandria, under whose names Arianizing sentences circulated (Athanasius having to write a defence of Dionysius for that very reason); Hilary of Poitiers, whose book was so falsified by his enemies — his secretary bribed — that he was excommunicated on the strength of words inserted into his own manuscript; and the letters of Cyprian, into which heretics at Constantinople slipped a treatise of Tertullian on the Trinity and sold the compound cheaply through the city. He closes with a forgery of “recent memory” — the erasure-and-rewrite of a book of Athanasius to discredit the phrase Homo Dominicus in a creed drafted for Pope Damasus — a document, as it happens, drafted by the young Jerome himself.1 Against a fourth-century backdrop of hand-copied texts and bitter doctrinal warfare, the general possibility Rufinus asserts — that a revered author's works could be doctored to make him vouch for a heresy — is not paranoia but a known hazard, and one Jerome elsewhere conceded in principle: the NPNF editor notes that the licence Rufinus claimed for a translator to smooth an original was the very licence Jerome had defended in his On the Best Method of Translating.4

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The narrow, defensible core of the thesis is that some ancient texts, Origen's among them, suffered corruption, and that a translator meeting a jarring line was not always wrong to suspect it. That much is sound, and modern scholarship has in fact swung back toward Rufinus on the connected question of his fidelity as a translator: where the Peri Archon survives in Greek (fragments of Books 3 and 4 in the Philocalia; quotations in Justinian's Letter to Mennas), Rufinus can be checked, and Henri Crouzel, John Behr, and others have argued that the “hermeneutic of suspicion” toward him — entrenched by Koetschau's critical edition and Butterworth's English translation, which distrusted Rufinus so completely that they spliced hostile testimonia from Origen's opponents (Jerome's Letter to Avitus, Justinian, the anathemas of 553) into the running text as though they were Origen's own words — overshot. Rufinus proves a more careful translator than that tradition allowed.5

What is overstated. But that is a verdict about translator-reliability and isolated passages, not about Rufinus's governing thesis — and the governing thesis fails on three counts.

The doctrines are integral, not intrusive. The pre-existence and fall of rational souls, the subordination of the Son to the Father, and the apokatastasis or restitution of all things are not stray sentences at odds with the rest of Origen; they are the load-bearing timbers of his cosmology, and they recur across the works whose Greek is not in doubt — the Contra Celsum, the Commentary on John, the homilies. A doctrine that pervades an author's authenticated corpus cannot be quarantined to a few lines a heretic penciled in. To excise Origen's characteristic teaching as “foreign and not genuine” is not to restore Origen but to manufacture a more orthodox one.5

The Greek control shows softening, not de-interpolation. The one test that could vindicate the thesis — comparison of Rufinus's Latin with the surviving Greek and with Jerome's rival literal fragments (in his Letter 124 to Avitus) — goes against it: at the pressure points the Latin reads as Origen's own thought toned toward orthodoxy, not as a text with a heretic's graft cut away. And Rufinus's own preface says as much: he did not merely delete suspected intrusions but “set” passages “down in terms agreeing with the rule of faith” and added clarifying words from elsewhere. Rewriting an author into orthodoxy is a confession of softening, whatever theory of interpolation is invoked to justify it.2

The thesis proves too much. Jerome's sharpest rejoinder is structural. If every offensive line in a book may be assigned to an unknown corrupter, no author can ever be convicted of anything he wrote:

If, I reply, we admit that everything in a book which is offensive is corruptly inserted by others, nothing will remain belonging to the author under whose name the book passes, but everything can be assigned to those by whom it is supposed to have been corrupted.

— Jerome, Apology against Rufinus, II.17. The rule, Jerome saw, is unfalsifiable and therefore useless as a canon of authenticity; and turned on Rufinus's own labour it yields the opposite result — “what you cut out was not due to the heretics, but to Origen himself, and… you translated the bad things he had written because you considered them good.”6 Jerome overstates in the other direction — his blanket charge that Rufinus falsified everything is itself the excess modern editors inherited and that Crouzel and Behr have had to correct — but on the central point he is right: the heresies were Origen's.

Assessment

Weak. As the general account of Origen's heterodoxy that Rufinus needed it to be — the premise that entitled him to prune and rewrite — the interpolation thesis does not hold. Origen's signature doctrines are integral to his system and present in his undisputed Greek; the surviving Greek shows Rufinus softening his author rather than removing intruders; his preface concedes the method; and Jerome's structural objection, that the thesis can absolve any author of anything, lands. Yet it earns weak rather than unsupported, because its foundations are not fictions: interpolation of ancient texts was a genuine and documented practice, Rufinus's parade of precedents is real, Origen himself left a firsthand complaint of being falsified, and the wholesale distrust of Rufinus that Jerome pioneered has itself been rolled back by modern textual scholarship, which finds him a more faithful translator than his enemies allowed and leaves room for corruption in particular passages. What survives, then, is a narrow and reasonable claim — that some specific lines may be corrupt and that Origen's hostile witnesses are not to be trusted uncritically — wrongly inflated into a sweeping licence to launder a theologian's whole system. Rufinus's overreach is flagged on his page; Jerome's counter-overreach — that Rufinus falsified everything — on his.
