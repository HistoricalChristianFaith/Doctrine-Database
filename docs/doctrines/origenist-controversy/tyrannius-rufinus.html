Rufinus of Aquileia in the Origenist Controversy

Dates: c. 344/345–411 · Wikipedia
Summary: Tyrannius Rufinus, monk, priest, and translator, was Jerome's closest friend of youth and later his bitterest opponent. His long formation in the Greek East — above all his years in Egypt under Didymus the Blind — gave him the lifelong devotion to Origen that would set the two men against each other after 393. This page covers the friendship's origins (to 386); his role in the rupture is added as the timeline advances.

Origins and formation (c. 344–386)

Birth near Aquileia and baptism there

Rufinus was born about the year 344 or 345 at Concordia, in the north-Italian territory between Aquileia and Altinum, and was baptized about his twenty-eighth year while living in the monastic community at Aquileia.1 Jerome, writing in 374 of the newly-baptized Rufinus, set his friend's purity against his own sense of sin:

He has but now washed himself and is clean, yea, is made white as snow; whilst I, stained with every sin, wait day and night with trembling to pay the uttermost farthing.

— Jerome, Letter 4 (to Florentius), §2, source.

The Aquileia ascetic circle

At Aquileia, about 370–373, Rufinus belonged to the ascetic brotherhood that also gathered Jerome, Bonosus, Hylas, and Heliodorus — the friendship's high point.2 When the company scattered, Rufinus “joined the noble Roman lady, Melania, in her pilgrimage to the East” about 372.3

Egypt: Didymus the Blind and the desert

In Egypt Rufinus studied for some six years at Alexandria — a pupil of Didymus the Blind (and later of Theophilus) — and lived among the desert hermits, drinking in the love of Origen that would mark him for life; he suffered imprisonment and banishment under Lucius, the Arian bishop of Alexandria.4

The Mount of Olives and ordination at Jerusalem

About 379–380 Melania settled on the Mount of Olives, where Rufinus gathered a community of monks that he would lead for some eighteen years; he was ordained priest at Jerusalem about 386, around the time John succeeded Cyril in that see.5 When Jerome arrived at Bethlehem, a few miles off, that same year, the old friendship was renewed — “though not apparently with all its former warmth.”6

Notes

  • The Aquileia-circle Bonosus — Jerome's boyhood friend, later a hermit on an Adriatic island — is a different man from Bonosus of Sardica, the later Helvidian bishop.
  • Rufinus's Egyptian formation under Didymus is the seed of the whole controversy: it is the source of the Origenism that Jerome, once an admirer of Origen himself, would later attack.
