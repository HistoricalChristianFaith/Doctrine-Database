Rufinus of Aquileia in the Origenist Controversy

Dates: c. 344/345–411 · Wikipedia
Summary: Tyrannius Rufinus, monk, priest, and translator, was Jerome's closest friend of youth and later his bitterest opponent. His long formation in the Greek East — above all his years in Egypt under Didymus the Blind — gave him the lifelong devotion to Origen that would set the two men against each other after 393. This page covers the friendship's origins and the first open breach — his rebuff of the heresy-hunter Atarbius, his siding with Bishop John of Jerusalem against Epiphanius, and the tense reconciliation of Easter 397 — and then the spark that reopened it all: his Latin translation of Origen's On First Principles and its preface naming Jerome as his precedent (397–399); and finally the open literary war — his Apology to Anastasius, his Apology against Jerome turning the heresy-charge back on his accuser, and the silence a bishop's plea won from him while Jerome fought on (399–403).

Origins and formation (c. 344–386)

Birth near Aquileia and baptism there

Rufinus was born about the year 344 or 345 at Concordia, in the north-Italian territory between Aquileia and Altinum, and was baptized about his twenty-eighth year while living in the monastic community at Aquileia.1 Jerome, writing in 374 of the newly-baptized Rufinus, set his friend's purity against his own sense of sin:

He has but now washed himself and is clean, yea, is made white as snow; whilst I, stained with every sin, wait day and night with trembling to pay the uttermost farthing.

— Jerome, Letter 4 (to Florentius), §2, source.

The Aquileia ascetic circle

At Aquileia, about 370–373, Rufinus belonged to the ascetic brotherhood that also gathered Jerome, Bonosus, Hylas, and Heliodorus — the friendship's high point.2 When the company scattered, Rufinus “joined the noble Roman lady, Melania, in her pilgrimage to the East” about 372.3

Egypt: Didymus the Blind and the desert

In Egypt Rufinus studied for some six years at Alexandria — a pupil of Didymus the Blind (and later of Theophilus) — and lived among the desert hermits, drinking in the love of Origen that would mark him for life; he suffered imprisonment and banishment under Lucius, the Arian bishop of Alexandria.4

The Mount of Olives and ordination at Jerusalem

About 379–380 Melania settled on the Mount of Olives, where Rufinus gathered a community of monks that he would lead for some eighteen years; he was ordained priest at Jerusalem about 386, around the time John succeeded Cyril in that see.5 When Jerome arrived at Bethlehem, a few miles off, that same year, the old friendship was renewed — “though not apparently with all its former warmth.”6

The rift opens (392–397)

Atarbius rebuffed; siding with Bishop John

About 392, when the monk Atarbius came to Jerusalem demanding that both he and Jerome subscribe a repudiation of Origen, Rufinus — unlike Jerome, who complied — refused him an interview and “treated him with merited scorn.” When Epiphanius of Salamis then preached against Origen in Bishop John's church (393–394), Rufinus stood with John, appearing, in Fremantle's account, “as the chief of a chorus of presbyters who applauded their own bishop and derided Epiphanius as a ‘silly old man.’”7

The reconciliation of Easter 397

Reconciled to Jerome at the Church of the Resurrection at Easter 397, Rufinus set out for Italy with Melania, Jerome accompanying him part of the way. The peace did not last: within a few years Rufinus's Latin translation of Origen's On First Principles — and its preface, which appealed to Jerome's own past praise of Origen — would reopen the quarrel in earnest.8

The spark: Rufinus's Latin Origen (397–399)

Italy, Pinetum, and Macarius

Late in 397 Rufinus reached Italy with Melania and was welcomed by Paulinus of Nola. Settling for a time at Pinetum, near Terracina, he was pressed by the monk Macarius, whose “persistence” was so great that “even want of ability cannot resist it,” to give Origen to Latin readers. There he translated the martyr Pamphilus's Apology for Origen and “supplemented” it “by a short treatise” of his own, On the Falsification of the Works of Origen, arguing that heretics had corrupted Origen's genuine text.9

The preface to the Peri Archon that named Jerome

At Macarius's urging Rufinus then translated Origen's Peri Archon (On First Principles), openly smoothing away what he took to be heretical interpolations. His preface to Macarius pointed — without naming him — to Jerome as the precedent who had done the same:

He has rendered into Latin more than seventy of Origen's homiletical treatises and a few also of his commentaries upon the apostle; and in these wherever the Greek text presents a stumbling block, he has smoothed it down in his version and has so emended the language used that a Latin writer can find no word that is at variance with our faith.

— Rufinus, Preface to the Translation of Origen's Peri Archon (Jerome, Letter 80), source. Rufinus was throwing back at Jerome his own past enthusiasm — the glowing preface in which Jerome had said that, “while in his other books Origen surpasses all former writers, in dealing with the Song of Songs he surpasses himself.” This public reminder of Jerome's Origenism was the spark of the open war.10

Withdrawal to Aquileia

When Jerome answered furiously from Bethlehem and his friends at Rome suppressed the private letter of peace Jerome had meant for him, Rufinus withdrew from Rome to Aquileia, where he was welcomed by its bishop, Chromatius.11

The open war (399–403)

The Apology to Anastasius (400)

When the friendly Pope Siricius died in 399 and Anastasius I — closer to Jerome's Roman circle — succeeded him, Rufinus's accusers pressed their case at Rome. Rather than travel there (“It was impossible for me to do this in person,” he wrote, newly home after thirty years abroad), Rufinus sent Anastasius a written profession of faith. He confessed the resurrection of “this very flesh in which we now live,” a coming judgment reaching even the devil, and — on the soul's origin, where he was most suspected — owned that after reading every opinion he was “up to the present moment unable to hold any of them as certain and absolute.” As for Origen he claimed only the translator's part:

I am not a defender or a champion of Origen; nor am I the first who has translated his works. Others before me had done the very same thing, and I did it, the last of many, at the request of my brethren.

— Rufinus, Apology to Anastasius, §7, source. His faith, he closed, was that “held by the Church of Rome, by that of Alexandria, and by my own church of Aquileia; and which is also preached at Jerusalem.”12

The Apology against Jerome (400/401)

From Aquileia, at the end of 400, Rufinus answered Jerome directly in two books of an Apology addressed to his patron Apronianus. He turned the heresy-charge back on his accuser: Jerome had pointed to his own Commentary on the Epistle to the Ephesians as proof that he opposed Origen — and it was exactly there, Rufinus retorted, that Jerome had taught Origen's own doctrines of pre-existent souls and universal restoration:

You made an exception in favour of the Commentaries on the Ephesians, in which you imagined that you had written more correctly. But even you must have seen … how like they are all through to Origen's views; and, indeed, how they contain something more extreme than the views of which you demand the condemnation.

— Rufinus, Apology against Jerome, II.28, source.13

Anastasius's coldness, and the silence Chromatius asked

Rome gave Rufinus no comfort. Writing to John of Jerusalem, who had interceded for him, Anastasius condemned the translation and washed his hands of the translator — “I neither know nor wish to know either what he is doing or where he is living” — leaving Rufinus, as he put it, to “consider where he may obtain absolution.” When Jerome answered his Apology with three books of invective, Bishop Chromatius of Aquileia begged both men to let the quarrel die. Rufinus heeded him and made no public reply; Jerome did not, and had the last word. The two never reconciled.14
