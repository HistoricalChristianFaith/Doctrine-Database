Jerome in the Origenist Controversy

Dates: c. 347–420 · Wikipedia
Summary: Jerome of Stridon — monk, scholar, and translator of the Vulgate — was in his youth Rufinus's dearest friend and himself an ardent student of Origen. The letters he sent from the Syrian desert in 374, aching for Rufinus to join him, are the warmest record of that friendship. This page covers those origins and the first open breach — his siding with Epiphanius against Bishop John of Jerusalem, his tract Against John, and the tense reconciliation of Easter 397.

Origins and friendship (c. 370–386)

The Aquileia circle and its breakup

At Aquileia, about 370–373, Jerome shared the ascetic life with Rufinus, Bonosus, Hylas, and Heliodorus.1 When the brotherhood was scattered and Jerome driven east, he wrote to Rufinus of the wrench with characteristic feeling:

After that sudden whirlwind dragged me from your side, severing with its impious wrench the bonds of affection in which we were knit together, “The dark blue raincloud lowered o'er my head: On all sides were the seas, on all the sky.”

— Jerome, Letter 3 (to Rufinus the Monk), §3, source. In the same letter, learning that Rufinus had reached the monks of Egypt, he wrote that he longed to see him “more than the storm-tossed mariner looks for his haven.”

The letters from Syria (374): urging Rufinus east

From Antioch and the desert of Chalcis, Jerome pressed Rufinus — then in Egypt — to come to Syria, and commended him to Florentius at Jerusalem, praising the newly-baptized Rufinus as one made “white as snow.”2 A few months later, still hoping for a reunion, he asked Florentius to have copied for him the works of Hilary of Poitiers — the treatise On Synods and the commentary on the Psalms, which Jerome had once transcribed with his own hand at Trier — and noted that Rufinus held a borrowed copy of Tertullian:

I entreat you, therefore, to ask him to allow you to have the commentaries of the reverend Rhetitius, bishop of Augustodunum, copied, in which he has so eloquently explained the Song of Songs. A countryman of the aforesaid brother Rufinus, the old man Paul, writes that Rufinus has his copy of Tertullian, and urgently requests that this may be returned.

— Jerome, Letter 5 (to Florentius), §2, source.

Bethlehem, 386: neighbours again

After his own years in the East and at Rome, Jerome settled at Bethlehem with Paula in 386, a few miles from Rufinus's community on the Mount of Olives; the old friendship was renewed — “though not apparently with all its former warmth” — for some seven years of quiet before Origen divided them.3

The rift opens (392–397)

Epiphanius, and the ordination of Jerome's brother

When Epiphanius of Salamis came to Jerusalem and preached against Origen in Bishop John's own church (393–394), Jerome took Epiphanius's side against John and Rufinus. To place the Bethlehem monastery beyond John's reach, Epiphanius uncanonically ordained Jerome's brother Paulinian a priest within John's diocese — an act that enraged the bishop and broke Jerome openly with him. Epiphanius's half-apologetic letter to John, which Jerome himself turned into Latin (his Letter 51), names the friend who would become Jerome's great opponent:

But may God free you, my brother, and the holy people of Christ which is intrusted to you, and all the brothers who are with you, and especially the presbyter Rufinus, from the heresy of Origen, and other heresies, and from the perdition to which they lead.

— Epiphanius to John, in Jerome, Letter 51, §6, source.4

Against John of Jerusalem (c. 396)

About 396 Jerome answered John's complaints to Rome with a fierce tract, To Pammachius Against John of Jerusalem, which scorned the bishop and, in a phrase he turned again and again against Rufinus, derided “your friends, who grin like dogs and turn up their noses.” The treatise was so bitter that, when the quarrel was patched up, Jerome broke it off and left it unpublished.5

The reconciliation of Easter 397

Before Rufinus left for Italy at Easter 397, the two were formally reconciled at the Church of the Resurrection, joining hands at a solemn eucharist; Jerome walked his old friend part of the way on his journey before their final parting. The peace would not hold.6

Notes
