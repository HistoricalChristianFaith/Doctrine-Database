Andrew

Andrew was Simon Peter's brother, a fisherman of Bethsaida, and — in John's Gospel — the “first-called” of the disciples, who heard John the Baptist point to Jesus and at once went to fetch his brother. Beyond that the New Testament is silent about him; everything the church came to “know” of his mission and death grew up afterward. This page is not a life of Andrew so much as a history of what was remembered and claimed about him — his field of labour, his cross, and the great see that later called him its founder — set out earliest source first, so that the spare Gospel notices, the sober but bare tradition of the early Fathers, the vivid apocryphal romance, and the late legend can each be weighed on its own date. This is a biographical tradition-timeline: its “witnesses” are the datable sources that report on Andrew, and the contested questions — was he really crucified at Patras? on an X-shaped cross? did he found the church of Byzantium? — are weighed on their own argument pages below rather than settled by assertion. For the shared ladder of sources and how far to trust each rung, see the apostles hub.

Timeline

The New Testament (c. 30–90)

The New Testament gives Andrew a clear but slight portrait. He is a fisherman of Bethsaida and the brother of Simon Peter, called with him from the nets; John's Gospel adds his one distinctive scene — he is first a disciple of John the Baptist, and when the Baptist points to “the Lamb of God” Andrew follows Jesus and then finds Simon: “We have found the Messias … And he brought him to Jesus.” Because he was called before his brother, Greek tradition later surnamed him Prōtoklētos, “the first-called.” He appears twice more in John — pointing out the boy with the loaves, and bringing word of the enquiring Greeks — and is named in all four lists of the Twelve. Of his mission or death the New Testament says nothing.1

Origen, preserved by Eusebius (c. 230)

Origen, quoted a century later by Eusebius, gives the earliest datable notice of an Andrew mission field. In the apostles' “division of the world,” “Scythia” — the coast north of the Black Sea — “was allotted to Andrew,” just as Parthia went to Thomas and Asia to John. This oldest and soberest layer knows nothing of Achaia, of Patras, or of a crucifixion; it is the seed of Andrew's later patronage of the Black Sea lands and of Russia.2

The Acts of Andrew (c. 150–210)

The apocryphal Acts of Andrew — a second-century romance, surviving in fragments and in a later Greek Acts and Martyrdom — supplies almost everything the tradition tells of Andrew's end: a mission in Achaia, a clash with the proconsul Aegeates at Patras over the worship of idols, a long dialogue on the “mystery of the cross,” and a death in which Andrew is “lifted on the cross” and bound by ropes — not nailed — so as to prolong his suffering. It is hagiographical romance of low biographical value, but it is the wellspring of the Patras–crucifixion tradition. Notably, the earliest form of the death-story says nothing of the cross's shape.3

Jerome (395)

Jerome, arguing in a letter to Marcella that the risen Christ is everywhere at once, lists the apostles by their fields — Christ was “with Thomas in India, with Peter at Rome, with Paul in Illyricum, with Titus in Crete, with Andrew in Achaia.” It is an incidental line, not a martyrdom account, but it independently attaches Andrew's ministry to Achaia, corroborating the region (if not the manner) of the Acts' Patras story.4

The apostle-lists and the Byzantine tradition (5th–10th c.)

A family of late catalogues — Pseudo-Dorotheus of Tyre and kindred apostle-lists — assigns Andrew a fuller itinerary and, above all, makes him the founder of the church of Byzantium, who ordained Stachys (the man Paul greets in Romans 16:9) as its first bishop at Argyropolis. The older layer knew only that Andrew passed through Byzantium on the way from Pontus to Achaia; the foundation of the see is a later development, spreading only from the end of the eighth century and generally accepted in the tenth. The city's Andrean cult was real (his relics were carried to the Church of the Holy Apostles in 357), and Constantinople in time made the legend a charter of its apostolic standing against Rome — but a seventh-century source names Stachys while knowing nothing of the foundation, and these lists are late, mutually dependent church memory, not history.5

The Golden Legend (c. 1260)

Jacobus de Voragine's Golden Legend gives the fullest and most colourful life — Andrew converting the proconsul's wife Maximilla, disputing with Aegeas, hanging alive on the cross and preaching to the crowds for two days. It is cited here as late medieval hagiography, more than a millennium after the events, and it is in this era of art and legend, not in any ancient source, that the diagonal saltire becomes his cross.6

Arguments

Was Andrew martyred by crucifixion at Patras? (assessment: plausible)

The Patras martyrdom is a fixed and unrivalled tradition — one city, one province, one proconsul, one manner — corroborated in its region by Jerome and graded “more plausible than not” by the best modern survey. But its whole detailed weight rests on the legendary Acts of Andrew, and an older tradition placed his labours in Scythia instead. Plausible — more likely than not, well short of the near-certainty of the best-attested apostolic deaths.7

The X-shaped “St Andrew's cross” (assessment: weak)

The saltire tradition must be kept apart from the fact of the crucifixion. The earliest death-narratives leave the cross's shape unmentioned, and the first source that does describe it — Gregory of Tours' sixth-century epitome — calls it an ordinary upright cross; the diagonal, X-shaped cross is a later iconographic development, on the Catholic Encyclopedia's own admission “no older than the fourteenth century.” Even its strongest form — that Andrew chose an unlike cross out of unworthiness, as Peter is said to have — is a late motive borrowed from Peter's legend. Weak — a fine emblem, a poor historical report.8

Did Andrew found the church of Byzantium? (assessment: weak)

The Byzantium-foundation claim — that Andrew planted the church of the future Constantinople and ordained Stachys its first bishop — must be split from the milder tradition that he merely passed through the town. The passage is old and not absurd: Byzantium sits on the route from his Black Sea mission to Achaia, and the sixth-century epitome of the Acts already lists it among his stops. But the foundation and ordination is a late accretion — unknown before the end of the eighth century, absent from a seventh-century source that names Stachys yet knows nothing of it, and pressed into the rivalry with Rome only later still. Weak — church memory and ecclesiastical charter, not history.9
