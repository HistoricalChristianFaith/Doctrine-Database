Was James son of Alphaeus the same man as James the brother of the Lord?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — the fusion of the apostle James son of Alphaeus with James the brother of the Lord (James the Just), made doctrine by Jerome and standard for centuries in the Latin West, requires that the “brethren of the Lord” include members of the Twelve; but the Gospels set the brethren apart from the Twelve and among the unbelievers before the resurrection, the completing equation Alphaeus = Clopas is philologically strained, and the East never made the fusion. On its best available case the two Jameses are better kept distinct.

Related arguments: the cousin theory (on the perpetual-virginity timeline) — see also Do the identifications that complete Jerome’s cousin-theory hold? (Alphaeus = Clopas) · Could the Lord’s brethren have been among the Twelve? · Does the shared manner of death support identifying the two Jameses?

The claim

But other of the apostles saw I none, save James the Lord’s brother.

— Galatians 1:19. The claim under test is the harmonization that made the silent apostle famous: that James “the son of Alphaeus” of the apostle-lists is one and the same person as James “the Lord’s brother,” surnamed the Just, who led the church at Jerusalem and was martyred there about the year 62. If they are one man, the apostle with no story inherits the whole life of James the Just — the bishopric, the sanctity, the death at the temple. If they are two, the son of Alphaeus is left as blank as the Gospel lists found him. Jerome forged the identification in his reply to Helvidius (c. 383) and restated it firmly thereafter, and it became the settled reading of the Latin West.1

The evidence for

The case is a chain of convergences, and it is not baseless. First, the names line up. At the cross stands “Mary the mother of James the little and of Joses” (Mark 15:40); the Lord’s brethren are named “James, and Joses, and Simon, and Judas” (Matthew 13:55) — the same leading pair, James and Joses. If that Mary is not the Virgin but her kinswoman, the brethren become cousins, and their James becomes “James the little.” Second, one further identification — that “Alphaeus,” father of the apostle, and “Clopas,” husband of that Mary (John 19:25), are one name in two Greek forms of a single Aramaic original — then makes James the little the apostle James son of Alphaeus. Third, after James son of Zebedee is killed (Acts 12:2), the “James” who leads Jerusalem (Acts 15:13) is named without qualification, as though only one prominent James remained — and Paul’s “save James the Lord’s brother” can be read as ranking him among the apostles. Fourth, the identification is not merely medieval: the fusion is already at work in Pseudo-Hippolytus (who gives the son of Alphaeus the Just’s Jerusalem death), and the Alphaeus = Clopas equation is stated by Chrysostom and Theodoret in the fourth and fifth centuries. Cumulatively the reading lets the brethren be believers and leaders after the resurrection — James of Jerusalem chief among them — without positing later children of Mary; that coherence, and its service to the perpetual virginity, recommended it to the Latin West.2

The evidence against

Each link in the chain is an inference, and the strongest of them break. The brethren are set apart from the Twelve. Only shortly before the crucifixion the Lord’s brethren “did not believe in him” (John 7:5); after the Ascension they are listed with but distinct from the apostles — “these all continued… with the women, and Mary the mother of Jesus, and with his brethren” (Acts 1:14); and Paul in 1 Corinthians 9:5 ranks “the brethren of the Lord” as a class beside “the other apostles.” That the brethren, James included, were not of the Twelve is the sounder reading of the data (weighed on its own page, assessment sound) — and it alone is fatal to the fusion, since the son of Alphaeus is of the Twelve. Galatians 1:19 does not make James an apostle. The Greek “I saw no other of the apostles save James” is naturally read as an exception of person seen, not of category — “I saw no other apostle, but I did see James” — and cannot be pressed to prove James was counted among them. Alphaeus is not Clopas. The Aramaic behind Clopas (QLWPY, attested in a Murabbaʿat document) is a distinct name from that behind Alphaeus (HLPY); Luke, who calls this man Cleopas (Luke 24:18), does not also call him Alphaeus. The equation was “unknown to Jerome himself” and is philologically doubtful (weighed here, assessment weak). “James the little” distinguishes, it does not identify: the epithet marks stature and serves to tell this James apart from the Lord’s brother, not to equate them. And the two death-traditions cancel out. The apostle-lists themselves cannot agree whether the son of Alphaeus was stoned at Jerusalem (which is James the Just’s death, borrowed) or crucified in Egypt — the mark of a memory filled in, not preserved. Finally, the East never made the fusion, and to this day keeps James son of Alphaeus and James the brother of the Lord as two saints with two feast-days.3

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The shared names — James and Joses in both the brethren-list and at the cross — are a real and non-trivial datum, and a fusion of the leading Jameses is a coherent way to keep the brethren believers without later sons of Mary. The reading was held by great teachers and a whole Latin tradition; it is not absurd, and no single verse simply refutes it.

What it cannot reach. The fusion is load-bearing only if a chain of separate identifications all hold at once — that Mary of James is the Virgin’s sister, that Alphaeus is Clopas, that “the little” ranks rather than distinguishes, that Galatians 1:19 enrolls James among the apostles — and each of these is the less-probable reading on its own evidence, while the governing fact runs the other way: the Gospels hold the Lord’s brethren apart from the Twelve, so the brother of the Lord cannot simply be the apostle. Tellingly, the most economical modern form of the cousin-reading (Blinzler’s) keeps the fusion only by discarding the Alphaeus = Clopas prop — and still must overrule the separation of the brethren from the Twelve. The whole tradition most invested in the identification, Roman Catholic scholarship, has largely let it go: the Jerusalem leader is now generally taken to be James the Lord’s brother, not reckoned among the Twelve, and the apostle son of Alphaeus a distinct and unknown man. The identification is a harmonization the evidence does not support, not a fact the evidence compels.4

Assessment

Weak. The equation of James son of Alphaeus with James the brother of the Lord held the Latin West for over a millennium and is coherent as a harmonization, but it does not hold on its own evidence: it turns on a chain of identifications each less probable than its rival, and it runs against the Gospels’ own separation of the Lord’s brethren from the Twelve. The apostle James son of Alphaeus and James the Just are better kept distinct — which leaves the apostle almost entirely unknown, and most of his traditional “life” the borrowed property of another man.
