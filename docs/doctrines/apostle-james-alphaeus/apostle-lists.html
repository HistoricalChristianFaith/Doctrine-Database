The apostle-lists: a borrowed martyrdom

Dates: uncertain (the apostle-list literature, c. 3rd–8th centuries) · Pseudo-Hippolytus, On the Twelve Apostles
Summary: The layer where the silent apostle first grows a death — and the death is not his own. The late, mutually-dependent catalogues assign James son of Alphaeus a martyrdom, but they cannot agree on it: one strand stones him at Jerusalem (which is the death of James the Just), another crucifies him in Egypt. The disagreement is the tell that his biography is being filled in after the fact.

Claims & evidence

Pseudo-Hippolytus: stoned at Jerusalem, buried beside the temple

The catalogue that goes under the name of Hippolytus gives the apostle a Jerusalem death that is, detail for detail, the martyrdom Hegesippus assigns to James the brother of the Lord — stoned in the city, buried by the sanctuary.

And James the son of Alphaeus, when preaching in Jerusalem, was stoned to death by the Jews, and was buried there beside the temple.

— Pseudo-Hippolytus, On the Twelve Apostles, 9 (ANF vol. 5). Set this beside Hegesippus’s account of James the Just — cast from the temple, stoned, clubbed, and “buried on the spot, by the temple” (Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History 2.23) — and the borrowing is plain: the list has simply given the son of Alphaeus the other James’s end.

The rival strand: crucified in Egypt

Other branches of the same literature keep James son of Alphaeus distinct from James the Just and send him not to Jerusalem but to Egypt, reporting that he was crucified at Ostrakine (Ostracine) in Lower Egypt while preaching there. This is the death the Eastern tradition tends to preserve for the apostle in his own right — and it flatly contradicts the Jerusalem stoning.

Neither notice is early or independent; both come from the catalogue layer, centuries after the events, and the two cannot both be true of one man. Their very incompatibility is evidence that no genuine memory of this apostle’s death survived — only the pull of two rival identifications, one that fused him with the Lord’s brother and one that did not.

Notes

  • Late, dependent, and divided. The apostle-lists are of uncertain authorship and date, lean on one another, and are judged by critics worthless as independent history; their worth is as evidence for how the church came to remember the apostles. On the whole class, see the apostles hub.
  • The Jerusalem death is a giveaway of the fusion. That Pseudo-Hippolytus assigns the son of Alphaeus exactly the martyrdom of James the Just shows the two Jameses already run together in this layer — the same fusion Jerome would make doctrine, weighed on the argument page. The parallel deaths are also weighed, from the other side, on the perpetual-virginity timeline (“Does the shared manner of death support identifying the two Jameses?”, assessment weak).
  • The Golden Legend chooses the Jerusalem death. The medieval legend follows the fused strand, giving “St James the Less” the temple martyrdom and the fuller’s club of James the Just (see the Golden Legend page).
