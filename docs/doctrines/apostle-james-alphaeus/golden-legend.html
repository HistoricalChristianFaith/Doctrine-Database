The Golden Legend: the fusion completed

Dates: c. 1260 (Jacobus de Voragine, Legenda Aurea) · the Golden Legend, life of St James the Less
Summary: The medieval legendary gives the Western fusion its finished form. Its life of “St James the Less” simply stacks four identifications into one man — the son of Alphaeus, the brother of the Lord, James the Just, and the first bishop of Jerusalem — and gives him the death of James the Just as Hegesippus told it. It is late hagiography, and every biographical detail in it belongs by origin to the brother of the Lord.

Claims & evidence

Four Jameses folded into one

The Legend runs the identifications together as settled fact, explaining each of the man’s titles in turn:

He was also called James the son of Alpheus… He was called also the brother of our Lord, because he resembled much well our Lord in body, in visage, face and of manner… He was called James the Just for his right great holiness… he sang in Jerusalem the first mass that ever was sung therein, and he was first bishop of Jerusalem.

— Jacobus de Voragine, the Golden Legend, tr. William Caxton, life of St James the Less. The apostle of the bare Gospel lists has here absorbed the sanctity, the priesthood, and the see of James the Just — the fusion at its most complete.

The death of James the Just, given to the apostle

The martyrdom the Legend narrates is, again, that of James the brother of the Lord in Hegesippus: cast down from a height, stoned by the crowd, and finished with a blow from a fuller’s club.

… there was a man in that company took a fuller’s staff and smote him on the head, that his brain fell all abroad, and thus by martyrdom he finished his life.

— the Golden Legend, life of St James the Less. The fuller’s club that killed James the Just became, through this identification, the emblem by which “James the Less” is known in Western art — and so the patron of fullers.

Notes

  • Late legend, not early witness. The Golden Legend is a thirteenth-century compilation, more than a millennium after the events; it is cited here as evidence for how the Western church came to picture the apostle, never as a source for his life. On the whole class of late hagiography, see the apostles hub.
  • Every detail is borrowed. The holiness, the first mass, the bishopric of Jerusalem, and the death by the fuller’s club all belong, in their earliest sources, to James the brother of the Lord — drawn from Hegesippus (Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History 2.23). Strip the borrowing away and the apostle James son of Alphaeus is left as blank as the Gospel lists found him.
  • Whether the fusion holds. The identification the Legend takes for granted is exactly the question weighed on the argument page (assessment weak), and across the perpetual-virginity timeline.
