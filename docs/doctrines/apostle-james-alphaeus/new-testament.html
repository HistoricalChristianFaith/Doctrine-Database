The New Testament on James son of Alphaeus

Dates: c. 30–90 (the events, and the writing of the Gospels and Acts) · Wikipedia
Summary: The bedrock, and it is barely anything. James the son of Alphaeus is named only in the four lists of the Twelve, always ninth, and nothing else about him is recorded — no saying, no deed, no mission, no death. The Gospels also name a “James the less,” son of a Mary, at the cross, and a distinct “James the brother of the Lord” who led the Jerusalem church. Sorting these Jameses is the whole problem, and the New Testament itself keeps the brother of the Lord among the unbelieving brethren, set apart from the Twelve.

Claims & evidence

Named in all four lists of the Twelve — and nowhere else

James son of Alphaeus appears in every list of the apostles and at no other point in the New Testament. In each he stands ninth, heading the third group of four (the group that also contains Thaddaeus/Jude and Simon the Zealot). His father’s name, Alphaeus, is all the personal detail the lists supply.

… James the son of Alphaeus, and Lebbaeus, whose surname was Thaddaeus; Simon the Canaanite, and Judas Iscariot…

— Matthew 10:3–4 (cf. Mark 3:18; Luke 6:15; Acts 1:13). Of no other apostle who stands in all four lists does the New Testament record so little.

“James the less” at the cross — a second obscure James

Among the women who watched the crucifixion Mark names “Mary the mother of James the less and of Joses.” The Greek is Iakōbos ho mikros — “James the little” (the KJV’s “less” renders a Latin comparative that the Greek positive does not carry). The epithet exists precisely to distinguish this James from others of the name.

There were also women looking on afar off: among whom was Mary Magdalene, and Mary the mother of James the less and of Joses, and Salome…

— Mark 15:40 (cf. Matthew 27:56). The later tradition would take this “James the little” to be the son of Alphaeus — and, through his mother Mary, to be the Lord’s brother as well. Whether “the little” can bear that weight is weighed on the perpetual-virginity timeline (“Are ‘James the little’ and Joses the Lord’s brothers?”, assessment weak).

James the brother of the Lord — a third James, set apart from the Twelve

The best-known first-century James is neither of these: it is James the brother of the Lord, whom Paul met at Jerusalem and who presided over the council of Acts 15.

But other of the apostles saw I none, save James the Lord’s brother.

— Galatians 1:19. This James rose to lead the mother church (Acts 15:13; Acts 21:18). But in the Gospels the “brethren of the Lord” — James, Joses, Simon, and Judas (Matthew 13:55) — are a group distinct from the Twelve, and are named among those who did not yet believe in him (John 7:5); after the Ascension they are listed alongside, but separately from, the apostles (Acts 1:14).

Notes

  • Three Jameses, one blank apostle. The New Testament gives James son of Zebedee a story (called with John, martyred by Herod, Acts 12:2) and James the brother of the Lord a large one (Jerusalem’s leader). To James son of Alphaeus it gives only a name and a rank. Everything on his mission and death traced on the summary timeline belongs to the later tradition — and much of it was borrowed from James the brother of the Lord.
  • The Alphaeus of Matthew, and the Alphaeus of Levi. Mark also calls the tax-collector “Levi the son of Alphaeus” (Mark 2:14). Whether that is the same Alphaeus — making James and Levi/Matthew brothers — the New Testament never says; the church never drew that inference, and it is generally treated as a coincidence of a common name (see the Matthew = Levi argument).
  • Why the identification is tempting, and why it is not stated. The names line up — a James son of a Mary (Mark 15:40), a James the Lord’s brother (Galatians 1:19), a James son of Alphaeus (the lists) — but the New Testament itself never equates any two of them, and its own habit is to keep the Lord’s brethren apart from the Twelve. Whether the equation can be built anyway is weighed on the argument page.
