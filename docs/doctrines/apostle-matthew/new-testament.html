The New Testament on Matthew

Dates: c. 30–90 (the events, and the writing of the Gospels and Acts) · Wikipedia
Summary: Matthew is named in all four lists of the Twelve and given one narrative: his call from the toll-booth. Matthew’s own Gospel alone tags him “the publican” in the list. The same call-scene appears in Mark and Luke under a different name, Levi — the New Testament’s own seed of the question whether Matthew and Levi are one man. Of his life after the call, Scripture is silent.

Claims & evidence

Called from the receipt of custom — a tax collector

Matthew’s Gospel tells his call in a single verse: a man at his toll-booth, summoned with two words, who rises and follows. In the ancient world a publican (Greek telōnēs) — a collector of tolls and customs for the Roman system — was reckoned among the worst of sinners, a class grouped with harlots; that Jesus called one, and dined with his fellow tax-collectors, is the scandal the scene turns on.

And as Jesus passed forth from thence, he saw a man, named Matthew, sitting at the receipt of custom: and he saith unto him, Follow me. And he arose, and followed him.

— Matthew 9:9. The feast that follows draws the Pharisees’ complaint, “Why eateth your Master with publicans and sinners?” and the answer, “They that be whole need not a physician, but they that are sick” (Matthew 9:11–13).

“Matthew the publican” in the list of the Twelve

Matthew appears in every list of the apostles. Only in Matthew’s own Gospel is he marked with his old trade — “Matthew the publican” — a note of self-abasement often read as a signature of authorship, since the other lists spare him the title.

Philip, and Bartholomew; Thomas, and Matthew the publican; James the son of Alphaeus, and Lebbaeus, whose surname was Thaddaeus;

— Matthew 10:3. In Mark he is simply “Matthew” (Mark 3:18), in Luke “Matthew” (Luke 6:15), and in Acts, among the Eleven in the upper room, “Bartholomew, and Matthew” (Acts 1:13).

The same scene, a different name: Levi in Mark and Luke

Mark and Luke tell the identical call-story — the toll-booth, the “Follow me,” the rising and following, the feast with publicans — but call the man Levi. Mark names his father:

And as he passed by, he saw Levi the son of Alphaeus sitting at the receipt of custom, and said unto him, Follow me. And he arose and followed him.

— Mark 2:14. Luke tells it the same way and adds the feast: “And after these things he went forth, and saw a publican, named Levi, sitting at the receipt of custom: and he said unto him, Follow me. And he left all, rose up, and followed him” (Luke 5:27–28); “And Levi made him a great feast in his own house” (Luke 5:29). The word for word agreement of the scene, with only the name changed, is what drives the identification of Matthew with Levi.

Notes

  • “Son of Alphaeus.” Mark calls Levi “the son of Alphaeus” — the same patronymic borne by another of the Twelve, James the son of Alphaeus. Whether that makes Matthew/Levi a brother of James is unclear: “Alphaeus” was a common name, and the New Testament never links the two apostles.
  • Whether Matthew is Levi. The Synoptics call the man at the toll-booth “Matthew” in one Gospel and “Levi” in two, in one and the same scene; but no text states the equation, and “Levi” is never listed among the Twelve. Whether they are one man is weighed on the argument page.
  • The total silence after the call. Beyond the call-scene and his place in the lists, the New Testament records not one word or deed of Matthew. His authorship of the first Gospel, his mission, and his death — all traced on the summary timeline — belong to the later tradition.
