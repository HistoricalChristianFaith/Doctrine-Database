Clement of Alexandria on Matthew

Dates: c. 200 (the Stromata) · Wikipedia
Summary: In a discussion of what it means to “confess” Christ, Clement quotes the Valentinian teacher Heracleon, who observed that not everyone saved confessed the faith aloud before the authorities; some — “Matthew, Philip, Thomas, Levi, and many others” — confessed instead by their lives. The natural reading is that these apostles were not put to death for the faith. It is the earliest surviving hint of how Matthew’s life ended, and it points away from martyrdom — and, in listing “Matthew” and “Levi” side by side, it also bears on the identity question.

Claims & evidence

Heracleon’s list of those who confessed by conduct, not by the voice

Clement is expounding Christ’s saying that whoever confesses him before men will be confessed before the Father. He cites Heracleon — “the most distinguished of the school of Valentinus” — who distinguishes two kinds of confession, one made by faith and conduct and one made aloud before the magistrates:

The confession that is made with the voice, and before the authorities, is what the most reckon the only confession. Not soundly…

The public confession “with the voice” is the martyr’s confession before a tribunal, Heracleon argues, and it is not the lot of all the saved — some confess only by their lives. He then names his examples:

Of whom are Matthew, Philip, Thomas, Levi, and many others.

— Heracleon, quoted in Clement of Alexandria, Stromata 4.9. These apostles are offered precisely as men who did not make the open, martyr’s confession before the authorities — the point of Heracleon’s distinction. (The older English rendering above garbles the pivotal negative — the Greek says not all the saved confessed “with the voice,” naming these as the exceptions — but the sense, and the reason the passage is cited for Matthew’s natural death, is not in doubt.) Clement quotes Heracleon to correct him on a further point about confession, but does not dispute the roster.

Notes

  • The earliest word on Matthew’s death — and it is not martyrdom. Later tradition would make Matthew a martyr in Ethiopia; but this note, two centuries closer to the events, groups him with those who did not die under persecution. It is slender and comes at second hand, through a heretical teacher; still, it is early, and it weighs against the martyr-legend. See the argument page on the Ethiopian martyrdom.
  • Matthew and Levi named as two. The list sets “Matthew” and “Levi” apart, as though they were different men — a small counter-datum to the usual identification of the two. Not much can be built on a Valentinian’s list, but it shows the equation was not universal in the early church. See the argument page on Matthew and Levi.
  • The source, weighed. Heracleon was a Gnostic, and Clement quotes him to argue with him; the testimony is therefore worth only so much. But on this incidental point — who did and did not die a martyr’s death — a hostile or heterodox witness had no motive to invent, which is part of why the note is taken seriously.
