Papias, and the patristic chain: a Hebrew Matthew

Dates: Papias c. 125 (preserved by Eusebius, c. 325); the chain runs to Jerome, 393 · Wikipedia
Summary: The earliest notice of Matthew’s book is one sentence of Papias of Hierapolis: Matthew “put together the oracles in the Hebrew language.” That sentence became the fountainhead of a unanimous patristic tradition — Irenaeus, Origen, Eusebius, Jerome — that the first Gospel was written first in Hebrew (or Aramaic) for Jewish believers. A remarkable corollary is Eusebius’s report that Pantaenus found a Hebrew Matthew in “India,” left there by the apostle Bartholomew. This page gathers the testimony; what Papias’s “oracles” were, and whether a Hebrew Matthew ever existed, is weighed on the argument page.

Claims & evidence

Papias (c. 125): Matthew “put together the oracles in the Hebrew language”

Papias, bishop of Hierapolis and a collector of oral traditions from those who had known the apostles, wrote a lost work whose fragments Eusebius preserves. Of Matthew he says only this:

So then Matthew wrote the oracles in the Hebrew language, and every one interpreted them as he was able.

— Papias, in Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History 3.39.16. The Greek word rendered “oracles” is logia — the same word Papias used for the title of his own book, “Expositions of the Oracles [logia] of the Lord” — and everything downstream, and every modern dispute, turns on what he meant by it and by “the Hebrew language.”

Irenaeus (c. 180): “a written Gospel among the Hebrews in their own dialect”

A generation later Irenaeus states it as a plain fact of Gospel origins, dating it to the Roman preaching of Peter and Paul:

Matthew also issued a written Gospel among the Hebrews in their own dialect, while Peter and Paul were preaching at Rome, and laying the foundations of the Church.

— Irenaeus, Against Heresies 3.1.1 (also preserved in Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History 5.8). Here the logia have become “a written Gospel.”

Origen (c. 230): written by Matthew the publican, in Hebrew, for Jewish converts

Origen, in his commentary on Matthew, gives the order of the four Gospels; Eusebius quotes him:

…the first was written by Matthew, who was once a publican, but afterwards an apostle of Jesus Christ, and it was prepared for the converts from Judaism, and published in the Hebrew language.

— Origen, in Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History 6.25.4.

Eusebius (c. 325): Matthew wrote before leaving the Hebrews for other peoples

Eusebius adds a rationale — Matthew wrote his Gospel down as a parting gift to the Jewish church when his own preaching moved on:

For Matthew, who had at first preached to the Hebrews, when he was about to go to other peoples, committed his Gospel to writing in his native tongue, and thus compensated those whom he was obliged to leave for the loss of his presence.

— Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History 3.24.6.

The corollary: Pantaenus finds a Hebrew Matthew in “India”

Eusebius reports that Pantaenus, the head of the Alexandrian school, travelled east about 190 and there found a Hebrew Matthew already in circulation — carried, the story ran, by the apostle Bartholomew:

Pantaenus… is said to have gone to India. It is reported that among persons there who knew of Christ, he found the Gospel according to Matthew, which had anticipated his own arrival. For Bartholomew, one of the apostles, had preached to them, and left with them the writing of Matthew in the Hebrew language, which they had preserved till that time.

— Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History 5.10.3. Whatever its historical worth, the notice shows how firmly the “Hebrew Matthew” was believed to be a real, physical book by the late second century.

Notes

  • One sentence, a whole tradition. Every later Father who says Matthew wrote in Hebrew may be depending, directly or at a remove, on Papias’s single line — which is why the modern question is whether the unanimity is independent testimony or the echo of one ambiguous source.
  • “Hebrew” = Aramaic? In this period “the Hebrew dialect” often meant the Aramaic actually spoken in Judea; the Fathers do not tell us which they meant, and the two were not always distinguished.
  • What Papias meant by logia. The word can mean “sayings,” “oracles,” or by extension a whole book; some read it as the canonical Gospel, others as a lost sayings-collection, others as a book of Old-Testament proof-texts. The dispute is weighed on the argument page.
