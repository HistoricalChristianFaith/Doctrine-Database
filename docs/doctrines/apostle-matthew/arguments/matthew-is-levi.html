Was Matthew the tax collector the same man as Levi?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: plausible — the majority and probably-correct reading, resting not on silence but on a single call-scene that Matthew, Mark, and Luke tell in near-identical words with only the name changed; yet it is an identification no Gospel states, “Levi” never stands in a list of the Twelve, and one early witness names Matthew and Levi as if two men. Probable, not proven.

The claim

And as he passed by, he saw Levi the son of Alphaeus sitting at the receipt of custom, and said unto him, Follow me. And he arose and followed him.

— Mark 2:14. The claim under test is the traditional identification: that the tax-collector called from the toll-booth — whom Matthew’s Gospel names Matthew and Mark and Luke name Levi — is one man, so that the apostle Matthew and the publican Levi are the same person under two names.1

The evidence for

The case rests on the scene, not on a chain of inferences. It is one call-story, told three times. In Matthew 9, Mark 2, and Luke 5 the elements are the same and in the same order: a tax-collector sitting “at the receipt of custom,” the two-word summons “Follow me,” the man rising and following at once, and a feast with publicans and sinners that draws the Pharisees’ complaint and Jesus’ reply about the physician and the sick. The only substantive difference between the three tellings is the name — Matthew in the first Gospel, Levi in the other two. Matthew’s Gospel makes the substitution deliberately. Where Mark and Luke write “Levi” in the call-scene, the First Gospel writes “Matthew,” and then alone among the lists tags the apostle “Matthew the publican” — tying the man at the booth to the name in the Twelve. Two names for one Jewish man was ordinary. A first-century Jew could bear a Hebrew name and a second name (Simon/Peter, Saul/Paul, Joseph/Barsabbas), so “Levi” and “Matthew” sit easily on one person. On these grounds the identification became the universal tradition — Jerome states it flatly, “Matthew, also called Levi” — and remains the majority view among modern commentators.2

The evidence against

The identification is natural but not stated, and the loose ends are real. No Gospel equates them, and “Levi” is never one of the Twelve. Every list of the apostles names Matthew; none names Levi. If the evangelists knew the publican Levi to be the apostle Matthew, they never say so, and Mark — who names Levi “the son of Alphaeus” in the call — does not put that Levi in his own list of the Twelve. An alternative reading is available. Some have held that Levi was a disciple called by Jesus but not numbered among the Twelve, and that Matthew’s Gospel, wishing to attach the well-known call-scene to its named apostle, simply rewrote “Levi” as “Matthew” — a redactional move, not a report that the two names belonged to one historical man. An early witness lists them apart. The Valentinian Heracleon, quoted by Clement of Alexandria, names “Matthew, Philip, Thomas, Levi, and many others” in one breath — treating Matthew and Levi as two people. Little can be built on a Gnostic’s list, but it shows the equation was not felt as obvious in the early church.3

Adversarial assessment

What holds. Unlike the identification of Bartholomew with Nathanael — which rests on two complementary silences — the Matthew–Levi equation rests on a shared scene. Three Gospels narrate one and the same call with only the name varying, and the First Gospel’s own substitution of “Matthew” for “Levi,” joined to its unique “Matthew the publican,” reads as a deliberate claim that the tax-collector of the story is the apostle. That is a genuinely strong convergence, and it is why the identification has been the church’s settled view and remains the majority scholarly position. It is more likely than not.

What it cannot reach. The equation is still an inference the text never makes explicit. That “Levi” is absent from every apostle-list leaves room for the reading on which Levi and Matthew are two different tax-collector disciples, and Matthew’s substitution is a literary act rather than a biographical identity; Heracleon’s list shows at least one early reader took them as two. None of this defeats the identification — the simplest account of one thrice-told scene is that it concerns one man — but it keeps the conclusion at probable rather than certain. The most that can be said is that Matthew almost certainly is the Levi of the call-story as the First Evangelist intends him, and very probably was so in fact.4

Assessment

Plausible. The identification of Matthew with Levi is coherent, economical, and the majority view, and — resting on one call-scene told three times rather than on silence — it is the stronger of the Gospels’ two apostle-identity questions and is probably correct. But it is an equation no Gospel states, “Levi” never appears among the Twelve, and one early witness lists the two names apart. A probable identification, not a demonstrated fact.
