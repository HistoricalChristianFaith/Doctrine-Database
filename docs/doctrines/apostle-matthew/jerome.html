Jerome on Matthew

Dates: 393 (De Viris Illustribus, “On Illustrious Men”) · Wikipedia
Summary: Jerome opens his catalogue of Christian writers with Peter, James, and — at number three — Matthew, whom he treats as the church’s first evangelist. In four compact lines he asserts both of the questions that hang over Matthew as settled fact: that Matthew is the Levi of the other Gospels, and that he wrote his Gospel first in Hebrew for Jewish believers. Jerome adds that he had himself handled a Hebrew text of it — a claim that ties the “Hebrew Matthew” to the apocryphal Gospel of the Hebrews and complicates the whole tradition.

Claims & evidence

“Matthew, also called Levi” — a Hebrew Gospel for the circumcision

Jerome states the identity and the Hebrew original without qualification, and dates the composition to Judea before the Greek translation:

Matthew, also called Levi, apostle and aforetimes publican, composed a gospel of Christ at first published in Judea in Hebrew for the sake of those of the circumcision who believed, but this was afterwards translated into Greek though by what author is uncertain.

— Jerome, Lives of Illustrious Men 3. The header of the chapter is blunter still: “Matthew, surnamed Levi.”

The Hebrew text Jerome claims to have seen

Jerome then reports that the Hebrew original survived and that he had access to it — a copy kept at Caesarea and one used by the Nazarene sect at Beroea in Syria, which he was allowed to transcribe:

The Hebrew itself has been preserved until the present day in the library at Cæsarea which Pamphilus so diligently gathered. I have also had the opportunity of having the volume described to me by the Nazarenes of Berœa, a city of Syria, who use it.

— Jerome, Lives of Illustrious Men 3. Elsewhere Jerome says outright that he translated this “Gospel according to the Hebrews” into Greek and Latin — identifying, or at least entangling, the lost Hebrew Matthew with the apocryphal Gospel of the Hebrews used by Jewish Christians.

Notes

  • Both cruxes asserted as fact. Jerome is the writer who most firmly fixes, for the Latin West, the two traditional claims about Matthew — that he is Levi, and that his Gospel is a translation from Hebrew. Both are weighed as questions on their own pages (Matthew and Levi; a Hebrew Matthew).
  • The Gospel-of-the-Hebrews problem. By equating the “Hebrew Matthew” with the text the Nazarenes used, Jerome may have confused the canonical Gospel’s supposed original with a distinct Jewish-Christian gospel. Modern scholars generally think he did, which undercuts his report as evidence for a Hebrew canonical Matthew — a point developed on the argument page.
  • What Jerome still shows. Whatever the confusion, Jerome is firm proof that by the late fourth century the whole church took Matthew for the author of the first Gospel and believed a Hebrew form of it had once existed — the tradition at its most confident, just before critical scrutiny.
