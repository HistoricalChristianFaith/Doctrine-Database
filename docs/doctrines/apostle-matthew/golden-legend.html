The Golden Legend on Matthew

Dates: c. 1260 (Jacobus de Voragine, Legenda Aurea) · Wikipedia
Summary: The fullest medieval account of Matthew, and the one the Latin West inherited: a romance of his mission to Ethiopia, his contest with two sorcerers, his conversion of a king and the consecration of the king’s daughter Iphigenia to virginity, and his martyrdom at the altar when the next king wished to marry her. It is late hagiography — twelve centuries after the events, gathered from the apostle-lists and older apocryphal Acts — and is cited here for what the church came to tell about Matthew, not as evidence for what happened.

The legend

Matthew, the Golden Legend relates, came to preach in Ethiopia, in the city of Nadaber, where he lodged with the eunuch of Queen Candace whom Philip had baptized. There he confronted two enchanters, Zaroes and Arphaxat, who deceived the people with their arts and had themselves honoured as gods; Matthew undid their spells, healing where they had harmed, and mastered the fire-breathing serpents they loosed. When he raised the dead son of King Egippus to life, the sorcerers fled to Persia (where, the legend adds, Simon and Jude would later defeat them), and the king with his wife and his daughter Iphigenia and all Ethiopia were baptized. Matthew, it says, taught there thirty-three years, built churches, and consecrated Iphigenia as head of more than two hundred virgins vowed to God.

After Egippus died, Hirtacus came to the throne and coveted Iphigenia, offering Matthew half his realm if he would win her consent to marriage. Matthew, preaching in the church on the goodness of lawful marriage but the greater good of a vow kept to God, refused to release her — and Iphigenia, vowed to Christ, would not consent. Enraged, Hirtacus sent a swordsman who struck the apostle down as he stood at the altar, having just celebrated the mysteries: “and thus he received the crown of martyrdom,” the tradition placing his death about the year 68. When the king then tried to burn Iphigenia and her virgins, the fire turned and consumed his own palace instead; his son was seized by a demon, and the king himself, struck with leprosy, died by his own hand.

Notes

  • The emblems. From this legend and the apostle-lists behind it come Matthew’s traditional attributes in Western art: the angel or winged man (his Gospel-symbol from Revelation’s four living creatures) and the sword or halberd of his martyrdom.
  • Late and derivative. The tale descends from apocryphal Acts of Matthew and the mission-lists that placed him in Ethiopia; none of it can be traced to the first centuries, and it stands a thousand years and more from the events. On the value of such hagiography, see the apostles hub.
  • Against the earliest hint. The martyrdom the Golden Legend makes central is exactly what the earliest surviving notice of Matthew’s death — Clement of Alexandria’s, c. 200 — tells against. The tension is weighed on the argument page.
