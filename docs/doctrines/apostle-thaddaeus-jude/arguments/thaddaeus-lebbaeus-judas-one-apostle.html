Are Thaddaeus, Lebbaeus, and Judas of James one and the same apostle?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: plausible — that the “Thaddaeus” (or “Lebbaeus”) of Matthew and Mark is the same apostle as the “Judas of James” of Luke and Acts is the traditional harmonization, and it is a good one: the name stands in the identical list-slot, John independently attests a second apostolic Judas “not Iscariot,” and “Lebbaeus” and “Thaddaeus” are textual variants of one name, not two men. It remains an inference — the lists do give different names, and no text equates them — so it is coherent and probably right without being certain.

Related arguments: the name and identity of Jude — see also Did the apostle Jude write the Epistle of Jude?

The claim

Judas saith unto him, not Iscariot, Lord, how is it that thou wilt manifest thyself unto us, and not unto the world?

— John 14:22. The four lists of the Twelve divide into three fixed groups of four; in the third group, after James the son of Alphaeus, Matthew and Mark name “Thaddaeus” (Matthew’s Received Text “Lebbaeus, whose surname was Thaddaeus”), while Luke and Acts, in exactly that place, name “Judas of James.” The claim under test is the harmonization that has stood since antiquity: that these are not three or four apostles but one, a single member of the Twelve who bore more than one name.1

The evidence for

The case is strong for a harmonization. First, the slot is identical. In all four lists the disputed name occupies the same position — third group, second member, between James of Alphaeus and Simon the Zealot — so the lists are describing the same twelve men in the same order, and differ only in what they call this one. Second, John supplies independent confirmation. Without listing the Twelve, John names a “Judas, not Iscariot” among the disciples at the Last Supper (John 14:22) — corroborating from a fourth Gospel that a second apostolic Judas, distinct from the traitor, stood in the circle, precisely as Luke and Acts report. Third, “Lebbaeus” and “Thaddaeus” are one name in the manuscripts, not two men. The earliest Greek witnesses of Matthew read only “Thaddaeus”; the Western text (Codex Bezae) reads only “Lebbaeus”; the conflated “Lebbaeus, who was called Thaddaeus” of the Byzantine text and the KJV is a later fusion of the two competing shorter readings — so the tradition never meant two people, only two bynames. Fourth, the Fathers harmonized without strain. Jerome, commenting on the list, took the man to have borne several names:

He is called Judas son of James by the evangelist Luke, and elsewhere is named Lebbaeus, which means “little heart.” One must believe he had three names.

— Jerome, Commentary on Matthew 10.4. Multiple names for one man were common enough among the Twelve (Simon/Peter/Cephas, Levi/Matthew, Nathanael/Bartholomew, Thomas/Didymus), so a “man of three names” is no anomaly.2

The evidence against

What checks the reading from certainty is simply that it is an inference the text never states. The lists really do differ. Matthew and Mark have no Judas but Iscariot; Luke and Acts have no Thaddaeus at all. Harmonizing them requires the assumption that one evangelist preferred a byname where another gave the patronymic — reasonable, but an assumption. A minority of critics read the divergence as a real seam, a sign that the lists reached the evangelists in slightly different forms, and that the membership of the Twelve was recalled with some variation at the edges; on that view “Thaddaeus” and “Judas of James” need not be forced into one person. And the identification cannot be proved from any saying or scene, since John’s “Judas not Iscariot” confirms a second Judas but never connects him to the name Thaddaeus. The harmonization is the most economical account of the data, but it rests on convergence, not on a text that names the man twice.3

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The single fixed list-slot, John’s independent second Judas, and the manuscript fact that Lebbaeus and Thaddaeus are variants of one name together make a genuinely strong case that one apostle is in view under several names. Nothing in the Gospels tells against it, and the alternative — that the evangelists disagreed about who the twelfth-but-one apostle was — is the harder claim.

What it cannot reach. None of this rises to demonstration. The identification is a harmonization of parallel lists, not a datum any evangelist states, and a sober reading must leave room for the lists simply preserving different names. This is the internal knot — whether the apostle-lists describe one man — and it is answerable with fair confidence. The external and far weaker leap, that this apostle also wrote the Epistle of Jude and was a brother of the Lord, is a separate question with a separate (and poorer) answer, weighed on its own page.4

Assessment

Plausible — indeed probably right. That Thaddaeus, Lebbaeus, and Judas of James name one apostle of the Twelve is the most economical reading of four parallel lists, is confirmed in part by John’s “Judas not Iscariot,” and rests on the manuscript truth that Lebbaeus and Thaddaeus are two bynames, not two men. It falls short of certainty only because the lists give different names in the slot and no text equates them — a harmonization coherent and likely, not proven.
