The apostle-lists and the Golden Legend

Dates: the apostle-lists, uncertain (c. 4th–7th c.); the Golden Legend, c. 1260 · Christian Iconography (Caxton text)
Summary: Where the New Testament gives Jude no mission and no death, the late catalogues supply both — and they pair him with Simon the Zealot. The Byzantine and Latin apostle-lists scatter his labours across Mesopotamia, Persia, and Armenia; the medieval Golden Legend gives the fullest form, sending Simon and Jude together into Persia to a martyr’s death at the hands of pagan priests. This is the layer that fixed their joint feast on 28 October and gave Jude the club and Simon the saw as their emblems in Western art. It is late legend — a millennium and more after the events — and is set out here as church memory, not history.

Claims & evidence

The apostle-lists: a mission scattered east

The family of late apostle-catalogues — Pseudo-Hippolytus’s On the Twelve and On the Seventy, Pseudo-Dorotheus, the Latin Breviarium Apostolorum, and their kin — assign “Jude” (variously Thaddaeus, Lebbaeus, or Judas of James) a field and a manner of death, but they do not agree. Some send him to Mesopotamia and the regions of the Euphrates; some to Persia; some couple him with Simon; the Edessene strand collapses him into Addai of the seventy (see the Abgar page). These catalogues are mutually dependent, of uncertain date and authorship, and are judged unreliable as history — valuable only as evidence for how the church came to remember the apostle. On the whole layer and how far to trust it, see the apostles hub.

The Golden Legend: Simon and Jude in Persia

Jacobus de Voragine’s Golden Legend (c. 1260) gathers the strands into a single dramatic life of “Saints Simon and Jude.” The two apostles, having preached apart, meet in Persia and there confront a pair of sorcerers who dog their steps:

… from thence came they into Persia, and found there two enchanters, Zaroes and Arphaxat…

After many wonders and disputations, the pagan priests of the sun and moon turn the crowd against them, and the apostles are killed together in the temple:

And when the bishops saw this, they ran upon the apostles and hewed them to death anon. In the same hour, which was fair and clear, came so great thunder and lightning that the temple was smitten a-three, and the two enchanters were turned into coals…

— the Golden Legend, tr. William Caxton, life of SS. Simon and Jude. It is late hagiography, and its geography and villains are the stuff of romance; it is cited here for what it fixed in the Western imagination, not as testimony to the apostle’s end.

The feast, and the emblems

From this joint martyrdom the Western Church keeps Simon and Jude together on 28 October. And because their bodies were said to have been mutilated, art gave each a tool of his death as his attribute: a saw for Simon (“his usual attribute is the saw, since his body was said to have been sawed to pieces”) and a club or halberd for Jude. These emblems — not the Golden Legend’s own words, but the later iconographic tradition — are why a club identifies Jude in a Western altarpiece, just as a fuller’s club identifies the other James.

Notes

  • Two tools, two traditions. The clean picture of “Jude clubbed, Simon sawn” is the iconographic convention recorded in later reference works (the Catholic Encyclopedia on St Simon gives Simon the saw and the 28 October feast); the Golden Legend’s own text says only that the priests “hewed them to death.” Both are late, and neither rests on early evidence.
  • Persia is one guess among several. That Simon and Jude died in Persia is the Golden Legend’s choice; the apostle-lists variously place Jude in Mesopotamia, Armenia, or Edessa, and no early source records his death at all. Sean McDowell, who grades every apostle’s martyrdom-tradition, rates the case for Jude’s martyrdom among the weakest — the traditions are late, divergent, and legendary. The pairing with Simon is itself a product of this layer, not of the New Testament, where the two are never linked.
