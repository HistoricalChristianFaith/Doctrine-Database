Eusebius and the letters of Abgar

Dates: c. 325 (Eusebius, Church History; the Abgar correspondence it quotes purports to date c. 29) · Wikipedia (Abgar legend)
Summary: The earliest connected story attached to any “Thaddaeus” is the legend of King Abgar of Edessa, whom a Thaddaeus is said to have healed and converted. Eusebius gives it in full, claiming to translate documents from the royal archives of Edessa — but his Thaddaeus is not one of the Twelve. Eusebius twice calls him “one of the seventy,” sent to Edessa by the apostle Thomas; and the Syriac source behind the tale, the Doctrine of Addai, names the missionary Addai and likewise counts him among the seventy-two. So the very first “life of Thaddaeus” belongs, on its own telling, to a different and lesser figure — and the letters at its heart were judged forgeries already in antiquity.

Claims & evidence

Thaddaeus “one of the seventy,” sent by Thomas

Eusebius introduces Thaddaeus not among the Twelve but among the wider band of disciples, and promises the Edessa story:

They say that Thaddeus also was one of them [the seventy], concerning whom I shall presently relate an account which has come down to us.

— Eusebius, Church History 1.12.3. He then tells it: after the resurrection,

Thomas, one of the twelve apostles, under divine impulse sent Thaddeus, who was also numbered among the seventy disciples of Christ, to Edessa, as a preacher and evangelist of the teaching of Christ.

— Church History 1.13.4. So in Eusebius the Edessa missionary is a disciple of the seventy, dispatched by an apostle — not himself one of the Twelve.

The correspondence of Abgar and Jesus — “from the archives of Edessa”

The centre of the tale is an exchange of letters. King Abgar, mortally ill, writes to Jesus begging to be healed and offering him refuge; Jesus writes back blessing him and promising to send a disciple after his ascension. Eusebius says he found the letters in the public registers of Edessa and translated them from Syriac:

You have written evidence of these things taken from the archives of Edessa, which was at that time a royal city. For in the public registers there, which contain accounts of ancient times and the acts of Abgarus, these things have been found preserved down to the present time.

— Church History 1.13.5. The appended narrative recounts how Thaddaeus, lodging with Tobias, laid his hand on Abgar “in his name” and cured him, then preached the coming, death, descent, and resurrection of Christ to the assembled city. The account closes: “These things were done in the three hundred and fortieth year” — the year 340 of the Seleucid era, about A.D. 29.

Judas Thomas, and the Syriac Doctrine of Addai

The Syriac form of the story adds a further tangle of names. It opens: “After the ascension of Jesus, Judas, who was also called Thomas, sent to him Thaddeus, an apostle, one of the Seventy” (Church History 1.13.11). Here the sender Thomas himself bears the name Judas — “Judas Thomas,” the “twin,” as the Acts of Thomas also call him — so that two apostolic Judases hover over the mission. The independent Syriac source Eusebius drew on, the Doctrine of Addai, names the healer of Abgar “Addai” (the Syriac form of Thaddaeus) and, like Eusebius, expressly reckons him one of the seventy-two, not of the Twelve:

After that Christ had ascended to heaven, Judas Thomas sent to Abgar, Addai the Apostle, who was one of the seventy-two Apostles.

— The Doctrine of Addai, the Apostle, tr. George Phillips (London, 1876). Whether the apostle Thaddaeus and this Addai of the seventy are the same man — the point on which the whole Edessene claim turns — is weighed on the Abgar-legend argument page.

Notes

  • The letters were judged apocryphal in antiquity. The supposed correspondence of Abgar and Jesus was formally listed among the writings to be rejected as apocryphal in the sixth-century Decretum Gelasianum (“Epistle of Jesus to Abgar — apocryphal; Epistle of Abgar to Jesus — apocryphal”), and no critic now defends its authenticity. Its value is as evidence for how early and how strongly Edessa claimed an apostolic founder, not as a record of the apostle’s acts. On the apocryphal-Acts layer generally, and how far to trust it, see the apostles hub.
  • Eusebius’s own confusion. The oldest editors noticed that Eusebius has “sundered” Thaddaeus from the Twelve and demoted him to the seventy — an error like his splitting of Peter and Cephas — while the Syriac narrative he quotes keeps calling the man simply an “apostle.” The muddle is itself the point: even the earliest teller of the Thaddaeus legend was unsure whether his hero was one of the Twelve or a lesser disciple of the same name.
  • A later royal Abgar, and a real early Edessene church. Historians locate the seed of the legend in a later Christian king, Abgar the Great (late second century), at whose court Christianity flourished — the pious fiction was then projected back onto his namesake, the Abgar contemporary with Jesus. That Edessa was an early centre of Syriac Christianity is not in doubt; that an apostle of the Twelve planted it is another matter.
