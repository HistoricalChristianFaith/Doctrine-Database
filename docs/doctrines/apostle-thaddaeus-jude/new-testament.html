The New Testament on Thaddaeus / Jude

Dates: c. 30–90 (the events, and the writing of the Gospels and Acts) · Wikipedia
Summary: This apostle’s whole problem is his name. He stands tenth in every list of the Twelve, heading no group and saying nothing — but the lists do not agree what to call him. Matthew and Mark name a “Thaddaeus” (some manuscripts “Lebbaeus”) in the slot where Luke and Acts put “Judas of James.” John’s Gospel lets a “Judas, not Iscariot” ask one question at the Last Supper. The church harmonized all of these into a single apostle of several names, and then went further and identified him with Jude the author of the Epistle of Jude — though that Jude calls himself “brother of James,” and is usually reckoned a brother of the Lord, set apart from the Twelve. Sorting the names is the whole story.

Claims & evidence

“Thaddaeus” (or “Lebbaeus”) in Matthew and Mark

In the first two Gospels the tenth apostle — standing in the third group of four, after James the son of Alphaeus — is named Thaddaeus. The Received Text of Matthew doubles the name:

… James the son of Alphaeus, and Lebbaeus, whose surname was Thaddaeus; Simon the Canaanite, and Judas Iscariot, who also betrayed him.

— Matthew 10:3–4. Mark has simply “Thaddaeus” (Mark 3:18). The double name in Matthew is a textual matter: the oldest Greek witnesses read only “Thaddaeus,” a “Western” strand (Codex Bezae) reads only “Lebbaeus,” and the conflated “Lebbaeus whose surname was Thaddaeus” of the Received Text (and the KJV) is a later harmonizing reading — so “Lebbaeus” and “Thaddaeus” are two names for one man in the same list-slot, not two men.

“Judas of James” in Luke and Acts — the same slot, a different name

Luke’s two lists put no Thaddaeus in that place at all. Instead they name a second Judas — carefully distinguished from the traitor:

Matthew and Thomas, James the son of Alphaeus, and Simon called Zelotes, and Judas the brother of James, and Judas Iscariot, which also was the traitor.

— Luke 6:15–16 (cf. Acts 1:13). The Greek is Ioudas Iakōbou — literally “Judas of James,” a bare genitive. The KJV supplies “brother of James,” but the natural sense of such a genitive is “son of James” (as the RV, RSV, and most moderns render it), and the difference will matter greatly when this Judas is later identified with the Jude who wrote an epistle “brother of James.” Either way, Luke’s “Judas of James” occupies exactly the place Matthew and Mark give to Thaddaeus.

“Judas, not Iscariot” at the Last Supper

John’s Gospel never lists the Twelve, but it names this apostle once, and confirms that a second Judas was among them — giving him the one saying the New Testament records from his lips:

Judas saith unto him, not Iscariot, Lord, how is it that thou wilt manifest thyself unto us, and not unto the world?

— John 14:22. That John is at pains to add “not Iscariot” shows that the name Judas needed disambiguating — independent confirmation that an apostle called Judas, distinct from the betrayer, stood among the Twelve, just as Luke and Acts report.

Jude the Lord’s brother, and the Epistle of Jude — a separate knot

The New Testament also knows a Jude who is not presented as one of the Twelve: among the “brethren of the Lord” named at Nazareth is a Judas.

Is not this the carpenter’s son? is not his mother called Mary? and his brethren, James, and Joses, and Simon, and Judas?

— Matthew 13:55 (cf. Mark 6:3). The short Epistle of Jude opens by naming its author from this family:

Jude, the servant of Jesus Christ, and brother of James…

— Jude 1. “Brother of James” most naturally means the brother of James the Just, the Lord’s brother who led the Jerusalem church — so the epistle’s Jude is generally taken to be Judas the brother of the Lord, one of the “brethren” whom the Gospels set apart from, and not yet believing among, the Twelve. Whether that Jude can also be the apostle “Judas of James” is a further identification, weighed on its own page.

Notes

  • One apostle, three or four names. Thaddaeus (Matthew, Mark), Lebbaeus (a variant of the same slot), Judas of James (Luke, Acts), and “Judas not Iscariot” (John) are traditionally taken to be one man — the harmonization weighed on its own page (assessment plausible). Of his life, mission, or death the New Testament records nothing but the one question he asked at the supper.
  • Two knots, kept distinct here. The first knot is internal to the apostle-lists: are Thaddaeus, Lebbaeus, and Judas of James one apostle? The second is the leap outward: is that apostle also Jude the author of the epistle, and so a brother of the Lord? The two are separate questions with separate answers — the first reasonably strong, the second much weaker — and this page’s two argument pages keep them apart. On the wider debate over whether the Lord’s “brethren” belonged to the Twelve at all, see the perpetual-virginity timeline.
  • “Judas of James”: son or brother? The bare genitive Iakōbou is most naturally “son of James”; only the wish to make this apostle the epistle-writer (“Jude brother of James”) drives the KJV’s “brother of James” here. The grammar is the hinge of the whole identity question, and is weighed on the epistle-authorship page.
