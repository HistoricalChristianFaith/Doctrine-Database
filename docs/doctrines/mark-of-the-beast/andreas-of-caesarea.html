Andreas of Caesarea on the Mark of the Beast and the Number 666

Dates: c. 614 · Wikipedia
Summary: Andrew (Andreas) of Caesarea inherits Oecumenius's list of candidate names for the beast, prunes it, and adds a principled refusal to identify the beast at all: the name is providentially concealed, left out of the divine book on purpose, and time and experience — not learned guesswork — will finally disclose it.

Claims & evidence

The name is providentially concealed; do not presume to fix it

"For the sober-minded, time and experience will reveal the actual significance of the number and the truth of whatever has been written about it. For, were it necessary, as some of the teachers say, that such a name be clearly known, the seer would have revealed it. But the divine grace did not consent that the name of the destroyer be noted in the divine book."

— Andrew of Caesarea, Commentary on the Apocalypse, on 13:18, trans. Eugenia Scarvelis Constantinou, Fathers of the Church 123 (CUA Press, 2011), source. Against "some of the teachers" who press a particular decipherment, Andrew argues the omission is deliberate: had God meant the name to be known now, the seer would have written it plainly. It is withheld, and only the unfolding of history will make it certain.

The mark on the hand and forehead

"And he will eagerly strive to place the mark of the ruinous name of the apostate upon everyone. He will make the mark upon their right hands to bring to an end the doing of good works, and upon their foreheads in order to teach those deceived to be bold in their deception and darkness. But those who have had their faces sealed with the divine light will not receive the mark."

— Andrew of Caesarea, Commentary on the Apocalypse, on 13:16–17, trans. Eugenia Scarvelis Constantinou, Fathers of the Church 123 (CUA Press, 2011), source. Andrew reads the two placements morally: the mark on the right hand ends the doing of good works, the mark on the forehead emboldens the deceived in their error — while those already sealed with the divine light are exempt and cannot receive it.

Notes

  • The mature Greek "reticence" tradition. Andrew carries a canon of candidate names for 666 — Lampetis, Teitan, Lateinos, Benedictus, and various descriptive epithets — but asserts none of them. He rehearses the options only to decline them, folding the whole exercise under his rule that the name is providentially withheld. That inherited list did not originate with Andrew: it was already assembled by Oecumenius, his slightly earlier Greek predecessor, whom Andrew reworks throughout (Max Nelson, "A Concealed Claudian," Journal of Theological Studies 76.1 (2025): 109–137, source).
  • He names no emperor. Unlike the Latin line that ran the number to Nero Caesar, Andrew identifies the beast with no historical ruler at all. The candidate names he lists are treated as possibilities to be left open, not as a solution — the point of his commentary here is precisely that the beast's name is not yet to be known.
