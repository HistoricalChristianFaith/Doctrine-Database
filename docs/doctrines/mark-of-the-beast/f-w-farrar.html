F. W. Farrar on the Mark of the Beast and the Number 666

Dates: 1882 (The Early Days of Christianity) · Wikipedia
Summary: The English cleric and popular historian who did more than anyone to carry the nineteenth-century German discovery — that 666 ciphers Neron Qesar in Hebrew letters — into wide English readership. In The Early Days of Christianity (1882) Farrar lays out the Nero gematria in plain terms and adds the "double solution": the same name, spelled the Latin way without the final Hebrew nun, yields the 616 of the variant tradition.

Claims & evidence

666 is Neron Kesar, the name a Jew would give Nero

"No Jew ever thought of Nero except as 'Neron Kesar,' and this gives at once — נרון קסר = 50 + 200 + 6 + 50 + 100 + 60 + 200 = 666."

— F. W. Farrar, The Early Days of Christianity (London: Cassell, 1882), 471. Farrar spells the imperial name in Hebrew consonants — NRWN QSR — and sums the letters (nun 50, resh 200, waw 6, nun 50; qoph 100, samekh 60, resh 200) to exactly 666. The point he presses is that a first-century Jew would have written and reckoned the name in just this form.

The 616 variant is the same name in its Latin (Nero, not Neron) spelling

On the following page Farrar accounts for the ancient variant reading 616 as the very same cipher. A Jewish-Christian scribe, he suggests, knew that "to Romans, and Gentiles generally, the name was always Nero Cæsar, not Neron"; dropping the final Hebrew nun (ן = 50) from Neron lowers the total from 666 to 616, and such a copyist "may have altered the reading because he imagined that… it made the solution more suitable and easy."
— Farrar, The Early Days of Christianity, 473 (running head "Nero Redivivus"). This is the celebrated "double solution": one name, two spellings, matching both numbers the manuscripts preserve — a coincidence no rival candidate (Teitan, Lateinos, Euanthas) reproduces.

Notes

  • A popularizer, not the discoverer. The Hebrew-gematria solution was found independently by four German scholars in the 1830s (C. F. A. Fritzsche 1831, Ferdinand Benary 1836, Ferdinand Hitzig and Eduard Reuss 1837); Farrar's service was to give it a wide English audience. The full case for the identification — and its limits — is weighed at the Nero-Caesar argument, with the 616 variant treated on its own at Does the 616 variant confirm the Nero cipher?
  • Identity and date. Farrar tied the number to his early (Neronian) dating of Revelation, reading the beast against Nero and the turmoil of AD 68–69; that dating argument is a distinct question, weighed on the date of Revelation page, where Farrar appears alongside Moses Stuart and the Cambridge early-daters. A name fixes a person, not a moment: the identification can be sound while the inference to a Neronian date is not.
