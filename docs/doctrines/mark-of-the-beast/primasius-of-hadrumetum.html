Primasius of Hadrumetum on the Mark of the Beast and the Number 666

Dates: c. 560 · Wikipedia
Summary: The African bishop Primasius reckons 666 "according to the Greek" and offers two descriptive names, both totalling the number: ΑΝΤΕΜΟΣ (Antemos), "contrary to honor," and ΑΡΝΟΥΜΕ (Arnoume), "I deny." Both fit the Antichrist — the one who usurps the honor owed to God and denies the faith that is proper to Christ. Drawing on Tyconius and Augustine, he knew the interpolated name Genseric but deliberately let it fall, the Vandal king having turned out not to be the Antichrist after all.

Claims & evidence

The number reckoned "according to the Greek": Antemos = 666, "contrary to honor"

"And the number, it says, is 666, which we will reckon according to the Greek, especially since he was writing to Asia and said according to the manner of their own language, 'I am the alpha and the omega.'… The name is ΑΝΤΕΜΟΣ, which, if you compute the numbers that correspond to these Greek letters, fulfills the number 666 (I + L + CCC + V + XL + LXX + CC). This name is interpreted as 'contrary to honor,' and so he is said to be contrary to that honor that belongs to God alone, and for this reason he is said to be inept, insolent and inconstant, for no honor pertains to him, but only anathema."

— Primasius, Commentary on the Apocalypse, on 13:18 (trans. William C. Weinrich). Like the Greek commentators, Primasius counts by Greek letter-values — noting John wrote "to Asia" — and produces not a proper name but a descriptive epithet: ΑΝΤΕΜΟΣ, "contrary to honor," fitting for the one who arrogates to himself the honor owed to God alone.

A second name, Arnoume = 666, "I deny"

"There is another name that gives the sum of the same number and that might rightfully be advanced, and that is ΑΡΝΟΥΜΕ, which also renders 666 (I + C + L + LXX + CCCC + XL + V). The interpretation of this is 'I deny.' And it is not surprising that the antichrist is worthy of this name, that is, of denial, since the name of belief is proper to Christ… And so, whether one would speak of ΑΝΤΕΜΟΣ, that is, 'contrary to honor,' or ΑΡΝΟΥΜΕ, that is, 'I deny,' either can aptly refer to the antichrist."

— Primasius, Commentary on the Apocalypse, on 13:18 (trans. William C. Weinrich). Primasius offers his two candidates as parallels, not as a fixed identification — each a word that describes the Antichrist's character: he is "contrary to honor" and he is a denier, over against Christ, to whom belief properly belongs. The method is the descriptive-name technique of the Greek tradition, put to work in Latin.

Notes

  • He knew "Genseric" and dropped it. The received text of Victorinus's commentary, in Jerome's recension, had listed Genseric — the Vandal king who sacked Rome in 455 — among the names summing to 666. Primasius was aware of the name but declined to carry it forward: history had disproved it, the Vandal not having proved to be the Antichrist. That he pruned an inherited candidate on these grounds is drawn out by Max Nelson, "A Concealed Claudian: The Meaning of 666 in Revelation," Journal of Theological Studies 76.1 (2025): 109–137, at Oxford Academic.
  • A hinge between the Greek names and the Latin symbolists. Primasius stands with Oecumenius and Andreas in the descriptive-name method — epithets rather than a single proper name — while writing in the Latin, Tyconian-Augustinian world. His pair Antemos/Arnoume was gathered up by Bede a century and a half later (alongside "Titan"), and thence into the medieval handbooks; see the mark-of-the-beast timeline.
  • He names no emperor, and no Nero. The two names are moral descriptions of the Antichrist's character, not the cipher of a Roman ruler. Primasius nowhere reaches the reading — recovered only in the nineteenth century — that 666 spells Nero Caesar.
