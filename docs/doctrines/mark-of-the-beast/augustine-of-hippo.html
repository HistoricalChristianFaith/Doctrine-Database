Augustine of Hippo on the Mark of the Beast and the Number 666

Dates: c. 420 · Wikipedia
Summary: With Augustine the gematria drops out entirely. Following the Donatist Tyconius, whose lost Exposition turned the Apocalypse into a drama of the two cities, Augustine reads the beast in City of God 20.9 as "the godless city as opposed to the city of God," its image as the hypocrites who bear "a false face" of faith, and the refusal of its mark on hand and forehead as the faithful's refusal of "the stigma of false faith and bad morals." He calculates no name for the number: the whole letter-counting enterprise is simply set aside for a moral and ecclesial reading.

Claims & evidence

The beast is the godless city; its image, the false-faced Christian

"As to the identity of the beast in question, there is need of very careful study. Nevertheless, consistently with sound faith, one may take it to be the godless city as opposed to the city of God, men and women without faith as opposed to those who believe. The beast's 'image,' I think, is his deception as found, for example, in such people as profess the faith yet live like pagans. For they pretend to be what, in fact, they are not, and are called Christians, not because of full faith but of false face."

— Augustine, City of God 20.9. Augustine takes the beast not as a coming individual to be deciphered but as the corporate civitas terrena — the whole society of the unbelieving set against the city of God — and its "image" as the counterfeit believer within the church: those who wear the name Christian over a pagan life, "a false face" rather than full faith.

The refused mark is the stigma of false faith and bad morals

"Those who do not follow the beast or his image are surely those who follow Paul's admonition: 'Do not bear the yoke with unbelievers.' Their not worshiping means their not agreeing with, their not becoming subjects to, unbelievers. Their not accepting his 'mark' upon their 'foreheads' and 'hands' means that they refuse the stigma of false faith and bad morals. Such people, alive or dead, keep themselves aloof from such evils and so reign with Christ, even now."

— Augustine, City of God 20.9. The mark is read wholly spiritually: not a brand or an imperial stamp but the inward "stigma of false faith and bad morals." Its two placements are moralized — the forehead for confession, the hand for works — so that refusing the mark simply is refusing to consent to unbelief. There is no number to compute here at all; the passage has become an exhortation to persevere in the true faith.

Notes

  • The number is set aside, not solved. Where the Greek and Latin commentators piled up candidate names each summing to 666, Augustine calculates none. On the Tyconian reading the number is not a cipher for a person but part of a symbolic portrait of the two cities, so the gematria that occupied Irenaeus, Hippolytus, Victorinus, Oecumenius and Primasius has no work to do. This spiritual line, not the name-hunt, is what would dominate Western exegesis for a millennium.
  • The Tyconian frame. Augustine's reading descends from the Donatist exegete Tyconius (c. 385), whose Exposition of the Apocalypse is lost but survives in its Catholic borrowers. Tyconius already took the beast's seven heads "not in regard to number but as a sign of the universality of that earthly kingdom that is hostile to the Lamb" and read the beast corporately — the seedbed of Augustine's two-cities interpretation (Tyconius, Commentary on the Apocalypse, on 13:3–4, trans. Francis X. Gumerlock and David C. Robinson, Fathers of the Church 134 (Washington, DC: Catholic University of America Press, 2017)).
  • Caesarius of Arles carries the same line. A century after Augustine, Caesarius of Arles (c. 542) restates the reading almost word for word: the beast is "the city of the impious itself… called 'Babylon,' which is interpreted as 'Confusion,'" and its image is "an imitation" among "those people who confess the catholic faith but live the life of infidelity. For they feign to be what they are not, and they are called Christians, not by way of the true image but by way of a false image" — the mark refused "on their forehead, on account of the confession, and on their hand, on account of works" (Caesarius of Arles, Exposition on the Apocalypse, Homily 11, on 13:15–16, trans. William C. Weinrich, Latin Commentaries on Revelation, Ancient Christian Texts (Downers Grove, IL: IVP Academic, 2011)). The close verbal agreement shows a settled Tyconian-Augustinian tradition, not three independent readings.
  • He names no emperor — and no Nero. Like the Greek reticence tradition, but for a different reason, Augustine identifies the beast with no historical ruler: the corporate reading makes the question of which man beside the point. On the various decipherments, see the mark-of-the-beast timeline.
