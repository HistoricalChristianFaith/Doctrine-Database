Does the 616 variant confirm the Nero cipher?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: plausible — the well-known variant that reads the number as 616 rather than 666 is elegantly explained as the same name, Nero Caesar, in its shorter Latin spelling (Nero without the final Hebrew nun = 50), so that the two numbers become one name in two languages — an ancient manuscript "check" that corroborates the Nero identification. The reading is coherent and widely held, but it does not clinch: a real rival explains 616 of Gaius Caesar (Caligula), and the oldest witness we have (P115) reads 616, reopening the question of which number is even original. So it strengthens the Nero case without settling it. · the corroboration is argued by Metzger and Bauckham; the text-critical reopening by David Parker on the publication of P115

Related arguments: the Nero cipher — see also Is the number of the beast (666/616) the name "Nero Caesar"?

The claim

"…the Greek form Neron Caesar written in Hebrew characters (נרון קסר) is equivalent to 666, whereas the Latin form Nero Caesar (נרו קסר) is equivalent to 616."

— Bruce M. Metzger, A Textual Commentary on the Greek New Testament, on Revelation 13:18.1 The claim is that the variant is not a rival reading so much as a rival spelling: whoever wrote 616 was ciphering the same emperor in Latin, and the coincidence that one name yields both attested numbers is what makes the Nero solution uniquely strong.

Who advances it

The corroboration argument is standard in the critical commentaries — Metzger's Textual Commentary notes the double equivalence, and Bauckham makes it the linchpin of his case that the beast is Nero. The variant itself became a live text-critical question again in 2000, when David Parker published P115 (P.Oxy. 4499) — the oldest and, on his assessment, textually best witness to this part of Revelation — which reads 616, prompting a renewed debate over whether 616 might be the original number and 666 the "improvement."2

The evidence for

The variant is ancient and well-attested, not a late curiosity. Irenaeus already knew and rejected 616 around 180, so it is at least second-century; it is now read in P115 (c. 225–275) and Codex Ephraemi Rescriptus (C, fifth century), against 666 in P47, Sinaiticus, Alexandrinus, and the mass of later witnesses. (Codex Vaticanus, sometimes carelessly cited here, is irrelevant — it does not contain Revelation at all.) On the Nero solution the two numbers are not a problem but a signature: Neron Qesar in Hebrew letters totals 666, and the Latin Nero — the form a Latin-speaker would use, dropping the final nun — totals 616. That a single name accounts for both readings, in the two languages of the empire, is a coincidence no rival candidate reproduces, and it is the strongest single piece of evidence that the number ciphers Nero at all.3

Adversarial assessment

What holds. As an explanation of why the tradition carries two numbers, the Nero reading is excellent. Competing solutions must treat 616 as a meaningless scribal slip; the Nero reading turns it into confirmation, and does so without special pleading, since the Latin spelling is exactly what a Latin milieu would produce. If the number is a name at all, its coming in two forms that resolve to one name is powerful.

What is contested. Two things stop the variant from clinching the case. First, the double solution is not the only account of 616. J. Neville Birdsall and others read 616, by a different gematria, as Gaius Caesar (Caligula) — a rival that, whatever its own merits, shows 616 does not point to Nero by necessity. Second, and more unsettling for the argument as usually framed: if P115 is the oldest and best witness and it reads 616, then 616 may be the original reading — in which case the "corroboration" runs backwards, with 666 as the later, Hebraizing adjustment. (Parker's own preference is reported inconsistently in the secondary literature, and P115's reading is partly reconstructed from a lacuna, so this should be held with care.) Either way, the variant cannot do more than corroborate: it is fully consistent with the Nero identification and is its best external support, but it presupposes that identification rather than proving it, and it leaves the priority of the two numbers genuinely open.4

Assessment

Plausible — leaning probable. The reading of 616 as the Latin spelling of the same Nero Caesar that yields 666 in Hebrew is coherent, economical, and the majority view, and it is the single best reason to think the number ciphers Nero at all. It stops short of settled because a real alternative reads 616 of Caligula, and because the discovery that the oldest witness reads 616 keeps the question of the original number open — so the variant strengthens the Nero identification it accompanies without independently proving it. Note that this, like its companion, bears on the beast's identity, not the book's date.5
