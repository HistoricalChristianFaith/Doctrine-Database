Hippolytus of Rome on the Mark of the Beast and the Number 666

Dates: c. 200 · Wikipedia
Summary: Hippolytus follows Irenaeus's candidate names for 666 but ventures his own, ΛΑΤΕΙΝΟΣ / Latinus, offered only conjecturally. Separately he reads the beast's mark literally — a compelled act of apostate sacrifice, prefigured by Antiochus Epiphanes — and solves the seal's number as the word "I deny."

Claims & evidence

The name is, conjecturally, Latinus

"Many names indeed we find, the letters of which are the equivalent of this number: such as, for instance, the word Titan, an ancient and notable name; or Evanthas, for it too makes up the same number; and many others which might be found."

"If, then, we take the name as the name of a single man, it becomes Latinus. Wherefore we ought neither to give it out as if this were certainly his name, nor again ignore the fact that he may not be otherwise designated."

— Hippolytus, Treatise on Christ and Antichrist §§48–50, trans. ANF, source. Hippolytus reproduces Irenaeus's short-list (Titan, Evanthas) but adds a candidate of his own — Latinus, the Latin or Roman kingdom — while being careful to mark it as a guess rather than a certainty.

The mark is compelled sacrifice, on the type of Antiochus

"For, being full of guile, and exalting himself against the servants of God, with the wish to afflict them and persecute them out of the world, because they give not glory to him, he will order incense-pans to be set up by all everywhere, that no man among the saints may be able to buy or sell without first sacrificing; for this is what is meant by the mark received upon the right hand. And the word—'in their forehead'—indicates that all are crowned, and put on a crown of fire, and not of life, but of death. For in this wise, too, did Antiochus Epiphanes the king of Syria, the descendant of Alexander of Macedon, devise measures against the Jews."

— Hippolytus, Treatise on Christ and Antichrist §49, trans. ANF, source. The mark is taken concretely: not a secret brand but the public, coerced act of offering incense — buying and selling barred to those who will not sacrifice — on the model of Antiochus IV, whose decree that all sacrifice or die (1–2 Maccabees) Hippolytus reads as the type of the Antichrist's compulsion.

The seal's number is the word "I deny"

"And his seal upon the forehead and upon the right hand is the number, 'Six hundred threescore and six.' And I have an opinion as to this number, though I do not know the matter for certain; for many names have been found in this number when it is expressed in writing. Still we say that perhaps the scription of this same seal will give us the word I deny. For even in recent days, by means of his ministers-that is to say, the idolaters-that bitter adversary took up the word deny, when the lawless pressed upon the witnesses of Christ, with the adjuration, 'Deny thy God, the crucified One.'"

"Of such kind, in the time of that hater of all good, will be the seal, the tenor of which will be this: I deny the Maker of heaven and earth, I deny the baptism, I deny my (former) service, and attach myself to thee, and I believe in thee."

— Hippolytus, Dubious and Spurious Pieces (Fragments on Revelation), trans. ANF, source. Here the number is solved not by a proper name but by a renunciation formula: the seal is the apostate's spoken denial of God, baptism, and former service — the very words the persecutors extracted from the confessors.

Notes

  • A future, personal Antichrist — not Nero. Like Irenaeus, Hippolytus expects a future individual Antichrist and does not identify him with Nero or any past emperor. His "Latinus" is the Latin / Roman-kingdom reading of the number — the same candidate later re-pointed by Reformation polemic at the papacy, though Hippolytus himself means the Roman power, not the bishop of Rome.
  • On the source of the "I deny" solution. Hippolytus's genuine Treatise on Christ and Antichrist (the Latinus and Antiochus passages) is cited above from the Ante-Nicene Fathers edition online. The "I deny" solution, by contrast, comes from a piece printed among the Dubious and Spurious fragments attached to his name — so it carries less weight as securely Hippolytan, and is flagged as such here.
  • The "I deny" reading has a numerical afterlife. The idea that 666 spells a word of denial rather than a name resurfaces later and is worked out arithmetically — in Primasius (ΑΡΝΟΥΜΕ, "I deny") and in Bede — making Hippolytus an early witness to this whole family of solutions on the mark-of-the-beast timeline.
