Arethas of Caesarea on the Mark of the Beast and the Number 666

Dates: c. 900 · Wikipedia
Summary: The scholar-archbishop Arethas of Caesarea carries the Greek tradition into the tenth century by reworking the commentary of Andreas of Caesarea. He prunes the inherited catalogue of descriptive names that each sum to 666, keeping the familiar candidates — Teitan, Lateinos, "the victor" — but adds no new identification of his own and endorses his predecessors' reticence: the true name is reserved, not to be fixed. With Arethas the Byzantine reading stabilizes into a settled canon of options, none asserted.

Claims & evidence

A reworking of Andreas, pruning the descriptive-name list

Arethas's scholia on the Apocalypse are largely a re-edition of Andreas of Caesarea, whom he abridges and rearranges. On 13:18 he transmits the descriptive-name method the Greek commentators had used since Oecumenius — epithets whose letters total 666, such as Teitan, Lateinos, and Ο Νικητης ("the victor") — while trimming the list he inherited rather than lengthening it. He proposes no fresh candidate and adds no new decipherment of his own.1

The name is reserved: the reticence tradition endorsed

With the list pruned, Arethas keeps Andreas's principle intact: the beast's name is providentially withheld, and the interpreter should not presume to supply it. He rehearses the candidates only to leave them open, so that the Byzantine reading settles into a stable canon of possibilities held in suspense — the mature form of the Greek refusal to name a single historical figure.2

Notes

  • Web-sourced. Arethas's scholia are edited in Patrologia Graeca 106; the account of his relation to Andreas and Oecumenius, and of his handling of the descriptive names, follows the survey in Max Nelson, "A Concealed Claudian," cited below. No firsthand English rendering of his note on 13:18 is quoted here.
  • He names no emperor, and no Nero. Like Oecumenius and Andreas before him, Arethas identifies the beast with no Roman ruler. The candidate names remain descriptive epithets left unassigned, never the cipher of Nero Caesar that modern criticism would recover a thousand years later.
