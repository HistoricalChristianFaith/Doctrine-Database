Heinrich Bullinger on the Mark of the Beast and the Number 666

Dates: c. 1557 (Latin, Basel; English trans. 1561) · Wikipedia
Summary: Zwingli's successor at Zürich and, in his hundred sermons on the Apocalypse (Latin 1557; English 1561), one of the fullest early statements of the Reformation reading of Revelation 13. Bullinger takes over Irenaeus's ancient candidate Lateinos — "the Latin kingdom" — and re-points it from pagan Rome to papal Rome: the mark of the beast is submission to the Pope, and the 666 that spells "Latin" fits the church that does everything in the Latin tongue.

Claims & evidence

The mark of the beast is submission to the Pope

"There followeth againe of the fierſe crueltie, and blouddy tiranny, which Antichriſt practiſeth agaynſt the Chriſtians, that is to ſaye agaynſte them whiche will not receyue the marke of the beaſte: that is, whiche will not proſtitute themſelues to the luſte of the Pope, and the ſeate of peſtilēce.… For he hath the marke of the beaſt, which acknowledgeth the ſeate, and profeſſeth the faith of Rome… He hath the name of the beaſte, who ſo euer he be that will be named an obedient childe of the holy See of Rome, and acknowledgeth the Pope to be head of the vniuerſall church."

— Heinrich Bullinger, A Hundred Sermons vpon the Apocalips of Iesu Christe (trans. John Daus; London: John Day, 1561), Sermon 61 ("Of the marke and numbre of the name of the beaſte"), on Revelation 13:16–18, fol. 424. Bullinger reads the buying-and-selling ban of 13:17 as the coercion of the papal see: to bear the mark is to acknowledge Rome and obey the Pope. (The long ſ is the original long-s; a mark over a vowel is the printer's contraction, e.g. peſtilēce = "pestilence.")

666 = Lateinos: the number of the Latin church

"…conteineth the nōbre of .666. And very like it is to be true.… For we ſee that the church of Rome is called the latin churche, and the Pope the high Biſſhop of the latin church. We ſee al ſeruice in churches ſayde in the latin tongue.… no man ſhall ſerue in this churche, excepte he be a latiniſte."

— Bullinger, A Hundred Sermons vpon the Apocalips, Sermon 61, fol. 428. The identification is Irenaeus's Greek name Λατεινος (Lateinos = 666), which Irenaeus had glossed as "the last kingdom… the Latins are they who at present bear rule." Bullinger keeps the arithmetic and the "Latin" gloss but turns the referent: not the pagan Roman empire but the Latin-rite papal church, whose services and whole polity are conducted in Latin.

Notes

  • The Reformation historicist line. Bullinger's reading became the common Protestant one. The Geneva Bible's note on 13:18 states flatly that 666 "signifieth Lateinus, or Latin, which noteth the Pope or Antichrist who useth in all things the Latin tongue"; John Napier's A Plaine Discovery (1593) and Joseph Mede's Key of the Revelation (Eng. 1643) built whole historicist systems on the same identification. The number had become a Protestant polemic.
  • An old name, re-aimed. Bullinger invents nothing arithmetically — Lateinos is as old as Irenaeus (c. 180). What is new is the target: the sixteenth-century Reformers read the "Latin kingdom" as the papacy rather than as Rome-the-empire. This makes the reading a re-application of an ancient cipher, not a fresh decipherment; its weakness is the weakness of all such gematria (many names total 666), and it competes with the modern Neron Qesar solution weighed at the Nero-Caesar argument.
  • He names no emperor. Like the fathers before him, Bullinger does not read Nero; unlike them, he fixes the beast on a living institution — the papal see — rather than leaving the name reserved. See the doctrine timeline: the mark of the beast and the number 666.
