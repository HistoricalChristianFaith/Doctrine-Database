Oecumenius on the Mark of the Beast and the Number 666

Dates: c. 550 · Wikipedia
Summary: In the earliest surviving Greek commentary on Revelation, Oecumenius reckons 666 by ordinary Greek isopsephy, defends the spelling ΤΕΙΤΑΝ, and lists many names and phrases that each total 666 — concluding that the fitting choice is left to God, the true name deliberately unrevealed. He originates a whole tradition of descriptive names for the beast, and he names no emperor.

Claims & evidence

666 is read by ordinary Greek reckoning; the candidate names are Lampetis, Benedictus, Titan

"Here it says is wisdom: let him calculate the number of the beast and through the calculation let him find it. For I will not say, it says, a foreign and unusual calculation, nor one named with concealment and doubling, but a calculation well-worn and known to men, which sums to the number six hundred sixty-six. This number also signifies many other proper and common names, and it also signifies these: as principal names Lampetis, Benedictus, Titan."

— Commentary on Revelation, on Revelation 13:18, source. Oecumenius reads 666 by plain Greek isopsephy — each letter's numeral value summed — not by any cryptic scheme, and he defends spelling Titan with the diphthong (ΤΕΙΤΑΝ), deriving it grammatically so the numbers still add to 666. Titan is the same guess Irenaeus had floated centuries earlier; alongside it Oecumenius offers Lampetis and Benedictus as further names that total the number.

Many descriptive names fit; the true one is left to God

"Then the unstable one, a wicked guide, truly harmful, ancient slanderer, an unjust lamb; for perhaps by these names he would be called by those opposing him.… Therefore, with many names having been found, the authority to attach the more fitting one rests with the one who wills it to the accursed one."

— Commentary on Revelation, on Revelation 13:18, source. Beyond the proper names, Oecumenius compiles a set of descriptive epithets that each total 666 — "the Victor," the unstable one, "a wicked guide," "ancient slanderer," "an unjust lamb" — and then declines to fix on any one of them: the fitting name, he says, is left to the will of God, the true name deliberately unrevealed. This reticence, not a confident identification, is his conclusion.

Notes

  • Oecumenius, not Andrew of Caesarea, originated the descriptive-name list. The catalogue of descriptive epithets each summing to 666 is often attributed to Andrew of Caesarea (Andreas), but it begins here with Oecumenius; Andreas and, later, Arethas took over and pruned the list rather than invented it. See Max Nelson, "A Concealed Claudian: The Meaning of 666 in Revelation," Journal of Theological Studies 76.1 (2025): 109–137, at Oxford Academic.
  • He names no emperor. Oecumenius stays with grammatical and descriptive candidates and consciously withholds a single identification, leaving the "more fitting" name to God. This inaugurates a reticence tradition in the Greek commentators — a deliberate refusal to settle 666 on one historical figure — and in particular he does not name Nero, the reading canvassed on the mark-of-the-beast timeline.
