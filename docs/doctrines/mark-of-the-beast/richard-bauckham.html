Richard Bauckham on the Mark of the Beast and the Number 666

Dates: 1993 (The Climax of Prophecy) · Wikipedia
Summary: The British New Testament scholar whose essay "Nomen Bestiae: The Number of the Beast" in The Climax of Prophecy (1993) is the standard modern treatment of the Nero solution. He takes 666 to be the gematria of Nero Caesar in Hebrew letters, judges it "eminently the most preferable" of the proposed solutions because it alone yields a name, and explains the 616 variant as the same name in its Latin spelling.

Claims & evidence

666 is Nero Caesar in Hebrew letters — the most preferable solution

"The solution to the riddle of 666 which has been most widely accepted since it was first suggested in 1831 is that 666 is the sum of the letters of Nero Caesar written in Hebrew characters as נרון קסר (נ = 50 + ר = 200 + ו = 6 + ן = 50 + ק = 100 + ס = 60 + ר = 200). Few of the many other solutions by gematria which have been proposed offer a name, which the phrase 'the number of his name' (Rev. 13:17; 15:2) requires, and of those few which do this seems eminently the most preferable."

— Richard Bauckham, The Climax of Prophecy: Studies on the Book of Revelation (Edinburgh: T&T Clark, 1993), 387. Bauckham's test is not that a number can be made to fit — many can — but that the text asks for a name ("the number of his name"), and that of the few solutions that actually produce one, Nero Caesar is the best.

The 616 variant is the same name in its Latin spelling

Bauckham accounts for the ancient variant 616 as the identical cipher in a different spelling: when the name is written in the Latin form Nero rather than the Greek/Hebrew Neron, the final Hebrew nun (ן = 50) drops away and the total falls from 666 to 616. One name, two spellings, both numbers the manuscripts preserve — the "double solution" that no rival candidate reproduces.
— Bauckham, The Climax of Prophecy, 384–390. (The primary volume is borrow-only online; the 616 explanation is given here in summary, the verbatim Neron Qesar statement above being the one reproduced in full. Bauckham argues at length, pp. 384–452, that the beast is modelled on Nero throughout Revelation.)

Notes

  • The representative modern witness. Bauckham's treatment is now the one most cited, and the identification he defends is held across the scholarly aisle: David Aune's Word commentary (1998) and Craig Koester's Anchor Yale (2014) read 666 the same way — notably, none of the three is an early-dater, so the reading does not depend on a Neronian date for the book. The full adjudication, with the objections, is at the Nero-Caesar argument; the variant is weighed at Does the 616 variant confirm the Nero cipher?
  • A name and a symbol. Bauckham does not treat the cipher and the number's symbolism as rivals: he reads 666 as Neron Qesar and as the 36th triangular number (36 being the 8th, tied to the beast as "the eighth," Rev 17:11). That both/and is the ground for taking the symbolic reading as a complement to the cipher rather than its replacement — see Is 666 symbolic rather than a name-cipher? (assessment: plausible).
  • Identity, not date. That the beast is Nero (the cipher, the "wounded head," the feared return) is, for Bauckham, a portrait drawn throughout the book; it is a separate step to infer that the book was therefore written under Nero. That dating inference is weighed — and found weak — on the date of Revelation page. See the doctrine timeline: the mark of the beast and the number 666.
