Bede on the Mark of the Beast and the Number 666

Dates: c. 730 · Wikipedia
Summary: In his Commentary on Revelation, Bede gathers the Greek and Latin candidate names for the beast — Titan, Antemos, Arnoume — into one place, then turns to the reading that would outlast the name-games: the symbolic one. Six is the number "by which the world was made," the counterfeit "perfection of the work," and 666 recalls the annual six hundred sixty-six talents of gold owed to Solomon — tribute due the true king that the "deceiving tyrant" will presume to usurp.

Claims & evidence

The Greek and Latin candidate names: Titan (giant), Antemos ("contrary to honor"), Arnoume ("I deny")

"Here is wisdom. Let him who has understanding, etc. This number is said to be found among the Greeks in the name Titan, that is, giant… Τ equals 300, Ε equals 5, Ι equals 10, Τ equals 300, Α equals 1, Ν equals 50. It is thought that the Antichrist will assume this name as if he surpasses all in power… Primasius also mentions another name that completes the same number: Α equals 1, Ν equals 50, Τ equals 300, Ε equals 5, Μ equals 40, Ο equals 70, Σ equals 200, which signifies 'contrary to honor'. Also, the word Α equals 1, Ρ equals 100, Ν equals 50, Ο equals 70, Υ equals 400, Μ equals 40, Ε equals 5, meaning 'I deny'."

— Commentary on Revelation (Expositio Apocalypseos), on Revelation 13:18, source. Bede credits the two Latin names — ANTEMOS ("contrary to honor") and ARNOUME ("I deny") — to Primasius; only the Greek TITAN carries the older weight going back to Irenaeus.

The symbolic reading: six as the perfection of the work, and Solomon's 666 talents

"…who does not know that the number six, by which the world was made, signifies the perfection of the work?… Moreover, the weight of gold that came to Solomon each year was six hundred sixty-six talents. Therefore, what is rightly owed and paid as tribute to the true king, that same tribute the deceiving tyrant will presume to demand."

— Commentary on Revelation (Expositio Apocalypseos), on Revelation 13:18, source. Rather than resting on any single cipher, Bede reads the number itself as a counterfeit: six is the number of the world's making, so 666 signals a false "perfection of the work," and the six hundred sixty-six talents brought yearly to Solomon (1 Kings 10:14) become the tribute owed the true king that the "deceiving tyrant" usurps.

Notes

  • The great synthesizer, and the clearest early witness to the symbolic reading. Bede draws the Latin tradition together — Primasius above all — but his lasting contribution here is to set the symbolic, non-cipher reading of 666 alongside the name-ciphers: the number as the counterfeit "perfection of the work." That is the line modern scholarship revives; G. K. Beale, for instance, argues the number is a symbol of unremitting incompleteness and failure rather than a name to decode. The reading is weighed at Is 666 symbolic — a triple falling-short of seven — rather than a name-cipher? (assessment: plausible). See the summary timeline.
  • Bede does not give "DICLUX." A common conflation credits Bede with the Latin cipher DICLUX (whose numeral letters total 666). He does not — that solution belongs to Victorinus of Pettau and to Beatus of Liébana. Bede's Latin names here are Antemos and Arnoume (from Primasius), not DICLUX.
  • Bede names no emperor. Like all the fathers who handle 13:18, Bede offers no imperial identification — not Nero, not any other — but works with generic candidate names and the symbolic sense. On the modern Nero-cipher case, see the argument page 666 as "Nero Caesar".
