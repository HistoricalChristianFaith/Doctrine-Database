Victorinus of Pettau on the Mark of the Beast and the Number 666

Dates: c. 300 (d. c. 304) · Wikipedia
Summary: In the earliest surviving verse-by-verse commentary on the Apocalypse, Victorinus prefers the Greek name τειταν (Teitan — "which the Gentiles call Sol and Phœbus") and converts it by antiphrasis into the Latin cipher DICLUX (D+I+C+L+V+X = 666, from dic lux, "say light"), since Antichrist "transforms himself into an angel of light."

Claims & evidence

Teitan is the likeliest name

"As they have it reckoned from the Greek characters, they thus find it among many to be τειταν, for τειταν has this number, which the Gentiles call Sol and Phœbus; and it is reckoned in Greek thus: τ three hundred, ε five, ι ten, τ three hundred, α one, ν fifty — which taken together become six hundred and sixty-six. For as far as belongs to the Greek letters, they fill up this number and name."

— Commentary on the Apocalypse of the Blessed John, on Revelation 13:18, trans. ANF, source. Teitan (the sun-god, Sol/Phœbus) had already been floated as a solution in the second century; Victorinus is our earliest verse-commentary to record it, treating it as the most fitting of the names that tally to 666.

By antiphrasis, the Latin cipher DICLUX

"which name if you wish to turn into Latin, it is understood by the antiphrase DICLUX, which letters are reckoned in this manner: since D figures five hundred, I one, C a hundred, L fifty, V five, X ten — which by the reckoning up of the letters makes similarly six hundred and sixty-six, that is, what in Greek gives τειταν, to wit, what in Latin is called DICLUX; by which name, expressed by antiphrases, we understand Antichrist, who, although he be cut off from the supernal light, and deprived thereof, yet transforms himself into an angel of light, daring to call himself light."

— Commentary on the Apocalypse of the Blessed John, on Revelation 13:18, trans. ANF, source. DICLUX is read as the Latin dic lux, "say light" — a name Antichrist claims for himself by antiphrasis (calling himself the opposite of what he is). Its six letters are Roman numerals that sum to the required total: D (500) + I (1) + C (100) + L (50) + V (5) + X (10) = 666.

Notes

  • The earliest surviving Apocalypse commentary. Writing in Latin at the end of the third century, Victorinus is the first to comment on Revelation verse by verse. He reads the number as belonging to a future, personal Antichrist, and — unlike the later Nero-Caesar solution — in solving 666 he does not reach for Nero, choosing instead a name (Teitan / DICLUX) for the coming deceiver.
  • Text-critical caveat: Genseric and Antemos are later interpolations, not Victorinus. The received text goes on to offer two further names that tally to 666 — a "certain Greek codex" reading αντεμος (Antemos) and "another name in Gothic," γενσήρικος (Genseric). These are demonstrably later additions, not the words of Victorinus: Genseric the Vandal sacked Rome in 455, roughly a century and a half after Victorinus died c. 304, and the naming of a fifth-century Gothic king cannot come from a third-century hand. They belong to the later recension of the commentary (Jerome revised Victorinus's text) rather than to the author himself. The ANF editor draws the same conclusion: "if the Victorinus mentioned by Jerome be the same as our author, the mention of Genseric proves the subsequent interpolation of his works" (General Notes by the American Editor, ANF Vol. 7). Credit Teitan/DICLUX to Victorinus; do not credit him with Genseric or Antemos.
  • See also Victorinus's page on the date of Revelation: the same commentary is the first witness to attach John's vision, not merely his presence on Patmos, to Domitian by name.
