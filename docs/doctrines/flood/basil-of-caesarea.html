Basil of Caesarea on the Historicity of Noah's Flood

Dates: c. 375 (On the Holy Spirit; the letter to Optimus c. 377; d. 379) · Wikipedia · back to timeline
Summary: Basil never wrote a treatise on the Flood — his Hexaemeron stops at the sixth day and never reaches Noah — so his witness is the more telling for being incidental: in passing, in a Trinitarian treatise and an exegetical letter, the great Cappadocian simply assumes the Flood as a real, datable, worldwide event. In On the Holy Spirit he uses "what happened in Noah's time" and the destruction of Sodom as fixed chronological markers in real history, ordered by their distance from the present. In his letter to Optimus he reads the generations "from Cain to the flood" as a literal count and treats the deluge as a real punishment "brought on the whole earth, because sin was everywhere spread abroad" — contrasting that bygone cataclysm with Christ, who "takes away the sin of the world." The Flood is never argued for; it is the common ground from which he argues.

Claims & evidence

The Flood was a real punishment "brought on the whole earth," set in a literal generational chronology

There are, however, some who have gone so far as the following explanation, which does not jar with the doctrine of the Church; from Cain to the flood, they say, seven generations passed by, and the punishment was brought on the whole earth, because sin was everywhere spread abroad. But the sin of Lamech requires for its cure not a Flood, but Him Who Himself takes away the sin of the world.

— Letter 260.5 (to Optimus the bishop, c. 377), NPNF² 8, historicalchristian.faith. Answering Optimus's questions on hard verses of Genesis and Luke, Basil glosses Lamech's "seventy and sevenfold" (Gen 4:24). He reports — with approval, as a reading that "does not jar with the doctrine of the Church" — that the seven generations counted "from Cain to the flood" are matched by the sevenfold avenging, the Flood itself being the "punishment brought on the whole earth" once "sin was everywhere spread abroad." The deluge is treated as a real, worldwide judgment standing at a fixed point in an exact generational chronology (Basil goes straight on to "count the generations from Adam to the coming of Christ… according to the genealogy of Luke," finding Christ born in the seventy-seventh). The Flood's reality and its universal reach are not in question; they are the premise of the exegesis, and the foil for the Cross — Lamech's sin needs not "a Flood, but Him Who… takes away the sin of the world."

"What happened in Noah's time" is a fixed point of history, ordered before Sodom

For instance, what happened in Noah's time is prior to what happened to the men of Sodom, inasmuch as Noah is more remote from our own day; and, again, the events of the history of the men of Sodom are posterior, because they seem in a sense to approach nearer to our own day.

— On the Holy Spirit 6 (c. 375), NPNF² 8, historicalchristian.faith. The setting could hardly be further from flood-apologetics: Basil is arguing that the Son is not "later" than the Father, and that temporal "before/after" cannot be predicated of the eternal generation. To illustrate ordinary temporal priority he reaches, as for the most obvious of examples, to the Flood and Sodom — "what happened in Noah's time" and "the events of the history of the men of Sodom" — events whose pastness and order he and his readers take entirely for granted. That the Flood serves here as a self-evidently historical reference point, in a treatise with no stake in the question, is exactly its weight: for Basil the deluge is not a thesis to be defended but a date everyone knows.

Notes

  • Basil's Hexaemeron, the nine homilies on the six days of creation (c. 378), never reaches the Flood — it closes with the land animals of the sixth day — so the patristic survey-lists that file Basil among the Genesis "literalists" cannot rest his Flood-witness on it. The actual evidence lies off to the side, in the letter to Optimus and On the Holy Spirit, where the Flood appears not as a topic but as an assumption. (His brother Gregory of Nyssa and friend Gregory of Nazianzus round out the Cappadocian circle that read Genesis the same way.)
  • He bears on the historic-consensus thesis (assessment: sound) precisely because his testimony is incidental: a leading fourth-century Greek theologian, in works on the Trinity and on the exegesis of Genesis, treats the Flood as a real, worldwide, datable event without pausing to argue it — the historicity simply assumed, East as well as West, and so deeply assumed that it can do duty as a fixed marker in arguments about something else entirely.
  • On extent he weighs, lightly, toward the period's default: the Flood is the "punishment brought on the whole earth" — bearing on geographic globality vs. anthropological universality (assessment: contested), though the phrase carries the worldwide picture in passing, as the moral totality of the judgment ("sin was everywhere spread abroad"), not as a separately argued geographic thesis.
