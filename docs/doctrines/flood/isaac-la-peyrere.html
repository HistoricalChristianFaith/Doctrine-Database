Isaac La Peyrère on the Historicity of Noah's Flood

Dates: c. 1655 (lived 1596–1676) · Wikipedia
Summary: La Peyrère, a French Calvinist scholar and secretary to the Prince de Condé, is the first denier of the universal Flood on this timeline — the hinge the "who first denied the Flood?" question turns on. In Prae-Adamitae (Amsterdam, 1655; English Men before Adam, 1656) he argued that men existed before Adam — Adam being the father of the Jews only, not of all mankind — and drew from it the consequence that the Deluge judged the Jews' sins alone and overflowed only "Palestine, and the Land of the Jews," leaving the pre-Adamite Gentile nations of the wider world untouched. He denied the Flood's universality, not its reality: a real Noah and a real (local) Deluge remained. The book was condemned and publicly burned at Paris in 1656; La Peyrère was arrested, recanted before Pope Alexander VII, and converted to Catholicism (1657), but his method — corrupt biblical texts, denial that Moses wrote a universal history — fed Spinoza, Richard Simon, and the rise of higher criticism.

Claims & evidence

The Flood overflowed "only Palestine, and the Land of the Jews," not the whole earth

Give us leave to discusse this last miracle of the flood of Noah, which is believ'd to have overflow'd the kingdoms of the earth with most mighty overflowings, and which I rather believe overflow'd only Palestine, and the Land of the Jews. The causes of this conjecture are chiefly, the causes of the Deluge…

— A Theological System upon that Presupposition, that Men were before Adam (in Men before Adam, London, 1656), Book 4, ch. 7, p. 239, Internet Archive. La Peyrère opens the chapter — headed "That the Flood of Noah was not upon the whole earth, but only upon the Land of the Jews. Not to destroy all men, but only the Jews" — by making the Flood turn on its cause: the sins for which it was sent were the Jews' sins, so the judgment fell only where those sinners were. He states the thesis flatly a few pages on: "if we will take notice of the thing and not of the words, it will appear, that the Deluge came only upon the Land of the Jews, and not upon the whole world."1

Because there were men before Adam: Adam "the first and father of the Jews, not of all men"

…the design of Moses in setting down Adam, was not to mention the first of mankinde, but the Father of the Jews, whose peculiar Historie he wrote, and not the History of all Nations.

— A Theological System, Book 3, p. 218 (cf. the chapter heading, p. 135: "Adam was the first and father of the Jews, not of all men"), Internet Archive. This is the root of the local Flood. If Adam fathered the Jews only and Gentile nations already peopled the earth, then the Flood — a punishment of the Jews — need not, and on La Peyrère's reading did not, reach them: "God did not that time send the fear of the Jews upon the Nations that dwelt in China, America, the South, or Greenland." He reads the universal-sounding language of Genesis 7 as the "high stile" of a writer describing his own land — "the high mountains of Palestine… the whole heaven, was that which covered Palestine" — since "it is ordinary to assign every Country its own heaven."2

The Deluge was "peculiar to the Jews, not universal in all Nations"

It is clear, that this Deluge was peculiar to the Jews, not universal in all Nations, by that which is written of the Dove, which Noe sent out, and she return'd to him at night.

— A Theological System, Book 4, ch. 8, p. 244, Internet Archive. His props are exegetical and physical: the dove returned with a green olive branch that a year-long, mud-burying worldwide flood could not have spared, so (he argues) it "flew over all the waters of the Deluge, and in the higher fields of Asia" found the branch on land the flood never reached; and the "natural descent of waters" from the ark's resting-place tells the same way. The event is real — a real Noah, a real ark, real waters — but bounded to one region. He is thus the first to break the universal reading while still affirming the Deluge itself: he denies its universality, not its reality.3

Notes

  • La Peyrère is the first denier of the universal Flood on this timeline — after roughly fifteen centuries in which no Father, Doctor, or scholastic had denied the historical, humanity-destroying Deluge. He is the hinge behind the argument that the first systematic, influential break with the universal reading is early-modern, not patristic: see Who first denied the historical Flood — and is the first real denial early-modern, not patristic? (assessment: sound), and the historic-consensus thesis (assessment: sound), whose hinge his 1655 local flood marks.
  • His denial is of universality, not reality. He still affirmed a real Noah and a real Deluge — only a local one — so the outright denial that any such flood happened (the Flood as pure legend) is a later, eighteenth- and nineteenth-century development out of comparative mythology and higher criticism, currents his own work helped seed. This pushes the denial of the Flood's historicity even further from antiquity.
  • His local flood must not be confused with the ancient, orthodox limiting of the Flood's extent. Writers like Augustine (reporting the Olympus exemption), Cajetan, and Tostatus exempted particular regions from the waters while affirming a worldwide, all-men-destroying Deluge — a refinement of the geography weighed on the extent argument (assessment: contested). La Peyrère's move is different in kind: not an exemption at the margins but a Flood confined to one nation, sparing whole peoples of non-Adamic men — a position with no ancient parallel, and one that only his pre-Adamite frame makes possible. The patristic allegorists, by contrast, layered figure atop an affirmed history and denied nothing: allegory was layered on history, not a denial of it (assessment: sound).
  • A near-contemporary, Isaac Vossius (1618–1689), reached a local, non-universal flood by an independent route in his Dissertatio de vera aetate mundi (The Hague, 1659) and the writings around it — arguing from the longer Septuagint chronology and the reported antiquity of China — so that Martin Schook wrote a whole treatise (1662) against his "local and partial deluge." The two Isaacs together mark the seventeenth-century emergence of radical biblical criticism.
  • The reception is itself evidence of how novel the denial was: the book was condemned and publicly burned at Paris in 1656, its author imprisoned some six months in the Spanish Netherlands, then made to recant before Pope Alexander VII and receive Catholic communion (1657). That so radical a thesis had to be invented, was burned at once, and required dismantling the whole frame of sacred history to reach a local flood is exactly what one expects of the first serious denial — not of a reading with any patristic pedigree.
