Jerome on the Historicity of Noah's Flood

Dates: c. 392 · Wikipedia
Summary: Jerome reads the Flood as real, dated history, and reads it as a philologist — his chief flood text, the Hebrew Questions on Genesis (Quaestiones Hebraicae in Genesim, c. 391–393), works the narrative verse by verse against the Hebrew. His signature contribution is a correction of a popular error: the "hundred and twenty years" of Genesis 6:3 were not a shortening of the human lifespan ("as many wrongly assert") but a real reprieve granted to a real generation for repentance — a point he proves from the real lifespans men still reached after the Flood (Abraham 175 years, others 200 and 300), and which he says God cut short by twenty years, bringing the Flood in the hundredth year. He treats the ark's construction, the waters returning to "the hidden veins of the abyss," and the raven and dove as a real sequence of real events, and — like Ambrose and Augustine — lays figure atop the history: the ark's three hundred cubits are the cross (the letter T), the eight souls saved the "octave" of baptism (1 Pet 3:20–21). On the disputed identity of the "sons of God" his philology leans toward the angelic reading, but that is a question within the history, not a doubt about it.

Claims & evidence

The 120 years of Genesis 6:3 were a real reprieve for repentance — not a contraction of the human lifespan

Furthermore, so that it does not appear cruel on His part for not giving a place of repentance to sinners, He added: But their days shall be one hundred and twenty years. This is, they will have 120 years to do penance. Therefore, human life, as many err, is not condensed into 120 years: but to that generation, 120 years were given for penance. For we find that after the flood, Abraham lived for 175 years, and others for over 200 and 300 years. But because they refused to do penance, God did not want to wait for the appointed time: but with 20 years cut off, He brought about the flood in the 100th year designated for doing penance.

— Hebrew Questions on Genesis, on Gen 6:3, historicalchristian.faith. This is the argument of a man treating the chronology as fact: the 120 years are a real span of real calendar time, answerable against the real post-Flood ages of real men, and God's cutting the term to 100 years is a datable adjustment to a datable judgment. Jerome makes the same historical reading elsewhere, telling Daniel's reader that "prior to the Deluge one hundred and twenty years were appointed for men to come to repentance… and inasmuch as they refused to repent even within so long an interval… God did not wait for the remaining twenty years… but brought on the punishment earlier."1

Noah was a real man, "righteous in his generation" — relative, not perfected, righteousness

Noah was a righteous and blameless man in his generation, pleasing to God. Specifically, it says 'in his generation' to show that his righteousness was not according to the perfected righteousness, but according to the righteousness of his generation. And this is what is said in Hebrew: Noah was a righteous man, blameless in his generations, he walked with God; that is, he followed in his footsteps.

— Hebrew Questions on Genesis, on Gen 6:9, historicalchristian.faith. Jerome weighs the exact force of "in his generation" — a datum only worth adjudicating if Noah is a historical figure in a historical setting, measured against the men actually alive with him.2

The ark's construction and the receding waters are worked as real detail against the Hebrew

(Verse 16.) Assembling, you shall make an ark, and you shall finish it with a cubit from above. As for the reason why it says, assembling, you shall make an ark, in Hebrew it has, 'you shall make the middle of the ark,' which Symmachus interpreted more clearly, saying 'transparent,' that is, making the ark clear, intending for a window to be understood.

— Hebrew Questions on Genesis, on Gen 6:16 (cf. 6:14, the "square wooden planks… coated with pitch in Hebrew"), historicalchristian.faith. Jerome does the same for the abating of the Flood, tracing the water back into the earth by way of Ecclesiastes: "the closed and sealed fountains were opened… all waters and torrents return to the hidden veins of the abyss" (on Gen 8:2), and tracks the sending of the raven and the dove "after forty days" (on Gen 8:6). He reads the ark and its Flood as a mechanism with real parts and a real timetable, correcting the Latin against the Hebrew as he goes.3

Figure atop the history: the ark's numbers are the cross, penance, and Christ

We read in Genesis that the ark that Noah built was three hundred cubits long, fifty cubits wide and thirty cubits high. Notice the mystical significance of the numbers. In the number fifty, penance is symbolized because the fiftieth psalm of King David is the prayer of his repentance. Three hundred contains the symbol of crucifixion. The letter T is the sign for three hundred, whence Ezekiel says, "Mark THAV on the forehead of those who moan"… we repent in the number fifty; then, through penance, we arrive at the mystery of the cross… As a matter of fact, when Jesus was baptized, according to Luke, "he was thirty years of age."

— Jerome, Homily 84 (on the ark, Gen 6:14), as gathered in Ancient Christian Commentary on Scripture: Genesis 1–11, ed. Andrew Louth (InterVarsity, 2001); the passage displays at historicalchristian.faith. This is the same figure-atop-history reading Ambrose and Augustine give the ark's three hundred cubits — the Greek tau, shaped like a cross, standing for 300 — but Jerome draws it, as they do, from the real dimensions of a real vessel, not in place of them.4

The eight souls saved are the "octave" of baptism (1 Peter 3:20–21)

[We see that] certain psalms are titled "for the octave." This is the day on which the synagogue comes to an end and the church is born. This is the day in the number of which eight souls were preserved in the ark of Noah, and "its counterpart, the church," says Peter, "now saves you."

— Jerome, Homily 93 (on Gen 7:13), displayed at historicalchristian.faith. Jerome reaches the New Testament's own flood-typology — Noah's eight souls carried through the water as the figure of the baptism that "now saves you" (1 Pet 3:20–21). The figure presupposes the eight real persons and the real ark it reads; the type is layered on the history, not substituted for it.5

The "sons of God" (Gen 6:2, 4): Jerome's philology leans angelic — a question within the history

The Hebrew word Elohim, is of common gender: and both God and gods are called by this name. Hence, Aquila, in the plural number, dared to call them Sons of gods, understanding them to mean the Holy Ones or Angels… Therefore, Symmachus, following a similar interpretation, says: The sons of the powerful saw the daughters of men… And it is fitting for the name of falling ones to apply to angels and the children of the saints.

— Hebrew Questions on Genesis, on Gen 6:2 and 6:4, historicalchristian.faith. Where Chrysostom insists the "sons of God" were the historical Sethites and calls the angelic reading "fanciful," Jerome's survey of Aquila and Symmachus inclines the other way — but the dispute is over the identity of the actors within the Flood narrative, not over whether it happened. His fuller treatment of this question is on the nephilim timeline; here it only marks that a witness can read the pre-Flood generation one way and still read the Flood itself as real, dated history.6

Notes

  • Jerome slots on the timeline between John Chrysostom (c. 388), the Antiochene literalist, and Augustine (c. 417). The anchor date c. 392 tracks his primary flood text, the Hebrew Questions on Genesis; the two homilies (the ark's numbers, the octave of baptism) are later, from his Bethlehem preaching, and only reinforce the same figure-atop-history pattern.
  • He belongs with the "allegorists who keep the history" — Origen, Ambrose, and Augustine — on the argument Was patristic allegory of the Flood a denial of its history? (assessment: sound). Jerome's ark-numbers-as-cross reading is as figurative as any, yet it is drawn out of the real dimensions of a real ark, and his labor over the chronology of Genesis 6–8 in the Hebrew Questions is the labor of a man reading historia.
  • His correction of the 120-years error — that Genesis 6:3 grants a real generation a real reprieve, proved by the real ages men reached after the Flood — is a pointed instance of the historic-consensus thesis (assessment: sound): the pre-modern interpreters, philologists included, read the Flood as a datable event in ordinary time, not a myth to be dissolved.
  • On the identity of the "sons of God" Jerome leans angelic (via Aquila's "sons of gods" and Symmachus's "sons of the powerful"), against Chrysostom's strict Sethite reading — see his nephilim detail page. This is a divergence inside the shared conviction that the narrative is history: the two Fathers dispute who the actors were, not whether the Flood occurred.
