The Formula of Reunion on Theotokos

Dates: 433 (the reconciliation of Cyril of Alexandria with John of Antioch and the Eastern bishops, two years after Ephesus) · Wikipedia
Summary: Ephesus (431) had deposed Nestorius but left Cyril and the Antiochene bishops under John of Antioch in mutual excommunication. The Formula of Reunion — an Antiochene creed that Cyril accepted in his letter Laetentur caeli (“Let the heavens rejoice”) — healed the schism. It is the pivotal datum for this timeline because it shows the very party that had resisted the word’s imposition now confessing “Theotokos” in a jointly agreed creed — and doing so in language (“a union of two natures”) that pre-figures Chalcedon and vindicates the legitimate half of the Antiochene concern.

Claims & evidence

The reunited churches confess Mary “Mother of God”

We confess, therefore, our Lord Jesus Christ, the Only Begotten Son of God, perfect God, and perfect Man of a reasonable soul and flesh consisting; begotten before the ages of the Father according to his Divinity, and in the last days, for us and for our salvation, of Mary the Virgin according to his humanity, of the same substance with his Father according to his Divinity, and of the same substance with us according to his humanity; for there became a union of two natures. Wherefore we confess one Christ, one Son, one Lord.

According to this understanding of this unmixed union, we confess the holy Virgin to be Mother of God; because God the Word was incarnate and became Man, and from this conception he united the temple taken from her with himself.

— the Formula of Reunion, embedded in Cyril’s Letter to John of Antioch, source. The creed the Eastern bishops had drafted — and which Cyril now subscribed — states “Theotokos” outright, but frames it exactly as the title had always been meant on this timeline: Mary is called Mother of God because the Word was incarnate of her, not because the Godhead took its origin from her. The characteristically Antiochene “temple” (naos) image — the humanity as the temple the Word “took from her” — is kept, but it is now yoked to a single subject (“one Christ, one Son, one Lord”) rather than pointing to two.

The disputed word accepted by the party that had resisted it

The letter opens on Cyril’s note of jubilation — “Let the heavens rejoice, and let the earth be glad” — because the schism between Alexandria and Antioch was over.1 What makes the moment decisive for the history of “Theotokos” is who was now confessing it. The Antiochenes — John of Antioch, and with him Theodoret of Cyrus, who had written a point-by-point Reproof of Cyril’s anathemas — had objected not to Mary’s dignity but to a term they feared confused the natures. In the Formula they accept the word within a creed that also secures their own legitimate worry: that the divine and human be neither divided into two sons nor blurred into one nature.

The two-natures language that pre-figures Chalcedon

make some things of the Evangelical and Apostolic teaching about the Lord common as pertaining to the one person, and other things they divide as to the two natures, and attribute the worthy ones to God on account of the Divinity of Christ, and the lowly ones on account of his humanity.

— the same letter. This rule for reading Scripture — refer the exalted sayings to the Divinity, the lowly to the humanity, while keeping one person — is precisely the qualification that Chalcedon (451) would write into its Definition (“Mother of God according to his manhood”) and that Leo’s Tome (449) would press. The Formula is the hinge at which the two Christologies converge on a common confession of the title.

Notes

  • The Formula honours the concern raised by Nestorius and the Antiochenes without vindicating his refusal of the word: it keeps “Theotokos” while guarding against the Godhead “originating” from Mary. See the argument Was Nestorius condemned for a Christology he did not hold? — the Formula is the concrete settlement in which the Antiochene concern was honoured (as it would be again at Chalcedon’s “according to his manhood”) even as the disputed word stood.
  • Nestorius himself was not restored by the reunion; the price of peace, for some Antiochenes, was assent to his deposition. Theodoret, a chief drafter of the Formula, would be rehabilitated at Chalcedon only after anathematizing Nestorius by name.
  • Scholars debate whether Laetentur caeli marks a real shift in Cyril’s Christology toward two-natures language or chiefly an act of ecclesiastical peace-making; either way, its plain effect on this timeline is that the title “Theotokos” passed from a contested Alexandrian slogan into a creed jointly owned East and West.
