Ambrose of Milan on Theotokos

Dates: c. 380 (the treatise On the Faith [De Fide], addressed to the Emperor Gratian, written c. 378–380) · bishop of Milan c. 374–397 · Wikipedia
Summary: Ambrose, the great Latin bishop, gives the Western, pre-Ephesus statement of the God-bearing point — and states it with a precision that anticipates the councils. Expounding Paul’s “God sent His Son, made of a woman” (Galatians 4:4) against the Arians, he insists the one born of Mary is the Father’s own Son, whose generation is eternal — that is, God — while carefully locating the birth “not of the Godhead, but of the putting on of a body.” That distinction is exactly the one Cyril and Chalcedon would later make to guard the title Theotokos.

Claims & evidence

“Made of a woman”: the eternal Son, born of Mary in the flesh

“But when the fulness of time was come, God sent His Son, made of a woman, made under the Law.” “His Son,” observe, not as one of many, not as His in common with another, but His own, and in saying “His Son,” the Apostle showed that it is of the Son’s nature that His generation is eternal. Him the Apostle has affirmed to have been afterwards “made” of a woman, in order that the making might be understood not of the Godhead, but of the putting on of a body—“made of a woman,” then, by taking on of flesh; “made under the Law” through observance of the Law.

— On the Faith (De Fide) I.14.94, source. The whole point turns on the identity of the one born. Because the Son “made of a woman” is the Father’s own Son, whose “generation is eternal,” the child Mary bore is God the Word himself — the incarnational, not the merely human-descent, reading of “born of a woman.” Yet Ambrose is equally careful that Mary is not the origin of the Godhead: the being “made” is “not of the Godhead, but of the putting on of a body.” This is the God-bearing confession with its guard already attached — a full half-century before Cyril would say the Virgin is Mother of God “not as if the nature of the Word had the beginning of its existence from the flesh,” and before Chalcedon would qualify the title “according to his manhood.”

Notes

  • Ambrose expounds the same verse Paul supplies the whole timeline — Galatians 4:4, “made of a woman” — which the fathers read as the incarnational key: the eternal Son takes flesh of Mary. His careful separation of what is said of the “Godhead” from what is said of the “body” is precisely the logic the councils would later formalize to keep Theotokos a statement about the Son. See the argument that “Theotokos” is a claim about Christ, not an exaltation of Mary.
  • Ambrose does not use the Greek word Theotokos here; writing in Latin, he makes the doctrinal claim it carries — that the one born of Mary is God the Son — through his exegesis of “made of a woman.”
  • Ambrose appears across this wiki as a witness on several timelines (the New Eve, the perpetual virginity of Mary, the intermediate state, and others); here the interest is only in his testimony that the child born of the Virgin is God.
