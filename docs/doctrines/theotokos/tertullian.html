Tertullian on Theotokos

Dates: c. 210 (the North-African advocate flourished c. 197–220; On the Flesh of Christ, the anti-docetic treatise that carries this passage, is dated c. 206–212) · Wikipedia
Summary: Tertullian is the earliest witness on this timeline to the claim the title would later carry — that the one the Virgin bore is God — voiced roughly a century before the word Theotokos itself is first attested (in Alexander of Alexandria). Arguing against Marcion and the docetists that Christ took real flesh, he reads Isaiah’s sign of the virgin conceiving as fulfilled in “Emmanuel, God with us” — so that in the child born of Mary, he says plainly, “God was born.” The point is not Mary’s honour but the reality of the Incarnation: the one she bore is truly God, truly enfleshed.

Claims & evidence

The virgin bore “Emmanuel, God with us”: “in this man God was born”

He who was going to consecrate a new order of birth, must Himself be born after a novel fashion, concerning which Isaiah foretold how that the Lord Himself would give the sign. What, then, is the sign? “Behold a virgin shall conceive and bear a son.” Accordingly, a virgin did conceive and bear “Emmanuel, God with us.” This is the new nativity; a man is born in God. And in this man God was born, taking the flesh of an ancient race, without the help, however, of the ancient seed…

— On the Flesh of Christ 17, source. The context is polemical, not devotional: Tertullian is proving against Marcion and the Valentinians — who made Christ’s flesh a phantom — that the Son of God was really born of the Virgin. His warrant is the Emmanuel prophecy (Isaiah 7:14), which he takes to be fulfilled at the Nativity: the virgin “did conceive and bear ‘Emmanuel, God with us.’” The load-bearing sentence for this timeline is the next one — “in this man God was born” — the God-bearing claim itself: the one the Virgin brings forth is not a man merely indwelt by God but God made flesh. Tertullian is drawing on the two-natures grammar he had forged elsewhere (one person, God and man), so his “a man is born in God… in this man God was born” asserts a single subject, born of Mary, who is at once God and man.

Notes

  • Tertullian never uses the word Theotokos (“God-bearer”), which is not attested until the fourth century (Alexander of Alexandria, c. 324). What he supplies is the underlying confession — that the child born of the Virgin is God — showing the doctrinal content predates and drives the later coinage of the term. On the question of the word’s prehistory, see the argument on whether “Theotokos” was in use before the fourth century; on the content-precedes-word thesis, the argument that “Theotokos” is a claim about Christ, not an exaltation of Mary.
  • The very same paragraph of On the Flesh of Christ 17 supplies Tertullian’s statement of the Eve–Mary typology (“As Eve had believed the serpent, so Mary believed the angel”), for which he appears on the Mary as the New Eve timeline; here the interest is only in the God-bearing confession that opens the paragraph.
  • Tertullian appears across this wiki as a witness on several timelines (the canon, the perpetual virginity of Mary, and others); here the interest is only in his testimony to the claim that the one Mary bore is God.
