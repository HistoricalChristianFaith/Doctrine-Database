Theodoret of Cyrus on the title Theotokos

Dates: c. 431 (his Counter-Statements — the point-by-point Reproof of Cyril’s Twelve Anathemas, written at Cyrrhus at the request of John of Antioch as the storm broke over Ephesus; Theodoret c. 393 – c. 458/466, bishop of Cyrrhus) · Wikipedia
Summary: Theodoret is the sharpest voice of the Antiochene party that resisted Cyril’s anathemas before submitting — and his arc embodies the whole controversy’s resolution. He wrote a line-by-line rebuttal of Cyril’s Twelve Anathemas, defending the concerns Nestorius had raised, yet in that very rebuttal he confesses the Virgin Theotokos — on his own carefully guarded terms: not because she bore God “in natural manner,” but on account of “the union.” He then helped draft the Formula of Reunion (433) that reconciled Antioch and Alexandria, and was finally rehabilitated at the Council of Chalcedon (451) only after he had, under pressure, pronounced anathema on Nestorius. So the one witness holds together the timeline’s two poles: the Antiochene fear the title might make the Godhead originate from Mary, and the church’s insistence that, rightly understood, the title is the very confession of the Incarnation.

Claims & evidence

The Antiochene resists — the Reproof of Cyril’s First Anathema

Cyril’s first anathematism had bound the whole church to the word: whoever “refuses to confess that the Emmanuel is in truth God, and therefore that the holy Virgin is Mother of God (Theotokos)… let him be anathema.”1 At John of Antioch’s request Theodoret answered the Twelve one by one. His objection is not to the title but to Cyril’s way of grounding it — the language of a “natural union” that, to Antiochene ears, threatened to make the impassible Word originate from the Virgin and be changed into flesh. Against Anathema I he sets down the whole Antiochene grammar of the Incarnation: the Word “was not made flesh by mutation, but took flesh… He Himself is not naturally conceived of the Virgin, fashioned, formed, and deriving beginning of existence from her… but He fashioned for Himself a temple in the Virgin’s womb.”2

Yet he confesses the Virgin Theotokos — on his own terms

What makes Theodoret so instructive is that this arch-critic of Cyril nonetheless keeps the word. In the same reproof, having lodged all his cautions, he affirms the very title Cyril demanded — but qualified precisely to bar the sense he feared:

Wherefore also we style that holy Virgin θεοτόκος, not because she gave birth in natural manner to God, but to man united to the God that had fashioned Him.

— Theodoret, Counter-Statements, against Anathema I.2 He then draws the distinction that is the heart of the Antiochene reading — that the two possible titles answer to two different truths about the child, and only one of them is admissible:

we call the Virgin not mother of man (ανθρωποτόκος) but mother of God (θεοτόκος), applying the former title to the fashioning and conception, but the latter to the union.

— Theodoret, Counter-Statements, against Anathema I.2 So the child “is called Emmanuel on account of God Who assumed, and the Virgin θεοτόκος on account of the union of the form of God with the conceived form of a servant.”2 This is not Nestorius refusing the word; it is an Antiochene accepting it while insisting it name the union of the natures rather than a birth of the Godhead. The gap between Theodoret and Cyril here is real — Theodoret still speaks of a “temple” the Word inhabits, language Cyril distrusted — but on the title itself their confession converges: Mary is Theotokos because the one she bore is God the Word made flesh, not because the Godhead took its origin from her.

Reunion (433) and rehabilitation at Chalcedon (451)

The breach between Antioch and Alexandria that Ephesus opened was healed two years later by the Formula of Reunion (433), a document Theodoret himself had a hand in drafting: “a union of two natures took place. Therefore we confess one Christ, one Son, one Lord,” and — decisively for this timeline — “we confess the holy virgin to be the mother of God because God the Word took flesh and became man and from his very conception united to himself the temple he took from her.”3 The compromise satisfied both Cyril and the eastern bishops; Theodoret signed it, though he long refused to add his name to the personal condemnation of Nestorius that Cyril’s party also demanded.

That refusal cost him. When the Council of Chalcedon (451) convened, the anti-Nestorian bishops would not seat him until he anathematized his old associate by name. At the eighth session (26 October 451) he tried to state his orthodoxy at length — “I was brought up by the orthodox, I was taught by the orthodox, I have preached orthodoxy” — but the assembly shouted him down, demanding a plain curse. He gave it:

Anathema to Nestorius and to whoever denies that the Holy Virgin Mary is the Parent of God, and who divides the only begotten Son into two Sons.

— Theodoret at the eighth session of Chalcedon (451).4 Upon these words he was declared orthodox and restored to his see. “Parent of God” here renders the disputed Theotokos: the price of the Antiochene’s rehabilitation was, in the end, the confession of the very title he had spent twenty years defending in his own guarded idiom.

Notes

  • A witness who embodies the resolution. The Theotokos timeline runs from Cyril’s anathemas (430) and Ephesus (431) straight to Chalcedon (451); Theodoret fills the gap with the Antiochene side that actually resisted Cyril before submitting. His trajectory — Reproof → Formula of Reunion → anathema at Chalcedon — is the controversy’s arc in a single life, and it shows the title winning not by crushing the Antiochene concern but by absorbing it: Chalcedon confesses Mary Theotokos “according to his manhood,” which is Theodoret’s own point that the word names the union, not a birth of the Godhead.
  • An anathema with a reservation? Scholars have long noted that Theodoret probably meant his curse conditionally — against Nestorius if Nestorius had in fact taught what was imputed to him — for he never granted that Nestorius really held “two Sons.” That reticence is why his anti-Cyrilline writings, though he himself was rehabilitated at Chalcedon, were posthumously condemned in the Three-Chapter Controversy at the Second Council of Constantinople (553). The Antiochene reading of Nestorius’s concern is weighed on the argument page “Was Nestorius condemned at Ephesus for a Christology he did not hold?” (assessment: contested).
  • Dating. The Counter-Statements belong to c. 430–431, in the immediate aftermath of Cyril’s Twelve Anathemas and around the Council of Ephesus; that is the act by which Theodoret is slotted on this timeline (his wider corpus is anchored later, c. 458). He appears on this wiki also as a witness on the perpetual-virginity timeline (his Pauline commentary, c. 440), where his life-dates and role are given consistently.
