Gregory of Nazianzus on Theotokos

Dates: c. 382 (Letter 101) · c. 329–390 · Wikipedia
Summary: Half a century before Ephesus, Gregory makes acceptance of “Mother of God” a test of orthodoxy: to deny Mary the title is to be “severed from the Godhead” — because the title guards the real union of God and manhood in the one Christ.

Claims & evidence

To deny Mary as Mother of God is to be “severed from the Godhead”

If anyone does not believe that Holy Mary is the Mother of God, he is severed from the Godhead. If anyone should assert that He passed through the Virgin as through a channel, and was not at once divinely and humanly formed in her (divinely, because without the intervention of a man; humanly, because in accordance with the laws of gestation), he is in like manner godless.

— Letter 101, to Cledonius against Apollinarius, source. The anathema-style test is aimed at Apollinarian and docetic Christologies: Gregory yokes “Mother of God” to his insistence that the Son was “at once divinely and humanly formed in her,” not merely channelled through her. The title is thus already, in 382, functioning as the shorthand for a whole Christology — exactly the use Cyril and the Council of Ephesus would defend against Nestorius.

Notes

  • Gregory also appears on this wiki as a witness on the Old Testament canon timeline (his canon-poem lists a Hebrew-sized Old Testament) — a separate question from his Christology here.
  • Letter 101 was later read at the Council of Ephesus and Chalcedon as a patristic authority for the two-natures Christology the title Theotokos was coined to protect.
