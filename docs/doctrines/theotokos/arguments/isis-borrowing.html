Did “Theotokos” borrow the pagan title of Isis?

Related arguments: the origins of the title — see also Was “Theotokos” in use before the fourth century?

Type: history-of-religions genealogical argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — that the coinage of Theotokos was borrowed from the pagan title of the Egyptian goddess Isis does not hold as a claim about the word: the exact term is unattested for Isis, and the demonstrable driver of Christian usage is anti-Arian and anti-Nestorian Christology, in which the word surfaces (Alexander of Alexandria, the 320s) precisely to exclude the goddess-mother idea. A genuinely separate and more credible question — whether the image of Isis nursing Horus shaped the nursing-Madonna type — is contested, but it concerns iconography, not the title, and the images post-date the word.

The claim

A recurring history-of-religions thesis holds that Christian devotion to Mary is pagan goddess-worship rebranded, and that Theotokos is the tell: Isis was the “mother of the god,” her cult saturated Alexandria — the very city where the title first appears, in Alexander, Athanasius, and Cyril — and images of Isis suckling the infant Horus anticipate the Madonna and Child. Coining “God-bearer” for Mary, on this reading, transferred a pagan epithet to a Christian figure. The thesis has a scholarly pedigree in R. E. Witt’s Isis in the Ancient World and Stephen Benko’s The Virgin Goddess, which trace “a direct line… from the goddess-cults of the ancients” to the veneration of Mary.1 The interpretation this argument weighs is the genealogical one: that the title Theotokos itself was derived from Isis’s. Two things must be kept apart, because they have different answers — the derivation of the word, and the influence of the image.

The word: Isis was never called “Theotokos”

The exact Greek word Θεοτόκος is not attested for Isis. Her real titles are lexically different: the Egyptian mut-netjer, “Mother of the God” — a kinship term, and specifically mother of Horus, the divine child — and the Greek mētēr theōn, “mother of the gods” (plural), which enters the Isis cult late and by syncretism with the Phrygian Cybele/Magna Mater, not from Isis’s own native repertoire.2 The distinction is not pedantry. “Mother of a/the god” is a relational, family claim; Theotokos is a compound built on the verb of bearing/childbirth — “the one who gave birth to God” — and it makes a point Isis’s titles never do: that the one born is himself God. A real semantic resonance in the Alexandrian air (goddesses called “mother of a god,” “queen of heaven,” “great mother”) is background, not a source; resonance is not derivation, and the specific word was not lying ready-made in the Isis cult to be lifted.

The demonstrable driver: Christology, which forecloses the goddess reading

Where the title actually comes from is not obscure. It is a christological safeguard, forged in the anti-Arian and then the anti-Nestorian controversies to assert that the one Mary bore is God the Word made flesh — against Arius, who made the Son a creature, and against a division of Christ into two subjects. Its first certain literary use is Alexander of Alexandria’s (the 320s), inside the Arian crisis, exactly where the christological account predicts, not in some devotional borrowing from the surrounding cult.3 And when the word was defined, Cyril of Alexandria — himself the bishop of Isis-haunted Alexandria — took pains to say it does not mean the divinity originated from Mary: she is Mother of God “not as if the nature of the Word had the beginning of its existence from the flesh.”4 That is the precise content a mother-of-a-god cult would assert — a divine being originating from the goddess — and it is the precise content the Christian definition was written to deny. The word’s doctrinal career runs the opposite way from a pagan loan: modern scholarship across the confessions reads it as a christological term, the touchstone of the single-subject Christology, not a mariological import.5

The separate, stronger question: the nursing image

What the thesis does have going for it lies in a different register, and it must not be smuggled in to rescue the word. The image of Isis lactans — enthroned, suckling the infant Horus — is an ancient and widespread type, and a genuine iconographic debt of the nursing-Madonna (Maria lactans) type to it is defensible: Thomas F. Mathews and Norman Muller have argued the visual form carried over into early Marian images.6 But two limits keep even this from underwriting the borrowing-of-the-word thesis. First, borrowing a visual type is not adopting a cult: Sabrina Higgins presses a chronological gap — the uncontested Maria lactans in Egypt appears only from about the seventh century, some three hundred years after Isis lactans fades — and argues the image, where taken up, was read within a thoroughly christological frame.7 Second, and decisively for this argument: the nursing image is later than the word. The title was fixed by christological controversy in the fourth and fifth centuries; the Egyptian nursing-Madonna is later still — so the iconography, whatever its debts, cannot be the source of a title that was already secure before it.

Assessment

Weak as a genealogy of the title. The exact word Theotokos is unattested for Isis (whose epithets, mut-netjer and the borrowed mētēr theōn, are relational, not childbearing terms); the word surfaces securely inside the Arian controversy and is defined by Cyril precisely to exclude the notion that divinity originated from Mary — the very thing a mother-goddess cult asserts. The Alexandrian Isis-cult setting is real background, but background is not derivation. What survives adversarial pressure is only the narrower, iconographic claim — that the Isis-lactans visual type shaped the nursing-Madonna — which is genuinely contested (defensible as image-borrowing, doubtful as cult-continuity, and chronologically too late to have generated the word). Treating that separate and later iconographic question as if it established the derivation of the title conflates two different arguments.
