Was Nestorius condemned at Ephesus for a Christology he did not hold?

Type: historical-interpretive argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — the thesis splits into two claims that must be rated apart. That Nestorius’s mature Christology, as it survives in his own apologia the Bazaar of Heracleides, comes strikingly close to what Chalcedon would define — “one prosôpon,” “not two Sons or two Christs,” the natures preserved without confusion — is plausible, and the older charge that he taught two Sons as a bald doctrine is hard to sustain from the recovered text. But that he was therefore orthodox by Ephesus’s or Cyril’s lights, and condemned unjustly, is contested: he never withdrew his refusal of Theotokos, his “prosôpon of union” is not obviously the single subsisting subject Cyril and Chalcedon confessed, and his earliest surviving replies read worse than the Bazaar. Real scholars line up on both sides; neither has closed the case. Hence a partial rehabilitation — his central concern was legitimate, his terminology fell short of what the councils secured.

The claim

That two natures should be united in one prosôpon makes not two Sons or two Christs, the diversities of each one of the natures being preserved.

— Nestorius, The Bazaar of Heracleides (written in exile, c. 450), Book 2, part 1.1 The rehabilitation thesis holds that the man deposed at Ephesus (431) for splitting Christ into “two sons” did not in fact hold that heresy. On this reading his refusal of the bare word Theotokos was a terminological caution — a guard against an Apollinarian or theopaschite confusion in which the impassible Godhead is made to be born and to suffer — not the two-subject Christology imputed to him; and his own later account of the faith, long lost and recovered only in the twentieth century, confesses one Christ in language close to Chalcedon’s. The question is not whether the title Theotokos is right (it is; that is settled on the timeline), but whether the person condemned for denying it believed what his judges said he believed.

Who advances it

The reassessment is a distinctly modern, largely Western development, and it turns almost entirely on one recovered book. Nestorius’s works had been ordered burnt after Ephesus; what survived in Greek and Latin was mostly the hostile dossier his enemies preserved. Then, around the turn of the twentieth century, a single Syriac manuscript of his own long apologia — written in exile under the pseudonym “Heracleides of Damascus” — came to light in the mountains of Kurdistan.2 Friedrich Loofs, who had already gathered the fragments in his Nestoriana (1905), and after him J. F. Bethune-Baker (Nestorius and His Teaching, 1908), argued from it that Nestorius had been essentially orthodox and badly used. The most emphatic statement of the case is Milton Anastos’s “Nestorius Was Orthodox” (1962); Roberta Chesnut’s study of the “two prosôpa” in the Bazaar (1978) gave the prosopic Christology its most careful analysis.3 Against them stands the traditional-critical line — John McGuckin’s study of Cyril and the controversy is its fullest modern statement — which grants the Bazaar’s better moments but holds that Nestorius’s underlying scheme was genuinely defective, whatever his intentions.4

The evidence that he was misrepresented

The Bazaar repeatedly disowns the heresy attached to his name. Nestorius makes the confession of one Son a premise: “Thou wilt confess aloud with us that there are not two Gods the Words or two Sons or two only begottens, but one.”5 He locates the union not in the natures but in a single prosôpon: “I said and affirmed that the union is in the one prosôpon of the Messiah, and I made known in every way that God the Word was made man.”6 And he insists the two natures issue in one Christ, not two — “makes not two Sons or two Christs, the diversities of each one of the natures being preserved.”1 This last phrasing is close in both grammar and intent to Chalcedon’s “one and the same Christ… in two natures… the distinction of natures being in no way abolished by the union.” His stated fear, too, was one the church came to share: that “God-bearer” taken alone might make the divine nature originate from Mary or drag the impassible Word into birth and death — the very worry Chalcedon would honour by qualifying the title, confessing Mary Theotokos “according to his manhood.” On the friendliest reading, Nestorius and his judges were separated less by faith than by vocabulary: he heard in Cyril’s “hypostatic union” the confusion of natures he abhorred, and reached for “conjunction” to keep them distinct.

The evidence that the condemnation was not a mere misunderstanding

Three things resist the full acquittal. First, he never took back the refusal that started the fire. Even in his conciliatory Third Letter to Celestine (430) he still ranked the disputed word below his own: “the term Theotokos is inferior to the term Christotokos, as the latter is mentioned by the angels and the gospels”7 — and the sermons that opened the crisis had gone further, treating “Mother of God” as barely tolerable. Second, the terminology is not innocent. His preference for a weaker word for the union — conjunction (synapheia) over Cyril’s henosis — and his suspicion of “hypostatic union,” together with his account of Christ as a prosôpon of union formed from two natural prosôpa, strike his critics as a union of appearance and honour rather than of a single subsisting subject; on that reading he implicitly professes the two-subject Christology he verbally denies.4 Third, his earliest surviving words read worse than the Bazaar: the replies he sent Cyril before his deposition contain the material later read as “two persons,” so the Bazaar may record a position he reached under pressure as much as one he always held. Cyril, for his part, condemned not a phantom but a real programme — his First Anathematism binds precisely the refusal to confess the Virgin Theotokos because “in the flesh she bore the Word of God made flesh.”8

Adversarial assessment

Sub-claim (a): did his mature Christology match Chalcedon? — plausible. The recovered Bazaar makes the crude charge (“Nestorius taught two Sons as a doctrine”) very hard to defend: he denies it in terms, confesses one prosôpon, and preserves the two natures unconfused — formally not far from the Definition of 451. Yet “matches Chalcedon” overstates: Chalcedon confessed one Christ in one hypostasis, whereas Nestorius’s union is a prosôpon assembled from two natural prosôpa, and whether that names the same single subject or only a conjoined pair is exactly what critics still deny. So the interpretation is coherent and seriously defensible — plausible — without being probable to the point of consensus. Sub-claim (b): was his early Christology heretical, and his condemnation unjust? — contested / not confirmable. Here the evidence genuinely divides. His concern was legitimate and his later confession largely sound, which cuts toward injustice; but he kept attacking Cyril, never recanted the refusal of Theotokos, and left early statements open to the worst construction, which cuts the other way. The honest verdict is a partial rehabilitation: the council rightly secured the title and the one-subject confession it protects, and Nestorius really did resist both at the time — but the caricature that made him a deliberate teacher of “two sons” does not survive his own book.

What both sides should concede. The rehabilitators are right that the recovered text refutes the bald heresy and that Nestorius’s guard against a passible, Mary-originated Godhead was a real Christian instinct — one Chalcedon vindicated by adding “according to his manhood.” The traditional side is right that a legitimate worry does not make a sufficient Christology: Nestorius could not find words for the single subject that the confession of Theotokos presupposes, and his refusal of the title, however well-meant, would have left the Incarnation unsayable. The dispute is strong by the historian’s lights (the man was not the monster of the acts) and strong by the dogmatician’s (the scheme could not carry the one Christ), and neither refutes the other.

Assessment

Contested. The flagship “Nestorius was misrepresented” thesis is really two claims. That his mature Christology, as the Bazaar of Heracleides preserves it, disowns “two Sons” and confesses one Christ in two unconfused natures — close to Chalcedon — is plausible, and the old picture of a bishop who set out to divide Christ does not survive his own words. But that he was therefore orthodox on the terms of his own day and condemned unjustly is contested / unconfirmable: he never withdrew his refusal of Theotokos, his “prosôpon of union” is not evidently the single subject the councils confessed, and his earliest replies read worse than the apologia. Genuine scholars hold each side. The defensible resolution is a partial rehabilitation: his central concern — that the impassible God not be said, without qualification, to be born and to die — was legitimate and was honoured at Chalcedon (“according to his manhood”), while his terminology still fell short of what Ephesus and Chalcedon secured.
