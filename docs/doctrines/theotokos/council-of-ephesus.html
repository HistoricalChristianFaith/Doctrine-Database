The Council of Ephesus on Theotokos

Dates: 431 (the third ecumenical council, convened by Theodosius II at Ephesus) · Wikipedia
Summary: The council that dogmatized the title. Meeting under Cyril’s presidency, it upheld the Twelve Anathematisms, deposed Nestorius, and fixed “Mother of God” as the church’s confession — the settlement the earlier witnesses had been building toward.

Claims & evidence

Nestorius deposed for “impious doctrines”

Compelled thereto by the canons and by the letter of our most holy father and fellow-servant Coelestine, the Roman bishop, we have come, with many tears, to this sorrowful sentence against him, namely, that our Lord Jesus Christ, whom he has blasphemed, decrees by the holy Synod that Nestorius be excluded from the episcopal dignity, and from all priestly communion.

— Decree of the Council Against Nestorius, source. The deposition was the council’s verdict on the Christology behind Nestorius’s refusal of Theotokos: because he “had held and published impious doctrines” that were held to divide the one Christ, he was stripped of his see. Cyril’s Second Letter to Nestorius was read into the acts and approved as orthodox; the title was thereby vindicated conciliarly.

Notes

  • Ephesus is the doctrinal hinge of this timeline: what Alexander used, Gregory tested, and Cyril defended, the council made binding. Twenty years later the Council of Chalcedon (451) reaffirmed “Theotokos” within its two-natures Definition.
  • The status of Nestorius himself — whether justly condemned, and whether the “two sons” charged against him was his real teaching — is contested to this day (the Church of the East still honours him) and is weighed as an argument off the timeline, not settled here.
