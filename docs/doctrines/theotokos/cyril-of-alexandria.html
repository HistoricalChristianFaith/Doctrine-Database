Cyril of Alexandria on Theotokos

Dates: c. 430 (the Third Letter to Nestorius) · bishop of Alexandria 412–444 · Wikipedia
Summary: The champion of the title against Nestorius. Cyril presses “Mother of God” as a strictly Christological claim — that the one Mary bore is God the Word made flesh — and makes confession of it the first of his Twelve Anathematisms, adopted at Ephesus.

Claims & evidence

She is called Mother of God because she bore God made one with flesh

And since the holy Virgin brought forth corporally God made one with flesh according to nature, for this reason we also call her Mother of God, not as if the nature of the Word had the beginning of its existence from the flesh.

— Third Letter to Nestorius (with the Twelve Anathematisms), source. Cyril is careful to strip the title of the misreading Nestorius feared — it does not make Mary the source of the Word’s divine nature (“not as if the nature of the Word had the beginning of its existence from the flesh”). It affirms only that the one she bore is God: the identity of the child, not the origin of the Godhead.

The First Anathema: confess the Virgin as Mother of God, or be anathema

If anyone will not confess that the Emmanuel is very God, and that therefore the Holy Virgin is the Mother of God (Theotokos), inasmuch as in the flesh she bore the Word of God made flesh [as it is written, “The Word was made flesh”]: let him be anathema.

— First of the Twelve Anathematisms, appended to the same letter, source. The anathema binds the title to John 1:14: because “the Word was made flesh,” the one born of Mary is very God, and so she is truly Theotokos. This canon, read into the acts at Ephesus in 431, made the word a formal test of communion.

Notes
