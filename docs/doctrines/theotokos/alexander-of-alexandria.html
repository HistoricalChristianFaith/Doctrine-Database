Alexander of Alexandria on Theotokos

Dates: c. 324 (the letter) · bishop of Alexandria c. 313–328 · Wikipedia
Summary: In the encyclical against Arius he calls Mary “Mother of God” — the earliest certain literary use of the title in a surviving Christian text — and does so to affirm not Mary but the eternal divinity of the Son who took a real body from her.

Claims & evidence

Christ “carried a body, of Mary Mother of God”

After this we know of the resurrection of the dead, the first-fruits of which was our Lord Jesus Christ, who in very deed, and not in appearance merely, carried a body, of Mary Mother of God, who in the end of the world came to the human race to put away sin, was crucified and died, and yet did He not thus perceive any detriment to His divinity, being raised from the dead, taken up into heaven, seated at the right hand of majesty.

— Epistles on the Arian Heresy, source. The phrase is embedded in a wholly Christological argument: Alexander’s point is that the one born, crucified, and raised is truly God, who took no “detriment to His divinity” in taking flesh. The title Theotokos here is a claim about the identity of the child, not a devotional honour paid to the mother — the same logic Cyril would make explicit a century later in the Nestorian controversy.

Notes

  • This is generally cited as the first datable, undisputed use of “Mother of God” (Θεοτόκος) in a literary source. Earlier claimed uses — Hippolytus, Origen (via Socrates, HE 7.32), Dionysius of Alexandria, Pierius — are textually disputed and weighed separately as an argument, not slotted here as firm witnesses.
  • The word’s antiquity in prayer (rather than in doctrinal prose) turns on the fragment Sub Tuum Praesidium / P.Ryl. 470, whose contested dating is set out on the prayer to the saints timeline; that debate bears on when the title was invoked, this page on when it was argued.
