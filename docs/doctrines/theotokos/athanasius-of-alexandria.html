Athanasius of Alexandria on Theotokos

Dates: c. 340 (the Third Discourse Against the Arians) · bishop of Alexandria 328–373 · Wikipedia
Summary: Alexander’s deacon and successor carries the title forward in the same anti-Arian service. Athanasius uses “Mary Bearer of God” (Θεοτόκος, rendered “Bearer of God” in this translation) as shorthand for the Incarnation itself — the eternal Word taking flesh of the Virgin — not as a title of honour for Mary.

Claims & evidence

The “double account of the Saviour”: eternal God, then flesh of Mary Bearer of God

Now the scope and character of Holy Scripture, as we have often said, is this,—it contains a double account of the Saviour; that He was ever God, and is the Son, being the Father’s Word and Radiance and Wisdom; and that afterwards for us He took flesh of a Virgin, Mary Bearer of God, and was made man.

— Four Discourses Against the Arians III.29, source. The title does its work inside a Christological rule: Scripture speaks of the Saviour two ways — as the ever-existing God, and as the one who “took flesh of a Virgin” and “was made man.” That the Virgin is “Bearer of God” is simply the second half of that account — the one born of her is the eternal Word, made man for us. (The rendering “Bearer of God” here translates the Greek Theotokos.)

The title recurs as a settled phrase

whereas the flesh is born of Mary Bearer of God, He Himself is said to have been born, who furnishes to others an origin of being.

— Four Discourses Against the Arians III.33, source. Athanasius reaches for “Mary Bearer of God” more than once in the Third Discourse, and always in this same frame: it is the flesh that is born of her, while the one born is he “who furnishes to others an origin of being.” The word is already a familiar, load-bearing term in fourth-century Alexandria — a full century before Ephesus made it a formal test.

Notes

  • Athanasius stands in direct continuity with Alexander of Alexandria, whose deacon he was at Nicæa and whom he succeeded in the see; both deploy the title against Arius, to confess the child’s eternal divinity.
  • Athanasius appears across this wiki as a witness on several timelines (the canon, the Old Testament canon, and others); here the interest is only in his use of the title Theotokos.
