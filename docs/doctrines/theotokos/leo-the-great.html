Leo the Great on Theotokos (Mary as Mother of God)

Dates: c. 449 · Wikipedia
Summary: Pope Leo I (Leo the Great) is the Latin, Western hinge between Ephesus (431) and Chalcedon (451). His Christmas preaching calls Mary plainly “the Mother of God,” and his Tome to Flavian (Letter 28, 449) — the document Chalcedon acclaimed (“Peter has spoken through Leo”) and whose language its Definition took up — grounds that title exactly as this timeline’s thesis holds: through the unity of the one person of Christ in two natures, so that what is proper to the manhood (being born of the Virgin) is truly said of the one Son of God. He never makes “Mother of God” a claim about Mary’s dignity in herself; it is a confession of who her child is.

Claims & evidence

Mary is “the Mother of God” — and it is no loss of honour, because of who she bears (Nativity Sermon, c. 440)

A royal Virgin of the stem of David is chosen, to be impregnated with the sacred seed and to conceive the Divinely-human offspring in mind first and then in body. And lest in ignorance of the heavenly counsel she should tremble at so strange a result, she learns from converse with the angel that what is to be wrought in her is of the Holy Ghost. Nor does she believe it loss of honour that she is soon to be the Mother of God.

— Sermon 21, On the Feast of the Nativity, 1, §I, historicalchristian.faith. Delivered at Rome (this is generally taken as Leo’s first surviving Christmas sermon, c. 440). Leo uses the title without controversy or apology — the point of the passage is not to magnify Mary but to fix the identity of the child (“the Divinely-human offspring,” conceived “of the Holy Ghost”). That the title is “no loss of honour” to a virgin is the whole of its Marian resonance here; its weight falls on the Son.

The unity of the one person makes what belongs to the manhood true of the Son of God (the Tome, 449)

Therefore in consequence of this unity of person which is to be understood in both natures, we read of the Son of Man also descending from heaven, when the Son of God took flesh from the Virgin who bore Him. And again the Son of God is said to have been crucified and buried, although it was not actually in His Divinity whereby the Only-begotten is co-eternal and con-substantial with the Father, but in His weak human nature that He suffered these things.

— Letter 28 to Flavian, commonly called “The Tome” (449), §5, historicalchristian.faith. This is the grammatical engine behind “Mother of God”: because there is one person (“one and the same is truly Son of God and truly son of man”), properties of the manhood are rightly predicated of the divine subject. So “the Son of God took flesh from the Virgin who bore Him” — Mary bears the one who is God the Word, though not the origin of his Godhead. Leo insists in the same letter that “each form does what is proper to it with the co-operation of the other,” keeping the two natures unconfused; the title never means that the Godhead began from Mary, only that the one she bore is God.

Born “of the Holy Spirit and the Virgin Mary,” without loss of her virginity (the Tome, 449)

the only begotten of the eternal Father was born eternal of the Holy Spirit and the Virgin Mary. And this nativity which took place in time took nothing from, and added nothing to that divine and eternal birth… He was conceived of the Holy Spirit within the womb of His Virgin Mother, who brought Him forth without the loss of her virginity, even as she conceived Him without its loss.

— Letter 28 to Flavian (The Tome) (449), §§2, 4, historicalchristian.faith. The temporal birth “took nothing from, and added nothing to” the eternal generation — the same guard the timeline’s witnesses raise against reading “Mother of God” as making Mary the source of the divine nature. The one who is “born eternal… of the Father” is the one born in time “of the Virgin Mary.”

Notes

  • Leo is the Western witness the timeline otherwise lacks between Ephesus and Chalcedon, and the bridge into Chalcedon’s qualifying phrase. The two-natures Christology of the Tome shaped the Council of Chalcedon’s Definition, which the council acclaimed with the cry “Peter has spoken through Leo”; that Definition confesses Mary “the Mother of God according to his manhood” — the very qualification (“in His weak human nature that He suffered these things”) Leo draws here.
  • His interest is christological, not Marian: the title lands on the Son’s identity, and its Marian side is at most that bearing God is “no loss of honour.” He never argues from the title to a further dignity of Mary in herself — which is precisely the reading this timeline defends (see the argument “Is ‘Theotokos’ a claim about Christ, not an exaltation of Mary?”).
  • Leo appears elsewhere on this wiki as a witness — e.g. on the intermediate state (the descent) and the ministerial priesthood — reflecting the same insistence on the reality of Christ’s incarnation, death, and resurrection that drives the Tome.
