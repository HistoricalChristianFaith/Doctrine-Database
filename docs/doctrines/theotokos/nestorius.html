Nestorius on Theotokos

Dates: c. 428–430 (patriarch of Constantinople 428–431; the letter quoted is the Third Letter to Celestine, late 430) · Wikipedia
Summary: The title’s great objector — and the reason it became a defined dogma. Nestorius did not simply reject Theotokos; he judged it inferior to the scriptural Christotokos (“Christ-bearer”), fearing that “God-bearer” alone would confuse the two natures. He is present on this timeline as the foil whose challenge forced the church to say precisely what the word does and does not mean.

Claims & evidence

He allows Theotokos — but ranks it below Christotokos

In the case of the term Theotokos, I am not opposed to those who want to say it, unless it should advance to the confusion of natures in the manner of the madness of Apollinaris or Arius. Nonetheless, I have no doubt that the term Theotokos is inferior to the term Christotokos, as the latter is mentioned by the angels and the gospels.

— Third Letter to Celestine (late 430), trans. Mark DelCogliano, source.1 His stated position is more guarded than the caricature that reached posterity: he does not forbid “Mother of God,” but subordinates it to “Mother of Christ,” which he thinks safer because it is the term Scripture uses and because it names the whole Christ — God and man together — without seeming to derive the Godhead from Mary.

Why he prefers “Christ-bearer”: it fences off two heresies at once

the term Christotokos keeps the assertion of both parties to the proper limits, because it both removes the blasphemy of Paul of Samosata, who claimed that Christ the Lord of all was simply a human being, and also flees the wickedness of Arius and Apollinaris.

— Third Letter to Celestine, source. Nestorius casts himself as a mediator between two errors — those who would say only “Mother of God” and those who would say only “Mother of a man” (Anthropotokos) — and offers Christotokos as the term that satisfies both. His opponents heard something else: that in dividing the ways of speaking he had divided the one Christ into two subjects, a God and a man merely conjoined.

Notes

  • Whether Nestorius actually taught “two sons” or was condemned on a construction of his words he rejected is itself contested — he protested to the end that he was misunderstood, and his apologia the Bazaar of Heracleides (written in exile and recovered only in the twentieth century) disowns “two Sons” and confesses one prosôpon. That question is weighed off this timeline: Was Nestorius condemned at Ephesus for a Christology he did not hold? (assessment: contested — a partial rehabilitation; his mature Christology approaches Chalcedon, but he never withdrew his refusal of Theotokos and his terminology fell short of what the councils secured).
  • Cyril of Alexandria answered him directly; the Council of Ephesus (431) deposed him and dogmatized the title he had ranked second.
