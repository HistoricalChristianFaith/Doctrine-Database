Proclus of Constantinople on Theotokos

Dates: c. 429 (Homily 1, On the Holy Virgin Theotokos, preached in the Great Church of Constantinople) · archbishop of Constantinople 434–446 · Wikipedia
Summary: The homily that lit the fuse. Preaching a feast of the Virgin in his own cathedral — by the tradition, with Nestorius himself present on the throne — Proclus praised Mary as Theotokos and grounded the title in the paradox of the Incarnation, a public provocation that turned a simmering dispute into open controversy.

Claims & evidence

A festival of the Virgin, summoning praise

The Virgin’s festival, my brethren, summons us today to words of praise, and the present feast has benefits to bestow on those who assemble to keep it. And surely this is right, for its subject is chastity.

— Homily 1, On the Holy Virgin Theotokos, trans. Nicholas Constas, source. The homily takes its very title from the contested word, and its argument turns the title into Christology: Mary could be called “God-bearer” precisely because the one she bore was God the Word made flesh — “the natures came together and the union remained unconfused.” Where Nestorius weighed Theotokos against Christotokos and preferred the latter, Proclus preached the disputed title in celebration, from the capital’s chief pulpit.

Why it mattered

Delivered (by the ancient report) in Nestorius’s own presence, the sermon forced the patriarch’s hand and carried the quarrel over the word out of the schools and into the life of the church — the occasion that drew Cyril of Alexandria in and led, within two years, to the Council of Ephesus. Proclus later succeeded to the see of Constantinople (434–446), from which the title he had defended was by then the settled confession of the church.
