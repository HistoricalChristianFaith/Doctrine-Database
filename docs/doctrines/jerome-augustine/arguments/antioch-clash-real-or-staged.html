The clash at Antioch (Galatians 2:11–14): a real rebuke, or a staged pretense?

Type: interpretive dispute (off-timeline) · Assessment: sound — the reading Augustine defended, that Peter genuinely erred at Antioch and Paul genuinely and truthfully corrected him, is the plain sense of the text and the settled verdict of later interpreters; Jerome's reading — that the confrontation was a prudent, pre-arranged pretense in which neither apostle really sinned — had a genuine patristic pedigree (Origen and the Greeks) and Jerome himself declined to hold it “finally and irrevocably,” but it does not hold on its best case · the two readings are pressed by Jerome (staged) and Augustine (real).

The text at issue

But when Peter was come to Antioch, I withstood him to the face, because he was to be blamed. For before that certain came from James, he did eat with the Gentiles: but when they were come, he withdrew and separated himself, fearing them which were of the circumcision. And the other Jews dissembled likewise with him; insomuch that Barnabas also was carried away with their dissimulation. But when I saw that they walked not uprightly according to the truth of the gospel, I said unto Peter before them all, If thou, being a Jew, livest after the manner of Gentiles, and not as do the Jews, why compellest thou the Gentiles to live as do the Jews?

— Galatians 2:11–14 (KJV).1 The dispute is over what actually happened. Did Peter commit a real fault, correctly rebuked by Paul in public? Or did the two apostles only appear to quarrel — Peter play-acting the Judaizer, Paul play-acting the corrector — in a staged scene contrived for the good of the onlookers, so that neither in fact did anything wrong?

Who advances each reading

The staged reading is Jerome's, set out in his commentary on Galatians and defended at length in his Letter 112 to Augustine (404). Jerome did not invent it: he presents it as the reading of the Greek fathers before him, above all Origen — “that illustrious man wrote five volumes on the Epistle of Paul to the Galatians” — and also Didymus, Apollinaris of Laodicea, Eusebius of Emesa, Theodorus of Heraclea, and “John, who has long governed the Church of Constantinople” (Chrysostom).2 The real-rebuke reading is Augustine's, pressed in his Letters 28, 40, and 82 to Jerome: Peter erred, Paul rebuked him truthfully, and the narrative records a real event.3

The staged reading, at its strongest

Jerome's case is not a mere evasion, and it deserves its steelman. Its engine is a genuine difficulty: how can Peter, the chief of the apostles, have publicly erred in a matter of the gospel — and how can Paul have blamed in Peter (the eating with Gentiles, then the withdrawal) conduct that Paul himself elsewhere practiced, becoming “to the Jews … as a Jew” (1 Cor 9:20)? On Jerome's reading the tension dissolves: both apostles knew perfectly well that the ceremonial law was abrogated; Peter withdrew not from conviction but as a calculated accommodation to the Jewish believers he had been sent to win, and Paul's “rebuke” was a matching piece of stagecraft, a public correction played out so that the Gentile converts would not be drawn back under the law. As Jerome puts it, Peter

… was thoroughly aware of the abrogation of the law of Moses, but was compelled by fear to pretend to observe it …

— so that “neither did Peter sin, nor did Paul rebuke presumptuously a greater than himself.”4 Three things commend the reading. First, its pedigree: it was not idiosyncratic but the majority verdict of the Greek exegetical tradition, and a father as careful as Chrysostom held a version of it precisely to guard the dignity of both apostles. Second, its apologetic motive was real: the pagan critic Porphyry had seized on a quarrel between the two chief apostles as proof that the Church rested on a squabble, and reading the scene as a controlled “economy” answered him. Third, Jerome himself held it loosely — he told Augustine he “did not adopt finally and irrevocably that which I had read in these Greek authors, but had propounded what I had read, leaving to the reader's own judgment whether it should be rejected or approved.”5

The real-rebuke reading, at its strongest

Augustine's case is the plain sense of the passage, and it is powerful on two fronts — the exegetical and the theological.

The text. Paul says Peter “was to be blamed,” that he “withdrew and separated himself, fearing them which were of the circumcision,” that the others were “carried away with their dissimulation,” and that “they walked not uprightly according to the truth of the gospel.” None of this reads as a rehearsed tableau; it reads as a report of a genuine failure of nerve, named as such. And Paul frames the whole Antioch narrative under a solemn oath sworn a few verses earlier — “the things which I write unto you, behold, before God, I lie not” (Gal 1:20) — which the staged reading must quietly suspend.6

The stakes. Augustine's deeper objection is that the staged reading buys the apostles' dignity at a ruinous price: it makes an inspired writer record, and endorse, a deliberate deception. Once that is admitted anywhere, he argues, nothing in Scripture is safe from the same solvent:

For if you once admit into such a high sanctuary of authority one false statement as made in the way of duty, there will not be left a single sentence of those books which, if appearing to any one difficult in practice or hard to believe, may not by the same fatal rule be explained away, as a statement in which, intentionally, and under a sense of duty, the author declared what was not true.

— Augustine, Letter 28, §3. He would not concede it: “I read that Paul wrote this, and I do not believe that he lied.” Peter really erred; Paul really and truthfully rebuked him; and the episode is the more edifying, not less, for being real — a lesson that even the chief apostle could be corrected, and humbly accept correction, for the truth of the gospel.7

Adversarial assessment

Where Jerome's reading fails. The staged interpretation founders on the very words Paul chose. “Fearing,” “dissimulation,” “walked not uprightly,” “to be blamed” — this is the vocabulary of fault, not of a benevolent charade, and to read it as its opposite is to make the text mean the reverse of what it says. Worse, as Augustine saw, it does not merely misread one passage; it introduces a principle — that a sacred author may state what is false out of a sense of duty — that no reader could contain to this passage alone. And it purchases apostolic dignity in a currency the gospel does not accept: a Peter who feigns to keep the law to deceive the weak is no more admirable than a Peter who wavered and was corrected — arguably less. Even the harmonizing pressure that motivated the reading cuts the other way: the honest answer to Porphyry is not that the apostles staged a mock quarrel, but that the apostles were men, that one of them faltered, and that the Scripture unflinchingly says so.

What is nonetheless true in Jerome's account. The difficulty Jerome felt is real, and his reading was neither baseless nor eccentric — it descended from Origen through the mainstream of Greek exegesis, and it sprang from a proper reverence for Peter and a live concern to answer the pagan critics. It is best judged not false in the way a factual error is false, but a less-probable reading that solves a genuine problem at too high a cost. And it is worth recording that Jerome, unlike some who held it, declined to make it a dogma: he had, he said, only “propounded what I had read,” leaving the reader free to reject it — a hedge that softens the charge against him even as it concedes the ground.

Assessment

Sound (for the real-rebuke reading). That Peter genuinely erred at Antioch and that Paul genuinely, truthfully, and publicly corrected him is the plain sense of Galatians 2 and has become the settled verdict of later interpretation; Augustine won the exegetical argument, and his companion principle — that inerrancy belongs to the canonical books alone, and that admitting one dutiful falsehood there unravels the whole — became the classic Latin statement of the point. Jerome's staged reading, though it carried the authority of Origen and the Greek fathers and answered a real apologetic worry, does not survive the words Paul actually wrote, and Jerome himself never pressed it as more than a reported opinion. The disagreement is flagged on both men's pages — as the sounder instinct on Augustine's, and as the losing (but pedigreed, and self-qualified) reading on Jerome's.
