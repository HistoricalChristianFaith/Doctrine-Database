The clash at Antioch (Galatians 2:11–14): a staged pretense, in which neither apostle really erred?

Type: interpretive argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — the “economy” reading Jerome took from the Greek fathers, that Peter only feigned to keep the law and Paul only appeared to rebuke him, so that neither apostle really sinned, has a genuine patristic pedigree (Origen, Chrysostom, and the Greek mainstream) and answered a live pagan objection — but it does not hold on its best case: it reads the vocabulary of fault Paul chose as its opposite, suspends the oath of Galatians 1:20, and buys apostolic dignity at the ruinous price of an inspired writer endorsing a deception. Jerome himself declined to hold it “finally and irrevocably.”

Related arguments: the Antioch clash — see also a real rebuke of a genuine error (Augustine)

The text at issue

But when Peter was come to Antioch, I withstood him to the face, because he was to be blamed. For before that certain came from James, he did eat with the Gentiles: but when they were come, he withdrew and separated himself, fearing them which were of the circumcision. And the other Jews dissembled likewise with him; insomuch that Barnabas also was carried away with their dissimulation. But when I saw that they walked not uprightly according to the truth of the gospel, I said unto Peter before them all, If thou, being a Jew, livest after the manner of Gentiles, and not as do the Jews, why compellest thou the Gentiles to live as do the Jews?

— Galatians 2:11–14 (KJV).1 On the reading defended here, this scene is not what it appears: Peter did not really lapse and Paul did not really correct him. The two apostles only seemed to quarrel — Peter play-acting the Judaizer, Paul play-acting the corrector — in a controlled performance contrived for the good of the onlookers, so that neither in fact did anything wrong.

Who advances the reading

The staged interpretation is Jerome's, set out in his commentary on Galatians and defended at length in his Letter 112 to Augustine (404). Jerome did not invent it: he presents it as the reading of the Greek fathers before him, above all Origen — “that illustrious man wrote five volumes on the Epistle of Paul to the Galatians” — and also Didymus, Apollinaris of Laodicea, Eusebius of Emesa, Theodorus of Heraclea, and “John, who has long governed the Church of Constantinople” (Chrysostom).2

The reading, at its strongest

Jerome's case is not a mere evasion, and it deserves its steelman. Its engine is a genuine difficulty: how can Peter, the chief of the apostles, have publicly erred in a matter of the gospel — and how can Paul have blamed in Peter (the eating with Gentiles, then the withdrawal) conduct that Paul himself elsewhere practiced, becoming “to the Jews … as a Jew” (1 Cor 9:20)? On the “economy” reading the tension dissolves: both apostles knew perfectly well that the ceremonial law was abrogated; Peter withdrew not from conviction but as a calculated accommodation to the Jewish believers he had been sent to win, and Paul's “rebuke” was a matching piece of stagecraft, a public correction played out so that the Gentile converts would not be drawn back under the law. As Jerome puts it, Peter

… was thoroughly aware of the abrogation of the law of Moses, but was compelled by fear to pretend to observe it …

— so that “neither did Peter sin, nor did Paul rebuke presumptuously a greater than himself.”3 Three things commend the reading. First, its pedigree: it was not idiosyncratic but the majority verdict of the Greek exegetical tradition, and a father as careful as Chrysostom held a version of it precisely to guard the dignity of both apostles. Second, its apologetic motive was real: the pagan critic Porphyry had seized on a quarrel between the two chief apostles as proof that the Church rested on a squabble, and reading the scene as a controlled “economy” answered him. Third, Jerome himself held it loosely — he told Augustine he “did not adopt finally and irrevocably that which I had read in these Greek authors, but had propounded what I had read, leaving to the reader's own judgment whether it should be rejected or approved.”4

Adversarial assessment

Where it fails. The staged interpretation founders on the very words Paul chose. “Fearing,” “dissimulation,” “walked not uprightly,” “to be blamed” — this is the vocabulary of fault, not of a benevolent charade, and to read it as its opposite is to make the text mean the reverse of what it says. Paul, moreover, frames the whole Antioch narrative under a solemn oath sworn a few verses earlier — “the things which I write unto you, behold, before God, I lie not” (Gal 1:20) — which the staged reading must quietly suspend. Worse, as Augustine saw, it does not merely misread one passage; it introduces a principle — that a sacred author may state what is false out of a sense of duty — that no reader could contain to this passage alone. And it purchases apostolic dignity in a currency the gospel does not accept: a Peter who feigns to keep the law to deceive the weak is no more admirable than a Peter who wavered and was corrected — arguably less. Even the harmonizing pressure that motivated the reading cuts the other way: the honest answer to Porphyry is not that the apostles staged a mock quarrel, but that the apostles were men, that one of them faltered, and that the Scripture unflinchingly says so.5

What is nonetheless true in Jerome's account. The difficulty Jerome felt is real, and his reading was neither baseless nor eccentric — it descended from Origen through the mainstream of Greek exegesis, and it sprang from a proper reverence for Peter and a live concern to answer the pagan critics. It is best judged not false in the way a factual error is false, but a less-probable reading that solves a genuine problem at too high a cost. And it is worth recording that Jerome, unlike some who held it, declined to make it a dogma: he had, he said, only “propounded what I had read,” leaving the reader free to reject it — a hedge that softens the charge against him even as it concedes the ground.

Assessment

Weak — a pedigreed and self-qualified reading that does not hold on its best case. The “economy” interpretation carried the authority of Origen and the Greek fathers and answered a real apologetic worry, and Jerome himself never pressed it as more than a reported opinion; those facts keep it honourable and explain why so careful a tradition held it. But it does not survive the words Paul actually wrote: the vocabulary of fault, the oath of Galatians 1:20, and the ruinous principle it smuggles in — that an inspired writer might record and endorse a dutiful deception — all tell against it. The rival real-rebuke reading is the plain sense and the settled verdict of later interpretation. Jerome's misstep is flagged on his own page as the losing (but pedigreed, and self-qualified) side of the dispute.
