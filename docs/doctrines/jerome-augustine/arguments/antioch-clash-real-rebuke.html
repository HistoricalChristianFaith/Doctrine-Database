The clash at Antioch (Galatians 2:11–14): a real rebuke of a genuine error?

Type: interpretive argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: sound — the reading Augustine defended, that Peter genuinely erred at Antioch and Paul genuinely and truthfully corrected him in public, is the plain sense of the text and the settled verdict of later interpretation. It rests on the vocabulary of fault Paul chose (“fearing,” “dissimulation,” “walked not uprightly”), on the oath of Galatians 1:20, and on the principle — which became the classic Latin statement of the point — that admitting one dutiful falsehood into the sacred books unravels the whole.

Related arguments: the Antioch clash — see also a staged pretense, neither apostle really erring (Jerome)

The text at issue

But when Peter was come to Antioch, I withstood him to the face, because he was to be blamed. For before that certain came from James, he did eat with the Gentiles: but when they were come, he withdrew and separated himself, fearing them which were of the circumcision. And the other Jews dissembled likewise with him; insomuch that Barnabas also was carried away with their dissimulation. But when I saw that they walked not uprightly according to the truth of the gospel, I said unto Peter before them all, If thou, being a Jew, livest after the manner of Gentiles, and not as do the Jews, why compellest thou the Gentiles to live as do the Jews?

— Galatians 2:11–14 (KJV).1 On the reading defended here, this is a report of what actually happened: Peter committed a real fault — a failure of nerve before the circumcision party — and Paul rebuked him truthfully and to his face, for the truth of the gospel. Nothing was staged.

Who advances the reading

The real-rebuke reading is Augustine's, pressed in his Letters 28, 40, and 82 to Jerome: Peter erred, Paul rebuked him truthfully, and the narrative records a real event. Augustine wrote to ask Jerome outright to recant the rival “economy” reading — to sing, in the Greek phrase, a palinode — and stood on the plain sense against the whole Greek pedigree Jerome invoked.2

The reading, at its strongest

Augustine's case is the plain sense of the passage, and it is powerful on two fronts — the exegetical and the theological.

The text. Paul says Peter “was to be blamed,” that he “withdrew and separated himself, fearing them which were of the circumcision,” that the others were “carried away with their dissimulation,” and that “they walked not uprightly according to the truth of the gospel.” None of this reads as a rehearsed tableau; it reads as a report of a genuine failure of nerve, named as such. And Paul frames the whole Antioch narrative under a solemn oath sworn a few verses earlier — “the things which I write unto you, behold, before God, I lie not” (Gal 1:20) — which the staged reading must quietly suspend.3

The stakes. Augustine's deeper objection is that the staged reading buys the apostles' dignity at a ruinous price: it makes an inspired writer record, and endorse, a deliberate deception. Once that is admitted anywhere, he argues, nothing in Scripture is safe from the same solvent:

For if you once admit into such a high sanctuary of authority one false statement as made in the way of duty, there will not be left a single sentence of those books which, if appearing to any one difficult in practice or hard to believe, may not by the same fatal rule be explained away, as a statement in which, intentionally, and under a sense of duty, the author declared what was not true.

— Augustine, Letter 28, §3. He would not concede it: “I read that Paul wrote this, and I do not believe that he lied.” Peter really erred; Paul really and truthfully rebuked him; and the episode is the more edifying, not less, for being real — a lesson that even the chief apostle could be corrected, and humbly accept correction, for the truth of the gospel.4

Adversarial assessment

The rival, and why it loses. The one serious competitor is the staged “economy” reading Jerome took from Origen and the Greek fathers — that Peter only feigned to keep the law and Paul only appeared to rebuke him, so that neither really sinned. That reading is not baseless: it descends from a careful tradition, it answered the pagan critic Porphyry, and it solves a real difficulty (how the chief apostle could publicly err, and how Paul could blame in Peter what he himself practiced in becoming “to the Jews … as a Jew”). But it solves the problem at too high a cost, and it founders on the words Paul actually chose: to read “fearing,” “dissimulation,” and “walked not uprightly” as a benevolent charade is to make the text mean the reverse of what it says, and the principle it smuggles in — that a sacred author may state what is false out of a sense of duty — is one no reader could contain to this passage alone. The honest answer to Porphyry is not a mock quarrel but the truth: the apostles were men, one faltered, and Scripture unflinchingly says so.5

One caveat on the framing. Augustine had the sounder reading of this text, and won it in the long run; but he pressed the underlying claim broadly — that any dutiful falsehood anywhere unravels all of Scripture — and that broad claim is a strong one that later readers have sometimes thought overstated. The verdict here is on the exegesis of Galatians 2, where the evidence is squarely with him; the wider inerrancy principle is a separate and more contestable matter, noted on his page.

Assessment

Sound. That Peter genuinely erred at Antioch and that Paul genuinely, truthfully, and publicly corrected him is the plain sense of Galatians 2 and has become the settled verdict of later interpretation; Augustine won the exegetical argument, and his companion principle — that inerrancy belongs to the canonical books alone, and that admitting one dutiful falsehood there unravels the whole — became the classic Latin statement of the point. The rival staged reading, though it carried the authority of Origen and the Greek fathers and answered a real apologetic worry, does not survive the words Paul actually wrote, and Jerome himself never pressed it as more than a reported opinion. The disagreement is flagged on both men's pages — as the sounder instinct on Augustine's, and as the losing (but pedigreed, and self-qualified) reading on Jerome's.
