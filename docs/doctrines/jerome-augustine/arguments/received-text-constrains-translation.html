Is the churches' settled text a legitimate constraint on a new translation?

Type: interpretive argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — Augustine's objection to Jerome's new Latin Old Testament was not that it was inaccurate but that it was unfamiliar: the version the congregations had memorised and sung had a claim of its own, and a scholar working alone could not be checked by the churches that would have to live with his results. As a rule of pastoral prudence the argument is strong and has been vindicated by later practice; as a rule of rendering — keep the old word even where it is wrong — it subordinates the sense of Scripture to habit, and Augustine pressed it that far.

Related arguments: translating the Old Testament — see also what the plant of Jonah 4:6 actually was · whether the Latin Old Testament should be made from the Hebrew

The claim

… my only reason for objecting to the public reading of your translation from the Hebrew in our churches was, lest, bringing forward anything which was, as it were, new and opposed to the authority of the Septuagint version, we should trouble by serious cause of offence the flocks of Christ, whose ears and hearts have become accustomed to listen to that version to which the seal of approbation was given by the apostles themselves.

— Augustine, Letter 82 (to Jerome), §35. The claim is that a translator is answerable not only to the original but to the receiving church: a text long read, chanted and catechised from is not a neutral object that may be silently replaced.1

Who advances it

The argument is Augustine's, made three times over a decade of letters to Jerome and never withdrawn even after he had conceded the scholarly merits of Jerome's project. He first put it as a worry about church unity — a Latin Bible following the Hebrew would divide the Latin churches from the Greek — then illustrated it with the riot at Oea, and finally stated it as the sole ground of his objection in Letter 82. It is the ancestor of every later argument that the liturgical and memorised text of a community constrains its revisers.2

The case, at its strongest

The demonstrated cost. Augustine did not argue in the abstract. A bishop in his province introduced Jerome's version, hit one unfamiliar word in Jonah, and nearly lost his congregation over it — “there arose such a tumult in the congregation, especially among the Greeks … that the bishop was compelled to ask the testimony of the Jewish residents.” The man had to retract the reading. Whatever the merits of the new rendering, the transition had been managed in a way that put a church at risk, and the risk was borne by the bishop, not the translator.3

The verifiability problem. Augustine's sharpest point is epistemic rather than sentimental. A Latin version made from the Greek can be audited by anyone: “the discrepancy is easily condemned in a Latin version by the production of the original in Greek, which is a language very widely known.” A version from the Hebrew cannot be:

… if any one has been disturbed by the occurrence of something to which he was not accustomed in the translation taken from the Hebrew, and alleges that the new translation is wrong, it will be found difficult, if not impossible, to get at the Hebrew documents by which the version to which exception is taken may be defended. … Besides all this, Jews, if consulted as to the meaning of the Hebrew text, may give a different opinion from yours: in which case it will seem as if your presence were indispensable, as being the only one who could refute their view.

— Augustine, Letter 71 (to Jerome), §4. This is an argument about accountability: a version the receiving community has no means of checking rests on the personal authority of one man, and Jerome's own answer — “ask the Jews what is the meaning of the original” — was refuted at Oea, where they asked the Jews and got the wrong answer.4

The vindication in practice. The Church has behaved, on the whole, as Augustine advised. Jerome's own Psalter from the Hebrew never displaced the older Latin Psalter made from the Greek in the liturgy; it was the Gallican Psalter, revised from the Septuagint column of the Hexapla, that was carried in the Vulgate and sung in choir for more than a millennium — the one book of the Old Testament where the argument from familiarity simply won. The same instinct governs modern revision: committees weigh the retention of well-known renderings, and translations that break sharply with a memorised text are routinely resisted for reasons that have nothing to do with accuracy.5

Adversarial assessment

What holds. As a claim about the process of revision, Augustine is right, and Jerome never really answered him. A translator working for the churches owes them an account of what he has changed and why; a rendering sprung on a congregation mid-lesson, with no note, no explanation and no one present who can defend it, is a pastoral failure whether or not it is a philological success. The verifiability point is stronger still and has aged well: it is the reason critical apparatus, footnotes and marginal alternatives exist. Jerome had used exactly that device in his earlier version from the Greek, with its asterisks and obelisks, and Augustine's complaint in Letter 71 is precisely that the new version from the Hebrew had dropped it.6

Where it fails. Augustine did not stop at process. Told that the Hebrew word in Jonah was neither gourd nor ivy but a third plant, he answered that he would keep “gourd” anyway — “I would prefer to call it ‘gourd’ in all our Latin versions.” In that form the argument makes the received rendering effectively unrevisable, since every correction is by definition unfamiliar; it converts a reason for caution into a reason for never changing anything, and it gives custom a veto over the sense of the text. An error that has been sung for generations is still an error, and the fact that it is well-loved is an argument for explaining the correction, not for suppressing it. The riot at Oea shows how not to introduce a revision; it does not show that revision was wrong.7

The premise underneath. Augustine's version of the argument leans on a further claim — that the Septuagint carries “the seal of approbation … given by the apostles themselves,” and (in the City of God) that its translators were themselves prophetically guided. That is a separate question, weighed on this wiki under the Septuagint's origin and the Old Testament canon. The pastoral argument does not need it: a text can have a real claim on a community by long use alone, without being inspired in its translation.8

Assessment

Contested. Judged as translation theory the question has no settled winner, and the two disciplines that judge it disagree. By the lights of textual scholarship Augustine loses: accuracy is what a translation is for, and a rule that freezes a known error because congregations are used to it cannot be defended. By the lights of pastoral and liturgical practice he wins, and has largely been followed — the Gallican Psalter outlived the Hebraica veritas in the choir, and no responsible modern revision ignores what a community has memorised. The durable form of his argument is procedural rather than substantive: the received text constrains how a revision is introduced (with annotation, explanation and the means for the church to check it), not whether a demonstrated mistranslation may be corrected. Augustine himself pressed past that line in Letter 82 §35, which is an overreach and is noted on his page; the rival case for going to the Hebrew is weighed separately, and the word that started the fight turns out to have been mistranslated by both sides.
