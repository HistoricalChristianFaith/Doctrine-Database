The plant of Jonah 4:6: the castor-oil plant, and neither “gourd” nor “ivy”?

Type: interpretive argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: sound — the Hebrew qiqayon that shaded Jonah is, on the best available evidence, the castor-oil plant (Ricinus communis), which is what Jerome himself described when pressed. Both renderings that Africa fought over — the Old Latin and Septuagint cucurbita (“gourd”) and Jerome's own printed hedera (“ivy”) — name the wrong plant, and Jerome conceded as much even while keeping “ivy” in his text.

Related arguments: translating the Old Testament — see also whether the churches' settled text constrains a translator · whether the Latin Old Testament should be made from the Hebrew

The text at issue

And the LORD God prepared a gourd, and made it to come up over Jonah, that it might be a shadow over his head, to deliver him from his grief. So Jonah was exceeding glad of the gourd.

— Jonah 4:6 (KJV). The Hebrew word is qiqayon, a word that occurs nowhere else in the Bible. The Septuagint made it κολοκύνθη, “gourd,” and the Old Latin followed with cucurbita; Aquila and the later Greek revisers made it κισσός, “ivy,” and Jerome's Vulgate followed them with hedera. That single word emptied a church: when a bishop at Oea read Jerome's version aloud, the congregation rioted, and he had to restore the familiar “gourd” “as he desired not to be left without a congregation — a calamity which he narrowly escaped.”1

Who advances the identification

In its ancient form the identification is Jerome's own. He never claimed that hedera was the plant; challenged over it, he described a third thing altogether, naming it by its Hebrew and Syriac names and explaining that he printed “ivy” only for want of a Latin word and to avoid standing alone against every previous translator. Modern lexicography has converged on the same plant Jerome described, the castor-oil plant or Ricinus communis, and the Talmudic tradition had already identified the qiqayon of Jonah with the plant that yields the oil of kik.2

The evidence for

Jerome's description, made from the plant itself. Twice — in his commentary on Jonah and again in his reply to Augustine — Jerome gives a botanist's account of what he had seen growing in Palestine:

… the Hebrew manuscript has “ciceion,” which is in the Syriac tongue, as now spoken, “ciceia.” It is a kind of shrub having large leaves like a vine, and when planted it quickly springs up to the size of a small tree, standing upright by its own stem, without requiring any support of canes or poles, as both gourds and ivy do.

— Jerome, Letter 112 (to Augustine), §22. The commentary adds that it is “often found growing in Palestine especially in sandy areas,” that it “casts a large shadow and is supported by a trunk,” and that from seed “in a few days it can be seen to have grown from a seedling to a bush.” Every clause of that fits Ricinus communis: a fast-growing shrub of wadi banks and sandy ground in the Levant, self-supporting on a woody stem, with very large palmate leaves — the plant Latin herbals call palma Christi.3

The word. Hebrew qiqayon answers to Egyptian kiki, the name Herodotus, Dioscorides, Strabo and Pliny all give to the Egyptian oil-plant, and to Akkadian kukkanitu; Jerome's Syriac and Punic ciceia is the same word still in local use. The rabbis made the same identification independently, glossing the qiqayon of Jonah by the oil of kik; modern Hebrew still calls the castor-oil plant kikayon.4

What the two rival renderings require. Both losing candidates are trailing plants. A gourd and an ivy alike creep unless they are given canes, poles or a trellis — a point Jerome presses hard, since Jonah's plant came up over his head in a night with nothing to climb. Neither Greek nor Latin translator claimed access to better information than the Hebrew word itself; Aquila's “ivy” was already a guess at an unknown, and it is the guess Jerome inherited rather than a finding he made.5

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The lexical case is strong and comes from three independent directions — the Egyptian and Akkadian cognates, the rabbinic gloss, and Jerome's own autopsy of a plant growing in Palestine that matches the castor bean in habit, leaf, speed of growth and self-supporting stem. Nothing in the narrative resists it: a plant that shoots up quickly and withers when a worm strikes it (Jonah 4:7) is exactly the shape of a Ricinus miracle: a shrub standing on one soft stalk is precisely the kind of plant a boring worm can kill outright, as the next verse says this one did.

What is less certain than it looks. Ancient plant-names are slippery, and a hapax legomenon can never be pinned with the confidence of a common noun. A minority of commentators still prefer the bottle gourd (Cucurbita lagenaria, Arabic qarʻah), which in Palestine and Mesopotamia is routinely trained over exactly the kind of field-booth Jonah had built — so the Septuagint's “gourd” may reflect a real Egyptian-Jewish judgment about what shades a shelter rather than a shot in the dark. That was Augustine's instinct: “I do not think that the Seventy would have rendered it thus at random, had they not known that the plant was something like a gourd.” The reply is that the Septuagint's translators were rendering a word they did not know into a culture that grew gourds, and that the independent Egyptian and Semitic evidence for kiki was not available to them.6

Where Jerome overreached, and where he did not. Jerome was right about the plant and wrong in his printed text: having identified a shrub that is neither gourd nor ivy, he set down hedera anyway — “that I might not differ from all other translators” — and then defended the rendering as though the Hebrew supported it. That is a translator's compromise dressed as a finding, and it left him defending a word he had himself shown to be wrong. His polemical explanation of the affair — that the Jews of Oea answered “either through malice or ignorance,” to “make sport of the gourd-planters” — also does more work than the evidence carries: consulted Jews with no Hebrew scroll to hand, asked in Latin or Punic about a Latin word, could easily have given an honest and wrong answer. Both points are noted on his page; neither touches the identification itself.7

Assessment

Sound. That the qiqayon of Jonah 4:6 is the castor-oil plant is the mainstream lexical judgment, resting on the Egyptian and Akkadian cognates, the rabbinic identification, and Jerome's own eyewitness description of the shrub — and the bottle-gourd alternative, though not absurd, has only the Septuagint's guess and the shape of a field-booth behind it. Certainty is not available for a word that occurs once in the Hebrew Bible, and the sensible modern practice is to transliterate or annotate rather than to name a familiar European plant. The irony of the quarrel is that neither party held the right word: the congregation at Oea rioted for a gourd that is not there, and Jerome defended an ivy he had himself just disproved. What the riot actually decided was not botany but whether a settled liturgical text may be revised at all, and behind that, which text a Latin Old Testament should be made from.
