Jerome in the correspondence with Augustine

Dates: c. 347–420 · Wikipedia
Summary: Jerome of Stridon — monk of Bethlehem, translator of the Vulgate, and the foremost biblical scholar of the Latin world — was the elder and the expert in this exchange, and at first its prickliest party. This page follows his side: the guarded, wounded, sometimes waspish replies of 402–404, in which he defended his reading of the Antioch dispute and his translation from the Hebrew and warned the young Augustine off; the thaw of 405; and the unguarded warmth of his last years, when the two were allies against the Pelagians.

The old scholar challenged (402–404)

By the time Augustine's criticisms reached him, Augustine was a rising bishop and Jerome a famous but aging and embattled scholar, lately scarred by his war with Rufinus. He did not take correction from a younger man gracefully. His first reply (402) mixed friendship with a veteran's condescension:

… do not because you are young challenge a veteran in the field of Scripture.

— Jerome, Letter 102 (to Augustine), §2, source. He cited “the proverb, The tired ox treads with a firmer step,” and pleaded his age: “I have had my time, and have run my course to the utmost of my strength. It is but fair that I should rest.”1 What especially rankled was that Augustine's criticism seemed to reach everyone before it reached him:

I am at a loss to express my surprise that the same letter is reported to be in the possession of most of the Christians in Rome, and throughout Italy, and has come to every one but myself, to whom alone it was ostensibly sent.

— Jerome, Letter 105 (to Augustine), §1, source. He suspected the whole affair was a young man's bid “to become famous at my expense,” and asked to be left in peace — that Augustine “desist from annoying an old man, who seeks retirement in his monastic cell.”2

The Galatians defense (404)

Jerome's long reply of 404 met the exegetical charge head-on. He had read the confrontation of Peter by Paul at Antioch (Gal 2:11–14) as a matter of prudent management, not real fault — Peter only pretending to keep the law he knew to be abrogated, Paul only appearing to rebuke him — and he insisted he had not invented this but inherited it from the Greek fathers, Origen chief among them, along with Didymus, Apollinaris of Laodicea, Eusebius of Emesa, Theodorus of Heraclea, and “John, who has long governed the Church of Constantinople” (Chrysostom):

… Peter was thoroughly aware of the abrogation of the law of Moses, but was compelled by fear to pretend to observe it …

— Jerome, Letter 112 (to Augustine), §8, source. So, he concluded, “neither did Peter sin, nor did Paul rebuke presumptuously a greater than himself.” Yet he held the reading loosely, telling Augustine he had “not adopt[ed] finally and irrevocably that which I had read in these Greek authors, but had propounded what I had read, leaving to the reader's own judgment whether it should be rejected or approved.” Whether that reading holds — and it does not, on its best case — is weighed on its own page (assessment: sound, for the rival real-rebuke reading); Jerome's loss of the exegetical argument, and his honest hedge, are noted there.3

Hebrew verity — and the old-soldier plea

On translation Jerome was unmovable. Against Augustine's plea that he render the Greek Septuagint, he defended his new version from the Hebrew — the Hebraica veritas — explaining that “translating from the Hebrew itself, I have expressed what I understood it to mean, being careful to preserve rather the exact sense than the order of the words,” and defending his “ivy” for the plant in Jonah (against the old Latin “gourd”) from the Hebrew ciceion. And he begged the younger man, again, to let him be:

… have some consideration for a soldier who is now old and has long retired from active service, and not to force him to take the field and again expose his life to the chances of war.

— Jerome, Letter 112 (to Augustine), §22, source. Jerome's Hebrew–versus–Greek stand is the same one at issue across the wiki's pages on the Septuagint's origin and the Old Testament canon, where his “Hebrew verity” is weighed at length.4

The thaw (405)

Once Augustine's irenic Letter 82 arrived — conceding nothing on the substance but framing the whole quarrel with grace — the heat went out of Jerome. His answer proposed a truce:

… let there be sincere brotherliness between us; and henceforth let us exchange letters, not of controversy, but of mutual charity.

— Jerome, Letter 115 (to Augustine), §1, source. “Let us,” he added, “exercise ourselves in the field of Scripture without wounding each other.”5

The warm alliance (416–419)

The truce ripened into friendship. When the Pelagian controversy drew the two together, Jerome — whose own Bethlehem monastery would be raided by Pelagian partisans — welcomed Augustine's envoy Orosius and wrote with an affection his earlier letters had withheld:

I am resolved to love you, to look up to you, to reverence and admire you, and to defend your opinions as my own.

— Jerome, Letter 134 (to Augustine), §1, source. In his last letters he hailed Augustine as “the restorer of the ancient faith,” renowned “throughout the whole world” and abhorred by “all heretics,” and longed, across the sea, to “take the wings of a dove and fly to be folded in your embrace” — crediting Augustine's “co-operation and … leadership” with “strangling the heresy of Celestius.” Jerome died at Bethlehem in 420; the old rival had become, at the last, his dearest correspondent.6

Notes

  • Jerome's other great falling-out — with his boyhood friend Rufinus, over Origen — ran through these very same years; Augustine wrote grieving it and urging peace. That story is told on its own timeline, where Jerome also has a detail page.
  • The pattern of Jerome's conduct here rhymes with his Rufinus quarrel: a scholar quick to feel slighted, sharp in reply, and slow to forgive a challenge to his learning — yet capable, once the pressure eased, of real warmth. With Augustine the reconciliation held; with Rufinus it never came.
  • Jerome's Antioch reading is the losing side of the argument page, but it is treated there as a pedigreed and self-qualified reading, not a blunder: it descended from Origen and the Greek mainstream, answered a live pagan objection, and Jerome himself refused to make it a dogma.
