Bartholomew

Bartholomew is the most shadowy of the Twelve. His name is not a name at all but a patronymic — bar-Tolmai, “son of Tolmai” — and the New Testament tells us nothing else about him: he stands in the three Synoptic lists and in Acts, always next to Philip, and then vanishes. Everything the church came to say of him — that he was really the Nathanael whom Philip fetched to Jesus in John’s Gospel, that he carried the faith to “India” and left a Hebrew Gospel of Matthew there, that he was flayed alive in Armenia — grew up afterward, and each claim must be weighed on its own date. This page is not a life of Bartholomew so much as a history of what was remembered and claimed about him, set out earliest source first: the bare Gospel notices, the early Alexandrian report of an eastern mission, the mutually-dependent apostle-lists that fixed Armenia and a martyr’s death, the medieval legend that supplied the flaying, and the modern scholarship that grades them all. This is a biographical tradition-timeline: its “witnesses” are the datable sources that report on Bartholomew, and the contested questions — was he Nathanael? where did he preach? was he flayed? — are weighed on their own argument pages below rather than settled by assertion. For the shared ladder of sources and how far to trust each rung, see the apostles hub.

Timeline

The New Testament (c. 30–90)

The New Testament names Bartholomew four times and never once describes him. He appears only in the lists of the Twelve — in Matthew, Mark, and Luke he is paired directly with Philip, and in Acts he stands a little apart, after Thomas — and no word survives of anything he said, did, went, or suffered. His name is a patronymic, “son of Tolmai,” which means he must also have borne a personal name the lists do not give. John’s Gospel, which never mentions a Bartholomew, instead tells of Nathanael of Cana, whom Philip fetches to Jesus with “We have found him, of whom Moses in the law, and the prophets, did write,” and who is later named among the disciples who go fishing with Peter. That Philip’s list-partner Bartholomew and Philip’s convert Nathanael are one man is the great harmonizing guess on which most of the later tradition hangs.1

Pantaenus’s report, preserved by Eusebius (c. 180, reported c. 325)

The earliest tradition of where Bartholomew went comes from Pantaenus, the head of the catechetical school of Alexandria, who (Eusebius reports) travelled as a missionary “as far as India” about the year 180 and found there a Gospel of Matthew in Hebrew that the Christians said “Bartholomew, one of the apostles, had preached to them, and left with them… which they had preserved till that time.” Jerome repeats the story two centuries later. It is the one early, sober notice of a Bartholomew mission — but “India” in ancient usage was elastic, stretching to Arabia Felix and Ethiopia, so where exactly Pantaenus went, and whether the eastern Christians had really named Bartholomew, are open questions.2

The apostle-lists (uncertain date, mutually dependent)

The apostle-list literature — Pseudo-Hippolytus On the Twelve Apostles, Pseudo-Dorotheus, the Byzantine and Latin catalogues — is where Bartholomew’s biography hardens into a mission field and a death. Pseudo-Hippolytus keeps the India of Pantaenus but adds the end: “Bartholomew, again, preached to the Indians, to whom he also gave the Gospel according to Matthew, and was crucified with his head downward, and was buried in Allanum, a town of the great Armenia.” Here Armenia enters, and here the martyrdom is first stated — though as crucifixion, not flaying. These lists are late, of uncertain date, and copy one another; they are church memory, not history, and they do not agree on how he died.3

The Golden Legend (c. 1260)

Jacobus de Voragine’s Golden Legend gives the full romance that fixed Bartholomew in Christian art: in “India” he casts a demon out of the idol Astaroth, heals the mad daughter of King Polymius, converts the king, and is seized at the urging of the king’s brother Astyages, who has him put to death at Albanopolis in Armenia. The Legend openly records that the sources disagree on the manner — “the blessed Dorotheus saith that he was crucified… S. Theoderus saith that he was flayed, and it is read in many books that he was beheaded only” — and reconciles them by having him crucified, taken down, flayed, and beheaded. It is late medieval hagiography, a millennium and more after the events, but it is the wellspring of the flayed-alive Bartholomew of Michelangelo and the sculptors.4

Modern scholarship

Critical study grades this tradition low. Bartholomew is one of the apostles for whom no early, independent testimony to a death survives at all; Sean McDowell, weighing each martyrdom account on a probability scale, places Bartholomew’s among the least secure. The very disagreement of the sources — crucified, flayed, or beheaded; in India or in Armenia — is itself the tell of a memory filled in after the fact. What can be said is modest: a Bartholomew was numbered among the Twelve, an early tradition sent him east, and by the Middle Ages the church remembered him as a martyr of Armenia, flayed for his faith.5

Arguments

Was Bartholomew the Nathanael of John’s Gospel? (assessment: plausible)

The Nathanael identification is the hinge of the whole tradition, because only through it does the silent Bartholomew acquire a call, a home town, and a scene in the Gospels. The case is a neat convergence: the Synoptics list Bartholomew but never mention Nathanael; John tells of Nathanael but never a Bartholomew; Philip, Bartholomew’s list-partner, is the very man who brings Nathanael to Jesus; and Nathanael appears among a group of apostles in John 21. Against it: no ancient writer equates the two — the identification surfaces only with the medieval harmonists — and John never calls Nathanael one of the Twelve. Coherent and widely held, but resting on inference and unattested in antiquity. Plausible.6

Did Bartholomew preach in India or in Armenia? (assessment: contested)

The mission field is genuinely disputed. The earliest witness, Pantaenus, points to “India” — but the word was stretched in antiquity over Arabia and Ethiopia, and some suspect Pantaenus mistook the Thomas-Christians’ “Mar Thoma” for “Bar Tolmai.” The later and firmer tradition, held to this day by the Armenian Apostolic Church, makes Armenia the field of his labour and death, alongside Thaddaeus. Early evidence for one, weighty tradition for the other, and no clean way to reconcile the routes. Contested.7

Was Bartholomew flayed alive in Armenia? (assessment: weak)

The flaying is the most famous thing “known” about Bartholomew and the least supported. No source within centuries of his life reports his death at all; when accounts do appear they disagree flatly — crucified head-downward (Pseudo-Hippolytus), beheaded (the apostle-lists), or flayed (the apocryphal Martyrdom and the Golden Legend) — and the flaying that conquered Christian art rests only on that late, legendary strand. Weak — a vivid late legend with no early testimony behind it.8
