The Council of Laodicea on the Old Testament Canon

Dates: c. 363–364 (a regional synod of some two dozen bishops of Phrygia Pacatiana in Asia Minor) · Wikipedia · New Testament canon page
Verdict on the deuterocanon: NO — a Hebrew-sized Old Testament, the disputed books absent, with only Baruch and the Epistle of Jeremiah kept (folded into Jeremiah). A regional Eastern synod, a few years before Athanasius's Festal Letter, rules that "only the canonical" books "of the New and Old Testament" may be read in church (canon 59), and — in the much-disputed canon 60 — appends a book-list whose Old Testament runs the twenty-two-book Hebrew reckoning: Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, and the Maccabees all absent, though Baruch and the Epistle of Jeremiah are retained, carried inside "Jeremiah" as the Greek Bibles carried them. It is the Eastern list-side counterpart to the West's African councils — the same generation, the opposite verdict — and, because a later note appended it to the Trullan and Nicene-II canon collections, the one short-canon list that acquired quasi-conciliar standing in the East.

Canon 59 — only "the canonical" books in church

The fifty-ninth canon lays down the governing rule, the same "read in church" test the African councils would frame positively a generation later, here stated as a prohibition:1

Let no private psalms nor any uncanonical books be read in the church, but only the Canonical Books of the New and Old Testament.

Canon 59 names no books; it only draws the boundary. What fills it is the list of canon 60 — but that list's genuineness is itself contested (see the note below).

Canon 60 — the Old Testament list, the deuterocanon absent

The sixtieth canon supplies the catalogue. Its Old Testament is the Hebrew twenty-two, book for book, with the deuterocanonical histories and Wisdom books nowhere in it:2

And these are the books of the Old Testament which ought to be read: 1, Genesis of the world; 2, The Exodus from Egypt; 3, Leviticus; 4, Numbers; 5, Deuteronomy; 6, Joshua, the son of Nun; 7, Judges, Ruth; 8, Esther; 9, Of the Kings, First and Second; 10, Of the Kings, Third and Fourth; 11, Chronicles, First and Second; 12, Esdras, First and Second; 13, The Book of Psalms; 14, The Proverbs of Solomon; 15, Ecclesiastes; 16, The Song of Songs; 17, Job; 18, The Twelve Prophets; 19, Isaiah; 20, Jeremiah, and Baruch, the Lamentations, and the Epistle; 21, Ezekiel; 22, Daniel.

So Tobit, Judith, the Wisdom of Solomon, Sirach (Ecclesiasticus), and the two books of Maccabees — the whole of the disputed longer canon that the African councils would list flatly among the Scriptures — are simply absent. The one exception is item 20: "Jeremiah, and Baruch, the Lamentations, and the Epistle" keeps Baruch and the Epistle of Jeremiah in the canon, but folded inside the single entry "Jeremiah," exactly as Origen, Cyril, and Athanasius counted their Jeremiah — a book of the Hebrew twenty-two carrying two Greek accretions under its name, not two extra books added to it. The list thus lands on the same twenty-two-book reckoning as its Eastern neighbours, on the "list" side of the list-versus-usage divide these fathers embody.

The doubtful genuineness of canon 60

Canon 60 is not securely part of the synod's acts. Percival's own edition heads it "[N.B.—This Canon is of most questionable genuineness]," and the standard objection is that the book-list is missing from a range of early manuscripts and canon-collections of Laodicea — it is absent from the Latin versions of Dionysius Exiguus and John of Antioch, and Westcott judged "the external evidence… decidedly against the authenticity" of the enumeration, which many hold was appended later to spell out the "canonical books" canon 59 had left unspecified.3 The verdict on this page therefore attaches to canon 60 as it stands in the received text: even bracketed as a possible later gloss, it is an early witness — whether of c. 363 or of a somewhat later hand — to the Hebrew-sized Eastern reckoning that Cyril (350) and Athanasius (367) also drew, and canon 59's own "only the Canonical Books" boundary is not in doubt.

Notes

  • The list-side of the divide. Laodicea belongs with the Eastern list-makers who ranked the deuterocanon out — Cyril, Athanasius, Gregory of Nazianzus, Amphilochius — against the "usage" side (Basil, and the Western councils) that went on quoting those books as Scripture. Its retention of Baruch and the Epistle of Jeremiah under "Jeremiah" is the same half-step Athanasius takes, whose canonical Jeremiah likewise carried Baruch folded in.
  • The same witness, two doctrines. This page treats Laodicea's Old-Testament list; the parallel New Testament canon page treats canon 60's New Testament — the present twenty-seven minus the Apocalypse, the sharpest conciliar instance of the Eastern hesitancy over Revelation that Cyril also shows. Both rest on the one disputed canon 60.
  • Ratified en bloc, centuries later. The Council in Trullo (692), canon 2, confirmed the canons of Laodicea alongside those of Carthage and Athanasius — lists that contradict each other on precisely the disputed books — so that Laodicea's short Old Testament and Carthage's long one both came to carry Eastern conciliar authority. The bishops received the earlier councils' canons "in block," not adjudicating between their rival Scripture-lists (see the Quinisext witness page).
