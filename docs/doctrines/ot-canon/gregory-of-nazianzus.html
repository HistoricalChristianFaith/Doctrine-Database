Gregory of Nazianzus on the Old Testament Canon

Dates: c. 389 AD (the canon list is the verse poem Concerning the Genuine Books of Divinely Inspired Scripture [Carmina dogmatica I.1.12], a didactic poem composed in his retirement years at Cappadocia, c. 383–389; Gregory c. 329–390, "the Theologian," one of the Cappadocian Fathers and briefly archbishop of Constantinople) · Wikipedia · back to timeline
Verdict on the deuterocanon: NO — a strict Hebrew-sized canon, narrower than most. Gregory versifies the Old Testament for a friend and the disputed Greek books are simply not there: no Tobit, no Judith, no Wisdom of Solomon, no Sirach, no Maccabees. His list runs even shorter than his neighbours' — it omits Esther entirely, fixing the total at twenty-two "corresponding to the number of the Hebrew letters." He warns "let not extraneous books seduce your mind. For many malignant writings have been disseminated." An Eastern Hebrew-canon witness of a piece with his cousin Amphilochius of Iconium, alongside Cyril of Jerusalem, Athanasius, and Epiphanius.

Claims & evidence

The twenty-two books — "corresponding to the number of the Hebrew letters"

Casting the canon as a teaching poem for a friend, Gregory opens by charging him to rehearse "the divine oracles" and to refuse the books that fall outside the list, then recites the Old Testament book by book and seals it with the Hebrew-letter count:1

The divine oracles should always on the tongue and in the mind be rehearsed… But let not extraneous books seduce your mind. For many malignant writings have been disseminated. Accept, o friend, this my approved number. These are all twelve of the historical books, of the most ancient Hebrew wisdom: First there is Genesis, then Exodus, Leviticus too. Then Numbers, and the Second Law. Then Joshua and Judges. Ruth is eighth. The ninth and tenth books [are] the acts of Kings, and [the eleventh is] Chronicles. Last you have Ezra. The poetic books are five: Job being first, then [the Psalms of] David; and three of Solomon, Ecclesiastes, Canticles and Proverbs. And similarly five of prophetic inspiration. There are the Twelve written in one book: Hosea and Amos, and Micah the third; then Joel, and Jonah, Obadiah, Nahum also, and Habakkuk, and Zephaniah, Haggai, then Zechariah, and Malachi. All these are one. The second is of Isaiah. Then the one called as an infant, Jeremiah, Then Ezekiel, and the gift of Daniel. I count therefore, twenty-two of the ancient books, corresponding to the number of the Hebrew letters.

The structure is the familiar Eastern one: twelve historical books (with Ruth folded after Judges, the paired books of Kings counted with Chronicles, and "Ezra" standing for Ezra–Nehemiah), five poetic, and five prophetic (the Twelve Minor Prophets as a single book, then Isaiah, Jeremiah, Ezekiel, Daniel). The result is the protocanonical Hebrew canon — the same boundary Cyril and Athanasius draw on this timeline, and the near-twin of his cousin Amphilochius's verse list.

The deuterocanon is absent

Counted out against the disputed books, the verdict is plain: no Tobit, no Judith, no Wisdom of Solomon, no Sirach, no Maccabees — the very Greek books a Septuagint-sized canon would carry are nowhere in the poem.1 Nor, unlike Cyril's Jeremiah, is the list even softened by a folded-in Baruch or Epistle of Jeremiah: Gregory names "Jeremiah" alone. And the warning that frames the recitation is aimed at exactly this surplus — "let not extraneous books seduce your mind. For many malignant writings have been disseminated" — so that the "approved number" is offered as a fence against the books outside it.

Narrower than his neighbours — Esther omitted entirely

Gregory's list is stricter even than the other Hebrew-canon Fathers on one protocanonical book: it omits Esther altogether. Where Cyril counts Esther as his twelfth book and Amphilochius appends it as a book "some approve," Gregory does not name it at all — his enumeration runs from Genesis to Daniel without it, and lands on twenty-two precisely because Esther is left out.1 In this he tracks Athanasius, whose festal list likewise drops Esther from the canon proper. A Father this sparing — declining even a protocanonical book that the rest of the Eastern tradition kept — plainly left no room for Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, or the Maccabees, none of which he so much as mentions.

Notes

  • A canon in verse, paired with Amphilochius. The poem's striking literary form — the Scripture canon set out as didactic verse — is shared in this era only with the Iambics for Seleucus of Amphilochius of Iconium, Gregory's cousin and fellow associate of Basil. The two poems form a tight Cappadocian testimony to the shorter Old Testament, fixing the books "one by one" so a pupil "may learn them clearly" rather than by any conciliar decree — the characteristic fourth-century Eastern mode.
  • How the count reaches twenty-two. Gregory groups the books so that Ruth attaches to Judges, the two books of Kings (1–2 Samuel + 1–2 Kings) go with Chronicles, Ezra stands for Ezra–Nehemiah, and the Twelve Minor Prophets count as one. With Esther omitted, the historical, poetic, and prophetic groups total exactly twenty-two — the number of letters in the Hebrew alphabet, the same numerological frame Cyril, Athanasius, and Jerome use, though their lists keep Esther and reach twenty-two by a different reckoning.
  • The New Testament is treated elsewhere. The same poem goes on to list the New Testament books, where Gregory's catalogue famously omits the Apocalypse — a matter belonging to the New-Testament-canon question, not the deuterocanon, and so not pressed here.
  • A genuinely strict witness. Among the Hebrew-canon Fathers gathered on this timeline, Gregory's is the leanest Old Testament: no deuterocanon, no bundled Baruch, and not even Esther. He is the cleanest available counter-witness to a Septuagint-sized canon — though, like the rest of the Eastern circle, he read his Scriptures from the Greek Bible that also carried the books he leaves off the list.
