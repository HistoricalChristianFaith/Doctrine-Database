The Glossa Ordinaria on the Old Testament Canon

Dates: The standard glossed Bible of the medieval Latin West; its core compiled at the cathedral school of Laon c. 1120–1135 (Anselm of Laon, d. 1117; his brother Ralph of Laon, d. c. 1133; Gilbert the Universal, d. 1134), reaching its settled "ordinary" Parisian form by the later twelfth century, then diffused in manuscript across every medieval school and printed from the editio princeps of Adolf Rusch (Strasbourg, 1480/81) · Wikipedia
Verdict on the deuterocanon: NO — but Jerome transmitted, not an independent verdict. The glossed Bible carried Jerome's own Vulgate prefaces as front matter, so its book-prologues ranking Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, and the Maccabees outside the canon — read "for the edification of the people, not for confirming the authority of ecclesiastical doctrines" — are one voice (Jerome's) diffused through every medieval schoolroom, not a fresh medieval judgment reached anew. Its real significance is about the ubiquity of Jerome's distinction, not a count of independent minds — and the sharpest, most systematized "not in the canon" rubrics apologists quote come from a late printed edition (Basel, 1498), not the twelfth-century gloss.

Claims & evidence

The glossed Bible: Scripture ringed by the Fathers

The Glossa Ordinaria ("the ordinary/standard gloss") was the medieval West's default study Bible: the Vulgate text laid out in a large central column, with brief word-level notes written between the lines (the interlinear gloss) and longer patristic excerpts — Jerome, Augustine, Gregory, Bede, Isidore, Rabanus Maurus — arrayed around the margins (the marginal gloss). Assembled at the cathedral school of Laon in the early twelfth century and standardized at Paris by the century's end, it became, in the words of the popular apologetic, "the standard commentary used in every single center of theological learning for hundreds of years."1 Its authorship was for centuries misassigned to the ninth-century monk Walafrid Strabo — an attribution first floated by Johannes Trithemius around 1500 and printed in the gloss itself only from the 1590 Paris edition, then queried by Samuel Berger (1893) and dismantled by Ceslas Spicq (1944) and Beryl Smalley, who reassigned the work to the school of Anselm of Laon (d. 1117), his brother Ralph of Laon, and Gilbert the Universal (d. 1134, to whom the gloss on Lamentations is firmly credited). The point is not antiquarian: "Strabo, ninth century" made the Glossa look like an early and independent Carolingian witness against the deuterocanon; correctly dated to the twelfth-century Laon compilation, it is a witness to how Jerome's distinction was transmitted, not to a fresh verdict.2

The canon-prologues are Jerome's prefaces, carried as front matter

The Glossa's Old-Testament book-prologues largely reproduce Jerome's Vulgate prefaces. The line apologists quote as the Glossa's verdict — that the Church reads the disputed books "for the edification of the people, not for confirming the authority of ecclesiastical doctrines" — is verbatim Jerome, from his Prologue to the Books of Solomon:

Sicut ergo Iudith et Tobi et Macchabeorum libros legit quidem Ecclesia, sed inter canonicas scripturas non recipit, sic et haec duo volumina legat ad aedificationem plebis, non ad auctoritatem ecclesiasticorum dogmatum confirmandam.

— Jerome, Prologus in libris Salomonis (the Vulgate preface the Glossa carries).3 In English: "Therefore, just as the Church reads the books of Judith and Tobit and the Maccabees but does not receive them among the canonical Scriptures, so let it read these two volumes [Wisdom and Sirach] for the edification of the people, not for confirming the authority of ecclesiastical doctrines." The companion formula sits in Jerome's Prologus Galeatus, likewise transmitted in the front matter: "Igitur Sapientia, quae vulgo Salomonis inscribitur, et Iesu filii Sirach liber et Iudith et Tobias et Pastor non sunt in canone" — "Therefore Wisdom, which is commonly ascribed to Solomon, and the book of Jesus son of Sirach, and Judith and Tobias and the Shepherd are not in the canon."4 So the Glossa's "not in the canon" is not the medieval schoolmen weighing the canon afresh and siding against the deuterocanon; it is Jerome's own preface, riding in the Bible's front matter into every school that used the standard gloss. The very same church that copied these prologues also read the disputed books in worship and bound them in every Vulgate — the graded, dual pattern this timeline traces from Athanasius and Rufinus onward, not a bare rejection.

One voice diffused, not a second witness

This is why the Glossa cannot be counted as an independent authority against the deuterocanon. The 1913 Catholic Encyclopedia grants the fact and dissolves its force in one sentence: the "Glossa Ordinaria… embodied the prefaces in which the Doctor of Bethlehem had written in terms derogatory to the deuteros, and thus perpetuated and diffused his unfavourable view."5 The Glossa's significance, then, is a fact about circulation, not about how many minds independently judged the canon: Jerome's distinction was ubiquitous in the medieval West precisely because his prologues rode in every glossed Bible. That is the crux of the argument weighed on the summary's "52 ecclesiastical writers and the Glossa Ordinaria" page — the Glossa is one more channel of a single Hieronymian voice, alongside the Complutensian Polyglot and Cardinal Cajetan, not a tally of independent rejecters.

The sharpest "not in the canon" rubrics are a late printed accretion

Protestant surveys often quote the Glossa as prefacing each disputed book with a blunt rubric — "Here begins the book of Tobit, which is not in the canon; here begins the book of Judith, which is not in the canon," and likewise for Wisdom, Ecclesiasticus, and the Maccabees — together with a fuller framing prologue distinguishing "the canonical and the non-canonical books, which the Hebrews separate from the canon."6 ⚠ Unverified against the twelfth-century gloss. William Webster, who presses this evidence hardest, cites it from a late printed edition — the Basel Bible of 1498 (Froben & Petri, with Nicholas of Lyra's Postilla) — not from a twelfth-century manuscript or from Rusch's 1480/81 editio princeps. The substance (Jerome's "not in the canon") travels with the Vulgate from the start; but the sharper, systematized framing sometimes quoted as "the Glossa's" is a feature of the printed tradition, and its wording should be checked folio-by-folio against a digitized edition before it is leaned on as the medieval gloss's own voice.

Notes

  • Where it sits on the timeline. Between the Masoretic Text (c. 1000) and the ecumenical "Yes" of Florence (1442). Through the long medieval lull the Glossa is the visible carrier of the learned Hieronymian distinction — not because a fresh council or school ruled against the deuterocanon, but because Jerome's prologues sat in the front matter of the Bible every schoolman read.
  • Not Walafrid Strabo. The traditional "Strabo, ninth century" attribution is discredited; the Glossa is a twelfth-century compilation of the school of Laon. Dating matters to the argument: it is a witness to the transmission of Jerome, not an independent early-medieval verdict.
  • Keep diffusion and count apart. The verifiable claim is that the Glossa carried Jerome's canon-distinction into every medieval school. The over-pressed claim is that the Glossa is a second, independent witness against the deuterocanon: it is not — it is Jerome's single voice diffused. The strong-form apologetic use is weighed and found overstated on the medieval-mainstream argument page.
  • Text provenance. Jerome's prologues are quoted from the Vulgate prefaces (bible-researcher.com; The Latin Library). A digitized glossed Bible carrying the disputed-book rubrics is the Basel 1498 edition at e-rara.ch; the editio princeps is reproduced in Karlfried Froehlich & Margaret T. Gibson, eds., Biblia Latina cum Glossa Ordinaria (facsimile of Rusch, Strasbourg 1480/81; Turnhout: Brepols, 1992).
