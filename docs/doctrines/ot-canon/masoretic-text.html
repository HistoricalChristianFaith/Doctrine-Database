The Masoretic Text on the Old Testament Canon

Dates: c. 1000 AD (the Masoretic scribal work spans the 7th–10th centuries; the standard codices are the Aleppo Codex, c. 920, and the Leningrad Codex, 1008/1009) · Wikipedia · back to timeline
Verdict on the deuterocanon: NO. The Masoretic Text — the authoritative Hebrew (and Aramaic) text that Rabbinic Judaism fixed letter-by-letter and that most Protestant Old Testaments translate — carries only the twenty-four books of the Hebrew Bible. Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch, and 1–2 Maccabees are not in it. This is the Judaism-side marker for the medieval period: not a fresh ruling on the canon but the crystallized form of the Hebrew-sized Scripture whose boundary the rabbinic stream had already drawn (see the "Council of Jamnia" marker). Its weight on this timeline is that it became the canon-defining text for the synagogue and, at the Reformation, for the Protestant churches.

Claims & evidence

What the Masoretic Text is

The Masoretic Text is "the authoritative Hebrew and Aramaic text of the 24 books of the Hebrew Bible (Tanakh) in Rabbinic Judaism."1 It is the work of the Masoretes — scribal scholars working "between the 7th and 10th centuries" of the Common Era, chiefly at Tiberias and Jerusalem — who not only transmitted the consonantal Hebrew but added the vowel-points, accents, and marginal apparatus (the masora) that fixed its reading down to the letter. The most prominent was Aaron ben Moses ben Asher of the Ben Asher family, whose codex came to be reckoned the standard; the two great surviving witnesses are the tenth-century Aleppo Codex and the early-eleventh-century Leningrad Codex, "the oldest-known complete copy," dated 1008/1009.2

It contains the Hebrew canon only — no deuterocanon

The twenty-four books the Masoretes counted are the protocanonical books of the Hebrew Bible alone: the Law, the Prophets, and the Writings, closing (in the Jewish order) with Chronicles. The deuterocanonical books the Greek Septuagint carried — Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch, and the Maccabees — have no place in it.1 The Masoretes did not decide this; they inherited and preserved a boundary the Pharisaic / rabbinic stream had long since settled. What the Masoretic Text adds to the story is permanence: it gave that Hebrew-sized canon its definitive, standardized written form.

It became the text of the synagogue — and of most Protestant Bibles

The Masoretic Text "defines both the Jewish scriptural canon and its precise letter-text," and it is the textual basis from which "most Protestant translations of the Old Testament" are made.1 That is why this medieval Jewish artifact matters for the Christian canon question: when the Reformers returned ad fontes to the Hebrew — appealing to Jerome's Hebraica veritas against the Latin Vulgate's longer canon — the Hebrew they returned to was the Masoretic Text, and its twenty-four-book table of contents became the thirty-nine-book Protestant Old Testament. The Reformation "No" to the deuterocanon (the Thirty-Nine Articles, the Westminster Confession) is, at bottom, a decision to make the Masoretic canon the church's Old Testament.

The fidelity of its transmission

The Masoretes' renown rests on the extraordinary care of their copying, and the twentieth-century manuscript finds largely vindicated it. "Most of the Qumran fragments can be classified as being closer to the Masoretic Text than to any other text group," even though the Dead Sea Scrolls also show that "multiple versions of the Hebrew scriptures already existed" in antiquity.3 The most striking confirmation is the En-Gedi scroll, a charred Leviticus scroll of roughly the third or fourth century AD that was virtually read by computed-tomography imaging: its text proved "completely identical with the consonantal framework of the Masoretic Text" — an unbroken consonantal transmission across some seven or eight centuries before the Masoretes' own codices.4

Notes

  • Why this sits on a Christian-canon timeline. The deuterocanon dispute is, at bottom, a quarrel between two Old Testaments — the broader Greek Septuagint the church inherited and the narrower Hebrew text the synagogue kept and the Masoretes perfected. The Masoretic Text is the mature form of that Hebrew Old Testament, and it is the canon Protestantism adopted. It belongs here as the Judaism-side anchor for the medieval period and the textual hinge on which the later Protestant "No" turns.
  • Ties into the Septuagint-versus-Hebrew quarrel. Because the Masoretic Text both omits these books and diverges from the Greek elsewhere, it belongs to the larger dispute gathered on the Rabbinic Corruption of Scripture and Origin and Inspiration of the Septuagint pages; the MT-versus-LXX corruption case is weighed there and is not re-argued here. The fidelity evidence above bears only on whether the Masoretes faithfully transmitted their Hebrew text — not on whether that text or the Septuagint preserves the older reading where the two diverge.
  • Fidelity is not the same as canon-rightness. The En-Gedi and Qumran data show the Masoretes copied accurately; they do not, by themselves, settle which canon is correct. A perfectly transmitted Hebrew text still tells us only what the Hebrew tradition contained — the question this timeline asks is whether the church's canon should be measured by that Hebrew table of contents or by the Greek Bible it actually read, the very Jerome-versus-Augustine fault line carried down to the Reformation.
