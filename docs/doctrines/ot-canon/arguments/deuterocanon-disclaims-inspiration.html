Do the deuterocanonical books disclaim their own inspiration, excluding themselves from the canon?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — as a disqualifier. The passages adduced are real: 2 Maccabees ends with an author's apology ("if I have done well … it is what I desired; but if slenderly and meanly, it is that which I could attain unto," 15:38) and opens with an editor's preface describing his book as a one-volume abridgment of Jason of Cyrene's five (2:23–28); the Sirach prologue has the grandson-translator apologize for rendering imperfections; and 1 Maccabees thrice laments that prophecy had ceased (9:27; 4:46; 14:41). But the inference built on them — that the books thereby disclaim inspiration and so exclude themselves — does not hold on its best case. The colophon and the preface are an authorial modesty topos and an editor's account of his method, not a denial of inspiration; pressed as a disqualifier the criterion threatens the protocanon, since Luke 1:1–4 is likewise a researched, edited preface and no one unbooks Luke. The "prophecy had ceased" laments are a historical observation about the intertestamental gap — the same observation protocanonical Psalm 74:9 makes ("there is no more any prophet") inside an inspired book — not a self-canceling verdict on the book that reports it · back to timeline · the genuine residue (no protocanonical book carries a colophon like 2 Maccabees 15:38, and the cessation-of-prophecy laments fit the Hebrew-canon dating) is a contributing datum to the books' graded standing, weighed across the timeline (Jerome, the Masoretic canon), not an independent disqualifier

Here the sub-claim is the Protestant removal argument from the books' own words: that several deuterocanonical books say things about themselves — authorial apologies, an editor's note, laments that prophecy had ended — that amount to a disavowal of inspired or prophetic status, so the books exclude themselves from the canon.

The claim

The argument runs: certain deuterocanonical books testify against themselves. Where a canonical prophet writes "Thus saith the LORD," these books instead apologize for their prose, describe themselves as condensations of other men's work, and admit that the age of prophecy was over. So — the argument concludes — the books make no claim to inspiration; indeed they quietly deny it, and a book that disclaims inspired status cannot belong in the canon.1 Four passages are adduced:

  • 2 Maccabees 15:38–39 — the author's closing apology: "And if I have done well, and as is fitting the story, it is that which I desired: but if slenderly and meanly, it is that which I could attain unto. … even so speech finely framed delighteth the ears of them that read the story. And here shall be an end."2
  • 2 Maccabees 2:23–28 — the epitomator's preface: "All these things … being declared by Jason of Cyrene in five books, we will assay to abridge in one volume. … to us, that have taken upon us this painful labour of abridging, it was not easy, but a matter of sweat and watching … Leaving to the author the exact handling of every particular, and labouring to follow the rules of an abridgement."3
  • The Prologue to Sirach — the grandson-translator distinguishes "the law … the prophets … and the rest of the books" and apologizes for translation: "I intreat you … to pardon us, wherein we may seem to come short of some words, which we have laboured to interpret. For the same things uttered in Hebrew, and translated into another tongue, have not the same force in them."4
  • 1 Maccabees 9:27, 4:46, 14:41 — the book repeatedly records that prophecy had ceased: "So was there a great affliction in Israel, the like whereof was not since the time that a prophet was not seen among them" (9:27); the defiled altar-stones are laid aside "until there should come a prophet to shew what should be done with them" (4:46); Simon is made governor and high priest "until there should arise a faithful prophet" (14:41).5

The premises are true and worth conceding plainly: these passages say exactly what the proponent quotes. The question is whether they carry the conclusion — whether an authorial apology, an editor's method-note, and a historian's report that "no prophet is seen" add up to a book's disclaiming its own inspiration.

Who advances it

The proponents. "They themselves claimed not to be inspired" is a stock Protestant removal argument, common in popular apologetics and catechesis. It has a learned seed in the Reformers' attention to exactly these texts: Luther placed the books between the Testaments as "not held equal to the Holy Scriptures, and yet profitable and good to read," and John Calvin, answering the Catholic appeal to 2 Maccabees, turned "the writer's own closing apology (2 Macc 15:38) against the book," reasoning that an author who asks pardon for his prose does not write as a man moved by the Holy Spirit.6 The same removal argument is pressed today in popular apologetics: Mike Winger argues that none of the deuterocanonical books claim to be inspired Scripture the way Joshua, the books of Moses, or the Psalms do, and that 1 Maccabees' own notice that prophecy had ceased (9:27) disqualifies it; Bible.org's canonicity lesson reasons that the apocryphal writers never claimed divine inspiration and that the books themselves register the silent intertestamental age in which the people awaited a coming prophet (1 Maccabees 4:46); and James White, in his debate with Gary Michuta, turns the same 2 Maccabees 15:38 colophon against the book — the companion half of his "the books refute themselves" thesis.6 The adversaries. Catholic and Orthodox apologists answer that none of these passages is a statement about inspiration at all: the Maccabean colophon is a conventional literary modesty formula, the preface is an editor's ordinary description of his task, the Sirach apology concerns the difficulty of Greek translation, and the cessation-of-prophecy laments are historical observations about the period — the very kind of observation an inspired historian could make. Mainstream scholarship reads the 2 Maccabees frame the same way: the closing apology and the opening preface are the standard captatio benevolentiae of a Hellenistic historical epitome, bracketing the book as a piece of Greek-style historiography.7

The evidence for the affirmative reading

The asymmetry is real, and worth conceding. No protocanonical book ends the way 2 Maccabees does — with the author stepping out from behind his narrative to apologize for the quality of his writing and to hope he has not done it "slenderly and meanly." The prophets speak with the borrowed authority of "Thus saith the LORD"; the Maccabean epitomator speaks in his own voice, about his own "sweat and watching." That contrast is genuine and the timeline's Hebrew-minded witnesses register an instinct in its vicinity.2

The cessation-of-prophecy laments fit the Hebrew-canon dating. When 1 Maccabees records that affliction came "the like whereof was not since the time that a prophet was not seen," and twice defers a decision "until there should arise a faithful prophet," it places its own events after the recognized prophetic age — the very period the Hebrew-canon tradition holds the line of inspired Scripture had closed. The book's own chronological self-location coheres with the judgment of Jerome and the Masoretic canon that the disputed books fall outside the prophetic era.5

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The passages are quoted accurately, and the residue they leave is real: the 2 Maccabees colophon has no protocanonical parallel, and the 1 Maccabees laments do place the narrative after the close of the prophetic age. As observations, both stand.

What overstates. The disqualifying inference rests on reading each passage as a statement about inspiration, and on that point all four joints give way.

  1. An author's modesty is not a denial of inspiration — and the criterion threatens the protocanon. The closing apology of 2 Maccabees ("if slenderly and meanly, it is that which I could attain unto") and the preface's account of "this painful labour of abridging" are an authorial modesty formula and a description of literary method — the standard captatio benevolentiae that frames a Hellenistic historical epitome — not a self-rating of inspired status. If a researched, self-conscious, humanly-laboured preface disqualified a book, it would disqualify Luke 1:1–4, where the evangelist describes "many" prior accounts, his own investigation "of all things from the very first," and his decision to "write … in order" for Theophilus — a preface formally just like the epitomator's, in a book no one removes. The Spirit's inspiring of a text and a human author's editing, abridging, and apologizing for it are not rivals; Scripture contains both at once.8

  2. "Prophecy had ceased" is a historical observation, not a canonicity disclaimer — and the protocanon makes it too. The 1 Maccabees laments report a fact about the intertestamental period: no recognized prophet was then active. That a book narrates the absence of living prophecy says nothing about whether that book is itself inspired — and the move proves too much, because protocanonical Psalm 74:9 voices the identical lament from inside the canon: "We see not our signs: there is no more any prophet: neither is there among us any that knoweth how long." If observing that prophecy has lapsed disqualified a book, it would disqualify the Psalter. The laments bear on the books' date, which the timeline already weighs; they do not bear on self-exclusion.9

  3. The Sirach prologue apologizes for translation, and testifies to the book's high standing. The grandson's request "to pardon us, wherein we may seem to come short of some words, which we have laboured to interpret" is about the difficulty of carrying Hebrew "into another tongue" — a translator's humility about his Greek, not the grandfather disclaiming the wisdom he wrote. The prologue in fact ranks the grandfather's work alongside "the law … the prophets, and the rest of the books" as fit "for learning and wisdom," and the famous threefold formula is cited in canon scholarship as early evidence for the tripartite Hebrew Scripture — the opposite of a book talking itself out of esteem.4

  4. Being an abridgment is not a defect that unbooks a text. The preface's "we will assay to abridge in one volume" describes the genre of the work (an epitome of Jason of Cyrene), not a confession of un-inspiredness. The protocanonical historical books are themselves compiled and excerpted from named sources — Kings and Chronicles refer the reader again and again to "the book of the chronicles of the kings" — without anyone supposing the compiling hand cancels the inspiration. Composition from sources is how much of the Old Testament's history was written; it is not a self-disqualification.10

What the passages really show. Strip the inspiration-reading away and a true, modest residue remains: the deuterocanonical books wear their human authorship on their sleeve more openly than most of the protocanon — an epitomator who apologizes, a translator who asks indulgence, a historian who notes the silence of prophecy in his own day. That is real evidence about the books' literary character and date, and it coheres with the Hebrew-canon witnesses this timeline traces (Jerome, the Masoretic canon). But it is evidence for a graded standing reached on other grounds, not for a self-exclusion: none of the four passages is a statement about inspiration, and read as one, the criterion sweeps in Luke and the Psalter. The canon question turns on weighing the books' reception, list by list — not on mistaking an author's humility for a book's verdict on itself.1

Assessment

Weak — as a disqualifier. The interpretation that the deuterocanonical books disclaim their own inspiration, and so exclude themselves from the canon, does not hold on its best available case. The passages are genuine, but none is a statement about inspiration: the 2 Maccabees colophon and preface are a conventional authorial modesty formula and an editor's account of his method (the same self-conscious preface stands at the head of Luke); the Sirach prologue apologizes for the difficulty of translation while ranking the book "for learning and wisdom"; and the 1 Maccabees laments that prophecy had ceased are historical observations about the intertestamental gap — the identical lament that protocanonical Psalm 74:9 makes from inside the canon. Pressed as a disqualifier, the criterion proves too much, threatening Luke, the Psalter, and the compiled protocanonical histories. What survives — and it is sound, not nothing — is the narrower observation the argument starts from: no protocanonical book ends with an apology like 2 Maccabees 15:38, and the cessation-of-prophecy laments cohere with the Hebrew-canon dating of the disputed books. That residue is a contributing datum to the graded standing the Hebrew-minded Fathers assigned these books (read and used, ranked below the canon), weighed across this timeline — not an independent proof that the books unbook themselves. (Its companion removal arguments from the witness of the earliest church — apostolic silence (weak) and the patristic canon-lists (contested) — and the proof-text arguments on Tobit (weak) and 2 Maccabees (contested) are weighed on their own pages.)
