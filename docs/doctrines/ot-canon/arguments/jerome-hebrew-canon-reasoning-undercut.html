Was Jerome "wrong for his reasons"? Did later evidence undercut his case for a Hebrew-only Old Testament canon?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — for the interpretation it serves (that the Hebrew-only canon loses its foundation once Jerome's grounds for it are tested against later evidence). Jerome's reasons divide cleanly, and so does the verdict. His philological judgments — Tobit a "Chaldean" (Aramaic) book, 1 Maccabees Hebrew but 2 Maccabees Greek, Wisdom a Greek composition "never among the Hebrews," Sirach a book he had himself "found in Hebrew" — were not refuted by the twentieth century's manuscript finds but strikingly confirmed by them, so the popular form of the "proven wrong" charge (that the Dead Sea Scrolls embarrassed Jerome's language-judgments) is itself mistaken. Where Jerome's reasoning is genuinely undercut is at a different joint: his historical premise that the apostolic church should defer to a single, fixed, authoritative Jewish canon. There was no such closed canon in the first century; the rabbinic table hardened later, and the "Council of Jamnia" that supposedly closed it is a discarded hypothesis — and the very Hebrew-and-Aramaic Jewish library at Qumran kept Tobit and Sirach, books the later rabbinic canon excluded. So on his premise Jerome did reason from ground modern scholarship has cut away; but the charge misfires on his philology, and being wrong about the premise does not by itself overturn his conclusion, which remains the serious live position this timeline traces

Here the sub-claim is the charge — put sharply, that "Jerome was wrong for his reasons" — that the considered case the West's greatest biblical scholar made for a Hebrew-sized Old Testament rests on premises later evidence has overturned, so the Hebrew-only canon is left without its scholarly footing.

The claim

The Jerome-versus-Augustine quarrel is the fault line this whole timeline traces: Jerome would measure the Old Testament by the Hebrew that the Jews kept, Augustine by the books the catholic churches received. The argument here is not the broad question "was the Hebrew-canon conclusion right?" — that is the live dispute the timeline runs end to end. It is the sharper, examinable thesis that Jerome's specific grounds were mistaken: that when one lays out the actual reasons the man gave, later scholarship has knocked the props away, so his "No" was right-sounding but wrongly reasoned. Jerome's reasons come in two kinds, and they must be kept apart:

  • The governing principle — Hebraica veritas. The Old Testament is to be reckoned by "the truth of the Hebrew," the text the Jewish people preserved; "whatever is outside of these is set aside among the apocrypha." The disputed books — Wisdom, Sirach, Judith, Tobit, the Maccabees, the Shepherd — "are not in the canon."1
  • The historical assumption behind it. That there was a fixed, authoritative Hebrew/Jewish canon — a settled "catalogue of Divine Scriptures" — to which the apostolic church should defer, the same table the Jews "exclude" these books from.2
  • The philological judgments that policed the line. Tobit is a book "written in Chaldean words"; 1 Maccabees "I have found… (is) Hebrew, the Second is Greek, which may also be proven by their styles"; the Wisdom of Solomon "was never among the Hebrews, the very style of which reeks of Greek eloquence"; Sirach he had himself "found in Hebrew, titled not Ecclesiasticus as among the Latins, but Parables."3

The "proven wrong for his reasons" charge, in the form it usually takes, presses the third kind: that the Dead Sea Scrolls and later finds exposed Jerome's language-judgments — books he is supposed to have dismissed as Greek or "Chaldean" curiosities turned out to have genuine Hebrew and Aramaic originals, so his sorting of canonical from apocryphal by provenance collapses. That is the version to test first, because it is the most cited — and, as it happens, the one that does not survive contact with the evidence.

Where the charge misfires: Jerome's philology was vindicated, not refuted

The twentieth century's manuscript discoveries did not embarrass Jerome's identifications of these books' languages. They confirmed them — point for point.

  1. Tobit — "Chaldean" (Aramaic), and so it proved. Jerome translated Tobit from "a book written in Chaldean words," i.e. Aramaic. For centuries no Semitic Tobit was known and the book was widely treated as a Greek production; then Qumran Cave 4 yielded five ancient copies — four in Aramaic (4Q196–199) and one in Hebrew (4Q200), published by Joseph Fitzmyer in 1995. The Semitic, and specifically Aramaic, original Jerome reports is exactly what the scrolls attest. His broad linguistic judgment was right; the Scrolls vindicated it rather than overturning it.4

  2. Sirach — Jerome had seen the Hebrew. Far from dismissing Sirach as a Greek book, Jerome states in his Solomon preface that he "found" it "in Hebrew." The grandson's Greek prologue had always said as much — that he was rendering his grandfather's Hebrew — and the manuscripts have now caught up with both: the Cairo Geniza copies recovered from 1896 (about two-thirds of the book), the Masada scroll found in 1964 (Sir 39:27–44:17, first century BC), and Qumran fragments (2Q18; the Sirach 51 acrostic in the Great Psalms Scroll, 11QPsa). The Hebrew original of Ben Sira is no longer in doubt — and Jerome had already reported handling it.5

  3. 1 Maccabees Hebrew, 2 Maccabees Greek — the modern consensus, stated in advance. Jerome's terse note that the first book is Hebrew and the second Greek, "which may also be proven by their styles," is precisely what scholarship now holds: 1 Maccabees was composed in Hebrew (the Vorlage lost, the book surviving in Greek translation), while 2 Maccabees is a Greek work from the start, the epitomist's abridgement of the five volumes of Jason of Cyrene. Jerome read the styles correctly.6

  4. Wisdom of Solomon — Greek, "never among the Hebrews," and so the consensus runs. Of the one disputed book Jerome flatly called Greek — the Wisdom of Solomon, whose "style… reeks of Greek eloquence" — modern scholarship agrees without serious dissent: Wisdom was composed in Greek in Hellenistic Alexandria, not translated from a Hebrew original (the old Hebrew-Vorlage thesis "convinced nobody"). Here too Jerome's ear was right.7

So the popular "proven wrong" story has the facts almost backwards. Jerome did not file the disputed books as apocryphal because he wrongly thought them all Greek; he knew Tobit and Sirach and 1 Maccabees had Semitic originals, said so, and ruled them out anyway. His criterion was never "Greek language, therefore not Scripture" — it was "outside the Hebrew canon the Jews keep, therefore apocrypha," a quite different test. The five language-identifications he left on record line up strikingly with the manuscript and consensus evidence that followed; on philology the Dead Sea Scrolls are a feather in his cap, not a thorn. (One honest qualification: Jerome's Aramaic Tobit was not textually identical to the Qumran copies — his shorter recension differs from the longer text the scrolls support — so the vindication is of the language and the Semitic original, not of his particular manuscript.)4

Where the charge lands: the premise of a fixed Jewish canon to defer to

The reason that genuinely does not survive later scholarship is not philological but historical — the assumption under the whole Hebraica veritas program. Jerome wrote as though there were a single, closed, authoritative Jewish canon, a fixed "catalogue of Divine Scriptures," that the church could simply consult and conform to. The twentieth century has dismantled exactly that picture.

  1. There was no closed Jewish canon in the apostolic age. "There is no scholarly consensus as to when the canon of the Hebrew Bible was fixed"; the leading recent treatment (Timothy Lim) finds, down to about 100 AD, "not one official 'canon' accepted by all Jews" but a plurality of authoritative collections, with the rabbinic table closing only in the second or third century. The Greek-speaking diaspora read a Septuagint that carried the disputed books. The fixed Hebrew table Jerome deferred to as the ancient standard is, on the dating, the outcome of a process still unfinished in the apostolic generation — and its definitive form is the medieval Masoretic text, centuries after him.8

  2. The "council" that supposedly closed it never sat. The tidy conciliar moment Jerome's premise wants — a Jewish synod that ratified the Hebrew table and shut the disputed books out — is the "Council of Jamnia," a hypothesis Heinrich Graetz proposed in 1871 and Jack P. Lewis dismantled in 1964; canon scholarship now treats it as discarded. No dated Jewish council closed the canon, and none is on record excluding Tobit and its companions. The premise of a settled, ratified Jewish canon to which the church owed deference rests on an event modern scholarship has deleted.9

  3. "What the Jews kept in Hebrew" was wider than the rabbinic canon — and it included these very books. This is the sharpest turn of the knife, and it cuts on Jerome's own ground. His test equated the canon with the Hebrew the Jews preserved; but the one first-century Jewish library we can actually read — the Dead Sea Scrolls — preserved, in Hebrew and Aramaic, copies of Tobit and Sirach (alongside Jubilees, 1 Enoch, and other works), some of them apparently treated as authoritative by that community. The Jews of Qumran did keep, in the Hebrew tongue, books the later rabbinic canon would exclude. So "the Hebrew the Jews kept" was never coextensive with "the rabbinic twenty-two": Jerome's principle, pressed strictly, does not yield his list, because some of the books outside that list were exactly the Hebrew-and-Aramaic Jewish writings his principle privileges. The decisive cut came later, from the rabbis, not from the mere fact of a Hebrew original.10

Adversarial assessment

What the charge gets right. On its best case — set aside the misfired philological version — there is a real and important hit. Jerome reasoned from a premise modern scholarship rejects: that a fixed, authoritative Jewish canon already stood in the apostolic age for the church to defer to. It did not; the rabbinic table hardened afterward, partly in the second-century world, without the dated council the older picture supplied, and the one readable first-century Jewish collection kept in Hebrew some of the very books Jerome filed "among the apocrypha." To the degree his case leaned on deference to a settled synagogue canon — and it leaned heavily — it leaned on ground that has been cut away. This is the defensible residue of "wrong for his reasons," and it is genuine.

Where the charge overreaches. Three things keep it from carrying the day.

  1. The popular form attacks the wrong reason. The version most often pressed — that the Scrolls caught Jerome out on the languages of the books — is false. His five recorded identifications (Aramaic Tobit, Hebrew Sirach, Hebrew 1 Maccabees, Greek 2 Maccabees, Greek Wisdom) were confirmed, not refuted, by the later evidence. A charge that has to misdescribe its target's reasoning to land is not a strong charge.

  2. The exclusion is real even without the council. The collapse of Jamnia does not hand the disputed books to Jerome's opponents. The rabbinic stream did exclude Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch, and the Maccabees; the Hebrew Bible that emerged really is the shorter one. What modern scholarship denies is the tidy, datable, ratified closure Jerome assumed, not the eventual shape of the Jewish canon. So his conclusion (a Hebrew-sized Old Testament) is left standing even though one of his premises (a canon already closed and authoritative in the apostolic age) is not.

  3. A flawed premise does not settle the canon question against him. Being wrong about Jamnia, or about how fixed the first-century canon was, does not by itself decide whether the church ought to measure its Old Testament by the Hebrew. That larger question Jerome's instinct still presses with real force — it is the considered program of the age's foremost biblical scholar, set against Augustine's reception-criterion, and it is precisely the dispute this timeline traces with no clear winner from the fourth century to Trent. One can grant that Jerome's historical picture was dated and still hold that the reading he served is live.

Two questions, two answers. Keeping the steelman's discipline: was the charge as usually stated sound? No — it misfires on the philology, which Jerome got right. Was Jerome's reasoning, on its best reconstruction, partly undercut by later evidence? Yes — his premise of a fixed apostolic-age Jewish canon to defer to is genuinely dated, and his own "Hebrew the Jews kept" criterion, pressed strictly, no longer cleanly yields his list. But the conclusion survives the premise's failure, and the underlying interpretation — measure the Old Testament by the Hebrew — remains the serious, live position the timeline documents.

Assessment

Contested. The "Jerome was wrong for his reasons" thesis is half-right and half-mistaken, and the verdict turns on which reason is in view. As a charge against his philology — the form that invokes the Dead Sea Scrolls to embarrass his language-judgments — it fails outright: his identifications of these books' tongues (Aramaic Tobit, Hebrew Sirach, Hebrew 1 Maccabees, Greek 2 Maccabees, Greek Wisdom) were confirmed by the later manuscript finds and consensus, and he never sorted canonical from apocryphal by Greekness in the first place. As a charge against his historical premise — that a fixed, authoritative Jewish canon already stood for the apostolic church to defer to — it lands: there was no closed first-century canon, the canon-closing "Council of Jamnia" is a discarded hypothesis, and the Hebrew-and-Aramaic Jewish library at Qumran kept Tobit and Sirach, books the later rabbinic table excluded, so "the Hebrew the Jews kept" was never the same as "the rabbinic canon." Yet that genuine flaw does not dispose of his case: the rabbinic exclusion is real even without a council, a dated premise does not settle whether the church should measure the Old Testament by the Hebrew, and the interpretation Jerome served remains the considered program of the West's greatest biblical scholar, set against Augustine's reception-criterion in the dispute this timeline runs to Trent. A real blow to one premise, a miss on the others, the conclusion left standing — no clear winner.
