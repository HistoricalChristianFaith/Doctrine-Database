Did "not canonical" encode a substantive authority judgment (not a mere filing category) — and does "cannot establish doctrine" entail "not really Scripture"?

Type: methodological argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — for the interpretation it serves (that when the Fathers called a book "not canonical," they were passing a substantive theological judgment — this book may not confirm doctrine — not sorting it onto a neutral shelf; and that a book which cannot ground doctrine "cannot function as Scripture in any meaningful sense"). The reading splits into two claims with different fates. Its first half is sound: the "not canonical / ecclesiastical" tier really is defined by a denial of doctrine-proving authority, not by a filing convenience — Rufinus, Jerome, and the medieval scholastics say so in words, so this is a genuine answer to the Catholic "'canon' is mere technical terminology" move. But its second half — the sharp inference cannot-confirm-doctrine ⇒ not-Scripture-in-any-meaningful-sense — overreaches: the very Fathers who withhold these books from dogma-proving read them in worship, quote them in their own voice as the Spirit's words, and in fact settle doctrine from them. "Cannot be the first court of doctrinal appeal" is a real demotion in authority; it is not the modern verdict "not inspired Scripture." A true observation about the tier, wrapped in an inference the same Fathers' practice refutes.

Related arguments: reading the patristic witness — see also does the Fathers' word "canon" mean the modern definition? (sound); what did the "ecclesiastical" (read-but-not-canonical) category mean? (sound); does quoting a book "as Scripture" prove a Father held it canonical? (plausible); did the early church build doctrine only on the undisputed books? (contested); did the early church accept the Apocrypha out of ignorance of Hebrew? (weak); is the canon "discovered, not decided," so patristic use was honest error? (contested).

The claim

Two mirror-image moves meet on this point, and the argument weighed here is the shorter-canon side of the pair. The Catholic move (Gary Michuta's) says: "canon" is a technical word for an authoritative list, and technical words drift; the question that actually decides Scripture is Paul's — is the book inspired (θεόπνευστος)? So a book can be filed outside "the canon" and still be inspired Scripture; being off the list is a matter of category, not of status. The shorter-canon rejoinder answers that this drives a wedge the Fathers did not know: when they called a book "not canonical," they were not shelving it under a harmless heading — they were saying this book does not carry the authority to establish doctrine. And then the load-bearing inference: a writing that "cannot establish doctrine cannot function as Scripture in any meaningful sense," because "Scripture" just is the God-breathed word by which the church proves what it must believe. On this reading the read-but-not-canonical tier is a substantive theological demotion — a verdict that these books are not Scripture in the operative sense — not the neutral filing-category the Catholic side needs it to be.1

Who advances it

The rejoinder is a staple of the Reformation and shorter-canon case rather than the thesis of a single timeline witness, pressed in its modern form by James White (in his debate with Michuta) and behind him by the whole line that reads Rufinus, Jerome, and the medieval "ecclesiastical" distinction as a ranking. Its classic statement is John Calvin's, against Trent: the disputed books are the "ecclesiastical books, which might indeed be read to the people, but were not entitled to establish doctrine" — and, Calvin's implied point, a book barred from establishing doctrine is not the Bible's peer. The mirror Catholic position it answers is Michuta's "the canon is technical terminology… go by the scriptural definition — is it inspired?" — the move that pries "inspired" loose from "canonical" so the middle tier can be inspired Scripture kept off a merely-technical list.1

The evidence for

The tier is named by a denial of authority, not by a filing convenience. The first half of the claim is solid, and the Fathers supply it in their own words. The middle rank is not marked "miscellaneous" or "later" or "shorter"; it is marked not for confirming doctrine. Rufinus, who coined the label, defines the "ecclesiastical" books as those "read in the Churches, but not appealed to for the confirmation of doctrine"; Jerome says the church reads Judith, Tobit, and the Maccabees "but not for confirming the authority of ecclesiastical dogmas"; Athanasius sets the read tier against the "canonized" books that are the rule. The medieval West kept the distinction on exactly this axis: the Ordinary Gloss and the great commentators (Hugh of St. Cher, Nicholas of Lyra) file Wisdom, Sirach, Judith, Tobit, and the Maccabees as books read "for instruction in manners, not… for deciding controversies respecting the faith." So the tier is not a neutral bin; it is constituted by the withdrawal of doctrine-proving authority. To that extent the rejoinder is right against the "mere technical terminology" reading: "non-canonical" here carries a real theological verdict about a book's probative weight.2

And "establishing doctrine" is close to the core of what "Scripture" is for. The inference is not frivolous. If 2 Timothy 3:16 is right that Scripture is God-breathed and therefore "profitable… for doctrine, for reproof, for correction," then the power to settle what the church must believe is not an optional extra of a Scriptural book but near its defining office. A writing solemnly barred from that office does look, on its face, to have been ranked short of full Scriptural standing — which is just what the shorter-canon side concludes.3

Where the inference overreaches

The same Fathers read and quote these books as Scripture — so "not-for-doctrine" is not "not-Scripture" for them. The sharp step falls at the point where it needs the Fathers to agree that a non-dogma-proving book is not really Scripture. They plainly do not. The men who file these books below the canon turn around and read them in worship and cite them in their own voice as the word of God. Athanasius grounds his account of idolatry on the Wisdom of Solomon ("Scripture taught us beforehand"), chains Sirach as "the words spoken by the Spirit," and sets Tobit under "it is written." Rufinus, who coined ecclesiastici, introduces Sirach elsewhere with the plainest formula — "for thus the Scripture says (ita enim Scriptura dicit)." A man does not call a book the voice of the Spirit and mean that it is Scripture "in no meaningful sense." So the inference reads a modern inspired/uninspired binary into a line the Fathers drew inside a graded view of Scripture: the "canon" they exclude these books from is the narrower rule from which dogma is proved, not the blanket category "inspired writing."4

And they in fact use the disputed books doctrinally. Worse for the inference, the premise it rests on — that these books never do doctrinal work — is false as a description of patristic practice. Hippolytus reads Wisdom as "the prophecy… which speaketh of Christ"; Cyprian cites Tobit and Sirach as "Holy Scripture" to settle points of alms and patience; Basil argues doctrine from Judith and Baruch in On the Holy Spirit; the defenders of Nicaea reach for Wisdom; and Jerome — author of the very embargo formula — elsewhere quotes Sirach as "Holy Scripture says" and Wisdom under "it is written." If the disputed books "cannot establish doctrine," a great many Fathers, the framer of the rule included, did not get the memo. The most the record supports is that some Fathers would not make these books the first court of doctrinal appeal — a demotion in rank and reliability, not a denial that they are Scripture. (Whether even that restraint was a settled principle is weighed on the "doctrine only from the undisputed books" page.)5

The read/inspired distinction the inference denies is coherent — and is the Fathers' own. The whole force of the sharp step is that there is no daylight between "confirms doctrine" and "is Scripture," so a book that lacks the first lacks the second. But that daylight is exactly what a graded canon is. One can hold a book inspired, edifying, quotable, and read in church, yet not place it first among the witnesses one deploys to clinch a contested dogma — because it is late, or disputed among the churches, or absent from the Jews' reckoning. That is a judgment about a book's probative standing in argument, not about whether God breathed it. Michuta's counter-move is right to this extent: "canonical" and "inspired" did come apart for these Fathers, which is why they could rank a book below the doctrine-proving canon and still quote it as the Spirit's word. The shorter-canon inference collapses the very distinction the middle tier was invented to mark.4

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The first half of the claim is sound, and it is a real corrective against the flattest Catholic move. When the Fathers say a book is "not canonical," they are not sorting it under a bureaucratic heading with no theological freight; the label is built from a denial of doctrine-proving authority (Rufinus, Jerome, the medieval Gloss). So the answer to "wasn't 'canon' just technical terminology?" is: the term is technical, but the judgment it encodes is substantive — these books were ranked, by these Fathers, below the rule from which dogma is proved. Anyone who treats the read-but-not-canonical tier as a mere filing category has missed what the Fathers were saying.

What is overstated. The leap from "these books were not first for proving doctrine" to "these books were not Scripture in any meaningful sense" is where the argument breaks, and it breaks on the proponents' own witnesses. (1) The Fathers who deny these books doctrine-proving primacy nonetheless read them in worship and cite them in their own voice as the word of the Spirit — so they did not equate "non-canonical" with "non-Scripture." (2) They in fact settle doctrine from the disputed books all over the timeline, Jerome included — so the premise that these books "cannot establish doctrine" is not even true as description. (3) The inference silently swaps a judgment about probative authority (which court of appeal, and how weighty) for a judgment about inspiration (God-breathed or not), which are precisely the two things a graded canon holds apart. The sound residue is the modest one: "non-canonical" was an authority-verdict, and the disputed books sat lower on the ladder of doctrinal appeal — not the strong one, that they were thereby not Scripture at all.

A real demotion, wrongly measured. The hinge is what "cannot function as Scripture in any meaningful sense" smuggles in. On a two-bucket picture — inspired canon vs. rejected human writing — barring a book from doctrine does push it toward the second bucket, and the inference looks clean. But the Fathers ran a three-tier picture, and on it the middle rank is meaningfully Scriptural: read liturgically, cited as the Spirit's words, drawn on for doctrine in practice, yet not made the decisive proof in a contested case. Measure the demotion against the graded scheme the Fathers actually used, and it is a demotion in rank, not an eviction from Scripture. The argument's error is not in seeing a demotion; it is in reading a modern binary's meaning into it.

Assessment

Contested. The interpretation has a true half and an overstated half, and the verdict tracks the split. True: "not canonical" was a substantive judgment about a book's authority to establish doctrine, not a neutral filing category — Rufinus, Jerome, and the medieval scholastics define the tier by exactly that denial, so the rejoinder rightly refuses the "mere technical terminology" reading. Overstated: the sharp inference that a book which cannot ground doctrine "cannot function as Scripture in any meaningful sense" collapses a distinction the same Fathers kept open. They read these books in worship, quoted them as the words of the Spirit, and in practice proved doctrine from them — Jerome, who framed the embargo, among them — so for them "non-canonical" plainly did not mean "not Scripture." The step trades a judgment about probative rank for a judgment about inspiration, and reads a two-bucket binary into a three-tier scheme. The demotion is real; its measure is wrong. (This is the same graded standing weighed, from the tier's side, on the "ecclesiastical category" page (sound) and, from the practice's side, on the "doctrine only from the undisputed books" page (contested); flag on the shorter-canon proponents the slide from "not first for doctrine" to "not really Scripture.")
