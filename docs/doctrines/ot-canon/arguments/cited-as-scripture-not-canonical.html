Does a Father's quoting a book "as Scripture" prove he held it canonical — or must usage be weighed against his canon list?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline; methodological) · Assessment: plausible — the core distinction is sound and is the working method of canon-list scholarship: quoting a book "as it is written" is a looser act than ranking it in a deliberate canon list, and the two demonstrably come apart — Athanasius files Wisdom, Sirach, and Tobit below the canon in Festal Letter 39 yet cites all three in his own voice as Scripture. So a citation formula does not by itself prove a Father reckoned a book canonical, and the Catholic case overreads the patristic usage when it treats every "Scripture says" as a canon-verdict. But the further claim that the canon lists are therefore the controlling evidence — the datum that settles a Father's canon over against his usage — overreaches at two joints: most Fathers left no list at all, so the rule decides nothing for the majority of witnesses; and the very Athanasius case that severs usage from canonicity severs list-exclusion from rejection just as cleanly (if quoting a book as Scripture doesn't prove he held it canonical, his Hebrew-sized list doesn't prove he rejected it as Scripture). Sound as a caution against equating citation with canonicity; a plausible-but-not-decisive methodological preference when pressed as the rule that adjudicates the canon · advanced by John Meade

See also: this is the method underneath the whole list-versus-usage strain weighed across this doctrine. It controls the reading of the patristic data gathered on the "did the early church reject the deuterocanon?" argument (contested), and it is the general form of the single-Father test on "is Athanasius a witness for the Protestant canon?" (contested) and its companion on what divides his "canon" and "read" tiers (plausible). The terminological question of what the patristic "read" / "ecclesiastical" middle tier meant is weighed alongside it.

The sub-claim here is not about any one book or Father but about how to read the evidence: when a Father quotes Tobit or Wisdom under a Scripture formula, has he thereby told us he holds it canonical — or is the question still open until we consult his canon list?

The claim

Much of the Catholic case for the deuterocanon is built from usage: Father after Father — Clement of Rome, Irenaeus, Clement of Alexandria, Cyprian, and more — cites the disputed books as "Scripture," as "it is written," as the voice of the prophets, to settle doctrine. The inference urged is: they quoted these books as Scripture, therefore they held them canonical. The interpretation weighed here, advanced by the Protestant Old-Testament scholar John Meade (co-author of the standard reference on the early canon lists), denies the inference. Citation-as-Scripture and canonical status are two different things, it holds, and conflating them is a methodological error:

  1. A canon list is a deliberate, reflective act — a Father sitting down to enumerate "the books" and to fix their number and bounds. A citation formula ("the Scripture says," "as it is written") is looser: it marks a book as authoritative, edifying, prophetic, useful — without committing the writer to a ruling on its place in the canon.
  2. The two demonstrably diverge. Meade's worked example is Athanasius, who in Festal Letter 39 (367) files Wisdom, Sirach, Judith, and Tobit in a tier "not… included in the Canon, but appointed by the Fathers to be read," yet across his other works quotes Wisdom, Sirach, and Tobit in his own voice as Scripture. If citation proved canonicity, Athanasius would contradict himself; since it does not, the two are distinct.
  3. Therefore the controlling evidence for what a Father held canonical is his explicit canon list, where he left one — not the looser fact that he cited a book as Scripture. To weigh the canon question you must compare a Father's usage against his list, and the early lists are Hebrew-sized.1

Premises (1) and (2) are true and should be granted plainly. The weight of the argument rests on (3) — on how far the lists may be made the controlling evidence, decisive against usage and across the whole patristic field.

Who advances it

The proponent. John Meade states the principle in his video "Which Old Testament Canon is Right?" (with the Athanasius example as the pivot) and in his popular writing, where he keeps "copying and reading these books" sharply apart from the canon question — "whether they considered them as having authority or not is a separate question." Its rigorous form is the method of Edmon Gallagher & John Meade, The Biblical Canon Lists from Early Christianity (Oxford, 2017), which assembles the deliberate canon catalogues — Melito, Origen, Cyril, Athanasius, Jerome, Rufinus, Augustine, and the rest — as the primary evidence for the shape of the early canon, precisely because a list is a different and more probative kind of act than a stray citation. The same volume registers the converse datum on which the principle leans: the earliest authors "never cite the deuterocanonical books as scripture" with a formula, yet "do allude to them" — citation and allusion themselves are graded, not one thing.1

The adversary. Gary Michuta resists making the lists controlling. He argues that a Father who uses a book to settle doctrine, under "it is written," has treated it as Scripture in substance whatever a later-systematized "list" says; that the patristic "canon" is often a narrower, measured category than "inspired Scripture," so its boundary is not the inspiration boundary; and — the decisive practical point — that many Fathers left no list at all, so for them usage is the only evidence there is.2

What holds — citation does not equal a canon-verdict

The core of the principle is sound and important, and it is a genuine corrective to the way the deuterocanon's defenders sometimes read the patristic record. The Athanasius case proves it cleanly. In Festal Letter 39 Athanasius names Wisdom, Sirach, Judith, and Tobit and sets them expressly "not… included in the Canon" — and in his controversial and pastoral works he nonetheless grounds his account of idolatry on the Wisdom of Solomon ("Scripture taught us beforehand long ago, when it said…"), quotes Wisdom 9:2 with "it is written" among texts "divine Scripture recognises," chains Sirach 15:9 as "the words spoken by the Spirit," and sets Tobit 4:18 under "it is written." One and the same Father, then, both excludes these books from his canon list and cites them as Scripture. It follows with force that, for him, "may be cited as inspired Scripture" and "belongs in the canon" were not the same predicate — and that one cannot read a canon-verdict straight off a citation formula.3

Generalized, the caution is just: the patristic "Scripture" vocabulary is broad. The same writers who call Sirach "Scripture" quote 1 Enoch as prophecy (Jude 14–15) and pagan poets without canonizing them; "the Scripture says" can mark a text as authoritative or prophetic without ranking it among "the books." So a careful canon-history keeps the two registers — deliberate enumeration and working citation — distinct, and does not let a heap of "Scripture says" citations substitute for the question of what a Father, asked to draw up the canon, actually drew up. To that extent Meade is right, and the all-usage form of the Catholic argument really does overreach.

What overstates — the lists are not, by themselves, the controlling evidence

The overreach lies one step on, in premise (3): the move from "citation does not prove canonicity" to "the lists are the controlling evidence" — the datum that decides a Father's canon, against his usage and across the board. Three things hold it back.

  1. The severing cuts both ways. The Athanasius example is double-edged, and the principle keeps only one edge. If quoting Wisdom as Scripture does not prove Athanasius held it canonical, then by the very same logic his Hebrew-sized list does not prove he rejected it as Scripture — his own pen, citing it as the word of God, forbids that reading. The list tells us he did not enumerate the book among "the canon"; it does not tell us he reckoned it un-inspired or sub-scriptural, and his usage shows he did not. So the lists and the usage are both partial witnesses to a graded standing that neither captures alone. The method that makes the list controlling quietly treats list-exclusion as rejection — the very inference (usage→status) it forbids in the other direction.4

  2. Most Fathers left no list. A rule that says "consult the canon list" has nothing to consult for the majority of the witnesses on this timeline — Clement of Rome, Irenaeus, Clement of Alexandria, Cyprian, Ambrose, Chrysostom, and the rest draw up no canon catalogue at all. For them, usage is not the looser evidence to be corrected by the list; it is the only evidence there is. The principle, pressed as the universal control, would have us declare the canon question simply unanswerable for most of the ancient church — which is not a neutral result but a thumb on the scale, since it discounts precisely the broad, Septuagint-using practice that ran beside the learned lists.5

  3. The lists are themselves a particular kind of artifact — not a pure read-out of a Father's working canon. The Hebrew-sized lists are, in large part, the learned reproduction of an inherited Jewish enumeration: the count fixed at "twenty-two… the number of the letters among the Hebrews," handed down from Melito and Origen to Cyril, Athanasius, and Gregory of Nazianzus. That a Father recites this scholarly count tells us he knew and honored the Hebrew reckoning; it does not by itself tell us he withheld Scripture-status from the books the same church read in the Septuagint and that he himself quotes. Rufinus is the clean illustration: he reproduces the Hebrew list, then files the deuterocanon in a named middle class, "Ecclesiastical," read in the churches though not adduced to confirm doctrine — a graded verdict the bare word "list" flattens. The list is evidence; it is not the whole of a Father's mind about these books, and making it the sole control mistakes one genre of evidence for the verdict.6

Answering the proponent

Meade's distinction survives all three objections; only the controlling-evidence corollary is touched. It remains true, and worth insisting on against loose apologetics, that citing a book as Scripture is not the same act as enumerating it in a canon, and that the early lists are Hebrew-sized — that much the objections concede and the Athanasius case proves. What they deny is that the lists, so understood, hand victory to the shorter canon. They do not, because (a) the same severing shows the lists do not establish rejection, only non-enumeration; (b) the rule is silent for the many Fathers who left no list, where the broad Septuagint-using practice is the only datum; and (c) the lists encode an inherited Jewish count, not necessarily each Father's own working canon. The right conclusion is not "lists trump usage" but "lists and usage are two partial witnesses to a graded standing" — exactly the divided picture the deuterocanon in fact had: enumerated below the canon by a learned stream, read and cited as Scripture by the wider church, and ranked in an explicit middle tier by those (Athanasius, Rufinus) who held both facts at once. The methodological premise is a real check on one side's over-reading; it is not the canon-deciding instrument it is sometimes made to be — and, pressed evenhandedly, it disciplines the Protestant inference from the lists every bit as much as the Catholic inference from usage.7

Assessment

Plausible. Take the principle in two halves. The distinction it rests on — that quoting a book "as Scripture" is a looser act than ranking it in a deliberate canon list, so a citation formula does not by itself prove a Father held the book canonical — is sound, and is the working method of the field's standard reference (Gallagher & Meade). Athanasius proves it: he excludes Wisdom, Sirach, and Tobit from his canon list and cites all three as Scripture, so for him the two predicates plainly came apart. Pressed against the all-usage form of the Catholic case, the corrective is just. The distinctive further claim — that the canon lists are therefore the controlling evidence, decisive over a Father's usage and across the patristic field — is the part to hold loosely. It runs aground on the very case that motivates it (the severing that detaches usage from canonicity equally detaches list-exclusion from rejection), it has nothing to control for the majority of Fathers who left no list, and it treats an inherited Hebrew enumeration as if it were each writer's own working canon. So: the caution against equating citation with canonicity is sound; the elevation of the lists to the deciding evidence is a coherent but non-decisive methodological preference — and an evenhanded application of the principle ends not in the shorter canon but in the graded standing both the lists and the usage, read together, attest.
