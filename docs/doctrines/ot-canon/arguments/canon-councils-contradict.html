Do the Catholic canon councils contradict each other? — "Trent anathematizes Hippo and Carthage" and the conciliar-infallibility objection

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — granting the established fact that the lists are not identical over the Greek 1 Esdras, the further inference that the Catholic councils therefore contradict one another does not hold: Trent withheld judgment on 3 Esdras rather than condemning it (so there is no defined proposition for the African list to clash with), Trent's anathema falls on subtracting from its list, not on a council that received more, and the load-bearing premise that Second Nicaea (787) dogmatized 1 Esdras as Scripture overreads a ratification the specialists read as general, not specific.

Related arguments: the canon councils & 1 Esdras — see also Does Trent's Old Testament canon differ from Hippo and Carthage's? — the 1 Esdras question

This page takes up the inference that some draw from the bare list-difference established on its companion page, "Does Trent's Old Testament canon differ from Hippo and Carthage's?" (assessment: sound). That page grants what the specialists grant — that the African councils' "two books of Esdras" almost certainly included the Greek 1 Esdras (Esdras A / Latin 3 Esdras), which Trent's enumeration does not, so the two catalogues are genuinely not the same list. The question here is the next step: does that non-identity show the councils contradict one another — that Trent's anathema falls on Hippo and Carthage, and that the supposedly infallible Catholic councils mutually exclude one another, so a Catholic must "pick and choose" which to believe? (The distinct scope objection — that the African councils were provincial, not universal — is weighed on the "first universal, binding definition" page.)

The claim

The inference comes in three escalating forms. James White put the sharpest version as a cross-examination dilemma in his 2004 debate with Gary Michuta:

Given that Pope Innocent I and Pope Gelasius both affirmed the canon list of Hippo and Carthage, yet Trent and every pope since then has affirmed a different list that excludes the extra material in Esdras, which popes were right and which popes were wrong on this issue?

— The Great Debate IX: Is the Apocrypha Scripture? (White vs. Michuta, 2004).1 The "which were right and which were wrong?" frame already presupposes a conflict: if the lists merely differed without colliding, no pope would have to be wrong. Mike Winger draws the conclusion explicitly in the language of the decree — because Trent's list "disagrees with Hippo and Carthage," Trent is therefore anathematizing the earlier councils, so a Catholic "can't pretend that one supports the other."2 Steve Christie presses the strongest form, aimed at conciliar infallibility itself: he asks which ecumenical council was infallible — Second Nicaea (787), which (he says) ratified Hippo and Carthage and so affirmed that 1 Esdras is Scripture, or the later Florence and Trent, which say it is not — and which of these "mutually contradicting infallible councils" the Catholic chooses to believe.3

Who advances it

The premise (the lists differ) traces to William Webster and is carried by James White and Mike Winger; the inference built on it (the councils therefore conflict) is the polemical payload those proponents attach, and Christie sharpens it into an attack on conciliar infallibility. On the Catholic side it is answered by Gary Michuta (in the 2004 debate) and Dave Armstrong, who reply that the canons are harmonizable, that the earlier councils never adjudicated the disputed unit, and that Catholic infallibility is located in the binding ecumenical definition, not in every provincial reckoning.

The evidence for

The lists really are not identical. The argument's one solid foundation is the factual premise weighed on the companion page and found sound: on the leading scholarship (Pierre-Maurice Bogaert, followed by Edmon Gallagher and John Meade in the standard reference work on the early canon lists), the African councils' "two books of Esdras" comprised the Greek 1 Esdras (Esdras A) and Ezra–Nehemiah as one book, whereas Trent's Vulgate-based enumeration names only Ezra and Nehemiah and passes the Greek 1 Esdras over. So an earlier council did receive, among its Esdras books, a book Trent's defined list omits — and Augustine, the hand behind the African lists, even cites the matter unique to that book (the contest of the three guardsmen) as possible prophecy of Christ (City of God 18.36). To that extent the proponents are standing on real ground: there is a genuine difference, and it touches a book one set of councils received as scriptural.4

Adversarial assessment

What holds. Non-identity is real, and it is fair to say there is a difference in reception across the councils — one that survives to this day in the divergence between a Catholicism that lists 1 Esdras outside the canon and an Eastern Orthodoxy that still prints Esdras A as canonical Scripture. A bare appeal that treats "Hippo, Carthage, Florence, and Trent all affirmed the same canon" as a seamless identity does overstate the record at this one seam. The proponents are right that a Catholic cannot wave the difference away.

Why it does not reach contradiction. Three things have to be true for "the councils contradict each other" to follow, and each fails.

  1. Trent defined no proposition for the African list to collide with. A contradiction needs one council to affirm what another denies. But Trent did not deny that 1 Esdras is Scripture; it simply did not list it. The council had the wider Latin appendix (3 and 4 Esdras, the Prayer of Manasseh) before it and chose to pass over it in silence — only a small minority voted to reject those books explicitly — and the Clementine Vulgate of 1592 then relegated them to a non-canonical appendix "lest they utterly perish," a printer's settlement, not a conciliar anathema. So there is no defined Tridentine sentence "1 Esdras is not Scripture" standing opposite the African "two books of Esdras." A later council that names a core and leaves a boundary unit unaddressed has narrowed an earlier, looser reckoning, not reversed it. Gary Michuta presses exactly this: the Africans, too, "passed over in silence; they didn't pass judgment" on the precise Esdras enumeration — under-specification on both ends, not opposed verdicts.5

  2. Trent's anathema runs the other direction. Winger's "Trent anathematizes Hippo and Carthage" misreads the decree. Trent's penalty attaches to whoever does "not receive, as sacred and canonical, the said books entire with all their parts" — it falls on subtraction, on rejecting a book Trent lists. It says nothing against a council that received more. Hippo and Carthage received every book on Trent's list (and then some), so they fall under no Tridentine anathema; if anyone's reception sits crosswise to that anathema it is the Reformers who removed books, not the African fathers who kept them. An anathema against denying a defined list cannot retroactively condemn a council that defined no list at all and ruled the disputed unit neither in nor out.6

  3. The "infallible Nicaea II affirmed 1 Esdras" premise overreads the ratification. Christie's gotcha needs Second Nicaea (787) to have dogmatized the African Esdras enumeration. It did no such thing in any focused sense. Nicaea II's first canon ratified the earlier canons en bloc — and, through the Council in Trullo (692) whose canon 2 it received, it ratified at once the canons of Carthage and of Laodicea, Athanasius, and Amphilochius (which exclude or tier the deuterocanon) and Apostolic Canon 85 (which includes 3 Maccabees, a book never received in the West). As Henry Percival observes, that leaves "four [five if we accept the Laodicean list as genuine] different canons of Holy Scripture, all having the approval of the Council in Trullo and of the Seventh Ecumenical" — from which "there seems but one conclusion possible: that the approval given was not specific but general." A ratification that simultaneously endorses mutually inconsistent lists cannot be a precise dogmatic decree that "1 Esdras is Scripture"; it is a general reception of an inherited corpus for use in the Church. Tellingly, this is the very point William Webster — the source of the whole Protestant argument — quotes Percival to make, which is why it cannot be deployed to manufacture a sharp "Nicaea-said-yes / Trent-said-no" collision.7

What the infallibility form forgets. Even granting a list-difference over a boundary book, the "which infallible council do you pick?" dilemma misfires on its own terms. Catholic teaching locates conciliar infallibility in the binding ecumenical definition, not in every provincial canon or every act of general ratification — which is precisely why the same tradition can say the canon was "not infallible in the highest sense until Trent." The African councils were regional; Nicaea II's reception of their canons was general. So there is no pair of infallible definitions in conflict to choose between. And the East, which does hold Esdras A canonical, suffers no contradiction at all, since it never issued a Tridentine close excluding it — the residue is a real difference between two communions over one book, not a self-contradiction within either.8

Assessment

Weak. The inference inherits a sound premise — the lists are genuinely not identical over the Greek 1 Esdras (weighed on the companion page) — and then loads onto it more than it can carry. "Contradiction" requires one council to deny what another affirms, but Trent defined no proposition denying 1 Esdras: it withheld judgment on 3 Esdras (the Clementine appendix is a later editorial settlement, not a conciliar condemnation), so the most the record shows is non-identity over a unit neither set of councils adjudicated — development and later specification, not opposed verdicts. Winger's "Trent anathematizes Hippo and Carthage" reverses the decree, whose anathema falls on subtracting from the defined list, not on a council that received more. And Christie's conciliar-infallibility form rests on the overstated premise that Second Nicaea (787) dogmatized 1 Esdras as Scripture, when the specialists (Percival, on the very canon Webster cites) read Nicaea II's blanket ratification — which embraced mutually inconsistent lists, some excluding the deuterocanon outright — as general, not a focused definition; and the dilemma further forgets that Catholic infallibility is located in the binding ecumenical definition, not in provincial canons, so there is no pair of infallible definitions to choose between. What survives is the modest, already-granted point that the catalogues differ at one seam — and a real, ongoing difference between East and West over 1 Esdras — not the collision of "mutually contradicting infallible councils" the argument advertises. Flag on the proponents' framing Winger's inverted anathema and Christie's unverified "Nicaea II dogmatized 1 Esdras" premise; the sound kernel belongs to the non-identity page, not here.
