Did the early church reject the deuterocanon as Scripture, making its later inclusion an accretion?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — the early witness is genuinely divided, and the verdict turns on which form of the claim is in view. The narrow, true form is that a learned, Hebrew-canon-conscious stream of Fathers (Melito, Origen, Cyril, Athanasius, Epiphanius, Jerome, Rufinus) consistently ranked the disputed books below a canon measured by the Hebrew table — a real and early pattern, strongest where the Fathers had actually investigated the Hebrew enumeration. An argument page weighs a single sub-claim adduced in support of a main interpretation — a proof-text, a parallel, a historical thesis — and renders an adversarial assessment of the interpretation it serves. Unlike a person-detail page it sits across the timeline rather than at one date. Here the sub-claim is the Protestant removal argument from antiquity: that the earliest Fathers and canon-lists excluded the deuterocanon, so its later inclusion is an accretion, not the primitive faith. Every factual claim is sourced; the proponent's framing is weighed on its best available case.

The claim

The argument is a historical one, marshaled against the deuterocanon's canonicity:

  1. The earliest Christian Old-Testament lists — Melito's (c. 170), Origen's (c. 240), Cyril's, Athanasius's, Epiphanius's, Jerome's, Rufinus's — are Hebrew-sized: they either omit Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, and the Maccabees outright, or file them in a tier expressly below the canon.
  2. Therefore the primitive church did not reckon these books Scripture; their later reception (the African councils, Florence, Trent) is a development — an accretion onto an originally narrower canon.1

Premise (1) is true and worth conceding plainly: the Hebrew-canon lists are real, early, and a recurring feature of the most learned patristic witness. The question is whether they carry the conclusion — whether the early church as such excluded the deuterocanon, or whether a canon-conscious minority tiered books the wider church received and read as Scripture.

Who advances it

The proponents. "The early church didn't believe they were part of Scripture" is one of the standard Protestant arguments for the Reformation's return to the Hebrew canon, repeated in popular apologetics and catechesis and resting on exactly the patristic lists this timeline records. Its serious scholarly form is Roger Beckwith's The Old Testament Canon of the New Testament Church (1985), which reads the Hebrew-sized Fathers as preserving the apostolic canon. On this timeline the same instinct stands behind every Hebrew-minded witness — Melito, Origen, Cyril, Athanasius, Epiphanius, Jerome, Rufinus.1 The adversaries. Catholic and Orthodox apologists answer that the church's working Bible was the Greek Septuagint, which contained the disputed books; that the great Christian codices carry them interleaved with the protocanon, not set apart; and that a whole Western and liturgical stream — the African councils, Augustine, Innocent I — received them among the Scriptures from the late fourth century on. Mainstream canon scholarship frames the era not as exclusion but as a long, unsettled division.2

The evidence for the affirmative reading

The Hebrew-sized lists are real, and they come from the church's most learned canon-watchers. This is the argument's true core and should not be minimized. The earliest extant Christian list of the Old Testament — Melito of Sardis (c. 170), who travelled East "to the place where these things were preached" to learn the books "accurately" — is Hebrew-sized, with Tobit, Judith, Sirach, Baruch, and the Maccabees all absent. Origen (c. 240) counts "twenty-two, as the Hebrews have handed them down." Cyril of Jerusalem (c. 350) charges his catechumens to "have nothing to do with the apocryphal writings." Athanasius (367), Epiphanius (c. 385), and Rufinus (c. 404) each set the disputed books in an explicit tier below the canon, and Jerome (c. 390) draws the hardest line of all, supplying the very word — apocrypha — the Protestant Bibles would use.3 Pressed to its strongest form, the argument is not "no one received these books" but "the Fathers who actually investigated the Hebrew enumeration consistently ranked them below it." That pattern is genuine, and it is the spine of the Reformation's case.

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The narrow, graded thesis is sound: a learned, Hebrew-canon-conscious stream of Fathers did rank the deuterocanon below the canon, and the formal Eastern lists are Hebrew-sized. As a claim about that stream — and about the absence of an undisputed, universal reception of these books as fully canonical before the fourth-century Western councils — the argument stands, and it coheres with everything this timeline records of Melito, Origen, Cyril, Athanasius, Epiphanius, Jerome, and Rufinus.

What overstates. The flat, universal conclusion — that the early church as such rejected these books, so their later use is an accretion — gives way at three joints.

  1. The books were in the church's working Bible from the start; they were not "added" later. The Old Testament the early church actually read was the Greek Septuagint, and the Septuagint contained Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch, and the Maccabees. The three great fourth-/fifth-century Christian Bibles — Codices Vaticanus, Sinaiticus, and Alexandrinus — carry these books interleaved with the protocanon, not bracketed off in an appendix. Whatever tiering the canon-lists imposed, the disputed books were a long-standing inheritance within the church's Scriptures, not a foreign import smuggled in at Hippo or Trent. The "accretion" framing inverts the chronology: the late development is not the inclusion of these books but the Reformation's removal of them.4

  2. A robust, equally early stream received them among the Scriptures. The witness is divided, not one-sided. Earlier than any canon-list, the disputed books are already in use: Clement of Rome (c. 96), in the earliest post-apostolic writing, weaves the Wisdom of Solomon into his appeal to the Corinthians beside the Gospel and the Psalms and holds up "the blessed Judith" as a pattern of faith — the first Christian witness to the deuterocanon functioning as edifying, quotable Scripture. A generation later Polycarp of Smyrna (c. 125), in his Epistle to the Philippians, urges almsgiving with Tobit's own maxim — "alms delivers from death" (Tob 4:10; 12:9) — braided indistinguishably into a chain of phrases from 1 Peter and Isaiah. The Didache (c. 100) likewise embeds a line of Sirach (4:31) in its catalogue of moral duties — though its weight is softer, since that line sits inside the inherited Jewish "Two Ways" material and may travel as common ethical tradition rather than a deliberate citation. The same word "Wisdom" that Melito most likely meant for Proverbs, the contemporary Western Muratorian Fragment (c. 170) ranks as the deuterocanonical Wisdom of Solomon among the received books. The earliest Latin Father, Tertullian (c. 210) — writing from the very Carthage whose councils would later enshrine the longer canon — quotes Baruch (the Letter of Jeremiah) as the words of "Jeremias" beside Daniel and ranks the Wisdom of Solomon among "our Christian authorities," while adducing Judith and Susanna among the saints. His successor at Carthage, Cyprian (c. 253), goes further still, citing six disputed books with his fullest formulae — Tobit, Sirach, the Wisdom of Solomon, 1 Maccabees, the Letter of Jeremiah (Baruch 6), and the Greek Daniel (Bel and the Dragon) — as “Holy Scripture,” “the sacred Scripture,” and what “the Holy Spirit… by Jeremiah… teaches,” the same labels he reserves for Moses and the Gospel. From the late fourth century the Western and African churches level the disputed books fully into the canon: the councils of Hippo and Carthage (393–419) list Tobit, Judith, and the Maccabees outright among the Canonical Scriptures; Augustine reckons them by a rule of reception ("prefer those that are received by all the catholic churches"); and a sitting bishop of Rome, Innocent I (405), appends a canon-list that files them among the histories and the books of Solomon with no second tier. This is not a medieval novelty: it is a fourth-century reception running in parallel with — and against — the Hebrew-sized lists.5

  3. Even the "excluders" kept reading the books as Scripture in church. The patristic verdict is rarely the flat "No" the argument needs; it is graded. Origen counts a Hebrew twenty-two yet refuses to "reject as spurious the copies in use in our Churches," defending the Greek Susanna against Julius Africanus and — across his homilies and treatises — quoting Tobit, Sirach, Baruch, the Maccabees, Wisdom, and Judith as graphē, while of Susanna he says outright that those who deny it "excise [it] from the list of divine books. But we both receive it." Athanasius files the deuterocanon among books "appointed by the Fathers to be read." Rufinus calls them "Ecclesiastical" — "read in the Churches, but not appealed to for the confirmation of doctrine." Even Jerome, the hardest voice, translated Tobit and Judith at his bishops' command and granted the church "reads" them. "Below the canon" in these Fathers means read, used, and cited — a graded standing, not a rejection. The argument quietly converts a tier into an exclusion.6

What the divided witness really shows. Strip the universal claim away and an accurate, modest residue remains: the early church was divided on the deuterocanon. A learned, Hebrew-conscious minority — at its sharpest in Jerome — tiered the books below a canon measured by the Hebrew table, while the wider church read them in worship and a strong Western stream received them as Scripture outright. That is precisely the picture this timeline documents, and it is what the Eastern Synod of Jerusalem would later concede in 1672 — "not always have all of these been considered on the same level as the rest" — before overruling the unevenness by the breadth of churchly use. The history will support the graded thesis the Hebrew-minded Fathers actually held; it will not support the flat verdict that the early church rejected these books as Scripture.7

Assessment

Contested. The early witness is genuinely divided, and the assessment turns on which form of the claim is weighed. As a claim about the Hebrew-conscious stream — that the most learned canon-watchers from Melito to Jerome ranked the deuterocanon below a Hebrew-sized canon, and that no undisputed universal reception of these books as fully canonical predates the fourth-century Western councils — the argument is strong, and it is the historical backbone of the Reformation's case. As the flat, universal claim that "the early church did not believe these were Scripture," it does not hold: the books stood in the Septuagint the church read and in the codices it copied; a parallel, equally early Western and liturgical stream (the African councils, Augustine, Innocent I) received them among the Scriptures; and even the Hebrew-minded Fathers read and cited them, ranking them in a tier rather than rejecting them. The honest finding is the one the Synod of Jerusalem itself conceded — that these books were not "always … considered on the same level as the rest" — so the deuterocanon is best described not as a late addition to the canon but as a long-disputed inheritance within it: tiered by a minority, used by the majority, leveled up in the West and (later) down in Reformed Protestantism. Strong by the Hebraist Fathers' lights, weak as a universal verdict; no clear winner.
