"Therefore also said the wisdom of God" (Luke 11:49): does Jesus quote a written source outside the Old Testament canon?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak. At Luke 11:49 a saying — "I will send them prophets and apostles, and some of them they shall slay and persecute" — is ascribed to "the wisdom of God" (hē sophia tou theou), yet stands at no exact Old-Testament locus. The puzzle is real and genuinely unsettled. But the interpretation pressed onto it — that "the wisdom of God" names a written source outside the Old-Testament canon (a lost wisdom book) that Jesus quotes as Scripture — is weak: the patristic reading takes "the wisdom of God" to be Christ himself speaking in his own person (so the parallel at Matthew 23:34 has Jesus say "I send"), and the dominant modern reading takes it as personified Wisdom (Sophia) speaking — on neither is a quoted extra-canonical writing in view · Related arguments: New-Testament quotations with no Old-Testament source — see also the centerpiece Ephesians 5:14, 1 Corinthians 2:9, and John 7:38

Here the sub-claim is that Luke 11:49 — a "Scripture"-like saying ascribed to "the wisdom of God" with no Old-Testament locus — helps show the apostolic "Scripture" reaching beyond the Hebrew canon.

The claim

"Therefore also said the wisdom of God, I will send them prophets and apostles, and some of them they shall slay and persecute."

— Luke 11:49 (KJV). The attribution formula "said the wisdom of God" introduces a first-person saying that appears in no surviving Old-Testament book; the words quoted in the Septuagint that elsewhere head Gospel citations are absent. As one sibling of the cluster headed by Ephesians 5:14, the verse is enlisted to show the New Testament quoting, as authoritative, words with no canonical source — and, on the strongest form of the reading, citing a lost written wisdom-text.1

The patristic record

The Fathers read "the wisdom of God" as Christ himself. Ambrose of Milan, expounding Luke, takes the speaker to be the Lord in his own person: "Therefore Wisdom sends apostles and prophets to them. Who is Wisdom if not Christ? Finally, in Matthew you have: Behold, I send you prophets and wise men." Bede follows him exactly — "He calls himself the wisdom of God, for he is indeed the power of God and the wisdom of God" (1 Corinthians 1:24) — and turns the verse against the Marcionites: because the same Wisdom of God sent both the prophets and the apostles, there are not "two Gods," one of the Law and another of the Gospel. On this reading "said the wisdom of God" is not a citation of any book but Jesus speaking as the divine Wisdom; the decisive proof is the parallel, where Matthew drops the attribution and has Jesus say it himself: "Behold, I send unto you prophets, and wise men, and scribes" (Matthew 23:34).2

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The datum is real and, here, genuinely hard: the saying is introduced as the utterance of "the wisdom of God," and it stands at no exact Old-Testament locus — not in Proverbs, not in the wisdom books, not in the Septuagint. Of the four cluster-verses this is the one where the source is least recoverable, and serious scholarship concedes the attribution may point to a now-lost writing.

What is overstated. The leap to "Jesus quotes a non-canonical writing as Scripture" outruns the evidence, because the best explanations do not require a quoted book at all.

  1. The patristic reading needs no source-document: Christ is the Wisdom of God. Ambrose and Bede take "the wisdom of God" as a title of the speaker — Jesus uttering the saying in his own divine person — which the Matthean parallel confirms by putting the very words in Jesus' mouth ("I send"). On this reading there is no quotation and so no missing book; the formula identifies the speaker, not a written authority.

  2. The dominant modern reading also needs no book: personified Wisdom speaks. Because the saying stands in Matthew and Luke but not Mark, source critics assign it to the sayings tradition (Q), and the wording "the Wisdom of God said" is widely read as personified Wisdom (Sophia) speaking — the same figure who speaks in Proverbs 8 — with Matthew redacting the saying onto Jesus' own lips. Whether the words come from a lost wisdom writing, or are a free composition placed in Wisdom's mouth, is acknowledged as genuinely uncertain; but the leading options are speaker-identifications and free sapiential composition, not the citation of an extra-canonical book.

  3. A formula marks a saying, not a canon-verdict. Even if a lost source lay behind the words, ascribing a saying to "the wisdom of God" would no more canonize a book than the New Testament's quoting 1 Enoch (Jude 14–15) or the pagan poets canonizes them. The strong, wider-canon thesis — that the apostles received a particular non-canonical writing as Scripture — does not follow from the bare attribution.

What survives. A real but modest residue, and the largest of the cluster: the source of Luke 11:49 is genuinely unrecoverable, and the saying is introduced with an authority-formula ("said the wisdom of God") that the Septuagint cannot satisfy. That complicates any picture of a hard-edged, fully-closed apostolic canon. But it does not establish a quoted non-canonical book: the patristic and the mainstream modern readings alike take the formula to name a speaker — Christ, or personified Wisdom — not a writing.

Assessment

Weak. That Luke 11:49 ascribes to "the wisdom of God" a saying with no Old-Testament locus is sound, and its source is the least recoverable in the cluster; but the inference that Jesus thereby quotes a non-canonical writing as Scripture is weak. The patristic reading (Ambrose, Bede) takes "the wisdom of God" to be Christ speaking in his own person — confirmed by the parallel at Matthew 23:34, where Jesus says "I send" — and the dominant modern reading takes it as personified Wisdom (Sophia) speaking; on neither is a quoted book in view. The genuine uncertainty about the source leaves a soft-boundary point standing, but not the wider-canon claim. (A sibling of the cluster headed by Ephesians 5:14; like 1 Corinthians 2:9 and John 7:38, the specific wider-canon inference does not hold, though the source here is harder to pin than in either.)
