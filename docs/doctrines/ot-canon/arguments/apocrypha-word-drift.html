Had the word "apocrypha" drifted to mean "inspired-but-hidden" by the eve of Trent — so calling the deuterocanon "apocrypha" can't be read as rejecting it?

Type: methodological argument (terminological; off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — for the load-bearing form of the claim. That the word apocrypha is genuinely polysemous, and that its valence shifts by author and era, is true and defensible (and is the sound finding weighed on the companion "canon"-word page): so a bare "he called it apocrypha" is by itself weak evidence of anything until one asks what that writer meant by it. But the specific interpretation this argument serves — that by the fifteenth century the term had been so twisted, chiefly by medieval readers trying to make Jerome orthodox, that it had come to mean "inspired Scripture that is somehow hidden," to the point that fathers at the Council of Trent argued for the full canonicity of the deuterocanon while still calling it Apocrypha — is not borne out. The strong empirical claim is unlocated in the sources and runs against the actual Trent record: the documented grading-down party there (Seripando, in the train of Jerome and Cajetan) used ecclesiastical, not apocrypha, and lowered the books' authority rather than raising it; and across the Latin West that inherited Jerome, apocrypha / "not in the canon" kept its lower-tier, not-for-doctrine sense — the drift, where the word moved at all, ran the opposite way from what the argument needs.

(sound); does quoting a book "as Scripture" prove a Father held it canonical? (plausible); did the early church build doctrine only on the undisputed books? The claim

For example, the word Apocrypha by the time of the 15th century had become so twisted—this is usually due to medieval writers who tried to make Jerome fit into an orthodox stance—that they would twist his words on calling the deuterocanonical books Apocrypha to mean something like "inspired scripture that is somehow hidden." By the time of the Council of Trent, the word Apocrypha had been so twisted that you would actually find fathers in the Council of Trent who argued for the full divinity and canonicity of these books, calling them Apocrypha.

— Gary Michuta, The Great Debate IX: Is the Apocrypha Scripture? (Michuta vs. James White, 20 May 2004), recording.1 The interpretation this serves is a solvent for a whole family of shorter-canon proof-texts that lean on the word: where a father, a preface, or the Glossa labels one of the disputed books "apocryphal," the apologist reads that as "not Scripture." If the term had drifted far enough that a fifteenth- or sixteenth-century writer could call a book "apocrypha" and mean it was inspired, then that label proves nothing, and the shorter-canon appeals to it collapse. (This is a different word from the κανών/"canon" studied on the companion page; here the question is the career of apocrypha itself.)

Who advances it

The form weighed here is Gary Michuta's, pressed in his 2004 debate with James White and standing behind the terminological sections of his Why Catholic Bibles Are Bigger and The Case for the Deuterocanon, where he notes (rightly, and more cautiously than in the debate) that "the meaning of the term 'apocrypha' has changed over time" and that the word means "'hidden' or perhaps 'not recognized.'"2 The cautious statement — the word is polysemous — is uncontroversial and shared by every historian of the canon. The debate statement adds a strong, datable empirical thesis on top of it: a specific direction of drift ("inspired-but-hidden") and a specific proof (Trent fathers using the word that way). It is that added thesis, not the bare polysemy, that is load-bearing for the apologetic payoff, and it is that thesis this page tests.

The evidence for

The word really is polysemous, and its early career really does begin at "hidden." ἀπόκρυφος means "hidden away, concealed." Its earliest religious use is honorific-esoteric: writings withheld from common use because thought to hold mysterious or too-profound lore — the sense of 4 Ezra 14, where Ezra is told to publish the twenty-four public books but keep back "the seventy that were written last" for "the wise among your people," since "in them is the spring of understanding." Bruce Metzger's standard Introduction to the Apocrypha opens on exactly this: the books were "hidden" or withdrawn because regarded as containing esoteric lore.3 So the raw material for a semantic argument is real: apocrypha did not always mean "spurious trash," and it never carried a single fixed valence. To that extent the point that "he called it apocrypha" cannot be read off flatly is well taken — and it is the genuinely sound residue of the argument, treated more fully on the "canon"-word and "ecclesiastical"-category pages.

Adversarial assessment

The direction of the patristic drift is the opposite of "inspired-but-hidden." By the fourth century the esteemed "hidden" sense had, in Christian usage, largely given way not to "inspired" but to "spurious / heretical." The hinge witness is the very letter the deuterocanon's defenders lean on elsewhere: Athanasius's Festal Letter 39 (367) runs three tiers — the canonical books; a middle tier "appointed by the Fathers to be read" (Wisdom, Sirach, Esther, Judith, Tobit, the Didache, the Shepherd), which he pointedly does not call apocrypha; and, below both, the apocrypha, which "are the invention of heretics, who indeed write them whenever they wish, bestowing upon them their approval, and assigning to them a date." He closes: "The former, my brethren, are included in the Canon, the latter being merely read; nor is there any place a mention of secret writings" — apocrypha as the discarded, forged category beneath the read-but-not-canonical books, not a synonym for inspired-but-hidden ones.4

Jerome's coinage — the very use the medievals were supposedly "twisting" toward orthodoxy — puts the deuterocanon at the bottom, not the top. Jerome's Prologus Galeatus (c. 391) is the text that fixes the stringent Latin sense: "what is outside of them must be placed aside among the Apocryphal writings" (quicquid extra hos est, inter apocrifa seponendum), and then, by name, "Wisdom… and the book of Jesus the Son of Sirach, and Judith, and Tobias, and the Shepherd are not in the canon." Jerome is applying apocrypha more harshly to the deuterocanon than his contemporaries did; the medieval and later Western reception did not follow him into calling these books inspired-but-hidden — it either kept his lower-tier "read but not for doctrine" grading or muted his harshness while retaining the demotion. There is no documented arc by which "make Jerome orthodox" turned his apocrypha into "inspired."5

On the eve of and into the Reformation, "apocrypha" hardens as the label for the lower tier, not a covert "inspired." Cardinal Cajetan (d. 1534), writing just before Trent, explicitly followed Jerome: the deuterocanonical books are "not canonical — that is, not in the nature of a rule for confirming matters of faith — yet they may be called canonical… for the edification of the faithful." When Luther gathered the same books between the Testaments in 1534 he headed the section "Apocrypha: that is, books which are not held equal to the Holy Scriptures, and yet are profitable and good to read," and Coverdale (1535) followed with "not reckoned to be of like authority with the other books of the Bible." Every one of these near-Trent uses of apocrypha means below canonical Scripture, read for edification — the exact opposite pole from "inspired-but-hidden."6

The load-bearing claim about Trent is unlocated and contradicted by the record. The documented father who pressed a distinction at Trent, Cardinal Girolamo Seripando, did not call the deuterocanon "apocrypha" while affirming its full canonicity; he did the reverse. Following Jerome, Rufinus, and Cajetan, he wanted the protocanonical books classed as canonici et authentici and the deuterocanon as merely canonici et ecclesiastici — belonging to a rule of conduct (canon morum) rather than the rule of faith (canon fidei), "suited for the edification of the people, but… not sufficient to prove a dogma." The word the grading-down party used was ecclesiastical, and it lowered the books. The council rejected that distinction: the decree of Session IV (8 April 1546) received all the listed books "as sacred and canonical… entire with all their parts," used the word apocrypha for none of them, and attached an anathema to anyone who does not so receive them — carried by roughly twenty-four placet to fifteen against, with sixteen abstentions. No conciliar father is on record calling the deuterocanon "apocrypha" while defending its canonicity; the claim appears to travel only in the oral-debate literature, and the acta as digested by Hubert Jedin point the other way.7

What the sound kernel actually licenses. Granting all the real polysemy, the honest inference is narrow: because apocrypha carried more than one sense, a bare occurrence of the word is not self-interpreting, and one must read each writer's own definition before scoring his label as a rejection. That is a caution against a lazy word-count, and it is sound. It does not establish the strong thing the argument needs — that the default late-medieval and Reformation-era sense had inverted toward "inspired," so that the label reliably fails to signal demotion. In the Latin West of Jerome's train the default ran the other way, and where a writer defined his terms (Cajetan, Luther, Seripando's party), "apocrypha" or "ecclesiastical" meant below the canon, read for edification, not for doctrine.

Assessment

Weak, in the form that does the apologetic work. The bare observation underneath it — that apocrypha is polysemous and its valence is author- and era-dependent, so a lone use of the word is not a knockdown — is true, but it is a different and much narrower claim, and it is weighed as sound on the "canon"-word page. The interpretation actually asserted here — that the term had drifted to "inspired-but-hidden" by the eve of Trent, evidenced by Trent fathers affirming canonicity while calling the books "apocrypha" — is not supported: the load-bearing Trent claim is unlocated and cuts against the record (the documented distinction-party used ecclesiastical and graded the books down; the decree dropped apocrypha entirely), and across the Latin West the word's deuterocanon-sense, from Jerome through Cajetan and into Luther, kept its lower-tier, not-for-doctrine valence. The drift, where it moved the word at all, ran opposite to the argument's need. So the solvent does not dissolve the shorter-canon labels: it remains legitimate to ask what a given writer meant by "apocrypha," but the answer, for the Western witnesses in view, is usually "read, edifying, below the canon" — not "inspired." Michuta's cautious statement that the word's meaning shifted should be credited; his strong Trent claim should be flagged as unsourced and, as stated, contrary to the acta.
