Did prophecy cease after Malachi — does that disqualify the deuterocanon from the canon?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — for the inference the proponents draw. The premise is sound and well-attested: a major Jewish stream held that prophetic inspiration lapsed with the last prophets Haggai, Zechariah, and Malachi, and the deuterocanonical books were written after that. But the argument needs two further links the proponents simply assume — that cessation of prophecy entails closure of the canon, and that this closure was understood to exclude the later books as non-Scripture — and both fail on the evidence. The very rabbinic corpus that preserves the cessation tradition still cites Ben Sira as a verse of the Writings and still debated Qohelet and the Song of Songs into the second century; 1 Maccabees itself records that prophecy had ceased, so the deuterocanon is a witness to the cessation idea, not a violator of it; and the late-rabbinic move that would bar "everything written after" Sirach would, on its makers' own terms, bar the Christian Gospels too. What survives is the narrower, true point that a Jewish stream graded the later writings below the prophetic books — a contributing datum to the deuterocanon's graded standing, not a decisive disqualifier

The claim

The argument runs as a syllogism. Jewish tradition holds that prophetic inspiration ceased with the last prophets — Haggai, Zechariah, and Malachi, in the mid-fifth century BC. The Babylonian Talmud states it plainly:

"After the last prophets Haggai, Zechariah, and Malachi died, the Divine Spirit of prophetic revelation departed from the Jewish people…"

— b. Yoma 9b; the same tradition stands at b. Sotah 48b and, in its earliest form, at Tosefta Sotah 13:2–3.1 A companion passage counts the Holy Spirit of prophecy among the things the Second Temple lacked that the First had possessed: "the Ark… fire; and the Divine Presence; and the Divine Spirit; and the Urim VeTummim."2 The deuterocanonical books (Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch, 1–2 Maccabees) were all composed after that cutoff. Therefore, the argument concludes, the Jews did not — and the church should not — reckon them inspired Scripture: no prophet, no Scripture. The sub-claim weighed here is precisely that inference: that the cessation of prophecy after Malachi disqualifies the later-written deuterocanon from the canon.

Who advances it

In its careful, scholarly form it is Roger Beckwith's, who in The Old Testament Canon of the New Testament Church gathers the rabbinic cessation statements (including Tosefta Sotah 13:2) to argue that the canon was effectively closed before the deuterocanon was written. Popularly, it is a recurring prop under the master "the Jews rejected the deuterocanon" claim. James White, in his debate with Gary Michuta, reads the rabbinic sources aloud: "…from the Babylonian Talmud: our rabbis taught, since the death of the last prophets Haggai, Zechariah, and Malachi the Holy Spirit of prophetic inspiration departed from Israel," and, citing the five-things passage, "the Second Temple lacked five things which the First Temple possessed… the fire, the ark, the urim and the thummim, the oil of anointing, and the Holy Spirit of prophecy." CARM (Matt Slick) states it flatly — "the Jewish Community acknowledged that the prophetic gifts had ceased in Israel before the Apocrypha was written" — listing among the marks that divide the deuterocanon from the canonical books that "they are not prophetic… and they even acknowledge that there were no prophets in Israel at their time (cf. 1 Macc. 9:27; 14:41)," and crediting the argument to Geisler and MacKenzie.3 Mike Winger makes the same move in popular form, telling viewers that 1 Maccabees cannot be evidence of God still speaking when its own author reports that the prophets had ceased — "since the time that a prophet was not seen among them" (9:27) — so the book "disqualifies" itself as Scripture.3 The adversarial side — Gary Michuta and Trent Horn for the Catholic case, and the mainstream canon historians (Sundberg, Lim, McDonald) — answers that the cessation tradition is real but proves nothing about which books are Scripture, and that the rabbinic corpus itself refutes the tidy inference.4

The evidence for

The cessation premise is genuine, early, and well-attested — the argument's real strength. This is no invention of modern apologists. The notion that prophecy lapsed after Haggai, Zechariah, and Malachi is woven through the Second-Temple and rabbinic literature, and it is independently confirmed by the deuterocanon's own books. 1 Maccabees — itself written after Malachi — three times registers the absence of prophets: there was "a great affliction in Israel, the like whereof was not since the time that a prophet was not seen among them" (9:27); the defiled altar-stones are stored "until there should come a prophet to shew what should be done with them" (4:46); and Simon is made leader and high priest "until there should arise a faithful prophet" (14:41). So the author of a deuterocanonical book plainly knew himself to be living in an age without prophets. Josephus says the same when he bounds the divinely-credited books at Artaxerxes "because there hath not been an exact succession of prophets since that time." That a major Jewish stream graded the post-prophetic writings below the prophetic ones is therefore sound, and it is the real datum the argument trades on — the Hebrew-measure reading this timeline treats as a serious, live position.5

Adversarial assessment

What holds. That a recognized Jewish tradition held prophecy to have ceased after Malachi, and that the deuterocanonical books were written after that cutoff, is true and undisputed. So is the modest conclusion that follows: a major stream of Judaism ranked these later books below the prophetic Scriptures — a real contribution to their graded standing. The trouble is that the argument is enlisted to prove far more: that cessation closed the canon against the deuterocanon as non-Scripture. That inference fails at two joints.

  1. Cessation of prophecy does not entail closure of the canon — and the same corpus that says prophecy ceased still treated later books as Scripture. "No living prophet now" and "no more inspired books may be received" are two different claims, and the second does not follow from the first; books already written by recognized authors could still be weighed, debated, and received long after the gift of prophecy was thought to have lapsed. The decisive evidence is internal to the rabbinic literature itself. The very Talmud that preserves the cessation tradition, at Bava Kamma 92b, quotes Ben Sira (Sirach — a deuterocanonical book written after Malachi) with a Scripture-introduction formula, ranking it among "the Writings": "it is triplicated in the Writings, as it is written…" And the same tradition still debated the status of Qohelet (Ecclesiastes) and the Song of Songs in the second century — Mishnah Yadayim 3:5 records the dispute over whether they "render the hands impure," with Rabbi Akiva insisting "all the writings are holy, but the Song of Songs is the holy of holies." A tradition still arguing over which protocanonical books are Scripture, and still citing a deuterocanonical one as a verse of the Writings, plainly had not derived a single clean, closed, deuterocanon-excluding list from "prophecy ceased." The cessation tradition and a settled canon simply did not move in lockstep.6

  2. The deuterocanon is a witness to the cessation idea, not a violator of it — and pressed as a canon-rule the argument proves too much. The proponents cite 1 Maccabees 9:27 and 14:41 to show that the later books "acknowledge there were no prophets" — but that cuts against the conclusion as much as for it. The book that records the absence of prophets is one of the very books said to be disqualified by that absence; its author evidently saw no contradiction between writing an authoritative sacred history and living after the prophets. "Written in a prophet-less age" was, for him, simply the truth about his own time, not a confession of non-Scripture. And the rule, taken strictly, recoils on those who wield it. The late-rabbinic ban — "he who reads the outside books" (m. Sanhedrin 10:1; b. Sanhedrin 100b), aimed in part at Ben Sira and "everything written after" it — would, on its makers' own terms, exclude the Christian Gospels and Epistles too, all of them written long after Malachi. An argument that, pressed to fix a binding canon for the church, would unmake the New Testament has overshot its mark. The narrower point Josephus actually makes is the failure of the exact succession of prophets, not of prophecy as such — he claims a prophetic gift for himself and reports later prophetic figures — so even his version will not bear the flat "all prophecy ceased after Malachi" the popular form loads onto it.7

Proponent overreach to flag. White's reading of the Talmud is accurate as far as the cessation tradition goes, but he and CARM both slide from the sound premise ("prophecy had ceased") to the unproven conclusion ("therefore the later books are not Scripture and the Jews rejected them as such") without supplying the missing link — and CARM's own proof-texts, 1 Maccabees 9:27 and 14:41, are deuterocanonical verses, so the argument quietly relies on the authority of the books it means to exclude. What every framing is entitled to is the narrower, true point: a major Jewish stream held the post-Malachi writings to be of lesser, non-prophetic authority — a real contribution to the deuterocanon's graded standing, not a proof that the canon was shut against them.8

Assessment

Weak — as a decisive disqualifier; the premise it rests on is sound. That a recognized Jewish tradition held prophecy to have ceased after Haggai, Zechariah, and Malachi, and that the deuterocanon was written after that, is true, early, and confirmed by 1 Maccabees itself; and the modest conclusion that a major Jewish stream graded these books below the prophetic Scriptures is a genuine datum, converging with the Hebrew-measure reading this timeline traces from Jerome. But the inference the proponents draw — that cessation closed the canon against the deuterocanon as non-Scripture — does not hold on its best case. Cessation of prophecy is not closure of the canon; the same rabbinic corpus that preserves the cessation tradition cites Ben Sira as a verse of the Writings and still debated Qohelet and the Song into the second century; the deuterocanon is a witness to the cessation idea rather than a violator of it; and the rule, pressed to bind the church, would exclude the Gospels too. Trust the premise as evidence for the books' graded standing; flag — on the proponents' accounts (White, CARM) — the unsupported leap from "prophecy ceased" to "the canon was shut."
