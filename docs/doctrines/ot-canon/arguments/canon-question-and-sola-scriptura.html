Does the deuterocanon dispute undermine sola scriptura — can Scripture settle its own table of contents?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — for the underlying tension; weak for the polemical conclusion drawn from it. The argument names a real and much-discussed problem: Scripture contains no inspired list of its own books, so the canon cannot be derived from Scripture, and the deuterocanon dispute shows that even among Christians the table of contents is genuinely contested — a standing strain on the principle that Scripture is the sole final rule. That much is a serious epistemological tension with strong advocates on both sides and no clear winner. But the leap from "there is a canon-question" to "the whole Protestant experiment fails" does not hold on its best case: uncertainty over a handful of books at the margin does not dissolve the canon's settled core or make every doctrine undecidable; the Catholic alternative faces its own dating and regress problems (the canon was not dogmatically defined for the Latin West until Trent, 1546 — after the Reformation challenge); and "removed books" begs the question against the Hebrew-canon view, on which the Reformers restored rather than subtracted · back to timeline · advanced by Catholic apologists (Devin Rose, Dave Armstrong), answered by Reformed theologians (R. C. Sproul, Michael Kruger)

An argument page weighs a single sub-claim adduced in support of a main interpretation — a proof-text, a parallel, a historical thesis — and renders an adversarial assessment of the interpretation it serves. Unlike a person-detail page it sits across the timeline rather than at one date. Here the sub-claim is not a single proof-text but a meta-argument: that the very fact this timeline documents — a canon disputed for fifteen centuries and still contested at the Reformation — turns back on the Protestant rule of faith and undoes it. The reception-history it presupposes is documented across the timeline and not re-argued here; this page weighs whether that history carries the conclusion built on it.

The claim

The argument runs from a fact this whole timeline establishes to a sweeping conclusion about authority:

  1. Sola scriptura makes Scripture the sole final authority for doctrine — what is "biblical" is the test of what may bind the conscience.
  2. But Scripture nowhere lists its own books: there is no inspired table of contents. The boundary of the Bible must therefore be received from somewhere outside Scripture — from the church, from tradition, from history.
  3. And that boundary is not even agreed: the deuterocanon dispute this timeline traces shows the table of contents genuinely contested among Christians down to (and past) the Reformation, with the Reformers removing seven books bound in the Western Bible for a thousand years.
  4. Therefore a Protestant cannot, on Scripture alone, settle whether a doctrine — purgatory, say — is "biblical," because the table of contents of Scripture is itself in dispute and cannot be fixed by Scripture. The rule of faith collapses into circularity: "the whole Protestant experiment fails."1

The dilemma is often pressed in a sharper form. Is the canon-list itself authoritative or not? If it is not authoritative, then which books are Scripture becomes a matter of mere opinion. If it is authoritative, then a Protestant is treating an extra-biblical judgment as binding — and has thereby conceded an authority alongside Scripture, contradicting the sola. Either way, the apologist concludes, the principle is scuttled.1

Who advances it

The proponents. This is one of the most widely circulated Catholic apologetic arguments. Devin Rose presses the circularity directly: "a consistent Protestant argument for selecting the canon of Scripture … must itself come from Scripture, which would create a circular argument," and ties it to the deuterocanon — "by the end of the Reformation Protestantism had removed seven books … from the Old Testament canon." Dave Armstrong runs it as a flat "conundrum": "Scripture alone cannot establish the biblical canon."1 Strikingly, the force of the premise is granted by thoughtful Protestants. C. Michael Patton, defending sola scriptura, calls the question "the most persuasive argument against sola Scriptura that there is," and concedes that "the list on page 23 of my Bible is not part of the canon … the list itself is not part of the inspired word of God"; the concession traces to R. C. Sproul's famous formula that the Protestant has "a fallible collection of infallible books."2 The adversaries. The reply is carried above all by Michael J. Kruger, whose Canon Revisited (2012) and The Question of Canon (2013) are book-length answers to exactly this challenge, and by the older evangelical rejoinder of Norman Geisler and Ralph MacKenzie, that Rome faces the same epistemic problem one level up — "a fallible belief about an infallible authority" — so that "we are all in the same boat."3

The evidence for

The premise is true, and it is the strongest form of the canon-question. No book of Scripture contains a list of the books of Scripture; the sixty-six-book (or seventy-three-book) table of contents is the church's, not a revealed text. So the canon — the precondition for appealing to Scripture at all — is one thing the sola scriptura principle cannot itself supply. This is not a Catholic invention but a point Reformed writers concede in their own words (Sproul's "fallible collection," Patton's "page 23"), which is why it is the most serious version of the objection rather than a debater's trick.2

The dispute is real, and this timeline is the evidence. The argument's factual backbone is exactly what the page above documents: for fifteen centuries there was no single fixed list; Hebrew-minded Fathers (Melito, Origen, Jerome) ranked the disputed books below a Hebrew-sized canon, while the African councils, Augustine, and Florence received them among the Scriptures, and the question was still live enough for Luther and Trent to answer it in opposite directions in the same generation. The canon's edge genuinely was — and on the deuterocanon still is — contested across the great communions.

So the tension is genuine. If the boundary of Scripture must be drawn by a judgment Scripture does not itself contain, and that judgment is disputed, then the Protestant who wants to settle a doctrine "by Scripture alone" does have a prior question to answer about which Scripture — and cannot answer it by simply opening the book. That much of the argument stands, and it is why the canon-question has generated a serious literature on both sides rather than a quick dismissal.4

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The core premise is sound and important: there is no inspired table of contents, the canon cannot be derived from Scripture, and some judgment external to the text — historical, ecclesial — is unavoidable in recognizing it. The deuterocanon dispute is a real, unresolved instance of that, traced list by list on this timeline. A Protestant who claims to settle everything "by Scripture alone" while taking the extent of Scripture on the church's word does owe an account of that, and the better Reformed writers know it. As a pointer to a genuine epistemological tension, the argument is not weak.

What overstates. The leap from that tension to "the whole Protestant experiment fails" overreaches on four counts.

  1. Marginal uncertainty does not dissolve the settled core. The dispute is over roughly seven Old Testament books and a few passages; the protocanonical Old Testament and the entire New Testament are received in common by Catholics, Orthodox, and Protestants alike. A doctrine's scriptural standing can be tested on the protocanon and the deuterocanon separately — and indeed the worked example the proponent chooses, purgatory, turns on a deuterocanonical proof-text (2 Maccabees 12:43–45) that this site weighs on its own merits, finding it contested rather than decisive even granting the book. "The table of contents is unsettled at the edge" simply does not entail "no doctrine is decidable"; the overwhelming bulk of the canon, on which the great doctrines rest, is not in play.4

  2. The Catholic alternative faces the same regress, plus a dating problem. If "Scripture cannot ground its own canon" is fatal, the parallel question is fatal to the rival: how is the infallible church identified, and on what infallible ground, without already using some fallible historical judgment? Geisler and MacKenzie press exactly this — the Catholic has "a fallible belief about an infallible authority" no less than the Protestant — so the objection, if it proves anything, proves too much, sinking every boat in the river. And the historical record this timeline keeps complicates the "the Church always knew" story: the Latin West did not dogmatically define the deuterocanon until Trent in 1546 — after, and in answer to, the Reformation — while Florence (1442) had professed it only inside a failed reunion bull. The canon Rome calls settled-from-the-first was itself fixed under anathema only once the Reformers forced the question.5

  3. "Removed books" begs the disputed question. The argument's rhetorical engine is that the Reformers subtracted from a settled Bible. But that is the very point in dispute. On the Hebrew-canon view — Jerome's Hebraica veritas, the position of a whole stream of Fathers and of the Jews "unto whom were committed the oracles of God" — the Reformers restored an older and narrower canon and demoted books a learned tradition had always tiered below it. Whether that is "removal" or "restoration" is exactly what the canon dispute is about; the argument cannot help itself to "removal" as a premise without assuming its own conclusion. (The same question-begging runs the companion Vulgate burden-of-proof and Romans 3:2 arguments.)6

  4. Recognition is not derivation, and sola scriptura never claimed otherwise. The argument treats "the canon is not proved from Scripture" as if it refuted a doctrine that said it was. But the mature Reformed account does not ground the canon in a Scriptural list; it grounds Scripture's authority in God, received through the Spirit's internal witness and the church's ministerial (not magisterial) testimony — the Westminster Confession's "our full persuasion and assurance … is from the inward work of the Holy Spirit," not from "the testimony of any man or church." Kruger's "self-authenticating" model presses this: the canonical books bear intrinsic marks (divine qualities, apostolic origin, corporate reception) by which the church recognizes what it did not create — so the church's role is real but witness-bearing, not canon-conferring. One may judge that model contested; but it shows the inference "no inspired table of contents ⇒ the rule of faith collapses" is not valid. Sola scriptura is a claim about Scripture's supreme authority in settling doctrine, not a claim that the canon is a theorem derived from Scripture's text — and the canon-question, pressed at its hardest, lands on the recognition of Scripture, a problem every tradition shares, not on the authority of Scripture once recognized.7

What the dispute really turns on. Strip the "experiment fails" rhetoric away and what remains is the canon-question proper: the boundary of Scripture cannot be read off Scripture, so it is recognized by a judgment — the Spirit's witness plus the church's reception on the Protestant account, the magisterium's definition on the Catholic — and the two accounts of that recognition are the actual point at issue, a genuine and unsettled debate in which neither side escapes some circularity. That is a serious tension, honestly conceded by the better Protestant writers; it is not the same thing as showing that the disputed seven books leave the gospel's doctrines undecidable or the rule of faith incoherent.

Assessment

Contested — for the underlying tension; weak for the "whole experiment fails" conclusion. The serious form of the argument identifies something true and much-discussed: Scripture has no inspired table of contents, the canon cannot be derived from Scripture, and the deuterocanon dispute this timeline documents is a real, unresolved case of the table of contents being contested among Christians — a standing strain on sola scriptura that thoughtful Protestants (Sproul, Patton, Kruger) concede and engage rather than wave away, and on which the rival Protestant and Catholic accounts of how the canon is recognized remain genuinely disputed with no clear winner. But the polemical conclusion is conjecture beyond what the canon-uncertainty establishes. Uncertainty at the margin — seven books — does not dissolve the canon's common core or render doctrines undecidable (purgatory's own deuterocanonical proof-text is weighed on its merits regardless); the Catholic alternative faces the same recognition-regress one level up and the awkward fact that its canon was dogmatically fixed only at Trent in 1546, in answer to the Reformation it is meant to refute; "removed books" begs the Hebrew-canon question on which the Reformers claimed to be restoring; and the argument mistakes a claim about Scripture's authority for a claim that the canon is derived from Scripture, which no careful defender of the principle holds. The tension is real and worth taking seriously; the demolition is overstated. Flag the "the whole Protestant experiment fails" leap as the proponent's overreach, not as what the canon-question proves.
