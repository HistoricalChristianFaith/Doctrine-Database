Are the Fathers' "twenty-two / twenty-four" book-counts alphabet-numerology, not canon boundaries excluding the deuterocanon?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — the observation is sound (the totals are alphabet-driven and elastic, and the bare number will not bear a hard itemized exclusion), but the thesis it serves — that the counts are numerology instead of a canon judgment — does not hold: even at their elastic maximum the enumerations still exclude Wisdom, Sirach, and the Maccabees, and the mnemonic tracks the very Hebrew canon it is accused of not drawing. Advanced by Gary Michuta (against James White).

The claim

Hilary of Poitiers … cites that there are 22 books, corresponding to the 22 letters of the Hebrew alphabet. And they say, "Look, there—he affirms that the canon had been closed to exclude the Deuterocanonical books." Well, if you continue reading in that same passage, he says, "But if you want to add Tobit and Judith so as to make 24 and correspond to the Greek alphabet, then you could do that." … The fact of the matter is, Hilary of Poitiers wasn't concerned about which books were in the canon. What he was concerned with was the mystical correspondence of numbers, letters of the alphabet, and books. The same is true of Origen … he accidentally omits the Twelve Minor Prophets, which count as one book. And then, when he comes up with only 21 names at the end, he has to come up with a 22nd, so he adds Maccabees and gives it a Hebrew name, even though he says it was excluded from the canon. … The same is true with Epiphanius.

— Gary Michuta, in the 2004 debate with James White, Is the Apocrypha Scripture?, and in Why Catholic Bibles Are Bigger.1 The sub-claim weighed here is the general one Michuta draws from these three cases: that when a Father numbers the Old Testament at "twenty-two" (the Hebrew letters) or "twenty-four" (the Greek), the count is mystical numerology — a correspondence of books to letters, to the works of creation, to the generations from Adam — and therefore not a canon boundary drawn to exclude the deuterocanon.

Who advances it

The reading is Gary Michuta's, pressed in his 2004 debate with James White and in his book Why Catholic Bibles Are Bigger; it is a stock move in the Catholic case, meant to blunt the standard Protestant appeal to the Hebrew-sized patristic lists (Melito to Jerome) as proof that the early church excluded the disputed books. Its adversary here is James White, who answers that an alphabet-tied count is a mnemonic for a real list, not an invented number: Josephus's twenty-two and the Fathers' twenty-two converge on the same books precisely because that is what the Jews handed down, and Hilary himself says the Law "was divided into 22 books so that it might agree with the number of letters" — the number tracking a list, not floating free of one.2

The evidence for

The numbers really are alphabet-driven, and the Fathers say so. This is the sound core of Michuta's point, and it should be granted without grudging. The Fathers do not derive "twenty-two" by counting an independently-fixed shelf and finding it comes to twenty-two; they announce the number as a correspondence and then fit the books to it. Hilary opens, "The reason for reckoning twenty-two books of the Old Testament is that this corresponds with the number of the [Hebrew] letters," and closes by noting that "some add Tobit and Judith to make twenty-four books, according to the number of the Greek letters" — so the total is explicitly elastic, sliding from twenty-two to twenty-four to match whichever alphabet is in view.3 Epiphanius is the most florid: in On Weights and Measures he piles the same number everywhere — "the twenty-two works that God did in the six days of the making of the world … the twenty-two generations from Adam to Israel … the twenty-two signs of the letters from alef to taw … and the twenty-two books from Genesis to Esther" — a full-blown numerological scheme in which the canon-count is one instance of a cosmic pattern.4 And Origen's list shows the books being bent to hit the target: Ruth is folded into Judges, the paired Kings and Chronicles each counted once, Lamentations and "the epistle" bundled into Jeremiah — all to make the shelf come out to the twenty-two letters "as the Hebrews have handed them down."5 So Michuta is right that the number is a fixed frame the books are fitted into, and right that one cannot read a hard, itemized "these six are excluded" straight off the bare figure "twenty-two."

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The alphabet-and-elastic observation is correct and useful. The totals are chosen for symbolic correspondence, they float between twenty-two and twenty-four, and the fitting of the books to the number (folding Ruth, bundling the epistle) is real. Against a crude Protestant use — "the Fathers counted twenty-two, therefore they itemized and rejected exactly Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch, and 1–2 Maccabees" — the caution bites: the bare number does not do that work, and Hilary's own willingness to slide to twenty-four shows the boundary is softer than a flat exclusion. That much tracks the graded, not-flat reading this timeline already assigns the Hebrew-sized lists (the early-church-rejected and Josephus arguments are both rated only contested for just this reason).

Where the thesis gives way. Michuta's load-bearing step is not "the count is numerological" but "the count is numerological instead of a canon judgment — the Fathers 'weren't concerned about which books were in the canon.'" That step fails on the very texts he cites, on three counts.

  1. The numerology packages a canon verdict it does not dissolve. Grant that the number is alphabet-driven; the enumeration under it still renders a substantive judgment. Even at the elastic maximum — Hilary's twenty-four — the additions stop at Tobit and Judith; Wisdom, Sirach, and the Maccabees never appear at any count. Epiphanius is decisive here: he names the Wisdom of Solomon and the Wisdom of Sirach and rules them, in so many words, "helpful and useful, but … not included in the number of the recognized … not kept in the chest, that is, in the ark of the covenant." That is a canon-verdict rendered explicitly, the numerology notwithstanding — the opposite of "not concerned about which books were in the canon."6

  2. Origen, his best exhibit, cuts the other way. Michuta says Origen, one name short, "adds Maccabees … to reach 22." But Origen's own words file the Maccabees outside the count: "And besides these there are the Maccabees, which are entitled Sarbeth Sabanaiel" (ἔξω δὲ τούτων ἐστὶ τὰ Μακκαβαϊκά) — the deliberate gesture by which a book is marked off from the canonical list, not slipped into it. The "one short" appearance is an artifact of transmission: the list as Eusebius preserves it drops the Twelve Minor Prophets, a scribal lapse (otherwise the twenty-one named books fall short of twenty-two), not a genuine omission by Origen — and once the Twelve are restored, Origen has his twenty-two canonical books plus Maccabees named as lying beyond them. So the datum Michuta reads as "numerology forcing a book in" is, corrected, "a Father with a full Hebrew canon expressly excluding the Maccabees" — the exhibit refutes the thesis it was offered for.7

  3. Mnemonic and canon-boundary are not rivals. The dichotomy the argument needs — either the count is numerology or it is a canon list — is false. The alphabet-count is not arbitrary: the Hebrew canon the Fathers inherited from the synagogue, folded to match the letters (Ruth into Judges, Lamentations into Jeremiah), genuinely comes to twenty-two. The number is a memory-frame for that real list, which is exactly why the patristic "twenty-two" and Josephus's "twenty-two" land on the same books. White's rejoinder is decisive on this narrow point, and it is Hilary's own logic: the Law "was divided into twenty-two books so that it might agree with the number of the letters" — the division tracks the letters because the list was already there to be so divided. A mnemonic tied to the alphabet is not an invented number; it is a real enumeration wearing a memory-aid.8

What survives. The narrower, true residue is worth stating cleanly, because it is what a fair Protestant use should concede and a fair Catholic use should stop at: the bare figure "twenty-two / twenty-four" is a mnemonic total, it is elastic, and it cannot by itself be pressed as a deliberate, itemized rejection of each disputed book. But that residue is already the graded reading this timeline traces — it does not establish Michuta's stronger claim that the Fathers rendered no canon judgment at all. On the texts he adduces they plainly did: Hilary stops his own recitation at twenty-two and ascribes the twenty-four to "some"; Epiphanius rules the Wisdoms "not of the recognized"; Origen files the Maccabees "besides these." The numerology is real and the exclusion is real, and the two coexist without strain.

Assessment

Weak. The interpretation Michuta advances — that the patristic twenty-two / twenty-four counts are alphabet-numerology rather than canon boundaries, so the Fathers "weren't concerned about which books were in the canon" — does not hold on its best case. Its premise is sound: the totals are letter-driven, elastic, and fitted, and the bare number will not bear a hard itemized exclusion. But the conclusion overshoots the premise. The enumerations under the numbers exclude the deuterocanon proper at every count (Wisdom, Sirach, Maccabees never enter; Hilary's twenty-four adds only Tobit and Judith); Epiphanius renders the verdict in words ("not … of the recognized"); and Origen — Michuta's showcase — expressly files the Maccabees "besides these," his apparent one-short being a scribal drop of the Twelve, not a book forced in to fill the alphabet. Trust the caution that the raw count is a mnemonic and should not be over-pressed — that residue is real and matches the contested rating the Hebrew-sized lists carry elsewhere on this timeline. Reject the stronger dichotomy: numerology and canon-boundary are not rivals here but the same Hebrew list under a memory-frame, as Hilary's own "so that it might agree with the number of the letters" concedes.
