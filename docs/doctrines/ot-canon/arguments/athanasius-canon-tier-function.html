What divides Athanasius's "canon" and "read" tiers — function (evangelizing Jews vs. catechizing converts), not inspiration?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: plausible — the interpretation gets its premise right and one of its two halves cleanly. Athanasius's line between the canon proper and the books "appointed by the Fathers to be read… for instruction" is not a line between the inspired and the uninspired — he cites the lower-tier Wisdom, Sirach, and Tobit in his own voice as Scripture — so the distinction is one of function, and the proposed function of the read tier (catechesis for newcomers) is well-grounded, with a clean second-century parallel in Origen. The proposed function of the canon tier — that it is specifically the list usable for evangelizing Jews — is the weaker half: it reads a selection-criterion out of what is really a sufficiency-and-authority argument, and it fits the list imperfectly (Athanasius keeps Baruch, which no Jewish canon held, and drops Esther, which it ultimately retained). Coherent and partly grounded, but the canon-tier mechanism is a conjecture beyond what the letter states · the witness in full: Athanasius of Alexandria

Related arguments: Athanasius's three tiers — see also Did Athanasius reject the deuterocanon — is his Festal Letter 39 a witness for the Protestant canon?

This is the narrower companion to the "is Athanasius a witness for the Protestant canon?" question. That page weighs whether his list makes him a Protestant-canon witness and finds the claim contested; this page asks the next question down — granting the three tiers, why are the top two split? — and weighs the interpretation that the dividing line is function, not inspiration.

The claim

Athanasius's thirty-ninth Festal Letter (367) sorts the Old-Testament books into three ranks: the canon proper (the twenty-two Hebrew-reckoned books), a middle tier of books "not… included in the Canon, but appointed by the Fathers to be read by those who newly join us, and who wish for instruction in the word of godliness" (Wisdom, Sirach, Esther, Judith, Tobit, the Teaching of the Apostles, the Shepherd), and a third tier of "apocryphal writings… an invention of heretics." The interpretation weighed here takes the modern two-bucket scheme (canonical-inspired vs. apocryphal-rejected) off the table at the outset — Athanasius cannot be splitting his top two tiers by inspiration, since he elsewhere quotes three of the middle-tier books in his own voice as Scripture to settle doctrine. So the split must be one of function, and the proposal names the function of each tier:

  • The canon = the books usable for evangelizing Jews. §6 calls the canonical books "fountains of salvation," in which "alone is proclaimed the doctrine of godliness," and grounds them in Jesus' disputes with unbelieving Jews — he "put to shame the Sadducees" ("You err, not knowing the Scriptures") and "reproved the Jews" ("Search the Scriptures… these are they that testify of Me"). On this reading the canon list is essentially the list the Jews themselves received — the books that carry weight in argument with Jews — which is why it tracks the Hebrew reckoning.
  • The "read" tier = catechesis for new Christians — formation in "the word of godliness," not polemic with outsiders, exactly as Athanasius's own words for the tier say.

The interpretation is the constructive flip-side of the Catholic answer to "did Athanasius reject the deuterocanon?": where that answer insists his exclusion is from a measured "canon" category and not from Scripture, this presses on to say what the category is for.1

Who advances it

The premise — that Athanasius's "canon" is a narrower, functional category than the modern "inspired Scripture," and that his middle "read" tier is catechetical instruction rather than the Protestant rejected-Apocrypha — is Gary Michuta's, argued across his two videos "Did Athanasius Reject the Deuterocanon?" and his books on the deuterocanon. The further step that names the canon tier specifically as the evangelism-of-Jews list is a reading drawn from the rhetoric of §6 itself, where Athanasius anchors the canon in Jesus' Scripture-disputes with Sadducees and Jews. It is a minority, apologetic construction, not the mainstream scholarly account of why Athanasius's canon is Hebrew-shaped — which credits the inherited learned tradition of the twenty-two-book enumeration (Melito, Origen, Cyril) rather than a fresh apologetic selection.1

The evidence for

The premise is secure: the line is not inspiration. The whole interpretation rests on a point that is independently established on Athanasius's witness page and conceded by his critics: he cites the freestanding middle-tier books in his own voice, under Scripture formulae, in doctrinal controversy addressed to bishops and an emperor. He grounds his account of idolatry's origin on the Wisdom of Solomon 14 ("Scripture taught us beforehand long ago, when it said…"), names and quotes Wisdom 9:2 with "it is written" among texts "divine Scripture recognises," chains Sirach 15:9 as "the words spoken by the Spirit," and sets Tobit 4:18 under "it is written." A man who quotes these books as inspired Scripture cannot be ranking them below the canon because they are uninspired. So whatever the canon/read line tracks, it is not the modern inspired/uninspired divide — and the search for a functional explanation is well-motivated.2

The catechesis reading of the "read" tier is more than coherent — it is what Athanasius says, and it has a clean precedent. The middle tier is defined in the letter itself by its function: the books are "appointed by the Fathers to be read by those who newly join us, and who wish for instruction in the word of godliness." That is a description of catechumen formation, not of a rejected residue. And a century and a half earlier Origen had described the same practice from the other side, in his Homily on Numbers, sorting the church's reading into "milk" for beginners and "solid food" for the mature:

"…the food some have in the Word of God is milk, that is, the clearer and simpler doctrine… And so, when some such reading from the divine books is recited, in which there seems to be nothing obscure, they gladly receive it, for example, the brief books of Esther, Judith, or even Tobit, or the precepts of the book of Wisdom. But if the book of Leviticus is read to him his mind immediately stumbles…"

Origen's "milk" books for beginners — Esther, Judith, Tobit, Wisdom — are very nearly Athanasius's "read for instruction" tier (the same set, minus Sirach). The proposal's catechesis half is therefore not a guess but a recovery of a documented churchly practice that runs straight into Athanasius's wording.3

The canon-tier half has real textual purchase, too. §6 does not anchor the canon in abstract inspiration but in its probative use against unbelief: the books by which the Lord "put to shame the Sadducees" and "reproved the Jews." And the canon really does track the Hebrew reckoning that Jews received, and that — on a striking point of fit — Athanasius's demotion of Esther matches: Esther's authority was genuinely unsettled among Jews. Melito of Sardis, reporting the books he "learned accurately" in the East c. 170, likewise omits Esther; and the rabbis were still disputing whether Esther "defiles the hands" (i.e. ranks as sacred Scripture) into the Amoraic period — Samuel (3rd c.) is reported as denying it. So Athanasius's filing Esther below the canon, far from a blunder, lines up with a book whose Jewish standing was contested in exactly the centuries before him.4

Adversarial assessment

What holds. Two things stand. First, the premise: the canon/read split is not a split by inspiration. That is decisively shown by Athanasius's own-voice citations and is the interpretation's strongest ground. Second, the catechesis reading of the read tier: it is the letter's own stated rationale ("read by those who newly join us… for instruction"), reinforced by Origen's milk-and-solid-food parallel naming the very same books. On the read tier the proposal is not merely plausible but probable.

What is overstated or imprecise. The canon-tier half — "the list selected for evangelizing Jews" — outruns the text at two joints.

  1. §6 argues sufficiency and authority, not a selection-criterion. "These are fountains of salvation… in these alone is proclaimed the doctrine of godliness. Let no man add to these, neither let him take ought from these" is a claim that the canon is complete and exclusive for the church's own doctrine — addressed to the Christian readers of a festal letter, not a memo on which books to deploy against Jews. The appeal to Jesus shaming the Sadducees and reproving the Jews functions to prove the books' authority and christological sufficiency ("you err, not knowing the Scriptures"; "they… testify of Me"), the way a Father proves any point from a Gospel scene. To read it as disclosing that Athanasius chose the twenty-two because Jews accept them is to convert an argument about the canon's authority into an argument about its provenance — a step the text invites but does not take. The simpler and mainstream account is that Athanasius inherited the Hebrew-shaped enumeration from the learned tradition (Melito, Origen, Cyril) and, like them, took it to be the right canon for the church as such, not a tactical Jewish-apologetics kit.
  2. The list fits the "Jewish list" reading imperfectly, in both directions. If the canon were simply "what the Jews receive," it should not contain Baruch and the Epistle of Jeremiah — books no Jewish canon ever held — yet Athanasius keeps both, folded inside his one book of Jeremiah (as Origen and Cyril had). Their presence reflects the Greek manuscript tradition he received, not a judgment that they persuade Jews; which is just to say his canon is the inherited Hebrew enumeration with its Greek Jeremiah-appendices riding along, not a freshly curated list of Jewish-accepted books. And the Esther fit, impressive at first, cuts the other way on inspection: the settled Jewish canon ultimately retained Esther, so "the list the Jews accepted" is a moving target — the reading matches a contested moment, not the rabbinic table the argument elsewhere wants Athanasius to be tracking. The demotion of Esther is better explained by the same Hebrew-canon-conscious hesitation visible in Melito and Gregory of Nazianzus than by a deliberate evangelism-filter.

Neither objection touches the premise or the catechesis half; both bear only on the specific claim that the canon tier was selected for Jewish evangelism. That claim is coherent and not ruled out — §6's rhetoric genuinely leans toward disputes with Jews, and the Esther datum genuinely fits — but it is a conjecture about Athanasius's motive that the letter neither states nor needs.

Assessment

Plausible. The interpretation's foundation is sound and its account of the read tier is probable: Athanasius does not divide his top two ranks by inspiration (he cites the lower books as Scripture in his own voice), and the middle tier is, by his own words and by Origen's parallel, catechetical instruction for newcomers — emphatically not the Protestant rejected-Apocrypha. Trust that much fully. The distinctive further claim — that the canon tier is specifically the list curated for evangelizing Jews — is the part to hold loosely: it reads a selection-motive out of an §6 passage that is really arguing the canon's sufficiency and authority, and it fits the list only roughly, since Athanasius keeps Baruch (un-Jewish) and drops Esther (which the Jewish canon kept). The better-attested explanation of the canon's Hebrew shape is the inherited enumeration tradition, taken as the right canon for the church, not a tactical apologetic selection. So: the line is function, not inspiration — secure; the read tier is catechesis — probable; the canon tier is "for evangelizing Jews" — a plausible conjecture beyond the evidence, not the letter's own claim.
