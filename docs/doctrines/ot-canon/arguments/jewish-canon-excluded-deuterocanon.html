Did the Jews reject the deuterocanon — was the first-century Jewish canon closed and Hebrew-only?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — for the headline historical claim as stated, that a settled, closed, Hebrew-only Jewish canon already stood in the apostolic age and deliberately excluded the deuterocanon, so the church should follow it. An argument page weighs a single sub-claim adduced in support of a main interpretation — a proof-text, a parallel, a historical thesis — and renders an adversarial assessment of the interpretation it serves. Unlike a person-detail page it sits across the timeline rather than at one date. Here the sub-claim is the headline deuterocanon-removal argument: that the Jewish people, to whom the Old Testament was entrusted, recognized a closed, Hebrew-only canon that rejected the deuterocanon, so the church should exclude it too. Every factual claim is sourced; the proponents' own words are quoted verbatim and weighed on their best available case.

The claim

This is the umbrella under which a whole family of canon arguments shelters — the single most common popular case for the Protestant Old Testament. It is a syllogism: God committed His oracles to the Jews (Romans 3:2); the Jews recognized a canon that excludes the deuterocanon; therefore the church should follow the Jewish canon and exclude those books. Its scholarly form is F. F. Bruce's:

The books of the Apocrypha were not included in the Hebrew Bible, nor were they regarded as canonical by the leaders of official Judaism anywhere. Even Jews who wrote in Greek at the beginning of our era, like Philo and Josephus, recognized only the Canon of the Hebrew Bible, although they used the "Septuagint" translation. The books of the Apocrypha … first received canonical recognition from Greek-speaking Christians.

— F. F. Bruce, "The Canon of Scripture," Inter-Varsity (Autumn 1954).1 The popular forms reduce it to a slogan — "the Jews themselves rejected the Apocrypha" — and lean on the Romans 3:2 prong to make the rejection binding on the church.

Who advances it

The proponents. The argument carries a large modern-apologist roster. In its scholarly dress it is F. F. Bruce's (above). Popularly: CARM (Matt Slick) — "Many ancient Jews rejected the Apocrypha as Scripture … the 'oracles of God' were given to the Jews (Rom. 3:2), and they rejected the Old Testament Apocrypha"; Allen Parr — Protestants reject the Apocrypha "because the Jews themselves rejected the Apocrypha and did not consider these books as part of their Old Testament canon"; Mike Winger — "the oracles of God were committed to the Jews, and if the Jews say that wasn't one of the oracles, then I should take that word"; James White — "these books … were not accepted as canonical scripture by the Jews, to whom the oracles of God had been committed"; GotQuestions — "the nation of Israel … never accepted them as true books of the Hebrew Bible"; and Focus on the Family — "the Old Testament was already accepted and codified in the books accepted by the Jewish people as divinely inspired."2 The argument splits into prongs that each have their own home: the Romans 3:2 major premise is weighed on the oracles-of-God page; the props the proponents marshal for the minor premise — Josephus's tidy count of twenty-two books, Philo's never quoting the deuterocanon, the belief that prophecy had ceased after Malachi, and the supposed canon-closing "Council of Jamnia" — are each weighed separately. This page tests the umbrella itself: whether the minor premise is true — whether "the Jews" of the apostolic age held a closed, Hebrew-only canon from which these books had been rejected.

The adversaries. Catholic, Orthodox, and a wide swath of mainstream canon scholarship answer that the syllogism projects a later settlement backward: there was no single, fixed Jewish canon in the first century to reject anything from; "the Jews" were a plurality of communities with overlapping but not identical Scriptures; and the Hebrew-only table is a post-70, rabbinic development, fixed after — and partly in reaction to — the church's use of the Greek Bible. The pivotal study is Albert Sundberg's 1964 Harvard dissertation, which is double-edged: it demolished the old "Alexandrian canon" hypothesis the deuterocanon's defenders had relied on, but its positive thesis cuts the other way — that no Jewish canon, Palestinian or Alexandrian, was yet closed in the first century.3

The evidence for the affirmative reading

A real Jewish stream did keep a shorter, Hebrew-measured Scripture. The argument is not built on nothing. Around the close of the first century, Josephus tells a Roman audience that the Jews hold "only twenty-two books," divinely given and unaltered, and treats the post-Artaxerxes writings as a lower order — a Hebrew-sized reckoning that does not include the deuterocanon as such (Against Apion 1.37–43). The deuterocanonical books are, in fact, absent from the Hebrew Bible that the Pharisaic-rabbinic tradition transmitted; that tradition became normative Judaism, and its canon — the later Tanakh — excludes them. Philo of Alexandria, writing in Greek, quotes the Pentateuch constantly and never cites a deuterocanonical book as Scripture. And the rabbis articulate a belief that prophecy had ceased in Israel after Haggai, Zechariah, and Malachi — a frame on which writings of the intertestamental centuries (Maccabees, Sirach, Wisdom) would fall outside the age of inspiration. Read together, these strands show that a genuine, ancient, Hebrew-measured Jewish reckoning of Scripture existed, and that it is the lineal ancestor of the rabbinic canon the Reformers later invoked.4 That much is sound, and it is the real strength the argument trades on — and it is exactly the Hebrew-measure reading that this timeline treats as a serious, live position from Jerome onward.

Adversarial assessment

What holds. A Hebrew-only Jewish canon is no fiction — it is the canon of the rabbinic tradition that became normative Judaism, and Josephus and Philo do attest a stream that measured Scripture by a shorter, Hebrew table and never reckoned the deuterocanon among the inspired books. The deuterocanon really is absent from the Tanakh. And the deeper interpretation the argument serves — that the Christian Old Testament should be measured by the Hebrew — has the best of ancient company in Jerome's Hebraica veritas.

Where the claim, as stated, gives way. The syllogism needs more than "a Hebrew-only canon existed eventually." It needs a settled, closed, Hebrew-only Jewish canon already in force in the apostolic age, uniform across "the Jews," from which the deuterocanon had been deliberately rejected. On that historical claim it fails — on four counts.

  1. There was no fixed, closed Jewish canon in the first century. The leading canon scholarship denies the premise the argument rests on. Sundberg's study dismantled the notion of a settled "Alexandrian canon" and argued the Palestinian canon too remained open — the Law and the Prophets fixed, the Writings still fluid — with closure a later, rabbinic development. Timothy Lim states the consensus flatly: "throughout the post-exilic period up to around 100 CE there was not one official 'canon' accepted by all Jews," but a plurality of authoritative collections held by Pharisees, Sadducees, Essenes, the Qumran community, Samaritans, and the Greek-speaking Diaspora. Lee McDonald reaches the same verdict from the range of scriptural books at Qumran, and turns the silence back on the closed-canon position: the evidence "simply is not there to make the jump backward to the first century to argue for a fully closed canon at that early date." "The Jews rejected it" presupposes precisely the fixed table the period's historians say did not yet exist.5

  2. The edges of the canon were still disputed inside Judaism into the second century — and not only at the deuterocanon. The Mishnah preserves live rabbinic disagreement over whether Ecclesiastes and the Song of Songs "defile the hands" — i.e. are sacred Scripture (Yadayim 3:5) — and the disputes reach Esther and Ezekiel too; Rabbi Akiba has to insist on the Song's holiness against contemporaries who doubted it. A community still arguing over whether its own protocanonical books are Scripture had not closed its canon; "rejected the deuterocanon" imposes a decided edge on a table whose edge was demonstrably not yet decided. The same sources cut the other way on the disputed books: the Talmud cites Sirach among the Writings, and Epiphanius reports Jews of his own day still disputing the standing of Wisdom and Sirach — the rabbinic exclusion was a process, not a first-century fact already in hand.6

  3. "The Jews" were not one community with one list — and some Jews used the deuterocanon as Scripture. The argument trades on a monolithic "official Judaism" that the evidence does not supply. The Hebrew- and Aramaic-reading Jewish library at Qumran kept Tobit (in Aramaic and Hebrew, 4Q196–200), Ben Sira / Sirach (2Q18; also at Masada), and the Epistle of Jeremiah (7Q2) — deuterocanonical books copied and treasured by a Palestinian Jewish community a century and more before the rabbinic table hardened. The Greek-speaking Diaspora read the Septuagint, in whose world these books lived and were used. To speak of "the Jewish canon" in the apostolic age is to collapse Pharisees, Sadducees, Essenes, the Qumran sect, and Hellenistic Jews into a single verdict none of them had yet jointly rendered.7

  4. The Hebrew-only canon hardened after Christ — and deferring to it begs the question Augustine answered. The fixed rabbinic table is a post-70 development of the Pharisaic stream that became normative Judaism, given its definitive medieval form in the Masoretic Text; the canon-closing "Council of Jamnia" once invoked to date it to c. 90 AD is a discarded hypothesis. So the canon to which the argument defers was settled after, and partly against, the church's use of the Greek Scriptures. For a Christian to fix the Old Testament by that later ruling is to hand the decision to the synagogue that had, on the church's own reckoning, rejected the Messiah — the very move Augustine turned aside when he granted that the Maccabees stand outside the Hebrew table yet "are held as canonical, not by the Jews, but by the Church." That this is the umbrella's deepest weakness is the same point made narrowly against the Romans 3:2 prong.8

Proponent overreach to flag. Bruce's "regarded as canonical by the leaders of official Judaism anywhere" smuggles in the very "official Judaism" with a fixed canon that the first-century evidence withholds — and Bruce himself, on the Jamnia question, judged it "probably unwise to talk as if there were a Council or Synod of Jamnia," conceding the canon was not closed by any datable Jewish act. The flat popular slogans — "the Jews rejected the Apocrypha," "the nation of Israel never accepted them," "already accepted and codified" — assert as a single past event what was in fact a long, uneven, post-Christian settlement. These framings are flagged on the proponents' accounts; the defensible residue is the narrower Hebrew-measure point, not the headline.9

Assessment

Weak — for the headline claim as stated. The minor premise of the most common deuterocanon-removal syllogism — that a closed, Hebrew-only Jewish canon already stood in the apostolic age and deliberately excluded these books — does not hold on its best case: the leading scholarship (Sundberg, Lim, McDonald, Barton) holds that no Jewish canon was yet fixed in the early first century; the Writings were still disputed inside Judaism into the second (the Mishnah's quarrels over Ecclesiastes and the Song); "the Jews" were a plurality of communities, some of whom — the Hebrew-reading sectarians of Qumran among them — kept Tobit and Sirach; and the Hebrew-only table is a post-70 rabbinic settlement, fixed after and partly against the church's Greek Bible, so deferring to it begs the question Augustine answered ("not by the Jews, but by the Church"). What survives is the narrower, true point the umbrella overshoots: a major Jewish stream — Josephus, and the Pharisaic-rabbinic tradition that became normative — measured Scripture by a shorter, Hebrew table and never reckoned the deuterocanon among the inspired books. That is a real contribution to the books' graded standing, and it is the Hebrew-measure reading set against Augustine's reception-criterion in the genuinely contested dispute this timeline traces from Jerome to Trent — weighed on the Romans 3:2 and Jerome pages. The flat "the Jews rejected it," asserting a settled first-century verdict, is the part to drop.
