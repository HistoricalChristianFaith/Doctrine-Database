Do the Apostolic Fathers show the deuterocanon used "commensurate with Scripture"? — Brabban's dissertation vs. Beckwith's survey

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline; empirical) · Assessment: contested — the modest reading holds and the strong one overshoots. The earliest post-apostolic writers really do draw on several disputed books as authoritative, edifying, sometimes prophetic material — Clement of Rome weaving the Wisdom of Solomon into authoritative argument and holding up "the blessed Judith," Polycarp pressing Tobit's "alms delivers from death" — so the "these books were added later" story is false at its root, and the dissertation Gary Michuta cites (R. J. Brabban's exhaustive study of the Apostolic Fathers) documents that usage competently. But the further claim it is enlisted for — that the Apostolic Fathers used the deuterocanon identically to canonical Scripture and never treated it as less, so their practice evidences the Catholic canon — outruns the evidence at two joints. Brabban's own conclusion overshoots the Catholic canon: he finds the Fathers' working "canon" to have included not only Wisdom, Sirach, Judith, and Tobit but 4 Esdras, 1 Enoch, and an expanded Jeremiah — books Rome does not canonize — so the finding attests a broad, fluid, Septuagint-plus body of edifying writings, not the fixed forty-six-book Old Testament of Trent. And "never treated as less than Scripture" trades on an argument from silence: the Apostolic Fathers tiered nothing, protocanon or deutero, because the grading vocabulary is a later development, so the absence of a demotion in a period that demoted nothing proves little. Roger Beckwith's counter-survey (pp. 387–389) reads the same earliest stratum as thinner still. Sound against the "accretion" thesis; overstated as proof the Apostolic Fathers held the Catholic canon · advanced by Gary Michuta (on Brabban)

See also: this is the earliest-stratum, scholarly-source form of the usage question weighed across this doctrine. It is the Apostolic-Fathers slice of "did the early church reject the deuterocanon?" (contested), and it turns on the same methodological hinge as "does quoting a book 'as Scripture' prove a Father held it canonical?" (plausible) — where the Beckwith pp. 388–389 tally and Michuta's "impossible to list them all" rejoinder are also weighed. The converse case that the apostles' own Greek Bible makes these books their Scripture is "was the apostles' Bible the Septuagint?" (contested).

The sub-claim here is narrow and empirical: what does the earliest Christian evidence — the Apostolic Fathers, c. 96–150, a lifetime before the first Christian canon-list — actually show about the standing of the disputed books? Two named scholars are set against each other: R. J. Brabban, whose dissertation Michuta reads as proving the books were used "identically" to Scripture, and Roger Beckwith, whose survey White reads as showing the usage was mostly historical and the "it is written" formula a minority case.

The claim

So, what do we find in the Apostolic Fathers? I'd like to refer you to a doctoral dissertation written by R. J. Brabban, who, after an exhaustive study of the Apostolic Fathers, concludes that the Deuterocanonical books: "Were indeed known and used in a manner identical to those books now recognized as canonical or Scripture." … Brabban further concludes that the Deuterocanonical books are never refuted by the Apostolic Fathers, are never reduced to inferior sources, are never dismissed as inaccurate, are never treated as less than Scripture.

— Gary Michuta, opening statement in The Great Debate IX: Is the Apocrypha Scripture? (Michuta–White, 2014), video.1 The assertion is that a thorough, book-by-book study of the earliest post-apostolic writers shows the deuterocanon functioning as Scripture without qualification — and that this is the empirical backbone of the wider Catholic thesis that, until Jerome, "every early Christian writer who used the Deuterocanonical books did so in a manner commensurate with sacred Scripture."

Who advances it

The proponent. Gary Michuta rests the Apostolic-Fathers leg of his case on Ralph J. Brabban II, The Use of the Apocrypha and Pseudepigrapha in the Writings of the Apostolic Fathers — a doctoral dissertation submitted to the faculty of Baylor University (Waco, Texas; UMI Dissertation Information Service, 1984). In Why Catholic Bibles Are Bigger Michuta quotes Brabban's own summary conclusion: after "a very thorough study of the sources of the Apostolic Fathers," their "'canon' must have included Wisdom, Sirach, Judith, Esther (expanded version), Tobit, 4 Esdras, (1) Enoch, an expanded Jeremiah, and perhaps others as well."2 (In the debate Michuta reports the same author's surname as "Brabban" in one place and "Brendon" in another; the correct name is Ralph J. Brabban, later a professor of Bible at Chowan University.)3

The adversary. James White answers with Roger Beckwith, The Old Testament Canon of the New Testament Church (1985). Auditing the second-century evidence (Beckwith, pp. 388–389), White presses that a number of the early writers used the disputed books "only for historical means, not for scriptural means," and that those who applied the strict "it is written" citation formula to them "were actually in the minority at that point, not the majority" — while conceding that some, using the Septuagint, did hold them canonical: "no one's ever denied that."4

The evidence for

The core datum is real and this timeline records it independently of Brabban. In the earliest post-apostolic writing, Clement of Rome (c. 96) fuses two verses of the Wisdom of Solomon (12:12; 11:21) into authoritative argument in the same breath as Matthew and the Psalms, with nothing marking the Wisdom material off as lesser, and holds up "the blessed Judith" beside Esther as a pattern of faith. A generation later Polycarp of Smyrna (c. 125) urges almsgiving with Tobit's own maxim — "alms delivers from death" (Tobit 4:10; 12:9) — braided indistinguishably into a chain of phrases from 1 Peter and Isaiah. Michuta adds Barnabas 6:7, where the "let us bind the just one, for he is troublesome to us" testimony is read as a prophecy of Christ's sufferings drawn from Wisdom 2:12 (though this one is contested: Westcott, Beckwith, and others take the source to be Isaiah 3:9–10, and even Michuta's own apparatus grants the relationship is "disputed"). Softer echoes gather around these — the Didache's line of Sirach 4:31 inside its "Two Ways" ethics, a possible 2 Clement allusion to Tobit, the Shepherd of Hermas's apparent use of 2 Maccabees 7:28 on creation from nothing. Brabban's dissertation is a genuine, competent, book-by-book catalogue of exactly this material, and its central negative finding is fair: the Apostolic Fathers nowhere pause to impugn or segregate the disputed books.5

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The dissertation delivers its modest and important conclusion: the deuterocanon was not smuggled into the church late. From the first generation the Apostolic Fathers drew on Wisdom, Judith, Tobit, and Sirach as authoritative, edifying, sometimes prophetic material, woven undifferentiated into apostolic and prophetic counsel. That is a real corrective to any "these books were added at Hippo or Trent" story, and it is the same "using" stream this timeline traces from Clement of Rome forward. Brabban is a real source, honestly represented on that point, and the claim that no Apostolic Father is on record demoting these books is true.

What is overstated or imprecise. The strong form — "used identically to canonical Scripture," "never treated as less than Scripture," proving the Fathers held the Catholic canon — goes past what the evidence, and Brabban's own conclusion, will bear.

  1. Brabban's finding overshoots the Catholic canon, so it cannot prove it. The very sentence Michuta quotes has Brabban concluding that the Apostolic Fathers' working "canon" included not only the Tridentine deuterocanon but 4 Esdras (2 Esdras), 1 Enoch, and an expanded Jeremiah — books the Roman Church does not receive. If the Fathers' undifferentiated use of a book proves they held it Scripture "identical" to the canonical, it proves it for 1 Enoch and 4 Esdras as much as for Tobit — and Michuta must himself disown that conclusion. The honest reading of Brabban's data is therefore the one it actually supports: the earliest church drew on a broad, fluid, Septuagint-plus body of edifying writings whose edges were not yet drawn, not the fixed forty-six-book Old Testament defined fifteen centuries later. Read strictly, the dissertation evidences an open boundary, which cuts against a settled apostolic canon of either size.

  2. "Never treated as less than Scripture" is an argument from silence. The Apostolic Fathers tiered nothing — they drew up no canon-lists and applied no grading vocabulary to any book, protocanon or deutero — because the apparatus of ranking ("canonical" versus "ecclesiastical," "read but not for confirming doctrine") is a third- and fourth-century development, seen first in Origen, Athanasius, and Rufinus. That the earliest Fathers never demoted the disputed books is thus nearly vacuous: in that period nobody demoted anything, and no book was yet being asked the canon question. Absence of a qualification, where qualifying was not yet the practice, is weak evidence of positive canonical equality — as this timeline notes of each such witness, they render "no canon-list," so "no verdict."

  3. Beckwith reads the same earliest stratum as thinner. Auditing the passages actually adduced for the deuterocanon's canonicity, Beckwith finds many are drawn from Greek additions to canonical books (not the disputed books proper), or are not quotations at all, or "give any indication that the book is regarded as Scripture." On his tally Justin Martyr and Theophilus of Antioch never cite the apocrypha; 3 and 4 Maccabees are never referred to by anyone; Judith and 1–2 Maccabees are "little used, and only as historical sources"; and "until the final years of the second century, there is only one isolated example of any of the books being treated as Scripture." That verdict is itself contestable — Beckwith writes as a shorter-canon partisan, some of his exclusions are strained, and his survey ranges over second-century writers generally rather than the Apostolic Fathers proper, so he and Brabban partly talk past each other on scope. But it is enough to deny the strong claim: on the best skeptical audit the earliest usage is patchy and often merely historical, not the uniform Scripture-grade citation "impossible to list."

  4. The source and the upgrade. Brabban is an unpublished 1984 Baylor doctoral dissertation (UMI) — a careful catalogue of allusions, not a peer-reviewed verdict on canonicity, and hard to consult. Michuta's move is to upgrade its descriptive result (the Fathers' working body of writings was broad and included the disputed books) into a canonical verdict (they used them "identically" to Scripture, evidencing the Catholic canon) — a step the data do not license, and one the dissertation's own inclusion of Enoch and 4 Esdras positively resists. The companion overstatement is the "it is written (gegraptai) … impossible to list them all" rhetoric: applied to the earliest period it inflates a formula that, on Beckwith's audit, marked the disputed books in only a minority of the earliest cases.

Assessment

Contested. Split the interpretation in two. The modest reading — that the Apostolic Fathers drew on several disputed books (Wisdom, Judith, Tobit, Sirach) as authoritative, edifying, sometimes prophetic material from the first generation, so their later use is no late accretion — is sound, and Brabban documents it. The strong reading Michuta builds on Brabban — that the Fathers used the deuterocanon identically to canonical Scripture and never treated it as less, evidencing the Catholic canon — does not hold: Brabban's own conclusion overshoots the Tridentine canon (1 Enoch, 4 Esdras, expanded Jeremiah), attesting a fluid Septuagint-plus body rather than a fixed list; "never treated as less" is an argument from silence in a period that tiered nothing; and Beckwith's audit reads the same stratum as thinner and largely historical. The Apostolic-Fathers evidence thus cuts genuinely both ways — decisive against "added later," inconclusive for "the Catholic canon" — with no clear winner. Flag on Michuta's framing the two overstatements: the upgrade of Brabban's descriptive breadth into a canonical verdict (and the silent dropping of the very Enoch and 4 Esdras that same finding includes), and the "impossible to list them all" gloss on the earliest gegraptai usage.
