Does the rabbinic ban on the “outside books” and the Gospels confirm the deuterocanon was in scriptural use?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — for the strong conclusion the argument is enlisted to carry. The inference is Gary Michuta's: because Rabbi Akiva's circle had to ban the “outside books” and the Gospels, those books “must” have been in live use as Scripture — “you don't ban what nobody treats as authoritative” — so the ban retroactively attests the deuterocanon's scriptural standing in the apostolic milieu, and the shorter Jewish canon is no neutral witness. As a proof that the books were read and known, and read as Scripture by some (Christians, and some Jews), the inference is sound and modest. As a proof of what the strong form needs — that the deuterocanon held canonical standing among Jews and was then strategically excised — it does not hold: a ban attests use, not canonicity; a Hebrew-sized short list without the deuterocanon is attested by Josephus and 4 Ezra before the ban, so nothing canonical was there to lose; the ban's referent is itself disputed (the Talmud glosses the “external books” as sifrei minim, heretical/Christian writings); and the inference proves too much — by the same logic the ban on the Gospels would prove the Gospels were once Jewish Scripture. The sound residue is narrow; the apologetic payload is not delivered

The claim

The argument is an inference from a prohibition. Around the Bar Kokhba generation (c. AD 130) the early rabbis ruled that certain books “do not defile the hands” — i.e. are not sacred Scripture — and that one who reads them “has no share in the World-to-Come.” The Tosefta Yadayim 2:13 bars, in a single breath, “the scrolls of the heretics” (the gilyonim, widely read as a pun on euangelion, the Gospels) and “the scrolls of Ben Sira and all scrolls written from that point forward.” Michuta's move is to run the prohibition backward into evidence of practice:

“…it must have been common knowledge even at this early date that the Christians accepted the Deuterocanon and used it as Scripture (along with the Gospels), otherwise, there would have been no need to rule against them; secondly that at least some Jews must also have shared that acceptance, otherwise Akiba's decree would have been superfluous.”

— Gary Michuta, Why Catholic Bibles Are Bigger (2007), pp. 68–70.1 Put as a slogan: you don't ban what nobody treats as authoritative; the very need for the prohibition confirms the books were being read as Scripture, and by Jews as well as Christians. The interpretation this serves is apologetic and pointed at the deuterocanon: if the rabbis had to exclude Sirach and its kind, then those books enjoyed a scriptural standing in the apostolic milieu that the later, shorter Jewish canon then stripped away — so that canon is not a neutral witness to an “original” table from which the deuterocanon was always absent, but a late boundary drawn against books already in use.

Who advances it

The inference is Gary Michuta's signature move in Why Catholic Bibles Are Bigger and in his 2004 debate with James White, where he presses it as a rhetorical question: “If there was unanimous Jewish acceptance that the Deuterocanonical books were never Scripture and that Christians did not accept these books as Scripture — remember, this is around 132 AD — why in the world would Rabbi Akiba make a declaration to forbid these books or exclude them from inspiration?” It is a stock Catholic/Orthodox rejoinder to the Protestant appeal to “the Jewish canon,” and it leans on the same nineteenth-century reading (Graetz, the 1906 Jewish Encyclopedia) that dates the exclusion to Akiva's circle — the Jewish Encyclopedia itself noting that the decree “excluded the Gospels and other heretical (Christian) scriptures” alongside Ben Sira.2

The standing reply is James White's, in the same debate: a decree lists books “because there were those who believed or made the claim that they were canonical — there's no question about that,” but “it doesn't follow… that even this decree had any kind of binding force,” nor that it means “anything relevant to the Apocryphal books being considered canonical by any Jewish individuals whatsoever.” That is the crux: everyone grants the ban implies use; the dispute is whether it implies canonicity, and whose.3

The evidence for

Prohibitions do imply a practice worth prohibiting — and here the practice is partly documented. The core logic is sound: rabbis do not trouble to legislate against books nobody is reading. The Tosefta's cutoff — “Ben Sira and all scrolls written from that point forward” — is drawn at exactly the moment (Ben Sira, early second century BC) beyond which the Greek “extras” and the Christian writings both fall, and it names them in one breath with the gilyonim; the rabbis were plainly reacting to texts in live circulation among Jews and Christians, not to a dead letter. And the deuterocanon half of the inference has independent corroboration that some Jews did treat these books as Scripture: the Talmud itself, having ruled Ben Sira out, goes on quoting it as Scripture — among the Writings with the canonical formula “as it is written” (b. Bava Kamma 92b, citing Sirach 13:15–17), and again at b. Eruvin 65a, where Rashi, unable to find the quoted line anywhere in the Hebrew Scriptures, traces it to “the book of Ben Sira.” The ban presupposes, and the later citations confirm, that Ben Sira was being handled as Scripture by some within Judaism. To that narrow extent the inference lands: the deuterocanon was not merely known but, for some, read as Scripture.4

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The ban attests use. That the deuterocanon (and the Gospels) were circulating and being read as authoritative by some — certainly by Christians, and by at least some Jews — is a fair reading of a prohibition aimed at them, and the Talmud's own continued citation of Ben Sira as Scripture corroborates it for that book. Michuta is right that a decree against a set of books is not consistent with those books being unknown or universally disregarded.

Where the strong conclusion fails. The apologetic payload is not “the books were used” but “the books held canonical standing among Jews, which the rabbis then excised, so the shorter canon is no neutral witness.” The step from the first to the second does not survive.

  1. A ban attests use, not canonicity — and its very existence marks the books as disputed, not settled. “Read,” “known,” “popular,” “treated as Scripture by some” are not the same as “held as inspired Scripture by the community.” That the rabbis had to rule against Ben Sira shows precisely that its standing was contested — a book whose scriptural status is being denied by the authorities is, by that fact, not the community's settled canon. The ban is evidence of a boundary being drawn, and a boundary is drawn across a live edge, not across a closed one.5

  2. A Hebrew-sized short list without the deuterocanon predates the ban — so there was no prior Jewish canonicity to strip. The strong inference needs the deuterocanon to have been in the Jewish canon before the rabbis “took it out.” But Josephus already counts “only twenty-two books” divinely accredited, with the post-Artaxerxes writings a lower order (Against Apion 1.37–43, c. AD 95), and 4 Ezra 14 knows twenty-four public books (c. AD 100) — both roughly contemporary with, or prior to, Akiva, and neither reckoning the deuterocanon among the inspired. A short, Hebrew-measured canon is visible in the record before the ban it is said to be reacting against, in the Pharisaic–rabbinic stream that became normative. If the books were never counted in that stream's canon, barring their authoritative use confirms an existing edge; it does not amputate a received one.6

  3. The ban's referent is itself disputed — it may target heretical/Christian books, not the Apocrypha as such. The Babylonian Talmud glosses Akiva's “external books” (sefarim ha-chitzonim) as the “books of the heretics” (sifrei minim) — on the standard reading, Christian writings — with Rav Yosef adding only that Ben Sira too is problematic (b. Sanhedrin 100b). So the primary target of Akiva's curse is arguably sectarian/Christian scriptures, and the deuterocanon's inclusion is a secondary, contested gloss. The inference that “the ban proves the deuterocanon's Jewish scriptural standing” rests on a referent the sources do not univocally supply.7

  4. The inference proves too much. The very decree that names Ben Sira names the gilyonim — the Gospels — in the same breath. If “the rabbis banned it, therefore it was Jewish Scripture” licenses the conclusion for Sirach, it licenses the same conclusion for the Gospels: the ban would prove the Gospels were once held as Jewish Scripture and then excised — which no one maintains. A rule of inference that yields an absurd result for one item on the list is unreliable for the others. What the ban shows uniformly is use worth prohibiting, not prior canonical standing; the apologist cannot take the strong reading for Ben Sira while declining it for the Gospels beside it.8

Proponent overreach to flag. Michuta's “must have been” and “otherwise Akiba's decree would have been superfluous” press a real but modest datum — the books were in use — into a claim about prior canonicity for Jews that the ban cannot bear. This is a less-probable inference, not a false statement: it is true that some Jews used Ben Sira as Scripture (the Talmud shows it), and Michuta is right against the flat “the Jews always and unanimously rejected these books.” What overshoots is the leap from “used by some / disputed” to “canonical, then excised,” and from there to “the shorter Jewish canon is no neutral witness” — a conclusion that requires a prior canonical standing the pre-ban short lists (Josephus, 4 Ezra) show the deuterocanon did not have in the normative stream. The honest residue is the one White and Michuta both actually grant: the ban attests that the books were read, and read as Scripture by some; it settles nothing about whose canon they belonged to.9

Assessment

Weak — for the strong conclusion the argument is built to carry. The inference from the rabbinic ban is sound at its base: prohibitions imply a practice worth prohibiting, and the deuterocanon (with the Gospels) was plainly in live circulation, read as Scripture by Christians and by at least some Jews — a use the Talmud's own continued citation of Ben Sira independently confirms. That narrow point is granted, and it does tell against the flat claim that “the Jews” uniformly and always disregarded these books. But the apologetic payload — that the ban retroactively attests the deuterocanon's canonical standing among Jews, so the shorter Jewish canon is a late excision rather than a neutral witness — does not follow. A ban marks a book as disputed, not as settled Scripture; a Hebrew-sized short list without the deuterocanon is attested by Josephus and 4 Ezra before the ban, so nothing canonical was there to be stripped; the ban's referent is disputed (the Talmud reads the “external books” as heretical/Christian writings); and the inference proves too much, since the same logic would make the Gospels former Jewish Scripture. So the ban confirms scriptural use, plausibly including scriptural use by some Jews — but it does not establish canonicity for Jews, and it is a weak prop for the strong conclusion while soundly supporting only the narrower one. What should be trusted: the books were used, and disputed, not universally ignored. What must be flagged on Michuta: the slide from “used / disputed” to “canonical, then excised.”
