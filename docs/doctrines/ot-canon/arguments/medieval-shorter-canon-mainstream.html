Do the "52 ecclesiastical writers" and the Glossa Ordinaria show the shorter Hebrew canon was the medieval Western mainstream up to Trent?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — the verdict turns on which form of the claim is weighed. The narrow, true form — that a continuous, learned, Hebrew-canon-conscious current ran through the medieval Latin West, from Jerome and Gregory the Great through Hugh of St. Victor, Nicholas of Lyra, and Cardinal Cajetan, tiering the disputed books below a canon measured by the Hebrew table — is sound, and conceded even by Catholic reference works (Bruce Metzger calls it a "continuous succession"; the 1913 Catholic Encyclopedia grants "hesitation … all through the Middle Ages"). The strong form the talking point actually presses — that "as many as 52 major ecclesiastical writers" plus the Glossa Ordinaria show the shorter canon was the medieval mainstream, the longer canon an eccentric minority overturned only at Trent — overstates the same evidence three ways: the "52" is an inflated, un-sourced tally; most of its names simply reproduce Jerome's Vulgate-preface formula rather than reaching an independent verdict (the "parroting" problem the opponent raised in the very debate); and the Glossa's "not in the canon" rubrics are Jerome's own prologues carried in the Bible's front matter — one witness transmitted, not a fresh medieval judgment — while the same church read the disputed books in worship throughout. Genuinely disputed: strong as reception-history (a real Hieronymian stream), weak as a headcount of independent rejecters · back to timeline

(weak), which weighs the same Hieronymian current as it bears on the Council of Trent; it turns on the same methodological questions gathered under reading the patristic witness — especially does quoting a book "as Scripture" prove a Father held it canonical? (plausible), what did the "ecclesiastical" (read-but-not-canonical) category mean? (sound), and does the Fathers' word "canon" mean the modern definition? (sound) — and it shares the divided-record finding of did the early church reject the deuterocanon? (contested), carried forward into the medieval West.

The sub-claim is a reception-history one, marshaled against the deuterocanon's canonicity: that the shorter (Hebrew/Hieronymian) canon was not an eccentric minority but the medieval Western mainstream — the standard scholarly position from Jerome to the eve of Trent — so Trent's 1546 close was the novelty and its longer canon the innovation.

The claim

The argument's sharpest popular form is James White's, pressed against Gary Michuta in The Great Debate IX: Is the Apocrypha Scripture? (2004). Trent's fathers, White argues, ignored an overwhelming prior consensus:

… surely they could not have been unaware of the long list of scholars down through the years who had rejected those books — some count as many as 52 major writers during that time period. Surely they knew that the Glossa Ordinaria introduced each book of the Apocrypha with the assertion that it was not a part of the canon.

— James White, The Great Debate IX, 2004. And again, gathering the strands into one crescendo:

… the 52 ecclesiastical writers — including Pope Gregory the Great — rejecting these books … the Glossa Ordinaria, … the standard commentary used in every single center of theological learning for hundreds of years — and everyone starts saying, "This is not in the canon. This is not in the canon. This is not in the canon."

Two props carry the weight: a tally ("as many as 52 major writers") and a textbook (the Glossa Ordinaria, the standard medieval glossed Bible). Together they are meant to show that the shorter canon was the settled learning of the medieval West, so that Trent's longer canon — not the Reformers' Hebrew one — is the late departure.1

Who advances it

White is the popularizer; behind him stands a documented Reformed apparatus. The medieval list is compiled and pressed hardest by William Webster (The Old Testament Canon and the Apocrypha, and with David T. King in Holy Scripture: The Ground and Pillar of Our Faith), whose essay "From Jerome to the Reformation" enumerates the Western and Eastern writers who followed Jerome's Hebrew canon and reproduces the Glossa's prefaces. The deeper root is the Anglican bishop John Cosin, whose Scholastical History of the Canon of the Holy Scripture (1657) tabulates ecclesiastical writers century by century as they distinguish the canonical from the apocryphal. The scholarly anchor both sides invoke is Bruce Metzger, who grants a "continuous succession" of learned Western theologians maintaining the Hebrew canon down to the Reformation. So the claim is not a bare apologetic flourish: it rests on a real, traceable current of medieval opinion — which is exactly why its narrow form must be conceded before its strong form is weighed.2

The evidence for the affirmative reading

The Hieronymian stream is real, continuous, and learned — this is the argument's true core. A line of the West's most respected teachers did keep Jerome's distinction alive across the whole millennium. On this timeline the current is embodied in Jerome himself (c. 390, the Prologus Galeatus setting the disputed books "among the apocrypha"), Rufinus (c. 404, the "Ecclesiastical" middle rank), and Gregory the Great (c. 600, calling 1 Maccabees a book "not… Canonical"). Beyond the timeline, Webster's roll runs on through the medieval schools: Bede, Alcuin, Haymo of Halberstadt, Hugh and Richard of St. Victor, Peter Comestor, Hugh of St. Cher, Nicholas of Lyra (whose Postilla was the dominant late-medieval commentary), William of Ockham, Antoninus of Florence, Dionysius the Carthusian, and — on the very eve of Trent — Cardinal Cajetan (1532) and Cardinal Ximénes' Complutensian Polyglot (1514–17), both restating Jerome's shorter canon. Metzger's summary is fair to this much: "a continuous succession of the more learned Fathers and theologians in the West maintained the distinctive and unique authority of the books of the Hebrew canon." And the Glossa Ordinaria — the standard glossed Bible read in every medieval school — did carry, as front matter to Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, and the Maccabees, prologues declaring them "not in the canon." Pressed to its strongest, the argument is not "no one in the Middle Ages received these books" but "the learned Western tradition kept ranking them below the Hebrew canon, right up to Trent." That pattern is genuine, and it is the medieval spine of the Reformation's case.3

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The narrow thesis is sound and should be conceded plainly: a continuous, respectable Hieronymian current did run through the medieval Latin West, and the disputed books really were tiered below the Hebrew canon by a long line of its most learned teachers. Even Catholic reference works grant it. The 1913 Catholic Encyclopedia concedes that "in the Latin Church, all through the Middle Ages we find evidence of hesitation about the character of the deuterocanonicals," with "a current… distinctly unfavourable to their authority and sacredness," and names Thomas Aquinas among the hesitant; and the New Catholic Encyclopedia (1967) grants that the church's own binding "decision was not given until rather late… at the Council of Trent." As a claim about that stream — and about the absence of a settled, undisputed medieval consensus for full canonicity before 1546 — the argument stands.4

What overstates. The strong conclusion — that this stream was the medieval mainstream, a count of independent authorities so lopsided that Trent's longer canon is the eccentric novelty — gives way at three joints.

  1. The "52" is an inflated, un-sourced tally. No primary text states the figure; "some count as many as 52" is White's own hedge, and the number cannot be traced to Webster, Cosin, or any survey in the form of a verified count — it is a round-up of a compiled list, not a datum. What the sources actually supply is a roll of roughly two dozen named writers, gathered precisely to make the current look like a consensus. Counting heads is the wrong test in any case, since the heads are not independent (next point) — but even at face value the tally is a rhetorical figure, not a measured one.4

  2. The list is not 52 independent witnesses; most simply repeat Jerome's preface. This is the decisive deflation, and it was pressed by the opponent in the very debate. Michuta answered White on the spot: "you have 50 major theological writers who denied the Deuterocanonical books. But what you find — and I'm the type who actually looks up all these 50 people — is that the vast majority of them will parrot Jerome's words, but in their own writings, they affirm the inspiration of the books." The point is not partisan: the Reformed side effectively concedes the lineage — "Yes, they follow Jerome. That is the argument. It's not a refutation of it." But a list that all descends from one preface is one opinion transmitted, not fifty-two verdicts reached; the medieval writers largely inherited Jerome's distinction with the Vulgate itself, whose front matter they were copying. The tally converts the wide circulation of a single source into a false show of independent consensus.5

  3. "Not in the canon" did not mean "not Scripture," and the same church read the books throughout. Many of the very writers on the list quote the disputed books as Scripture in their own theology — Jerome himself prefaces Sirach 22:6 "Holy scripture says" and Gregory the Great calls the Wisdom of Solomon what "the Truth says through His Scripture," even as each files the books below the Hebrew canon. Their "canon" is the narrower, technical rule from which doctrine is proved, not the modern blanket "inspired Scripture"; a book could be "not canonical" in that sense and still be read, cited, and honored (the distinction weighed on the "ecclesiastical category" and "canon"-word pages). And the wider medieval church never stopped using these books: the same 1913 Catholic Encyclopedia grants "there is no lack of evidence that during this long period the deuteros were read in the churches of Western Christendom," and they stood in every Vulgate, were enumerated by the ecumenical Council of Florence (1442), and shaped the West's liturgy. The situation was not shorter-canon dominance but a genuine dual canon — a learned scholarly reservation running alongside a broad liturgical and practical reception — which is why it took a council to close it.6

The Glossa is Jerome transmitted, not an independent verdict — and its dramatic prologue is a late printed accretion. The Glossa Ordinaria's "not in the canon" rubrics are the same phenomenon in miniature. The Glossa is not the work of one Walafrid Strabo (the old attribution, long discredited) but a twelfth-century compilation of the school of Anselm of Laon, and its Old-Testament book-prologues largely reproduce Jerome's prefaces. The famous line apologists quote — that the church reads Judith, Tobit, and the Maccabees "for the edification of the people, not for confirming the authority of ecclesiastical doctrines" — is verbatim Jerome, from his Prologue to the Books of Solomon, carried in the Bible's front matter. So the Catholic Encyclopedia concedes the fact while dissolving its force: the "Glossa Ordinaria… embodied the prefaces in which the Doctor of Bethlehem had written in terms derogatory to the deuteros, and thus perpetuated and diffused his unfavourable view." The Glossa is one more channel of Jerome's single voice, not a second witness — the same one-source-echoed problem as the "52." (A textual wrinkle sharpens the point: the long canon-prologue Webster reproduces is drawn from a late printed edition of c. 1498, not from the twelfth-century gloss or its 1480–81 editio princeps — so even the wording sometimes cited as "the Glossa's" is in part a later accretion.) The Glossa's real significance is what it says about diffusion, not about a count of minds: Jerome's distinction was ubiquitous because his prologues rode in every Latin Bible — which is a fact about the Vulgate's front matter, not about how many independent scholars weighed the canon and sided against the deuterocanon.7

What the medieval record really shows. Strip the "mainstream" framing away and an accurate residue remains, and it is the same shape as the patristic one: the medieval West held two canons at once. A learned Hieronymian current — genuine, continuous, and respectable — ranked the disputed books below the Hebrew table and kept saying so, largely in Jerome's own borrowed words; and the wider church read those same books in worship, copied them in every Vulgate, and professed them at Florence. That is not a mainstream against a fringe; it is an unsettled division that Trent finally closed. The evidence will support the modest thesis — a real, learned shorter-canon tradition persisted to 1546 — and will not support the strong one, that a lopsided count of independent authorities makes the longer canon the late departure.

Assessment

Contested. The medieval witness is genuinely divided, and the verdict turns on which form of the claim is weighed. As a claim about the Hieronymian stream — that a continuous, learned, Hebrew-canon-conscious current ran through the medieval Latin West, tiering the disputed books below the Hebrew canon from Jerome to Cajetan — the argument is sound, and it is conceded even by Catholic reference works (Metzger's "continuous succession"; the 1913 Catholic Encyclopedia's "hesitation… all through the Middle Ages"; the New Catholic Encyclopedia's "not… until… Trent"). As the strong headcount — that "52 ecclesiastical writers" plus the Glossa Ordinaria prove the shorter canon was the medieval mainstream and the longer one an eccentric novelty — it does not hold: the "52" is an inflated, un-sourced tally; most of its names reproduce Jerome's single Vulgate-preface formula rather than reaching independent verdicts (the "parroting" problem the opponent pressed in the debate, and the Reformed side effectively grants); "not in the canon" in their narrower sense was not "not Scripture," and the same church read the disputed books throughout and professed them at Florence; and the Glossa's rubrics are Jerome's own prologues carried in the Bible's front matter, one voice diffused rather than many minds counted. The honest finding is a dual canon — a learned scholarly reservation alongside a broad liturgical reception — not shorter-canon dominance: strong by reception-history's lights (the stream is real), weak as a census of independent rejecters; no clear winner. Flag on the proponents' framing the slide from "a continuous learned current kept Jerome's distinction" (true) to "52 independent writers and the Glossa rejected the books, so the shorter canon was mainstream" (overstated). The companion inference — that this makes Trent's 1546 definition the novelty — is weighed on the Trent-novelty page (weak).
