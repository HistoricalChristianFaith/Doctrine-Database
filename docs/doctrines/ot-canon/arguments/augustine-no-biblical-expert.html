Should Augustine's canon witness be discounted because he was "no biblical expert" and could not read Hebrew, unlike Jerome?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — for the interpretation it serves (that Augustine the non-Hebraist is outranked on the canon by Jerome the Hebraist, so where they disagree Jerome's "No" should win and the African councils' "Yes" should be discounted as Augustine-driven and "not universal"). The competence the argument invokes is real but beside the point: Augustine's canon ran not on Hebrew enumeration but on breadth of churchly reception (On Christian Doctrine II.8.12), a test for which Hebrew philology is not decisive — so "he couldn't read Hebrew" attacks a qualification his argument never needed. And the longer canon was no one man's product: it was in Western use before and beyond Augustine (Cyprian, the Cheltenham List, Ambrose, the Roman/Damasine list, Innocent I) and rests on the Septuagint the whole church read, so the councils ratified a reception already in hand. What survives is the true and serious residue — Jerome was the superior philologist, and his Hebrew-canon instinct is the live position this timeline traces — but the dispute is over the criterion, not the men's competence · advanced by CARM and James R. White

Related arguments: Augustine's canon witness — see also did Augustine accept the deuterocanon only because he mistakenly thought it stood in the Hebrew canon?

The claim

The Christian Apologetics & Research Ministry (CARM) grants that the disputed books were received by a string of Western councils, then discounts that reception in two moves — a comparative-competence move and a not-universal move:1

It is true that the Catholic Church accepted the Apocryphal books at earlier councils at Rome (A.D. 382), Hippo (A.D. 393), Carthage (A.D. 397), and Florence (A.D. 1442). However, these were not universal Church councils and the earlier councils were influenced heavily by Augustine, who was no Biblical expert, compared to the scholar Jerome, who rejected the Apocrypha as part of the Old Testament Canon.

— CARM, "Reasons why the Apocrypha does not belong in the Bible." James White presses the same competence point in debate, supplying the specific deficiency CARM leaves general: "Augustine could not read Hebrew."2 The structure of the argument is comparative and quasi-ad hominem: on the canon question two contemporaries disagree, and one of them — Jerome — was the age's foremost Hebrew scholar while the other read no Hebrew and "was no Biblical expert"; therefore the expert's verdict ("No" to the Apocrypha) should be preferred to the non-expert's, and the councils that enacted Augustine's "Yes" should be discounted both as his product and as merely regional ("not universal").

Who advances it

The comparative-competence move is CARM's in print; the sharpened "could not read Hebrew" form is James White's, pressed in his 2014 debate with Gary Michuta and woven through his canon writing.2 It is the companion of White's sibling charge that Augustine kept the disputed books only because he wrongly thought they stood in the Hebrew canon — weighed on its own page. Where that argument attacks Augustine's knowledge, this one attacks his standing: both reconstruct Augustine so that his "Yes" need not be reckoned a considered vote for the longer canon. The older confessional tradition behind both — distinguishing the senses in which Augustine called books "canonical" — is the line of William Whitaker and Archibald Alexander.

The evidence for

The premises that are true. Three planks under the argument are sound, and the assessment keeps them. (1) Jerome genuinely was the superior philologist — the one Latin Father who learned Hebrew, translated iuxta Hebraeos, and made the Hebrew the measure of his Old Testament. (2) Augustine genuinely could not read Hebrew; he worked from the Old Latin and the Greek, and deferred to Jerome's learning in matters of language, as their correspondence shows. (3) Augustine genuinely was the driving theological figure at the African synods of Hippo (393) and Carthage (397), whose canon-list matches his own in On Christian Doctrine book for book. So the comparison is not invented: on Hebrew philology Jerome outranked Augustine, and Augustine did stand behind the African lists. The Hebrew-canon instinct Jerome championed is the serious, live position this whole timeline traces — not a foil to be knocked down.

Adversarial assessment

What holds. Jerome was the better Hebraist; Augustine read no Hebrew; Augustine shaped the African councils. To that extent the argument's factual scaffolding stands, and the Hebrew-canon position it serves is a genuine contender, not a strawman.

Where it breaks (1): the canon question Augustine answered was never one of Hebrew philology. The argument assumes that competence on the canon means competence in Hebrew — that the better Hebraist is, by that fact, the better judge of the canon's edge. But that assumes the very thing in dispute. Augustine did not fix his canon by enumerating the Hebrew Bible; he fixed it by breadth of churchly reception. In On Christian Doctrine II.8.12 the rule is explicit: "prefer those that are received by all the catholic churches to those which some do not receive," weighing a book by how widely and anciently the churches use it.3 On a reception test, what settles a book is the testimony of the churches, not the contents of the Hebrew scroll — and reading Hebrew is not what qualifies one to weigh that testimony. Hebrew philology is decisive for Jerome's Hebraica veritas; it is beside the point of a criterion that asks what the churches received. So "Augustine could not read Hebrew" impeaches a qualification his argument never relied on — a genetic move that attacks the man's equipment for a task he was not performing.

Where it breaks (2): "no Biblical expert" is false, and trades on a smuggled definition. Augustine was one of the most formidable expositors of Scripture the church has produced — On Christian Doctrine is itself a manual of biblical interpretation, and his commentaries, sermons, and controversial works turn on the text page after page. To call him "no Biblical expert" is sustainable only by quietly redefining "Biblical expert" as "Hebraist," which is precisely the point at issue: it presupposes that the Hebrew table is the measure of the canon, the conclusion the argument is supposed to establish. Strip the smuggled definition and the charge dissolves — a man may be a master of Scripture without reading Hebrew, as the Greek Fathers who read no Hebrew were masters of it.

Where it breaks (3): the longer canon was not Augustine's personal product. The argument reduces a broad Western consensus to one influential man, so that discounting Augustine discounts the canon. The record will not bear the reduction. The deuterocanon was received in the West before Augustine was born and beyond his reach: Cyprian of Carthage (c. 253) cites the disputed books with the very formulae he reserves for Moses and the Gospel; the African Cheltenham List (c. 360) carries them; Ambrose of Milan — Augustine's own bishop, who baptized him — cites them as "Scripture" and "it is written"; the Roman Damasine list (382) and Innocent I (405) receive them from the see of Rome; and behind them all stands the Septuagint the whole church read in worship, which simply contained the books. Augustine's contribution was to state the reception-criterion the churches had long applied, not to invent the canon it yielded. The African councils, as their own record shows, ratified a reception already in hand — even deferring their list to Rome and "the Church beyond the sea" for confirmation in 419 — rather than legislating a new one; the canon would stand on this Western stream whether or not Augustine had ever weighed in.4

Where it breaks (4): "not universal councils" is half-true and double-edged. That Hippo and Carthage were regional African synods is true and worth conceding — they did not bind the whole church, and the deuterocanon's status remained genuinely disputed for a millennium after. But the point cuts less far than the argument needs. First, the comparison is uneven: Jerome convened no council at all, so the contrast is not council versus council but a regional conciliar consensus, backed by Rome, against one scholar's preface. Second, CARM's own list overreaches: the Council of Florence (1442), which it lumps with the regional synods as "not universal," was an ecumenical council, and its bull Cantate Domino is an ecumenical profession enumerating the full canon — so the "not universal" charge simply fails for the last item on its own list, and Trent (1546) would later define the canon ecumenically and under anathema. The regional character of Hippo and Carthage tells against treating them as the moment the canon was settled for all; it does nothing to show their verdict was wrong, or that it was Augustine's private opinion writ large.5

And Jerome is not the clean counter-witness the comparison needs. Even the expert whose authority the argument borrows did not simply "reject the Apocrypha" and walk away. Jerome translated Tobit and Judith into Latin (however grudgingly), quoted Wisdom and Sirach, and noted that the church read Judith — and the church's later judgment, East and West, went against his stricter line. More to the point, Jerome's "No" was itself the product of a criterion — the Hebrew measure he chose — not a neutral datum his Hebrew gave him. A perfectly competent Hebraist who weighed the canon by churchly reception, as Augustine did, would reach Augustine's result. So the men's relative skill in Hebrew does not adjudicate the dispute; the choice of standard does. That is the real fault line this timeline traces, and it is not closed by ranking the disputants.6

Assessment

Weak. The argument's factual core is sound and is kept: Jerome was the superior Hebraist, Augustine read no Hebrew, and the African councils were regional and Augustine-shaped. But the discounting conclusion built on it does not hold. It impeaches Augustine for a competence his canon-argument never invoked — his test was breadth of churchly reception, not Hebrew enumeration, a standard for which reading Hebrew is not decisive — and it sustains "no Biblical expert" only by redefining the phrase to mean "Hebraist," begging the very question at issue. It reduces a broad Western reception (Cyprian, the Cheltenham List, Ambrose, Rome, Innocent I, and the Septuagint behind them all) to one man's influence, when the councils ratified a canon already in hand. And its "not universal" rider, true of Hippo and Carthage, misfires on the ecumenical Florence it wrongly lumps with them. What is left is not a reason to discount Augustine's witness but the genuine, unresolved disagreement this timeline exists to trace — Augustine's reception-criterion against Jerome's Hebraica veritas — a dispute over the standard for the canon, in which the two men's relative command of Hebrew settles nothing, because a reception test does not run on Hebrew.
