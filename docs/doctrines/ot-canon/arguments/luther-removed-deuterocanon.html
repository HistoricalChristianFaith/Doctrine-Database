Did Martin Luther remove the deuterocanonical books from the Bible?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — for the bare "removed / cut out" framing. The headline Catholic-apologetic trope is that Martin Luther removed (or "took out," or "cut out") the seven Old-Testament deuterocanonical books from Scripture — the exact mirror of the Protestant "Trent added the books in 1546" slogan, the same dispute told from the opposite side. As a description of what Luther did it does not hold on its best case, and for the same reason its twin fails: it confuses demotion-and-relocation with excision. Luther did not delete the books. In his 1534 German Bible he gathered them out of the Old Testament proper, set them between the Testaments under the heading Apocrypha — "not held equal to the Holy Scriptures, and yet… profitable and good to read" — and wrote each a preface: kept in the Bible, demoted from canon, not removed from it. The books then stood in the Lutheran and wider Protestant Bibles — the King James included — for nearly three centuries; their actual disappearance from most Protestant Bibles is a nineteenth-century story (the British and Foreign Bible Society's 1826 decision), not Luther's act. An argument page weighs a single sub-claim adduced in support of a main interpretation — a proof-text, a parallel, a historical thesis — and renders an adversarial assessment of the interpretation it serves. Unlike a person-detail page it sits across the timeline rather than at one date. Here the sub-claim is a historical thesis about a single agent and act: that Martin Luther removed the deuterocanon from the Bible. The question of why the Reformers tiered the books down — the Hebrew canon, the proof-texts for purgatory and almsgiving — is weighed on its own pages (the 2 Maccabees, Tobit, self-disclaiming, and Hebrew-canon arguments); this page takes only the headline "removed / cut out versus demoted-and-relocated" question.

The claim

The trope is one of the most common in popular Catholic apologetics, the standing answer to the Protestant charge that Rome added books at Trent: it was not Rome that changed the Bible, the reply runs, but Luther who took the books out. Jimmy Akin's widely reprinted "Defending the Deuterocanonicals" states it plainly, and ties the removal directly to doctrine:

The deuterocanonicals teach Catholic doctrine, and for this reason they were taken out of the Old Testament by Martin Luther and placed in an appendix without page numbers.

And, on Tobit and 2 Maccabees specifically:

Why would Martin Luther cut out this book when it is so clearly held up as an example to us by the New Testament? Simple: A few chapters later it endorses the practice of praying for the dead… Since Luther chose to reject the historic Christian teaching of purgatory… he had to remove that book from the Bible and appendicize it.

— Jimmy Akin, "Defending the Deuterocanonicals."1 The operative words are taken out, cut out, remove. In its sharp popular form the claim is not that Luther demoted the books or relocated them but that he excised them — that the seven books were in the Bible and a Reformer took them out.

Who advances it

The "Luther removed them" line is the reflexive Catholic-apologetic counter to the Protestant "added at Trent" trope, and it circulates as widely. But its more careful Catholic proponents do not say Luther deleted the books — and one of them says so against his own side. Gary Michuta, a Catholic apologist who has written a whole book defending the deuterocanon (Why Catholic Bibles Are Bigger), pauses to correct the slogan his own camp repeats:

Catholic apologists sometimes claim that Martin Luther removed the deuterocanonical books from Scripture. This is not entirely true. Luther's German translation of the Scriptures included the deuterocanon.

— Gary Michuta, Why Catholic Bibles Are Bigger.2 Michuta's concession is the tell, and it is a fair witness precisely because it comes from inside the camp that profits by the slogan. Once "Luther's translation included the deuterocanon" is granted, the dispute is no longer about presence ("took them out") but about rank ("not held equal to the Holy Scriptures") and placement (gathered into their own section) — and those are different claims with different answers. As with the Trent-added trope, the careful form survives the evidence; the bare slogan does not.

The evidence for

There is a real change underneath the slogan, and it was Luther's doing. The trope is not spun from nothing. Luther did three things to the disputed books that genuinely altered how a Bible presented the canon, and a flat denial ("Luther changed nothing") would be as false as the slogan.

  1. He denied them canonical, doctrine-establishing status. When his opponent Johann Eck pressed 2 Maccabees 12 for purgatory at the Leipzig Disputation (1519), Luther answered — in print, in his 1521 Defense and Explanation of All the Articles — that the book carries no such weight: the passage "proves nothing, for that book is not among the books of Holy Scripture, and, as St. Jerome says, it is not found in a Hebrew version… this book deserves little authority." That is a real demotion of the book's rank, on the Hieronymian Hebrew-canon ground.3

  2. He physically segregated them. In the 1534 German Bible Luther lifted Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch, and 1–2 Maccabees out of their interleaved Vulgate places, set them together between the Old and New Testaments, and headed the section "Apocrypha — that is, books which are not held equal to the Holy Scriptures, and yet are profitable and good to read." The arrangement made Jerome's verbal two-tier ranking into the visible layout of a printed page, and it became the model for the vernacular Protestant Bibles that followed.4

  3. He attached prefaces marking the demotion. To Judith and Tobit he wrote prefaces reading them as edifying sacred poem and comedy rather than history — "a fine, good, holy, useful book," yet not the canonical Scripture by which doctrine is established — and he dropped 1–2 Esdras altogether.4

So a real act stands behind the trope: Luther demoted the deuterocanon from canon and set it apart. But that is a claim about a book's rank and place, not about taking it out of the Bible — and it is precisely the point the slogan's "removed… cut out… took out" language overshoots.

Adversarial assessment

What holds. Two things. First, Luther really did deny the deuterocanon canonical, doctrinal authority — explicitly, against Eck, on 2 Maccabees, and across his Bible's prefaces. Second, he really did physically segregate the books into a labelled inter-testamental section, a genuine change in how the printed canon was presented and the seed of the Reformed confessions' harder line. The soft form of the Catholic point — "Luther demoted the books from canon and set them apart" — is true, and is the substance the witness page documents.

What is overstated. The slogan slides from that true, modest point to a false, dramatic one — that Luther excised the books, that he "took them out" of the Bible. Three facts rule the dramatic form out.

  1. Luther kept the books in his Bible. The 1534 Luther Bible contains Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch, and 1–2 Maccabees — printed, bound, prefaced, and commended "to read." A book gathered into its own section of a Bible, with a heading explaining why it is there, has been relocated and ranked, not removed. The concession comes even from a Catholic defender of the deuterocanon: Michuta's "Luther's German translation… included the deuterocanon" is the same admission Allen Parr makes on the Protestant side about Trent ("these books were in the Bible"). The two careful proponents, from opposite camps, agree on the fact the two slogans both deny.5

  2. The books stood in the Protestant Bibles for nearly three centuries after Luther. The Apocrypha section was the rule, not the exception, in Protestant Bibles from Luther through the seventeenth century: the Swedish (1541), Danish (1550), Geneva, Bishops', and King James Bibles all carried it. Every King James Bible printed before 1666 included the Apocrypha between the Testaments, and in 1615 Archbishop George Abbot forbade, on pain of a year's imprisonment, the printing of any Bible without it. Whatever Luther did in 1534, the books he is said to have "removed" were still bound in the English Protestant Bible a century later.6

  3. The actual removal is a nineteenth-century story, and not Luther's. The disappearance of the Apocrypha from most Protestant Bibles dates to the "Apocrypha controversy" of the 1820s: in 1826 the British and Foreign Bible Society resolved that its funds be applied "to the printing and circulation of the Canonical Books of Scripture, to the exclusion of those Books and parts of Books usually termed Apocryphal," a decision driven as much by printing economy and Reformed-Puritan pressure as by doctrine; the American Bible Society followed in 1827. The sixty-six-book Protestant Bible most readers now hold is a product of 1826, not of Wittenberg in 1534 — so the agent the slogan names is off by some three hundred years. (Akin himself reaches for this nineteenth-century mechanism — the appendix "was dropped… in 1827 by the British and Foreign Bible Society" — but misdates it by a year and to the wrong society: the BFBS resolution was 1826; 1827 is the year the American Bible Society followed. The point survives the slip — the books left the common Protestant Bible in the 1820s, three centuries after Luther — but the precise agent and date are not the ones the slogan supplies.)7

What the dispute really turns on. Strip the "removed" framing away and the genuine question that remains is not whether Luther took the books out of the Bible — he did not — but whether his demotion of them from canonical, doctrine-establishing status was warranted: that is the Hebrew-canon question this timeline traces at length (Romans 3:2, the first-century Jewish canon, Jerome's reasons), together with the proof-text disputes the demotion was bound up with (2 Maccabees and Tobit). The bare "Luther removed the books" slogan collapses that real argument over rank into a false picture of a sixteenth-century excision — exactly as its mirror, "Trent added the books," collapses a dispute over rank into a false picture of a sixteenth-century insertion.

Assessment

Weak — for the "Luther removed (took out, cut out) the deuterocanon" framing. As a description of what happened the slogan does not hold: it confuses demoting and relocating a book with excising it. Luther's 1534 German Bible contains the deuterocanon — gathered into a separate inter-testamental section headed "Apocrypha… not held equal to the Holy Scriptures, and yet… profitable and good to read," each book given a preface and commended to read — so the books were demoted and set apart, not taken out; they then stood in the Lutheran and wider Protestant Bibles (the King James included, by Abbot's 1615 order on pain of imprisonment) for nearly three centuries, and their actual disappearance is the nineteenth-century work of the British and Foreign Bible Society's 1826 resolution, not Luther's act. Even a Catholic defender of the deuterocanon, Gary Michuta, corrects his own side: "Catholic apologists sometimes claim that Martin Luther removed the deuterocanonical books from Scripture. This is not entirely true." What survives, and is sound, is the narrower and quite different claim — that Luther denied the books canonical, doctrine-establishing status and physically segregated them, a real change in how a printed Bible presented the canon — but that is demotion, not removal. Flag on the proponents' framing (Jimmy Akin's "taken out… cut out… remove that book from the Bible") the slide from "demoted from canon and set apart" (true) to "removed / cut out of the Bible" (false). The argument is the exact mirror of the Protestant "Trent added the books" trope, and over-reads in the identical way.
