Was the deuterocanon's removal doctrinally motivated — proof the books carry real weight?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — for the inference the argument exists to serve. The descriptive half has real support and is granted: the doctrinal content of the deuterocanon — purgatory and prayer for the dead (2 Maccabees 12), the intercession of the departed saints (2 Maccabees 15), almsgiving as satisfaction (Tobit 12), free will (Sirach 15) — was genuinely one of the factors in the Protestant hostility to the books, and Bruce Metzger and the mainstream grant that for the Reformers the canon question and the Reformation's doctrinal disputes ran together. What does not follow is the conclusion Gary Michuta draws from it — that a doctrinally-driven removal shows "there is something very important about them… that just doesn't fit into Protestant theology," i.e. that the books carry canonical weight the church cannot dismiss. A book's teaching a contested doctrine is equally consistent with the Reformers' own thesis (uninspired books that teach things the Hebrew canon and the New Testament do not), so the motive is symmetrical evidence and settles nothing about inspiration; motive-hunting cuts both ways (Trent's affirmation was as doctrinally driven as the Reformers' rejection); and the stated primary ground — for the Reformers the Hebrew canon and non-inspiration, for the 1820s Scottish Bible societies non-inspiration plus printing economy — is about inspiration, not doctrinal convenience, so the gloss that the societies "pointed out these books… do teach Catholic doctrine" inverts an objection whose actual burden was that the books are uninspired · advanced by Gary Michuta, resting the point on Bruce Metzger and on "two Scottish Bible societies."

Where those companion pages take a causal or timing thesis about a single agent (Luther, Trent), this page takes the wider inference Michuta builds on top of the whole story: not "who removed the books" or "when," but the claim that the removal was driven by the books' Catholic doctrinal content across the board — the sixteenth-century Reformers and the nineteenth-century Bible societies alike — and that this doctrinal motive is itself evidence for the books' theological weight. The sub-claim is therefore an evidential one: does a doctrinally-motivated removal show the deuterocanon carries real, hard-to-dismiss authority?

The claim

The argument runs in three steps. First, a datum: the deuterocanon teaches doctrines distinctive to Catholicism — purgatory and prayer for the dead, the intercession of the saints, satisfaction and merit through almsgiving, free will. Second, a historical motive: the Reformers set the books aside because of that content, and the point is pressed on two authorities — Bruce Metzger, and "two Scottish Bible societies" of the 1820s. Third, an inference: that a removal driven by doctrine shows the books matter, that they carry a weight Protestantism had to be rid of. Michuta states the whole chain in his debate with James White:

The Deuterocanonical books teach doctrines such as purgatory, free will, and the intercession of the saints. This has been recognized from the very beginning in Protestantism. In fact, as Bruce Metzger notes, the Reformers wished to correct the Church from doctrinal abuses, so first they had to determine which books are indeed Scripture. Then, they began to disparage the Deuterocanonical books specifically because they teach things like purgatory, intercession, and so on.

— and draws the payoff from the two societies and the staged demotion:

those two Scottish Bible societies pointed out that these books do claim inspiration and they do teach Catholic doctrine… the reason the Reformers opted to first put them on a lower level than Scripture, then eventually move them to the back of the Bible, and then eventually not only get rid of them but also remove all cross-references to these books, indicates that there is something very important about them — something that just doesn't fit into Protestant theology.

The load-bearing word is indicates: the doctrinally-driven removal is offered not merely as history but as evidence — the books were set aside because they "just doesn't fit into Protestant theology," which is taken to show they hold a truth Protestantism could not accommodate.1

Who advances it

The framing is Gary Michuta's, a Catholic defender of the deuterocanon's canonicity, and it is the general thesis under the two sharper motive charges he and others press at single targets (Luther and 2 Maccabees; Trent and the seven books). What makes the general form worth weighing on its own is that it reaches past any one agent to a pattern — Reformers in the 1500s, Bible societies in the 1820s — and converts the pattern into an argument for the books' substance. It leans on two supports that give it the look of scholarly and historical backing: Bruce Metzger, the Protestant textual scholar whose An Introduction to the Apocrypha is a standard handbook, and "two Scottish Bible societies" whose secession over the Apocrypha is a real and documented episode. Both supports are genuine data; the question is whether they carry the inference.2

The evidence for

The descriptive core is real: doctrine genuinely was one factor, and Metzger says so. The claim is not spun from nothing. Metzger, in the Reformation section of An Introduction to the Apocrypha, sets out exactly the entanglement Michuta names: needing to know which books could establish doctrine, the Reformers reopened the canon, and the dispute over purgatory and prayers for the dead turned on the authority of 2 Maccabees 12:43–45. The books really do carry the distinctively Catholic proof-texts — 2 Maccabees 12 for prayer and offering for the dead, 2 Maccabees 15:12–16 for the departed saints (Onias and Jeremiah) interceding for the living, Tobit 12:9 for almsgiving that "purge[s] away all sin," Sirach 15:14–17 for the freedom of the will — and those texts were live stakes in the Reformation controversies over purgatory, merit, and grace. So the entanglement of canon and doctrine is a matter of record, conceded across this timeline: the Reformers' hostility to the deuterocanon was partly doctrinal, and Michuta can cite a Protestant authority for it.3

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The descriptive half. Doctrine was one of the factors in the Protestant setting-aside of the deuterocanon; Metzger grants the canon-and-doctrine entanglement; and the books do concentrate the proof-texts for the very Catholic doctrines the Reformation denied. "The books' doctrinal content was part of why they became controversial" is fair and granted.

What does not follow. The step from that datum to the conclusion the argument exists to serve — that the doctrinally-driven removal shows the books carry canonical weight. Three things break the inference.

  1. The motive is symmetrical — it fits the Protestant thesis exactly as well. That a book teaches a contested doctrine, and was set aside amid a fight over that doctrine, is equally consistent with the Reformers' own account: these are uninspired books that teach things the church's Hebrew canon and the New Testament do not, so their doctrinal peculiarity is precisely what one would expect of non-canonical writings, not evidence of hidden authority. The datum under-determines the verdict: "the books teach purgatory, and were removed" is compatible with "and that shows they are weighty Scripture" and with "and that is one more reason to doubt they are Scripture." Michuta's inference simply assumes the first reading; the datum does not supply it. To infer canonicity from the fact that a book carries a disputed doctrine is to argue in the reverse-genetic direction — "it was rejected under doctrinal pressure, therefore it is authoritative" — which no more establishes the books than the parallel Protestant move ("Trent affirmed them under doctrinal pressure, therefore they are spurious") establishes the opposite. Motive settles neither; the question is decided by whether the books are inspired, weighed across this timeline, not by who was cross about which doctrine.4

  2. The stated primary ground was inspiration, not doctrinal convenience — and the "two Scottish societies" gloss inverts their actual objection. The Reformers' explicit reason for demoting the deuterocanon was the Hebrew canon and the books' want of inspired, prophetic authority — Luther's answer to the 2 Maccabees proof-text argues, on its face, that purgatory fails even granting the book's authority, which is the opposite of "he had to be rid of the book to escape the verse." And the nineteenth-century case is plainer still against Michuta's reading. The "two Scottish Bible societies" are the Edinburgh and Glasgow Bible Societies, which seceded from the British and Foreign Bible Society in the 1820s Apocrypha controversy (and later formed the Scottish Bible Society). Their stated grounds were that the Apocrypha is not divinely inspired and so should not be bound with and circulated as the Word of God, together with the plain economy of not paying to print uninspired matter and the complaint that the parent Society had exceeded its mandate. That is the reverse of Michuta's gloss that they "pointed out that these books do claim inspiration": the societies' whole burden was that the books lack inspiration. Their objection is evidence for the Protestant reading, not against it.5

  3. Metzger, read whole, does not underwrite the payoff. Michuta's supporting authority makes the historical point — canon and doctrine were entangled for the Reformers — and stops there; he does not draw, and his account does not license, the further inference that the entanglement vindicates the books. Metzger's own position is the standard Protestant one: the Reformers rightly followed the shorter Hebrew canon, and the deuterocanon, however useful, is not the basis of doctrine. To cite Metzger for the datum and then attach a conclusion Metzger rejects is to borrow the authority without the verdict. The most the citation yields is the granted middle ground: the doctrinal fight shaped the emphasis and timing of the Protestant "No," which is a claim about the controversy's occasion, not about the books' authority.6

What the dispute really turns on. Strip away the "indicates there is something very important about them" and the genuine questions are the ones the timeline already weighs: whether the deuterocanon is inspired Scripture (the Hebrew-canon and reception-history arguments — Romans 3:2, the first-century Jewish canon, Jerome's reasons), and whether the proof-texts actually carry their doctrines (the 2 Maccabees and Tobit pages). The motive-and-weight argument tries to short-circuit those questions with a single move — the books were removed because of their doctrine, therefore their doctrine has weight — but the premise is symmetrical and the authorities adduced for it point the other way.

Assessment

Weak — for the inference that a doctrinally-motivated removal shows the deuterocanon carries real canonical weight. What is granted, and true, is the narrower descriptive point: the books' distinctive doctrinal content (purgatory and prayer for the dead in 2 Maccabees 12, the interceding saints in 2 Maccabees 15, atoning almsgiving in Tobit 12, free will in Sirach 15) was genuinely one of the factors in the Protestant hostility to them, and Bruce Metzger grants that for the Reformers the canon question and the Reformation doctrinal disputes ran together. But the load-bearing inference does not hold. The motive is symmetrical — a book's teaching a contested doctrine fits the Reformers' own thesis (uninspired books teaching what the Hebrew canon and New Testament do not) exactly as well as it fits Michuta's, so it decides nothing about inspiration; motive-hunting cuts both ways, since Trent's affirmation of the books was as doctrinally driven as the Reformers' rejection; the stated primary ground was inspiration and the Hebrew canon, not doctrinal convenience; and the two Scottish Bible societies invoked for the point (Edinburgh and Glasgow, seceding in the 1820s Apocrypha controversy) objected precisely that the books are uninspired — the reverse of the gloss that they "pointed out these books do claim inspiration." Metzger, read whole, supplies the datum but not the payoff: he holds the standard Protestant verdict that the shorter Hebrew canon was rightly followed. Flag on the proponent's framing the slide from "the books' doctrine was part of why they were controversial" (true, and granted) to "the removal was driven by their Catholic doctrine, which shows they don't fit Protestant theology and so carry a weight Protestantism had to be rid of" (a non-sequitur, and against the actual stated grounds of both the Reformers and the Scottish societies).
