Does the deuterocanon's thousand years in the Vulgate put the burden of proof on those who would remove it?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — for the burden-of-proof framing specifically. The bare historical fact the argument rests on is sound: the deuterocanon was bound in the Latin Vulgate, the working Bible of Western Christendom, from late antiquity to the Reformation — roughly a thousand years of de facto inclusion, given conciliar and liturgical form by the African councils, Innocent I, Augustine, and the Council of Florence. But the move built on top of it — that inclusion is therefore the neutral status quo and the whole burden of proof falls on those who would remove the books — does not hold on its best case. It is a dialectical framing, not evidence; "status quo" is itself the disputed point, since the Reformers claimed to be restoring an older Hebrew canon; the Vulgate's own prefatory matter — Jerome's prologues — flagged these very books as apocrypha, read but not for doctrine; binding books in one codex never meant holding them equally canonical (the canonical-versus-ecclesiastical tiering of Rufinus and Athanasius); and the premise is Western-only — the Greek East never had "the Vulgate" at all. What survives is the real but modest presumptive weight of long reception, which this timeline already traces as the actual criterion — not a burden-shift that settles the canon by default · the long Western reception is affirmed in substance by Augustine, the African councils, Innocent I, and Florence; the burden-of-proof framing is contested by Jerome's own prologues and by Luther and the Reformers

Here the sub-claim is not a proof-text but a historical-dialectical thesis: that the deuterocanon's long inclusion in the Vulgate makes inclusion the default position, shifting the burden of proof onto anyone who would take the books out. The bare reception-history it appeals to is documented across the timeline and not re-argued here; this page weighs whether that history carries the burden-of-proof conclusion built on it.

The claim

The argument is a short syllogism:

  1. The deuterocanonical books were bound in the Bible of Latin Christendom — the Vulgate — from the settling of the New Testament canon (c. 400) to the Reformation (c. 1500): roughly eleven centuries of unbroken inclusion.
  2. The default or neutral position in any dispute is the status quo; the burden of proof rests on the party seeking to overturn it.
  3. Therefore the burden of proof falls on those who would remove the deuterocanon, not on those who keep it — and absent a decisive case for removal, the books stand.1

Each premise has real substance. The Vulgate genuinely was the Bible of the Latin West for a millennium, and it genuinely did carry Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch, and 1–2 Maccabees among its Old-Testament books; the Council of Trent could anathematize anyone who did not receive them "as … contained in the old Latin vulgate edition," because that is exactly where the Latin church had read them for ages.2 And the presumption principle is a respectable one in the theory of argument: as Richard Whately put it, "the burden of proof lies on the side of him who would dispute" an existing institution or received opinion, and "a presumption … in favour of existing institutions" stands until positive grounds are shown against them.3 The question is whether stacking the two together yields the conclusion — whether the long Latin inclusion is the kind of "status quo" that throws the whole onus onto the remover.

Who advances it

The proponents. The burden-of-proof framing is a contemporary Catholic apologetic move, but its historical substance is old and stands on this timeline. Its engine is Augustine's reception criterion — weigh a book by the breadth and antiquity of its use, "prefer those that are received by all the catholic churches to those which some do not receive" — the same rule that carried the disputed books into the African councils (393–419), the canon-list of Innocent I (405), and, raised to an ecumenical profession, the Council of Florence (1442), whose "accepts and venerates" the Council of Trent then made dogma. Modern defenders (e.g. Gary Michuta, Why Catholics Have More) restate this as a presumption argument: the books were in the Bible the whole church used, so the Reformers, not the Catholics, were the innovators who must carry the proof.4 The adversaries. The sharpest is the Vulgate's own translator. Jerome, who gave the Latin West the very Bible the argument leans on, prefaced his translation with the Prologus Galeatus, ruling that "whatever is outside of these is set aside among the apocrypha" and that Wisdom, Sirach, Judith, and Tobit "are not in the canon" — read "for the strengthening of the people, but not for confirming the authority of ecclesiastical dogmas." Those prologues travelled with the Vulgate text through the Middle Ages, so the codex that "included" the books also carried, in its own front matter, the standing caveat that they were not canonical Scripture.5 The Reformers then pressed the deeper objection: they were not innovating but restoring. Luther returned the canon to the Hebrew table the Jews and the Hebrew-minded Fathers had kept, gathering the extra books into a separate "Apocrypha" — "not held equal to the Holy Scriptures, and yet … profitable and good to read" — exactly the rank Jerome and Rufinus had assigned them.6

The evidence for the affirmative reading

The Vulgate really was the Bible of the Latin West, and it really did carry these books. This is not in dispute. From its completion under Jerome (c. 405) the Vulgate became, over the following centuries, the dominant and then the official Bible of Western Christendom; for some eleven hundred years a Latin Christian who opened "the Bible" opened a book that contained Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch, and the Maccabees, threaded among the protocanonical histories and prophets, not set behind a divider. Trent's decree naming "the old Latin vulgate edition" as the standard text simply ratified a millennium of use.2

That long reception had real conciliar and liturgical weight. Inclusion was not mere inertia of copyists. The books were named among the Scriptures by a string of Western authorities the timeline records — the councils of Hippo and Carthage, Pope Innocent I, the theological judgment of Augustine, and at last the ecumenical Council of Florence — and they were read at Mass and sung in the Office for centuries. The reception-criterion that carried them is, moreover, the very test this timeline finds the canon question actually turning on: not date of composition, but breadth and antiquity of use.4

The presumption principle is sound in its own right. That a long-established practice enjoys a standing presumption, and that the innovator bears the burden of proof, is a recognized canon of argument, not special pleading; it is precisely the logic of reception that even the Reformers had to honor in reverse, framing their move not as a free deletion but as a recovery of something prior.3 So the affirmative reading is not nothing: a tradition a thousand years deep does place a genuine onus on the one who would overturn it.

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The historical core is sound and important. The Vulgate was the Western Bible for a millennium and carried the deuterocanon within it; that inclusion was conciliar and liturgical, not accidental; and a reception that long and that broad genuinely does carry presumptive weight — the same weight the timeline assigns it when it concludes the canon question turns on reception, not on date. Anyone arguing for removal does face a real burden, and the Reformers felt it.

What overstates. Four joints give way under the weight the burden-of-proof conclusion puts on them.

  1. It is a dialectical framing, not evidence. "The burden lies on the other side" decides nothing about whether the books belong in the canon; it only relocates the labor of argument. A presumption can be defeated, and the Reformers offered defeaters — the Hebrew canon, the books' own disclaimers, the Fathers' graded rankings. So even granting the presumption, the argument cannot conclude for inclusion; at most it says the case for removal must be made — which the timeline shows the Reformers attempting in detail, not waving away. Framed as if it settled the matter, it begs the question.

  2. "Status quo" is itself the disputed point, and it cuts both ways. The argument fixes the baseline at c. 400 and counts forward, so that inclusion looks like the resting state and exclusion the disturbance. But the Reformers denied exactly that framing: they claimed to be restoring the older Hebrew canon — the canon of the Jews "unto whom were committed the oracles of God," of Melito, Origen, Cyril, and Jerome's Hebraica veritas — which is older than the Vulgate's millennium. Whose status quo, then? Push the baseline back two more centuries and the "innovation" is the levelling-up of the disputed books into the canon at Hippo and Carthage, against the narrower reckoning the Hebrew-minded Fathers kept. A presumption argument that can be run from either baseline proves nothing for either side; it merely restates the dispute about which canon is original.7

  3. The Vulgate's own prologues qualified the books it carried. The premise treats "bound in the Vulgate" as if it meant "held to be canonical Scripture in the Vulgate." But the Vulgate is Jerome's, and Jerome prefaced it with the very prologues that rank these books below the canon — apocrypha, read for edification, "not for confirming … ecclesiastical dogmas." Those prefaces were copied alongside the biblical text throughout the Middle Ages, so the learned tradition of the Latin West preserved a standing doubt about the disputed books inside the very codex adduced as their warrant; on the eve of Trent, Cardinal Cajetan still cited Jerome's prologue to rank them as edifying but non-canonical. The Bible that "included" the deuterocanon thus testifies, in its own front matter, against equating inclusion with canonical parity.5

  4. Binding in one codex never meant equal canonicity, and the premise is Western-only. The whole first half of this timeline is a record of Fathers who read the disputed books in church while ranking them below the canon: Origen kept the Greek copies "in use in our Churches" while listing a Hebrew twenty-two; Athanasius filed Wisdom, Sirach, Judith, and Tobit as "not … included in the Canon, but appointed by the Fathers to be read"; Rufinus named the middle rank outright — "Ecclesiastical," not "Canonical," read in church "but not appealed to for the confirmation of doctrine." Inclusion in the reading-Bible and equal canonical authority are exactly the two things these witnesses keep apart, so the bare fact of binding cannot establish the parity the burden-shift needs. And the codex in question is the Latin one: the Greek East never had "the Vulgate," and ran its own, differently-shaped canon question down to the Synod of Jerusalem (1672) — which affirmed the books, but on Eastern grounds, and later Orthodoxy still ranks them as anaginoskomena below the protocanon. The premise, even at its strongest, establishes a Western status quo, not the universal Church's.8

What the dispute really turns on. Strip the burden-shift away and what remains is the genuine presumptive weight of reception — which is not a tie-breaker awarded by default but the actual, contestable criterion this timeline traces. Augustine's "received by all the catholic churches," Florence's "accepts and venerates," and the Synod of Jerusalem's "the same church … hath delivered these also" are reception arguments, and they have real force; but they are answered, not by a counter-presumption, but by the rival reception the Hebrew-minded Fathers and the Reformers invoke — the narrower canon "of whose authority was never any doubt in the Church." The canon question is thus decided by weighing those receptions against each other across the centuries, the work the timeline does — not by deciding in advance that one side need not argue.

Assessment

Weak — for the burden-of-proof framing specifically. The interpretation that the deuterocanon's long place in the Vulgate makes inclusion the default and throws the burden of proof onto those who would remove the books does not hold on its best available case. As a probative argument it founders on four counts: it is a dialectical relocation of labor, not evidence, and so cannot conclude for inclusion; "status quo" is the very point in dispute, and the Reformers ran the same presumption from an older Hebrew baseline they claimed to be restoring; the Vulgate's own Hieronymian prologues, carried with the text through the Middle Ages, rank the disputed books below the canon, so "bound in the Vulgate" never meant "held canonical in the Vulgate"; and the premise is Western-only, the Greek East never having had that Bible. What is sound is the historical fact underneath — the deuterocanon's millennium of conciliar and liturgical reception in the Latin West — and the real presumptive weight that reception carries. But that weight is exactly what this timeline already weighs, list by list and Father by Father, as the contested criterion of the canon; it is not a burden-shift that settles the question by default. The bare fact is sound; the polemical conclusion drawn from it is not. (Its companion arguments — whether the disputed books' own proof-texts bar or warrant the doctrines built on them — are weighed on the Tobit (weak) and 2 Maccabees (contested) pages.)
