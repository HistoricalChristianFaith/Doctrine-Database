Does a teaching's resting on a single passage make it “unbiblical”?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline; methodological) · Assessment: sound — the test “a doctrine found in only one passage is thereby unbiblical” is a fallacy: frequency of attestation is not a mark of canonicity or of truth, and applied evenhandedly it would gut doctrines every tradition holds (the triune baptismal formula of Matthew 28:19 is itself a single-passage teaching). What the principle does not do is settle the deuterocanon dispute: it shows that “2 Maccabees is the only book that prays for the dead” does not by itself disqualify the practice, not that the practice or the book is thereby established — the book's canonicity is the disputed premise, and the operative caution was always about a text's clarity, not its count · advanced by Trent Horn

The claim

Trinitarian formula only found in Matthew 28 — does that mean it's unbiblical because it's only found in one place in the Bible? No.

— Trent Horn, in a YouTube discussion of the deuterocanon and prayer for the dead (around 13:12).1 The methodological sub-claim under weight here quietly underwrites a whole family of canon objections — that 2 Maccabees is “the only book” that prays for the dead, that Tobit “alone” ties almsgiving to atonement — each treating uniqueness of attestation as if it were a disqualifier. The interpretation to test, stated cleanly: does the fact that a teaching rests on a single passage make it unbiblical, or not to be believed?

Who advances it

The point is a commonplace of Catholic and confessional reply to “the deuterocanon teaches X found nowhere else.” Horn states it in its sharpest form — turning the objector's own canon back on them: the explicit command to baptize “in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Ghost” occurs in exactly one verse of Scripture, Matthew 28:19, yet no one calls the triune baptismal formula “unbiblical” for being a hapax. The structure is a reductio: if “found in only one place” disqualified a teaching, it would disqualify teachings the objector himself holds.2

The evidence for

Frequency is not a canon-criterion, and never was. No church, council, or confession has ever made the number of times a teaching appears a test of its inspiration or truth. A thing stated once in Scripture is no less true than a thing stated ten times; the relevant questions are whether the text says it and whether the book is Scripture, not how often the point recurs. Treating “only one passage” as a strike against a doctrine is a species of the argument from silence — counting the texts that don't teach it as though their silence weighed against the one that does.

The Matthew 28:19 parallel is well chosen — and textually secure. The triune baptismal command is the charter of Christian baptism across East and West, confessed in every creed, and it appears in a single verse. Nor is it a shaky single verse: the formula stands in every known Greek manuscript and ancient version of Matthew, and was already the Church's baptismal practice by the Didache (c. 100) and quoted in full by Irenaeus (c. 180). The lone challenge to it — F. C. Conybeare's reading of Eusebius's pre-Nicene habit of citing the verse in a shorter “in my name” form — is a conjectural emendation with no manuscript support, and is precisely the kind of thin case that should be distinguished from a genuinely doubtful text such as the Comma Johanneum (1 John 5:7), which is absent from all early Greek manuscripts. So the example holds: a doctrine can rest on one passage and be both universally held and text-critically solid.3

The case generalizes well beyond Matthew 28. Christ's descent to the dead is confessed in the Apostles' Creed yet rests chiefly on one passage (1 Peter 3:19); the catching-up of living believers at the parousia rests on 1 Thessalonians 4:16–17; and — pointedly for this dispute — the protocanonical Paul invokes a rite on behalf of the dead, “baptized for the dead” (1 Corinthians 15:29), in a single, isolated, and notoriously obscure verse, without rebuke. If singular attestation disqualified a teaching, it would take these with it. So “2 Maccabees is the only book that prays for the dead” cannot, by that fact alone, make the practice unbiblical.4

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The narrow methodological claim is sound and, applied evenhandedly, decisive: uniqueness of attestation is not a disqualifier. “Found in only one place” is not a recognized canon-criterion, it is not a mark of falsity, and the objector who wields it against 2 Maccabees or Tobit would have to wield it against the triune baptismal formula, the descent, and Paul's own “baptism for the dead.” The hapax objection collapses under its own generality.

What the steelman on the other side actually amounts to — and it is not the count. There is a real hermeneutical caution buried under the bad version of this objection, but it concerns a text's clarity, not its frequency. The Reformation principle is the perspicuity of Scripture: not that everything is equally plain, but that what is necessary for salvation is plainly taught “in some place of Scripture or other” — a single clear text suffices, while building heavy doctrine on an obscure text warrants care. The Westminster divines' own foil for a “puzzling” text is, tellingly, 1 Corinthians 15:29 — the baptism-for-the-dead verse. So the legitimate worry is never “it appears only once” but “it appears once and unclearly, yet is asked to carry a great weight.” That distinction cuts both ways here: the bare fact that 2 Maccabees teaches prayer and offering for the dead is not obscure — both Luther and Calvin granted the text plainly teaches it — so the clarity-caution does not bite against the practice as such, only against the further leap to developed purgatory, which is weighed on its own page.5

What the point does not prove. The principle clears away a bad objection; it does not settle the canon question. To show that “single-passage” is no disqualifier is to show that the practice is not thereby unbiblical — not that it is thereby established. The Matthew 28:19 analogy carries only because Matthew's canonicity is undisputed; 2 Maccabees' and Tobit's canonicity is the very thing in question, so one cannot run from “the book teaches it” to “therefore it is biblical” without first settling whether the book is Scripture — and that turns, across this timeline, on reception, not on a proof-text's repetition count. Horn's reductio is valid against the objector's test; it leaves the underlying canon dispute exactly where it was.6

Assessment

Sound. As a test of doctrine, “it is found in only one passage, therefore it is unbiblical” is a fallacy, and Horn's Matthew 28:19 reductio exposes it cleanly: frequency of attestation is not a canon-criterion, not a mark of truth, and would, applied consistently, disqualify teachings every tradition confesses — the triune baptismal formula, the descent, the catching-up of believers, and Paul's own appeal to a rite for the dead. The genuine hermeneutical caution in the neighbourhood is about a text's clarity, not its count, and on the plain teaching of prayer and offering for the dead the deuterocanonical text is not obscure (both Reformers conceded it). Two limits keep this from doing more than it does, and neither dents the core: the point shows the practice is not disqualified by uniqueness, not that it is established; and the Matthew 28:19 parallel works only because that book's canonicity is settled, whereas the deuterocanon's is the disputed premise. So the methodological claim is sound and transferable — it answers the “teaches X found nowhere else” form of the deuterocanon objections — while the canon question itself is left to be decided, as ever, by reception. (The proof-texts it shields are weighed on their own merits: 2 Maccabees 12 on prayer for the dead, assessed contested, and Tobit 12:9 on atoning almsgiving, assessed weak for the disqualifying reading.)
