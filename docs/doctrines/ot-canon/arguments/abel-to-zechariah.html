Does "from the blood of Abel to the blood of Zechariah" delimit the Hebrew Old Testament canon, excluding the Apocrypha?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — as a demarcation of the canon. That Jesus names Abel and Zechariah as the first and last martyrs of Old-Testament history, summing up the generation's guilt in a beginning-to-end merism, is sound and is how the whole church read the saying. But the popular "bookends" inference — that, because Chronicles stands last in the Hebrew order and records a Zechariah's murder, Jesus was tracing the limits of the canon (Genesis to Chronicles) and so silently excluding Maccabees and the rest of the Apocrypha — does not hold on its best available case. It runs aground on three independent difficulties: no interpreter read the saying as a canon-boundary until 1780; the Genesis-to-Chronicles book-order it presupposes is not securely attested as dominant before the thirteenth century; and the Zechariah Jesus names ("son of Berechiah," murdered in the temple) is not unambiguously the Chronicles figure (Jehoiada's son), the very identification the argument needs · the stock Protestant argument, in scholarly form Roger Beckwith, popularly F. F. Bruce and Doug Wilson; the reading the timeline's Fathers (Origen, Jerome) did not take

Related arguments: Abel to Zechariah — see also which Zechariah does Jesus name — is he the Chronicles figure the argument needs? and did the Hebrew canon end with Chronicles in Jesus' day — was that order established and dominant before the first century?

Here the sub-claim is the popular Protestant "A-to-Z" or "bookends" argument: that Jesus' phrase "from the blood of Abel to the blood of Zechariah" (Luke 11:51 // Matthew 23:35) marks the first and last books of the Hebrew Bible and thereby fixes the canon's limits against the Apocrypha.

The claim

The argument runs as a short chain:

  1. The Hebrew Bible Jesus used ran Genesis to Chronicles — Chronicles, not Malachi, standing last.
  2. Abel's murder is recorded near the start of Genesis (Gen 4); the murder of a Zechariah is recorded near the end of Chronicles (2 Chron 24:20–22).
  3. So in naming "from the blood of Abel to the blood of Zechariah" Jesus was citing the first and last martyrs of the canon — its opening and closing books — and thereby implicitly delimiting it, Genesis to Chronicles, excluding later books such as 1–2 Maccabees.1

F. F. Bruce gave it its standard popular form: "when our Lord sums up all the martyrs of Old Testament times He does so by mentioning the first martyr in Genesis (Abel) and the last martyr in Chronicles (Zechariah) … The books of the Apocrypha were not included in the Hebrew Bible … Our Lord and the apostles certainly did not regard the apocryphal books as part of Holy Scripture." Doug Wilson puts the slogan plainly: the blood of Abel to the blood of Zechariah "was basically giving us the A to Z, the table of contents — Genesis to 2 Chronicles."1 The serious scholarly form is Roger Beckwith's, and it is deliberately more modest: the saying, he writes, "in all probability implies that for Jesus and his hearers the canon began with Genesis and ended with Chronicles," reflecting "the traditional Jewish arrangement of the books (recorded in the Talmud)."1 Two distinct claims are bundled here, and they need separating: (a) that Jesus names the first and last martyrs of the Old-Testament era — a beginning-to-end merism; and (b) that this merism tracks, and so attests, the canonical order Genesis–Chronicles, fixing the canon's edge. Claim (a) is uncontroversial. The argument's weight rests on (b).

Who advances it

The proponents. The bookends argument is among the most repeated Protestant arguments for the Hebrew canon. Its scholarly anchor is Roger Beckwith, The Old Testament Canon of the New Testament Church (1985); its most influential popularization is F. F. Bruce. From there it passes into a wide apologetic literature — Steve Christie's Why Protestant Bibles Are Smaller (2019), and a stream of video teachers and ministry sites (Doug Wilson, Mike Winger, Matt Whitman, Allen Parr, Bibleask, CARM).2 The popular retellings frequently garble the argument: several have Jesus delimiting the canon "from Genesis to Malachi" (Winger) or "Genesis all the way through … the book of Malachi" (Parr) — which, if the point depends on Chronicles standing last, refutes the argument rather than stating it.2 The adversaries. The reading is contested across the board by the scholarship that has actually traced the saying's history — Edmon Gallagher (whose two 2014 studies are the fullest treatment) and H. G. L. Peels — and answered in detail by Catholic apologists such as Gary Michuta.7 Tellingly, the difficulty is conceded even from inside the camp: Doug Wilson grants that the Zechariah Jesus names "was not a Z," i.e. cannot straightforwardly be the Chronicles figure, which "would mess with my chronological canonical argument."2

The evidence for the affirmative reading

The merism is real, and Chronicles does stand last in one ancient ordering. Steelmanned to Beckwith's form, the argument is not silly. A first-to-last figure ("from Abel … to Zechariah") is a natural way to span the whole of a body of writing, and there is an old Jewish arrangement in which Chronicles closes the third division (the Writings): the baraita in the Babylonian Talmud, Bava Batra 14b, lists the order of the Writings as ending "and Ezra and Chronicles." If that order were the one Jesus and his hearers knew, then naming a martyr from Genesis and a martyr from Chronicles would indeed run from the first book to the last — and a merism that spans Genesis-to-Chronicles would, at minimum, presuppose a canon shaped like the Hebrew one, narrower than the Greek collection with its Maccabean martyrs. This is the genuinely evidential core, and it is why Beckwith hedges to "in all probability implies."3

Adversarial assessment

What holds. Jesus names Abel and Zechariah as the first and last martyrs of Old-Testament history, summing up "all the righteous blood shed upon the earth" to indict "this generation." That much is plain and is exactly how the saying was read for seventeen centuries. Whether it does the further work the bookends argument needs — attesting and delimiting the canon — is where it fails. Three independent difficulties each cut against the demarcation reading; together they are decisive.

  1. The reception problem: no one read it as a canon-boundary until 1780. If Jesus were marking the limits of the canon, it is striking that no interpreter in the surviving record took him to be doing so for more than seventeen hundred years. The unbroken tradition reads the saying otherwise — as spanning the first to the last martyr, or the beginning of time to the present. Tertullian (c. 220) lists the righteous slain "from the beginning" down to Zechariah "butchered between the altar and the temple," then on to John the Baptist "at the close of the law and the prophets" — a sweep of martyrdom, not a table of contents. John Chrysostom (c. 407) takes the point to be "a holy man slain in a holy place," meant "to alarm" the Pharisees and "comfort His disciples." Jerome (c. 398), who of all the Fathers most insisted on the Hebrew canon and could have seized on a canon-boundary reading to support it, instead canvasses which Zechariah is meant and never suggests the verse marks the canon's edge. Remigius (c. 533) has the pair "denote all holy martyrs, both of lay and priestly order"; Calvin (1555) reads it of the murder of the prophets; John Lightfoot (1675) and J. D. Michaelis (1750) explain the pairing by the fact that Scripture records a cry "for vengeance" only over Abel and this Zechariah. The first interpreter on record to read "Abel to Zechariah" as marking the canon's boundaries is Johann Gottfried Eichhorn, in his Introduction to the Study of the Old Testament (1780). To accept the bookends argument one must hold that Jesus' first hearers understood a canon-demarcation in his words, that the meaning was then lost to the entire church within a generation or two, and that it was not recovered until the late Enlightenment — a heavy thing to suppose of a point alleged to be the plain sense.4

  2. The book-order problem: Genesis-to-Chronicles is not securely the order Jesus knew. The argument's first premise — that the Hebrew Bible ended with Chronicles — is asserted as though it were a fixed fact of "the" Jewish canon. It was not. Surveying the actual lists of Old-Testament books across the first millennium, no single sequence is dominant, and most do not put Chronicles last: Melito, Origen, Cyril of Jerusalem, Athanasius, Epiphanius, Jerome, Augustine, and the great codices all close on other books (commonly Daniel, Ezra, Esther, or Maccabees). The Chronicles-last order becomes common only after the Adath Deborim (1207) and standard only with the printing press. In the first millennium the only clear witnesses to a Chronicles-last sequence are the Talmud's Bava Batra 14b and (oddly for this argument) Innocent I's letter of 405 — which ends with Chronicles but includes the deuterocanon earlier in the list, and so does not support an Apocrypha-excluding canon at all. Worse for the argument, the very Talmudic context that supplies the Chronicles-last order also tells against an Apocrypha-free canon: the parallel tractate Bava Kamma treats Sirach as belonging to the Writings, and Epiphanius reports that in his day the Jews still disputed the standing of Sirach and Wisdom. So the premise the bookends argument leans on may itself be anachronistic: perhaps the reason no one before 1780 read "Abel to Zechariah" as the canon's edge is that Genesis-to-Chronicles only became a familiar order centuries after Christ. To rescue the argument one must hold that Bava Batra preserves a pre-Christian order that Jesus' hearers knew but that then vanished from both Judaism and Christianity for a millennium — a possible but unevidenced supposition.5

  3. The identity problem: the Zechariah Jesus names is not unambiguously the Chronicles figure. The argument needs the Zechariah of the saying to be Zechariah son of Jehoiada (2 Chron 24:20–22), so that he can serve as "the last book." But Matthew calls him "Zechariah son of Berechiah" (Matt 23:35), and the Chronicles figure is the son of Jehoiada, not Berechiah; the patronym matches instead the prophet Zechariah son of Berechiah (Zech 1:1), of whose murder Scripture says nothing. Luke's parallel (Luke 11:51) gives no patronym at all. Across church history the identification was genuinely open: Origen, Basil, Gregory of Nyssa, and Cyril of Alexandria identified him with the father of John the Baptist (martyred in the temple per the Protoevangelium of James); Josephus knew a Zechariah son of Baruch slain in the temple court in AD 67/68; Jerome and others argued for Jehoiada's son and were forced to explain away the patronym — Jerome's own proposal, that "Barachiah" was a second name for Jehoiada because it means "blessed of the Lord," he frankly admits to be a guess. Each route to "the Chronicles figure" requires either positing a scribal error in Matthew (against every major critical edition, which print "son of Berechiah") or a name-conflation device whose use in Jesus' own day is itself unproven. The argument simply assumes the identification its first premise needs, and even sympathetic proponents (Wilson, citing Gleason Archer) concede the patronym does not fit a clean "A-to-Z." The candidates are weighed in full on a dedicated argument page (assessment: contested — the Chronicles figure is the most probable referent, but the identification is disputed, and even granted it does not by itself carry the canon-demarcation inference).6

What the saying really shows. Strip the canon-demarcation inference away and the true residue is the one the whole tradition saw: a beginning-to-end indictment, naming the first and last righteous men whose murder Scripture records crying out for vengeance, to charge "this generation" with the whole history of prophet-killing. That reading is sound and needs none of the three contested supports. Whether it carries any further, faint presumption toward a Hebrew-shaped canon (Beckwith's hedged "in all probability") is at most a weak corroboration that depends entirely on the disputed book-order — and it is a long way from the popular claim that Jesus here "rejected the Apocrypha." The broader interpretation the bookends argument is enlisted to serve — that the Old Testament should be measured by the Hebrew canon — has serious support elsewhere (Jerome's Hebraica veritas, the patristic Hebrew-canon lists, the apostolic citation pattern); but this particular proof-text is not one of its load-bearing pillars.7

Assessment

Weak — as a demarcation of the canon. The interpretation that Jesus' "from the blood of Abel to the blood of Zechariah" marks the limits of the Hebrew canon and thereby excludes the Apocrypha does not hold on its best available case. It rests on three premises each independently doubtful: that the saying functions as a canon-boundary (a reading unattested before Eichhorn in 1780, against an unbroken tradition that read it as a first-to-last martyr merism); that the Hebrew Bible Jesus knew ended with Chronicles (an order not securely dominant before the thirteenth century, and whose one early Talmudic witness sits in a context that counts Sirach among the Writings); and that the Zechariah named is the Chronicles figure (whom Matthew's "son of Berechiah" does not fit without positing a scribal error or a name-conflation, a difficulty proponents themselves concede). What survives — and it is genuinely sound, not nothing — is the modest, universally-held point that Jesus names the first and last martyrs of the Old-Testament era to indict his generation; at most this carries a faint, order-dependent presumption toward a Hebrew-shaped canon, far short of a proof that the Apocrypha is here excluded. The Hebrew-canon position has stronger grounds (weighed on the Romans 3:2 and patristic canon-list argument pages, and across the timeline itself); the "A-to-Z bookends" proof-text is not one of them. Proponents who press it as a decisive exclusion of the Apocrypha — and especially those who muddle it into "Genesis to Malachi" — overstate a saying that the church for seventeen centuries never read as marking the canon at all.
