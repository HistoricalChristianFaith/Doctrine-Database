Were there “400 silent years” — no prophet at all between Malachi and Christ — closing the canon?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — for the literal form the popular proponents press, that prophecy fell wholly silent for “400 years” between Malachi and Christ, so the canon was sealed and nothing written in the interval (the deuterocanon) can be Scripture. The narrower premise behind it is sound and well-attested — a recognized Jewish tradition held that the line of canonical-grade, classical prophets ended with Haggai, Zechariah, and Malachi. But the flat “no prophetic voices at all” claim is refuted by the proponents' own favourite witness: Josephus, who anchors the cutoff, himself reports prophetic figures after Artaxerxes (Hyrcanus, Menahem the Essene, Jesus son of Ananus) and claims a prophetic gift for himself; the New Testament places prophets squarely in the supposed silence (Anna, John the Baptist, Jesus “taken for a prophet”); and Justin Martyr has an unbroken prophetic succession running until Christ, which he says even the Jews admit. And the inference itself does not follow even on the narrower premise — cessation of prophecy is not closure of the canon (weighed in full alongside) — while prophetic authorship was never the Hebrew canon's actual criterion (Agur and Lemuel's mother, not prophets, stand in Proverbs). What survives is the modest, true point that a major Jewish stream graded the post-Malachi writings below the prophetic books

An argument page weighs a single sub-claim adduced in support of a main interpretation — a proof-text, a parallel, a historical thesis — and renders an adversarial assessment of the interpretation it serves. Unlike a person-detail page it sits across the timeline rather than at one date. Every factual claim is sourced; the proponents' own words are quoted verbatim and weighed on their best available case.

The claim

A staple of the popular case against the deuterocanon is the “400 silent years”: that from Malachi (mid-fifth century BC) to the coming of Christ, God sent no prophet, so revelation paused and the Old Testament canon was already sealed before the deuterocanonical books were written. The cutoff is hung on Josephus, who bounds the divinely-credited books at the reign of Artaxerxes “because there hath not been an exact succession of prophets since that time.” Allen Parr presses it in its flat form:

“Josephus is recognizing that Malachi was the last prophet inspired by God, and there were no other prophetic voices between that 400 years…”

— from which the conclusion is drawn that the books written in that window cannot be inspired Scripture: no prophet, no Scripture.1 The sub-claim weighed here is the empirical one on which the rest leans: that there was a total prophetic silence — no prophet of any kind — across the centuries between Malachi and Christ, sealing the canon against the later books. (The further inference, that cessation of prophecy would by itself close the canon, is weighed on the companion page; here the question is whether the silence was real at all.)

Who advances it

The cutoff in its careful form is Roger Beckwith's and, behind him, the Second-Temple and rabbinic tradition that prophecy lapsed with the last prophets. Popularly the silence is stated flatly: Allen Parr — Malachi “the last prophet inspired by God,” “no other prophetic voices” in the 400 years; CARM (Matt Slick) — the deuterocanonical books “are not prophetic… and they even acknowledge that there were no prophets in Israel at their time (cf. 1 Macc. 9:27; 14:41)”; and the familiar pulpit shorthand of “the four hundred silent years.” The defensible core they all draw on is genuine: 1 Maccabees itself laments the absence of a prophet (4:46; 9:27; 14:41 — “until there should arise a faithful prophet”), and the rabbinic tradition says the Holy Spirit of prophecy departed with Haggai, Zechariah, and Malachi (Tosefta Sotah 13:2; b. Yoma 9b).2 The adversarial side — Gary Michuta and Trent Horn for the Catholic case — answers that “exact succession of prophets” is not “all prophecy,” that Josephus and the New Testament both report prophets in the “silent” window, and that prophetic authorship was never the canon's criterion in any case.3

The evidence for

The narrower premise — a recognized terminus for classical prophecy — is real and early, and that is the argument's true strength. Second-Temple and rabbinic Judaism widely held that the great line of writing prophets had a recognized end. 1 Maccabees, itself written after Malachi, three times registers that no prophet was then present: the defiled altar-stones are stored “until there should come a prophet” (4:46), the affliction is “such as was not since the time that a prophet was not seen among them” (9:27), and Simon is made leader “until there should arise a faithful prophet” (14:41). The rabbis say the same: with Haggai, Zechariah, and Malachi “the Holy Spirit ceased out of Israel” (Tosefta Sotah 13:2–3), only a lesser bat kol remaining. So the books of the interval were, in this stream, of a lower, non-prophetic order — the same Hebrew-measure reading this timeline traces from Jerome. Read as “no canonical-grade, scriptural prophet after Artaxerxes,” the claim is a serious, live position, and the strongest form of the argument.4

Adversarial assessment

What holds. That a recognized Jewish tradition held the classical prophetic succession to have ended with Malachi, and that the deuterocanon was written after that, is true and undisputed; so is the modest conclusion that this stream graded the later books below the prophetic Scriptures. The trouble lies in the flat popular form — that there were “no prophetic voices at all” for four centuries — and in the leap from a prophetic terminus to a sealed canon. Both give way.

  1. Josephus says “exact succession,” not “all prophecy” — and he himself reports later prophets. The very sentence the cutoff rests on is precise: the post-Artaxerxes books lack equal credit “because there hath not been an exact succession of prophets since that time” — the failure of the unbroken, official prophetic line, not the disappearance of prophecy as such. And Josephus is his own counter-witness. He reports that the Hasmonean John Hyrcanus (high priest c. 134–104 BC, well inside the “silent” window) “alone had three of the most desirable things in the world, the government of his nation, and the high-priesthood, and the gift of prophecy” (Jewish War I.2.8). He tells how Menahem the Essene, “who had… the foreknowledge of future events given him by God,” saw Herod as a schoolboy and “saluted him as king of the Jews” (Antiquities XV.10.5), adding that “many of these Essenes have, by their excellent virtue, been thought worthy of this knowledge of Divine revelations.” He records Jesus son of Ananus, “a plebeian, and an husbandman, who, four years before the war began” cried his doom-oracle over Jerusalem and the temple for seven years and five months (Jewish War VI.5.3). And Josephus claims a prophetic gift for himself, interpreting dreams and foretelling Vespasian's rise at Jotapata (Jewish War III.8.3). A man who reports four prophetic figures after Artaxerxes, one of them himself, plainly did not mean “no prophetic voices at all” — so the proof-text will not bear the flat silence Parr loads onto it.5

  2. The New Testament and Justin Martyr put prophets squarely in the “silent” window. The proponents are mostly Christians who confess the Gospels — and the Gospels report prophecy in the interval without a hint that it had been absent for centuries. Anna, “a prophetess, the daughter of Phanuel… of a great age,” is in the temple at Jesus' presentation (Luke 2:36–38); John the Baptist is “more than a prophet” (Matthew 11:9–10); the healed man calls Jesus “a prophet” (John 9:17), and nobody answers that there has been no prophet for four hundred years. Justin Martyr makes the point explicit against Trypho: there was an unbroken succession “in your nation” — “prophets in succession until John” — right up to Christ, who “put an end to his prophesying”: “there never failed to be a prophet among you… But after the manifestation and death of our Jesus Christ in your nation, there was and is nowhere any prophet” (Dialogue with Trypho 51–52). The cutoff Justin knows is Christ, not Malachi; the prophetic gifts then “formerly among your nation have been transferred to us” (Dialogue 82). The earliest Christian readers of these very Scriptures located the end of Jewish prophecy at the Messiah, four centuries past the date the “silent years” slogan assigns.6

  3. Prophetic authorship was never the Hebrew canon's criterion — so a cutoff on prophets does not bound the canon. Even granting the narrowest, soundest form — no canonical-grade prophet after Artaxerxes — the canon-closing inference still does not follow, for the Hebrew Bible itself does not draw its line at named prophets. The words of Agur son of Jakeh stand in Scripture as “the prophecy” (Proverbs 30:1), and “the words of king Lemuel, the prophecy that his mother taught him” in Proverbs 31:1 — yet neither Agur nor Lemuel's mother is anywhere counted among Israel's prophets. All Scripture is held to be prophetic in the broad sense of God-breathed, but not all Scripture is the work of a named prophet; so a terminus on the prophetic succession cannot, by itself, fix the extent of the canon. And the further leap — that cessation sealed the canon against the later books — fails on its own terms: the same rabbinic corpus that preserves the cessation tradition still cites Ben Sira as a verse of the Writings and still debated Qohelet and the Song into the second century (weighed in full on the companion page). Cessation of prophecy is simply not closure of the canon.7

Proponent overreach to flag. Parr's “no other prophetic voices between that 400 years” is an overstatement of his own best witness: Josephus, on whom Parr hangs the cutoff, reports prophetic figures across exactly that span and claims one for himself, and Josephus's actual words are the narrower “exact succession,” not a total silence. The defensible claim — the one every framing is entitled to — is the narrower “no canonical-grade prophet after Artaxerxes,” which speaks to the deuterocanon's graded standing, not to a sealed canon; the flat “400 silent years,” pressed as a premise, is simply not true to the sources the proponents themselves revere.8

Assessment

Weak — for the literal “total silence” form and the canon-closure it is made to prove; the narrower premise underneath it is sound. That a recognized Jewish tradition held the classical prophetic succession to have ended with Haggai, Zechariah, and Malachi, and that the deuterocanon was written afterward, is true and well-attested, and a genuine datum for those books' graded standing — converging with the Hebrew-measure reading this timeline traces from Jerome. But the flat popular claim of “400 silent years” with “no prophetic voices at all” does not hold: Josephus, the witness who anchors the cutoff, reports Hyrcanus, Menahem, and Jesus son of Ananus as prophetic figures after Artaxerxes and claims the gift for himself; the New Testament places Anna, John the Baptist, and a prophet-acclaimed Jesus in the supposed silence; and Justin Martyr runs the prophetic succession unbroken until Christ. Nor would even the narrower premise close the canon — prophetic authorship is not the Hebrew canon's criterion (Agur, Lemuel), and cessation of prophecy is not closure of the canon (weighed in full on the companion page). Trust the narrow, true point; flag — on the proponents' accounts (Parr) — the slide from a recognized prophetic terminus to a literal four-century silence their own sources deny.
