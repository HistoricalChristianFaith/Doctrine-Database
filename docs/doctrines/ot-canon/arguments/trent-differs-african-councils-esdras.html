Does the Council of Trent's Old Testament canon differ from Hippo and Carthage's? — the 1 Esdras question

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: sound — on the leading scholarly reading the African councils' "two books of Esdras" included the Greek 1 Esdras (Esdras A / Latin 3 Esdras), which Trent's list does not, so the two enumerations are not identical; but this establishes non-identity over one boundary book, not the contradiction the claim is usually built toward.

Related arguments: the canon councils & 1 Esdras — see also Do the Catholic canon councils contradict each other? — "Trent anathematizes Hippo and Carthage" and the conciliar-infallibility objection

The popular Protestant case sometimes presses past "Trent added the books" to a sharper, narrower point: that the canon defined at Trent (1546) is not even the same list the provincial councils of Hippo and Carthage ratified — so a Catholic who cites those councils to support the Tridentine canon is appealing to councils that endorsed a different catalogue. The whole weight rides on one book, Esdras. This page weighs only the factual premise: do the lists genuinely differ, and over which book? The further, ecclesiological inference some draw from it — that the councils therefore contradict one another, that Trent's anathema falls on Hippo and Carthage — is a separate claim weighed on its own terms. (The distinct scope objection — that Hippo and Carthage were provincial, not universal — is handled on the "first universal, binding definition" page.)

The claim

James White put the premise as a cross-examination challenge in his 2004 debate with Gary Michuta:

Given that Pope Innocent I and Pope Gelasius both affirmed the canon list of Hippo and Carthage, yet Trent and every pope since then has affirmed a different list that excludes the extra material in Esdras, which popes were right and which popes were wrong on this issue?

— The Great Debate IX: Is the Apocrypha Scripture? (White vs. Michuta, 2004).1 The mechanism behind "a different list" is a fact about which Bible each council worked from. Hippo and Carthage read the Greek Septuagint (in Old Latin dress), which carried two Esdras books: Esdras A, the Greek book of Ezra-additions (the apocryphal "1 Esdras" of the English Apocrypha, the Latin tradition's 3 Esdras), and Esdras B, the whole of canonical Ezra–Nehemiah translated as a single book. Trent, by contrast, worked from the Vulgate, where Jerome had split Ezra from Nehemiah and dropped the Greek additions — so Trent's "first book of Esdras, and the second which is entitled Nehemias" is protocanonical Ezra plus Nehemiah, and the decree silently omits 3 (and 4) Esdras. The conclusion: the African councils canonized a book — the Greek 1 Esdras / Latin 3 Esdras — that Trent does not. Mike Winger states the bare premise the same way, that the African councils "had a different list than the Catholic church had at the Council of Trent."2

The naming tangle

The dispute is impossible to follow without the Esdras nomenclature, which differs across every tradition:

  • Septuagint / Old Latin: Esdras A (Α) = the Greek apocryphal Ezra-book, with the famous contest of the three guardsmen ("the truth is strongest"); Esdras B (Β) = canonical Ezra–Nehemiah as one book.
  • Jerome's Vulgate: 1 Esdras = Ezra; 2 Esdras = Nehemiah; 3 Esdras = the Greek Esdras A, now demoted; 4 Esdras = the later Ezra apocalypse.
  • English Apocrypha (KJV): 1 Esdras = Greek Esdras A (= Vulgate 3 Esdras); 2 Esdras = the apocalypse (= Vulgate 4 Esdras).

So the single book that the argument turns on travels under three different numbers — Greek Esdras A, KJV 1 Esdras, Latin 3 Esdras. The question is whether the African councils' "two books of Esdras" counted that book as one of the two.3

Who advances it

The premise is a staple of the more textually-careful Reformed apologetics — James White (the 2004 Michuta debate, and the line of argument he credits to William Webster) and Mike Winger (How We Got the OT Canon) — answered on the Catholic side by Gary Michuta (in the same debate) and by Dave Armstrong, who treats the "two books of Esdras" identification as contested and the lists as harmonizable. The factual core, however, does not depend on either camp: it is the standard finding of the specialist literature on the early canon lists, which the apologists are downstream of.4

The evidence for

The leading scholarship reads the African "two Esdras" exactly as White does. The specialist treatment — Pierre-Maurice Bogaert's study of the numbering of the Esdras books in the Latin canon, followed by Edmon Gallagher and John Meade in The Biblical Canon Lists from Early Christianity (Oxford, 2017), the standard reference work on these lists — concludes that when the ancient canon lists, Greek or Latin, name "two books of Esdras," they mean Esdras A and Esdras B: the Greek 1 Esdras and Ezra–Nehemiah-as-one, not Ezra and Nehemiah counted apart. The argument rests on manuscript fact: in the surviving Greek pandect Bibles and the Old Latin, Greek Esdras always stands as Esdras A and the whole of Ezra–Nehemiah as Esdras B, and Latin manuscripts begin dividing Ezra from Nehemiah into two books only from about the tenth century. So a fourth-century African council enumerating "two books of Esdras" could not have meant the later Ezra/Nehemiah split — that division did not yet exist in the Latin Bibles it used. Jerome himself attests the older state of affairs: Ezra and Nehemiah "are united in a single book," he writes, so the two Esdras books "among Greeks and Latins" are Esdras A and Esdras B.5

Augustine — the hand behind the African lists — cites the Greek Esdras A as Scripture. The decisive corroboration is that the very theologian who drove the African councils treats the material unique to Esdras A as potentially prophetic Scripture. In The City of God 18.36 — the chapter titled "About Esdras and the Books of the Maccabees" — Augustine raises whether Esdras prophesied of Christ "in that passage where, on a question having arisen among certain young men as to what is the strongest thing, when one had said kings, another wine, the third women… yet that same third youth demonstrated that the truth is victorious over all. For by consulting the Gospel we learn that Christ is the Truth." That contest of the three guardsmen occurs only in the Greek 1 Esdras (Esdras A 3–4 = Latin 3 Esdras) — it is absent from canonical Ezra and Nehemiah. So Augustine, whose own canon (On Christian Doctrine 2.8) matches the African lists book for book, reads as scriptural a book Trent does not list. The same pattern holds across the Latin fathers, whose citations of "the book of Ezra" are overwhelmingly to Esdras A.6

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The factual premise is sound, and on the strongest evidence available it is the Catholic harmonization, not White's reading, that strains against the manuscripts. The African councils almost certainly counted the Greek 1 Esdras (Esdras A) among their "two books of Esdras"; Trent's enumeration names only Ezra and Nehemiah and silently passes over 3 Esdras; the Clementine Vulgate of 1592 then relegated 3 and 4 Esdras and the Prayer of Manasseh to a non-canonical appendix. So the enumerated lists are not identical, and the book the Africans counted that Trent's defined canon does not is precisely the Greek 1 Esdras. White's specific mechanism — Septuagint Esdras A + B versus the Vulgate's renumbered split — is correct, and tracks the specialist consensus.7

What is overstated. Two qualifications bound what the premise actually delivers — and both belong to the use the argument is put to, not to the bare fact of difference.

  1. "Trent excludes" is stronger than what Trent did. Trent did not condemn or formally reject 3 Esdras. The council had the wider Latin appendix (3 and 4 Esdras, the Prayer of Manasseh) under consideration and chose to pass over it in silence: only a small minority of bishops voted to reject those books explicitly, and the overwhelming majority withheld any decision, leaving the decree to define the protocanonical core without ruling the appendix in or out. So Trent's defined list omits the Greek 1 Esdras — it is not among the books received as "sacred and canonical" — but the council did not anathematize it. Gary Michuta presses exactly this: the Africans, too, "passed over in silence; they didn't pass judgment" on the precise Esdras enumeration, so the difference is one of under-specification, not opposed verdicts. That move bears on whether the lists contradict (the separate question), but it does not erase the difference in the lists themselves.8

  2. Non-identity is not contradiction, and the proponents trade on the slide. White frames the difference as a dilemma — "which popes were right and which were wrong?" — and the implication is that the Catholic appeal to Hippo and Carthage fails or even backfires. But the bare fact that two catalogues differ over a boundary book does not by itself show the councils conflict: a later council that names the protocanonical core and leaves a disputed unit unaddressed has narrowed an earlier, looser reckoning, not reversed it. Whether that narrowing is a genuine contradiction — and what it does to claims of conciliar consistency — is weighed separately. On the factual premise alone, White is right that the lists are not identical; the polemical freight he attaches to that fact is a different argument with a different (and weaker) result.9

Assessment

Sound. On the leading scholarly reading — Bogaert, followed by Gallagher and Meade in the standard reference work on the early canon lists — the "two books of Esdras" ratified at Hippo and Carthage (393–419) comprised the Greek 1 Esdras (Esdras A / Latin 3 Esdras) and Ezra–Nehemiah-as-one, because the Latin division of Ezra from Nehemiah does not appear until roughly the tenth century and so was unavailable to a fourth-century African council. Augustine, the hand behind those lists, cites the material unique to Esdras A as possible prophecy of Christ (City of God 18.36). Trent's 1546 enumeration, working from the Vulgate, names only Ezra and Nehemiah and passes the Greek 1 Esdras over in silence; the Clementine Vulgate then fixed it outside the canon. So the two enumerations are genuinely not identical, and the book that separates them is the Greek 1 Esdras — the premise holds, and it is the apologetic harmonization that runs against the manuscripts. What the premise does not by itself establish is two further things its proponents tend to load onto it: that Trent rejected 3 Esdras (it withheld judgment rather than condemning it), and that the lists therefore contradict so that the Catholic appeal to the African councils collapses (non-identity over an unadjudicated boundary book is not yet conflict). Flag on the proponents' framing the slide from the sound "the lists are not identical" to the further "so the councils contradict / the appeal backfires," which is a distinct claim assessed on its own.
