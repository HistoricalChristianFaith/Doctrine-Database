Aphrahat the Persian Sage on the Old Testament Canon

Dates: c. 337 AD (Aphrahat, also spelled Aphraates, “the Persian Sage” — a Syriac-speaking ascetic and bishop in the Sasanian Persian empire, east of the Roman frontier, c. 270–345; his twenty-three Demonstrations are the earliest substantial body of Syriac Christian theology, the first group, including Demonstration 5, Of Wars, dated by their author to AD 337) · Wikipedia · back to timeline
Summary: The first major theologian of the Syriac East, writing beyond the Roman world, treats the Maccabean history as sacred, prophecy-fulfilling Scripture. In Demonstration 5 (Of Wars) he reads the persecution under Antiochus Epiphanes — the martyrdom of Eleazar and “the sons of the blessed Samuna, seven in number,” and the worm-eaten death of the tyrant — as the very events foretold by Daniel and lamented in the Psalms, and he sees Antiochus’s rotting end (the death narrated in 2 Maccabees 9) as the heaven-sent judgment Daniel had prophesied, with Isaiah’s “worm Jacob” supplying the irony of the worms that devoured him. He draws up no canon-list and prefaces the Maccabean material with no “it is written” formula, so his witness is the softer, typological-and-historical kind — but it adds the Syriac/Eastern tradition, geographically distinct from the Greek and Latin witnesses, to the “using” side of the divide on the eve of Cyril’s Hebrew-sized list.

Claims & evidence

He reads the Maccabean martyrdoms as the fulfillment of Psalm and prophecy

And he slew righteous men and gave them to the birds of heaven and to the beasts of the earth. For in his days was fulfilled the word that David spoke: O God, the Gentiles have come into your inheritance, and have defiled Your holy temple. They have made Jerusalem desolate. They have given the dead bodies of Your servants as food to the birds of heaven, and the flesh of Your righteous ones to the beasts of the earth. They have poured out their blood like water round about Jerusalem, and there is none to bury them. For this was accomplished at that time, when the venerable and aged Eleazar was slain, and the sons of the blessed Samuna, seven in number, and when Judas (Maccabeus) and his brethren were struggling on behalf of their people, when they were dwelling in hiding-places.

— Demonstrations 5 (Of Wars), §20, in the Gwynn translation, Nicene and Post-Nicene Fathers, 2nd series, vol. 13, source.1 Aphrahat takes Psalm 79 (“O God, the Gentiles have come into your inheritance…”) and declares it “fulfilled” not in some other event but precisely “when the venerable and aged Eleazar was slain, and the sons of the blessed Samuna, seven in number” — the aged scribe Eleazar of 2 Maccabees 6:18–31 and the mother and her seven sons of 2 Maccabees 7 (named in the Syriac tradition for the mother, “Shmuni”) — alongside “Judas (Maccabeus) and his brethren.” The Maccabean martyr-history is handled as authoritative sacred history: the very record in which the Psalm finds its fulfillment.

He reads Antiochus’s worm-eaten death (2 Maccabees 9) as the judgment Daniel foretold

Again he said: The judgment was set and they took away his authority from him, to injure and destroy him until the end of the kingdom. For the judgment came upon Antiochus, a judgment from heaven; and he became sick with a grievous and evil sickness, and on account of the smell of him as he rotted, no man could approach him, for worms were crawling and falling from him and eating his flesh because he oppressed the worm Jacob. And his flesh rotted in his lifetime, because he caused the dead bodies of the sons of Jerusalem to rot and they were not buried.

— Demonstrations 5 (Of Wars), §20, source.2 The whole section is built on Daniel 7: Aphrahat identifies Antiochus with the “little horn” that “humbled three kings” and “exalted himself against the saints of the Most High” (Daniel 7:8, 21, 24–25), and he reads “The judgment was set and they took away his authority from him… until the end of the kingdom” (Daniel 7:26) as the sentence executed in the tyrant’s death. That death — sickness, an unbearable stench, worms breeding in and falling from the living body — is the worm-eaten end narrated in 2 Maccabees 9:5–12, here taken as plain history and woven into the prophetic argument. Isaiah’s “worm Jacob” (Isaiah 41:14) supplies the retributive irony: the worms devour him “because he oppressed the worm Jacob.” In a single breath Aphrahat treats the Maccabean narrative as the realization of Daniel’s vision — deuterocanonical history reckoned as prophecy come true.

Notes

  • Use, not a canon-list; historical-typological, not a Scripture-formula. Like Clement of Rome with “the blessed Judith,” Aphrahat adduces the Maccabean material without an explicit “it is written” or “the Scripture says,” and he enumerates no Old-Testament books. His witness is therefore softer than the explicit-formula citations of Clement of Alexandria or Cyprian: it rests on his treating the Maccabean martyrdoms and Antiochus’s death as authentic sacred history in which Psalm and prophecy are “fulfilled.” That is real evidential weight — the history is woven seamlessly with Daniel and the Psalms, undifferentiated from them — but it is the weight of use and prophetic fulfillment, not of a canon-verdict.
  • A Syriac/Eastern witness. Aphrahat’s value on this timeline is geographic breadth. He wrote in Syriac, inside the Sasanian Persian empire, outside the Roman world that produced the Greek and Latin witnesses gathered here. That the disputed Maccabean books were being read as prophecy-fulfilling history this far east — the same instinct seen in the Greek Dionysius and Methodius and the Latin Cyprian and Lactantius — shows the “using” practice was not confined to one linguistic tradition. (The later Syriac Peshitta Old Testament would itself carry several deuterocanonical books.)
  • Bears on the canon argument. He renders no canon-verdict, but he is a datapoint in the argument “Did the early church reject the deuterocanon…?” (assessed contested): a fourth-century Eastern voice, contemporary with Cyril’s Hebrew-sized list, treating Maccabean history as authoritative sacred narrative against the flat claim that the primitive church did not reckon these books Scripture.
