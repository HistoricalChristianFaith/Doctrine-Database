Augustine of Hippo on the Old Testament Canon

Dates: c. 397 (On Christian Doctrine 2.8.13) · 354–430 · Wikipedia · the African councils he stood behind · back to timeline
Verdict on the deuterocanon: YES — the full longer canon, on the breadth of its reception. Augustine, bishop of Hippo and the theological hand behind the African synods of Hippo and Carthage, sets out his own canon in On Christian Doctrine (c. 397) and names Tobias, Judith, the two books of Maccabees, and the two of Ezra flatly among the Scriptures, with Wisdom and Ecclesiasticus reckoned among the prophetical books. He does not rank them a tier below the rest, as Athanasius and Jerome did; he weighs a book by the breadth of its reception across the catholic churches — and on that test the deuterocanon stands inside the canon. A generation later, in The City of God, he frankly grants that the Maccabees are held canonical "not by the Jews, but by the Church."

Claims & evidence

The canon-list: a forty-four-book Old Testament with the deuterocanon inside it (c. 397)

In Book II of On Christian Doctrine, instructing the reader how to handle Scripture, Augustine runs out the whole Old Testament. He files Tobias, Judith, the two Maccabees, and the two of Ezra among the historical books, and sets Wisdom and Ecclesiasticus among the prophetical — the longer Septuagint canon, with no second rank:1

There are other books which seem to follow no regular order, and are connected neither with the order of the preceding books nor with one another, such as Job, and Tobias, and Esther, and Judith, and the two books of Maccabees, and the two of Ezra… Next are the Prophets, in which there is one book of the Psalms of David; and three books of Solomon, viz., Proverbs, Song of Songs, and Ecclesiastes. For two books, one called Wisdom and the other Ecclesiasticus, are ascribed to Solomon from a certain resemblance of style… Still they are to be reckoned among the prophetical books, since they have attained recognition as being authoritative… The authority of the Old Testament is contained within the limits of these forty-four books.

— On Christian Doctrine II.8.13, source. The list matches the canon the African councils promulgated, book for book — which is no coincidence, since Augustine was the driving theological figure at those synods.

The criterion: prefer what is received by all the catholic churches

What carries the disputed books into Augustine's canon is not a Hebrew enumeration but a rule of reception: the more widely and anciently a book is received, the higher its authority. He states the standard immediately before the list:2

Now, in regard to the canonical Scriptures, he must follow the judgment of the greater number of catholic churches… he will judge according to the following standard: to prefer those that are received by all the catholic churches to those which some do not receive. Among those, again, which are not received by all, he will prefer such as have the sanction of the greater number and those of greater authority, to such as are held by the smaller number and those of less authority.

— On Christian Doctrine II.8.12, source. This is the opposite test from Jerome's "Hebraica veritas": where Jerome measured the canon against the Hebrew the Jews kept, Augustine measures it against the breadth of the church's use. The two criteria yield the two canons, and the disagreement between these contemporaries is the fault line that runs to Trent and the Reformation.

The Maccabees: "held as canonical, not by the Jews, but by the Church" (c. 425)

Late in life, in The City of God, Augustine confronts the very objection the Reformers would later press — that the Maccabees are not in the Hebrew Scriptures — and answers it head-on. He concedes the historical point and turns it into a doctrine of churchly reception:3

the reckoning of their dates is found, not in the Holy Scriptures which are called canonical, but in others, among which are also the books of the Maccabees. These are held as canonical, not by the Jews, but by the Church, on account of the extreme and wonderful sufferings of certain martyrs, who, before Christ had come in the flesh, contended for the law of God even unto death, and endured most grievous and horrible evils.

— The City of God XVIII.36, source. The admission is candid: Augustine knows the Maccabees stand outside the Jewish reckoning. His canon rests not on that reckoning but on the church's reception — the same criterion stated in On Christian Doctrine, now applied to the single most contested book.

Notes

  • The theological hand behind the councils. Augustine was a priest at Hippo in 393 and bishop by 397, the driving figure of the North-African synods whose canon-lists match his own book for book. His witness and the conciliar lists are two forms of one verdict: the reception-criterion stated by a theologian, and the same canon enacted by a council.
  • Augustine against Jerome. The two greatest Latin Fathers of the age took opposite sides. Jerome, translating iuxta Hebraeos, set Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, and the Maccabees "among the apocrypha"; Augustine, weighing reception, kept them in the canon. Their exchange of letters over the translation of Scripture is the personal face of a disagreement that the Western church never fully resolved until Trent took Augustine's side and the Reformation took Jerome's.
  • The same witness, several doctrines. Augustine also retells the origin of the very Greek Bible that carried these books — see his page on the Origin of the Septuagint — and one of the disputed books he keeps, 2 Maccabees, supplies the classic proof-text for prayer for the dead, a practice he defends on his purgatory page. His canon and his doctrine pull in the same direction: the longer Old Testament, received from the Greek church, carries the practices the Reformation would contest along with the books.
