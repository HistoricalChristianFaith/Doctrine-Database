The Complutensian Polyglot (Cardinal Ximénez) on the Old Testament Canon

Dates: Old Testament volumes printed at Alcalá de Henares (Complutum) 1514–17, sanctioned by Pope Leo X (privilege traditionally dated 22 March 1520), published c. 1522; edited under Cardinal Francisco Jiménez de Cisneros, called Ximénez (c. 1436–1517) · Wikipedia
Verdict on the deuterocanon: NO — a graded distinction in Jerome's mould. The first printed polyglot Bible, a Catholic cardinal's project carried out with papal sanction, sets the books outside the Hebrew canon apart from the rest: its preface professes that the Church receives them "for the edification of the people" rather than "for confirming the authority of ecclesiastical dogmas," and it marks them on the page by printing them without a Hebrew column. It is a witness that the Hebrew-canon distinction was still voiced at the highest level of the Latin Church on the eve of Trent — but the papal-approval half of the popular apologetic point needs care: Leo X licensed a printing, he did not define a canon.

Claims & evidence

The preface Ad lectorem: the books "outside the canon," received for edification, not for confirming doctrine

At vero libri extra canonem, quos Ecclesia potius ad aedificationem populi quam ad autoritatem ecclesiasticorum dogmatum confirmandam recipit, Graecam tantum habent scripturam, sed cum duplici latina interpretatione, altera beati Hieronymi, altera interlineari de verbo ad verbum, eo modo quo in caeteris.

— Complutensian Polyglot Bible, second preface Ad lectorem ("To the Reader"), c. 1517; facsimile at the Internet Archive.1 In English: "But the books outside the canon, which the Church receives for the edification of the people rather than for confirming the authority of ecclesiastical dogmas, have only the Greek text, but with a double Latin translation — one of blessed Jerome's, the other interlinear, word for word, in the manner of the rest." The sentence is doing two things at once. It states a rank — these books are "outside the canon," received to edify rather than to establish doctrine — and it explains a layout: because they have no Hebrew original, they are printed in Greek with a double Latin column instead of the three-column Hebrew/Latin/Greek arrangement used for the canonical books. The verdict is carried as much by the typography as by the words: a reader could see which books fell "outside the canon" by the absence of the Hebrew.

Jerome's formula, not a fresh ruling

The wording is not the editors' own coinage. "Received… for the edification of the people, not for confirming the authority of ecclesiastical dogmas" is lifted almost verbatim from Jerome's preface to the books of Solomon, where he says the Church reads Wisdom and Sirach "for the edifying of the people, not for confirming the authority of ecclesiastical dogmas" (ad aedificationem plebis, non ad auctoritatem ecclesiasticorum dogmatum confirmandam).2 The Complutensian editors are consciously placing their edition in the Hieronymian line that ran through the medieval Glossa Ordinaria and would be voiced again, on the very eve of Trent, by Cardinal Cajetan. This is the same graded "No" as Rufinus's "Ecclesiastical, not Canonical" and Athanasius's "read, but not in the canon" — the books kept and honoured, but ranked a step below the Scriptures used to prove doctrine.

What the preface does not say: it names no books

A long apologetic tradition — repeated by Alexander McCaul and many after him — reports that Ximénez "in the preface… admonishes the reader that Judith, Tobit, Wisdom, Ecclesiasticus, Maccabees, with the additions to Esther and Daniel… are not canonical Scriptures."3 That is a fair summary of the effect, but not of the text: the prologue does not enumerate the disputed books one by one. It draws the line around a whole class — "the books outside the canon" — and lets the printing (Greek and Latin, no Hebrew) show which titles fall on the far side of it. The distinction matters for honesty: the Polyglot witnesses to a canon-principle (the Hebrew reckoning, in Jerome's grade), not to a name-by-name canon list.

Papal sanction — a privilege to print, not a definition of the canon

The rhetorical force of the case is that a cardinal's Bible, bearing a pope's approval, carried the shorter-canon distinction only a generation before Trent anathematized anyone who denied the deuterocanon. The fact is real, but its scope is narrower than the slogan. Leo X granted a printing privilege for the work (the date usually given is 22 March 1520; some scholars question it, since the presentation copy did not enter the Vatican Library until December 1521).4 Approving the publication of a scholarly masterpiece is not the same act as ratifying every critical note inside it. The Catholic apologist Gary Michuta concedes the first while denying the second:

[He] approved the Complutensian Polyglot, which isn't necessarily a dogmatic declaration of whatever opinions were in that book… he approved the work… without passing judgment on whatever critical notes were attached to those books.

— Gary Michuta, cross-examined by James White, The Great Debate IX (20 May 2004).5 Michuta adds that Ximénez's position was licit before Trent only because the canon "had not yet been defined" — and that "anathemas don't work in reverse": Trent's 1546 penalty cannot retroactively condemn a view held while the question was open. From the Protestant side James White presses the same facts to the opposite conclusion — that the Polyglot, "approved for publication by Pope Leo X… clearly and without apology rejected the apocryphal books as fully canonical, following the lead of Jerome."6 Both are right about the datum and disagree only about its weight: the Polyglot proves the distinction was still respectable and unpenalized in the Latin Church c. 1520; it does not prove any pope taught the shorter canon.

A canon still fluid: the Polyglot printed books Trent would drop

One further detail cuts against reading the Polyglot as an early Protestant canon. Its Greek Old Testament printed books that Trent's 1546 list would not include — 3 Esdras (Greek Esdras A) and 3 Maccabees, both carried in the Septuagint tradition; in the same debate Michuta notes the work ran "beyond even the Council of Trent" (naming 3 and 4 Maccabees).7 A Bible that both ranks the deuterocanon below the Hebrew books and prints texts wider than Trent's eventual canon is following a scholarly, reception-based instinct about a still-unsettled edge — Jerome's distinction applied by learned editors — not a fixed dogmatic list of either the Tridentine or the Reformation kind.

Notes

  • Where it sits on the timeline. Between the ecumenical "Yes" of Florence (1442) and Luther's printed "No" (1534). Florence had leveled the deuterocanon into the canon; the Complutensian, printed within Florence's own church and under a later pope, still voiced the graded Hieronymian distinction Florence had not silenced — the same undercurrent Cardinal Cajetan would restate in his 1532 commentaries. It is a standing counter-example to the claim that the shorter canon was a Protestant novelty.
  • Ximénez the man. Francisco Jiménez de Cisneros (c. 1436–1517), Archbishop of Toledo, Grand Inquisitor, and twice regent of Castile, founded the University of Alcalá and financed the Polyglot from his own revenues. He died in November 1517, months after the Old Testament volumes were printed and years before the work was published — so the "cardinal's Bible" was compiled under his direction but sanctioned and released after his death.
  • Keep the two halves apart. The strong, verifiable claim is that the Polyglot's preface set the deuterocanon "outside the canon" in Jerome's sense. The weaker, over-pressed claim is that "Pope Leo X approved the shorter canon." He approved the printing of a work that contained the distinction; he passed no judgment on the canon question, which Rome would define only at Trent. The honest witness is to the persistence of the distinction, not to a papal endorsement of it.
  • Translation and text provenance. The Latin of the preface is given from the facsimile of the first edition (Alcalá, 1514–17/1522) at the Internet Archive; the English renderings here are literal. The parallel to Jerome is to his Prologue to the Books of Solomon in the Vulgate prefaces.
