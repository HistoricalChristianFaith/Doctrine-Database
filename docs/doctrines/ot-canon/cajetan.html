Cardinal Cajetan (Thomas de Vio) on the Old Testament Canon

Dates: Thomas de Vio, called Cajetan (Gaetano), c. 1469–1534 — Dominican theologian, Master General of the order, cardinal (1517), and papal legate; the man who examined Luther at Augsburg in October 1518. The witness here is his In omnes authenticos Veteris Testamenti historiales libros commentarii ("Commentary on all the authentic historical books of the Old Testament"), dedicated to Pope Clement VII and published in 1532, slotted on the timeline at that year · Wikipedia
Verdict on the deuterocanon: NO — a graded distinction in Jerome's mould. Closing his commentary at the end of Esther, Cajetan follows Jerome expressly: Judith, Tobit, the Maccabees, Wisdom, and Sirach are "counted out of the canonical books" and stand among the apocrypha; the words of any council or Father that calls them canonical are "to be reduced to the correction of Jerome." They are canonical only in the second of two senses — a rule "for the edification of the faithful," not "for confirming matters of faith." Fourteen years before Trent anathematized that very position, one of Rome's most eminent theologians — and Luther's own interrogator — still held the shorter-canon distinction.

Claims & evidence

The comment at the end of Esther: two senses of "canonical," and Jerome as the measure

Et hoc in loco terminamus commentaria librorum historialium Veteris Testamenti. Nam reliqui (videlicet Judith, Tobiae, et Machabaeorum libri) a divo Hieronymo extra canonicos libros supputantur, et inter apocrypha locantur, cum Sapientia et Ecclesiastico, ut patet in Prologo Galeato. Nec turberis, novitie, si alicubi reperies libros istos inter canonicos supputari, vel in sacris conciliis vel in sacris doctoribus. Nam ad Hieronymi limam reducenda sunt tam verba conciliorum quam doctorum; et juxta illius sententiam, ad Chromatium et Heliodorum episcopos, libri isti (et si qui alii sunt in canone Bibliae similes) non sunt canonici, id est, non sunt regulares ad firmandum ea quae sunt fidei; possunt tamen dici canonici, id est, regulares ad aedificationem fidelium, utpote in canone Bibliae ad hoc recepti et auctorati. Cum hac distinctione discernere poteris dicta Augustini, et scripta in Provinciali Concilio Carthaginensi.

— Thomas de Vio (Cajetan), In omnes authenticos Veteris Testamenti historiales libros commentarii (1532), comment on the last chapter of Esther (In ult. cap. Esther, ad fin.).1 In William Whitaker's standard English: "Here we close our commentaries on the historical books of the old Testament. For the rest (that is, Judith, Tobit, and the books of Maccabees) are counted by St Jerome out of the canonical books, and are placed amongst the Apocrypha, along with Wisdom and Ecclesiasticus, as is plain from the Prologus Galeatus. Nor be thou disturbed, like a raw scholar, if thou shouldest find any where, either in the sacred councils or the sacred doctors, these books reckoned as canonical. For the words as well of councils as of doctors are to be reduced to the correction of Jerome. Now, according to his judgment, in the epistle to the bishops Chromatius and Heliodorus, these books (and any other like books in the canon of the bible) are not canonical, that is, not in the nature of a rule for confirming matters of faith. Yet, they may be called canonical, that is, in the nature of a rule for the edification of the faithful, as being received and authorised in the canon of the bible for that purpose. By the help of this distinction thou mayest see thy way clearly through that which Augustine says, and what is written in the provincial council of Carthage."2

What the passage does and does not settle

Two moves in Cajetan's words are worth marking. First, he makes Jerome the interpretive rule: wherever a council (the provincial council of Carthage) or a Father (Augustine) calls these books canonical, "the words as well of councils as of doctors are to be reduced to the correction of Jerome" — i.e. read through Jerome's distinction, not against it. This is the same subordination of the African-conciliar "Yes" to Jerome's grade that runs through the Complutensian Polyglot's preface and, before it, the medieval Glossa Ordinaria. Second, he does not simply strike the books out: they "may be called canonical" in a real if lower sense — "regular for the edification of the faithful," received into the canon of the Bible for that purpose. This is the two-tier reading, not a bare rejection: the deuterocanon is Scripture read in church, but not the Scripture from which one proves a disputed article of faith. It is the graded "No" of Jerome, Rufinus ("Ecclesiastical, not Canonical"), and Athanasius ("read, but not in the canon"), voiced by a cardinal on the very eve of Trent.

Cajetan's proto-/deutero-canonical language

Whitaker, quoting the passage, notes that Cajetan himself reached for the vocabulary later editions would standardise: he adopts the terms proto-canonical and deutero-canonical (which Whitaker traces to Sixtus of Siena and Thomas Stapleton), the first-order books being "those which are counted in the legitimate and genuine canon — that is, of the Hebrews."3 The label "deuterocanonical" that Catholic usage now applies to Tobit, Judith, and the rest thus began, in part, as a way of marking exactly the second-rank status Cajetan is here defending — a two-tier canon, not a flat one.

Notes

  • Where it sits on the timeline. Between the Complutensian Polyglot (printed 1514–17, published 1522) and Luther's printed "No" (1534), and fourteen years before the Council of Trent (1546) closed the question the other way. Cajetan, the Complutensian, and the Glossa Ordinaria together form the "pre-Trent Catholic shorter-canon" strand: the Hieronymian distinction was a live, unpenalized Catholic option right up to the Tridentine definition.
  • The man. Cajetan was no marginal figure. As Master General of the Dominicans and a cardinal he was among the leading theologians of his age; his commentary on Aquinas's Summa is still printed with the text. It was Cajetan whom Rome sent to interview Luther at Augsburg in 1518. That his shorter-canon view could be published in 1532, dedicated to the reigning pope, without censure is the point the Protestant case presses: the distinction was respectable in Rome until Trent removed the option.
  • Reconciling with the Trent page. The Council of Trent witness page already notes that "on the very eve of Trent, Cardinal Cajetan had restated the old Hieronymian view, ranking the deuterocanon for edification below the protocanon." This page supplies the primary text that footnote asserted — Cajetan's own words at the end of Esther — and shows the two-sense distinction verbatim.
  • Not a lone contrarian, and not read here as anti-Catholic. The point of the witness is historical, not polemical: it establishes that the graded "No" survived at the top of the Latin Church into the 1530s. Cajetan wrote before the canon was dogmatically defined; a Catholic could hold his view then and could not after 1546. That the same distinction appears in Jerome, the Glossa, the Complutensian editors, and Cajetan is what makes it a tradition rather than an eccentricity — and what the Tridentine minority who wished to keep Jerome's two tiers were appealing to.
  • Text and translation provenance. The Latin is given as transcribed in the footnotes of William Whitaker's A Disputation on Holy Scripture (Parker Society, 1849), which reproduces Cajetan's comment in ult. cap. Esther; the original stands in Cajetan's In omnes authenticos Veteris Testamenti historiales libros commentarii (1532). The English is Whitaker's own rendering, the version most often cited.
