Melito of Sardis on the Old Testament Canon

Dates: c. 170 AD (bishop of Sardis; the canon list is preserved in a letter to Onesimus quoted by Eusebius) · Wikipedia
Verdict on the deuterocanon: NO. Melito's letter to Onesimus carries the earliest surviving Christian list of the Old Testament books, and it is a Hebrew-sized canon: Tobit, Judith, Sirach, Baruch, and 1–2 Maccabees are all absent (so too Esther). The one apparent exception — "Wisdom" — is, on the usual reading, a second name for Proverbs, not the deuterocanonical Book of Wisdom. The list ends with "Esdras."

Claims & evidence

The earliest Christian Old Testament list

Melito, bishop of Sardis in Asia Minor, travelled "to the East" — to the land of the Scriptures — to settle for his correspondent Onesimus exactly which books make up the Old Testament and in what order. His report, preserved by Eusebius in the Ecclesiastical History (iv.26.13–14, written c. 324 AD), is the oldest extant catalogue of the Christian Old Testament.1 Melito frames it as the fruit of careful inquiry, not received convention:

"Accordingly when I went East and came to the place where these things were preached and done, I learned accurately the books of the Old Testament, and send them to thee as written below. Their names are as follows: Of Moses, five books: Genesis, Exodus, Numbers, Leviticus, Deuteronomy; Jesus Nave, Judges, Ruth; of Kings, four books; of Chronicles, two; the Psalms of David, the Proverbs of Solomon, Wisdom also, Ecclesiastes, Song of Songs, Job; of Prophets, Isaiah, Jeremiah; of the twelve prophets, one book; Daniel, Ezekiel, Esdras. From which also I have made the extracts, dividing them into six books."

The deuterocanon is absent

Run the list against the disputed books and the verdict is plain: no Tobit, no Judith, no Sirach (Ecclesiasticus), no Baruch, no 1–2 Maccabees. Esther is missing as well — an omission Melito shares with several later Hebrew-minded Fathers — and the catalogue closes at "Esdras" (Ezra, with Nehemiah reckoned within it). This is a Hebrew-sized canon, very close to the books of the later Jewish Bible, reached by a Christian bishop inquiring at the source roughly a century and a half before any council took up the question.2

"Wisdom" here means Proverbs, not the Book of Wisdom

The single word that could be read as a deuterocanonical book is "Wisdom also," set immediately after "the Proverbs of Solomon." On the standard reading this is not the deuterocanonical Wisdom of Solomon but an alternate ancient title for Proverbs itself: Proverbs was anciently called "the all-virtuous Wisdom" (ἡ πανάρετος Σοφία), and Eusebius elsewhere records that several early writers used "Wisdom" as a name for the same book. So "the Proverbs of Solomon, Wisdom also" most likely names one book under two titles — leaving Melito's list with no deuterocanonical work at all.3 (A minority take "Wisdom" as the separate Book of Wisdom; even so, the rest of the deuterocanon is still absent, and the verdict on Tobit — this timeline's bellwether — is unaffected.)

Notes

  • Reception, not composition. Melito stands at the head of the long line of Fathers who, knowing the Hebrew Scriptures, counted a narrower canon than the Greek Septuagint the church read in worship — the same pattern later voiced by Origen, Cyril of Jerusalem, Athanasius, and Jerome further down this timeline. The deuterocanon was inside the church's working Greek Bible from the outset; witnesses like Melito show that its canonical standing was already weighed against the Hebrew list this early.
  • Inquiry into the Hebrew enumeration — describing, not prescribing. Melito did not relay a received Christian list; he travelled "East… to the place where these things were preached" and "learned accurately" the books. He inquired, and the natural answer to Onesimus's question — which books make up the Old Testament — is a report of the Hebrew reckoning he found at the source, not a distinctively Christian ruling that dogmatically excludes the deuterocanon. As the earliest list this matters: it attests how the books were counted by the Jews more than it pronounces a settled church verdict, which narrows how much a Protestant-canon case can rest on it. Two counter-pulls keep the point from over-reaching: commending a list to a Christian inquirer who asked carries endorsing force, not neutral stenography; and the list is itself lightly Christianized — its order is not the Jewish tripartite arrangement, and "Wisdom" (on the minority reading that it is the Book of Wisdom rather than a second name for Proverbs) is no part of the Jewish table — so it is not a pure transcription of the synagogue's canon. The same descriptive cast marks Origen's list ("as the Hebrews have handed them down"); it does not carry to the openly prescriptive Cyril, Athanasius, and Jerome further down this timeline.4
  • A substantial Jewish community on his doorstep. Melito's Sardis was home to one of the largest and most prominent Jewish communities of the Greco-Roman Diaspora — a fact sometimes pressed against any picture of a bishop travelling East for want of any local access to the Jewish reckoning. Two cautions keep it honest. First, dating: the monumental synagogue unearthed at Sardis — the largest yet found outside the land of Israel — belongs, as it survives, to a later phase, acquired and converted by the Jewish community around the late third / turn of the fourth century; it is not a second-century building and must not be retrojected onto Melito's own day (fl. c. 170). What can honestly be said of his day rests on earlier evidence — Josephus preserves decrees of c. 50–48 BC recognizing a long-established, civically-recognized Jewish assembly at Sardis "from the beginning."5 Second, proximity is not welcome: by Melito's day Jewish–Christian relations at Sardis were strained — his own paschal homily On Pascha (Peri Pascha) is fiercely supersessionist — so learned Jews near at hand guaranteed no cordial exchange, which is itself a reason a bishop would travel to Palestine to settle the count at its source. The community's presence complicates the "he inquired out of sheer ignorance" reading without settling which books those Jews received — context bearing on the inquiry argument, decisive for neither side.6
  • Why Eusebius is the source. Melito's own works survive only in fragments; the canon list reaches us because Eusebius quoted the letter to Onesimus verbatim. The quotation is firsthand to Melito's words, transmitted through Eusebius — the citation below is to Eusebius's text.
