Cyril of Jerusalem on the Old Testament Canon

Dates: c. 350 AD (the Old Testament list is in Catechetical Lecture 4.33–37, the baptismal catechesis delivered at Jerusalem c. 348–350; Cyril c. 313–386) · Wikipedia · back to timeline · his New Testament canon page
Verdict on the deuterocanon: NO — with one bundled exception. Catechizing the candidates for baptism, Cyril gives an Eastern Old-Testament list of twenty-two books, the Hebrew-letter count. Tobit, Judith, Sirach, the Wisdom of Solomon, and 1–2 Maccabees are all absent, and he charges the catechumen to "have nothing to do with the apocryphal writings." Yet his Jeremiah carries Baruch and the Epistle of Jeremiah folded in — a deuterocanonical text kept inside a canonical book — and he credits the whole Hebrew-sized canon to the Septuagint's seventy-two translators. A near-neighbour Eastern witness to the Hebrew-canon pattern of Melito and Origen, a generation before Athanasius.

Claims & evidence

The twenty-two books — "study earnestly these only which we read openly in the Church"

In the fourth Catechetical Lecture, instructing the baptismal candidates "Of the Divine Scriptures," Cyril recites the Old Testament book by book and fixes the count at twenty-two, the number of the Hebrew letters:1

Of these read the two and twenty books, but have nothing to do with the apocryphal writings. Study earnestly these only which we read openly in the Church… And of the Old Testament, as we have said, study the two and twenty books… For of the Law the books of Moses are the first five, Genesis, Exodus, Leviticus, Numbers, Deuteronomy. And next, Joshua the son of Nave, and the book of Judges, including Ruth, counted as seventh. And of the other historical books, the first and second books of the Kings are among the Hebrews one book; also the third and fourth one book. And in like manner, the first and second of Chronicles are with them one book; and the first and second of Esdras are counted one. Esther is the twelfth book; and these are the Historical writings. But those which are written in verses are five, Job, and the book of Psalms, and Proverbs, and Ecclesiastes, and the Song of Songs, which is the seventeenth book. And after these come the five Prophetic books: of the Twelve Prophets one book, of Isaiah one, of Jeremiah one, including Baruch and Lamentations and the Epistle; then Ezekiel, and the Book of Daniel, the twenty-second of the Old Testament.

The deuterocanon is absent

Measured against the disputed books the verdict is plain: no Tobit, no Judith, no Sirach, no Wisdom of Solomon, no Maccabees. Cyril's twenty-two are the protocanonical Hebrew books (Ruth folded into Judges, the paired books of Kings, Chronicles, and Esdras each counted as one), and he draws the line sharply — "have nothing to do with the apocryphal writings," "study earnestly these only which we read openly in the Church."1 This is the same Hebrew-sized canon Melito had set and Origen had counted, and that Athanasius and Jerome would carry further down this timeline.

The bundled exception — "Jeremiah… including Baruch and… the Epistle"

Cyril's list is not perfectly clean of the Greek extras. His single book of Jeremiah is reckoned "including Baruch and Lamentations and the Epistle" — folding in both Baruch and the Epistle of Jeremiah (Baruch ch. 6 in the Vulgate numbering), two short deuterocanonical works, not as separate books but as appendices to Jeremiah, exactly as the Greek manuscripts transmitted them.1 So even a Father formally counting "the two and twenty books" carried a deuterocanonical text inside his canon — the same gesture by which Origen bundled "the epistle" with Jeremiah, and a sign of how the church's Greek Bible shaped the lists of those who preferred the Hebrew count. Baruch's place inside Jeremiah will recur all down the Eastern lists — Athanasius and Epiphanius keep it likewise.

A Hebrew count credited to the Septuagint

Cyril gives the Hebrew-sized canon, but he ascribes it to the Greek translation: just before reciting the list he retells the Letter of Aristeas legend — Ptolemy's seventy-two interpreters, each in a separate chamber on Pharos, producing translations that "agreed not only in the sense but even in words," so that "the translation of the Divine Scriptures, spoken by the Holy Ghost, was of the Holy Ghost accomplished" — and bids the catechumen "read the Divine Scriptures, the twenty-two books of the Old Testament, these that have been translated by the Seventy-two Interpreters."2 The combination is characteristic of the fourth-century Eastern witnesses: a Hebrew enumeration carried under a Septuagint pedigree. (The inspiration-of-the-Septuagint legend itself is gathered on the Origin and Inspiration of the Septuagint page and not re-argued here.)

Notes

  • The criterion is reading, not a council. Cyril draws the boundary by liturgical reception — read what "we read openly in the Church," because "the Apostles, and the bishops of old time… handed down these books"; the apocrypha are to be "put aside" and "read not even by thyself." That is the rule of reading, not of destruction, and his fuller charge on the point — with the New-Testament list that omits the Apocalypse — is treated on his New Testament canon page.
  • "Baruch included" is the one thing to watch. Cyril is sometimes cited flatly as a witness against the deuterocanon, and on Tobit, Judith, Sirach, Wisdom, and Maccabees that is right. But his Jeremiah carries Baruch and the Epistle of Jeremiah — so his canon is not quite the bare Protestant thirty-nine: it holds two deuterocanonical works, lodged inside a protocanonical book rather than listed on their own. The lead that brought this witness onto the timeline flagged exactly this Baruch inclusion.
  • Whose count, the Hebrew or the Greek? Cyril's twenty-two match the Hebrew reckoning (the same count Origen, Athanasius, and Jerome give), yet he credits them to the seventy-two translators of the Septuagint — the very Greek Bible that also carried Tobit, Judith, and the rest he excludes. The tension is not resolved on the page; it is the standard fourth-century posture of ranking by the Hebrew list while reading from the Greek.
