The "Council of Jamnia" on the Old Testament Canon

Dates: c. 90 AD (the supposed rabbinic gathering at Yavneh / Jabneh, c. 70–90 AD, after the fall of the Temple) · Wikipedia · back to timeline
Verdict on the deuterocanon: NO — but there was no council. The Hebrew (Pharisaic, proto-rabbinic) Scriptures that hardened into the later Jewish canon never carried Tobit or the other disputed books. But the once-standard story — that a formal Council of Jamnia sat about 90 AD and closed the Jewish canon, deliberately shutting out the Greek extras in reaction to the rise of Christianity — is now largely discredited. This is a Judaism-side marker, and it is double-edged: the rabbinic canon excluded the deuterocanon, yet the dramatic conciliar exclusion often invoked against those books did not happen as described. The popular apologetic claim built on this page — that a Council of Jamnia closed or affirmed the thirty-nine-book canon (Bible.org, Christianity Today) — is weighed adversarially on its own argument page (assessment: weak).

Claims & evidence

The "council" is a modern hypothesis, not an ancient record

The Council of Jamnia is not an event reported in the sources; it is a reconstruction. Heinrich Graetz proposed it in 1871, inferring from scattered rabbinic debates over a few books that a council convened at Yavneh around 70–90 AD and there fixed the Hebrew canon, in part as a reaction to the emerging Christian movement and its Greek Bible.1 The reconstruction became a textbook commonplace — and then, over the twentieth century, came apart under examination.

Modern scholarship has largely abandoned it

"The Jewish sources contain echoes of a debate about [biblical] books, but canonicity was not the issue and the debate was not connected with Jabneh… specific canonical discussion at Jabneh is attested only for Chronicles and the Song of Songs, and both circulated prior to Jabneh. No book is ever mentioned in the sources as being excluded from the canon at Jabneh."

So Albert C. Sundberg Jr. summarized the crux of Jack P. Lewis's landmark critique, "What Do We Mean by Jabneh?" (read before the Society of Biblical Literature in 1962, published 1964).2 Lewis showed that the rabbinic discussions assigned to Jabneh concern whether a couple of disputed books (Song of Songs, Ecclesiastes, later Esther) "defile the hands" — an argument within an already-settled body of Scripture, not a vote on what to admit or expel. W. M. Christie had first questioned the synod in 1925; F. F. Bruce judged it "probably unwise to talk as if there were a Council or Synod of Jamnia"; Sid Z. Leiman and Raymond E. Brown reached kindred conclusions.3 The upshot: no single dated council closed the Jewish canon, and certainly none is on record formally excluding Tobit and its companions.

When the Hebrew canon actually closed has no consensus

Strip away the Jamnia legend and what remains is a genuine uncertainty. "There is no scholarly consensus as to when the canon of the Hebrew Bible (or Tanakh) was fixed": proposals run from the Hasmonean period (c. 140–40 BC) to the second century AD and later, the one firm boundary being that the canon must postdate the final chapters of Daniel (c. 168–164 BC).4 What is not in serious doubt is the shape of the collection that emerged: the Pharisaic / rabbinic stream counted a Hebrew-sized Scripture that did not include Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch, or 1–2 Maccabees — the very books the Greek Septuagint carried. The exclusion is real; only the tidy conciliar moment is fiction.

Notes

  • Why this sits on a Christian-canon timeline. The deuterocanon dispute is, at bottom, a quarrel between two Old Testaments — the broader Greek Septuagint the church inherited and the narrower Hebrew text the synagogue kept. Jamnia is the moment Protestant polemic has traditionally pointed to for the Jewish "No." The point of this marker is that the Jewish "No" is real but diffuse (a canon that crystallized gradually, without these books) rather than the sharp, datable council the older argument supposed.
  • Ties into the Septuagint-versus-Hebrew quarrel. Because the rabbinic canon both omits these books and diverges from the Greek elsewhere, this marker belongs to the larger dispute gathered on the Rabbinic Corruption of Scripture page; the MT-versus-LXX corruption case is weighed there and is not re-argued here.
  • "No council" cuts both ways. The collapse of the Jamnia hypothesis does not hand the deuterocanon to the church — the Hebrew canon still lacks these books — but it does deny either side a clean ancient verdict to cite. The Jewish exclusion was never ratified by a council any more than the Christian inclusion was, until the later Christian councils further down this timeline.
