Rabbi Akiva ben Joseph on the Old Testament Canon

Dates: c. 50–135 AD (also spelled Akiba) · Wikipedia
Summary: The leading Tanna of the Yavneh generation and the sharpest named early-rabbinic voice against the “external books.” The Mishnah records his ruling that one who reads the sefarim ha-chitzonim (the books outside the Hebrew canon) forfeits a share in the world to come, while he stoutly defended the borderline Hebrew books (Song of Songs, Esther) inside the canon. He is a Judaism-side witness that the rabbinic canon excluded the Greek deuterocanonical books — but with two live qualifications: the precise referent of his “external books” (the Apocrypha broadly, or sectarian/Christian writings specifically) is disputed already within the Talmud, and his ban fell on treating those books as authoritative Scripture, not on ever reading them privately.

Claims & evidence

The named ruling against the “external books”

Rabbi Akiva says: Also one who reads external literature… has no share in the World-to-Come.

— Mishnah Sanhedrin 10:1 (Sefaria translation), source; the same ruling is carried in the Babylonian Talmud at b. Sanhedrin 90a. The Hebrew phrase rendered “external literature” is ha-sefarim ha-chitzonim — the “outside books,” i.e. those beyond the received Hebrew Scriptures; the word chitzonim (“outer”) itself echoes the Greek apokrypha. This is the earliest rabbinic pronouncement to name a whole category of writings and place it outside the canon — and to do so with unusual severity, ranking habitual reading of them alongside denying the Torah’s divine origin.1

What the “external books” are is disputed — already within the Talmud

The referent is not self-evident, and the tradition itself pulls two ways. The Babylonian Talmud, expounding Akiva’s dictum, glosses the “external books” as sifrei minim — the books of the heretics — and Rav Yosef then adds that even reading Ben Sira is problematic on account of certain of its verses.

The Sages taught… this [refers to the] books of the heretics [minim].

— Babylonian Talmud, Sanhedrin 100b (Sefaria), source. In the second century minim often meant Jewish-Christians or Gnostics, so on this gloss Akiva’s target is chiefly Christian and sectarian writings (the Gospels and the like), not the Greek Apocrypha as such. Yet Rav Yosef’s inclusion of Ben Sira, and the plain sense of “outside books,” keep the broader referent (the deuterocanon) in view. The honest reading is that the two overlap: Akiva’s ruling sidelines everything beyond the Hebrew canon, and which writings he had chiefly in mind is a genuinely open question — not to be flattened either into “he banned the Deuterocanon” or into “he only meant the Gospels.”2

His circle at Yavneh: the Gospels and the late books alike “do not defile the hands”

The margins [gilyonim] and the scrolls of the heretics do not defile the hands. The scrolls of Ben Sira and all scrolls that are written from that point forward — they do not defile the hands.

— Tosefta Yadayim 2:13 (Sefaria translation), source. “Defiles the hands” is the rabbinic idiom for canonical holiness — to say a book does not defile the hands is to deny it Scriptural status. This ruling is collective and anonymous (not Akiva by name), but it reflects the policy of his Yavneh circle, and it is among the earliest rabbinic texts to reject the Christian writings and the late Jewish books in one breath. The word gilyonim is itself contested: literally the blank “margins” of a scroll, it is widely read as a barbed pun on euangelion (“gospel”), so that the line targets the Gospels — but the identification is an interpretation, not a certainty. Ben Sira is named as the chronological cutoff: anything from his era (early second century BC) onward is out — which of course sweeps in the far later Christian writings.3

He kept the borderline Hebrew books in — and did not forbid private reading of the rest

…for all the writings are holy but the Song of Songs is the holy of holies.

— Mishnah Yadayim 3:5 (Sefaria translation), source. The same Akiva who barred the outside books was the fiercest defender of the disputed Hebrew ones: against those who doubted whether the Song of Songs (and Ecclesiastes, and elsewhere Esther) “defile the hands,” he insisted the Song was the “holy of holies,” securing it inside the canon. His policy was thus a two-sided one — tighten the Hebrew canon’s borders and exclude what lay beyond them. And the exclusion was of authoritative use, not of all reading: the Jewish Encyclopedia observes that Akiva “has… no objection to the private reading of the Apocrypha, as is evident from the fact that he himself makes frequent use of Ecclesiasticus [Ben Sira],” his objection being to reading non-canonical books “as if they were canonical.” He is not, then, a flat “rejecter of the Deuterocanon” who would have Jews never open the books — he barred them from the status of Scripture.4

Notes

  • Where he sits on the timeline. Akiva slots among the Judaism-side markers on the canon timeline, close to the “Council of Jamnia” (the Yavneh gathering whose canon-closing decree is now largely discredited), Josephus’s twenty-two-book count (c. AD 93), and the later Babylonian Talmud. He does not close a canon by decree — modern scholarship denies any such council did — but he is the sharpest named early voice giving the Hebrew-only canon its polemical edge.
  • The anti-Christian-motive question. Older scholarship (Graetz; the 1906 Jewish Encyclopedia) held that Akiva’s hostility to the outside books and to the Septuagint was partly strategic — a desire “to disarm Christians… who drew their proofs from the Apocrypha” and to free diaspora Jews “from the domination of the Septuagint” that had become the church’s Bible; his disciple Aquila produced, under Akiva’s guidance, a hyper-literal Greek version correcting readings (e.g. Isaiah 7:14) the church pressed. Many modern scholars grant that the conflict with Christianity influenced the process while cautioning that the deuterocanon was likely never in the Palestinian Hebrew canon to begin with, so the “rejection” largely confirmed prior practice. Whether Akiva’s ruling was aimed at Christian proof-texts is a live interpretive question, weighed apart from this witness page.
  • Do not overstate the “rejection.” Two cautions travel with any use of Akiva against the deuterocanon: the referent of his “external books” is disputed within the tradition itself (Apocrypha broadly vs. sectarian/Christian books), and his ban was on authoritative use, not on private reading — he quotes Ben Sira himself.
