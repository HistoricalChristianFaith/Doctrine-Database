Jerome on the Old Testament Canon

Dates: c. 390 AD (the Vulgate prefaces and the Hebraica veritas program, c. 391–405; Jerome c. 347–420) · Wikipedia
Verdict on the deuterocanon: NO — the Hebrew twenty-two only, the sharpest "No" on the timeline. The man who gave the Latin West its Bible draws the canon by the Hebrew reckoning and sets "whatever is outside of these… among the apocrypha": Wisdom, Sirach, Judith, Tobias, the Maccabees, and the Shepherd are "not in the canon." He is even harder than the Eastern Fathers who preceded him — where Cyril, Athanasius, and Epiphanius all folded Baruch into the canonical Jeremiah, Jerome omits it as "neither read nor found among the Hebrews." Yet his "No" is not a flat rejection: at his bishops' command he translated Tobit and Judith anyway, praised Judith and ranked Wisdom and Sirach as books to "read… for the strengthening of the people, but not for confirming the authority of ecclesiastical dogmas" — the very formula the Reformation confessions would borrow.

Claims & evidence

The canon: "whatever is outside of these is set aside among the apocrypha"

The Prologus Galeatus — the "helmeted" preface Jerome set at the head of the books he turned "from Hebrew into Latin" — fixes the Old Testament at the Hebrew count and names the books that fall outside it:1

This prologue to the Scriptures may be appropriate as a helmeted introduction to all the books which we turn from Hebrew into Latin, so we may be able to know whatever is outside of these is set aside among the apocrypha. Therefore, Wisdom, which is commonly ascribed to Solomon, and the book of Jesus son of Sirach, and Judith and Tobias, and The Shepherd are not in the canon. I have found the First Book of the Maccabees (is) Hebrew, the Second is Greek, which may also be proven by their styles.

He has just run the list out in the Hebrew order — "twenty-two scrolls… five of Moses, eight of the Prophets, nine of the Hagiographa" — counted by the twenty-two letters of the Hebrew alphabet, the same reckoning Origen, Cyril, Athanasius, and Epiphanius gave before him.1 What is new is the word: apocrypha. Where the Eastern Fathers ranked the disputed books below the canon as "read in the churches" or "useful," Jerome puts the whole deuterocanon on the far side of a hard line and supplies the term the Latin West — and the later Protestant Bibles — would use for it.

The hardest "No": even Baruch dropped

Every Eastern witness on this timeline kept one foot in the Greek by folding Baruch and the Epistle of Jeremiah into the canonical book of Jeremiah, just as the Greek manuscripts carried them. Jerome will not. In his preface to Jeremiah he states plainly that he has left Baruch out:2

And the Book of Baruch, his scribe, which is neither read nor found among the Hebrews, we have omitted, standing ready, because of these things, for all the curses from the jealous…

This makes Jerome the cleanest "No" on the page. Cyril, Athanasius, and Epiphanius all said "twenty-two" and yet quietly carried Baruch inside Jeremiah; Jerome means the Hebrew text and nothing but, and he knows the omission will draw "all the curses from the jealous." His rule is the Hebraica veritas — the "truth of the Hebrew" — pressed all the way to its edge, the program traced in full on his Rabbinic Corruption of Scripture page.

Yet he translated Tobit and Judith — at the bishops' command

Here the witness turns on itself. Having ruled Tobit and Judith out of the canon, Jerome nonetheless rendered both into Latin — not on his own initiative, but because the bishops Chromatius and Heliodorus demanded it. His prologue to Tobit is candid about the conflict:3

For you demand that I bring a book written in Chaldean words into Latin writing, indeed the book of Tobias, which the Hebrews exclude from the catalogue of Divine Scriptures, being mindful of those things which they have titled Hagiographa… For the studies of the Hebrews rebuke us and find fault with us, to translate this for the ears of Latins contrary to their canon. But it is better to be judging the opinion of the Pharisees to displease and to be subject to the commands of bishops.

The book is "contrary to their canon," he grants — and he translates it anyway, because the "commands of bishops" outweigh "the opinion of the Pharisees." The same posture governs his Judith prologue, where he again notes that "among the Hebrews the Book of Judith is found among the Hagiographa, the authority of which toward confirming those which have come into contention is judged less appropriate," yet does the work because his patrons asked.4 So the "No" is academic, not absolute: the Hebrew canon is the rule by which Jerome would argue, but the church's practice is the rule by which he works.

The famous formula: "read… for the strengthening of the people, not for… ecclesiastical dogmas"

Jerome's most quoted line on the deuterocanon stands in his preface to the books of Solomon, where he deals with the two Wisdom books. He has just judged the Wisdom of Solomon "falsely ascribed" and never "among the Hebrews"; then he sets the rule for both it and Sirach:5

Therefore, just as the Church also reads the books of Judith, Tobias, and the Maccabees, but does not receive them among the canonical Scriptures, so also one may read these two scrolls for the strengthening of the people, (but) not for confirming the authority of ecclesiastical dogmas.

This is the exact middle position the Eastern Fathers had been refining — not Scripture, not forbidden, but edifying — now stated as a working rule of two clauses: read them, but do not prove doctrine by them. It is also the single most consequential sentence on this page for what comes after: the Church of England's Article VI (1571) and the Westminster divines (1647) would lift Jerome's distinction almost verbatim — the deuterocanon read "for example of life and instruction of manners," but not "to establish any doctrine" — and cite Jerome by name as their patristic warrant.

The Nicaea report — weighed on its own page

The Judith prologue carries one more famous claim. Jerome gives, as his reason for translating a book he otherwise ranks below the canon, a conciliar precedent:4

But because this book is found by the Nicene Council to have been counted among the number of the Sacred Scriptures, I have acquiesced to your request…

The first ecumenical council published no canon-list, and Jerome's own prefaces rule Judith out of the canon, so the report cannot carry a Nicene canon-decree. The full adversarial weighing — the decree-reading assessed weak, the modest reading (that Nicaea merely cited Judith in argument) plausible — is set out on its own argument page and not re-argued here.6

The other side of the ledger: even Jerome's own pen sometimes calls these books "Scripture"

Jerome is the proponents' star witness for a Hebrew-only canon, and the prefaces above earn him the title. But the record is two-sided, and honesty requires the counter-instances. Away from the prefaces — in his letters and commentaries, where he is preaching and expounding rather than ruling on the canon — Jerome himself now and again reaches for the very books his Prologus Galeatus files "among the apocrypha," and cites them with the formulae of Scripture. Writing to Julian (c. 406) he introduces a maxim from Sirach with the plainest possible attribution:7

Holy scripture says: "a tale out of season is as musick in mourning."

Expounding Isaiah 1:24, he glosses the "mighty" with a line from the Wisdom of Solomon under the formula scriptum est — "it is written":8

… de quibus scriptum est: "Potentes potenter tormenta patientur."

— "… of whom it is written: 'The mighty shall be mightily tormented'" (Wisdom of Solomon 6:7). Writing to Oceanus (Letter 77 §4) he ranges a phrase of Baruch among the prophets' own proclamations:9

… and those of Baruch, "Arise, arise, O Jerusalem," and many other proclamations made by the trumpets of the prophets.

And in Letter 65 (on Psalm 44, to the virgin Principia) he sets Judith beside the canonical Ruth and Esther as women who gave their names to "sacred volumes":10

Ruth et Esther et Judith tantae gloriae sunt, ut sacris voluminibus nomina indiderint.

— "Ruth and Esther and Judith are of such glory that they gave their names to sacred volumes."

How much weight should these carry? Less than the prefaces, but not nothing. Jerome's considered, technical verdict — stated in the very prologues where he is drawing the canon — is the Hebrew twenty-two, and that is his settled position; the citations here are homiletical and exegetical, the looser "Scripture" of a man saturated in books the Church read aloud. They sit inside his own rule rather than breaking it: the deuterocanon is to be read, even quoted for edification, while not confirming ecclesiastical dogmas — and none of these four passages rests a doctrine on the book it cites. So the counter-instances neither overturn his "No" nor can they be quietly suppressed: the hardest rejecter on the timeline still, on occasion, called these books "Scripture." What they qualify is the proponents' clean appeal to Jerome — the same list-versus-usage strain that runs through Cyril, Hilary, Epiphanius, and Gregory of Nazianzus reaches even into the canon's sternest voice, and is weighed on the "the early church rejected the deuterocanon" argument page (assessment: contested).

Outside the prefaces: the same Hebrew canon, walked book-by-book for Paulinus

The Vulgate prefaces are not the only place Jerome's Hebrew canon shows itself. Writing to Paulinus of Nola in 394 — a letter (Letter 53) urging the bishop to a diligent study of Scripture — Jerome takes him on a tour of the whole Bible, book by book, pausing on each to draw out its spiritual sense. The Old Testament half of that survey ends where the Hebrew canon ends:11

Solomon, a lover of peace and of the Lord, corrects morals, teaches nature, unites Christ and the church, and sings a sweet marriage song to celebrate that holy bridal. Esther, a type of the church, frees her people from danger… The book of things omitted… is of such importance and value that without it any one who should claim to himself a knowledge of the scriptures would make himself a laughing stock in his own eyes… Ezra and Nehemiah, that is the Lord's helper and His consoler, are united in a single book.

He has walked the entire Old Testament to get there — the five books of Moses, Job, "Jesus the son of Nave" (Joshua), Judges with Ruth, Samuel and the four books of Kings (the Hebrew Malachim), the twelve prophets "compressed within the narrow limits of a single volume," then Isaiah, Jeremiah, Ezekiel, and Daniel, the Psalms of David, the three books of Solomon, Esther, Chronicles, and Ezra–Nehemiah — and from there he turns straight to the New Testament. Not one deuterocanonical book appears anywhere in the sweep: no Wisdom, no Sirach, no Tobit, no Judith, no Maccabees, no Baruch. And the very groupings are the Hebrew reckoning, not the Septuagint's — the twelve minor prophets counted as one book, Ezra and Nehemiah "united in a single book."11

How much should this carry? Less than the prefaces, but not nothing. Letter 53 is a protreptic on the study of Scripture, not a formal canon-list: Jerome nowhere uses the words "canon" or "apocrypha" here, draws no boundary line, and the absence of the deuterocanon is a silence rather than an express ruling. But it is a telling silence — when Jerome sets out to survey the whole Bible for a bishop's reading program, the books he tours are precisely the Hebrew twenty-two, and the catalogue stops exactly where the Hebrew canon stops. So the letter corroborates, outside the Vulgate prefaces, that the Hebrew canon was Jerome's settled frame even when he was not arguing the point — the same considered, technical canon that governs the prologues, surfacing now in his pastoral correspondence.

This second witness was put forward by the Presbyterian scholar Archibald Alexander, who read Letter 53 as giving "a catalogue of the books of the Old Testament, exactly corresponding with that which Protestants receive." So far the survey bears him out. But Alexander overreaches in the same breath: he has Jerome add that these books were, "agreeably to the tradition of our ancestors, … inspired by the Holy Spirit" — a clause found nowhere in Letter 53. The wording is not Jerome's at all but Rufinus's, the sentence that introduces the canon-list of his Commentary on the Apostles' Creed ("which, according to the tradition of our forefathers, are believed to have been inspired by the Holy Ghost, and have been handed down to the Churches of Christ"). Alexander has grafted Rufinus's inspiration-formula onto Jerome's survey. The catalogue is genuinely Jerome's and genuinely Hebrew-only; the "inspired by the Holy Spirit" gloss is borrowed — and borrowed, as it happens, from Jerome's great antagonist of these very years.12

Notes

  • NO to canonicity, the hardest version — but not to the books' worth. Jerome denies the deuterocanon canonical standing more sharply than anyone before him on this timeline: he calls the books "apocrypha," and unlike the Eastern Fathers he does not even keep Baruch inside Jeremiah. Yet he translates Tobit and Judith, praises "the widow Judith, an example of chastity," and grants Wisdom and Sirach a place in church reading. The honest summary is that he separates two questions the others left fused: a book may be read in the Church without being canonical Scripture.
  • The internal tension — and why it is not simple hypocrisy. Jerome rules Tobit and Judith outside the canon and then renders both into Latin; he says the deuterocanon is not for "confirming… ecclesiastical dogmas" and yet draws on its books elsewhere for edification. The appearance of inconsistency dissolves once his own distinction is granted: the Hebrew canon governs what may establish doctrine; the church's wider use governs what may be read for profit. He translates at the bishops' command while reserving his scholarly verdict — "to displease the opinion of the Pharisees… and be subject to the commands of bishops." Both Reformation Protestants (who quote the canon verdict) and Catholic apologists (who quote the translations and the praise of Judith) have claimed him; both are reading half the record.
  • The terminological legacy. Jerome did more than vote "No": he named the category. His Prologus Galeatus is where the disputed books are first set, as a class, "among the apocrypha," and his Solomon-preface formula (read for edification, not for doctrine) is the seed of the Protestant "Apocrypha" — the segregated, sub-canonical section between the Testaments. The Thirty-Nine Articles and the Westminster Confession both trace their wording to him.
  • How many "books of Ezra"? — the Latin numbering muddle. Jerome's own count of the Esdras books was not the one his readers later assumed. On Pierre-Maurice Bogaert's reconstruction, Jerome's first and second "books of Ezra" were Ezra–Nehemiah (a single book) and the Greek/Old-Latin Esdras A (the "3 Esdras" of the Vulgate appendix); his third book was the Apocalypse of Ezra (4 Ezra proper, minus the later additions); and what he called the fourth can only be guessed — perhaps 5 Ezra or 6 Ezra, or the two combined. The instability is not merely Jerome's: Ambrose cites one and the same verse, 4 Ezra 6:41, as the "third book" of Esdras in his Latin, where his modern translator renders "fourth." The lesson for any list-comparison argument is caution — "the books of Ezra" did not denote a fixed set across the Latin Fathers.13
  • Were his reasons sound? — the "wrong for his reasons" charge. A natural follow-up question is not whether Jerome's Hebrew-canon conclusion was right (the dispute the whole timeline traces) but whether his specific grounds hold up against later evidence. They divide. His philological judgments — Tobit a "Chaldean" (Aramaic) book, 1 Maccabees Hebrew but 2 Maccabees Greek, the Wisdom of Solomon a Greek composition, Sirach a book he had himself "found in Hebrew" — were strikingly vindicated by the twentieth century's manuscript finds (the Aramaic and Hebrew Tobit of Qumran, the Hebrew Ben Sira of the Cairo Geniza and Masada) and the modern consensus, not embarrassed by them. What modern scholarship does undercut is his historical premise — that a fixed, authoritative Jewish canon already stood for the apostolic church to defer to — since there was no closed first-century canon and the canon-closing "Council of Jamnia" is a discarded hypothesis. The full weighing is on the "Was Jerome wrong for his reasons?" argument page (assessment: contested).
  • How modern apologetics reads him — "translated it, then disclaimed it." Jerome's double posture — rendering Tobit, Judith, and the rest into Latin while denying them canonical standing — has made him the favorite patristic witness of popular Protestant apologetics, which presses exactly the prefaces set out above: that "even Jerome… said the Apocrypha is not Bible," translating it only because "he thought it had some value" while going "out of his way to say this thing isn't scripture" (Matt Whitman); that he rendered the books "with a note saying… these are not inspired writ" (Todd Friel); that he "wrote in there 'These aren't scripture'" though he held them "highly esteemed" (Mike Winger); and that the Vulgate carried them "despite Jerome's insistence that the Apocrypha did not belong in the Bible" (GotQuestions). The reading is fair to the prefaces — it is the considered, technical verdict this page documents — but it tends to pass over the other side of the ledger above: the bishops' command, the praise of "the widow Judith," and Jerome's own-pen citations of these books "as Scripture." The full adversarial weighing of the historical case these proponents build — that the early church rejected the deuterocanon — is on its argument page (assessment: contested).14
  • One proponent overstatement to flag: Jerome did not "invent the term apocrypha." In the same talk Mike Winger says Jerome "invented the term apocrypha," taking it from a Jewish practice of "hiding"/setting aside non-scriptural books rather than destroying them. The first half overstates. The Greek word apocrypha ("hidden things") predates Jerome by two centuries and was already a term of art for the heterodox "hidden" books: Irenaeus (c. 180) charges the Marcosians with adducing "an unspeakable number of apocryphal and spurious writings," and the word is standard after him in Clement of Alexandria, Tertullian, and Origen. What Jerome did — and it is considerable — was not to coin the word but to fix it, in the Prologus Galeatus, as the label for a non-canonical class of Old-Testament books in the Latin West, the sense the later Protestant "Apocrypha" inherited (the point the "terminological legacy" note above makes). So "named the category" is right; "invented the term" is not. Winger's accompanying etymology — that Jerome drew the word from a Jewish habit of setting such books aside — is plausible-sounding but is not something Jerome himself states, and is best weighed rather than asserted.15
  • Cross-links. Jerome's reversal of the Septuagint-versus-Hebrew polarity — trust the Hebrew, demote the Greek translators from prophets to interpreters — is treated in full on his Rabbinic Corruption of Scripture page; the inspiration of the Greek Bible whose canon he narrows is the subject of the Origin and Inspiration of the Septuagint timeline. (Jerome also appears, on a different question, on the Nephilim page.)
