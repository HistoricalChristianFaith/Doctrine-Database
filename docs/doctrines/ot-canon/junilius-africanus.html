Junilius Africanus on the Old Testament Canon

Dates: c. 542–549 (Junilius was quaestor sacri palatii — chief legal minister — at Justinian's court in Constantinople for seven years from c. 542; his Instituta Regularia Divinae Legis was composed in these years, its publication placed by Kihn c. 551) · Wikipedia
Verdict on the deuterocanon: NO, tiered — a narrow first class of "perfect authority" and a second class of "middle authority" ("added by many"), into which the deuterocanon and several protocanonical books are demoted. A North-African layman turned Justinian's quaestor, Junilius translated into Latin a Greek handbook of Scripture-teaching learned from Paul the Persian of the school of Nisibis, casting it as a catechism in question and answer. Near its start it grades the biblical books by authority: a first, canonical rank of "perfect authority," and a second rank of "middle authority" that "many add." Into that second rank he files not only the deuterocanonical histories (Tobit, Judith, Maccabees) and the Wisdom of Solomon, but even the protocanonical Chronicles, Job, Ezra, Esther, and Song of Songs — the narrowest first-canon of the Antiochene tradition — giving as his reason that "among the Hebrews too" these books "were received with this same distinction, as Jerome and the rest testify." It is the tiered "list" verdict in its most systematic sixth-century form, a near-contemporary of the Synopsis of Sacred Scripture.

Claims & evidence

Three grades of authority: "perfect," "middle," and "none"

Quomodo diuinorum librorum consideratur auctoritas? — Quia quaedam perfectae auctoritatis sunt, quaedam mediae, quaedam nullius.

— Instituta Regularia Divinae Legis I (on the authority of the divine books), Latin text ed. from H. Kihn, Georgetown Latin edition.1 "How is the authority of the divine books considered? — Because certain of them are of perfect authority, certain of middle authority, certain of none." The books "of perfect authority" are, he says, "those which we have enumerated as canonical in each class" (Quae canonica in singulis speciebus absolute numerauimus); those "of middle" authority are "those we said are added by many" (Quae adiungi a pluribus diximus). The whole apparatus is a graded canon: the disputed books are neither struck out nor made equal to the rest, but ranked in a second tier.

The historical books: the deuterocanon "added by many," on the Hebrews' authority

Adiungunt plures Paralipomenon II, Iob I, Tobiae I, Esdrae I, Iudith I, Esther I, Machabaeorum II… Quoniam apud Hebraeos quoque super hac differentia recipiebantur, sicut Hieronymus ceterique testantur.

— Instituta I.3 (the historical books), Georgetown Latin edition.2 After listing the canonical histories, Junilius adds: "Many add the two books of Chronicles, Job, Tobit, Ezra, Judith, Esther, and the two of Maccabees… because among the Hebrews too they were received with this same distinction, as Jerome and the rest testify." So the deuterocanonical Tobit, Judith, and the Maccabees are put in the second, "middle" rank — and, strikingly, the protocanonical Chronicles, Job, Ezra, and Esther are demoted with them, an unusually narrow first canon owing to his Antiochene, Theodoran source. His warrant is the Hebrew reckoning, cited (as Cyril and Athanasius also cite it) on the authority of Jerome — though, as modern editors note, this over-reads Jerome, who did not exclude Chronicles or Job.

The wisdom books: Proverbs and Sirach canonical, Wisdom of Solomon "added by many"

In duobus: Salomonis prouerbiorum liber I et Iesu, filii Sirach, liber I. Adiungunt quidam librum qui uocatur Sapientiae et Canticum canticorum.

— Instituta I.5 (the proverbial books), Georgetown Latin edition.3 Among the "proverbial" writings his first, canonical class holds just two: "Solomon's book of Proverbs, and the book of Jesus son of Sirach" — so the deuterocanonical Sirach (Ecclesiasticus) stands inside his canon, while "certain add the book called Wisdom and the Song of Songs" to the second rank. It is a telling inversion of the usual sorting: the deuterocanonical Sirach is kept, but the deuterocanonical Wisdom of Solomon — and the protocanonical Song of Songs — are ranked below it.

The Apocalypse "much doubted among the Easterns"

Ceterum de Ioannis Apocalypsi apud orientales admodum dubitatur.

— Instituta I (on the doctrinal books), Georgetown Latin edition.4 "For the rest, concerning John's Apocalypse there is much doubt among the Easterns." Reporting from Constantinople the same Eastern hesitancy over Revelation that Cyril and the Council of Laodicea show on the New-Testament side, Junilius carries the graded-canon habit across both Testaments.

Notes

  • A layman, and a Syriac-Antiochene canon. Junilius was no bishop but Justinian's quaestor; his handbook renders into Latin the teaching of Paul the Persian from the school of Nisibis, and so carries the narrow Antiochene canon of the school of Theodore of Mopsuestia into the Latin West, where Cassiodorus commended it (Institutes 1.10). His tiering resembles Athanasius's ("canon" vs. "read") and Rufinus's ("ecclesiastical" vs. "canonical"), but is pushed further, demoting even Chronicles, Job, Ezra, and Esther.
  • Over-reading Jerome. Junilius grounds the demotion on "the Hebrews… as Jerome and the rest testify," but Jerome ranked Chronicles, Job, Ezra, and Esther within the twenty-two-book Hebrew canon, not below it; the extra-narrow first class is Junilius's Antiochene inheritance read back onto the Hebrew list, a point his editors flag as a misunderstanding of Jerome.
  • Tobit dropped, then quoted. Tobit is absent from his enumeration but cited later in the treatise; Kihn judged the omission an early copyist's slip rather than a considered exclusion — "no writer of the time would have designedly refused to include Tobit even in his list of deuterocanonical books."
  • Near the Synopsis. Junilius sits beside the Synopsis of Sacred Scripture (c. 550) as a mid-sixth-century Eastern "list" witness — both grade the disputed books into a lower rank rather than deny or embrace them — a generation before Gregory the Great would call 1 Maccabees "not canonical" yet quote it in the West.
