The Council in Trullo (Quinisext) on the Old Testament Canon

Dates: 692 (the Quinisext Council, or Council in Trullo, convened by Justinian II at Constantinople to supply the disciplinary canons the Fifth and Sixth Ecumenical Councils had left unwritten) · Wikipedia
Verdict on the deuterocanon: both and neither — Trullo ratifies, en bloc, a set of earlier canon-catalogues that flatly contradict one another on the disputed books. The council's second canon confirms "the eighty-five canons" that had come down "in the name of the… Apostles," together with the canons of the councils of Laodicea and Carthage and of the Fathers Athanasius, Amphilochius, and Gregory the Theologian — each of which carries its own Scripture-list, and those lists do not agree: the Apostolic Canons and Carthage run the longer, deuterocanon-bearing Old Testament, while Laodicea, Athanasius, and Amphilochius rank the disputed books out or below. Trullo settles which collections carry authority without adjudicating between their rival canons — the Eastern church receiving the whole inheritance, contradictions and all. It is the conciliar root of the Eastern churches' enduring tolerance of a graded, unfixed Old-Testament boundary, the line that runs on to the Synod of Jerusalem (1672).

Canon 2 — the earlier canons confirmed en bloc

The second canon of Trullo is a master-list of the canonical collections the council receives. After confirming the eighty-five Apostolic Canons, it seals the canons of the councils and Fathers by name:1

It has also seemed good to this holy Council, that the eighty-five canons, received and ratified by the holy and blessed Fathers before us, and also handed down to us in the name of the holy and glorious Apostles should from this time forth remain firm and unshaken… But we set our seal likewise upon all the other holy canons set forth by our holy and blessed Fathers, that is, by the 318 holy God-bearing Fathers assembled at Nice, and those at Ancyra, further those at NeoCaesarea and likewise those at Gangra, and besides, those at Antioch in Syria: those too at Laodicea in Phrygia… and those of Carthage… of Athanasius, Archbishop of Alexandria; of Basil, Archbishop of Caesarea in Cappadocia; of Gregory, Bishop of Nyssa; of Gregory Theologus; of Amphilochius of Iconium…

The books named here are not a Scripture-list of Trullo's own making; they are the earlier collections, each of which contains a canon-catalogue — and those catalogues disagree. The eighty-fifth Apostolic Canon lists three books of Maccabees, Judith, and (as recommended reading) the Wisdom of Sirach; the African council of Carthage lists Tobit, Judith, the two Maccabees, and the Wisdom books among the Scriptures. But Laodicea's canon 60 omits the whole deuterocanon, Athanasius's Festal Letter ranks it "read" but "not in the Canon," Gregory the Theologian and Amphilochius give Hebrew-sized lists, and Amphilochius even flags several New Testament books as doubted. Trullo confirms them all.

Reception "in block," not adjudication

The council does not reconcile these lists; it receives them together. As Percival observes of exactly this feature — the Trullan confirmation of mutually contradictory conciliar canons — the point was never that every particular of every ratified canon bore the whole Church's express sanction, but that the earlier bodies of law were taken up as a settled inheritance:2

it was not intended that to each particular of each canon of each local synod adopted, the express sanction of the Universal Church was given, but that they were received in block as legislation well calculated for the good of the Church.

So the Eastern church, in its most authoritative disciplinary council, canonized rival Old-Testament catalogues side by side — and left the boundary graded and open rather than fixed. Because the Second Council of Nicaea (787) in its own first canon received the Trullan canons as those of the Sixth Ecumenical Council, this en-bloc ratification of conflicting lists passed into the settled law of the East.

Notes

  • Why it belongs on this timeline. Trullo issues no book-list of its own, but it is the conciliar act by which the East gave standing authority to catalogues that contradict each other on the deuterocanon — the reason the Eastern churches never produced a single fixed Old Testament of the Western, Tridentine kind. It is the hinge between the fourth-century Eastern "list" witnesses (Laodicea, Athanasius, Amphilochius) and the Eastern endpoint at the Synod of Jerusalem (1672), which at last enumerated a fuller canon for the Orthodox.
  • The same en-bloc habit at Nicaea II. The Trullan ratification is the mechanism by which, as this timeline notes elsewhere, Nicaea II's blanket confirmation embraced "mutually inconsistent lists" — the very point Percival draws from canon 2. See the discussion under the Arguments on the summary page.
  • New Testament, too. The conflict Trullo ratifies runs across both Testaments: the Apostolic Canons close the New Testament without the Apocalypse (adding two Epistles of Clement and the Constitutions), Laodicea likewise omits Revelation, while Athanasius and Carthage include it — another set of irreconcilable catalogues received together.
