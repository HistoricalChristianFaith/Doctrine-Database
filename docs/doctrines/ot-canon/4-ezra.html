4 Ezra (2 Esdras 14:44–48) on the Old Testament Canon

Dates: c. AD 100 (the Jewish apocalypse forming chapters 3–14 of the composite book called 2 Esdras; written after the fall of Jerusalem in AD 70, its "eagle vision" read as the Flavian dynasty) · Wikipedia
Verdict on the deuterocanon: No — but with two caveats. 4 Ezra gives the other first-century Jewish count beside Josephus: as its closing vision, God commands Ezra to publish exactly twenty-four books openly — the standard rabbinic reckoning of the Hebrew Bible, from which the Greek-only books are absent — while keeping "seventy" further books back for the wise. It is an independent witness of c. AD 100 to a closed, numbered public Scripture bounded at the Hebrew count. Two things keep it from being a tidy verdict against the deuterocanon, though: the "seventy" secret books are prized, not discarded (the passage divides public canon from esoteric wisdom, not Scripture from worthless books), and 4 Ezra is itself one of the books outside the Hebrew twenty-four — a work beyond the Hebrew canon testifying to that canon's boundary.

Claims & evidence

2 Esdras 14:44–48: a public canon of twenty-four, seventy kept back

The book closes with a scene of Scripture-making. The Law had been burned; at God's command in the burning bush, Ezra takes five scribes and, inspired by "a full cup… full as it were with water, but the colour of it was like fire," dictates for forty days. The tally and its division follow:

"So in forty days were written ninety-four books. And it came to pass when the forty days were fulfilled, that the Most High spake unto me saying: The twenty-four books that thou hast written publish, that the worthy and unworthy may read (therein): but the seventy last thou shalt keep, to deliver them to the wise among thy people. For in them is the spring of understanding, the fountain of wisdom, and the stream of knowledge. And I did so."

— 4 Ezra 14:44–48, in G. H. Box's translation (The Ezra-Apocalypse, 1912).1 Two numbers carry the weight. The twenty-four to be "read openly" — published for "the worthy and unworthy," i.e. the whole people — is the ordinary rabbinic count of the Hebrew Bible: "In the Talmud and Midrash," Box notes, "the O.T. is regularly termed 'the twenty-four holy Scriptures'… The number 24 is the ordinary reckoning of the O.T. books (5 + 8 + 11)." It is the same collection Josephus, a few years earlier, tallies as twenty-two — the two counts reconciled, as usual, by folding Ruth into Judges and Lamentations into Jeremiah to match the twenty-two letters of the Hebrew alphabet.2 None of the Greek-only books (Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch, 1–2 Maccabees) falls inside the published twenty-four. The seventy "last" are the esoteric writings — apocalypses of the kind 4 Ezra itself is — reserved for "the wise." So the author knows a public, enumerated canon distinct from a body of secret books: a second independent Jewish attestation, alongside Josephus, that by c. AD 100 the openly-received Scriptures of the synagogue formed a bounded, numbered list.

A textual note: "ninety-four" vs. the Latin's inflated total

The versions disagree on the arithmetic. The Syriac, Ethiopic, Arabic, and Armenian read "ninety-four" (94 = 24 published + 70 kept back) and name "twenty-four"; the Latin manuscripts on which the King James Apocrypha depends are corrupt here, giving an inflated total ("two hundred and four books" in the KJV, other Latin witnesses 904/970/973) and, at v. 45, merely "the first that thou hast written" with no number.3 Box judges the "94"/"24" reading "undoubtedly right," and suggests, with Hilgenfeld, that the number dropped out of the Latin and Ethiopic precisely because Christians reckoned the Hebrew books as twenty-two, not twenty-four — a scribal harmonization that, ironically, confirms how firmly "twenty-four" belonged to the Jewish reckoning the passage preserves.4

Notes

  • Why this sits on a Christian-canon timeline. The deuterocanon dispute is at bottom a quarrel between two Old Testaments — the broader Greek Septuagint the church inherited and the narrower Hebrew text the synagogue kept. 4 Ezra is a Jewish voice from the decades when that Hebrew list was hardening: independent of Josephus, and reaching the same closed, numbered short list. It is slotted here as a witness to the synagogue's public canon, not as a ruling in the later Christian controversy (which it predates).
  • What it does not settle. The passage neither condemns the extra books nor endorses the deuterocanon: its "seventy" secret books are held in high honour ("the spring of understanding… the fountain of wisdom"), so its interest is the boundary of the public canon, not a two-tier verdict on Tobit, Judith, or the Maccabees. It is a genuine early marker of a bounded, numbered list — weighed, with Josephus, on the argument page below — and no more than that.
  • The irony worth naming. 4 Ezra is itself one of the books outside the Hebrew twenty-four — it travels in the Latin and Slavonic Bibles as part of 2 Esdras (2 Esdras chs. 3–14), never admitted to the Hebrew canon and classed by Rome, at Trent, among the rejected apocrypha. So a book beyond the boundary is the one drawing it. That is a curiosity of provenance, not a defect in the testimony: the value of the datum is that a first-century Jewish author, whatever the standing of his own book, took the public Scriptures to number twenty-four.
  • Weighed adversarially in the Arguments. The closed-numbered-canon datum is adduced, beside Josephus, on the Josephus twenty-two-book argument (assessment: contested); the 22/24 reckoning is the same one handled there and on the Babylonian Talmud and Masoretic Text pages. The adjudication lives there and is not restated here.
