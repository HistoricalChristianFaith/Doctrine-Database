Judas Iscariot

Judas Iscariot is the one apostle everyone remembers for a single act — the betrayal — and the one whose story the New Testament tells at both ends: a name and a father at the start, and, uniquely among the Twelve, two accounts of his death. He stands last in every list, always tagged “who also betrayed him”; John adds that he kept the disciples’ purse and pilfered from it; the Synoptics fix his price at thirty pieces of silver. Then he dies — but the two death-notices do not read alike. Matthew has him fling the silver into the temple and hang himself, after which the priests buy a potter’s field with the blood-money and call it “the field of blood”; Acts has Judas himself buy a field and fall headlong, bursting open, the field named “field of blood” for the blood of his death. Papias, a generation later, supplies a third and grislier end — a monstrous swelling — and the Middle Ages an entire fabulous biography. This page sets out, earliest source first, how the memory of Judas was told and re-told: the spare but double-ended New Testament record, Papias’s swollen death, the 2nd-century Gnostic Gospel of Judas that made the traitor a hero, the medieval Oedipus-legend of the Golden Legend and Dante’s Satan-chewed Judas, and the modern scholarship that weighs the famous divergences. This is a biographical tradition-timeline: its “witnesses” are the datable sources that report on Judas, and the genuinely contested questions — do Matthew and Acts contradict on his death? on the field? did Matthew misattribute a prophecy? — are weighed on their own argument pages below, kindred to the Scripture-crux cases treated elsewhere on this wiki. Timeline

The New Testament (c. 30; written c. 50–95)

The New Testament gives Judas more than most of the Twelve: a surname (“Iscariot,” most likely ’îsh-Qêriyôth, “man of Kerioth” in Judah — perhaps the one Judean among Galileans), a father (Simon), a role (keeper of the common bag, and, John says, a thief), and a crime (handing Jesus over for thirty pieces of silver). It also, alone among the apostles, records his death twice. Matthew has Judas repent, throw the silver into the temple, and “hanged himself”; the priests then buy the potter’s field for the blood-money, so the field is called “the field of blood” and the whole tied to a prophecy of “Jeremy.” Acts, in Peter’s speech, has Judas himself “purchase a field with the reward of iniquity; and falling headlong, he burst asunder in the midst, and all his bowels gushed out,” the field named “Aceldama… the field of blood.” The two notices agree on a violent death and a bloody field but differ on how he died, who bought it, and why it bears its name — the divergences weighed under Arguments below.1

Papias of Hierapolis (c. 100–130)

Papias, among the earliest post-apostolic writers, preserves a third account of Judas’s end, surviving as a quotation in Apollinaris of Laodicea: not a hanging and not a plain fall, but a grotesque swelling — “his body having swollen to such an extent that he could not pass where a chariot could pass easily, he was crushed by the chariot, so that his bowels gushed out.” It keeps the gushing bowels of Acts but supplies a new cause, and later writers (Theophylact) piled on further horrors — worms, a stench, blindness. Within a generation or two of Acts, the manner of Judas’s death was already multiplying.2

The Gospel of Judas (2nd c.; Coptic copy c. 300)

Against the whole canonical picture stands a 2nd-century Gnostic text that turns the traitor into the hero. Denounced by Irenaeus about 180 and lost until a Coptic copy was published amid great publicity in 2006, the Gospel of Judas makes Judas the one disciple who truly knows Jesus — “you are from the immortal realm of Barbelo” — and, on its first reading, casts the betrayal as a service Jesus himself commanded, freeing his spirit from the flesh. (A revisionist school reads the Sethian author as in fact damning Judas.) It is late, Gnostic, and dependent on the canonical Gospels: no witness to the historical Judas, but a vivid witness to how a sect reimagined him.3

The Golden Legend and Dante (c. 1260–1320)

By the high Middle Ages Judas had acquired a full fabulous life. Jacobus de Voragine’s Golden Legend (c. 1260), telling it as the preamble to the life of Matthias, gives an Oedipus-tale: an infant exposed on the sea after a dream that he would “destroy their people,” who grows up to kill his own father and unknowingly marry his mother before coming to Christ for mercy — a story Voragine himself twice brands apocryphal and “not authentic.” Dante meanwhile fixed Judas at the absolute bottom of Hell, head-first in the central mouth of Satan, in the ring named Giudecca for him. It is late legend — church and poetic imagination, a millennium and more after the events — not history.4

Modern scholarship

Critical study returns Judas to the two spare, divergent death-notices and asks the obvious questions. On the manner of death, most agree Matthew’s hanging and Acts’ headlong fall are told for different theological ends — Matthew shadowing the traitor Ahithophel, who “hanged himself” (2 Samuel 17:23); Acts echoing the psalms of the wicked man’s ruin — and divide over whether they can still be harmonized (the old “hanged, then fell and burst”) or are simply two independent traditions. On the field, the divergence over who bought it and why it is named runs the same way. And Matthew’s ascription of the thirty-pieces oracle to “Jeremy” when the words are chiefly Zechariah’s is a long-noticed crux with several proposed solutions. What is not in doubt is the core: a member of the Twelve named Judas handed Jesus over and died soon after; the shape of that death is where the sources part company.5

Arguments

Two of them concern the same Matthew-versus-Acts divergence over Judas’s end and are best read together; a third concerns Matthew’s prophecy citation. Related arguments — the two accounts of Judas’s end

Matthew and Acts both report Judas’s death and the field bought with his betrayal-money, and both call the field “the field of blood” — but they diverge on how he died, who bought the field, and why it bears its name. The two arguments below weigh the two halves of that same divergence; each is contested, and they are best read as a pair.

Do Matthew and Acts contradict on how Judas died? (assessment: contested)

The manner of death — Matthew’s hanging against Acts’ falling headlong and bursting (and Papias’s third version, a monstrous swelling). The harmonizing reading (hanged, then the body fell and burst) is mechanically possible and has a long pedigree, and both notices are plainly shaped by Old Testament type — Matthew’s suicide echoing the traitor Ahithophel, Acts’ ruin echoing the Psalms — rather than reported as forensic detail. But the reconciling detail is imported, the two scenes even disagree on whether Judas repented, and a strong critical school reads them as two independent symbolic traditions. The interpretation is contested: no logical contradiction, but no compelling harmony either.6

The Field of Blood — who bought it, and why so named? (assessment: contested)

The field — Matthew’s priests buy the potter’s field with the returned blood-money; Acts’ Judas “purchased” a field, named for his bloody death. The buyer-divergence reconciles easily by agency (they bought it with his money, in effect his purchase), and a field can bear a blood-name on two counts at once — the stronger half of the harmonization. But Acts gives its own reason for the name, not Matthew’s, so “both are true” is a synthesis neither text makes, and the mainstream reading takes the two as separate etiologies of one real plot of ground. The interpretation is contested, though it leans more harmonizable than the manner-of-death.7

Did Matthew misattribute a Zechariah prophecy to “Jeremy the prophet”? (assessment: plausible)

The prophecy citation — Matthew 27:9–10 credits “Jeremy” with an oracle whose words are mostly Zechariah’s. The best reading takes it as a deliberate composite: Zechariah’s words set in a frame of Jeremian imagery (the potter, a purchased field, a place renamed for innocent blood, Jeremiah 18–19 and 32), credited to the senior/governing prophet — a technique Matthew and Mark both use elsewhere. This is the mainstream scholarly explanation and defeats the charge of a simple blunder; it falls short only of fixing why Jeremiah in particular is named, where several sub-explanations compete. The weaker fallbacks (Jeremiah as the head-scroll of the prophets; a lost Jeremiah apocryphon) are unnecessary. The interpretation is plausible.8
