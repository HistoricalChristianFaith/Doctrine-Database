Judas Iscariot

Judas Iscariot is the one apostle everyone remembers for a single act — the betrayal — and the one whose story the New Testament tells at both ends: a name and a father at the start, and, uniquely among the Twelve, two accounts of his death. He stands last in every list, always tagged “who also betrayed him”; John adds that he kept the disciples’ purse and pilfered from it; the Synoptics fix his price at thirty pieces of silver. Then he dies — but the two death-notices do not read alike. Matthew has him fling the silver into the temple and hang himself, after which the priests buy a potter’s field with the blood-money and call it “the field of blood”; Acts has Judas himself buy a field and fall headlong, bursting open, the field named “field of blood” for the blood of his death. Papias, a generation later, supplies a third and grislier end — a monstrous swelling — and the Middle Ages an entire fabulous biography. This page sets out, earliest source first, how the memory of Judas was told and re-told: the spare but double-ended New Testament record, Papias’s swollen death, the 2nd-century Gnostic Gospel of Judas that made the traitor a hero, the medieval Oedipus-legend of the Golden Legend and Dante’s Satan-chewed Judas, and the modern scholarship that weighs the famous divergences. This is a biographical tradition-timeline: its “witnesses” are the datable sources that report on Judas, and the genuinely contested questions — do Matthew and Acts contradict on his death? on the field? why does Matthew credit the oracle to Jeremiah? — are weighed on their own argument pages below, kindred to the Scripture-crux cases treated elsewhere on this wiki. Timeline

The New Testament (c. 30; written c. 50–95)

The New Testament gives Judas more than most of the Twelve: a surname (“Iscariot,” most likely ’îsh-Qêriyôth, “man of Kerioth” in Judah — perhaps the one Judean among Galileans), a father (Simon), a role (keeper of the common bag, and, John says, a thief), and a crime (handing Jesus over for thirty pieces of silver). It also, alone among the apostles, records his death twice. Matthew has Judas repent, throw the silver into the temple, and “hanged himself”; the priests then buy the potter’s field for the blood-money, so the field is called “the field of blood” and the whole tied to a prophecy of “Jeremy.” Acts, in Peter’s speech, has Judas himself “purchase a field with the reward of iniquity; and falling headlong, he burst asunder in the midst, and all his bowels gushed out,” the field named “Aceldama… the field of blood.” The two notices agree on a violent death and a bloody field but differ on how he died, who bought it, and why it bears its name — the divergences weighed under Arguments below.1

Papias of Hierapolis (c. 100–130)

Papias, among the earliest post-apostolic writers, preserves a third account of Judas’s end, surviving as a quotation in Apollinaris of Laodicea: not a hanging and not a plain fall, but a grotesque swelling — “his body having swollen to such an extent that he could not pass where a chariot could pass easily, he was crushed by the chariot, so that his bowels gushed out.” It keeps the gushing bowels of Acts but supplies a new cause, and later writers (Theophylact) piled on further horrors — worms, a stench, blindness. Within a generation or two of Acts, the manner of Judas’s death was already multiplying.2

The Gospel of Judas (2nd c.; Coptic copy c. 300)

Against the whole canonical picture stands a 2nd-century Gnostic text that turns the traitor into the hero. Denounced by Irenaeus about 180 and lost until a Coptic copy was published amid great publicity in 2006, the Gospel of Judas makes Judas the one disciple who truly knows Jesus — “you are from the immortal realm of Barbelo” — and, on its first reading, casts the betrayal as a service Jesus himself commanded, freeing his spirit from the flesh. (A revisionist school reads the Sethian author as in fact damning Judas.) It is late, Gnostic, and dependent on the canonical Gospels: no witness to the historical Judas, but a vivid witness to how a sect reimagined him.3

The Golden Legend and Dante (c. 1260–1320)

By the high Middle Ages Judas had acquired a full fabulous life. Jacobus de Voragine’s Golden Legend (c. 1260), telling it as the preamble to the life of Matthias, gives an Oedipus-tale: an infant exposed on the sea after a dream that he would “destroy their people,” who grows up to kill his own father and unknowingly marry his mother before coming to Christ for mercy — a story Voragine himself twice brands apocryphal and “not authentic.” Dante meanwhile fixed Judas at the absolute bottom of Hell, head-first in the central mouth of Satan, in the ring named Giudecca for him. It is late legend — church and poetic imagination, a millennium and more after the events — not history.4

Modern scholarship

Critical study returns Judas to the two spare, divergent death-notices and asks the obvious questions. On the manner of death, much turns on two observations: that each notice sits close to an Old Testament pattern — Matthew shadowing the traitor Ahithophel, who “hanged himself” (2 Samuel 17:23), and Acts using the very words of Wisdom 4:19 for the ruin of the ungodly — and that Acts’ famous “headlong” is a translator’s gloss for a word that means “prostrate,” so that it may describe the state of the body rather than a rival cause of death. Scholars divide accordingly between one death told twice and two independent traditions. On the field, the divergence over who bought it and why it is named runs the same way. And Matthew’s ascription of the thirty-pieces oracle to “Jeremy” when the words are chiefly Zechariah’s is a long-noticed crux with several proposed solutions. What is not in doubt is the core: a member of the Twelve named Judas handed Jesus over and died soon after; the shape of that death is where the sources part company.5

Arguments

Related arguments — the two accounts of Judas’s end

Matthew and Acts both report Judas’s death and the field bought with his betrayal-money, and both call the field “the field of blood” — but they diverge on how he died, who bought the field, and why it bears its name. The two arguments below weigh the two halves of that same divergence, and they are best read as a pair, because they come out the same way: in each, the divergence is thinner than the English translations make it look, the reconciling reading is the church’s ancient one rather than a modern repair, and in neither case is it demonstrated against the scholarship that reads the two notices as independent traditions.

Matthew’s hanging and Acts’ bursting: one death, told twice (assessment: plausible)

The manner of death — Acts does not in fact say Judas fell from a height. Prēnēs genomenos means “having become prostrate,” a description of the body’s state rather than a plunge, and “headlong” enters English only with the Geneva Bible; Matthew supplies the means, and a hanged body in Jerusalem at Passover would have been taken down within the day under the law. So the two notices can describe one death without importing anything, and the reading is embedded in the Latin text of Acts from the fourth century and stated outright in the Greek commentary tradition. It falls short of settled because Acts’ two operative words are the two words of Wisdom 4:19, a known pattern for the ruin of the wicked; because Papias soon tells a third version; and because most who work closely on Acts read the notices as independent traditions. The popular props — a decomposing corpse, a snapped rope, a cliff — do not bear weight; the interpretation stands without them, and is plausible.6

Matthew’s priests and Acts’ Judas: one purchase, one name (assessment: plausible)

The field — Acts does not say Judas bought the field, and it does not say why the field is called the field of blood. Ektēsato means “acquired, got” — Luke never uses it of a cash purchase — so the money-man is credited with what his money did, which is how the commentators from Chrysostom to Theophylact read it, as ordinary idiom rather than as a repair. And where Matthew gives a cause for the name (“wherefore that field was called…”), Acts gives only a result, with the subject unexpressed: the affair became known, so that the field was called Akeldama. The second etymology usually set against Matthew is an inference from Luke’s silence rather than a statement of his text. It falls short of settled because the only blood Acts’ own narrative supplies is Judas’s, so that inference is natural and ancient — Chrysostom drew it; because the Aramaic behind the name is unstable enough that both explanations may be later rationalizations; and because most who work closely on Acts read the two verses as separate traditions attached to one real plot of ground. The popular props — a Jewish “fiction of law,” a causative in the Greek, two different fields — do not bear weight; the interpretation stands without them, and is plausible.7

Why does Matthew credit the thirty-pieces oracle to “Jeremy the prophet”? (assessment: sound)

The prophecy citation — Matthew 27:9–10 credits “Jeremy” with an oracle whose words are mostly Zechariah’s. The best reading takes it as a deliberate composite: Zechariah’s words set in a frame of Jeremian imagery (the potter, a purchased field, a place renamed for innocent blood, Jeremiah 18–19 and 32), credited to the senior/governing prophet — a technique Matthew and Mark both use elsewhere. This is the mainstream explanation among Matthean commentators and defeats the charge of a simple blunder; what remains underdetermined is not whether the citation is deliberate but why Jeremiah in particular is named, where several sub-explanations compete. The weaker fallbacks (Jeremiah as the head-scroll of the prophets; a lost Jeremiah apocryphon) are unnecessary. The interpretation is sound.8
