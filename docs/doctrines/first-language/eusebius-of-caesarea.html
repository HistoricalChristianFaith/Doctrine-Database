Eusebius of Caesarea on the first language

Dates: c. 313–322 (Praeparatio Evangelica; Eusebius c. 260–339) · Wikipedia
Summary: Eusebius affirms the two narrow supports of the Hebrew-primacy case — the antiquity of the Hebrews and the derivation of their name (and tongue) from the patriarch Heber, "the man that passes over" — and preserves the Babel account of the one confused language. He does not state the developed thesis that Hebrew survived Babel in Heber's line; that is Augustine's. His witness is to the etymology and the antiquity, not to the strong claim.

Claims & evidence

The name "Hebrew" derives from Heber, "the man that passes over"

And any one diligently studying the Hebrew language would discover great correctness of names current among that people: since the very name which is the appellation of the whole race has been derived from Heber; and this means the man that "passes over," since both a passage and the one who passes over are called in the Hebrew language "Heber."

— Praeparatio Evangelica (Preparation for the Gospel) XI.6, "On the correctness of Hebrew names," trans. E. H. Gifford, tertullian.org. Eusebius roots the very name of the people, and their language, in Heber — the etymological hinge the Hebrew-primacy reading turns on. He frames it within the claim (following Moses, he says, before Plato) that Hebrew names are given "by nature and not conventionally," the opposite pole from the convention view.

Heber and Abraham are the ancient Hebrews from whom the name came, older than Moses

Now that Moses appeared in the world later in time than those former true Hebrews, Heber and Abraham, from whom the derived name has been applied to the people, and than all the other godly men of old, is manifest from his own history.

— Praeparatio Evangelica X.14, trans. Gifford, tertullian.org. Eusebius's apologetic burden — that the Hebrews and their wisdom are older than the Greeks — makes Heber the eponymous forefather of the race and its speech, reinforcing Hebrew's claim to antiquity.

The one language confused at Babel (preserving the older accounts)

And the place in which they built the tower is now called Babylon, because of the confusion of what at first was clear in their language. For the Hebrews call confusion "Babel."

— Praeparatio Evangelica IX.14, trans. Gifford, tertullian.org. Gathering Abydenus's pagan Babel-legend beside the scriptural account, Eusebius takes as common ground the one primeval speech "at first … clear," broken into the many tongues — the premise of the whole debate.

Notes

  • The narrow claim only. Eusebius supplies the etymology (Hebrew ← Heber) and the antiquity of the Hebrews, and he preserves the Babel narrative — but he does not develop the thesis that Hebrew was the primeval tongue kept through Heber's line across the confusion. That fully-formed argument belongs to Augustine (City of God 16.11); it should not be read back into Eusebius.
  • Eusebius's insistence that Hebrew names are "given by nature and not conventionally" sets him squarely against Camp C (language as human convention), though his positive concern is apologetic chronology, not a theory of the first tongue.
