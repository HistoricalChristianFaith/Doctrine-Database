Bede on the first language

Dates: c. 720 (Commentary on Genesis; Bede c. 673–735) · Wikipedia
Summary: Bede carries the Augustinian reading into the medieval West. On Genesis 11:1 he takes as given the one primeval speech — "the whole earth had one language and the same words" — broken at Babel as the punishment of pride, and sets Babel ("Confusion") against Jerusalem and Pentecost, where the Spirit reverses the scattering by uniting the tongues. He does not argue Hebrew primacy so much as assume the single original tongue and its confusion, in the line downstream of Augustine.

Claims & evidence

One language before Babel; its confusion the punishment of pride

Now the whole earth had one language and the same words. Because having anticipated saying that the sons of Noah had divided the earth according to clans, languages, territories, and nations, it returns to show how humans were separated from each other: where it is most clear that as long as the human race served its Creator with due humility, it also harmonized with itself in peaceful charity. But once it raised its neck against its Author in pride, it was soon justly punished and could not have peace even with itself.

— Commentary on Genesis (Hexaemeron), on Gen 11:1, historicalchristian.faith. Bede assumes the single primeval speech and, like Augustine, reads its division as a penalty for the pride of Babel — the confusion of tongues as the undoing of a peace the one language had expressed.

Babel ("Confusion") against Jerusalem and Pentecost

the grace of our Lord … who gave the knowledge of all languages to His disciples faithfully adhering to Him by sending the Spirit from above; thus by the wondrous transformation of the right hand of the Most High, just as here, through the division of languages due to pride, the nations were dispersed throughout the world, so there, due to the merit of humility, the diversity of languages brought together … resonated with one unvarying confession and faith … and deservedly so, this city, in which languages were divided and nations dispersed, is called Babylon, that is, Confusion; the other is called Jerusalem, that is, Vision of Peace, in which, through the united languages of all nations in the praise of God, concord was made.

— Commentary on Genesis, on Gen 11:1, historicalchristian.faith. Bede frames the whole history typologically: Babel's scattering of the one tongue is answered at Pentecost, where the Spirit does not restore a single language but gathers the many into one confession — Confusion healed by the Vision of Peace.

Notes

  • Bede's Genesis 11 comment attests the premise the Hebrew-primacy case rests on — one original tongue, confused at Babel — and stands avowedly in the Augustinian tradition that names that tongue Hebrew; here his own stress falls on the Babel → Pentecost typology rather than on identifying the language.
  • He is the terminus of this timeline's patristic-to-early-medieval Latin line: Augustine → Isidore → Bede, by whom the reading passes into the standard medieval commentary.
