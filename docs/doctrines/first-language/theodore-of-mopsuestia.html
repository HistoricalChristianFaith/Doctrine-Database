Theodore of Mopsuestia on the first language

Dates: c. 400 (commentary on Genesis; Theodore c. 350–428) · Wikipedia
Summary: Theodore is the fountainhead of the rival Camp B — that Aramaic, not Hebrew, was the first tongue. His reported reading makes the speech God used with Adam an Aramaic dialect ("Nabataean," "purer than Syriac"), the language of Babel that alone survived the confusion unchanged; Hebrew is then no primeval mother-tongue but a latecomer. His Greek Genesis commentary survives only in fragments, so the view reaches us through his East-Syrian heirs and a tenth-century Muslim bibliographer, not from a page in his own hand.

Claims & evidence

God spoke to Adam in Aramaic ("Nabataean"), the first and purest tongue — ⚠ unverified at first hand

God, Blessed and Exalted, addressed Adam in the Nabataean dialect, which was purer than the Syriac one. The people of Bābil also used to speak it. Then when God made a babel of tongues, the nations being scattered to their districts and localities, the language of the people of Bābil was unchanged, but the Nabataean spoken by the villagers became a broken Syriac incorrectly pronounced.

— Ibn al-Nadīm, Fihrist ("Remarks about the Syriac Script"), quoting "Theodore the Commentator" from his commentary on the first book of the Torah (c. 987), trans. Bayard Dodge, vol. 1, p. 22, kroraina.com (scan: archive.org). "Nabataean" here is the Aramaic of lower Mesopotamia; the claim is squarely an Aramaic-primacy thesis, the reverse of the Hebrew-primacy case that Augustine and Isidore build. The wording is verifiable in the Fihrist, but as a report of Theodore's own view it is a reception attribution, not confirmable from a surviving autograph — hence flagged unverified.

The identification and the East-Syrian reception

Samir Khalil Samir (Le Muséon 90, 1977) identified the "Theodore the Commentator" of the Fihrist as Theodore of Mopsuestia, the great Antiochene exegete whom the Church of the East revered simply as "the Interpreter." The same Aramaic-primacy reading is carried by his East-Syrian heirs — Isho’dad of Merv (Commentary on Genesis, CSCO 126, p. 134) and Theodore bar Koni — who transmit the view that the primeval tongue was Syriac/Aramaic rather than Hebrew. It is through this stream, not a firsthand Greek text, that Theodore becomes the head of Camp B.

Notes

  • Why the caveat. Theodore's Genesis commentary survives only in catena fragments, so the primeval-Aramaic thesis is known from its reception. Sergey Minov (Memory and Identity in the Syriac Cave of Treasures, Leiden: Brill, 2021, pp. 272–283) cautions that some positions later East-Syrians ascribed to "the Interpreter" may not be authentically his. The attribution is well-attested and credible, but the strongest wording is not verified from an autograph — so on the summary the primeval-Aramaic claim carries a ⚠ unverified flag.
  • Against Camp A. Where Josephus, Eusebius, Jerome, and Augustine root the very name and tongue "Hebrew" in the patriarch Heber, Theodore makes Aramaic the mother-speech and Hebrew a later branch — the position Theodoret of Cyrus gives its most developed patristic form a half-century on.
  • The afterlife. The Aramaic-primacy claim Theodore heads had a vigorous medieval life in the Christian and Muslim East: the same Fihrist passage reports a Christian legend that an angel taught Adam to write Syriac; a later story even inverts the Septuagint legend to make Syriac the tongue of revelation; and al-Mas‘ūdī restates Theodore's exact thesis a century on — drawing a Karaite rebuttal for Hebrew. These are gathered under Reception on the summary.
  • The rival etymology bound up with this camp — deriving "Hebrew" not from Heber but from ʿebrā / περάτης, "one who crosses over" (Abraham the Hebrew, Gen 14:13) — is weighed on its own argument page alongside the Heber derivation.
