Jacob of Edessa on the first language

Dates: c. 700 (Letter XIII to John the Stylite; Hexaemeron, c. 705–708; Jacob c. 633–708) · Wikipedia
Summary: Jacob is a Camp A witness of unusual value — a Syriac writer defending Hebrew as the Adamic, primeval language against the Syriac/Aramaic-primacy view that dominated his own tradition. Where nearly every other late-antique Syriac author (above all the Church of the East, following Theodore of Mopsuestia) made Aramaic the first tongue, Jacob insists Hebrew came first, "beloved of God," kept by the line of Eber after Babel. His stance is a deliberate, minority choice from inside the Syriac world.

Claims & evidence

Hebrew, not Syriac, was the first language — "beloved of God"

His correspondent John the Stylite of Litarba put two questions to him: whether the Hebrews were really named after Eber (Gen 10:21, 25), and whether Hebrew was the original language. Jacob answers both for Camp A. Hebrew, he replies, is indeed the original language of all — not Syriac/Aramaic, as (in Alison Salvesen's rendering) "many people erroneously suppose, even some great and illustrious ones." He finds sufficient proof in the testimony of "Clement the disciple of the apostle Peter" that the single language before the division of tongues was Hebrew, raḥīm l-ʾalāhā — "beloved of God."

— Jacob of Edessa, Letter XIII to John the Stylite, as translated and discussed in Alison G. Salvesen, "'Hebrew, Beloved of God': The Adamic Language in the Thought of Jacob, Bishop of Edessa (c. 633–708 CE)," in Hebrew between Jews and Christians (Berlin: De Gruyter, 2022), pp. 49–66, at 50–51 (open-access copy). Earlier in the same letter Jacob explains that after the dispersion from Babel the descendants of Eber remained in Chaldea, and "these people alone continued to speak the original Adamic language of Hebrew."

The Hebrews named after Eber — not from Abraham's "crossing"

To John's first question Jacob answers that the name "Hebrews" derives from the patriarch Eber, an ancestor of Abraham whose descendants preserved the first, Adamic language. He rejects the rival derivation — that "Hebrew" comes from Abraham's crossing (ʾēbar) the Euphrates — as the error of an "absurd" writer who leaned on the Septuagint's περάτης ("emigrant / one who crosses over") at Gen 14:13. Since that rival etymology is the one Theodore of Mopsuestia and Theodoret of Cyrus built into the Aramaic-primacy case, Jacob's reply is, in effect, a direct answer to Camp B from within the Syriac church.

The Hebrews "possess the first, Adamic tongue" (the Hexaemeron)

The same conviction surfaces in Jacob's last work, the Hexaemeron, where he explains that the Syriac word for "heaven," šēmāyā, is not native Aramaic at all but a loanword taken from Hebrew:

Now, among the Hebrews, who possess the first, Adamic tongue, this expression is used in the singular sense, while also being understood and written in the plural. For they say šōmā in the singular, and šōmāīm in the plural. And as far as it is possible to compare, this particular expression is a composite form from the term for water. For mā is how they say "water" in the singular, and māīm in the plural.

— Jacob of Edessa, Hexaemeron (ed. Chabot, CSCO 92, p. 76), trans. Salvesen, "'Hebrew, Beloved of God'," p. 57. The linguistic argument runs on the premise that Hebrew is prior and Syriac the borrower — the reverse of Camp B. Elsewhere in the same work he calls Hebrew "the ancient and revered language of the Hebrews."

Notes

  • Why he matters. Jacob's position is, as Salvesen notes, "an isolated one" among Syriac writers: Milka Rubin observes that "apart from Jacob, almost all Syriac writers support the view that Syriac or Aramaic is the original language of Creation." That makes him the strongest Camp A counter-witness from within the tradition that most favored Camp B — a Syriac bishop resisting his own tradition's pull.
  • His sources are Camp A's. Jacob leans not on Byzantine Greek authorities but on the pre-Chalcedonian Syrian Greek Eusebius of Emesa, on the sub-apostolic "Clement" (the Pseudo-Clementine tradition), and on Jubilees — the same stream that carries the Hebrew-primacy reading from Augustine into the Latin West.
  • Against Camp B by name. Salvesen reads the "absurd person" of Letter XIII as Theodore of Mopsuestia and his followers; Jacob's insistence on Hebrew is a deliberate, theologically charged answer to the Church of the East's Aramaic-primacy view. See Theodore of Mopsuestia and Theodoret of Cyrus on the other side, and Jerome and Isidore for the parallel Latin case.
  • Primary loci. Letter XIII to John the Stylite: William Wright, "Two Epistles of Mar Jacob, Bishop of Edessa," Journal of Sacred Literature and Biblical Record NS 10 (1867), 430–460 (Syriac); French translation by François Nau, "Traduction des lettres XII et XIII de Jacques d'Édesse," Revue de l'Orient Chrétien 10 (1905), 197–208, 258–282 (esp. 273–274), archive.org. The Hexaemeron: ed. Jean-Baptiste Chabot, Iacobi Edesseni Hexaemeron (CSCO 92, Paris, 1928); Latin trans. Arthur Vaschalde (CSCO 97, 1932).
