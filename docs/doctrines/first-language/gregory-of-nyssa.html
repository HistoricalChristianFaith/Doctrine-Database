Gregory of Nyssa on the first language

Dates: c. 381 (Against Eunomius, "Answer to Eunomius' Second Book"; Gregory c. 335–395) · Wikipedia
Summary: Gregory is the anchor of Camp C, which denies the shared premise of the other two camps — that any earthly tongue is the divinely-given primordial language. Answering Eunomius, who held that names were divinely instituted (a "name-realism" that made Hebrew the God-given first speech), Gregory argues that God gave only the faculty of speech; the particular words of every language are the invention of the human mind. He denies that God is a Hebrew-speaker and treats Hebrew itself as a comparatively recent, Mosaic-era tongue — one of the many languages that arose after Babel, not the mother of them all.

Claims & evidence

Babel confounds an existing tongue; God does not create languages

Gregory heads off the obvious objection — that the confusion of tongues shows God as the author of languages — by reading Genesis 11 precisely the other way: at Babel God confounded the one existing speech, He did not create new ones. The words themselves are human coinage, differing from nation to nation while the things named stay the same:

And if any one cites the confusion of tongues that took place at the building of the tower, as contradicting what I have said, not even there is God spoken of as creating men's languages, but as confounding the existing one, that all might not hear all. … For a stone or a stick does not seem one thing to one man and another to another, but the different peoples call them by different names. So that our position remains unshaken, that human language is the invention of the human mind or understanding.

— Gregory of Nyssa, Against Eunomius, "Answer to Eunomius' Second Book" §276, trans. H. C. Ogle & H. A. Wilson (NPNF2 5), ccel.org. The same passage is preserved verbatim in the commentary on Genesis 11:1–9 at historicalchristian.faith.

God is no Hebrew-speaker; Hebrew is a later tongue

The sting for Camp A is what follows. If human language is human invention, then no tongue — Hebrew included — is God's own; and Moses himself wrote not in some primordial divine speech but in one of the languages that arose after the tower, the vernacular he had grown up with:

But God, willing that men should speak different languages, gave human nature full liberty to formulate arbitrary sounds, so as to render their meaning more intelligible. Accordingly, Moses, who lived many generations after the building of the tower, uses one of the subsequent languages in his historical narrative of the creation, and attributes certain words to God, relating these things in his own tongue in which he had been brought up, and with which he was familiar …

— Answer to Eunomius' Second Book §276, ccel.org. Hebrew here is explicitly "one of the subsequent languages" — a tongue of the Mosaic era, not the speech of Eden. On this account the whole Hebrew-primacy edifice (Heber's line preserving a God-given first language) rests on a category mistake: God never spoke Hebrew, or any human tongue, at all.

The many names for "heaven": no language is privileged

Against Eunomius's claim that the fitness of names to things reveals God as their maker, Gregory presses the sheer multiplicity of words each nation uses for one and the same object — a diversity that makes nonsense of singling out any one tongue as the divine one:

For we call it Heaven, the Hebrew calls it Samaim, the Roman coelum, other names are given to it by the Syrian, the Mede, the Cappadocian, the African, the Scythian, the Thracian, the Egyptian … Which of these, then, tell me, is the appropriate word wherein the great wisdom of God is manifested? If you prefer the Greek to the rest, the Egyptian haply will confront you with his own. And if you give the first place to the Hebrew, there is the Syrian to claim precedence for his own word …

— Answer to Eunomius' Second Book, newadvent.org. The names, he concludes, are "adapted to the things according to the vernacular language of each several race" — God made the things, men made the words. The Hebrew's Samaim has no more claim to be the divine name of heaven than the Roman's coelum.

Notes

  • The target is Eunomius's name-realism. Gregory's argument is polemical, not a free-standing treatise on Babel. Eunomius held that names were instituted by God and encode the true natures of their objects — a doctrine that, applied to Scripture, underwrote the idea of Hebrew as the God-given primordial tongue. Gregory answers with a thoroughgoing conventionalism: language is ἐπίνοια, the mind's own devising, and God's role is to have given the capacity for speech, not the lexicon. His case tells decisively against that strong thesis; whether it also reaches the modest claim — that at least the revealed divine names carry a God-given significance, as Origen held — is weighed on its own page (assessment: contested), where the two theses are kept apart.
  • Where he sits on the timeline. Gregory does not take the Hebrew-vs-Syriac question and answer it differently — he rejects the question. Both Augustine and Isidore (Hebrew) and Theodore and Theodoret (Syriac) assume that some surviving tongue is the original, divinely-given speech; Gregory denies that any human tongue holds that rank. He therefore stands apart from both camps as the classic patristic statement that the first language was human convention, not a divine gift.
  • Text and dating. The passages are from the "Answer to Eunomius' Second Book" (Book II in the Jaeger renumbering of the Contra Eunomium; long printed as "Book XII" in older editions), c. 380–381. English in NPNF2 5, trans. H. C. Ogle and H. A. Wilson (CCEL / New Advent).
