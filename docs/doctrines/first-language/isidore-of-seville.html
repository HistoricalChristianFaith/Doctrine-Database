Isidore of Seville on the first language

Dates: c. 615–636 (Etymologiae; Isidore c. 560–636, archbishop of Seville) · Wikipedia
Summary: Isidore gives the most systematic statement of Hebrew primacy in the Latin tradition. In the Etymologies — the encyclopaedia that formed the medieval mind — he states flatly that before Babel "there was one language for all nations, which is called Hebrew," that Adam named the animals in Hebrew, "the language of all peoples" before the Flood, and that Hebrew is "the mother of all languages and letters." He also rebuts the Syriac/Chaldean rival head-on.

Claims & evidence

Before Babel there was one language, Hebrew

The diversity of languages arose with the building of the Tower after the Flood, for before the pride of that Tower divided human society, so that there arose a diversity of meaningful sounds, there was one language for all nations, which is called Hebrew. The patriarchs and prophets used this language not only in their speech, but also in the sacred writings.

— Etymologies IX.i.1 ("The languages of nations"), trans. Barney, Lewis, Beach & Berghof (Cambridge, 2006), sfponline.org (PDF). Isidore reproduces the Augustinian frame — one tongue until Babel, kept by the patriarchs — as settled encyclopaedic fact.

Adam named the animals in Hebrew, the language of all before the Flood

The different nations have also given names to each of the animals in their own languages – for Adam did not assign these names in the Latin or Greek language, or in any of the languages of foreign nations, but in that language which, before the Flood, was the language of all peoples, which is called Hebrew.

— Etymologies XII.i.2 (Animals), trans. Barney et al., sfponline.org (PDF). Isidore pushes Hebrew back past Babel to Eden itself: the primal name-giving of Genesis 2 was in Hebrew, "the language of all peoples" from the first.

Hebrew is the mother of all languages and letters

A transliterator fashioned the letter of one language from the similar sound of another language … hence we can know that the Hebrew language is the mother of all languages and letters.

— Etymologies I.iii.4 ("The common letters of the alphabet"), trans. Barney et al., sfponline.org (PDF). Isidore derives even the alphabets — Greek alpha from Hebrew aleph, and Latin from Greek — from Hebrew, so that Hebrew is parent not only of tongues but of scripts. The phrase echoes Jerome's omnium linguarum matrix.

Against the Syriac/Chaldean rival: Daniel's children had to be taught Chaldean

But some think that Hebrew is Chaldean, because Abraham sprang from the Chaldeans. However, if this is accepted, how is it that the Hebrew children in the Book of Daniel (1:4) are ordered to be taught the Chaldean language, which they do not know?

— Etymologies IX.i.9, trans. Barney et al., sfponline.org (PDF). Isidore meets Camp B (Syriac/Aramaic primacy) directly: if the Hebrew youths in Babylon had to be taught Chaldean (Dan 1:4), Chaldean was not their mother tongue — so Hebrew is not a dialect of Chaldean, but its own, older speech.

Notes

  • Isidore is the fullest and most influential Latin systematization of the reading Augustine anchored — and, through the Etymologies' medieval ubiquity, the channel by which "Hebrew was the first language" became a European commonplace.
  • His rebuttal at IX.i.9 shows the Syriac-primacy view was already a live rival to be answered, not a medieval afterthought — the very debate this doctrine's timeline tracks.
