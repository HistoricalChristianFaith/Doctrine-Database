The Pseudo-Clementine Recognitions on the first language

Dates: c. 3rd–4th c. (the underlying Greek romance; extant in Rufinus's Latin translation, c. 406) · Wikipedia
Summary: The Recognitions gives the earliest flat statement of the developed thesis: the Hebrew language was given by God to men and was the sole tongue of the race until idolatry and the dispersion. Where Josephus supplies the etymology, this text supplies the doctrine — a divinely-given original Hebrew, held until the sixteenth generation.

Claims & evidence

The Hebrew tongue, God-given, "bore sole sway" until idolatry

In the fourteenth generation one of the cursed progeny first erected an altar to demons, for the purpose of magical arts, and offered there bloody sacrifices. In the fifteenth generation, for the first time, men set up an idol and worshipped it. Until that time the Hebrew language, which had been given by God to men, bore sole sway. In the sixteenth generation the sons of men migrated from the east, and, coming to the lands that had been assigned to their fathers, each one marked the place of his own allotment by his own name.

— Recognitions of Clement I.30, newadvent.org. The claim is threefold and unqualified: Hebrew was the language of mankind; it was given by God, not humanly invented; and it "bore sole sway" until the generations of idolatry and dispersion — precisely the Hebrew-primacy reading, stated as fact.

Notes

  • This is the first witness on the timeline to assert not merely the Heber etymology but the full doctrine — a divinely-given primeval Hebrew — placing it against the rival Camp C view (the first-language debate's third camp) that language is human convention.
  • The Recognitions survives only in the Latin of Rufinus of Aquileia (c. 406); its Greek source is a 3rd–4th-century apostolic romance falsely ascribed to Clement of Rome. The witness is therefore to a widely-circulated tradition of the period, not to a named author.
