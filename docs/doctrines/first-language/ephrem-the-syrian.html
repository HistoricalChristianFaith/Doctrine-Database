Ephrem the Syrian on the first language

Dates: c. 363 (Commentary on Genesis; Ephrem c. 306–373) · Wikipedia
Summary: Ephrem is a caveat, not a Camp B witness. The greatest of the Syriac fathers holds that mankind once shared a single primeval speech and that one tongue alone survived the confusion of Babel — but in his authentic works he never names that surviving language, neither Hebrew nor Syriac. The strong Syriac-primacy claim later hung on his name is pseudo-Ephremic: it belongs to the Cave of Treasures, a sixth-/seventh-century narrative falsely ascribed to him, and should not be read back into Ephrem himself.

Claims & evidence

One primeval tongue, and one survivor at Babel — left unnamed

Commenting on Genesis 11, Ephrem takes the pre-Babel world to have shared "a single language," and reads the confusion as the loss of that original speech by every nation save one — without ever saying which nation kept it:

It is likely that they lost their common language when they received these new languages. For if their original language had not perished their first deed would not have come to nothing. It was when they lost their original language, which was lost by all the nations, with one exception, that their first building came to nought.

— Ephrem, Commentary on Genesis VIII (on Gen 11), trans. Edward G. Mathews & Joseph P. Amar, St. Ephrem the Syrian: Selected Prose Works (Fathers of the Church 91), historicalchristian.faith. The "one exception" is the crux: Ephrem affirms a surviving primeval tongue but leaves it anonymous. Yuliya Minets, surveying his authentic writings, concludes that "his story's primordial language remains nameless" — his genuine works are "surprisingly equivocal concerning the pre-Babel language."

Notes

  • Do not misattribute Ephrem to Camp B. The emphatic assertion that Syriac was the original tongue, older than Hebrew, comes from the Cave of Treasures — a pseudepigraphal history dated to the mid-sixth or early seventh century and ascribed to Ephrem, but not his. Sergey Minov (Memory and Identity in the Syriac Cave of Treasures, Leiden: Brill, 2021) situates that text in Syrian Orthodox circles of the period; it, not Ephrem, is the likely origin of the popular "Ephrem said Syriac came first" claim. Ephrem's own hymns and Genesis commentary do not name the primeval language.
  • Where he sits on the timeline. Ephrem shares the common premise of the whole discussion — one original speech, broken at Babel — with both camps, while declining the step that divides them. He therefore stands as a witness to the shared framework, and as a correction to a frequent misreading, rather than as a partisan of Hebrew (Augustine, Isidore) or of Aramaic (Theodore of Mopsuestia, Theodoret of Cyrus).
  • Source. Yuliya Minets, "The Primordial Language in Ephrem the Syrian," Hugoye: Journal of Syriac Studies 25:2 (2022), 313–363 (open-access copy); the passage quoted is Commentary on Genesis §8.3–8.4.
