John Chrysostom on the first language

Dates: c. 388 (the Homilies on Genesis, preached at Antioch; d. 407) · Wikipedia
Summary: Chrysostom holds the middle Camp A line — Hebrew via Heber — and holds it from within Antioch, the very school (Theodore, Theodoret) that pressed the rival Syriac-primacy view. In Homily 30 on the Tower of Babel (Gen 11:1–9) he treats the one pre-Babel tongue as a privilege given by God, and its confusion as a measured chastisement of human pride — a restraint on wickedness, not a bare curse. He endorses the tradition that Heber alone kept his pre-confusion language, so that it would stand as an enduring proof of the division of tongues — the Antiochene form of the "Hebrew through Heber" reading. He does, however, garble an etymology: elsewhere he takes the name "Abram" to mean "crossing," confusing it with 'Eber.

Claims & evidence

The shared tongue was a privilege from God; its confusion a chastisement of wickedness

This in fact is the way the Lord is accustomed to behave. This is what he did in the beginning in the case of the [first] woman as well. She had abused the status conferred on her, and for that reason he subjected her to her husband. Again, too, in the case of Adam, since he drew no advantage from the great ease he enjoyed and from life in the garden but rather rendered himself liable to punishment through the fall, God drove him out of the garden and inflicted on him everlasting punishment in the words "thorns and thistles let the earth yield." So when the people in the present case, who had been dignified with similarity of language, used the privilege given them for evil purposes, he put a stop to the impulse of their wickedness through creating differences in language. "Let us confuse their speech," he says, "so that they will be unable to understand one another's language." His purpose was that, just as similarity of language achieved their living together, so difference in language might cause dispersal among them.

— Homilies on Genesis 30.13 (on Gen 11:7), trans. Robert C. Hill, historicalchristian.faith.1 The one shared language is a "privilege given them" — a gift, "dignified with similarity of language" — and its confusion is purposive discipline that "put a stop to the impulse of their wickedness," not an arbitrary stroke. The parallels he chooses (Eve, Adam) are all penalties that fit an abused gift.

The building of Babel was human overreach — the pattern that drew the chastisement

Notice how the human race, instead of managing to keep to its own boundaries, always longs for more and reaches out for greater things. This is what the human race has lost in particular, not being prepared to recognize the limitations of its own condition but always lusting after more, entertaining ambitions beyond its capacity … See how in gradual stages it teaches us the instability of their attitude.

— Homilies on Genesis 30.5 (on Gen 11:2), trans. Hill, historicalchristian.faith.2 The confusion of tongues answers a specific fault — prideful reaching "beyond its capacity" — which is why Chrysostom reads it as a chastisement rather than a caprice.

Heber alone kept his pre-confusion language, as a standing proof of the division

Closing the same Babel homily, Chrysostom lists the reminders God left so that the memory of the confusion "would last for all time" — the conflict of tongues, the son's name Phalek ("division"), and the place-name Babylon ("Confusion") — and then adds Heber (Eber) to that list of standing signs:

Notice the extent of the action taken to ensure remembrance of it would last for all time: first the conflict of tongues, or rather prior to this the giving of the name—the name Phalek, remember, which Eber gave his son, means division. Next, the place name: the place was called Confusion, which is what Babylon means. Then, Eber kept the same language as he had had before, so that this too should prove an unmistakable sign of the conflict.

— Homilies on Genesis 30.13 (on Gen 11:8–9), trans. Robert C. Hill.3 So Heber (Eber) retained the language he had held before the confusion, and that retention is itself one of the lasting proofs that the tongues had truly been divided — the Antiochene form of the "Hebrew through Heber" middle line shared by Augustine and, before him, Origen. On this reading Hebrew is no latecomer but the primeval speech preserved in one line — the reverse of the Syriac-primacy thesis his Antiochene colleagues drew from the same chapter. Hill notes the remark "gives rise to some perplexity in his editors," who ask whether Chrysostom means no more than that "Eber is the eponymous ancestor of the Hebrews"; but the plain sense — that Eber alone kept his earlier tongue as an "unmistakable sign" of the division — is the reading Michael Eskhult too draws, grouping Chrysostom with Diodorus of Tarsus and Augustine as holding that the primeval language survived in the house of Heber.

Notes

  • A Camp A voice from inside Camp B's heartland. Chrysostom preached at Antioch, the school from which Theodore of Mopsuestia and Theodoret of Cyrus derived the rival claim that Aramaic/Syriac was the older tongue and Hebrew a later, "mixed" speech. That he keeps the Hebrew-via-Heber line makes him, like the later Syriac bishop Jacob of Edessa, a witness against the home-team view — though, unlike Jacob, he never argues it against the Syriac case explicitly.
  • ⚠ Overreach to flag — a garbled etymology. In his homilies on Abraham (Homilies on Genesis 35 and 40) Chrysostom is under the misapprehension that the name "Abram" itself means "crossing" in Hebrew and Syriac — plainly confusing it with 'Eber / 'ābar ("to cross over"), the root that actually underlies "Hebrew." The same slip recurs in the Sermons on Genesis (Περάτης, "Abram in the Syrians' tongue means 'the one beyond'"). The error is harmless to his conclusion — both names sit on the "crossing over" tradition either way — but it is a real etymological mistake: "Abram" does not mean "crossing."4
  • Chrysostom appears on this wiki on several other Genesis timelines, including the Flood and the Nephilim (where the same 67 Homilies on Genesis are dated, with Quasten and Hill, securely to his Antiochene years, c. 388).
