Epiphanius of Salamis on the first language

Dates: c. 375 (Panarion, Book I, begun c. 374–377; Epiphanius c. 310/320–403) · Wikipedia
Summary: Epiphanius is the clearest "agnostic" witness — he affirms the premise both Hebrew-primacy (Camp A) and Aramaic-primacy (Camp B) build on, that before Babel mankind was "of one speech and one language," yet he never names that primeval tongue. In the long proem to the Panarion he sketches the ages of the world as four "mother-sects" — Barbarism (Adam to Noah), Scythianism (Noah to the tower), Hellenism, and Judaism — and locates a proto-Christian godliness (θεοσεβεία) that runs from the creation, before the division of tongues and before Abraham. The first men, on his account, were "neither Jew nor Greek." He knows the Eber tradition — he calls Eber "the pious and godfearing" — but hangs no Hebrew-primacy claim on it.

Claims & evidence

One speech before Babel — left unnamed

Epiphanius reads the pre-Flood world (his "Barbarism") and the world down to the tower (his "Scythianism") as a single undivided humanity of one tongue. Of the age of Jared he writes that though wickedness had appeared, in speech and stock all were still one:

However there was no divergent opinion, no changed belief; there was one language, and one stock which had been planted on earth at that time.

— Panarion, Proem, Sect 1 (Barbarism) §1,4. And of the generations from Noah to the building of Babylon, citing Genesis 11:1:

And there was nothing on earth, no sect, no opinion clashing with another one, but only "men" were spoken of, "of one speech and one language." There were only ungodliness and godliness, the natural law and the natural error of each individual's will … There was no Judaism, no Hellenism, no other sect at all.

— Panarion, Proem, Sect 2 (Scythianism) §2,3. Both passages affirm a single primeval speech and its confusion at Babel — the framework the two naming camps share — but Epiphanius declines to say which language it was. He does not call it Hebrew, and he does not call it Syriac; the tongue stays anonymous, as it does in Ephrem.

Godliness before the division of tongues, before Abraham

His governing thesis is that true religion — the faith "now native to God's present day holy catholic church" — is not a late arrival but reaches back to the creation, threaded through these first ages before any nation, language, or sect had divided off:

But the character of true religion existed as did the natural law, and was practiced apart from these peoples, marking itself off amid Barbarism, Scythianism and Hellenism from the foundation of the world and onwards until it was combined with the true religion of Abraham.

— Panarion, Proem, Sect 3 (Hellenism) §3,9. Because this godliness precedes the confusion of tongues, the primeval language for Epiphanius is theologically incidental: what matters is that Adam and the patriarchs down to Eber kept the true worship, not that they spoke any privileged sacred speech. Adam, he argues, "without circumcision … was no Jew," and being no idolater "was no Hellene" either — so the first men belonged to no named nation or tongue.

Eber known, but not for his language

Epiphanius does register the figure on whom the Hebrew-primacy case turns. Tracing the line after the Flood he reaches "Shelah … the father of Eber, the pious and godfearing. Eber was the father of Peleg" (Panarion §2,2), and he counts Eber among the righteous "down to Abraham" (§2,6). But he draws the Camp A inference from none of it: where Eusebius, Augustine, and Jerome read Eber's name and Peleg's "division" as proof that Hebrew was the surviving mother-tongue, Epiphanius praises Eber's piety and says nothing of his speech. He is a witness who had the materials of the Hebrew thesis in hand and did not build it.

Notes

  • Where he sits on the timeline. Epiphanius shares the premise of the whole discussion — one original speech, broken at Babel — with both naming camps, while declining the step that divides them, exactly as Ephrem does a decade earlier. Together the two form the "agnostic" strand: the framework is agreed, the identity of the first tongue left open. He is not a partisan of Hebrew (Augustine, Isidore) or of Aramaic (Theodore of Mopsuestia, Theodoret of Cyrus), and he does not go as far as Gregory of Nyssa, who denies that any earthly tongue is a divine gift at all — Epiphanius simply leaves the question unraised.
  • Dating. The Panarion ("Medicine-chest against heresies") was composed c. 374–377; this material stands in its opening proem (the "mother-sects"), which frames the whole work. Epiphanius's later Weights and Measures (c. 392) is a separate treatise and does not develop a first-language doctrine.
  • Related agnostic / context witnesses. Gregory of Nazianzus, Oration 41 (On Pentecost) §16, sets "the old Confusion of tongues" at Babel against its reversal by "One Spirit" at Pentecost — the Babel↔Pentecost frame later taken up by Bede — but names no primeval language. Earlier still, Clement of Alexandria (Stromata 1.21) reports the "seventy-two dialects" produced at Babel and argues the antiquity of the Hebrew people, but makes no first-language claim, and Irenaeus (Against Heresies 2.24.2) defends the antiquity of the Hebrew alphabet — a point about script, not spoken speech. None of these three asserts the primeval tongue was Hebrew.
  • Source. Epiphanius, The Panarion of Epiphanius of Salamis, Book I (Sects 1–46), trans. Frank Williams, 2nd rev. ed. (Leiden: Brill, 2009), Proem, Sects 1–3, pp. 15–17 (PDF).
