Origen on the first language

Dates: c. 248 (Against Celsus / Contra Celsum; Origen c. 185–253) · Wikipedia
Summary: Origen straddles the debate. On the primeval-unity side he is Camp A-adjacent: mankind once shared "one divine language" that fractured at Babel, after which the nations were parcelled out among angels (Deut 32:8–9 LXX) while Israel/Jacob remained "the portion of the Lord." He does not put the name "Hebrew" on the surviving tongue — he calls it the "eastern language" kept by those who never left the east — but he sets it decisively apart. For on the other side Origen is the sharpest patristic voice for name-realism: the Hebrew divine names (Sabaoth, Adonai) are "possessed of great power" and lose it in translation, because names are by nature, not by convention. That anti-conventional stance is the direct foil to Gregory of Nyssa (Camp C).

Claims & evidence

One "divine language," Babel, and the nations allotted to angels

In Against Celsus V, defending the Mosaic account of the confusion of tongues, Origen reads Genesis 11 together with Deuteronomy 32:8 in the Septuagint ("He set the bounds of the people according to the number of the angels of God; and the portion was His people Jacob"). Behind the "historic narrative" he finds a mystery: one primeval speech, harmoniously shared, and lost by degrees as men turned from the "east":

All the people upon the earth are to be regarded as having used one divine language, and so long as they lived harmoniously together were preserved in the use of this divine language, and they remained without moving from the east so long as they were imbued with the sentiments of the "light" … each one was handed over (in proportion to the greater or less departure from the east which had taken place among them …) to angels of character more or less severe … and they were conducted by those angels, who imprinted on each his native language, to the different parts of the earth according to their deserts.

— Origen, Against Celsus V.30, trans. Frederick Crombie (ANF 4), newadvent.org. The survivors, he continues, are those who never migrated:

… those who preserved their original language continued, by reason of their not having migrated from the east, in possession of the east, and of their eastern language. And let him notice, that these alone became the portion of the Lord, and His people who were called Jacob, and Israel the cord of His inheritance.

— Against Celsus V.31, newadvent.org. The premise Origen shares with Camp A is here in full — one primeval tongue, preserved by one people through Babel — and he ties that people to Jacob/Israel, i.e. the Hebrews. But he stops short of Augustine's later, explicit step of naming the survivor "Hebrew" and running the etymology through Heber; Origen's surviving speech is "the eastern language," left unnamed.

Name-realism: Hebrew divine names have power, and lose it in translation

The complementary half of Origen's position appears in Against Celsus I, answering Celsus's claim that it "makes no difference" whether God is called Zeus, Sabaoth, or Adonai. Origen replies that the nature of names is "a deep and mysterious subject," and sides against the conventionalists (Aristotle, Epicurus): the Hebrew divine names are not interchangeable labels but carry real, untranslatable efficacy.

… the name Sabaoth, and Adonai, and the other names treated with so much reverence among the Hebrews, are not applicable to any ordinary created things, but belong to a secret theology which refers to the Framer of all things. These names, accordingly, when pronounced with that attendant train of circumstances which is appropriate to their nature, are possessed of great power.

— Against Celsus I.24, newadvent.org. And the efficacy is bound to the words themselves, not to what they signify — so translation destroys it:

… those who are skilled in the use of incantations, relate that the utterance of the same incantation in its proper language can accomplish what the spell professes to do; but when translated into any other tongue, it is observed to become inefficacious and feeble. And thus it is not the things signified, but the qualities and peculiarities of words, which possess a certain power for this or that purpose.

— Against Celsus I.25, newadvent.org. This is the exact reverse of Gregory of Nyssa's conventionalism. Where Gregory holds that "human language is the invention of the human mind" and that the Hebrew Samaim has no more claim than the Roman coelum, Origen holds that the Hebrew sacred names are effective by nature and cannot be swapped for equivalents. On the question of whether the first tongue is a divine gift or a human convention, Origen and Gregory mark the two poles.

Notes

  • Where he sits on the timeline. Origen is the earliest Christian witness here after Josephus, and the earliest to weld the primeval-unity narrative to Deuteronomy 32:8–9 and the allotment-to-angels scheme. He supplies the framework Camp A later fills in: one divine language, one surviving people (Jacob/Israel), a confusion that is punitive. What he does not supply is the name — that is Augustine's contribution, and it should not be read back into Origen.
  • Camp A by implication, not by assertion. Because he ties the surviving "eastern" tongue to Israel and treats the Hebrew divine names as uniquely potent, Origen plainly reckons Hebrew the privileged speech; but the caution is worth keeping — he never says "Hebrew" of the pre-Babel language, and his interest is allegorical (the "east" as the light, the migration as the soul's descent) as much as historical.
  • The foil to Camp C. Origen's name-realism is the anti-conventional pole against which Gregory of Nyssa's conventionalism is best read. The divine-gift reading Origen's name-realism serves is weighed on its own page, was the primordial language a divine gift? (assessment: plausible), against the rival conventionalist reading (assessment: sound).
  • Text and dating. Against Celsus (Contra Celsum) was written c. 248, near the end of Origen's life (c. 185–253). English in ANF 4, trans. Frederick Crombie (Book I / Book V).
