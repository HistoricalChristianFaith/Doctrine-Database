Theodoret of Cyrus on the first language

Dates: c. 453 (Questions on the Octateuch; Theodoret c. 393–458/466) · Wikipedia
Summary: A native Syrian bishop who read Syriac, Theodoret gives Camp B its most developed patristic form: Syriac (Aramaic) is more ancient than Hebrew — Hebrew is the sacred tongue, but not the oldest. He rejects the derivation of "Hebrew" from the patriarch Heber, and derives the name instead from a word for "crossing over," making Abraham "the Hebrew" the crosser of the Euphrates. He is heir to Theodore of Mopsuestia and stands squarely against the Hebrew-primacy line of Augustine and Isidore.

Claims & evidence

Syriac/Aramaic is more ancient than Hebrew — ⚠ unverified at first hand

In the Questions on Genesis §§60–61 Theodoret holds that Syriac (Aramaic) is the older speech and Hebrew a later, sacred tongue — venerable, but not the primeval mother-language. The exact English is locked in the modern critical edition (Robert C. Hill & John F. Petruccione, Theodoret of Cyrus: The Questions on the Octateuch, vol. 1, Washington: CUA Press, 2007; Greek in Patrologia Graeca 80), from which the wording is not freely extractable, so this claim is credited to §§60–61 but flagged unverified until the passage can be quoted firsthand. That Theodoret worked from the Syriac and the Greek versions and knew no Hebrew is well established, and fits a reading that placed Syriac at the root.

"Hebrew" is not from Heber — the Hebrews were not Eber's only descendants

Theodoret (Quaest. in Genesis 61) urges against it that the Hebrews were not the only descendants of Eber, and, therefore, could not appropriate his name.

— McClintock & Strong, Cyclopaedia of Biblical, Theological, and Ecclesiastical Literature, s.v. "Hebrew," biblicalcyclopedia.com, reporting Theodoret's objection. Where Camp A makes the patriarch Heber the eponym of the whole race and its tongue, Theodoret cuts the etymological hinge: many peoples descend from Eber, so his name cannot single out the Hebrews.

The name comes from "crossing over" — Abraham the Hebrew who crossed the Euphrates

In place of the Heber derivation Theodoret takes "Hebrew" from ʿebrā / περάτης ("crosser," "one from beyond") — Abraham "the Hebrew" (Gen 14:13), so named for crossing the Euphrates out of Chaldea (Questions on Genesis §62). The same "one who passes over" sense that Eusebius had attached to Heber, Theodoret redirects to Abraham, detaching name and tongue from the Hebrew-primacy line. (The rival etymologies — Heber vs. "crosser" — are weighed on their own argument page.)

Notes

  • Source-text gap. Theodoret's Questions on Genesis is not among his works surveyed in full here, and the modern English (Hill & Petruccione, 2007) is under copyright; §§60–62 are therefore cited from the Greek (PG 80) and the standard reference literature, with the primeval-Syriac wording marked ⚠ unverified on the summary pending a firsthand quotation.
  • Sacred, not oldest. Theodoret does not disparage Hebrew — he grants it is the holy tongue of Scripture. His point is chronological: Aramaic came first. This is the careful, sociolinguistic version of the thesis Theodore states more baldly.
