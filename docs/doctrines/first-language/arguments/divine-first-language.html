Was the primordial language a divine gift? (divine tongue / name-realism)

Related arguments: the origin of language — see also was language a human convention? (Gregory of Nyssa).

Type: underlying argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: plausible — the strong form of this reading (that God authored a specific primordial language, with words divinely instituted, so that Hebrew is literally God's own speech) does not hold on the exegesis, which has God confounding an existing tongue, not creating one; but the modest form — that at least the revealed divine names carry a God-given, non-arbitrary significance — is coherent and unrefuted, though on the evidence its proponents adduce it lands as plausible rather than proven · advanced (strong form) by Eunomius and the Pseudo-Clementine Recognitions, and (modest name-realism) by Origen; answered by Gregory of Nyssa's conventionalism.

The claim

Beneath the quarrel over which tongue came first (Hebrew? Aramaic?) lies a deeper one: did God give humanity a specific primordial language — words fitted by nature to their objects, divinely instituted rather than humanly coined — or only the faculty of speech, the words themselves being human convention? This page states the first reading: language, or at least the sacred names, is God's own institution. It is the old Cratylus question (naturalism about names) answered on the naturalist side, and mapped onto Genesis:

… the name Sabaoth, and Adonai, and the other names treated with so much reverence among the Hebrews, are not applicable to any ordinary created things, but belong to a secret theology which refers to the Framer of all things. These names, accordingly … are possessed of great power … it is not the things signified, but the qualities and peculiarities of words, which possess a certain power.

— Origen, Against Celsus I.24–25 (name-realism: words efficacious by nature), newadvent.org.1

Who advances it

The reading has a strong and a modest form, and they must be kept apart. The strong form is Eunomius's: names are divinely instituted and encode the true natures of their objects, so language (and pre-eminently the sacred tongue) is God's own institution — it is this thesis Gregory of Nyssa writes to refute.2 The same strong claim surfaces flatly in the Pseudo-Clementine lore, where "the Hebrew language, which had been given by God to men, bore sole sway" until the idolatry of the fifteenth generation (Recognitions I.30).3 The modest form is Origen's: he need not claim a whole God-authored language, only that the revealed divine names (Sabaoth, Adonai) carry real, untranslatable power — that here, at least, the word is by nature, not convention.4

The evidence for

Scripture puts naming near the origin: God creates by speaking, and the LORD brings the animals to Adam "to see what he would call them: and whatsoever Adam called every living creature, that was the name thereof" (Gen 2:19) — a scene long read as fitting, God-guided naming, not arbitrary labelling.5 And the reverence surrounding the divine names is a real datum: the Tetragrammaton was held too holy to pronounce, and translation was felt to lose something — the intuition Origen presses into a theory. On this reading the sacred names are not interchangeable ciphers; the word itself bears power. Held in its modest form, the claim asks only that some revealed words are given rather than coined — a far narrower thing than a whole God-authored tongue.

Adversarial assessment

The strong form fails on the exegesis. Pressed to its strong form — that God instituted the words of a language, so that a particular human tongue is divine — this reading does not hold, and it fails on the very text it leans on. Genesis 11:7 has God say "let us go down, and there confound their language" (Hebrew bālal, "to mix, confound"), not create one; the narrative gives God scrambling a speech men already shared, never instituting a lexicon. Hebrew, moreover, is demonstrably one Semitic language among cognate others (Aramaic, Arabic), sharing their grammar and drift, not a mother-tongue standing outside the family; and the arbitrariness of the linguistic sign — that the same object is coelum, Samaim, ouranos with no natural bond between sound and thing — is now a settled observation of linguistics, not merely a conjecture. This is Gregory of Nyssa's case, weighed on its own page; against the strong divine-tongue thesis it wins.6

But the modest name-realism survives. That God did not author Hebrew does not entail that no word anywhere is divinely instituted. Origen's datum is narrower and tougher: certain revealed names (the Tetragrammaton, the titles of God) are presented in Scripture as given, not coined, and are treated across Judaism and early Christianity as bearing a weight their translations do not carry. A thoroughgoing conventionalism must still absorb that, and Gregory's argument — built to refute Eunomius's whole-language claim — does not by itself reach it: one can hold that language in general is human convention and that God revealed particular names with particular force. The modest name-realism is compatible with most of what conventionalism proves.

Weigh the name-efficacy on its evidence, not on the metaphysics. That God could invest a name with power is not in question, and the claim is not weak merely for being supernatural. But possibility is not proof: the evidence Origen actually adduces — that spells work in their "proper language" and fail in translation — leans on the reported practice of incantation, a thin and dubious base, and the felt untranslatability of holy names is as easily explained by reverence and long usage as by an intrinsic natural power. So the modest thesis lands as coherent and unrefuted rather than demonstrated.

Assessment

Plausible. Taken in its strong form — that God instituted the words of a specific primordial language, making Hebrew literally His own speech — the reading fails: Genesis 11:7 has God confounding an existing tongue, not creating one, and Hebrew is plainly one Semitic language among its cognates, not their supernatural mother. But this argument is best weighed in its steelman, the modest name-realism Origen presses: that at least the revealed divine names carry a God-given, non-arbitrary significance. That claim a flat conventionalism must still account for; it is coherent and unrefuted, and it costs the concession that language is largely human convention nothing. On the evidence its proponents actually offer, though, it is plausible rather than proven — a live datum, not a demonstration. So the divine-gift reading rates plausible at its best, with its strong cousin failing on the exegesis. Its rival, Gregory's account of language as human convention, is weighed on its own page (assessment: sound).
