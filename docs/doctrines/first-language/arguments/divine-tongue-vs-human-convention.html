Was the primordial language a divine gift, or a human convention?

Type: underlying argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — the strong thesis that God authored a specific primordial tongue with non-arbitrary words (so that Hebrew is literally God's own language) does not hold on the exegesis, which says God confounded an existing speech, not that He created one; but the modest thesis — that at least the revealed divine names carry a God-given, non-arbitrary significance — is not refuted by conventionalism and remains a live datum, so strong voices sit on both sides with no patristic consensus · advanced (name-realism / divine tongue) by Eunomius and, for the efficacy of the divine names, Origen; answered (conventionalism) by Gregory of Nyssa.

The claim

Beneath the quarrel over which tongue came first (Hebrew? Aramaic?) lies a deeper one: did God give humanity a specific primordial language — words that are fitted by nature to their objects, divinely instituted rather than humanly coined — or only the faculty of speech, the words themselves being human convention? This is the old Cratylus question (naturalism vs. conventionalism about names) mapped onto Genesis. The two readings pull in opposite directions:

… the name Sabaoth, and Adonai, and the other names treated with so much reverence among the Hebrews, are not applicable to any ordinary created things, but belong to a secret theology which refers to the Framer of all things. These names, accordingly … are possessed of great power … it is not the things signified, but the qualities and peculiarities of words, which possess a certain power.

— Origen, Against Celsus I.24–25 (name-realism: words efficacious by nature), newadvent.org.

… not even there is God spoken of as creating men's languages, but as confounding the existing one … human language is the invention of the human mind or understanding.

— Gregory of Nyssa, Against Eunomius, "Answer to Eunomius' Second Book" §276 (conventionalism: God gave the capacity, men coined the words), ccel.org.

Who advances it

The divine-tongue side has a strong and a modest form, and they must be kept apart. The strong form is Eunomius's: names are divinely instituted and encode the true natures of their objects, so language (and pre-eminently the sacred tongue) is God's own institution — it is this thesis Gregory writes to refute.1 The same strong claim surfaces flatly in Camp A's lore, where "the Hebrew language, which had been given by God to men, bore sole sway" until the idolatry of the fifteenth generation (Pseudo-Clementine Recognitions I.30).2 The modest form is Origen's: he need not claim a whole God-authored language, only that the revealed divine names (Sabaoth, Adonai) carry real, untranslatable power — that here, at least, the word is by nature, not convention.3

Against both stands Gregory of Nyssa. Reading Genesis 11:7 — "let us go down, and there confound their language" — he insists the text has God confounding an existing tongue, not authoring new ones; the words of every language are ἐπίνοια, the mind's own devising. He drives it home against Hebrew primacy: God is no Hebrew-speaker, Moses "who lived many generations after the building of the tower" writes in one of the later tongues, and the sheer multiplicity of words for one thing (Hebrew Samaim, Roman coelum, and the Syrian's and Mede's besides) shows no tongue to be privileged.4

The evidence for

For a divine tongue / name-realism. Scripture puts naming near the origin: God creates by speaking, and the LORD brings the animals to Adam "to see what he would call them: and whatsoever Adam called every living creature, that was the name thereof" (Gen 2:19) — a scene long read as fitting, God-guided naming, not arbitrary labelling.5 And the reverence surrounding the divine names is a real datum: the Tetragrammaton was held too holy to pronounce, and translation was felt to lose something — the intuition Origen presses into a theory. On this reading the sacred names are not interchangeable ciphers; the word itself bears power.

For convention. Gregory's exegetical wedge is genuinely sharp. Genesis 11:7 says confound (Hebrew bālal), not create; the narrative gives God dispersing and scrambling a speech men already shared, never instituting a lexicon. Moses, writing Genesis in Hebrew, uses a Semitic tongue that sits among the world's languages rather than above them — so the very words he "puts in God's mouth" are his own vernacular, not a transcript of divine speech. And that one object bears countless unrelated names across the nations is exactly what a conventional, human-coined vocabulary predicts and a single God-given lexicon does not.6

Adversarial assessment

The two theses are not symmetric. Gregory's target is Eunomius's strong claim — that God instituted the words of a language, so that a particular human tongue is divine — and against that claim his case is strong. "Confound, not create" is the plain sense of Genesis 11:7; Hebrew is demonstrably one Semitic language among cognate others (Aramaic, Arabic), sharing their grammar and drift, not a mother-tongue standing outside the family; and the arbitrariness of the linguistic sign — that the same object is coelum, Samaim, ouranos with no natural link between sound and thing — is now a settled observation of linguistics, not merely Gregory's conjecture. On the strong thesis, the conventionalist has the better of it.

But conventionalism over-extends if it is pressed to dispatch the modest claim too. That God did not author Hebrew does not entail that no word anywhere is divinely instituted. Origen's datum is narrower and tougher: certain revealed names (the Tetragrammaton, the titles of God) are presented in Scripture as given, not coined, and are treated across Judaism and early Christianity as bearing a weight their translations do not carry. A thoroughgoing conventionalism must still account for that, and Gregory's argument — built to refute Eunomius — does not by itself reach it: one can hold that language in general is human convention and that God revealed particular names with particular force. The modest name-realism is compatible with most of what Gregory proves.

Weigh the name-efficacy on its evidence, not on the metaphysics. That God could invest a name with power is not in question here, and Origen's claim is not weak merely for being supernatural. But possibility is not proof: the evidence Origen actually adduces — that spells work in their "proper language" and fail in translation — leans on the reported practice of incantation, a thin and dubious base, and the felt untranslatability of holy names is as easily explained by reverence and long usage as by an intrinsic natural power. So the modest thesis lands as coherent and unrefuted rather than demonstrated — plausible, not established. Its strong cousin (a whole God-authored primordial language, non-arbitrary throughout) is the one that fails, and it fails on the exegesis Gregory presses, not on any naturalistic premise.

Assessment

Contested. This is a genuine, unresolved patristic dispute with strong voices on both sides. The strong divine-tongue thesis — that God instituted the words of a specific primordial language, making Hebrew literally His own speech — does not hold: Genesis 11:7 has God confounding an existing tongue, not creating one, and Hebrew is plainly one Semitic language among its cognates, not their supernatural mother. Gregory's conventionalism wins there. But he wins against Eunomius's overreach, not against the modest claim that at least the revealed divine names carry a God-given, non-arbitrary significance — a datum Origen presses that a flat conventionalism must still absorb, and which is coherent and unrefuted (though, on the evidence Origen offers, plausible rather than proven). Because the strong reading is weak and the modest reading is live, and no patristic consensus settled between the divine-gift and human-convention accounts of language, the question is best rated contested. Keep the two theses distinct: the concession that language is largely human convention costs the modest name-realism nothing.
