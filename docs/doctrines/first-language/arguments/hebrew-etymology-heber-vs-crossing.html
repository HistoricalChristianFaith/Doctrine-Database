The etymology of "Hebrew" — from the patriarch Heber, or from "crossing over"?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — the derivation of "Hebrew" from the patriarch Heber is grammatically possible and was the majority ancient reading, but it was disputed already in antiquity and is doubted by modern philology, and either way it under-determines the primacy claim it is made to serve · advanced by Augustine (with the wider Camp A line), against Theodoret of Cyrus.

The claim

… there was still the house of Heber in which the primitive language of the race survived … because … his family preserved that language which is not unreasonably believed to have been the common language of the race, it was on this account thenceforth named Hebrew.

— Augustine, The City of God 16.11, trans. Marcus Dods, newadvent.org. The name "Hebrew," on this reading, is an inheritance from the patriarch Heber (Eber; Gen 10:21, 24–25), in whose line the primeval tongue outlasted Babel — so the etymology itself testifies that Hebrew is that primeval tongue. The rival camp cuts the hinge: "Hebrew" names not descent from Heber but a crossing over — Abraham "the Hebrew" who crossed the Euphrates — and so carries no primacy claim at all.

Who advances it

The Heber derivation is the common property of Camp A: Josephus derives the people's name from "Heber, from whom they originally called the Jews Hebrews," Eusebius from "Heber … the man that passes over," Jerome and Isidore likewise, and Augustine argues it most fully — in City of God 16.3 he weighs and rejects the rival derivation from Abraham, fixing name and language on Heber.1 Against it stands Camp B: Theodoret of Cyrus rejects the eponym — "the Hebrews were not the only descendants of Eber, and, therefore, could not appropriate his name" — and derives "Hebrew" instead from ʿebrā / περάτης ("crosser," "one from beyond"), i.e. Abraham the Hebrew who crossed the river out of Chaldea (Gen 14:13), heir here to Theodore of Mopsuestia.2

The evidence for

For Heber. The gentilic ʿibrî ("Hebrew") can be formed straightforwardly from the personal name ʿēber, and the Genesis genealogy sets Eber at the head of the line (Gen 10:21, "the father of all the children of Eber") and puts his son Peleg — whose name glosses "division" — precisely at the Babel dispersion (Gen 10:25). Reading the family name off the patriarch who sits at the dividing of the tongues is the majority patristic and rabbinic tradition.3

For "crossing over." The name belongs to the root ʿ-b-r, "to cross, pass over" — and ʿēber itself means "the region beyond, across." Joshua 24:2–3 places Abraham's ancestors "on the other side of the flood" (ʿēber hannāhār, beyond the River), and Genesis 14:13 calls Abram hāʿibrî, which the Septuagint renders Ἁ περάτης, "the one who crossed over." The two etymologies are therefore not cleanly separable: because the patriarch's own name means "beyond," Camp A's "Heber" and Camp B's "crosser" both rest on the same root — which is why Eusebius could gloss Heber himself as "the man that passes over" while still counting the name a Hebrew-primacy proof.4

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The name genuinely belongs to the ʿ-b-r root, and the derivation from the patriarch is grammatically unobjectionable — a gentilic from an ancestor's name is an ordinary formation, and this was the reading of most ancient interpreters, Jewish and Christian alike. Nothing in the Hebrew forbids it.

What is overstated or imprecise. Neither ancient etymology is secure, and each carries an unrefuted objection. Modern philology largely treats the patriarch Eber as a back-formation — an eponym personified out of the ethnonym ʿibrî to explain it, rather than its true source — and tends to keep the geographic sense, "those from beyond the River," with its Akkadian parallel ʿēbir nāri / Eber-Nari.5 But the "beyond the River" reading has its own grammatical problem — ʿēber never stands alone for "beyond the river" (the phrase is always ʿēber hannāhār), and one would expect such a name to stick to those who stayed beyond the river, not to a people descended from one who left it — and the further proposal linking ʿibrî to the Ḥabiru/ʿApiru (a second-millennium social class of displaced peoples, not an ethnicity) is phonologically contested, not a settled anchor.6 So the dispute is real and unresolved on the linguistics alone. Theodoret's particular objection over-argues in the other direction: that "the Hebrews were not the only descendants of Eber" does not void the eponym, since peoples are routinely named for one ancestor among many (Israel was one of Eber's line with brothers to spare; Ishmael and Esau also descend from Abraham) — flagged on his detail page. Most important, the etymology under-determines the primacy claim either way: even granting "Hebrew ← Heber," that a people bears a patriarch's name does not show their tongue was the world's first — Augustine himself must add a separate argument (the confusion of tongues was a punishment from which God's people were spared) to get from the name to the doctrine, and Camp B grants Hebrew is ancient and sacred while still denying it was primeval.

Assessment

Contested. The derivation of "Hebrew" from the patriarch Heber is grammatically possible and was the majority patristic and rabbinic reading, but it was already disputed in antiquity (Theodoret) and is doubted by modern philology, which tends to read the patriarch Eber as a folk-etymological back-formation and to keep the "beyond-the-River" sense. The two ancient etymologies are not cleanly separable — both rest on the root "to cross over" — and each carries an unrefuted objection, so no reading clearly wins. And the whole dispute under-determines the first-language question: even if the name descends from Heber, a people's bearing a patriarch's name does not establish that their tongue was the world's first — a further, independent step. Treat the etymology as a genuine but inconclusive support for Hebrew primacy, not its decider.
