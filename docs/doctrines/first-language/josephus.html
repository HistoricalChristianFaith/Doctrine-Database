Josephus on the first language

Dates: c. AD 100 (Antiquities of the Jews, c. AD 93–94) · Wikipedia
Summary: Josephus is the Jewish backdrop to the Hebrew-primacy reading. He carries the etymology the Fathers would build on — the Jews were "called Hebrews" from the patriarch Heber (Eber) — and gives the fullest early narrative of Babel, where humanity, once "of one language," was scattered by the confusion of tongues. He does not yet say which tongue survived; that thesis is patristic. But he supplies both of its load-bearing texts: the name "Hebrew" from Heber, and the one primeval speech broken at Babel.

Claims & evidence

The Jews were called "Hebrews" from Heber

Sala was the son of Arphaxad; and his son was Heber, from whom they originally called the Jews Hebrews. Heber begat Joctan and Phaleg: he was called Phaleg, because he was born at the dispersion of the nations to their several countries; for Phaleg among the Hebrews signifies division.

— Antiquities of the Jews, Book I, ch. 6, §4, sacred-texts.com. Josephus derives the very name of the people from Heber (Eber), and reads Peleg's name (Gen 10:25) as marking the "dispersion of the nations" — the same etymological move (Heber → Hebrew, Peleg = "division") the Fathers would turn into an argument for Hebrew's primacy.

Mankind was "of one language" until the confusion at Babel

When God saw that they acted so madly, he did not resolve to destroy them utterly … but he caused a tumult among them, by producing in them diverse languages, and causing that, through the multitude of those languages, they should not be able to understand one another. The place wherein they built the tower is now called Babylon, because of the confusion of that language which they readily understood before; for the Hebrews mean by the word Babel, confusion.

— Antiquities of the Jews, Book I, ch. 4, §3, sacred-texts.com. The single primeval speech "which they readily understood before" is the premise of the whole debate; Josephus corroborates it from the pagan record too, quoting the Sibyl that "when all men were of one language, some of them built a high tower."

Notes

  • Josephus supplies the two proof-texts of the Hebrew-primacy case (Heber → "Hebrews," Gen 10:24; the one tongue confused at Babel, Gen 11:1–9) but stops short of the developed thesis that Hebrew itself was the primeval tongue preserved through Heber's line — that step is Pseudo-Clement's and, fully, Augustine's.
  • The strongest early Jewish statement of Hebrew primacy is not Josephus but the Book of Jubilees (2nd–1st c. BC), which calls Hebrew "the tongue of the creation," lost at Babel and revealed again to Abram (Jubilees 12:25–27) — the extrabiblical seed of the reading Josephus's etymology feeds.
  • Josephus appears on this wiki also as a witness on the Old Testament Canon, the Origin of the Septuagint, the Nephilim, and the Pillars of Seth timelines — the last three all drawing, like this one, on his primeval history in Antiquities Book I.
