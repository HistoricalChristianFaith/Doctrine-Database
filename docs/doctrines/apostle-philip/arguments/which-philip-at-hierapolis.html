Which Philip dwelt and died at Hierapolis — the apostle or the evangelist?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — a genuine and unresolved confusion in the sources. Two early, independent Asian witnesses call the Hierapolis figure “one of the twelve apostles,” yet the daughters they attach to him are the unmistakable signature of Philip the evangelist of Acts. Each reading has real support; neither drives the other from the field.

The claim

…among these are Philip, one of the twelve apostles, who sleeps in Hierapolis, and his two aged virgin daughters, and another daughter who lived in the Holy Spirit and now rests at Ephesus.

— Polycrates of Ephesus, in Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History 3.31, 3. The claim under test is the one the ancient sources make and the whole Asian tradition rests on: that the Philip remembered at Hierapolis — with his grave, his daughters, and (later) his martyrium — was Philip the apostle, one of the Twelve, and not Philip the deacon and evangelist of Acts.1

The evidence for (the apostle)

The identification with the apostle is not a late guess; it is what the earliest and most local witnesses say. Papias, bishop of Hierapolis c. 110–130 and a townsman of the daughters, is the first to place a Philip there, and Eusebius — reporting him — calls him “the Apostle Philip.” Polycrates of Ephesus, c. 190, an old man writing of his own province’s “great lights” and himself of a family of bishops, states flatly that this Philip was “one of the twelve apostles.” These are two independent Asian sources, close in time and place to the grave they describe, and both name the apostle. Eusebius, who had read far more of Papias and Polycrates than survives, “saw no difficulty” in the identification — there was no rival tradition of a second Philip at Hierapolis for him to weigh. A man of the Twelve retiring to Asia is entirely in character with the firm tradition that John and other apostles ended their days there.2

The evidence against (it was the evangelist)

The counter-case turns on a single, powerful coincidence: the daughters. Acts gives four prophesying virgin daughters not to the apostle but to Philip the evangelist, one of the seven, settled at Caesarea: “the same man had four daughters, virgins, which did prophesy” (Acts 21:8–9). The Dialogue of Gaius describes the Hierapolis family in exactly those terms — “four prophetesses, the daughters of Philip” — reproducing Luke’s number and description of the evangelist’s household. On the ordinary rules of evidence the man with four prophesying daughters is the man Acts says had four prophesying daughters. The critical editors of Eusebius draw the natural conclusion: “there is nothing more natural than that later generations should identify the evangelist with the apostle of the same name, and should assume the presence of the latter wherever the former was known to have been.” On this reading Papias knew and prized the daughters of the evangelist as living links to the apostolic age — and the honorific “apostle” crept in afterward.3

Adversarial assessment

Why it stays open. The two lines of evidence pull in opposite directions and are each hard to break. Against the evangelist-reading: it must treat the explicit “one of the twelve apostles” of a careful, local, early witness (Polycrates) as simple error — and Polycrates is not vague, distinguishing the daughters’ different fates and resting-places in a way that reads like real family knowledge, not a borrowing from Acts. Against the apostle-reading: the four-prophetess detail is too specific to Acts’s evangelist to be coincidence, and the very ease with which Eusebius identified the two men is what the critics say produced the error in the first place — an absence of doubt is not evidence of accuracy.

The knot. The sources themselves do not agree even on the daughters: Polycrates gives three (two aged virgins at Hierapolis, one at Ephesus) and does not call them prophetesses; Gaius gives four and does. That internal disagreement is itself a sign that two memories — the apostle’s and the evangelist’s — had already been folded together before our witnesses wrote. It is likely that a Philip lived, raised daughters, and was buried at Hierapolis; which Philip the tradition began with cannot be recovered with confidence from behind the conflation.4

Assessment

Contested. That the Hierapolis Philip was the apostle is asserted by two early and local witnesses and was undoubted in antiquity; that he was really the evangelist is the natural inference from the four-prophesying-daughters signature of Acts, and is the judgment of much modern criticism. Each reading survives the other’s best objection; the daughters’ own count and character are reported inconsistently, betraying a conflation already complete in our earliest sources. The honest verdict is that the two Philips cannot now be cleanly separated — a real and unresolved confusion, not a settled fact for either side.
