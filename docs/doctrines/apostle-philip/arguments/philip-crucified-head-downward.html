Was Philip crucified head-downward at Hierapolis?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — the residence and burial of a Philip at Hierapolis are early and firm, but the manner of death — hung head-downward from a tree by his pierced ankles — appears only in the fourth-to-fifth-century Acts of Philip, and the earliest witness has him simply “fall asleep” there. (Which Philip is buried at Hierapolis is a separate question, weighed here.)

The claim

And he ordered to bring clubs and strong cords; and after piercing Philip’s ankles they brought hooks, and put the cords through his ankles, and hung him head downwards on a tree that was before the door of the temple…

— the Acts of Philip (ANF vol. 8). The claim: Philip died a martyr at Hierapolis, crucified or hung head-downward under the city’s proconsul after converting his wife.1

The evidence for

Two things can be said in the tradition’s favour. First, that a Philip was buried at Hierapolis is among the best-attested facts about any apostle’s later life: Papias (c. 110–130), Polycrates (c. 190), and the Dialogue of Gaius (c. 200) all place his grave there, and a great octagonal martyrium stood on the hill above the city by the fifth century — confirmed, with a first-century tomb beside it, by the 2011 excavations of Francesco D’Andria. Second, veneration of Philip specifically as a martyr is old: the shrine is a martyrium, and the city kept a martyr’s cult. If the church of Hierapolis remembered its Philip as a martyr from early on, that memory deserves a hearing on its own terms.2

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The place is secure: a Philip, his daughters, and his grave belong to Hierapolis on early and independent testimony, and a martyr-cult grew up there. The city’s martyrium is real, and the supernatural is not the problem here — a first-century crucifixion under a Roman magistrate is ordinary enough.

What fails. The manner and even the fact of a martyrdom rest on almost nothing early. The earliest and best witness, Polycrates, says only that Philip “sleeps” (koimētai) at Hierapolis — the ordinary word for a peaceful death, and precisely not the language of martyrdom he might have been expected to use for a “great light” of Asia. Papias and Gaius likewise report a grave, not a killing. The whole vivid death-scene — the proconsul, the converted wife Nicanora, the pierced ankles, the head-downward tree, the preaching from the cross — enters only with the Acts of Philip, a late romance of low biographical value, thick with talking animals and dragons, and visibly modelled on the inverted-cross martyrdom already told of Peter. The fifth-century martyrium proves the church venerated Philip as a martyr; it cannot show that he was one, still less how he died. Critical surveys of the apostles’ deaths accordingly place Philip’s martyrdom in the lowest tier of reliability — a late and legendary tradition.3

Assessment

Weak. That Philip was crucified head-downward at Hierapolis is a fine and enduring image, but it hangs on the late Acts of Philip alone; the early witnesses attest a grave and a cult, not a martyrdom, and the best of them says Philip simply “fell asleep” there. The burial at Hierapolis is solid history; the inverted cross is late legend.
