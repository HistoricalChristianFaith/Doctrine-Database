Papias on Philip and his daughters at Hierapolis

Dates: c. 110–130 (Papias’s Expositions of the Oracles of the Lord, surviving only in fragments quoted by later writers) · Wikipedia
Summary: Papias, bishop of Hierapolis in Phrygia, is the earliest witness to a Philip settled at Hierapolis. Writing of the apostolic memory he had gathered, he names Philip among the apostles whose sayings he sought, and — as Eusebius reports him — he had heard wonders directly from “the daughters of Philip,” his neighbours: a man raised from the dead, and Justus Barsabas surviving a draught of poison. This is the root of the whole Asian Philip-tradition — and already the point where apostle and evangelist may be confused.

Claims & evidence

Philip among the apostles Papias sought out

In the preface to his lost work, Papias explains that he prized the living voice of those who had known the apostles, and lists the apostles whose words he pursued — Philip among them, in the company of Andrew, Peter, Thomas, James, John, and Matthew.

If, then, any one who had attended on the elders came, I asked minutely after their sayings,—what Andrew or Peter said, or what was said by Philip, or by Thomas, or by James, or by John, or by Matthew, or by any other of the Lord’s disciples… For I imagined that what was to be got from books was not so profitable to me as what came from the living and abiding voice.

— Papias, preface, in Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History 3.39, 4.

The daughters of Philip, and the wonders they told

Eusebius, drawing on Papias, reports that Philip and his daughters had lived at Hierapolis, and that Papias — their contemporary and townsman — had the stories from the daughters themselves. Eusebius calls this figure “the Apostle Philip”; Papias’s own reported words say only “Philip” and his daughters.

The residence of the Apostle Philip with his daughters in Hierapolis has been mentioned above. We must now point out how Papias, who lived at the same time, relates that he had received a wonderful narrative from the daughters of Philip. For he relates that a dead man was raised to life in his day. He also mentions another miracle relating to Justus, surnamed Barsabas, how he swallowed a deadly poison, and received no harm, on account of the grace of the Lord.

— Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History 3.39, 9 (on Justus Barsabas, cf. Acts 1:23 and the promise of Mark 16:18).

Notes

  • Earliest, and closest. Papias is not only the first witness to a Philip at Hierapolis but the closest — a resident of the same town, who claimed the daughters as informants. If any source could settle which Philip lived there, it would be this one; but the surviving words, as filtered through Eusebius, name only “Philip” and leave the “apostle” to Eusebius’s own gloss.
  • The daughters as tradents. That Philip’s daughters passed on apostolic stories at Hierapolis is itself the tradition’s backbone — and it is precisely the four prophesying daughters that Acts 21:9 gives to Philip the evangelist. Whether Papias’s Philip is the apostle or the evangelist is weighed on the argument page.
  • No word of a death. Papias reports Philip’s residence and his daughters’ memories, but nothing of a martyrdom — the manner of Philip’s death enters only with the much later Acts of Philip.
