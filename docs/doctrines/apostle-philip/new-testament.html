The New Testament on Philip

Dates: c. 30–90 (the events, and the writing of the Gospels and Acts) · Wikipedia
Summary: The bedrock. Philip of Bethsaida is one of the first Jesus calls; in John’s Gospel he fetches Nathanael, is tested at the feeding of the five thousand, is approached by the enquiring Greeks, and asks at the Last Supper, “Lord, shew us the Father.” He is named in all four lists of the Twelve, and the New Testament says nothing of where he went or how he died. The New Testament also names a second man of the same name — Philip the evangelist, one of the seven deacons — and telling the two apart is essential to reading the later tradition.

Claims & evidence

Of Bethsaida, called early, who brings Nathanael

Philip is of Bethsaida, the town of Andrew and Peter, and is called directly by Jesus with the same word later given to Peter and Matthew — “Follow me.” Like Andrew, his first act is to bring another: he finds Nathanael and answers his scorn for Nazareth with “Come and see.”

The day following Jesus would go forth into Galilee, and findeth Philip, and saith unto him, Follow me. Now Philip was of Bethsaida, the city of Andrew and Peter. Philip findeth Nathanael, and saith unto him, We have found him, of whom Moses in the law, and the prophets, did write, Jesus of Nazareth, the son of Joseph… Philip saith unto him, Come and see.

— John 1:43–46.

Tested before the feeding of the five thousand

Before the one miracle recorded in all four Gospels, John singles Philip out: Jesus asks him where bread is to be bought, “to prove him,” and Philip’s literal-minded answer measures the need in money.

When Jesus then lifted up his eyes, and saw a great company come unto him, he saith unto Philip, Whence shall we buy bread, that these may eat? And this he said to prove him: for he himself knew what he would do. Philip answered him, Two hundred pennyworth of bread is not sufficient for them, that every one of them may take a little.

— John 6:5–7.

Approached by the Greeks; and “shew us the Father”

Philip appears twice more in John. The Greeks who come up to the feast approach him first — perhaps for his Greek name — and he, with Andrew, brings them to Jesus. And at the Last Supper his request draws out one of the Gospel’s great sayings on Christ and the Father.

Philip saith unto him, Lord, shew us the Father, and it sufficeth us. Jesus saith unto him, Have I been so long time with you, and yet hast thou not known me, Philip? he that hath seen me hath seen the Father; and how sayest thou then, Shew us the Father?

— John 14:8–9; the Greeks, “The same came therefore to Philip… Sir, we would see Jesus” (John 12:20–22).

Named in all four lists of the Twelve — always beside Bartholomew

Philip stands fifth in every apostle-list, at the head of the second group of four, and in three of the four he is paired directly with Bartholomew — a pairing the later tradition would keep, sending the two out together.

Philip, and Bartholomew; Thomas, and Matthew the publican; James the son of Alphaeus, and Lebbaeus, whose surname was Thaddaeus…

— Matthew 10:3 (cf. Mark 3:18; Luke 6:14; Acts 1:13).

The other Philip: the evangelist and deacon

Acts names a second Philip, and never confuses him with the apostle. He is one of the seven appointed to serve tables (Acts 6:5), the evangelist who preaches in Samaria and baptizes the Ethiopian eunuch on the Gaza road, and who at last settles at Caesarea, where Paul lodges with him. There Luke notes the detail that would echo through the Philip-tradition of Asia:

And the next day we that were of Paul’s company departed, and came unto Caesarea: and we entered into the house of Philip the evangelist, which was one of the seven; and abode with him. And the same man had four daughters, virgins, which did prophesy.

— Acts 21:8–9 (the Samaritan and eunuch mission, Acts 8:5–40).

Notes

  • The silence about his end. As with most of the Twelve, the New Testament records Philip the apostle’s origin and call but nothing of a mission field or a death; everything on those subjects — Hierapolis, the daughters, the cross — belongs to the later tradition traced on the summary timeline.
  • Two Philips, four prophesying daughters. The one biographical detail Acts gives the evangelist — four unmarried, prophesying daughters — is exactly what the early Asian sources (Papias, Polycrates, the Dialogue of Gaius) attach to the Philip at Hierapolis, whom they call the apostle. Whether the two men were confused is weighed on the argument page.
  • The Greek name. “Philip” is Greek (“lover of horses”), and it is to Philip that the enquiring Greeks come — a small hint of the Hellenistic connections that both the apostle and the evangelist show in the sources.
