The Acts of Philip

Dates: c. 350–450 (an apocryphal romance in fifteen “Acts” plus a Martyrdom, surviving in Greek) · Wikipedia
Summary: The Acts of Philip is a late Greek romance that supplies the martyrdom the early sources never mention. Philip travels with the apostle Bartholomew and his own sister Mariamne — and a talking leopard and kid — to “Ophiorymos,” the “city of the serpent,” identified with Hierapolis, where the people worship a great viper. He converts the proconsul’s wife Nicanora, is seized, and is executed by being hung head-downward from a tree by his pierced ankles, preaching all the while. It is hagiographical romance of low biographical value, but it is the wellspring of Philip’s inverted cross.

Claims & evidence

The mission to the “city of the serpent”

In the surviving narrative Philip, Bartholomew, and Mariamne come to Ophiorymos/Hierapolis, a city given over to the worship of a viper. Philip’s preaching and a healing win over Nicanora, the wife of the proconsul — the conversion that turns the city against him and sets the martyrdom in motion. The tale is thick with the marks of romance: talking animals, dragons, and encratite (rigorously ascetic) teaching that later readers found suspect.

The martyrdom: hung head-downward by the ankles

The proconsul orders Philip hung head-downward from a tree before the temple, his ankles pierced and roped; Bartholomew is bound beside him. From the cross Philip continues to preach, and in the fuller Greek text he calls down the earth to swallow his persecutors, until Christ rebukes him for repaying evil with evil.

And he ordered to bring clubs and strong cords; and after piercing Philip’s ankles they brought hooks, and put the cords through his ankles, and hung him head downwards on a tree that was before the door of the temple; and they fixed pegs into the temple wall, and left him.

— the Acts of Philip (ANF vol. 8, tr. Alexander Walker). A fuller critical translation is François Bovon & Christopher R. Matthews, The Acts of Philip: A New Translation (Baylor University Press, 2012).

Notes

  • The source of the inverted cross. Every element of Philip’s celebrated martyrdom — the head-downward hanging, the pierced ankles, the preaching from the tree — comes from this late text and from nowhere earlier. Polycrates, three centuries before, had said only that Philip “sleeps” at Hierapolis. Whether the crucifixion is history is weighed on the argument page.
  • Low biographical value. The Acts of Philip belongs with the “Leucian” apocryphal Acts — romance-hagiography, valuable as evidence of how Philip was venerated, not as a report of his life. On the class as a whole, see the apostles hub.
  • Bartholomew and Mariamne. The text keeps the Gospel pairing of Philip with Bartholomew (the two stand side by side in three of the four apostle-lists) and adds Philip’s sister Mariamne — details that shape the later legend but rest on no early witness.
  • Ophiorymos and Hierapolis. Most manuscripts equate the fictional “city of the serpent” with Hierapolis, and some append a translation of Philip’s body from Ophiorymos to Hierapolis — the romance reaching to attach itself to the real cult-site the early sources already knew.
