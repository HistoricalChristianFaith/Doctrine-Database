Polycrates of Ephesus and the Dialogue of Gaius on Philip

Dates: c. 190–210 (Polycrates’s letter to Victor of Rome, c. 190; the Dialogue of Gaius, c. 200) · Wikipedia
Summary: Two early, independent Asian voices fix Philip’s home, grave, and daughters at Hierapolis. Polycrates, bishop of Ephesus, defending the Asian date of Easter, names “Philip, one of the twelve apostles,” buried at Hierapolis with his daughters; the anti-Montanist Dialogue of Gaius, quoting the Montanists’ own appeal, points to the tomb of Philip’s four prophesying daughters and their father there. Both are firsthand and valuable — and they disagree on the daughters, in a way that reopens the question of which Philip is meant.

Claims & evidence

Polycrates: Philip “one of the twelve apostles,” asleep at Hierapolis

In the Quartodeciman controversy Polycrates wrote to Victor of Rome, appealing to the “great lights” of Asia who kept Easter on the fourteenth of the moon. He heads the roll with Philip — whom he calls, plainly, one of the Twelve — and locates his grave and his daughters’ at Hierapolis and Ephesus.

For in Asia also great lights have fallen asleep… among these are Philip, one of the twelve apostles, who sleeps in Hierapolis, and his two aged virgin daughters, and another daughter who lived in the Holy Spirit and now rests at Ephesus.

— Polycrates of Ephesus, in Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History 3.31, 3 (the same letter is quoted again at 5.24, 2).

The Dialogue of Gaius: four prophetess-daughters, and their father’s tomb

Eusebius sets beside Polycrates a passage from the Dialogue of Gaius, a Roman churchman writing against the Montanists. To rebut the Montanist appeal to prophecy, Gaius points to the daughters of Philip — genuine prophetesses of the past — and to their tomb, and their father’s, at Hierapolis.

After him there were four prophetesses, the daughters of Philip, at Hierapolis in Asia. Their tomb is there and the tomb of their father.

— the Dialogue of Gaius, in Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History 3.31, 4.

Notes

  • Two independent early witnesses, one place. Polycrates (an Ephesian bishop with local family memory) and Gaius (a Roman drawing on a separate tradition) agree on Hierapolis as the home and grave of Philip and his daughters. This is the strongest early attestation the Philip-tradition has — and it is attestation to a burial, not a martyrdom.
  • The daughters don’t match. Polycrates gives three daughters — two who “grew old as virgins” and lie at Hierapolis, one who “lived in the Holy Spirit” and rests at Ephesus — and does not call them prophetesses. Gaius gives four, and calls them prophetesses — the very word and number Acts 21:9 uses of the daughters of Philip the evangelist. The discrepancy is a live thread in the two-Philips question.
  • “Sleeps,” not “martyred.” Polycrates’s word is the ordinary one for a peaceful death (“fallen asleep”). The earliest and best witness to Philip at Hierapolis knows nothing of a crucifixion; the martyrdom enters only with the late Acts of Philip.
