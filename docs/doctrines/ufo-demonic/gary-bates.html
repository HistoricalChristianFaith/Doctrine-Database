Gary Bates on UFOs and the Demonic

Dates: c. 2005 · creation.com
Summary: Gary Bates, a longtime CEO of Creation Ministries International, is the leading young-earth-creationist voice on the UFO question. His Alien Intrusion: UFOs and the Evolution Connection (2005; "updated and expanded" edition; 2018 documentary Alien Intrusion: Unmasking a Deception) argues that belief in extraterrestrials is a by-product of belief in evolution, that no biological aliens exist, and that the very real abduction phenomenon is a satanic/demonic deception — with the signature evidence that abduction episodes reportedly stop when the victim calls on the name of Jesus Christ.

Claims & evidence

Belief in aliens is a fruit of evolution — and undermines the gospel

Now life was either created or it evolved, and it is because of evolution that most people believe that life evolved elsewhere as well… most Christians wrongly believe God created such life elsewhere, "Otherwise why would He have made the universe so big?"… However, such an idea actually undermines the very gospel of Christ and the reason He came to Earth.

— Lifting the veil on the UFO phenomenon, creation.com. Bates's starting premise is young-earth: if life did not evolve, there is no reason to expect it on other planets, so the "aliens" cannot be what they claim to be.1

The abduction phenomenon is a satanic deception

His research, which specifically deals with the claims of people believing to have been "abducted by aliens", helped me expose the satanic deceptions that underpin these experiences in my book, Alien Intrusion: UFOs and the Evolution Connection.

— Lifting the veil on the UFO phenomenon, creation.com. Bates holds the experiences are real but spiritual: "There is an enormous spiritual battle going on… and it is more widespread than most people realize," the modern UFO movement functioning as "a substitute religion that consciously displaced Christianity and biblical authority."2

Abductions stop in the name of Jesus Christ

To date, he has worked with over 400 people who have had their "abduction" episodes halted in the name of Christ. One of Joe's cases involved a brand-new Christian called Bill D. During an alleged alien abduction he cried out "Jesus, help me!" and the encounter immediately stopped.

— Lifting the veil on the UFO phenomenon, creation.com. This is Bates's load-bearing empirical claim, drawn from the CE4 Research Group's Joe Jordan (a former MUFON state-section director): that the "aliens" answer to the authority of Christ betrays them as demonic rather than extraterrestrial. Bates adds that other investigators privately confirmed such cases but suppressed them as "religious in nature" and "not scientific."3

Aliens are the "age-old demonological phenomenon" in new dress

The UFOs do not seem to exist as tangible, manufactured objects… The thousands of contacts with the entities indicate that they are liars and put-on artists… The UFO manifestations seem to be, by and large, merely minor variations of the age-old demonological phenomenon.

— John A. Keel, quoted by Bates in Prepare ye the way — the aliens are coming!, creation.com. Bates enlists the secular "hostile witnesses" Keel (Operation Trojan Horse, 1970) and Jacques Vallée to establish that the phenomenon is deceptive and non-extraterrestrial, then supplies the theological identification they withhold. He likewise notes the continuity across the centuries — the phenomenon "once took the form of fairies or elves… airships or even flying canoes."4

Notes

  • The film. Bates wrote, produced, and directed the cinema documentary Alien Intrusion: Unmasking a Deception, which premiered worldwide in 2018.5
  • The "name of Jesus" claim is contested. Whether abductions demonstrably halt at the name of Christ — and what that would prove — is the strongest and the most disputed plank of the demonic reading; it is weighed in this doctrine's Arguments section rather than taken as established here.
  • The flat "no biological aliens exist" premise over-reaches. Bates ties the very existence of extraterrestrial life to evolutionary error, so that no creature elsewhere in the cosmos may be expected. That is a metaphysical over-claim beyond the abduction evidence, and a fellow supernaturalist, Michael Heiser, explicitly rejects it: a biological extraterrestrial "is not a problem for Christian theology," and only the anomalous / abduction subset points to the unseen realm. The demonic reading of the anomalous encounters does not require denying that life could exist elsewhere — see the identity-thesis argument page (assessment: contested).
  • Distinct from the Nephilim strand. Unlike Missler, Marzulli, and Horn, Bates does not build his case on a Genesis 6 Nephilim genealogy; his emphasis falls on the abduction data and the evolution/ET link, reading the entities simply as demons (fallen angels), not as the disembodied spirits of dead giants.
