C. S. Lewis on UFOs and the Demonic

Dates: c. 1938 (1898–1963) · Wikipedia
Summary: C. S. Lewis — the Oxford and Cambridge scholar, apologist, and novelist — is the distinguished antecedent of the whole "spirit, not spacecraft" reading, and the witness who shows it in its disciplined form. Across his Ransom trilogy (1938–1945) and the essay "Religion and Rocketry" (1958) he does two things the popular UFO writers later split apart: he imagines fallen angels masquerading as a superior non-human intelligence to be obeyed (the demonic-deception reading in fictional form), and he insists that genuine rational life elsewhere would be no threat to the faith — indeed some worlds may be unfallen, God's good creatures, not demons at all. He predates the flying-saucer-era Christian writers, who do not cite him; but his both-and is exactly the discrimination their strongest case needs.

Claims & evidence

Rational alien life is no threat to the faith — and some worlds may be unfallen

Writing against Fred Hoyle's claim that the vastness of space makes Christianity's story of Earth "silly," Lewis answered in "Religion and Rocketry" (1958) that the objection rests on a chain of unproven assumptions. The mere existence of other rational creatures, he argued, "would not raise a problem"; before it could threaten the faith one would "still need to know that they are fallen; then, that they have not been, or will not be, redeemed." A good God may simply have made worlds that never fell and so need no redemption — "If indeed we were unfallen, it would be another matter." So the modern equation "there cannot be real aliens, therefore these must be demons" is precisely the inference Lewis refuses: genuine extraterrestrial life would be God's creature.1

The vast distances may be God's "quarantine" of a fallen world

I have wondered before now whether the vast astronomical distances may not be God's quarantine precautions. They prevent the spiritual infection of a fallen species from spreading.

— Religion and Rocketry, in The World's Last Night and Other Essays (1960). Lewis's deepest worry runs the opposite way from the UFO writers': not that aliens would harm us, but that we, being fallen, would corrupt them — "We are fallen… Man destroys or enslaves every species he can." The quarantine image — a fallen world sealed off lest its infection spread — is the theological seed of his fiction, where Earth is a planet under containment.2

Earth the "silent planet": fallen angels can masquerade as a higher intelligence

In the Ransom trilogy — Out of the Silent Planet (1938), Perelandra (1943), That Hideous Strength (1945) — Lewis dramatized the very identity the modern writers press. Each planet is governed by an angelic Oyarsa; Earth's Oyarsa rebelled and became "the Bent One," so that Earth is Thulcandra, "the silent planet," under interplanetary quarantine, its air the prison of the "bent eldila." In That Hideous Strength those dark eldila present themselves to the villains of the N.I.C.E. as the "macrobes" — a superior, progressive non-human intelligence whose guidance they eagerly obey, never suspecting they are worshipping fallen angels. Yet the unfallen worlds Malacandra (Mars) and Perelandra (Venus) are real, good, and rational. The trilogy thus holds together exactly what the wholesale "aliens are demons" reading collapses: a deceiving intelligence that apes a higher power is the old enemy in a new costume, while other creatures may be unfallen children of the same Maker.3

Notes

  • Where he sits on this timeline. Lewis writes before the modern UFO literature and is not a source the flying-saucer writers quote; he belongs here as the reading's most distinguished antecedent and its corrective. He supplies the demonic-masquerade imagery the later writers make literal, but with a discipline they often drop: he never denies that biological — even unfallen — life could exist, and he treats the danger as running in both directions.
  • The both-and is the steelman. Lewis's position is the one the careful modern proponents converge on — Michael Heiser (a real alien "is not a problem for Christian theology"; only the anomalous subset looks demonic) and the Catholic astronomers (Funes, Consolmagno) who would treat an intelligent alien as God's creature. It is why the identity thesis is weighed as plausible in its scoped form — much of the anomalous, anti-gospel subset is deception, the rest of the cosmos left open — rather than in the wholesale form. See the argument page Are the anomalous "alien" encounters deceiving spirits in a modern mask? (assessment: plausible).
  • A caution against the over-reach. Lewis is the standing rebuke to the strongest writers' claim that no biological aliens can exist (Bates) or that "these are not space aliens" as settled fact (Marzulli): his whole essay is written to show that the faith has nothing to fear from real neighbours in the cosmos, and much to fear from what a fallen humanity would do to them.
