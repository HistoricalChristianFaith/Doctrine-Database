Jacques Vallée on UFOs and the Demonic

Dates: c. 1969 (b. 1939) · Wikipedia
Summary: Jacques Vallée — a French-American astronomer and computer scientist, not a Christian — is the pivotal secular source behind the Christian "aliens are demons" reading, though he draws no such conclusion himself. Against the extraterrestrial hypothesis he argues the UFO phenomenon is one continuous thread running through folklore, religious apparition, and modern abduction alike — an interdimensional "control system" conditioning human belief. Every Christian writer on this timeline co-opts that continuity and re-labels his "other dimension" as the spiritual/demonic realm.

Claims & evidence

Modern UFO reports are the ancient fairy-faith in a new costume ("Magonia")

In Passport to Magonia: From Folklore to Flying Saucers (1969), Vallée set hundreds of modern close-encounter and abduction reports beside medieval and folk accounts of fairies, elves, and aerial visitors — the "people of Magonia," the cloud-land whose ships were said to raid the fields — and argued they are a single phenomenon refracted through each age's imagery: emissaries from an unreachable realm who appear in human or monstrous form, work wonders, abduct, and seduce, and who shape whole civilizations through "mystical revelation."1 The book's index runs "fairies," "demons," "incubi," "Nephilim," and "interdimensional" together — the very list the Christian writers below inherit.

Five arguments against the extraterrestrial origin of UFOs

the accumulated data base exhibits several patterns tending to indicate that UFOs are real, represent a previously unrecognized phenomenon, and that the facts do not support the common concept of "space visitors."

— Five Arguments Against the Extraterrestrial Origin of Unidentified Flying Objects, Journal of Scientific Exploration 4 (1990). His five points, in his own words: close encounters are "far more numerous than required for any physical survey of the earth"; the humanoid body "is not likely to have originated on another planet and is not biologically adapted to space travel"; the "thousands of abduction reports" contradict "genetic or scientific experimentation… by an advanced race"; "the extension of the phenomenon throughout recorded human history demonstrates that UFOs are not a contemporary phenomenon"; and their "apparent ability… to manipulate space and time suggests radically different and richer alternatives" than interstellar travel.2

The phenomenon is a "control system" conditioning human belief

Rather than "successive waves of visitations from space," Vallée proposed in The Invisible College (1975) that the phenomenon behaves like a control system — one whose object, he suggested, "is human belief that is being controlled and conditioned," a "thermostat" nudging the belief-systems of entire cultures. He kept this resolutely secular: the "other dimension" is a physics we do not yet understand, not a spiritual realm.3

Notes

  • Not a demonologist. Vallée never says "demons," "Satan," or "fallen angels." His model is a cybernetic control system and an "interdimensional" physics. The demonic reading is supplied by the Christian writers who quote him — Missler and Eastman, Bates, Marzulli, Horn — who take his fairies-to-aliens continuity and re-identify the "other dimension" as the spiritual realm and its occupants as the deceiving spirits of Scripture. The move is a genuine borrowing of his data, not of his conclusion; the strength (and limits) of that borrowing is weighed in this timeline's Arguments.
  • Where a Christian reader hears "fallen angels wearing a modern mask," Vallée hears an unknown intelligence running a myth-making feedback loop. Both agree the phenomenon is not nuts-and-bolts spacecraft from another planet — which is why he is cited across the Christian literature despite his own agnosticism about what the "control system" ultimately is.
