Cyril of Alexandria on the Origin of the Septuagint

Dates: c. AD 444 · Against Julian (Contra Iulianum) · back to timeline
Summary: The scope widened from the Law to the Prophets. Cyril, patriarch of Alexandria, refers to the Septuagint origin in his great apology Against Julian, written near the end of his life to answer the apostate emperor's Against the Galileans. His distinctive contribution to the legend's growth is not a new miracle but a quiet expansion of what the Seventy are said to have done: where the Letter of Aristeas had the seventy-two render only the Law (the Pentateuch), Cyril writes as though their work took in "the holy prophets" as well — folding the whole Greek Old Testament into the one inspired translation. It is the scope-creep Justin and Clement had already begun, now settled into the assumption that "the Seventy" produced the entire Greek Bible. (This page rests on a secondary report; the primary passage is not yet confirmed against a direct edition — see the note below.)

Claims & evidence

The Seventy as translators of the Prophets, not the Law alone — ⚠ unverified

The point is registered by Abraham and David Wasserstein in their survey of the legend's transmission, who read Cyril's Against Julian as no longer restricting the seventy-two to the Pentateuch:

"Another writer of the same period, Cyril of Alexandria (390–444), similarly, is not quite sure that it was the Pentateuch that the seventy-two translated, including in their work also 'the holy prophets'."1

— reporting Contra Iulianum (Patrologia Graeca LXXVI, 521; cited from Wendland 1900). The observation matters for the timeline because the earliest source the tradition inherits, the Letter of Aristeas, is explicit that the king asked only for "the law of the Jews," and Aristeas's translators render the Law alone; the Prophets and the rest of the Greek Old Testament were rendered later and by other hands. Yet by Cyril's day "the Seventy" had become a standing name for the whole Greek Bible, so a writer could attribute the prophetic books to the same seventy-two without remark. Cyril does not argue the point — he simply assumes it — which is exactly why it is evidence of how far the legend had spread: its scope had grown silently from the Law to the entire canon. ⚠ The wording above is Wasserstein's report, not Cyril's own words; the primary passage in Against Julian has not yet been read in a direct edition (the relevant books survive only partially in English), so the claim is treated here as unverified pending a primary citation.

Notes

  • What is new, and what is not. Cyril adds no fresh element to the mechanism of the legend — no separate cells, no new physical detail, no inspiration formula is attested in the fragment as reported here. His contribution is to the legend's reach: the seventy-two, in his telling, stand behind the Prophets and not only the Law. This continues a widening already visible in Justin Martyr (who spoke of "the books of the prophets" sent to Ptolemy) and Clement of Alexandria ("the Scriptures both of the law and of the prophets"); in Cyril it has become the unspoken default.
  • Cells and inspiration: not attested here. For the through-line this timeline traces — whether each teller carries the separate-cells miracle and the inspiration claim — the passage reported on this page carries neither. Cyril's fuller discussion of the translation elsewhere in Against Julian (which reproduces the older account of Ptolemy and the elders) has not been examined here; what the dossier preserves is only the scope remark.
  • Date and text. Cyril completed Against Julian (twenty books, of which ten survive) in his last years; it is dated here at c. AD 444, the year of his death, placing him on the timeline after Augustine of Hippo (c. 425). The English-translated portions of the work cover mainly the earlier books; the passage at issue (PG 76, col. 521) lies in a part not yet consulted in a primary edition. Norman Russell's selections (Cyril of Alexandria, Routledge, 2000) and the Riedweg / Brüggemann–Kinzig critical edition (GCS, 2016–17) are the places to verify the wording.
