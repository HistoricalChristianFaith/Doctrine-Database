Dionysius bar Salibi on the Origin of the Septuagint (the Simeon question)

Dates: d. 1171 (Syriac Orthodox bishop of Amid/Diyarbakır; the great West-Syriac commentator of the twelfth century) · Wikipedia
Summary: In his Commentary on Luke 2:25, Dionysius bar Salibi preserves the fullest Syriac form of the question who was the Simeon of Luke 2? — a list of seven rival identifications, the same seven-fold catalogue found a quarter-millennium earlier in Moses bar Kepha (d. 903), lightly reordered. The Septuagint-translator legend is the fifth option, not established fact: one guess among seven, with the miraculous lifespan given here as 278 years (bar Kepha's 272). Two things the list shows: the Isaiah-7:14 form of the translator-legend was a fixed piece of Syriac Luke-exegesis, carried in a standard catena; and even as it circulated, the Syriac tradition still had no settled answer to Simeon's identity. Bar Salibi bears on the Simeon-the-Just legend, not on the origin of the Septuagint, which he does not retell; he has no timeline slot.

Claims & evidence

Seven rival identifications of the Gospel Simeon — the translator-legend is the fifth

"1. Some say, this is Simeon son of Jeshua son of Jozadak, in whose time the captivity went up from Babylon. And he lived five hundred years until he saw Christ.

2. Others: this Simeon was a righteous man who went down in the captivity and despaired of restoration. And a revelation came to him. And he remained alive from the time of Jeremiah and king Josiah until (that of) Christ, about 570 years.

3. Others: this Simeon was the son of Onias the high priest, and father of Jesus bar Asira who has the (Old Testament) book. And he was bound in spirit 219 years.

4. Others: he was high priest. And when he was reading in Isaiah and reached the place (7.14) 'Behold a virgin conceives and gives birth' he doubted and said, 'This cannot happen'. And at once he was bound that he should not taste death until this should happen.

5. Others: he was one of the 72 translators. And when he was rendering the book of Isaiah from the Hebrew language into the Greek at the command of Ptolemy in Egypt, he doubted (and said), 'A virgin cannot conceive and give birth'. And he was bound 278 years until Christ.

6. Others: the Spirit bound him before of this: that he should become a herald of Christ and herald him, so that not only by miracles might he be believed to be God, but also by heralds — that is, Gabriel, and Zechariah, and this Simeon.

7. Others: the Spirit bound him because of this: (it was) on account of his 'just'ness and 'righteous'ness — that is, that he should see Christ and (Christ) reward him."

— Commentary on Luke 2:25a (d. 1171), trans. J. F. Coakley from the Syriac (ed. A. Vaschalde, CSCO 113, pp. 283–284).1 The catalogue is offered as popular report — "some say… others… others" — not as a verdict; bar Salibi records the guesses and adjudicates none. The translator-legend (no. 5) sits between two other survival-legends and two allegorical readings of why the Spirit "bound" Simeon, exactly as one option among seven. Its distinctive shape is already the developed one: the man is one of the Seventy, his doubt is over the "virgin" of Isaiah as he renders it into Greek for Ptolemy, and he is "bound" for a span of years (278) until the birth. That is the very form the later Coptic-Arabic and Byzantine tellers would carry — present here, in Syriac, in a standard commentary, in the twelfth century.

The list is older than bar Salibi — a fixed catena reaching back to bar Kepha

The same seven-fold list appears, with only its order shuffled and the lifespan at 272 rather than 278 years, in the Book of Homilies attributed to Moses bar Kepha (d. 903), where it is introduced "from the commentary on Luke" — i.e. from bar Kepha's now-lost Luke commentary. Coakley judges the catalogue older than bar Salibi and most likely drawn from that lost source, so that bar Salibi is transmitting, not inventing: the seven identifications were a settled piece of West-Syriac Gospel-exegesis, copied from commentary to commentary.2 Two features matter for the assessment of the legend. First, the translator-legend is offered as one guess among seven, never as established fact — the Syriac tradition had no settled answer to who Simeon was, and the Septuagint form had no privileged claim among the rest. Second, the wandering lifespan — 272 in bar Kepha, 278 here, later 280 in Bar Hebraeus, 350 in the Arabic tellers, 360 in the modern Orthodox commemoration — marks a number no source could fix, the tell-tale drift of a legendary rather than a remembered datum.

Notes

  • Bar Salibi does not retell the origin of the Septuagint — the royal commission, the separate cells, the miracle of identical drafts — but only the Simeon-identity question at Luke 2:25. His testimony therefore bears on the Simeon-the-Just argument (assessment: weak), not on the origin timeline, and he carries no timeline slot. (His fellow Syriac bar Kepha holds a timeline place because his Hexaemeron retells the origin legend proper; the seven-fold Luke list is a separate matter.)
  • Option 3 and the Sirach-conflation. The third identification — Simeon "the son of Onias the high priest, and father of Jesus bar Asira [Ben Sira]" — is the guess George of the Arabs had already refuted on chronology in the eighth century, and the one Bar Hebraeus would later fuse with the translator-legend (no. 5), welding two of bar Salibi's separate options into a single composite Simeon. Bar Salibi, like bar Kepha, still keeps them apart.
  • Sourcing. The list is cited from Coakley's translation of Vaschalde's edition (CSCO 113); the underlying Syriac has not been independently collated against the manuscript.
