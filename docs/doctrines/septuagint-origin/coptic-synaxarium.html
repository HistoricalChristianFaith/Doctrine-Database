The Coptic Synaxarium on the Origin of the Septuagint (the sharpest Simeon-legend form)

Dates: c. 1300 — the Copto-Arabic (“Arab Jacobite”) Synaxarion, an anonymous liturgical compilation whose standard recension took shape across the thirteenth and fourteenth centuries; the primary redaction is credited to Anba Mīkhā’īl, bishop of Atrīb and Malīj (13th c.), with earlier and later hands · Claremont Coptic Encyclopedia
Summary: The reading appointed for 8 Amshīr (2 February), the feast of the Presentation, gives the Simeon-the-Just legend in its sharpest and most explicit form. Alone among the witnesses it names the proof-text outright — Simeon “struggled with the words of Isaiah [7:14]” — and alone dramatizes the fault as an act of the pen: fearing the king’s ridicule, Simeon “wrote, in place of ‘virgin’, ‘girl’,” was rebuked by an angel, and was kept alive some three hundred years until he held the Christ. It is not an early or independent testimony — a late, anonymous liturgical redaction, downstream of the Syriac and Arabic tellings that already carried the Isaiah-7:14 form a full three centuries earlier — but it is the vivid endpoint of the legend’s growth, the form in which the doubt is finally fastened, in so many words, onto “virgin.”

Claims & evidence

Simeon the translator writes “girl” for “virgin” at Isaiah 7:14, and is checked by an angel

“And when they all translated the whole of the law, Simeon the Just struggled with the words of Isaiah [7:14]: ‘Behold, a virgin shall conceive, and shall bear a son’; he was afraid to write ‘a virgin shall conceive’, lest the king make fun of him, and not accept what he had written, and think that he was misleading him in what he wrote; so he wrote, in place of ‘virgin’, ‘girl’. Then he had internal doubts and he said (to himself), that a virgin should give birth is something that cannot be. And while he was thinking this, God sent drowsiness down upon him, and he slept. And (in his sleep) an angel of God appeared to him and said to him, This is the one about whom you doubted that you would see death before you saw the masīh who is born of a virgin. And he lived after that for nearly three hundred years, until the Lord Masīh was born, and he went up with him… on this day to the Temple; and Simeon was blind, and when he took him in his arms he (was able to) see…”

— Coptic redaction of the Arab Jacobite Synaxarium, the eighth of Amshīr (2 February), trans. Abraham & David J. Wasserstein.1 The Synaxarion is a liturgical book — a day-by-day cycle of saints’ commemorations read aloud in the Coptic church — and this notice is the lection for the Presentation. It sets the legend in the frame the whole origin timeline traces: Ptolemy summons seventy learned Jews, separates them “by pairs in different places… that they should not act in concert,” and has them render the Law from Hebrew into Greek — and “this was the instruction of God, that the Law should be translated for the Christian nation which was to appear after many years.” Onto that origin story it grafts the Gospel Simeon of Luke 2 as one of the translators, and makes his hesitation over Isaiah’s “virgin” the pivot of the whole commemoration, closing with the Nunc Dimittis (“Now, O Lord, release thy servant… for my eyes have seen Your deliverance”) and Simeon’s word to Mary that “the lance of doubt will enter your heart.”

Why this form is the sharpest — and why that does not make it early

Two features set the Synaxarium apart from the other tellings. First, it is the only witness to name the verse: where Eutychius (c. 938) and al-Makīn (c. 1273) have Simeon grieve over the messianic prophecies “in general,” here the doubt is fixed expressly on Isaiah 7:14. Second, it alone makes the fault a written act — Simeon does not merely hesitate, he “wrote, in place of ‘virgin’, ‘girl’” before the angel corrects him — and supplies the angelic rebuke and the motive of fearing the king’s ridicule. This is the legend at its most dramatized and most pointed.

That sharpness is a mark of late development, not of early access. The explicitly-Isaiah-7:14 shape of the story is not a Coptic innovation: it is already present in Syriac, at c. 903, in Moses bar Kepha (“when he was rendering the book of Isaiah… he doubted and said, ‘A virgin cannot conceive and give birth’”), a full three centuries before the Synaxarion’s redaction. What the Synaxarium uniquely adds is the staging — the pen poised over “girl,” the ridiculing king, the rebuking angel. It is the vivid endpoint of a motif that grew as it travelled, the point at which the doubt is finally spelled out against “virgin” in so many words, and it survives in the living Coptic liturgy to this day.2

Notes

  • This witness is weighed on the Simeon-the-Just argument (assessment: weak), where it stands as the sharpest, most explicitly Isaiah-7:14 form of the legend. It carries no slot on the origin timeline proper: it is an anonymous, undated-in-itself liturgical compilation that feeds only the Simeon offshoot of the legend, not the origin story’s mainline growth, and it is chronologically the latest of the cluster — downstream of the Syriac (bar Kepha, bar Salibi, Bar Hebraeus), Byzantine (Kedrenos, Zigabenus), Melkite (Eutychius), and Coptic-Arabic (Ibn al-Rāhib, al-Makīn) tellings that precede it.
  • The date given above is the standard scholarly one for the Copto-Arabic Synaxarion’s recension, not a date the text carries for itself. Because it is an accreting liturgical collection rather than a single author’s work, its notices vary in age; the Presentation lection quoted here reflects the developed thirteenth-to-fourteenth-century form, and its miraculous lifespan (“nearly three hundred years”) is one more variant alongside the Syriac 272/278/280, Eutychius’s 350, and the modern Orthodox 360.
  • The English here follows the Wassersteins’ translation; the underlying edition is René Basset’s Arabic text with French translation in the Patrologia Orientalis. The Arabic has not been independently collated here against a critical edition.
