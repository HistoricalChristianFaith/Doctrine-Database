Euthymius Zigabenus on the Simeon-the-Just legend (the guarded Greek witness)

Dates: c. 1120 (the Commentary on the Four Gospels, on Luke 2:25); Euthymius c. 1050–1120, writing under Alexios I Komnenos (d. 1118) · Wikipedia
Summary: Euthymius Zigabenus is the second and last Byzantine Greek witness to the Simeon-the-Just legend — and the mirror image of George Kedrenos, the only other. Where Kedrenos asserts the legend with a false patristic pedigree (Simeon among the Seventy "as the divine Chrysostom says in his Hexaemeron"), Euthymius reports it with conspicuous reserve and no pedigree at all: he cites no father, no named authority, only "certain writings." His caution is itself the datum — the Greek branch of the legend is thin and unsure of itself, carried by two writers a century and a half after the explicitly Isaiah-7:14 form was already fixed in Syriac. Euthymius bears not on the origin of the Septuagint, which he does not retell, but on the Simeon-the-Just legend; he has no timeline slot.

Claims & evidence

"I too have found in certain writings… that he was among the seventy translators" — and doubted Isaiah's "virgin"

"I too have found in certain writings concerning this righteous man that he was among the seventy translators…"

— Euthymius Zigabenus, Commentarius in quatuor Evangelia, on Luke 2:25, in Patrologia Graeca 129, col. 890, as translated by J. F. Coakley.1 So Euthymius introduces the legend, and goes on to retell its core — that this Simeon, rendering the prophet Isaiah, stumbled over the word that "a virgin shall conceive" (Isaiah 7:14) and was kept alive until the prophecy he doubted was fulfilled in his arms. Two things mark his telling and set it apart from Kedrenos's. First, its hesitancy: the whole notice is framed as something merely "found in certain writings," a report he passes on rather than a fact he vouches for. Second, and decisively, its lack of pedigree: where Kedrenos props the story on a (spurious) Chrysostom, Euthymius names no father and no source beyond those anonymous "certain writings." He withholds exactly the authority Kedrenos manufactures.

The two together are the whole Byzantine reception — and it is late and thin

The legend stayed rare on the Greek side. Of all the Byzantine historians and commentators known today, only two carry it — George Kedrenos (c. 1050) and Euthymius Zigabenus (c. 1120) — and they disagree in temper: Kedrenos asserts the false pedigree, Zigabenus withholds any pedigree.2 That thinness matters for the assessment of the legend. The explicitly Isaiah-7:14 shape of the story — the translator stumbling over the "virgin" — is older than either Greek writer by a century and a half, already fixed in Syriac Luke-exegesis with Moses bar Kepha (d. 903) and Dionysius bar Salibi (d. 1171); and the whole identification post-dates the dated negative of George of the Arabs (d. 724), who canvassed the rival Simeon-identifications and knew no translator-form at all. The Byzantine branch is late and dependent, not the source — and Euthymius's own reserve, naming no authority, is a small confession of how little settled ground the Greek tradition felt beneath the tale.

Notes

  • Euthymius does not retell the origin of the Septuagint — the separate cells, the miracle of identical drafts — but reports only the Simeon offshoot, in passing, at Luke 2:25. His testimony therefore bears on the Simeon-the-Just argument (assessment: weak), not on the origin timeline, and he carries no timeline slot — the same standing as Kedrenos.
