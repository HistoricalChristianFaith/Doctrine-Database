Irenaeus on the Origin of the Septuagint

Dates: c. AD 175 · Wikipedia · back to timeline
Summary: The turning point. In Against Heresies III.21.2 Irenaeus is the first Christian writer to give the legend its full inspiration-miracle form: Ptolemy, fearing the elders might collude to conceal the truth, separates them from one another and orders each to write the whole translation alone; when their independent versions prove identical "in the very same words and the very same names, from beginning to end," even the Gentiles present recognize that "the Scriptures had been interpreted by the inspiration of God." Philo had the marvel of unanimity; Irenaeus adds the isolation that turns unanimity into proof — and he does so for a polemical end, to defend the Septuagint's "virgin" of Isaiah 7:14 against the Jewish proselytes Aquila and Theodotion, who rendered it "young woman."

Claims & evidence

The seventy elders separated to write alone, their identical result proving divine inspiration

"For before the Romans possessed their kingdom, while as yet the Macedonians held Asia, Ptolemy the son of Lagus, being anxious to adorn the library which he had founded in Alexandria, with a collection of the writings of all men, which were [works] of merit, made request to the people of Jerusalem, that they should have their Scriptures translated into the Greek language. And they—for at that time they were still subject to the Macedonians—sent to Ptolemy seventy of their elders, who were thoroughly skilled in the Scriptures and in both the languages, to carry out what he had desired. But he, wishing to test them individually, and fearing lest they might perchance, by taking counsel together, conceal the truth in the Scriptures, by their interpretation, separated them from each other, and commanded them all to write the same translation. He did this with respect to all the books. But when they came together in the same place before Ptolemy, and each of them compared his own interpretation with that of every other, God was indeed glorified, and the Scriptures were acknowledged as truly divine. For all of them read out the common translation [which they had prepared] in the very same words and the very same names, from beginning to end, so that even the Gentiles present perceived that the Scriptures had been interpreted by the inspiration of God."

— Against Heresies III.21.2, source. This is the hinge of the whole tradition. The earlier witnesses had the pieces separately: Aristeas and Josephus have the seventy-two elders but no marvel (their translators confer, reaching a text by collation); Philo has the marvel of identical language but his translators still work together "in a secret place." Irenaeus fuses them: by isolating the translators and then finding their separate drafts word-for-word identical, he converts Philo's inspiration into a public, testable demonstration of it. The physical "little cells" on Pharos, and the claim "we ourselves have seen the traces," are not yet here — that explicitness is added a generation later in the Cohortatio ad Graecos — but the logical machinery of the cells-miracle is now in place.

The precedent of Esdras: God can re-inspire a corrupted Scripture

"And there was nothing astonishing in God having done this—He who, when, during the captivity of the people under Nebuchadnezzar, the Scriptures had been corrupted, and when, after seventy years, the Jews had returned to their own land, then, in the times of Artaxerxes king of the Persians, inspired Esdras the priest, of the tribe of Levi, to recast all the words of the former prophets, and to re-establish with the people the Mosaic legislation."

— Against Heresies III.21.2, source. Irenaeus braces the Septuagint marvel with a second one: just as God once re-created the lost Scriptures through an inspired Ezra after the Babylonian captivity, so He guided the seventy. The pairing reveals the doctrine's deep premise — that the authoritative text of Scripture is something God produces and guards by inspiration, not merely a manuscript transmitted by scribes. That premise is exactly what makes a later, divergent Hebrew text look like corruption, the charge gathered on the Rabbinic Corruption page.

Notes

  • Cells: implicit. Inspiration: explicit and demonstrative. Measured against the timeline's through-line, Irenaeus is where the inspiration-marvel becomes a proof. He has the translators' separation ("separated them from each other") and the identical-result demonstration, but not yet the named architecture of the cells nor the "we have seen the traces" autopsy — those arrive with the Cohortatio ad Graecos (c. AD 260). So he stands one step beyond Philo and one step short of the fully built legend.
  • The polemical point: defending "virgin" against Aquila. Irenaeus does not tell the story for its own sake. In the immediately preceding section (III.21.1) he attacks "Theodotion the Ephesian… and Aquila of Pontus, both Jewish proselytes," for translating Isaiah 7:14 as "young woman shall conceive" instead of "virgin," and the Ebionites who follow them in denying the virgin birth. The Septuagint-inspiration legend is his answer: the older, divinely-guided Greek of the seventy is authoritative, and the post-Christian Jewish versions that revise it are not. This is the seed of the corruption charge — the inspired-LXX premise wielded against the synagogue's rival text — and Irenaeus is its early Christian spokesman.
  • Two slips of detail. Irenaeus writes "seventy of their elders" (not the seventy-two of Aristeas), the round number that gives the version its lasting name; and he assigns the commission to "Ptolemy the son of Lagus" — Ptolemy I Soter — rather than his son Ptolemy II Philadelphus, the king of the older tradition. The legend's particulars were already fluid in Christian hands, even as its theological point hardened.
