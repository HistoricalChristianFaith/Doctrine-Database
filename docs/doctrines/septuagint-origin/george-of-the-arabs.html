George, Bishop of the Arabs on the Origin of the Septuagint (the Simeon question)

Dates: c. 640–724 (bishop of the Arab tribes from 686; d. 724) · Wikipedia
Summary: George, a learned West Syriac bishop, is the earliest dated writer known to canvass who the Simeon of Luke 2 was. Answering the recluse Yeshu (Josua), he lists the rival identifications, rejects the one that made him Simon son of Sirach — on chronology and a false etymology — and concludes the Gospel Simeon "need have been neither a priest nor unusually old." He knows nothing of the later legend that folded Simeon into the seventy-two Septuagint translators, kept alive three centuries to render (and doubt) Isaiah 7:14's "virgin." As late as the early eighth century, then, that translator-form had not yet formed even in the Syriac tradition that would later carry it — which makes George the dated floor of the Simeon-the-Just legend.

Claims & evidence

The identity of the Gospel Simeon was unsettled — and no translator-legend is among the options

"Concerning this old man Simeon about whom you have asked, brother, whether he is Simon Sirach or not: you should know, O lover of truth, that people say many things about him. For some say that he is Simon Sirach, as indeed you, brother, have heard; some (say) that he lived 500 years, from the time of the captivity into Babylon until he received Christ in his arms in the temple and cried out to him saying 'Now, Lord, release thy servant in peace'; some (say) that he was a priest with Zechariah and shared with Joseph the secret of our Saviour's conception and birth from the Virgin."

— Letter to the recluse Yeshu (Josua), "On the old man Simeon who took our Lord in the temple" (c. 724), trans. J. F. Coakley.1 George canvasses three current guesses — Simeon the son of Sirach; a man kept alive five centuries from the Babylonian deportation; a priest who served alongside Zechariah and shared Joseph's secret — and treats them as popular report ("people say many things"), a tradition with no settled answer. Decisively for the growth of the legend charted on the origin timeline, the option that would later dominate the Christian East — that Simeon was one of Ptolemy's seventy-two translators, who stumbled over the "virgin" of Isaiah 7:14 — is simply absent from his list. It is the earliest dated soundings of the Simeon-identity question, and the translator-form is not yet in it.

The identification with Simon son of Sirach fails — on chronology and a false etymology

George does not merely report the guesses; he weighs and rejects the first of them. The identification of the Gospel Simeon with Simon son of Sirach (the sage behind Ecclesiasticus) cannot stand on the dates: Simeon Sirach, he argues, lived "more than 244 years before the coming of Christ in the flesh," demonstrably in "the year 65 of the Greeks [Seleucid era], in the days of King Ptolemy Euergetes of Egypt" — generations too early to have held the infant Christ. And he traces the whole confusion to a false etymology: the supposition that the name Sirach derives from ʿasir ("bound," "captive"), read back into a story of a man "bound" to long life. Stripped of that error, George concludes, the Simeon of Luke 2 "need have been neither a priest nor unusually old" — an ordinary devout Israelite, not a centuries-old survivor of the captivity nor a member of the priestly course.2 The same Sirach-conflation George refuted here would nonetheless be reasserted five centuries later by Bar Hebraeus (d. 1286), who welded "the father of Jesus the son of Asira [Ben Sira]" to the very translator-legend George never knew — the error surviving its own refutation.

Notes

  • George's testimony bears not on the origin of the Septuagint — which he nowhere retells — but on a separate question, the identity of the Simeon of Luke 2. It is weighed on the Simeon-the-Just argument (assessment: weak), where he stands as the dated negative floor: the Septuagint-translator identification, and its Isaiah-7:14 shape, are absent from the earliest soundings and first surface a full generation later, with the Syriac Moses bar Kepha (d. 903).
  • The Letter survives in Syriac (edited by P. A. de Lagarde, Analecta Syriaca, 1858) with a full German translation by Victor Ryssel (1891); the Simeon chapter and its place in the Syriac Luke-tradition are analyzed by J. F. Coakley (1981). The English renderings here follow those editions; the Syriac has not been independently collated against a critical edition.
  • George also refutes the "500 years / from the Babylonian captivity" survival guess by implication, in freeing the Gospel Simeon of the need to be "unusually old"; the priestly-with-Zechariah guess he leaves without the chronological objection but does not adopt.
