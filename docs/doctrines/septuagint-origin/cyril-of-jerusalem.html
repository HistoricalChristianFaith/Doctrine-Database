Cyril of Jerusalem on the Origin of the Septuagint

Dates: c. AD 350 · Catechetical Lecture 4.34 · back to timeline
Summary: The separate cells, and the Holy Ghost named. Cyril, bishop of Jerusalem, sets the origin story into the catechism itself — teaching candidates for baptism which Scriptures the Church receives, he tells them how the Greek came to be. His version is the fullest patristic narration so far: he restores the precise frame of Aristeas — Ptolemy Philadelphus, Demetrius Phalereus, Eleazar the High Priest, six interpreters from each of the twelve tribes, seventy-two days — and keeps the physical separate chambers on Pharos that the Cohortatio had made concrete; but he adds the explicit pneumatology none before him had stated outright: the translation "spoken by the Holy Ghost, was of the Holy Ghost accomplished."

Claims & evidence

The full Aristean frame, the separate chambers, and the Holy Ghost

"For after the death of Alexander, the king of the Macedonians, and the division of his kingdom into four principalities, into Babylonia, and Macedonia, and Asia, and Egypt, one of those who reigned over Egypt, Ptolemy Philadelphus, being a king very fond of learning, while collecting the books that were in every place, heard from Demetrius Phalereus, the curator of his library, of the Divine Scriptures of the Law and the Prophets, and judged it much nobler, not to get the books from the possessors by force against their will, but rather to propitiate them by gifts and friendship; and knowing that what is extorted is often adulterated, being given unwillingly, while that which is willingly supplied is freely given with all sincerity, he sent to Eleazar, who was then High Priest, a great many gifts for the Temple here at Jerusalem, and caused him to send him six interpreters from each of the twelve tribes of Israel for the translation. Then, further, to make experiment whether the books were Divine or not, he took precaution that those who had been sent should not combine among themselves, by assigning to each of the interpreters who had come his separate chamber in the island called Pharos, which lies over against Alexandria, and committed to each the whole Scriptures to translate. And when they had fulfilled the task in seventy-two days, he brought together all their translations, which they had made in different chambers without sending them one to another, and found that they agreed not only in the sense but even in words. For the process was no word-craft, nor contrivance of human devices: but the translation of the Divine Scriptures, spoken by the Holy Ghost, was of the Holy Ghost accomplished."

— Catechetical Lecture 4.34, source. Cyril's telling gathers the legend's strands into their fullest classical shape and adds the theological key. He recovers the detail the previous Christian tellers had lost or blurred: Aristeas's precise seventy-two (six from each of twelve tribes) rather than the round "seventy" of Irenaeus, Clement, and the Cohortatio; the High Priest Eleazar by name, whom Justin had garbled into "Herod" and others had dropped; the correct king, Ptolemy Philadelphus, against Irenaeus's Ptolemy I; and Demetrius the librarian. He keeps the physical isolation the Cohortatio had built — a "separate chamber" for each man on Pharos — so the agreement "not only in the sense but even in words" is the same unforeseeable-unanimity proof that Irenaeus introduced. What is new is the explicit naming of the agent: where Philo had said the translators wrote "as if some unseen prompter had suggested" their words and Irenaeus that the Gentiles "perceived … the inspiration of God," Cyril states it doctrinally — the work was "spoken by the Holy Ghost," "of the Holy Ghost accomplished" — the Septuagint's inspiration spelled out in the language of the Spirit and set, as catechesis, among the things a candidate for baptism is taught to believe.

Notes

  • Setting: the canon catechesis. The passage comes in the lecture "On the Divine Scriptures," where Cyril is instructing baptismal candidates which books the Church receives. The origin story is told to vouch for the Greek Old Testament the catechumens will hear read in church: it is not apologetic display for pagans (as in Justin, Tertullian, and the Cohortatio) nor polemic against the synagogue's rival versions (as in Irenaeus), but instruction for the faithful — which is why he can state the inspiration flatly as settled doctrine.
  • The through-line: cells kept, the Spirit named. The physical separate-chambers apparatus, made concrete by the Cohortatio, is here retained — one chamber per translator on Pharos — but Cyril makes no autopsy claim; he reports the cells as received history, not visible ruins. His own addition is the explicit pneumatology: the translation is "of the Holy Ghost accomplished," the inspiration attributed by name to the Spirit. On the timeline the miracle is now both physically detailed and doctrinally fixed.
  • The king's "experiment." Cyril gives the isolation a motive the earlier tellers only implied: Ptolemy separates the translators "to make experiment whether the books were Divine or not." Where Irenaeus's king feared the elders "might … conceal the truth," Cyril's king is staging a deliberate test of the Scriptures' divinity — and the unanimous result is its verdict. The moralizing aside, that Ptolemy preferred gifts to force "knowing that what is extorted is often adulterated," is Cyril's own embellishment of the courteous-king motif.
  • Date. The Catechetical Lectures were delivered to baptismal candidates at Jerusalem about AD 350 (some place them a few years earlier, c. 348); they are anchored here at c. 350, between Eusebius and Epiphanius.
