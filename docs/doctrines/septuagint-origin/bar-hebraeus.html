Bar Hebraeus on the Origin of the Septuagint (the Simeon legend)

Dates: c. 1226–1286 (Gregory Abū al-Faraj, "Bar Hebraeus," maphrian of the East; d. 30 July 1286) · Wikipedia
Summary: The great Syriac Orthodox polymath is the latest major teller of the Simeon-the-Just legend — and the one who does what the earlier Syriac list-makers had been careful not to do: he fuses two of their rival identifications into a single Simeon. In his Gospel commentary, the Storehouse of Mysteries (Awṣar Rāzē), at Luke 2:25, this Simeon is at once "the father of Jesus the son of Asira [Ben Sira]" and one of the seventy-two translators who doubted Isaiah's "virgin" and was bound 280 years to see the Christ. Where bar Kepha and bar Salibi had kept those as separate guesses, Bar Hebraeus welds them — reasserting as fact the very Sirach-conflation George of the Arabs had refuted on chronology five centuries earlier. He bears on the Simeon-the-Just legend, not on the origin of the Septuagint, and carries no timeline slot. (For his role on the Genesis-chronology strand, see his rabbinic-corruption page.)

Claims & evidence

The translator-legend fused with the "father of Ben Sira" — a fourfold Simeon, bound 280 years

"'There was a certain man in Jerusalem whose name was Simeon.' The same was the father of Jesus the son of Asira, and was one of those seventy and two interpreters who translated the Scriptures from Hebrew into Greek in the days of Ptolemy. And because he was doubtful concerning that verse: 'Behold, a virgin shall conceive and bear a son,' which is spoken in the prophet Isaiah, he was bound two hundred and eighty years to the sacrifice until he should see Him who was born of a virgin."

— Commentary on the Gospels from the Horreum Mysteriorum (the Storehouse of Mysteries, Awṣar Rāzē), on Luke 2:25 (d. 1286), trans. & ed. Wilmot Eardley W. Carr, p. 98.1 The move is small on the page but consequential for the legend. The Syriac catalogues bar Hebraeus inherited — the seven-fold list in bar Kepha and bar Salibi — had listed "the son of Onias… and father of Jesus bar Asira" (their no. 3) and "one of the 72 translators who doubted Isaiah" (their no. 5) as distinct guesses. Bar Hebraeus collapses the two into one identity, so that a single Simeon is simultaneously the parent of Ben Sira the sage and a member of Ptolemy's band — a composite the earlier tellers, for all their credulity about the translator-legend, had never asserted.

The Sirach-paternity had already been refuted — and is here reasserted as fact

The "father of Ben Sira" element bar Hebraeus welds in is precisely the identification George of the Arabs (d. 724) had labored to refute in the eighth century, on chronology (Simeon Sirach "lived more than 244 years before the coming of Christ… in the days of King Ptolemy Euergetes of Egypt") and as a false etymology (the guess that the name Sirach derives from ʻasir, "bound," read back into a story of a man "bound" to long life). Five centuries later Bar Hebraeus reasserts the refuted conflation as plain fact and, going further, bundles it with the translator-legend George never knew — the error surviving its own refutation, and gathering a second error to itself. His lifespan, 280 years, is one more point on the wandering scale the number never settled: 272 in bar Kepha, 278 in bar Salibi, 280 here, 350 in the Arabic tellers, 360 in living Orthodox devotion.2

Notes

  • Bar Hebraeus does not retell the origin of the Septuagint proper — the separate cells, the miracle of identical drafts — but folds only the Simeon offshoot into his Luke commentary. His testimony therefore bears on the Simeon-the-Just argument (assessment: weak), where he stands as the latest and most composite Syriac form, not on the origin timeline; he carries no timeline slot.
  • Two pages, two strands. Bar Hebraeus figures on this wiki twice, for two unrelated bodies of his work: here, for the Simeon legend in the Storehouse of Mysteries; and on his rabbinic-corruption page, for the Genesis 5–11 chronology in his Chronography, where he is a negative witness — he tabulates the divergent Septuagint and Hebrew totals but levels no charge of deliberate Jewish tampering. The two are distinct claims about distinct texts.
  • Sourcing. Cited from Carr's 1925 English edition of the Storehouse of Mysteries; the underlying Syriac has not been independently collated against a critical edition.
