Jean Bolland on the Simeon-the-Just legend (the earliest scholarly refutation)

Dates: 1665 (the Acta Sanctorum, October IV, 8 October, "On S. Simeon the Prophet of Jerusalem"); Jean Bolland 1596–1665, the Jesuit scholar who founded the critical hagiographical enterprise that bears his name (the Bollandists) · Wikipedia
Summary: Jean Bolland is the earliest scholarly refutation of the Simeon-the-Just legend. Editing the feast of the Gospel Simeon in the Acta Sanctorum, he quotes the fullest Byzantine teller, George Kedrenos, verbatim — and then demolishes the story on two counts: the patristic pedigree Kedrenos leans on (Simeon among the Seventy "as Chrysostom says in his Hexaemeron") is spurious, drawn from one of the many works falsely fathered on Chrysostom; and the miraculous lifespan is a conflation — the aged Simeon of the Nunc Dimittis confused with Simeon the Just, high priest of the Jews, the swollen age "a mere Talmudic invention." Where Kedrenos and Euthymius Zigabenus are the legend's two Greek believers, Bolland is its first critic. He is to the Simeon offshoot what Jerome ("the separate cells are a fable") is to the origin miracle: the dissenting voice. He bears not on the origin of the Septuagint, which he does not retell, but on the Simeon-the-Just legend; he has no timeline slot.

Claims & evidence

The Chrysostom pedigree is spurious — "it is not read in his genuine works that Simeon was among the seventy-two interpreters"

Bolland reproduces Kedrenos's notice in full — the "receiver of God, Simeon the Jew," one of the seventy interpreters under Ptolemy Philadelphus, who doubted Isaiah's word that a virgin should conceive and was kept alive until the oracle was fulfilled, "as D. Chrysostom reports in his work on the six days" (de sex diebus opere, the so-called Hexaemeron). Then he turns on the citation:

"But as regards St. Chrysostom, praised by Kedrenos in the Work of the Six Days — just as that holy Father was most famous for the multitude of his lucubrations, so a great many spurious offspring were ascribed to him, from one of which (for it is not read in his genuine works that Simeon was among the seventy-two interpreters) Kedrenos had excerpted this."

— Jean Bolland, Acta Sanctorum, October IV (8 October), "On S. Simeon the Prophet of Jerusalem," § II ("Refutantur aliquot de S. Simeone errores"), 1665.1 The point is decisive for the legend's Greek branch. Kedrenos gives the story its most confident form precisely because he props it on a named father; Bolland shows the prop to be a pseudo-Chrysostom, one of the spuria that had gathered under the archbishop's famous name. Chrysostom's genuine origin notice — his Homily 4 on Genesis, which does treat "the work of the six days" and does recount the Septuagint's origin — has no Simeon in it at all. The authority Kedrenos manufactures is exactly the authority the legend lacks.

The 350-year age is a conflation of two Simeons — "a mere Talmudic invention"

"…the great age of Simeon does not seem to have arisen from any other source than from the error by which Saint Simeon is confused with another Simon, surnamed the Just and high priest of the Jews, the father of Onias — … and it appears a mere Talmudic invention."

— Bolland, Acta Sanctorum, October IV, § II (1665).2 This is the pre-modern anticipation of the argument page's own central diagnosis. The three-century lifespan is not a datum to be explained but an artifact of a mistaken identity: fuse the aged Simeon of Luke 2 with Simeon the Just (Shimʻon ha-Tzaddik), the high priest at the head of the rabbinic chain of transmission (Pirqei Avot 1:2, c. 300 or c. 200 BC), and the two centuries between them have to be bridged by a miracle of longevity that no early source attests. Bolland traces the inflated age to that welding and dismisses it as a "Talmudic invention" — the same conflation of distinct Simeons the Simeon-the-Just argument sets out (point 1), and that the Syriac tellers had spread across seven rival identifications.

His own chronology dissolves the miracle

Bolland does not merely label the longevity a fable; he does the arithmetic. Following the Eusebian dating, he places the translation at about 283 BC — on which reckoning the Gospel Simeon, if he had really been one of the translators, would have been an ordinary old man of roughly sixty-seven at the Presentation, not a wonder of three-and-a-half centuries. The extravagant age appears only once the translator-Simeon is identified with the high priest; drop the conflation and the miracle it was invented to cover disappears with it. The demolition is thus complete: the pedigree is spurious, the identity is confused, and the chronology, honestly counted, needs no miracle at all.3

Notes

  • Bolland does not retell the origin of the Septuagint — the separate cells, the miracle of identical drafts — but weighs and rejects only the Simeon offshoot, in the course of editing the saint's feast. His testimony therefore bears on the Simeon-the-Just argument (assessment: weak), not on the origin timeline, and he carries no timeline slot — the same standing as the two Byzantine tellers he refutes, Kedrenos and Euthymius Zigabenus.
  • The skeptic's role. On the origin legend proper, this collection stages Jerome as the dissenting voice — the man who called the separate cells a fable. Bolland is the structural counterpart for the Simeon offshoot: the earliest critic to take the hagiographic legend apart with the tools of source-criticism and chronology, five centuries after its Greek and Syriac tellers and more than two before the modern collectors (the Wassersteins) gathered the whole cluster. His diagnosis — spurious source, conflated persons, invented age — is the one the modern assessment confirms.
  • Who Bolland was. Jean Bolland (1596–1665) was the Antwerp Jesuit who launched the Acta Sanctorum, the vast critical edition of the lives of the saints arranged by feast-day, from which the Bollandist tradition of scientific hagiography descends. His willingness to prune a devotional legend attached to a canonized saint — even one carried into the liturgy — is characteristic of that critical program.
  • Sourcing. The passage is cited here from the Latin of the Acta Sanctorum (October IV, 8 October) as reproduced at heiligenlexikon.de, with the English rendered from that Latin; it has not been independently collated against the printed folio.
