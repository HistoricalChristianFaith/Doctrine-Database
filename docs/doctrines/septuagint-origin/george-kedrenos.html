George Kedrenos on the Origin of the Septuagint (the Simeon legend)

Dates: fl. c. 1050 (Byzantine chronicler; the Synopsis Historiarum reaches to 1057) · Wikipedia
Summary: George Kedrenos is the fullest Byzantine Greek witness to the Simeon-the-Just legend — and the one who saddles it with a false patristic pedigree. Narrating the Presentation in his world-chronicle, he makes the Simeon of Luke 2 one of Ptolemy's seventy translators, who doubted Isaiah's "virgin" and was kept alive to see the Christ, "as the divine Chrysostom says in his Hexaemeron." The ascription is spurious: Jean Bolland, quoting Kedrenos verbatim in the Acta Sanctorum (1665), traced it to one of the many works falsely fathered on Chrysostom — "it is not read in his genuine works that Simeon was among the seventy-two interpreters" — and dismissed the swollen lifespan as a confusion of the Gospel Simeon with the high priest Simeon the Just. Kedrenos bears not on the origin of the Septuagint, which he does not retell, but on the Simeon-the-Just legend; he has no timeline slot.

Claims & evidence

Simeon of Luke 2 was one of the Seventy, kept alive to see the Christ he had doubted — "as Chrysostom says in his Hexaemeron"

"…the receiver of God, Symeon the Jew, was one of those seventy interpreters who had translated the Hebrew Old Testament into Greek for Ptolemy Philadelphus, king of Egypt, before the coming of Christ, as D. Chrysostom reports in his work on the six days. And here Simeon, when he had read the prophecy of Isaiah, that a virgin should be pregnant and give birth to a son named Immanuel, completely derogated his faith by saying that human nature would not allow a virgin to bear at all. Thus it was divinely revealed to him that he would live until the prophet's oracle was fulfilled… Seeing him therefore in the temple, a child forty days old, he took him in his arms, saying, 'Now, Lord, let your servant depart in peace according to your word.'"

— Synopsis Historiarum, pp. 327–328 (c. 1050), from the Latin of Immanuel Bekker's edition; the Greek (PG 121) opens "This Simeon θεοδόχος [God-receiver], being a Jew, was one of the seventy translators… as the divine Chrysostom says in his Hexaemeron."1 Kedrenos gives the legend its most confident Greek form: no "some say," no rival identifications — the Gospel Simeon simply is one of the Seventy, his three-century survival a settled fact, and the whole given the weight of a named patristic authority. His Chrysostom's genuine origin notice (Homily 4 on Genesis, which does treat "the work of the six days") tells the Septuagint's origin with no Simeon in it at all; the Hexaemeron Kedrenos cites is one of the many spuria that had gathered under Chrysostom's famous name.

The pedigree is false, and the longevity a conflation — so Bolland showed

"But as regards St. Chrysostom, praised by Kedrenos in the Work of the Six Days — just as that holy Father was most famous for the multitude of his lucubrations, so a great many spurious offspring were ascribed to him, from one of which (for it is not read in his genuine works that Simeon was among the seventy-two interpreters) Kedrenos had excerpted this… the great age of Simeon does not seem to have arisen from any other source than from the error by which Saint Simeon is confused with another Simon, surnamed the Just and high priest of the Jews… a mere Talmudic invention."

— Jean Bolland, Acta Sanctorum, October IV (8 October), "On S. Simeon the Prophet of Jerusalem" (1665), quoting Kedrenos verbatim and then rejecting both the source and the chronology.2 Bolland's two objections are the ones the Simeon argument turns on. The attribution fails because the citation is drawn from a pseudo-Chrysostom, not from the genuine works; and the chronology fails because the three-century lifespan arises only when the aged Simeon of the Nunc Dimittis is confused with Simeon the Just (Shimʻon ha-Tzaddik), the high priest at the head of the rabbinic chain of transmission — the same welding of distinct Simeons the Syriac tellers had spread across seven rival guesses. Kedrenos is thus a witness both to how far the legend had hardened on the Greek side by the eleventh century and to how early (1665) its unravelling began.

Notes

  • Kedrenos does not retell the origin of the Septuagint — the separate cells, the miracle of identical drafts — but reports only the Simeon offshoot, in passing, at the Presentation. His testimony therefore bears on the Simeon-the-Just argument (assessment: weak), not on the origin timeline, and he carries no timeline slot.
  • The only other Byzantine witness. The legend stayed rare on the Greek side. The one other Byzantine writer known to carry it, the early-twelfth-century commentator Euthymius Zigabenus, reports it in his Commentary on Luke with conspicuous reserve — "I too have found in certain writings concerning this righteous man that he was among the seventy translators" — and, tellingly, claims no Chrysostom and no named authority at all, only "certain writings." Where Kedrenos asserts the false pedigree, Zigabenus withholds any pedigree; the two together are the whole Byzantine reception.
  • The Syriac branch runs earlier. The explicitly Isaiah-7:14 form of the legend is older than Kedrenos by a century and a half, already fixed in Syriac Luke-exegesis with Moses bar Kepha (d. 903) and Dionysius bar Salibi (d. 1171); the Greek branch is late and dependent, not the source. And the whole identification post-dates the dated negative of George of the Arabs (d. 724), who canvassed the rival Simeon-identifications and knew no translator-form.
  • Sourcing. The English is cited from the Latin parallel of Bekker's Synopsis Historiarum (as quoted verbatim by Bolland) and from Coakley's rendering of the Greek (PG 121); the Greek/Latin has not been independently collated against a critical edition.
