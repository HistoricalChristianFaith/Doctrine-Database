Infant Baptism (paedobaptism)

Should the infant children of Christians be baptized, or does baptism belong only to those old enough to profess faith for themselves? The two axes on which views divide are who is a fit subject for baptism (a professing believer only, or a household including its children) and when it should be given (as early as possible, or deferred until a person can ask for it). Baby dedication — presenting an unbaptized child for blessing — is a subset of the same question. The witnesses below run earliest first.

Timeline

The Didache (The Teaching of the Twelve Apostles, c. 100) — neutral

The Didache, the earliest surviving Christian church order, preserves the oldest baptismal rubric we possess — and on the infant-baptism question it stands as a neutral witness, describing the rite without raising the age of its subjects. Coming to baptism only after the “Two Ways” catechesis (chapters 1–6), it directs: “concerning baptism, thus baptize ye: Having first said all these things, baptize into the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit, in living water… But before the baptism let the baptizer fast, and the baptized… one or two days before.”1 Two things follow. The rite it describes fits an instructed, professing candidate — one who has been taught and who can fast — which the credobaptist reads as the original norm and the paedobaptist as merely what a church order for adult converts would say, silent (not exclusionary) about believers’ newborns: the datum both sides claim on the dating argument. And its allowance of pouring “thrice upon the head” where immersion-water is lacking shows the apostolic-age church never made mode constitutive of a valid baptism — the point the immersion-mode argument turns on.2

Justin Martyr (the First Apology & Dialogue with Trypho, c. 155) — neutral

Justin gives the fullest surviving pre-Tertullian account of Christian baptism — and, like the Didache before him, he stands as a neutral, double-edged witness. His set-piece description of the rite portrays instructed adults: those who “are persuaded and believe that what we teach… is true” are, after fasting and prayer for the remission of past sins, “brought by us where there is water, and are regenerated” — the pre-200 datum the credobaptist reads as the original norm, and which the dating argument weighs on the critical side.3 Yet paedobaptists adduce two hints the other way: his mention of believers “who have been Christ’s disciples from childhood” and remain pure at sixty or seventy (First Apology 15), and his framing of baptism as the “spiritual circumcision” that replaces the carnal one (Dialogue 43) — circumcision having been given to infants, which is the earliest seed of the covenant-circumcision argument. Neither hint names an infant, and his own baptismal narrative describes professing believers, so Justin fixes what the mid-second-century rite looked like without settling whose children were brought to it.4

Irenaeus of Lyons (c. 180)

Irenaeus never discusses the baptism of infants directly, but in a famous sentence of Against Heresies II.22.4 he says Christ came to save "all… who through Him are born again to God — infants, and children, and boys, and youths, and old men."5 Because Irenaeus elsewhere defines baptism as "the new birth unto God,"6 paedobaptist readers count the infants here among the baptized-and-reborn, which would make Irenaeus a possible earliest — if implicit — witness to infant baptism, a generation before Tertullian. The inference is contested: the vocabulary is genuinely his, but the sentence's burden is recapitulation (Christ sanctifying every age by living through it), so whether it documents the practice or asserts that the incarnation redeems infancy is disputed — weighed on its argument page.7

Tertullian (c. 203)

Tertullian is the first Christian writer to discuss the baptism of little children explicitly — and he opposes it, holding that baptism is better delayed: weighing "the circumstances and disposition, and even age" of each person, he judges "the delay of baptism… preferable; principally, however, in the case of little children."8 His reasons are the risk to the sponsors who make the promises on the child's behalf, and his sense that the "innocent period of life" need not "hasten to the remission of sins" — better to let children "become Christians when they have become able to know Christ."9 Yet his very objection is the earliest firm evidence for the practice: he argues against a custom that plainly already existed and was common enough to need answering, placing infant baptism in North Africa by c. 200.10

Hippolytus of Rome (the Apostolic Tradition, c. 215)

Where Tertullian argues against the custom, the Apostolic Tradition — the church order traditionally ascribed to Hippolytus — simply prescribes it. Setting out the order of the paschal baptism, §21 directs: "first baptize the little ones; if they can speak for themselves, they shall do so; if not, their parents or other relatives shall speak for them," and only then the men and the women.11 Taken at face value this is the earliest descriptive witness to infant baptism — infants baptized first, with sponsors answering the credal questions on their behalf — recorded not as a novelty but as the settled shape of the rite, corroborating the practice Tertullian had opposed a decade earlier. The weight of the witness is qualified, however, by real scholarly doubt about the document: its Greek original is lost, it survives mainly in a fourth-century Latin version, and much recent work treats it as a composite church order layered over generations rather than one author's work of c. 215, so the passage cannot be securely dated to a single year.12

Origen (c. 248)

Origen is the first writer to claim infant baptism as a custom the Church received from the apostles. Commenting on Romans 6:6, he asks why the Law commands a sin offering for a newborn (Leviticus 12) — how can an infant have sinned? — and answers: “the church has received a tradition from the apostles to baptize even infants,” because the apostles “knew that there are real stains of sin in everyone which must be cleansed by water and the Spirit.”13 He treats the practice as universal and unremarkable elsewhere too: baptism “is given even to infants” by “the usage of the Church” (Homilies on Leviticus 8.3), and in Homilies on Luke 14 he reasons from the custom — if little children are baptized “for the remission of sins,” the “stains of birth” must be what the rite puts aside.14 Two caveats temper the marquee sentence: “5.9” is the commentary's section, not Romans 5:9, and that commentary survives for these books only in Rufinus's admittedly abridging Latin — though the independent Greek homilies attest the practice regardless.15

Cyprian of Carthage (c. 253)

Cyprian supplies the earliest conciliar witness. A bishop named Fidus had proposed that a newborn wait until the eighth day for baptism, on the analogy of Old-Testament circumcision; Cyprian, writing for a council of sixty-six African bishops, reports that they rejected the delay unanimously — “in this course which you thought was to be taken, no one agreed; but we all rather judge that the mercy and grace of God is not to be refused to any one born of man.”16 What is telling is what the letter never disputes: not whether infants are baptized (Fidus assumes it too) but only when. The eighth-day type is answered as a shadow fulfilled in Christ, and the newborn — who “has not sinned” but has “contracted the contagion of the ancient death” from Adam — is to be baptized without delay, so that “how much rather ought we to shrink from hindering an infant.”17 That an African synod of c. 253 could treat the timing of infant baptism as the only open question — a generation after Origen and about fifty years after Tertullian argued for delay — measures how settled the practice had become.

Cyril of Jerusalem (the Catechetical Lectures, c. 350) — the adult catechumenate

Cyril’s Jerusalem catechesis gives the fullest surviving picture of a fourth-century baptismal preparation — and, like the Didache and Justin before him, he stands as a neutral, double-edged witness. Every stage he describes presupposes a candidate old enough to answer for himself: enrollment, exorcism, a forty-day Lenten repentance — “thou hast forty days for repentance… But if thou persist in an evil purpose… the water will receive, but the Spirit will not accept thee” — and a personal profession at the font.18 He fixes the order the credobaptist presses: “Make ready the vessels of the soul, cleansed by unfeigned faith… Begin at once to wash your robes in repentance,” so that baptism works “in order that, the soul having been born again by faith, the body also might by the water partake of the grace.”19 He nowhere discusses infants; the witness is to what the adult road to the font looked like in mid-century Jerusalem — the professing-adult rite the credobaptist reads as the original norm and the paedobaptist as merely what a preparation for adult converts would say, silent (not exclusionary) about believers’ newborns: the datum both sides claim on the dating argument.

Basil of Caesarea (the Great, On the Holy Spirit & the Exhortation to Holy Baptism, c. 375) — adult baptism the norm

Basil — eldest of the three Cappadocian Fathers and Gregory of Nyssa’s brother — is a doubly telling witness: born into a famously devout Christian family, he was himself baptized only as an adult (c. 357, on his return from Athens), an instance of the very fourth-century deferral the historical arguments weigh, and he speaks of that baptism as “the beginning of life, and that day of regeneration the first of days.”20 His baptismal theology states the order the credobaptist presses: “Faith and baptism are two kindred and inseparable ways of salvation… first comes the confession, introducing us to salvation, and baptism follows, setting the seal upon our assent.”21 Yet his Exhortation to Holy Baptism is aimed not at infant baptism but at adult procrastination — grown catechumens, some “instructed in the divine word from… infancy” yet still unbaptized in old age: “when will you become a Christian?” he presses, for “the whole period of man’s life is the time for baptism.”22 He nowhere argues against the baptism of children; the delay he attacks is the adult’s, and the witness is to the settled fourth-century adult road to the font — real counterweight to an early-universal-norm reading, though (as Jeremias urged against Aland) equally readable as a temporary decline from an earlier custom.

Gregory of Nazianzus (c. 381)

Gregory, preaching On Holy Baptism at Constantinople, is a fourth-century Eastern witness that child baptism was accepted — contested, as at Carthage, only over timing. He tells parents not to fear consecrating even an infant: “let him be sanctified from his childhood; from his very tenderest age let him be consecrated by the Spirit… O what a small-souled mother, and of how little faith!”23 Facing the objection directly — “those who are still children… are we to baptize them too?” — he answers “Certainly, if any danger presses,” better “unconsciously sanctified than… unsealed and uninitiated”; and for the ordinary case (no danger) he counsels waiting “till the end of the third year, or a little more or less,” when a child can “answer something about the Sacrament.”24 The deferral is pastoral, not a denial: he never questions whether children are fit subjects, prescribes immediate baptism whenever life is at risk, and defers otherwise only so the child may grasp the rite's outlines — the Nicene and Post-Nicene Fathers editor notes that “S. Gregory did not reject infant Baptism… is clear” from just these directions.25

Gregory of Nyssa (On the Baptism of Christ, c. 383) — adult baptism the norm

Basil’s younger brother, the third Cappadocian, preaching on the Feast of Lights to a congregation of the baptized and the “uninitiated” catechumens still awaiting the font, frames baptism as a conscious regeneration — and, like Cyril and Basil, stands as a neutral witness to the ordinary adult road to baptism. His imagery assumes a grown subject with a moral history to be cleansed: baptism brings “him who bears the scars of sin, and has grown old in evil habits, to the innocence of the babe.”26 The rite turns on a profession the candidate makes for himself — “not receiving the sacrament in silence, but while there are spoken over us the Names of the Three Sacred Persons on Whom we believed” — and its reality is proved by the change of life that follows, the mark of “the newly-baptized man, who remembers his own profession, which he made to God.”27 He nowhere discusses infants; where his fellow Cappadocian Nazianzus does address children directly (emergency infant baptism without hesitation, elective baptism about age three), Nyssa simply describes and exhorts the professing-adult catechumenate — the same double-edged silence as the Didache and Justin, and part of the fourth-century deferral weighed on the antiquity-and-universality page.

Ambrose of Milan (c. 387)

Ambrose, bishop of Milan — and the man who baptized Augustine at the Milan font at Easter 387 — is a Western witness, a few years before Augustine, to the doctrine that underlies the practice. In De Abraham he expounds the command to circumcise the eight-day-old infant (Genesis 17) as a type of baptism, reasoning that no age is exempt from the sacrament because no age is exempt from sin: neither the aged proselyte nor the native-born infant is excluded, since every age is liable to sin and therefore fit for the sacrament.28 He clinches it from John 3:5, refusing to write any exception into Christ’s universal rule: “Unless a man be born again… No one is excepted, not the infant, not the one prevented by some necessity.”29 The witness is chiefly to the necessity and fittingness of infant baptism — the circumcision covenant-type Cyprian had used and Calvin would later systematize — rather than an explicit description of the rite; and on the infant who dies unbaptized Ambrose stops short of Augustine’s later severity, allowing a possible exemption from punishment but professing not to know “whether they have the honor of the kingdom.” The rationale is pointedly Western: where the Eastern Chrysostom would soon call infants “sinless” and ground their baptism in grace’s abundance, Ambrose grounds it in sin reaching even into infancy.

John Chrysostom (c. 390)

Chrysostom, preaching to catechumens at Antioch, takes infant baptism entirely for granted and turns it into an argument about the richness of the sacrament: because many suppose baptism gives nothing but the remission of sins, he points to infants as proof that it gives much more. “It is on this account that we baptize even infants, although they are sinless, that they may be given the further gifts of sanctification, justice, filial adoption, and inheritance, that they may be brothers and members of Christ, and become dwelling places for the Spirit.”30 The witness is the more telling for being incidental — the custom is a settled premise, not the thing to be proved — placing infant baptism as firmly in the fourth-century East as at Cyprian’s Carthage or Gregory’s Cappadocia. What is distinctively Eastern is his rationale: he calls infants “sinless” and pointedly leaves the remission of sins off the list of what their baptism confers, grounding the rite in the abundance of grace rather than in inherited guilt — a notable contrast with the Western original-sin framing of Cyprian’s “contagion of the ancient death” and, soon after, Augustine.31

The Council of Carthage (the foundling canon, September 401)

A generation before its more famous anti-Pelagian namesake, a North African council meeting at Carthage on 13 September 401 — convened chiefly to soften the church’s stance toward the Donatists — left an incidental but telling witness on infant baptism from the pastoral side. Africa’s bishops regularly took in abandoned children ransomed from barbarian raiders, and could not always know whether such a child had ever been baptized; the synod’s seventh canon ruled that where no reliable witnesses could confirm it and the children were unable, “on account of their tender age,” to answer for themselves, “all such children should be baptized without scruple, lest a hesitation should deprive them of the cleansing of the sacraments.”32 The ruling never argues that infants ought to be baptized — it assumes it throughout: the children in view cannot “answer concerning the giving of the sacraments to them,” yet the whole anxiety is that a doubtful case might leave one unbaptized, the doubtful re-baptism reckoned the lesser danger. Like Cyprian’s council of c. 253 (which settled when) and unlike the later Council of Carthage of 418 (which fixed why), this one touches only an individual case’s certainty — the infant’s fitness for the font being the unquestioned premise beneath all three.33

Jerome (c. 403)

Jerome shifts the register from whether or when to baptize to the parental duty to do so. Writing to Laeta (Letter 107, A.D. 403) on how to raise her infant daughter for Christ, he warns that the guilt of a withheld baptism falls on the parents: “perhaps you imagine that, if they are not baptized, the children of Christians are liable for their own sins; and that no guilt attaches to parents who withhold from baptism those who by reason of their tender age can offer no objection to it. The truth is that, as baptism ensures the salvation of the child, this in turn brings advantage to the parents.”34 Like Origen and Augustine, he presupposes the practice as wholly settled — the infant is a fit subject as a matter of course, and the only live question is the parents’ faithfulness in bringing her, so the witness is to the settled custom more than an argument for it. A decade or so later, in his Dialogue Against the Pelagians (c. 415), he makes that presupposed practice bear doctrinal weight: pressed “why little children are baptized,” his orthodox speaker answers “that their sins may be forgiven them,” grounds it in inherited Adamic guilt (Romans 5:14 — “he that is an infant is released in baptism from the chain which bound his father”), and marshals Cyprian’s letter to Fidus and Augustine’s treatises on infant baptism in support — the mature Western original-sin frame, with Jerome now a contemporary ally of Augustine against Pelagius.35

Augustine of Hippo (c. 412)

Augustine is the practice’s weightiest ancient witness and its great theological systematizer. Against the Donatists he treats infant baptism as the settled custom of the whole Church — “the firm tradition of the universal Church, in respect of the baptism of infants” — and lays down the rule that “what is held by the whole Church, and that not as instituted by Councils, but as a matter of invariable custom, is rightly held to have been handed down by apostolical authority.”36 Against the Pelagians he makes that universal practice the decisive argument for original sin: the Church everywhere baptizes infants for the remission of sins, yet infants commit no actual sins of their own, so — “inasmuch as infants are not held bound by any sins of their own actual life, it is the guilt of original sin which is healed in them by the grace of Him who saves them by the laver of regeneration.”37 In Letter 98 to Boniface he explains how a speechless infant is nonetheless truly baptized — a believer “through the sacrament of that faith,” his sponsors answering for him, delivered “from that condemnation which by one man entered into the world.”38 With Augustine the two lines the earlier witnesses had traced — the antiquity/universality of the custom and its link to inherited sin (already audible in Cyprian’s “contagion of the ancient death”) — are welded into the mature Western case, and the practice, never itself in question, becomes the immovable premise from which a whole doctrine is argued. The rationale is pointedly Western: where the Eastern Chrysostom called infants “sinless” and grounded their baptism in grace’s abundance, Augustine grounds it in guilt to be washed away.

Pelagius (c. 415)

Pelagius — Augustine’s great opponent, the man who denied that Adam’s guilt is inherited — is a hostile witness to how settled infant baptism had become: he conceded the practice outright and quarreled only over its meaning. His own confession of faith grants it in the plainest terms — infants “ought to be baptized for the remission of sins, according to the rule of the Church universal, and according to the meaning of the Gospel, we confess.”39 What he would not grant is that the sin so remitted is original: his disciple Coelestius said openly that the concession “was not admitted… with the view of our seeming to affirm sin by transmission,” and the positive Pelagian reading — a “novel opinion” that scandalized Augustine at Carthage — was “that infants are not baptized for the purpose of receiving remission of sin, but that they may be sanctified in Christ.”40 The witness is the stronger for being adverse: the leading theological dissident of the age, with every motive to be rid of the argument Augustine built on the rite, still could not deny the rite — the fourth- and fifth-century church argued over why it baptized infants, never whether. (A minority reading holds Pelagius opposed the practice; the mainstream judgment — and his own words — is that he kept it while denying its original-sin rationale.)

The Council of Milevum (the anti-Pelagian synod of Numidia, 416)

Two years before its more famous sequel at Carthage, the North-African church condemned the Pelagian re-reading of infant baptism at a pair of provincial synods in the autumn of 416 — one at Carthage, and one at Milevum (Mileve, in Numidia, Augustine’s own province), where some sixty bishops under the primate Silvanus rejected the doctrines of Pelagius and Coelestius as contrary to the catholic faith.41 As at every stage of the controversy, the practice of baptizing infants was never the question — only its meaning: the synods, and the accompanying letter of five bishops (Augustine among them) to Pope Innocent I, set out the doctrine of original sin, infant baptism, and grace, and Innocent, replying on 27 January 417, confirmed their decisions, rejected the Pelagian theory of infant baptism, and excommunicated Pelagius and Coelestius — the condemnation the plenary Council of Carthage of 418 would make binding conciliar law. Milevum’s own act was this synodal letter, not a canon of its own: the familiar anathema on any who deny that “infants fresh from their mothers’ wombs” should be baptized — sometimes cited as “Milevum, canon 3” — is in fact the second canon of the 418 Carthage synod, filed under Milevum’s name only because a later canon-collection grouped the two councils’ anti-Pelagian canons under one joint heading.42

The Council of Carthage (the anti-Pelagian canons, 418)

Where Cyprian’s council of 253 had settled the timing of infant baptism, the North African synods of the next century and a half fixed its rationale as binding doctrine — the conciliar counterpart to Augustine and the formal answer to Pelagius. A plenary council of more than two hundred African bishops, meeting at Carthage on 1 May 418 — the culmination of the anti-Pelagian councils of Carthage and Mileve (Milevum) in 416, whose synodal letters Pope Innocent I had confirmed — decreed in its second canon that “whosoever denies that infants newly from their mother’s wombs should be baptized, or says that baptism is for the remission of sins, but that they derive from Adam no original sin, which needs to be removed by the laver of regeneration… let him be anathema,” grounding the rule in Romans 5:12 as “the Catholic Church everywhere diffused has always understood it,” so that “even infants, who could have committed as yet no sin themselves, therefore are truly baptized for the remission of sins.”43 The canon condemns both errors Pelagius’s circle had floated — that the newborn need not be baptized at all, and (the live Pelagian claim) that they are baptized while carrying no inherited Adamic guilt — welding the practice, never itself in dispute, to the original-sin doctrine as a test of communion. It is Augustine’s argument from the baptismal liturgy made a conciliar canon; a further canon denying unbaptized infants a happy “middle place” outside the kingdom is of doubtful authenticity — absent from the early canon-collections of Dionysius and Isidore, and nothing here rests on it.44

Peter of Bruys (the Petrobrusians, c. 1131) — the first medieval opponent

For the seven centuries after the North African councils the paedobaptist consensus held with no serious challenger on record — until Peter of Bruys, an itinerant preacher in Provence and Languedoc, made the baptism of infants the first of the “errors” his opponents catalogued. He left no writings; his teaching survives in Peter the Venerable’s Contra Petrobrusianos (c. 1139–1140), whose lead charge is that among the Petrobrusians “the baptism of persons before they have reached the years of discretion is invalid,” their believers’ baptism resting on Mark 16:16, with those baptized in infancy re-baptized on coming to faith.45 This is a genuine anticipation of the credobaptist “No”: baptism is “a necessary condition for salvation, but… preceded by personal faith, so that its administration to infants is worthless” — the broad faith-precedes-baptism case, read off the same Mark 16:16 Menno would use four centuries later, and even the Petrobrusians’ insistence that they did not re-baptize (the infant washing having been no true baptism) prefigures the later Anabaptist self-understanding. It must, however, be kept distinct from the roughly contemporary dualist Cathars, who spurned water baptism as a thing of evil matter: Peter kept baptism and merely made it a believer’s rite — and he is a standing qualification to the claim that the practice went wholly uncontested before the Reformation.46

Petr Chelčický and the Unity of the Brethren (c. 1443) — a qualified dissent

A century before the Anabaptists, the Bohemian (Hussite) reformation produced a hedged anticipation of believers’ baptism. The lay theologian Petr Chelčický made unforced personal faith central — “by the use of force no man is brought to faith in Christ” — and preferred that baptism follow it, yet he neither forbade infant baptism nor demanded rebaptism.47 His heirs, the Unity of the Brethren (Unitas Fratrum, organized at Kunvald in 1457), went further in practice: they “rebaptized converts… because they considered personal faith an essential condition of the baptismal covenant… But they did not on this account reject infant baptism. Children were baptized soon after their birth… then… rebaptized… when old enough to exercise personal faith” — a middle position (infant baptism and adult rebaptism on profession) that they “relinquished by a formal act of the General Synod of 1534,” substituting confirmation, partly to distance themselves from the Anabaptists.48 The witness is real but qualified: an instinct that faith should precede the font, half-institutionalized and then walked back at the very moment the Swiss and Dutch radicals were raising adult baptism as their banner.

Andreas Bodenstein von Karlstadt (the abolition of infant baptism at Orlamünde, c. 1524) — the middle position

Before the Swiss radicals acted, Luther’s own early Wittenberg colleague had already moved — a step short of them. Karlstadt is the first magisterial-generation Reformer to reject infant baptism: at his parish of Orlamünde (1523–1524) he stopped baptizing infants, though “unlike the Anabaptists” he “spoke only incidentally” against the rite, classing it with circumcision as an “outward thing,” and holding “that it was better to postpone baptism until the candidate was sure of his faith.”49 His fullest statement, the Dialogus… vom Tauff der Kinder (Worms, 1527), argues from its very title that infant baptism rests on a fremder Glaube — an “alien faith,” the sponsors’ or the Church’s, and not the child’s own — the objection Luther would answer in 1529 by grounding baptism in God’s Word rather than the recipient’s faith. Yet — distinctively — Karlstadt neither rebaptized adults christened as infants nor was rebaptized himself, resembling in this the magisterial Reformers who held their Roman baptism valid: a stance between Zwingli, who defended the paedobaptist practice, and the Swiss Brethren, who made adult rebaptism their banner. Conrad Grebel wrote to him in 1524 and Felix Manz helped print his eucharistic tracts, so his pen carried the credobaptist question toward the men who would rebaptize that following January — the reason a standard study is titled Karlstadt as the Father of the Baptist Movements, though he stopped short of founding one.50

Wilhelm Reublin (Röubli, the first Zürich pastor to preach against infant baptism, c. 1524)

Before the founding act came the agitation that led to it. Reublin, a priest at Witikon in the villages around Zürich, was — on the record — the earliest of the Swiss radicals to move against the font: the reference tradition calls him “the first in the canton of Zürich… to preach against the baptism of infants,” and reports that he “began to call in question the Scriptural authority and the propriety of infant baptism.”51 This was no mere opinion: “acting on his advice several parents withheld their infants from christening and incurred severe punishment therefor,” the refusal spreading from Witikon to Zollikon until the Zürich council intervened in August 1524 — fining the refusers, briefly imprisoning Reublin, and warning him to desist. He was an antipedobaptist speaker at the disputation of 17 January 1525, and was expelled from the canton with Conrad Grebel and Felix Manz four days before the Swiss Brethren’s first believers’ baptism, at which he was present.52 His witness is an act before it is a text — like the rebaptism a few months later, it enacts the credobaptist “No” (the infant no fit subject of the font) before any confession or treatise spelled it out — and it was he who carried believers’ baptism outward, baptizing Hubmaier and some sixty others at Waldshut about Easter 1525. He left no surviving tract of his own on baptism: an agitator and evangelist, not a systematic writer, he broke with Zwingli over the same refusal to let the council set the pace of reform.

The Swiss Brethren (Conrad Grebel, George Blaurock & Felix Manz — the first believers’ baptism, 21 January 1525)

Between the confessions and treatises that argued the credobaptist case and the patristic paedobaptism they rejected stands the act that began the movement: the first known adult re-baptism since antiquity, and the origin-point of Anabaptism. When the Zürich council, after the disputation of 17 January 1525, ordered every unbaptized infant baptized within the week on pain of expulsion — a decree aimed at Zwingli’s former associates, who had refused to present their children (Conrad Grebel had left his newborn daughter Rachel unbaptized) — the radicals gathered on 21 January at the house of Felix Manz’s mother. The Chronicle of the Hutterian Brethren records what followed: after prayer, “George of the house of Jacob [George Blaurock] stood up and besought Conrad Grebel for God’s sake to baptize him with the true Christian baptism upon his faith and knowledge… Conrad baptized him, since at that time there was no ordained minister to perform such work,” and Blaurock in turn baptized the rest.53 The deed makes a believer’s own “faith and knowledge” the thing baptism answers to — which no infant can bring — and so enacts the exclusion that Schleitheim would confess two years later and Hubmaier, Menno, and Smyth would argue at length. It did not come from nowhere: Wilhelm Reublin had been preaching against infant baptism in the villages around Zürich since about 1523, and would baptize Hubmaier at Waldshut that Easter.54 Grebel (c. 1498–1526), Manz (c. 1498–1527, drowned in the Limmat as the first Protestant-executed Anabaptist martyr), and Blaurock (c. 1491–1529, burned) all died within a few years — but the church founded that January night is the fountainhead of the Swiss Brethren, and behind them the Mennonites, Hutterites, Amish, and Baptists.

Huldrych Zwingli (Of Baptism, 1525) — the covenant sign

Zwingli is the first magisterial Reformer to defend infant baptism against the emerging Anabaptists — and the first to ground it in the covenant, the case Calvin would systematize a decade later. When his own former associates among the Zürich radicals began rebaptizing one another in January 1525, he answered them in Of Baptism (Von der Taufe, May 1525) — the booklet Hubmaier refuted that July — and more fully in his Refutation of the Tricks of the Catabaptists (1527), where he argues from covenant standing rather than any act of the child’s: “The children of Christians are no less sons of God than the parents, just as in the Old Testament. Hence, since they are sons of God, who will forbid their baptism?”55 The backbone is the circumcision parallel: “Circumcision among the ancients (so far as it was sacramental) was the same as baptism with us. As that was given to infants so ought baptism to be administered to infants” — baptism now doing what circumcision once did, so the believer’s infant is its natural subject (the covenant-circumcision argument, weighed contested).56 Against the Brethren’s demand for prior faith, Zwingli reverses the order of dependence: one belongs to the covenant body first and the sign is added to a membership already held, so an infant is “not excluded because they cannot hear or understand… being of the body precedes bearing the mark of the body.” The sign seals covenant membership; it does not create it — and so, he concludes, Scripture does not require the recipient’s prior profession.57

Balthasar Hubmaier (On the Christian Baptism of Believers, 1525)

Two years before Schleitheim put the credobaptist position into a confession, Hubmaier — the Waldshut pastor and doctor of theology who became first-generation Anabaptism’s ablest theologian — had already argued it at treatise length. His On the Christian Baptism of Believers (1525), written in five days against Zwingli and reckoned by historians the most effective Reformation-era defence of believers’ baptism, defines the rite so as to require a personal confession no infant can make: to baptize “is to pour outward water over the confessor of his sins… and to inscribe him in the number of sinners upon his own confession and acknowledgment.”58 Against Zwingli he reads off Scripture a fixed order — “the word, hearing, faith, baptism, works” — and concludes that “since infants cannot be taught, they should not be baptised”; to baptize a child as a future believer, he says, “is like hanging out a hoop as a sign of future wine.”59 Both credobaptist proof-texts later weighed on this timeline are already whole here: the Great Commission read as a teach-then-baptize sequence that bars the un-teachable infant — the case Smyth would compress into a syllogism eighty years on — and the broader apostolic order of hearing and believing before the font. Unbowed to the end (his emblem was “Truth is immortal”), Hubmaier was burned at Vienna on 10 March 1528, his wife Elsbeth drowned in the Danube three days later.60

The Schleitheim Confession (Michael Sattler & the Swiss Brethren, 1527)

The Schleitheim Confession is the first witness here to reject infant baptism outright — the point where the long-settled question of when to baptize a child becomes a question of whether a child is a subject of baptism at all. Its opening article restricts baptism to those “who have learned repentance and amendment of life, and who believe truly that their sins are taken away by Christ… and who with this significance request it… and demand it for themselves. This excludes all infant baptism, the highest and chief abomination of the pope.”61 By making repentance, personal faith, and a conscious request — none of them possible for a newborn — constitutive of a valid baptism, the article denies that an infant can be baptized in any true sense, appealing to the apostolic pattern of preaching-then-belief (Matthew 28, Mark 16, Acts 2, 8, 16, 19).62 Adopted by a gathering of Swiss Brethren at Schleitheim on 24 February 1527 and now credited chiefly to the ex-Benedictine prior Michael Sattler — burned at the stake three months later — it is the foundational confessional statement of the credobaptist (believers’-baptism) position, breaking not only with Rome but with the magisterial Reformers Luther, Zwingli, and Calvin, who all retained the paedobaptist practice the patristic witnesses above had handed down.63

Martin Luther (the Large Catechism, 1529)

Two years after Schleitheim, Luther answers the Anabaptist objection directly in the fourth part of the Large Catechism, defending the paedobaptism the patristic witnesses above had handed down. He argues first from its fruits: “that the Baptism of infants is pleasing to Christ is sufficiently proved from His own work, namely, that God sanctifies many of them who have been thus baptized, and has given them the Holy Ghost” — naming Bernard, Gerson, and Hus as men of undeniable sanctity whose only baptism was in infancy, for God “can never be opposed to Himself… or for its promotion impart His grace and Spirit” to a rite He rejected.64 But his decisive move relocates the ground of a valid baptism away from the recipient’s faith and onto God’s Word: “everything depends upon the Word and command of God… Baptism is valid, even though faith be wanting. For my faith does not make Baptism, but receives it.”65 Where Schleitheim had made repentance, faith, and a personal request constitutive of baptism — and so excluded the newborn — Luther makes the Word constitutive and faith merely receptive, dissolving at its root the objection that a faithless infant cannot be baptized. The rationale is neither the Western original-sin argument of Augustine nor the Eastern “abundance of grace” of Chrysostom, but a sacramental one about what makes a baptism — the distinctively magisterial-Reformation defense of the practice.

John Calvin (the Institutes, 1536) — the covenant seal

Calvin gives paedobaptism its most sustained Reformed defense. He first dismantles the Anabaptist argument from silence: that no infant is named as baptized by the apostles proves nothing, for “if such kinds of argument were good, it would be necessary, in like manner, to interdict women from the Lord’s Supper, since we do not read that they were ever admitted to it in the days of the apostles” — and the baptized households of Acts (16:15, 16:32) are naturally read to include their children. The claim that paedobaptism went unknown for years after the resurrection he calls “a shameful falsehood, since there is no writer, however ancient, who does not trace its origin to the days of the apostles” — the same apostolic-antiquity appeal Origen and Augustine had made.66 His constructive ground, though, is neither antiquity nor (with Luther) the Word that makes the sacrament, but the covenant: baptism is “the impress of a seal” confirming the promise that the Lord will be “a God not to him only, but to his seed” (Genesis 17), so a believer’s child already stands within the covenant the sign seals and must not be denied it.67 This covenantal framing — baptism as the new-covenant successor to circumcision — became the defining shape of the Reformed case for infant baptism.

Menno Simons (the Foundation of Christian Doctrine, c. 1539)

Menno Simons — the ex-priest whose followers became the Mennonites — is the great Dutch codifier of the believers’-baptism position Hubmaier had argued and Schleitheim had confessed before him. In the baptism section of his Foundation of Christian Doctrine he fixes a scriptural order in which faith always precedes the font — “he that believeth and is baptized, shall be saved… Faith is before baptism” — and rests it on three pillars: “First, because of the divine commandment of our beloved Lord Jesus Christ, which can never be broken. Secondly, because of the teaching of the holy apostles. Thirdly, because of the practice of these same apostles.”68 From that order he excludes infants directly: because a child “cannot be made to understand any thing visible,” still less can it be “taught and instructed in invisible, celestial matters of the Spirit,” so to baptize it is to invert Christ’s appointed sequence and pervert the ordinance.69 Where Schleitheim had made repentance, faith, and a personal request constitutive of baptism, Menno reaches the same exclusion through the un-teachability of infancy — the broad faith-precedes-baptism case, with the appeal to Christ’s commission (Mark 16) that Smyth would soon compress into a Great-Commission syllogism. His name would pass to a whole tradition, but on baptism he systematizes what the first Anabaptists had already established.70

John Smyth (The Character of the Beast, 1609)

Smyth carries the credobaptist case from Schleitheim’s confessional assertion to a formal argument from the Great Commission. An English Separatist minister exiled at Amsterdam — and, holding his own infant baptism void, the self-baptizing “Se-Baptist” who gathered what is generally reckoned the first English Baptist church — he reasons against infant baptism from Matthew 28:19: “They only are to be baptized that are made Disciples by teaching. Infants cannot be made Disciples by teaching. Ergo, Infants are not to be baptized.”71 He then presses it from probability into prohibition: “Every precept affirmative conteyneth a negative vnder it,” so the command to “make Disciples by teaching & baptise them” itself forbids baptizing “those that are not made Disciples by teaching… & so infants are not to be baptized.”72 Where Schleitheim had made repentance, faith, and a personal request constitutive of baptism, Smyth grounds the exclusion in the order of the commission — teaching must precede baptism, and an infant cannot be taught — turning the same Great-Commission text the paedobaptists read as commissioning household baptism (the baptized “households” of Acts that Calvin pressed) into a bar against the practice. His syllogism is the seed of the “make-disciples-then-baptize excludes infants” reading of Matthew 28:19.

Thomas Helwys (A Declaration of Faith of English People Remaining at Amsterdam, 1611) — the first Baptist church on English soil

Helwys plants Smyth’s believers’-baptism principle in English soil and fixes it in a confession. His 1611 Declaration of Faith, the earliest Baptist confession, makes the exclusion of infants a standing article: baptism is received “upon the Confession of their faith and sins… according to the primitive Institution (Matthew 28:19) and practice (Acts 2:41),” and, being “the outward manifestation of dieing unto sin, and walking in newness of life (Romans 6),… therefore in no wise appertains to infants.”73 A Nottinghamshire gentleman who had funded the congregation’s exile, Helwys broke with Smyth when Smyth repudiated his own self-baptism and sought union with the Waterlander Mennonites, led a remnant of about a dozen back to England, and c. 1611–1612 gathered at Spitalfields (London) what is generally reckoned the first Baptist church on English ground — a General (Arminian) Baptist congregation.74 Where Smyth had reasoned the exclusion from the order of the Great Commission, Helwys does not add a new syllogism so much as institutionalize the position — resting it on the same proof-texts (Matthew 28:19; the Pentecost pattern of Acts 2:41, where confession precedes the font — the faith-precedes-baptism case) and on the Romans 6 death-and-resurrection symbolism a newborn cannot enact. His Short Declaration of the Mistery of Iniquity (1612) then couples believers’ baptism to the first English plea for total liberty of conscience — a stand that cost him imprisonment and death in Newgate.

John Spilsbury (A Treatise Concerning the Lawfull Subject of Baptisme, 1643, and the First London Baptist Confession, 1644) — the Particular Baptists and the recovery of immersion

Where Smyth’s self-baptism (1609) began the Arminian General Baptists who baptized by affusion, Spilsbury marks a distinct node in the credobaptist “No”: the Calvinistic (Particular) strand and the recovery of believers’ baptism by immersion. A London cobbler who seceded from an independent congregation over infant baptism, he led what is generally reckoned the first Particular Baptist church (its immersion usually dated to about 1638), and in his A Treatise Concerning the Lawfull Subject of Baptisme (1643) answered the covenant case on its own ground — holding the Reformed one-covenant theology but arguing that under the new covenant its membership is spiritual: none are Abraham’s seed but as they believe as he believed, so the sign belongs to the believing and not to the infant by descent (the credobaptist reply to the covenant-circumcision argument).75 His name stands among the subscribers of the First London Baptist Confession (1644), whose baptism articles make the position confessional: baptism is “to be dispensed only upon persons professing faith, or that are Disciples, or taught, who upon a profession of faith, ought to be baptized” (Art. 39, the first edition reading “onely”) — the newborn, professing none, excluded.76 And its manner “the Scripture holds out to be dipping or plunging the whole body under water: it being a sign, must answer the thing signified” — the death, burial, and resurrection of Romans 6 — the first English creed to make immersion confessional, and the crystallized form of the immersion-mode argument (weighed weak as a bar to infant baptism, since it settles how to baptize, not whom).77

Arguments

Sub-claims marshaled for (or against) infant baptism, each weighed adversarially on its own page.

Related arguments — the historical case

Three angles on the practice's origin. One weighs the dating of the earliest evidence — how old is the practice? — and finds it plausibly ancient: an established custom by c. 200 whose hostile earliest witness and untraced arrival push it back into the second century. A second weighs whether that early, uncontested universality proves the custom apostolic. The third runs the record the other way — the widespread patristic delay of baptism — and asks whether so much deferral, even in devout homes, shows infant baptism was never the universal apostolic default. The dates make infant baptism ancient (probable); neither the universality nor the delay of baptism, by itself, settles apostolic institution — both stall at the paradigm Jeremias–Aland standoff (contested).

  • Infant baptism is an ancient practice — established by c. 200 and probably reaching into the second century — plausible (the chronological wing: the firm explicit record begins c. 200, and the earliest witness — Tertullian — opposes the practice, his very objection presupposing an entrenched custom, which sets it before c. 200 and probably into the second century. So the practice is demonstrably ancient, not a late graft — the likelier reading of the dates, not merely a coherent one. What the dating cannot carry is the further step from ancient to apostolic: no explicit witness survives before c. 200, and the pre-200 baptismal descriptions we have — the Didache, Justin — portray professing adults, so strict apostolic institution stays contested on the companion universality page)
  • Does infant baptism's early, uncontested spread prove it apostolic? — contested (the breadth wing: the practice is early, universal by the fourth century, called apostolic by those nearest the source, and — tellingly — never the subject of a founding-controversy; the church argued only over when, never whether. Real weight against a late-innovation theory, but not proof: the New Testament is silent and the first explicit witness opposes it, universal custom need not be apostolic, and the "the church couldn't have erred 1300 years" form proves too much — by the Restorationist symmetry it must be weighed case by case, not treated as a trump)
  • Does the widespread patristic delay of baptism show infant baptism was not universal or apostolic? — contested (the credobaptist counter-move, run off the same record: for centuries baptism was routinely delayed, often to adulthood, even among the devout — Tertullian counsels it, the Cappadocians and Ambrose, Jerome, Rufinus, Chrysostom were baptized as adults, and Augustine's devout mother deferred his — which genuinely punctures the loose "always and everywhere" claim and forces even Jeremias to treat the delay as a temporary decline. But the strong thesis overreaches: the delay is an elective preference within a church that had infant baptism (prescribed at once whenever danger pressed), its motive was post-baptismal-sin anxiety not a denial that infants may be baptized, the first witness to delay (Tertullian) is arguing against a practice that already existed, and the tradition itself resisted the delay (Cyprian's council rejected even an eight-day wait). Wounds the universality case without proving absence — the same Jeremias–Aland standoff)

Related arguments — the order of faith and baptism

The two credobaptist arguments that the New Testament fixes an order — faith or discipleship first, then baptism — which a newborn cannot meet. One reads that order off the whole pattern of Acts ("repent… and be baptized"); the other off the grammar of the Great Commission ("make disciples… baptizing them"). Both come out contested — sound as accounts of how adult converts come to the font, but silent on the covenant status of believers' children, which is the point in dispute.

  • Does the New Testament order — repentance and faith, then baptism — exclude infants? — contested (the broad pattern-of-Acts case: every narrated baptism follows hearing and believing — “Repent, and be baptized” (Acts 2:38), “when they believed… were baptized” (Acts 8:12) — and no paedobaptist reverses it for an adult convert; but every clear instance is a first-generation adult convert and the texts are silent on believers' children, the covenant sign was given under the old covenant to a believer's eight-day-old son who could not yet believe, the paedobaptist tradition answers that the infant believes “by others” (Bede) or that the Word — not the recipient's faith — makes the baptism (Luther), and two of the four stock proof-texts (Mark 16:16, Acts 8:37) rest on disputed text)
  • Does the Great Commission's “make disciples… baptizing them” (Matthew 28:19) exclude infants? — contested (the credobaptist proof-text, from Smyth: baptism is subordinate to disciple-making, and Acts baptizes only those who hear and believe — an order even a paedobaptist father, Jerome, reads off the verse; but the two participles are coordinate means not a fixed sequence, the “teaching” participle is post-baptismal, the commission addresses the mission to the nations not the covenant status of believers' children, and Smyth's “affirmative precept forbids the un-taught” move is an argument from silence that proves too much — the verse neither commands nor forbids infant baptism)

Related arguments — the objective ground of baptism

Luther's two answers, in the Large Catechism (1529), to the Anabaptist objection that a faithless infant cannot be baptized — the paedobaptist rebuttal to the order-of-faith arguments above. Both locate the ground of a valid baptism outside the infant's own faith: one in God's Word, which makes the sacrament; the other in God's demonstrated action, the Spirit's fruits in those baptized as infants. The first comes out contested — strong within a sacramental framework, but resting on a premise the credobaptist denies; the second weak — the sanctity of the infant-baptized shows God works in them, not that infancy was the efficacious moment.

  • Does baptism's validity rest on God's Word, not the recipient's faith — so a faithless infant is truly baptized? — contested (Luther's decisive move: “my faith does not make Baptism, but receives it” — a sacrament is constituted by God's Word joined to the water, so validity hangs on God's act, not the recipient's faith, and the infant's incapacity is no bar. The validity-vs-fruitful-reception distinction is genuine, as old as Augustine's anti-Donatist teaching, and widely held; and it rightly exposes the credobaptist objection as assuming the very point at issue. But it does not settle the question — it rests on a sacramental realism the believers'-baptism tradition rejects, for which faith is part of what baptism is, not a hand that receives an already-real sacrament. Strong by sacramental lights, question-begging by credobaptist ones)
  • Does God's sanctifying of those baptized in infancy (Bernard, Gerson, Hus) prove infant baptism pleases Him? — weak (Luther's argument from fruits: God has visibly given His Spirit to men baptized only in infancy, and would not confirm a rite He rejected, so the practice pleases Him. As a rebuttal to “God rejects it” the sanctity is real; but the inference to “infant baptism was the efficacious act” is a non sequitur — all three were sanctified as adult believers, which the credobaptist grants — and the “the Church could not have erred so long” premise proves too much, the same over-reach weighed on the antiquity-and-universality page. Defensive, not probative)

Related arguments — the saving ground of baptism

Two positive rationales the ancient church gave for baptizing — and hurrying — the newborn, both resting on baptism's saving work rather than on the covenant sign or the infant's own faith. One reasons from the infant's need (it inherits Adam's sin and must have it remitted); the other from baptism's necessity (it is the regeneration without which none can enter the kingdom). Both come out contested — powerful within the Latin sacramental frame that drives the practice's urgency, disputed by the Christian East (which baptizes the same infants on other ground) and the credobaptist (who denies a rite regenerates a passive, faithless infant).

  • Does infants’ inherited sin (Psalm 51:5, Romans 5:12) require their baptism for the remission of sin? — contested (the oldest positive rationale in the fathers, and the engine of the practice's urgency: the newborn contracts Adam's sin and so needs baptism “for the remission of sins” — Cyprian, Origen, Augustine, the anti-Pelagian councils — and Scripture does teach a real inherited sinful condition. But the step to a damnable guilt needing sacramental remission is what the Christian East (which baptizes infants on other ground) and the credobaptist deny; the proof-texts carry real cruxes (Romans 5:12's “in whom all sinned”; Psalm 51:5 as hyperbole); and the strict “no baptism, therefore lost” form proves too much, softened by the tradition itself)
  • Does baptism's regenerating necessity (John 3:5, 1 Peter 3:21, Titus 3:5) require infants to be baptized? — contested (the mechanism behind that urgency: baptism is the regeneration — the new birth “of water,” the “washing of regeneration” that “now saves” — without which none enters the kingdom, so infants, who die in great numbers, must be baptized “without delay” (Cyprian) or “if any danger presses” (Nazianzus); the baptismal reading of the texts is the patristic majority. But the necessity it proves is that all need the new birth, not that infants are its subjects — that step it borrows from the inherited-sin and Word-not-faith grounds; John 3:5's “water” admits a natural-birth or Ezekiel-36 reading, 1 Peter 3:21's own rider foregrounds “the answer of a good conscience” (read even by the paedobaptist commentator Bede as requiring unfeigned faith), and the strict necessity-form proves too much — the unbaptized infant lost — softened by Augustine, Ambrose, and Nazianzus themselves)
  • Baptism as the new-covenant circumcision: does the covenant sign belong to believers' infants? — contested (the backbone of the Reformed case: one covenant of grace, its sign once given to believers' infants by command, Paul linking baptism to circumcision — strong systematically; but it rests on the disputed premise that new-covenant membership tracks physical descent, which the credo reading of Jeremiah 31 denies, and the proof-texts weren't read that way by the earliest exegetes)
  • Do the New Testament household baptisms imply infants were baptized? — contested (households held children and the oikos idiom treats a house as a unit, so some baptized household probably contained an infant; but every text that specifies describes hearing/believing members, none names an infant, and the earliest exegesis of these verses draws no infants from them — corroboration, not proof)
  • Does Paul’s calling believers’ children “holy” (1 Corinthians 7:14) warrant baptizing them? — weak (as a warrant for the font: the verse really does set a believer’s children in a “holy,” not “unclean,” category — genuine covenant standing that feeds the covenant argument; but the leap to “a saint, therefore baptized” equivocates on hagios and is undercut in the same verse, which calls the pagan spouse equally “sanctified” though none baptize him, and no ancient exegete — least of all Tertullian, who expounds the verse yet opposed the practice — read it as a baptismal title. Corroborates covenant status, does not warrant baptism)
  • Does “Suffer the little children to come unto me” (Mark 10:14 par.) support infant baptism? — weak (Christ genuinely receives and blesses infants and calls the kingdom theirs “of such,” but the text has no baptism in it, its lone patristic afterlife as a living custom is infant blessing not baptism, the fathers read “of such” as childlike character not literal infancy, Tertullian used the same verse against the practice, and the “kōlyō formula” bridge to the font is a contested conjecture — its real force is toward infant dedication)
  • Is baby dedication — the credobaptist alternative — grounded in the biblical presentation examples (Exodus 13, Samuel, the presentation of Jesus)? — weak (as scriptural or historical warrant for the modern rite: consecrating a child to God is a good, biblically congruent impulse God nowhere forbids — that much holds; but the texts don’t map. Exodus 13:2 and Luke 2:22–24 are the firstborn-son law of consecration-and-redemption, restricted to the male who opens the womb; Hannah’s Samuel is a permanent surrender to sanctuary service, the reverse of a keep-the-child ceremony; and the practice has no pre-Reformation precedent, arising in the 1520s as a substitute for the infant baptism the Anabaptists discarded. Lacking direct warrant but condemned by no text).
  • Does Irenaeus's "reborn to God" (Adv. Haer. II.22.4) witness infant baptism? — contested ("born again to God" is Irenaeus's own word for baptism, so infants stand among the baptized-reborn; but the sentence's burden is recapitulation, so whether it documents the practice is disputed)
  • Polycarp's “eighty-six years” of service (Martyrdom of Polycarp 9): does it show he was baptized as an infant? — weak (Jeremias's indirect datum: martyred c. 155 at about eighty-six, Polycarp says he “served” Christ all those years, so he must have been a Christian — and baptized — from infancy; but the chain needs four re-readable steps — that “eighty-six years” is his whole life not his years since conversion, that “served Him” dates his baptism rather than describing lifelong devotion, that a cradle-Christian was baptized in the cradle, and that the contested martyrdom-arithmetic holds — and even granting all four it yields only a child raised Christian from birth, not a recorded infant baptism. Suggestive corroboration within the historical case, no proof on its own)
  • Did the medieval Waldenses reject infant baptism? — weak (the successionist claim of a continuous believers’-baptism church: rests on Reinerius Saccho’s “the ablution given to infants profits nothing” (1254), a genuine but late, regional line denying baptism’s efficacy for infants, not a re-baptizing practice; against it, the founder Valdes approved infant baptism in writing (c. 1180), the Waldensian Rescriptum called it “necessary for all,” the late-medieval barbes took infants to Catholic fonts, and the proto-Baptist reading is a traceable modern construction resting partly on a mistranslation and on a conflation with the genuinely anti-paedobaptist Petrobrusians. Only a narrow, contested remnant survives — a later Lombard strand doubting infant baptism’s necessity; the dualist Paulicians/Bogomils/Cathars on the same roll fall away entirely, having spurned water baptism out of contempt for matter)
  • Does baptism require immersion — picturing death, burial, and resurrection — so that infant sprinkling fails the sign? — weak (the credobaptist mode argument: baptizō means to immerse, immersion was the early normal mode, and Paul's burial imagery (Romans 6:3–4; Colossians 2:12) fits it — all true and largely conceded; but the earliest church order already permits pouring where water is scarce (Didache 7), so the apostolic-age church never made mode constitutive of a valid baptism, and the argument targets the wrong axis: it concerns how to baptize, not whom — the Orthodox baptize infants by immersion, so the mode case leaves the subject question untouched. Its real force is toward immersion as the fuller sign)
  • Does Romans 6’s dying-and-rising (buried with Christ, walking in newness of life) require a believing subject, excluding infants? — contested (a double-edged text, pressed on both sides. The credobaptist reads the death-burial-resurrection of Romans 6:3–4 as a conscious dying-to-self and rising no infant can enact, so the rite presupposes a believing subject — sound as an account of the adult convert, and the moral emphasis is Chrysostom’s own. But the governing verbs are passive (a burial-with-Christ God works on the subject, not a feat the recipient performs), “walk in newness of life” is the fruit baptism sets going rather than a precondition, and Paul’s own next breath ties the same burial to “the circumcision made without hands” (Colossians 2:11–12), the covenant sign given to infants — so the apostle did not read “buried with Christ” as excluding the non-professing covenant member. Yet the paedo counter does not simply win either: Colossians 2:12 grounds the rising “through faith,” and its covenant-continuity premise is itself contested. Kin to the order-of-faith arguments)
  • Does Christ’s own baptism as an adult (Matthew 3:13–17) model believers’ baptism — or does his sinless reception of a baptism of repentance cut the other way? — contested (a double-edged text. The credo side takes Christ’s conscious adult baptism as a model — and the fathers do call it exemplary (“he did that which he commanded all his disciples to do,” Ambrosiaster); but the example they draw is of humility, not of baptismal age, no ancient reader inferred an infant-bar from it, credobaptists baptize professing children far short of Christ’s thirty, and the same Christ was given the covenant sign in infancy (Luke 2:21). The counter-edge is sharper: John’s was a “baptism of repentance” (Mark 1:4) yet the sinless Christ received it “to fulfil all righteousness,” not for his own sin (granted by Theodore, Chrysostom, Jerome) — so a valid reception need not presuppose the recipient’s repentance, loosening the faith-first prerequisite. But that edge overreaches too: Christ’s sinlessness (not an infant’s incapacity) is why repentance was absent, and John’s preparatory baptism is arguably not the Christian rite. Corroborative on each side, decisive for neither. Kin to the order-of-faith arguments)
