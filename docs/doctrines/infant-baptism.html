Infant Baptism (paedobaptism)

Should the infant children of Christians be baptized, or does baptism belong only to those old enough to profess faith for themselves? The two axes on which views divide are who is a fit subject for baptism (a professing believer only, or a household including its children) and when it should be given (as early as possible, or deferred until a person can ask for it). Baby dedication — presenting an unbaptized child for blessing — is a subset of the same question. The witnesses below run earliest first.

Timeline

Irenaeus of Lyons (c. 180)

Irenaeus never discusses the baptism of infants directly, but in a famous sentence of Against Heresies II.22.4 he says Christ came to save "all… who through Him are born again to God — infants, and children, and boys, and youths, and old men."1 Because Irenaeus elsewhere defines baptism as "the new birth unto God,"2 paedobaptist readers count the infants here among the baptized-and-reborn, which would make Irenaeus a possible earliest — if implicit — witness to infant baptism, a generation before Tertullian. The inference is contested: the vocabulary is genuinely his, but the sentence's burden is recapitulation (Christ sanctifying every age by living through it), so whether it documents the practice or asserts that the incarnation redeems infancy is disputed — weighed on its argument page.3

Tertullian (c. 203)

Tertullian is the first Christian writer to discuss the baptism of little children explicitly — and he opposes it, holding that baptism is better delayed: weighing "the circumstances and disposition, and even age" of each person, he judges "the delay of baptism… preferable; principally, however, in the case of little children."4 His reasons are the risk to the sponsors who make the promises on the child's behalf, and his sense that the "innocent period of life" need not "hasten to the remission of sins" — better to let children "become Christians when they have become able to know Christ."5 Yet his very objection is the earliest firm evidence for the practice: he argues against a custom that plainly already existed and was common enough to need answering, placing infant baptism in North Africa by c. 200.6

Hippolytus of Rome (the Apostolic Tradition, c. 215)

Where Tertullian argues against the custom, the Apostolic Tradition — the church order traditionally ascribed to Hippolytus — simply prescribes it. Setting out the order of the paschal baptism, §21 directs: "first baptize the little ones; if they can speak for themselves, they shall do so; if not, their parents or other relatives shall speak for them," and only then the men and the women.7 Taken at face value this is the earliest descriptive witness to infant baptism — infants baptized first, with sponsors answering the credal questions on their behalf — recorded not as a novelty but as the settled shape of the rite, corroborating the practice Tertullian had opposed a decade earlier. The weight of the witness is qualified, however, by real scholarly doubt about the document: its Greek original is lost, it survives mainly in a fourth-century Latin version, and much recent work treats it as a composite church order layered over generations rather than one author's work of c. 215, so the passage cannot be securely dated to a single year.8

Origen (c. 248)

Origen is the first writer to claim infant baptism as a custom the Church received from the apostles. Commenting on Romans 6:6, he asks why the Law commands a sin offering for a newborn (Leviticus 12) — how can an infant have sinned? — and answers: “the church has received a tradition from the apostles to baptize even infants,” because the apostles “knew that there are real stains of sin in everyone which must be cleansed by water and the Spirit.”9 He treats the practice as universal and unremarkable elsewhere too: baptism “is given even to infants” by “the usage of the Church” (Homilies on Leviticus 8.3), and in Homilies on Luke 14 he reasons from the custom — if little children are baptized “for the remission of sins,” the “stains of birth” must be what the rite puts aside.10 Two caveats temper the marquee sentence: “5.9” is the commentary's section, not Romans 5:9, and that commentary survives for these books only in Rufinus's admittedly abridging Latin — though the independent Greek homilies attest the practice regardless.11

Cyprian of Carthage (c. 253)

Cyprian supplies the earliest conciliar witness. A bishop named Fidus had proposed that a newborn wait until the eighth day for baptism, on the analogy of Old-Testament circumcision; Cyprian, writing for a council of sixty-six African bishops, reports that they rejected the delay unanimously — “in this course which you thought was to be taken, no one agreed; but we all rather judge that the mercy and grace of God is not to be refused to any one born of man.”12 What is telling is what the letter never disputes: not whether infants are baptized (Fidus assumes it too) but only when. The eighth-day type is answered as a shadow fulfilled in Christ, and the newborn — who “has not sinned” but has “contracted the contagion of the ancient death” from Adam — is to be baptized without delay, so that “how much rather ought we to shrink from hindering an infant.”13 That an African synod of c. 253 could treat the timing of infant baptism as the only open question — a generation after Origen and about fifty years after Tertullian argued for delay — measures how settled the practice had become.

Gregory of Nazianzus (c. 381)

Gregory, preaching On Holy Baptism at Constantinople, is a fourth-century Eastern witness that child baptism was accepted — contested, as at Carthage, only over timing. He tells parents not to fear consecrating even an infant: “let him be sanctified from his childhood; from his very tenderest age let him be consecrated by the Spirit… O what a small-souled mother, and of how little faith!”14 Facing the objection directly — “those who are still children… are we to baptize them too?” — he answers “Certainly, if any danger presses,” better “unconsciously sanctified than… unsealed and uninitiated”; and for the ordinary case (no danger) he counsels waiting “till the end of the third year, or a little more or less,” when a child can “answer something about the Sacrament.”15 The deferral is pastoral, not a denial: he never questions whether children are fit subjects, prescribes immediate baptism whenever life is at risk, and defers otherwise only so the child may grasp the rite's outlines — the Nicene and Post-Nicene Fathers editor notes that “S. Gregory did not reject infant Baptism… is clear” from just these directions.16

John Chrysostom (c. 390)

Chrysostom, preaching to catechumens at Antioch, takes infant baptism entirely for granted and turns it into an argument about the richness of the sacrament: because many suppose baptism gives nothing but the remission of sins, he points to infants as proof that it gives much more. “It is on this account that we baptize even infants, although they are sinless, that they may be given the further gifts of sanctification, justice, filial adoption, and inheritance, that they may be brothers and members of Christ, and become dwelling places for the Spirit.”17 The witness is the more telling for being incidental — the custom is a settled premise, not the thing to be proved — placing infant baptism as firmly in the fourth-century East as at Cyprian’s Carthage or Gregory’s Cappadocia. What is distinctively Eastern is his rationale: he calls infants “sinless” and pointedly leaves the remission of sins off the list of what their baptism confers, grounding the rite in the abundance of grace rather than in inherited guilt — a notable contrast with the Western original-sin framing of Cyprian’s “contagion of the ancient death” and, soon after, Augustine.18

Augustine of Hippo (c. 412)

Augustine is the practice’s weightiest ancient witness and its great theological systematizer. Against the Donatists he treats infant baptism as the settled custom of the whole Church — “the firm tradition of the universal Church, in respect of the baptism of infants” — and lays down the rule that “what is held by the whole Church, and that not as instituted by Councils, but as a matter of invariable custom, is rightly held to have been handed down by apostolical authority.”19 Against the Pelagians he makes that universal practice the decisive argument for original sin: the Church everywhere baptizes infants for the remission of sins, yet infants commit no actual sins of their own, so — “inasmuch as infants are not held bound by any sins of their own actual life, it is the guilt of original sin which is healed in them by the grace of Him who saves them by the laver of regeneration.”20 In Letter 98 to Boniface he explains how a speechless infant is nonetheless truly baptized — a believer “through the sacrament of that faith,” his sponsors answering for him, delivered “from that condemnation which by one man entered into the world.”21 With Augustine the two lines the earlier witnesses had traced — the antiquity/universality of the custom and its link to inherited sin (already audible in Cyprian’s “contagion of the ancient death”) — are welded into the mature Western case, and the practice, never itself in question, becomes the immovable premise from which a whole doctrine is argued. The rationale is pointedly Western: where the Eastern Chrysostom called infants “sinless” and grounded their baptism in grace’s abundance, Augustine grounds it in guilt to be washed away.

The Schleitheim Confession (Michael Sattler & the Swiss Brethren, 1527)

The Schleitheim Confession is the first witness here to reject infant baptism outright — the point where the long-settled question of when to baptize a child becomes a question of whether a child is a subject of baptism at all. Its opening article restricts baptism to those “who have learned repentance and amendment of life, and who believe truly that their sins are taken away by Christ… and who with this significance request it… and demand it for themselves. This excludes all infant baptism, the highest and chief abomination of the pope.”22 By making repentance, personal faith, and a conscious request — none of them possible for a newborn — constitutive of a valid baptism, the article denies that an infant can be baptized in any true sense, appealing to the apostolic pattern of preaching-then-belief (Matthew 28, Mark 16, Acts 2, 8, 16, 19).23 Adopted by a gathering of Swiss Brethren at Schleitheim on 24 February 1527 and now credited chiefly to the ex-Benedictine prior Michael Sattler — burned at the stake three months later — it is the foundational confessional statement of the credobaptist (believers’-baptism) position, breaking not only with Rome but with the magisterial Reformers Luther, Zwingli, and Calvin, who all retained the paedobaptist practice the patristic witnesses above had handed down.24

Martin Luther (the Large Catechism, 1529)

Two years after Schleitheim, Luther answers the Anabaptist objection directly in the fourth part of the Large Catechism, defending the paedobaptism the patristic witnesses above had handed down. He argues first from its fruits: “that the Baptism of infants is pleasing to Christ is sufficiently proved from His own work, namely, that God sanctifies many of them who have been thus baptized, and has given them the Holy Ghost” — naming Bernard, Gerson, and Hus as men of undeniable sanctity whose only baptism was in infancy, for God “can never be opposed to Himself… or for its promotion impart His grace and Spirit” to a rite He rejected.25 But his decisive move relocates the ground of a valid baptism away from the recipient’s faith and onto God’s Word: “everything depends upon the Word and command of God… Baptism is valid, even though faith be wanting. For my faith does not make Baptism, but receives it.”26 Where Schleitheim had made repentance, faith, and a personal request constitutive of baptism — and so excluded the newborn — Luther makes the Word constitutive and faith merely receptive, dissolving at its root the objection that a faithless infant cannot be baptized. The rationale is neither the Western original-sin argument of Augustine nor the Eastern “abundance of grace” of Chrysostom, but a sacramental one about what makes a baptism — the distinctively magisterial-Reformation defense of the practice.
