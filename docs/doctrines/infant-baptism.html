Infant Baptism (paedobaptism)

Should the infant children of Christians be baptized, or does baptism belong only to those old enough to profess faith for themselves? The two axes on which views divide are who is a fit subject for baptism (a professing believer only, or a household including its children) and when it should be given (as early as possible, or deferred until a person can ask for it). Baby dedication — presenting an unbaptized child for blessing — is a subset of the same question. The witnesses below run earliest first.

Timeline

Tertullian (c. 203)

Tertullian is the first Christian writer to discuss the baptism of little children explicitly — and he opposes it, holding that baptism is better delayed: weighing "the circumstances and disposition, and even age" of each person, he judges "the delay of baptism… preferable; principally, however, in the case of little children."1 His reasons are the risk to the sponsors who make the promises on the child's behalf, and his sense that the "innocent period of life" need not "hasten to the remission of sins" — better to let children "become Christians when they have become able to know Christ."2 Yet his very objection is the earliest firm evidence for the practice: he argues against a custom that plainly already existed and was common enough to need answering, placing infant baptism in North Africa by c. 200.3
