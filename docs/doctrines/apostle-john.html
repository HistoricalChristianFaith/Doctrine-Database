John, son of Zebedee

John the son of Zebedee — the younger brother of James the Great, a Galilean fisherman, and, with Peter and his brother, one of the three disciples Jesus kept nearest him — is remembered by the church as “the disciple whom Jesus loved,” the apostle entrusted with the mother of Jesus at the cross, the exile of Patmos, and the last of the Twelve to die, an old man at Ephesus under the emperor Trajan. Around that memory grew a vast tradition: the evangelist and seer to whom a Gospel, three epistles, and the Revelation were ascribed; the miracle-worker of legend who drank poison unharmed; the confessor plunged into boiling oil at Rome and delivered. This page is not so much a life of John as a history of what was remembered and claimed about him, set out earliest source first, so that the New Testament notices, the Asian tradition of his old age, the legends of his deliverances, and the vexed questions of authorship and death can each be weighed on its own date. This is a biographical tradition-timeline: its “witnesses” are the datable sources that report on John, and the contested questions — did he die peacefully at a great age or was he martyred early with his brother? was Papias's “John the elder” a second John? — are weighed on their own argument pages below rather than settled by assertion. For the shared ladder of sources and how far to trust each rung, see the apostles hub.

Timeline

The New Testament (c. 30–95)

The New Testament gives John a fuller portrait than most of the Twelve. He is a fisherman of Galilee, son of Zebedee and younger brother of James, called with his brother from the nets and surnamed with him a “son of thunder”; with Peter and James he forms the inner three admitted to the raising of Jairus's daughter, the Transfiguration, and Gethsemane. The Fourth Gospel never names him, but calls one figure “the disciple whom Jesus loved,” who leans on Jesus's breast at the supper, stands by the cross where Jesus commits his mother to him, outruns Peter to the empty tomb, and is the one whose testimony the Gospel claims to rest on. In Acts he is Peter's constant companion — at the Beautiful Gate, before the Sanhedrin, and sent down to Samaria — and Paul names him with Peter and James a “pillar” of the Jerusalem church. Revelation opens with its author, “John,” exiled “in the isle that is called Patmos, for the word of God.”1

Papias of Hierapolis, preserved by Eusebius (c. 100–130)

Papias, bishop of Hierapolis and a hearer of the elders of Asia, supplies the oldest post-apostolic memory of John — and the first tangle. In the one surviving sentence of his preface, he twice names a John: “what … John … said” among the departed apostles, and then “what things Aristion and the presbyter John, the disciples of the Lord,” are still saying — wording later read (by Eusebius, and by many moderns) as evidence of two Johns at Ephesus, an apostle and an “elder.” A separate, much later notice (the ninth-century chronicler George Hamartolus) claims Papias's second book reported that John, like his brother James, “was killed by the Jews” — the seed of the minority thesis that John was martyred early. Both threads run back to Papias and are weighed in the arguments below.2

Irenaeus of Lyons (c. 180)

Irenaeus, who had heard Polycarp of Smyrna — himself a hearer of John — gives the firmest early tradition: John “the disciple of the Lord, who also had leaned upon His breast, did himself publish a Gospel during his residence at Ephesus in Asia,” and “remained among them up to the times of Trajan.” He also preserves, on Polycarp's authority, the story of John fleeing the bath-house at Ephesus rather than share it with the heretic Cerinthus. Irenaeus is the anchor of the whole Ephesus tradition: John lived to a great age in Asia and died there under Trajan (98–117).3

Clement of Alexandria, preserved by Eusebius (c. 200)

Clement independently attests the same Ephesian old age. In the story of the young robber — “a tale, which is not a mere tale” — he has John return “after the tyrant's death” from Patmos to Ephesus and there travel the surrounding country, appointing bishops and, in one town, pursuing and reclaiming a young convert who had become a brigand. Clement names no emperor, but attests the exile-and-return and the aged John governing the Asian churches, and elsewhere notes that John, called back to episcopal work, was still vigorous in extreme old age.4

Tertullian (c. 200)

Tertullian, arguing that the great churches keep the apostles' own teaching, appeals to Rome, “where the Apostle John was first plunged, unhurt, into boiling oil, and thence remitted to his island-exile.” It is the earliest witness to the Roman boiling-oil legend (later localized at the Latin Gate) and, in the same breath, to the Patmos exile — told as an ordeal survived, in the manner of the martyr-deliverance stories.5

The Acts of John (c. 150–200)

The apocryphal Acts of John — part of the “Leucian” romance-corpus, and marked by encratite and docetic tendencies — supplies the most famous scenes: John raising the dead (Drusiana), wrecking the temple of Artemis at Ephesus by a word, and at the last his own peaceful “falling asleep” in a grave he digs himself. (The poisoned-cup ordeal, often pictured with John, is not in this work — it is attached to him only by the later apostle-lists and the Golden Legend.) It is romance-hagiography of low biographical value, condemned in antiquity for its heresy, and is cited here only as an early witness to how John was venerated — never as history. On the reliability of this whole layer, see the apostles hub.6

Victorinus of Pettau (c. 280)

Victorinus, in the earliest surviving commentary on Revelation, states plainly that John saw the Apocalypse while “condemned to the labour of the mines by Cæsar Domitian” on Patmos, and published it “when grown old” after his release — the first witness to attach the vision itself, by name, to Domitian and to date John's old age accordingly. His testimony is treated at length on the companion timeline for the date of Revelation (where he appears as a witness in his own right); it matters here because the Domitianic exile is one of the load-bearing supports of the old-age-at-Ephesus tradition.7

Eusebius of Caesarea (c. 325)

Eusebius gathers the tradition into a single connected account: John banished to Patmos under Domitian, released under Nerva, and living on at Ephesus until Trajan; the governor of the Asian churches; the seer of Revelation (whose authorship, however, Eusebius is willing to assign to the “other” John). He quotes Irenaeus and Clement, and preserves Polycrates of Ephesus (c. 190), who lists among Asia's buried luminaries “John, who was both a witness and a teacher, who reclined upon the bosom of the Lord … He fell asleep at Ephesus.” And it is Eusebius who, reading Papias, first argues for two Johns and “two tombs in Ephesus, each … called John's.”8

Jerome (393)

Jerome, in On Illustrious Men, condenses the Greek tradition for the Latin West: John wrote his Gospel last, at the bishops' request, against Cerinthus and the Ebionites; “in the fourteenth year … after Nero, Domitian having raised a second persecution he was banished to the island of Patmos, and wrote the Apocalypse”; released after Domitian's death, he “returned to Ephesus,” and, “continuing there until the time of the emperor Trajan, founded and built churches throughout all Asia, and, worn out by old age, died in the sixty-eighth year after our Lord's passion and was buried near the same city.”9

The Golden Legend (c. 1260)

Jacobus de Voragine's Golden Legend gathers the medieval life: John summoned to Rome and cast into a cauldron of boiling oil before the Latin Gate, from which he came out unhurt; his exile to Patmos and the writing of the Apocalypse; the poisoned-cup challenge before the temple of Diana (Aristodemus's ordeal), which he drinks unharmed; the raising of the dead; and his tranquil death in a grave dug at his word, his body afterward not found. It is cited here as late medieval hagiography, more than a millennium after the events, gathering the boiling oil, the poisoned cup, and the peaceful death into a single story.10

Arguments

Related arguments — the end of John

How did the last apostle die? The mainstream tradition — multiply attested across the second-century Asian church — has John living to a great age and dying peacefully at Ephesus under Trajan; a persistent minority thesis, resting on a Papias fragment and on Jesus's word about “the cup,” has him martyred early alongside his brother James. Each rival reading is weighed on its own page.

Did John die of old age at Ephesus? (assessment: sound)

John's old age and peaceful death at Ephesus is one of the best-attested biographical traditions of any apostle: Irenaeus (on Polycarp's authority), Polycrates of Ephesus, Clement of Alexandria, and the whole later tradition independently place John living into the reign of Trajan and dying at Ephesus, where two churches later claimed his tomb. Sound — early, multiple, and mutually independent attestation from within the very region concerned.11

Was John martyred early, with his brother James? (assessment: weak)

The early-martyrdom thesis steelmans the minority case: Jesus's promise that both sons of Zebedee would “drink of the cup” (Mark 10:39), and a report that Papias's second book said John “was killed by the Jews.” But the proof-text does not require simultaneous deaths, the Papias notice survives only in late, self-contradictory epitomes, and the thesis must overturn the strong, independent Ephesus tradition to stand. Weak — it does not hold on its best available case.12

Was Papias's “John the elder” a second John? (assessment: contested)

The “two Johns” question turns on the one surviving sentence of Papias, which seems to name a John twice, and on Eusebius's inference from it — backed by the “two tombs” at Ephesus — that an apostle John and a distinct “John the elder” both lived there. It bears directly on authorship: whether one man wrote the Gospel, the epistles, and Revelation, or these belong to two hands. The evidence genuinely divides scholars, with no clear winner. Contested.13
