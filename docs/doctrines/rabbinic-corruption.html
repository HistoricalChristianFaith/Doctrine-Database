The Rabbinic Corruption of Scripture (Septuagint vs. Masoretic Text)

From the second century on, Christian writers noticed that their Greek Old Testament — the Septuagint (LXX), the translation of "the seventy" — diverged from the Hebrew text the synagogue used, and that the divergences fell, suspiciously often, on the very passages adduced to prove Jesus the Christ: Isaiah 7:14 ("virgin" vs. "young woman"), Psalm 96:10 ("the Lord reigned from the wood"), the chronology of Genesis 5 and 11. Many of them drew the sharpest possible conclusion — that post-Second-Temple rabbinic Judaism (the proto-Masoretic tradition, later associated with Aquila and the Masoretes) had deliberately altered or cut the Hebrew text to deny Christ, while the older, pre-Christian LXX preserved the true reading. This page gathers the witnesses to that charge — earliest first — alongside the dissenting and the modern voices. The doctrine is specifically about alleged rabbinic tampering after AD 70, not textual transmission in general; the cruxes weigh the malice thesis against the modern text-critical finding (Emanuel Tov; the Dead Sea Scrolls) that the LXX faithfully renders a different Hebrew Vorlage — divergent text traditions, not corruption. The charge presupposes a companion doctrine — the Origin and Inspiration of the Septuagint, the tradition that the LXX was a divinely-guided, providentially-guarded translation — for only an inspired and authoritative Greek text makes a divergent Hebrew one look like tampering; that legend, and its growth across time, is traced there.

Timeline

The chronological witnesses are being added, earliest first. The governing theses they bear on — whether the divergences are deliberate corruption, and the status of the Septuagint legend, the Aquila revision, and the Genesis chronology — will be weighed below under Arguments & cruxes as the cruxes are worked.

Justin Martyr (c. 165)

Justin Martyr, debating the Jew Trypho, gives the earliest and fullest form of the charge: the Jewish teachers both disparage the Septuagint of "your seventy elders that were with Ptolemy" as "untrue in certain respects" and "have altogether taken away many Scriptures" from it — precisely those "by which this very man who was crucified is proved to have been set forth expressly as God, and man, and as being crucified, and as dying."1 He names particular excisions — a Jeremiah logion ("Come, let us lay on wood on His bread"), a one-line descent oracle, and the passover saying of Esdras — and insists the cutting is recent and incomplete, "still written in some copies… in the synagogues of the Jews (for it is only a short time since they were cut out)."2 His most-cited single example is the deletion of the words "from the wood" from Psalm 96:10 — to which even Trypho answers that the erasure "seems incredible."3 The charge is one of will as much as deed: had the teachers grasped the force of the texts they still admit, "be well assured they would have deleted them," and where they cannot delete they alter the reading (the Septuagint's "take away the righteous" against their "bind the righteous").4

Tractate Megillah (c. 200)

The Jewish witness from the other side of the dispute. The rabbinic tradition in b. Megillah 9a candidly admits the Greek diverges from the Hebrew, but credits the divergence to the seventy-two translators: shut in separate chambers for Ptolemy and translating "from memory," "the Holy One, blessed be He, sent into the heart of each of them a counsel, and they all agreed to have one mind, and changed" about fifteen specific passages.5 The listed changes are anti-anthropomorphic, anti-polytheistic, and court-diplomatic — "God created the world in the beginning" (against a separate creator), "I will make a man in an image" (singular, against "let us make"), and "the beast that has small feet" for the hare of Lev 11:6, because "Ptolemy's wife was named" Arnebeth — and not one is a christological proof-text.6 It cuts the opposite way from Justin's charge: the alteration is located in the Greek and sanctioned by God, not malicious tampering with the Hebrew — divergence by providential editing. The parallel to the Letter of Aristeas legend, and the seed of the Septuagint-legend crux below.

Eusebius of Caesarea (c. 325)

Eusebius of Caesarea, in Book 1 of his Chronicon, carries the charge from the messianic proof-texts to the chronology. Setting the Septuagint, Hebrew, and Samaritan reckonings of Genesis 5–11 side by side, he finds the Greek and the Hebrew agreeing in each patriarch's total lifespan but disagreeing — by a hundred years apiece — in the begetting-ages before the flood, so that the period from Adam to the death of Noah comes to 2,242 years in the Septuagint against 1,656 in the Hebrew, a 586-year gap.7 Because the begetting-age and the years-lived-after sum to the same total in both versions, and because the Hebrew matches the Greek precisely at Jared, Methuselah, and Lamech, he reasons that the Septuagint preserves the original and "is the best."8 The shorter Hebrew figures he reads as a deliberate abridgement — "we suspect that this was something which the Jews did. They made bold to shorten the time before the fathering of children to encourage early marriages" — the patristic root of the chronology strand of the corruption charge, and the seed of the Genesis-5/11 crux below.9

Epiphanius of Salamis (c. 392)

Epiphanius of Salamis, in his Treatise on Weights and Measures, narrows the charge from anonymous "teachers" to a single named agent: Aquila, whose hyper-literal second-century Greek revision the synagogue preferred to the Septuagint. Epiphanius tells it as a tale of apostasy — a baptized Christian expelled from the church for clinging to astrology, who turned Jew "embittered in mind… puffed up with vain jealousy" and learned Hebrew to make his version.10 That version, he charges, was made "not by the right motive, but (by the desire) so to distort certain of the words occurring in the translation of the seventy-two that he might proclaim the things testified to about Christ in the divine Scriptures to be fulfilled in some other way" — the divergence read as deliberate and aimed at the christological proof-texts, and the version pronounced "incorrect and perversely translated."11 He thus supplies the patristic person behind the corruption thesis and the chief witness for the Aquila-revision crux below — though the same treatise elsewhere uses Aquila to recover Hebrew words the Seventy had omitted, conceding the literalism it elsewhere calls malice.

Jerome (c. 400)

The dissenting voice. Jerome, abandoning the Septuagint as his base and translating the Old Testament afresh "from the Hebrew words," reverses the polarity of the charge: he appeals throughout his Vulgate prefaces to the Hebraica veritas — the "truth of the Hebrew" — ranks the versions Hebrew over Greek over Latin (the exact inverse of the corruption thesis), and makes the synagogue's text the court of last appeal, telling the doubter to "ask any one of the Hebrews, in whom you might place better faith" and protesting that he is "not at all aware of anything of the Hebrew to have been changed by me."12 He cuts away the legend that props the Greek's authority — the seventy translators shut in separate cells — as "lying," unattested before late report, and insists the Seventy "translated those things [they] understood" rather than prophesied, "for it is one thing to be a seer, another to be an interpreter."13 The word "corrupted" he turns against the Greek, but means its splintering into rival recensions in transmission ("this genuine and ancient translation is corrupted and violated"), the remedy for which is to go behind it to the Hebrew.14 He is no clean exoneration, though: he allows the Jews may have "afterward removed the testimonies used by the Apostles against them," and relays their own account of the Greek's softening of the Trinitarian texts for Ptolemy — but he locates such alteration in the translators' discretion, not in a rabbinic mutilation of the Hebrew, and his settled instinct is to trust the text the others accuse. He bears most directly on the Septuagint-legend crux below, and stands as the timeline's counterweight to the malice thesis.

Philoxenus of Mabbug (c. 523)

Philoxenus of Mabbug, the miaphysite bishop who commissioned the Philoxenian Syriac Bible, supplies the chronological argument beneath the corruption charge — though only secondhand, through Moses bar Kepha's later survey of the Greek versions. He is reported to rate the Septuagint best because it is the text "Our Lord and his disciples quote in the New Testament," and because, being made "long before the appearance of Our Lord," it gave the Jews "neither reason nor pretext to be moved by envy (to corrupt the text); but in the other translations the relevant passages were corrupted through the envy of the Jews."15 The reasoning is a dating filter: only a version made after Christ could have been knowingly bent against him, so the pre-Christian LXX is innocent by construction and the later revisions (Aquila and the rest) carry the charge — the same target as Epiphanius, now furnished with a rationale. ⚠ The attribution is unverified: it rests on bar Kepha as quoted by the Wassersteins, no direct Philoxenus text for it having been located.

Isidore of Seville (c. 636)

Isidore of Seville, in the Bible-book of his Etymologies (Book VI), carries the legend into the Latin West in its strongest form — but levels no charge. He relays the inspiration miracle at full strength: the seventy, sealed "each apart in his own chamber," rendered the text "through the Holy Spirit… in such a way that nothing was found in the manuscript of any of them that was different from the rest, even in the order of words" — the inspired, word-perfect Greek that is the very premise the corruption charge needs.16 Yet he never draws the conclusion. He lists the later versions (Aquila, Symmachus, Theodotion) flatly, as items in a bibliography with no hint of malice; he names one textual "corruption" and assigns it to the gentiles (healed by Ezra, "inspired with the divine spirit"); and he ranks Jerome's Hebrew-based Vulgate "deservedly preferred over the others… truer."17 He is the witness who held every ingredient of the thesis and inferred none of it — a measure of how much of the corruption charge is later argument rather than received tradition. His fuller role, as a witness to the inspiration legend itself, belongs to the companion doctrine, the Origin and Inspiration of the Septuagint.

Julian of Toledo (c. 686)

Julian of Toledo, archbishop of the Visigothic capital, takes the chronology strand born in Eusebius and builds a whole anti-Jewish treatise on it — the De comprobatione aetatis sextae, written c. 686 against Jews who reckoned from the shorter Hebrew totals that the world had not yet reached the sixth age in which Messiah comes. His program, stated in the preface, is the West's crispest version of the rule: "because of this difference of years which is found between the Hebrew and our codices, it is not the books of the Hebrew translation, but the codices of the Seventy translators, that are to be followed" — for the Seventy "translated… by the office of prophesying rather than of translating, the Lord revealing it to them."18 He brands the shorter Hebrew reckoning the "falsity of the Jews" (falsitas Judaeorum) — though his primary argument actually sidesteps the year-count, holding that the ages of the world are fixed by a "prefixed limit of generations," not by summed years, so the Hebrew totals prove nothing either way.19 The explicit tampering-motive on his pages he quotes from Augustine — the Jews "have changed certain things in their own codices, that our authority might be diminished" — but he flags it as merely reported opinion, and the Augustine and Jerome he marshals hedge where he does not: Augustine twice refuses to call the divergence a falsification and reads the extra hundred years as added on the Greek side, while Jerome defers to the Greek on this one chronological point alone. Julian supplies the polemical heat more than a new mechanism, and carries the charge into the seventh-century Latin West.20

Jacob of Edessa (c. 692)

Jacob of Edessa, the foremost Syriac scholar of his age and reviser of the Syriac Old Testament, carries the chronology strand born in Eusebius into the Syriac East and states its motive more nakedly than any witness before Agapius. In a scholion on Genesis he charges that the Hebrews, "wanting to pervert the computation of years, in order to show that Christ had not yet arrived," cut a hundred years from each antediluvian patriarch before his son was born — the bare textual observation is the Eusebian one (Adam begets Seth at 130 in the Hebrew, 230 in the Greek), but where Eusebius offered only a hesitant guess at the reason, Jacob fixes the purpose as the anti-messianic chronology Julian and Agapius would press.21 He adds that he had found the longer figure (230) even "in some sufficiently accurate Hebrew histories," an early form of the modern deflation camp's appeal to external witnesses for a long Hebrew chronology.22 The charge reaches us, though, only through a Genesis scholion transmitted by Assemani and translated by Adler, and under a documented attribution tangle — long filed under Ephraem the Syrian before modern scholarship reassigned it to Jacob — so its exact wording is cited secondhand and flagged accordingly on his page.

George Syncellus (c. 810)

George Syncellus, the Byzantine monk and chronographer, makes the Eusebian chronology charge the backbone of a great Byzantine universal history, the Ecloga Chronographica. Setting the Septuagint, the Jewish Hebrew, and the Samaritan reckonings of Genesis 5–11 side by side after Eusebius — the tables headed "From Eusebios" — he endorses the verdict as his settled conclusion: "there is no choice but to acknowledge that… the text of scripture used by the Jews has erred in its chronology from Adam to Abraham."23 "The Hebrew text used by the Jews is wrong," he adds; the Septuagint was made from "an ancient and a strictly accurate Hebrew text," and "it is this version that we too have used in our own chronography," the text "the Church of Christ… is devoted to."24 His charge, in the surviving critical text, is one of error — that the Jewish numbers are mistaken and the Greek original — rather than, explicitly, of deliberate falsification (the inflammatory "criminal act of the Jews" line later fathered on him cannot be found there); he sits, on the chronology strand, between the bare tabulators and the conspiracy-narrators, and carries it from the Latin West into the Greek East.

Hrabanus Maurus (c. 845)

Hrabanus Maurus, abbot of Fulda and archbishop of Mainz, carries the legend a step further west and a step later in time, copying it almost verbatim from Isidore into the Carolingian schools' standard encyclopedia, the De rerum naturis (De Universo). In Book V's chapter "On the library" he reproduces the inspiration miracle at full strength — the seventy, "separated each apart in his own cell… translated everything through the Holy Spirit in such a way that nothing was found in the manuscript of any one of them that differed from the rest, even in the order of the words" — the very premise the corruption charge needs.25 Like Isidore he draws none of the accusation from it: the later versions (Aquila, Symmachus, Theodotion) are listed flatly as a bibliography, the one "corruption" he names is by the gentiles (healed by Ezra), and Jerome's Hebrew-based Vulgate is "deservedly preferred" to all the Greek — the inverse of the corruption thesis.26 His one editorial touch even cuts against the legend: where Isidore states the separate-cells miracle as bare fact, Hrabanus inserts "as some assert" (ut quidam asserunt). He is the witness who held every ingredient of the thesis and inferred none of it — a Carolingian measure of how much of the charge is later argument; his fuller role belongs to the companion doctrine, the Origin and Inspiration of the Septuagint.

Moses bar Kepha (c. 903)

Moses bar Kepha, the ninth-century Syriac Orthodox bishop, is the channel through which the Philoxenus argument survives, and a witness in his own right. In a survey listing the ancient translations of the Bible into Greek he reports a version of the Septuagint legend (the seventy-two in separate chambers) and then, in a note of his own, ranks the Septuagint best of the versions — first on his own ground, "as is proven by the fact that it is this that Our Lord and his disciples quote in the New Testament," and then "according to Philoxenus of Mabbug," whose dating argument he quotes: the LXX "was made long before the appearance of Our Lord," so the Jews "had neither reason nor pretext to be moved by envy… but in the other translations the relevant passages were corrupted through the envy of the Jews."27 He delivers the verdict comparatively, as the top of a ranked field that also held Aquila and the other post-Christian revisions on which the charge falls — so the corruption thesis reaches the medieval Syriac church already systematized: legend, ranking, and rationale bound together. ⚠ Cited secondhand, via the Wassersteins; no accessible edition of bar Kepha's own survey has been located.

Agapius of Hierapolis (c. 942)

Agapius of Hierapolis, the Melkite bishop of Manbij — the old Mabbug, see of Philoxenus — gives the charge its fullest narrative form in the earliest Christian world history written in Arabic, the Kitāb al-ʿUnwān. There the two named high priests of the Gospel, Annas and Caiaphas, frightened after the Resurrection that elders of their own people had owned the Messiah, "hid the translation of the Torah, translated by the Seventy commentators… which were translated under King Ptolemy Philadelphus in the town of Alexandria" and "changed and mutilated in the Books of the Prophets all that they could, of what referred to the prophecies on the Messiah" — while the same passages "in the books of the Seventy, are, on the contrary, faithful and clear."28 The same conspirators, "having the libraries in their possession," he says, "cut off from the history from the world, from Adam to the time of Abraham, 1389 years," so that the period from Adam to the flood stands at 2,256 years in the Septuagint against 1,656 in "the Torah mutilated and reduced by the Jews" — the deletion made to argue that the Messiah "would come only in the last days… we are still in the middle of the days of the world."29 Agapius thus fuses both strands of the charge — the doctored messianic proof-texts and the shortened Genesis chronology — into one post-Resurrection plot with a named cast, a motive, and a method (he even narrates the excision of the "second Kainan," caught when the disciples demanded of his erased years, "where did you put them?"); the pre-Christian Greek, finished "approximately 300 years before the Advent of Christ," he holds innocent by the same dating filter reported of Philoxenus.30

Bar Hebraeus (c. 1286)

Bar Hebraeus, the Syriac Orthodox maphrian and polymath, stands at the far end of the chronology strand as a negative witness. His great Chronography follows the Septuagint's longer reckoning of Genesis 5–11 and flatly tabulates the divergent "Syrian and Hebrew" totals beside it — yet levels no charge of deliberate Jewish tampering.31 The modern deflation camp (Henry B. Smith Jr.) repeatedly lists him among the chronographers who held the rabbis "deliberately deflated" the chronology, but the listing does not survive inspection: Smith quotes no words of Bar Hebraeus, mislabels the Syriac maphrian an "Armenian annalist," and the one note in the Chronography explaining the divergences is the modern editor Bedjan's — which ascribes them to which version the author followed, not to corruption.32 Like Isidore and Hrabanus on the inspiration legend, he held every material of the charge and inferred none of it — the late-medieval measure of how thin the chronology strand's actual textual base is, and a corrective to the witness-list built on it.

Arguments & cruxes

Sub-claims marshaled for (or against) the corruption charge — which cut across the timeline rather than sitting at one date — are weighed adversarially here, each on its own page with an assessment of the interpretation it serves. The master corruption charge, the Septuagint-legend crux (shared with the Origin and Inspiration of the Septuagint), the Aquila-revision crux, and the reverse-charge crux (the mirror question — did the Jews accuse the Christians?) are grouped below as the "Related cruxes — the corruption charge" complex. The Genesis 5/11 chronology crux and the "second Cainan" crux, resting together on a separate text-critical problem, form the "Related cruxes — the Genesis chronology" complex after.

Related cruxes — the corruption charge

The cluster of sub-claims that bear directly on the master thesis — that the divergence between the older Greek and the rabbinic Hebrew is deliberate, malicious tampering with the Hebrew. The master charge is weighed against the modern text-critical account; the Septuagint-legend crux weighs the rival explanation the Jewish tradition itself gives (that the divergences are the translators' own sanctioned changes); the Aquila-revision crux takes the charge in its narrowest, best-documented form — a single named translator — and weighs distortion against translation method; and the reverse-charge crux holds the charge up to its mirror, asking whether the Jews brought the same accusation back against the Christians (they mostly did not, and not in the same key).

Did the Jews deliberately corrupt the Hebrew Old Testament text? (assessment: weak)

The master charge: deliberate rabbinic corruption — the single thesis the whole chorus of witnesses bears, that post-Second-Temple rabbinic Judaism maliciously altered the Hebrew text (the messianic proof-texts and the Genesis chronology) to deny Christ, while the older Septuagint preserved the true reading. The interpretation is weak. What holds is the observation underneath it: the Septuagint and the rabbinic Hebrew really do diverge, systematically and on the disputed passages. What fails is the inference. The Dead Sea Scrolls — Hebrew manuscripts sealed before AD 70 — already show the divergent text the Septuagint translates (4QSamuela siding with the Greek against the Masoretic Text; Jeremiah in a short and a long Hebrew edition), so the difference cannot be a post-Resurrection plot, and the now-mainstream account (Emanuel Tov) is that the Septuagint faithfully renders a different Hebrew Vorlage — divergent traditions, not corruption. On the charge's flagship example the direction even reverses (Psalm 96:10's "from the wood" is a Christian addition to the Greek, not a Jewish deletion from the Hebrew); its Isaiah 7:14 example confuses a translation choice ('almah → parthenos) with a textual one; and its "excised" messianic logia were never canonical Scripture. Only the Genesis 5/11 chronology rests on a genuinely open text-critical problem — and even there the evidence points more to Septuagint inflation than to Hebrew deletion, with the malicious motive pure conjecture.33

The Septuagint legend: were the seventy-two divinely moved to change the text? (assessment: plausible)

The Septuagint-legend crux — the premise the whole charge depends on, weighed from the other side. The Jewish tradition (the Tractate Megillah list, paralleled in the Letter of Aristeas frame) held that the seventy-two translators were divinely moved to change about fifteen specific passages for Ptolemy. The interpretation is plausible — at its kernel, not as the inspired history it is told as. A real subset of the listed "changes" are genuine, identifiable Septuagint translation choices that diverge from the proto-Masoretic Hebrew (Genesis 2:2's "sixth day"; the euphemism for the hare at Leviticus 11:6; the anti-anthropomorphic touches), so the tradition preserves a true memory that the divergence is the translators' deliberate work, not corruption of the Hebrew — the sound counter-witness the timeline leans on. But it is not sober history: the inspiration-miracle frame (separate cells, divinely-prompted unanimity) is a demonstrable later accretion, absent from the earliest source (Aristeas) and grown over centuries (the growth traced on the Origin and Inspiration of the Septuagint); the list is a fluid midrashic tradition whose count and contents drift across the rabbinic parallels; and several of its items (Genesis 1:1, 1:26, 11:7) describe an apologetic change the extant Greek never received. Tellingly, not one of the changes is a christological proof-text — so the legend cuts against the corruption charge it is enlisted to support.34

Aquila's revision: anti-Christian distortion, or literalizing toward the Hebrew? (assessment: weak)

The Aquila-revision crux — the master charge in its narrowest, best-documented form: not anonymous "teachers" mutilating the Hebrew, but a single named translator, Aquila (c. 130), whose Greek revision the synagogue preferred to the Septuagint. Epiphanius charges that he was "moved not by the right motive, but… so to distort certain of the words… that he might proclaim the things testified to about Christ… to be fulfilled in some other way." The interpretation that charge serves is weak. What holds is the kernel: Aquila's revision did move the Greek toward the proto-Masoretic Hebrew and away from the readings the Church used, in the anti-Christian milieu of Rabbi Akiba (whose disciple he was remembered to be), and his literal readings were serviceable to the synagogue's side of the proof-text dispute. What fails is the leap to "distortion." His divergences are the uniform output of a thoroughgoing literalizing method — particle for particle (the untranslatable 'et by syn), etymology for etymology — applied to every book of the Greek Bible, contested verses and uncontested alike; the method is older than Aquila and broader than the polemic (Barthélemy's first-century kaige revision toward the Hebrew, under the Palestinian rabbinate); his single most-cited "anti-Christian" reading, neanis ("young woman") at Isaiah 7:14, is the more accurate rendering of the unchanged Hebrew 'almah that the Septuagint glossed parthenos; and the Church's own scholars (Origen in the Hexapla, Jerome, even Epiphanius) used Aquila precisely for his fidelity to the Hebrew. The distortion reading is weak, the legitimate-literalizing reading sound; the honest residue is only that a defensible literal version, made in a hostile milieu, was doubtless preferred for readings that cut against the Christian proof — a use, not the genesis, of the version.35

The reverse charge: did the Jews accuse the Christians of corrupting Scripture? (assessment: weak)

The reverse-charge crux — the master charge held up to its mirror. If the Christians accused the Jews of mutilating the text, did the Jews return the charge against the Christians? As a symmetric accusation of deliberate textual tampering the interpretation is weak: the tampering charge ran overwhelmingly one way (Christian against Jew, with its own fixed vocabulary of excision), and the Jewish side answered the diverging texts in two other registers instead — by the Septuagint legend, which credits the divergences to the translators' own sanctioned work rather than to anyone's malice, and, far more substantially, by a charge of Christian misinterpretation and mistranslation (voiced already by Trypho over Isaiah 7:14's "young woman," and systematized in the medieval polemical anthologies), not mutilation of the shared Hebrew. Where antiquity's Jews branded Christian books as falsehood, the target was the gospels (the Tannaitic gilyonim ruling), not the Old Testament. A genuine counter-charge of Christian textual falsification does surface, but late — the high-medieval reversal Irven Resnick documents — not as an ancient mirror. The honest twist is that at its one clearest textual point the reverse charge is right: Psalm 96:10's "from the wood," the Christian side's flagship example of a Jewish deletion, is in fact a Christian addition — so the rarer, quieter reverse charge is better grounded than the loud one it answers.36

Related cruxes — the Genesis chronology

The two sub-claims that turn on the divergent numbers of the Genesis 5 and 11 genealogies — a separate text-critical problem from the messianic proof-texts above. The chronology crux weighs the whole divergence (was the Hebrew shortened or the Greek inflated, and was it tampering?); the "second Cainan" crux isolates the one name the charge specifies, the extra Kainan of Genesis 11, and asks whether his absence from the Hebrew is a Jewish deletion or a Greek-side addition.

Were the Genesis 5/11 patriarchal lifespans shortened, or inflated — and was it tampering? (assessment: contested)

The Genesis 5/11 chronology crux — the one strand of the master charge that rests on a real and still-unsettled text-critical problem. Eusebius, Julian of Toledo, and Agapius held that the Jews deliberately shortened the antediluvian begetting-ages of Genesis 5 and 11 (the Hebrew gives 586 fewer years from Adam to the flood than the Septuagint) to push the Messiah's appointed time into the future. The interpretation is contested. The divergence is real and too patterned to be accidental — across the three witnesses the begetting-ages differ by a clean hundred years apiece, totals running from ~2,008 years (Masoretic) to ~2,249 (Samaritan) to ~3,394 (Septuagint) for Adam to Abraham — so deliberate, systematic editing by someone is likely, the direction of it is genuinely open after seventeen centuries, and a serious modern camp (Henry B. Smith Jr.; Jeremy Sexton) revives the very Hebrew-deflation reading the Fathers intuited. But the deflation thesis is a strong minority view, not the established one: the text-critical mainstream (Hendel; Tov; Wevers) leans the other way (Septuagint inflation of a longer Hebrew Vorlage); the Samaritan Pentateuch's pre-Christian short figures show a short chronology owing nothing to anti-Christian malice; the "second Cainan" the charge names as a Jewish deletion is more likely a Greek-side addition (absent even from Eusebius's own chronicle); the divergence predates the Christian–Jewish polemic (Demetrius, 3rd c. BC), killing Agapius's post-Resurrection framing; and on the motive, Eusebius's "to encourage early marriages" is bare conjecture, while the modern camp's argued anti-Christian messianic-chronology motive (the post–Bar Kochba rabbis deferring the anno mundi 4000 messianic mark, re-reading Daniel 9 away from Christ) is serious but still presupposes the deflation reading it would explain. So the textual question is contested and live, the deliberate-anti-messianic-tampering reading short of proof. It shares its subject — the antediluvian genealogy — with the Flood doctrine's timeline.37

The "second Cainan" (Genesis 11 LXX / Luke 3:36) — original reading the Jews deleted, or a secondary plus? (assessment: weak)

The "second Cainan" crux — the one name the chronology charge specifies. Agapius's most concrete example is the extra Kainan inserted between Arpachshad and Shelah in Genesis 11 (and standing at Luke 3:36), which he says the Jews cut out of the Hebrew — caught when the disciples pressed, "you have cut out his name. But his years, where did you put them?" The interpretation that serves — an original reading deliberately deleted by the rabbis — is weak, even though the bare text-critical question is contested. On the underlying question, Kainan's originality has a real modern defense (Henry B. Smith Jr.; Jonathan Sarfati): he stands in nearly all Septuagint manuscripts and in the pre-Christian Demetrius (c. 220 BC) and Jubilees (c. 160 BC), so he is no Christian invention. But the standard judgment (Andrew Steinmann; Marshall Johnson; Cassuto) makes him a secondary harmonizing plus — absent from the Masoretic Text, the Samaritan Pentateuch, 1 Chronicles (twice), Josephus, Philo, the Vulgate, and the earliest Luke papyrus (𝔓75), his Genesis-11 figures merely duplicating Shelah's — so the "deletion" is more likely a Greek-side addition. And the corruption reading fails on its own terms: even Kainan's defenders posit an accidental loss centuries before the rabbis, not a deletion; the omission is shared by the very pro-Septuagint Fathers (Africanus, Eusebius, Jerome) who themselves rejected Kainan as an interpolation; Agapius's clinching appeal to the Samaritan Torah is mistaken (it omits Kainan too); and even Smith grants "the originality of the longer chronology is not dependent on Kainan." It intersects the genealogy of Luke 3:36, where the same reading is a New Testament textual question.38
