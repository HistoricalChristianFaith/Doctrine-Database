The Rabbinic Corruption of Scripture (Septuagint vs. Masoretic Text)

From the second century on, Christian writers noticed that their Greek Old Testament — the Septuagint (LXX), the translation of "the seventy" — diverged from the Hebrew text the synagogue used, and that the divergences fell, suspiciously often, on the very passages adduced to prove Jesus the Christ: Isaiah 7:14 ("virgin" vs. "young woman"), Psalm 96:10 ("the Lord reigned from the wood"), the chronology of Genesis 5 and 11. Many of them drew the sharpest possible conclusion — that post-Second-Temple rabbinic Judaism (the proto-Masoretic tradition, later associated with Aquila and the Masoretes) had deliberately altered or cut the Hebrew text to deny Christ, while the older, pre-Christian LXX preserved the true reading. This page gathers the witnesses to that charge — earliest first — alongside the dissenting and the modern voices. The doctrine is specifically about alleged rabbinic tampering after AD 70, not textual transmission in general; the cruxes weigh the malice thesis against the modern text-critical finding (Emanuel Tov; the Dead Sea Scrolls) that the LXX faithfully renders a different Hebrew Vorlage — divergent text traditions, not corruption.

Justin Martyr (c. 165)

Justin Martyr, debating the Jew Trypho, gives the earliest and fullest form of the charge: the Jewish teachers both disparage the Septuagint of "your seventy elders that were with Ptolemy" as "untrue in certain respects" and "have altogether taken away many Scriptures" from it — precisely those "by which this very man who was crucified is proved to have been set forth expressly as God, and man, and as being crucified, and as dying."1 He names particular excisions — a Jeremiah logion ("Come, let us lay on wood on His bread"), a one-line descent oracle, and the passover saying of Esdras — and insists the cutting is recent and incomplete, "still written in some copies… in the synagogues of the Jews (for it is only a short time since they were cut out)."2 His most-cited single example is the deletion of the words "from the wood" from Psalm 96:10 — to which even Trypho answers that the erasure "seems incredible."3 The charge is one of will as much as deed: had the teachers grasped the force of the texts they still admit, "be well assured they would have deleted them," and where they cannot delete they alter the reading (the Septuagint's "take away the righteous" against their "bind the righteous").4

Arguments & cruxes

Sub-claims marshaled for (or against) the corruption charge — which cut across the timeline rather than sitting at one date — will be weighed adversarially here, each on its own page with an assessment of the interpretation it serves. The master crux (did the Jews deliberately corrupt the Hebrew text?), the Septuagint-legend crux, the Aquila-revision crux, and the Genesis 5/11 chronology crux are queued and not yet worked.
