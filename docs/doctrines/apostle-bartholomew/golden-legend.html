The Golden Legend: the flaying of Bartholomew

Dates: c. 1260 (Jacobus de Voragine, Legenda Aurea) · the Golden Legend, tr. Caxton
Summary: The late-medieval romance that gave Bartholomew the vivid martyrdom Christian art remembers. In “India” he silences the demon in the idol Astaroth, heals the mad daughter of King Polemius, and converts the king; the king’s brother Astyages, enraged when his own idol is shattered, has Bartholomew beaten and flayed alive at Albanopolis in Armenia. The Legend itself notes that the sources disagree on the manner of death and tries to reconcile them. It is hagiography a millennium and more after the events, of no biographical worth — but it is the wellspring of the flayed Bartholomew.

Claims & evidence

India, the idol Astaroth, and King Polemius

The Legend opens with the elastic geography of the tradition and moves quickly to the miracle that converts a king.

S. Bartholomew, the apostle, went into India, which is in the end of the world.

There he confronts the idol Astaroth, in which a deceiving demon lodged:

In that idol dwelt a fiend that said that he could heal all manner sicknesses, but he lied, for he could not make them whole, but might better them but for a while.

When the demon is silenced and the idol’s frauds exposed, the king seeks out the apostle:

And when Polemius, king of that region, heard this thing, which had a daughter lunatic, he sent to the apostle, praying that he would come to him and heal his daughter.

— Jacobus de Voragine, the Golden Legend, life of St Bartholomew.

The flaying, ordered by the king’s brother Astyages

Polemius is converted, but his brother Astyages, seeing his own god thrown down, turns on the apostle and orders the death the Legend is famous for.

…commanded that the apostle should be beaten with staves, and that he should be flayed quick, and so it was done.

— the same, life of St Bartholomew.

The Legend’s own admission that the sources disagree

Remarkably, Jacobus pauses to note that the tradition never settled how Bartholomew died, and rehearses the rival accounts before harmonizing them.

There be divers opinions of the manner of his passion. For the blessed Dorotheus saith that he was crucified… S. Theoderus saith that he was flayed, and it is read in many books that he was beheaded only.

His reconciliation stitches all three together:

…some say that he was crucified and was taken down ere he died, and for to have greater torment he was flayed and at the last beheaded.

Of the place he follows Dorotheus: “He died in Alban, a city of great Armenia, crucified the head downward.”

Notes

  • Late legend, not early witness. The Golden Legend is c. 1260 — more than a thousand years after Bartholomew — and gathers older apocryphal Acts and apostle-lists into a single edifying story. It is cited here as hagiography, following the house treatment set out on the apostles hub.
  • The origin of the iconography. The flaying — not the crucifixion or beheading of the other accounts — is what triumphed in Christian art: Bartholomew holding his own skin and a flaying-knife in Michelangelo’s Last Judgment, and the écorché statue of Marco d’Agrate in Milan Cathedral. The image is medieval and legendary, and rests on this strand of the tradition, weighed on the argument page.
  • Its own honesty is telling. That the Legend must list crucifixion, flaying, and beheading and then splice them is itself evidence that no fixed memory of Bartholomew’s death survived to be reported.
