The apostle-lists: Armenia and a martyr’s death

Dates: uncertain (the apostle-list literature, c. 3rd–8th centuries) · Pseudo-Hippolytus, On the Twelve Apostles
Summary: The layer where Bartholomew’s bare name grows a biography. The family of late, mutually-dependent catalogues — Pseudo-Hippolytus, Pseudo-Dorotheus, the Byzantine and Latin lists — keeps the “India” of Pantaenus but adds Armenia, a martyrdom, and a burial place. They are church memory rather than history: of uncertain date, copying one another, and disagreeing on how he died.

Claims & evidence

Pseudo-Hippolytus: India, Armenia, and crucifixion head-downward

The catalogue that goes under the name of Hippolytus gives the fullest early form of the notice. It joins the eastern mission of Pantaenus’s report to a death and a grave in Armenia — and states the martyrdom as a crucifixion, not a flaying.

Bartholomew, again, preached to the Indians, to whom he also gave the Gospel according to Matthew, and was crucified with his head downward, and was buried in Allanum, a town of the great Armenia.

— Pseudo-Hippolytus, On the Twelve Apostles, 6 (ANF vol. 5). The place-name appears variously as Allanum, Alban, or Albanopolis of Armenia Major.

The wider catalogue tradition

The same field and death recur, with variations, across the related lists — Pseudo-Dorotheus of Tyre, the Byzantine Chronicon Paschale, the Latin Breviarium Apostolorum, and their kin. Some keep the crucifixion; others report that Bartholomew was flayed and then beheaded. It is from this catalogue layer, not from any early witness, that Armenia becomes Bartholomew’s settled country and Albanopolis his traditional place of death — the tradition the Armenian Apostolic Church would make its own, honouring Bartholomew with Thaddaeus as an apostle of Armenia.

Notes

  • Late, dependent, and divided. These lists are of uncertain authorship and date, lean on one another, and are judged by critics worthless as independent history — their worth is as evidence for how the church came to remember and commemorate the apostles. On the whole class, see the apostles hub.
  • The martyrdom is unstable from the start. Already in this layer the manner of death wavers between crucifixion, flaying, and beheading — the disagreement that the Golden Legend would later try to reconcile, and the reason the flaying is rated weak.
  • India versus Armenia. The lists hold both together — a mission to “India” and a death in Armenia — without explaining the route between them; the tension is weighed on the mission argument page.
