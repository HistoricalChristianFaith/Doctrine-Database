The New Testament on Bartholomew

Dates: c. 30–90 (the events, and the writing of the Gospels and Acts) · Wikipedia
Summary: The bedrock, and it is thin. Bartholomew is named only in the four lists of the Twelve — three times beside Philip — and nothing else about him is recorded: no saying, no deed, no mission, no death. His name is a patronymic, “son of Tolmai,” so he must also have had a personal name the lists omit. John’s Gospel, which never names a Bartholomew, tells instead of Nathanael, whom Philip brings to Jesus — the figure the later tradition would identify with Bartholomew.

Claims & evidence

Named in all four lists of the Twelve — three times beside Philip

Bartholomew appears in every list of the apostles and nowhere else in the New Testament. In Matthew, Mark, and Luke he stands immediately after Philip; only in Acts, which lists the Eleven after the resurrection, is he separated from Philip by Thomas. The pairing with Philip is the thread the later tradition pulls on.

Philip, and Bartholomew; Thomas, and Matthew the publican; James the son of Alphaeus, and Lebbaeus, whose surname was Thaddaeus…

— Matthew 10:3 (cf. Mark 3:18; Luke 6:14). In Acts the order shifts: “Philip, and Thomas, Bartholomew, and Matthew” (Acts 1:13).

“Bartholomew” is a patronymic, not a personal name

“Bartholomew” is the Aramaic bar-Tolmai, “son of Tolmai” (Tolmai/Talmai being an old Hebrew name). It identifies a man by his father, as “Bar-jona” does Simon Peter or “Barnabas” and “Barabbas” do others; it is not itself a given name. That leaves room — and is the linguistic opening for the whole later tradition — for Bartholomew to have borne a personal name the apostle-lists never trouble to give. The name most often supplied is Nathanael.

The other side of the pairing: John’s Nathanael

John’s Gospel never mentions a Bartholomew. It tells instead of Nathanael of Cana in Galilee, whom Philip — the very apostle the Synoptics pair with Bartholomew — fetches to Jesus, and whom Jesus greets as “an Israelite indeed, in whom is no guile.”

Philip findeth Nathanael, and saith unto him, We have found him, of whom Moses in the law, and the prophets, did write, Jesus of Nazareth, the son of Joseph. And Nathanael said unto him, Can there any good thing come out of Nazareth? Philip saith unto him, Come and see. Jesus saw Nathanael coming to him, and saith of him, Behold an Israelite indeed, in whom is no guile!

— John 1:45–47. Nathanael appears once more, among a group otherwise made up of named apostles, at the last miraculous draught of fishes: “There were together Simon Peter, and Thomas called Didymus, and Nathanael of Cana in Galilee, and the sons of Zebedee” (John 21:2).

Notes

  • The total silence about his life. Of no other apostle named in all four lists does the New Testament record so little — not a single word or act. Everything on his mission and death traced on the summary timeline belongs to the later tradition.
  • Why Nathanael is the natural candidate. The Synoptics list Bartholomew but never a Nathanael; John names Nathanael but never a Bartholomew; and Philip links the two. Whether that convergence proves them the same man is weighed on the argument page.
  • A caution about the fishing scene. That Nathanael fishes in John 21 with Peter, Thomas, and the sons of Zebedee suggests he belonged to the apostolic circle — but John never lists the Twelve and never calls Nathanael one of them, so the inference is strong, not certain.
