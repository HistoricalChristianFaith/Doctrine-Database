Pantaenus: Bartholomew’s Gospel in “India”

Dates: c. 180 (Pantaenus’s mission), reported by Eusebius c. 325 · Eusebius, Church History 5.10
Summary: The earliest and soberest notice of a Bartholomew mission. Pantaenus, head of the catechetical school of Alexandria, is said to have travelled “as far as India” about the year 180 and to have found Christians there already holding a Gospel of Matthew in Hebrew — left with them, they said, by the apostle Bartholomew. Eusebius relays it as report, and Jerome repeats it; it fixes an eastern mission for Bartholomew before any tradition of Armenia or of his death.

Claims & evidence

Pantaenus sent “as far as India”

Eusebius introduces Pantaenus as a former Stoic turned Christian teacher, so zealous for the gospel that he was made a missionary to the East.

He displayed such zeal for the divine Word, that he was appointed as a herald of the Gospel of Christ to the nations in the East, and was sent as far as India.

— Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History 5.10, 2.

The Hebrew Matthew that Bartholomew left

There, Eusebius reports, Pantaenus found the fruit of an earlier apostolic mission — a Hebrew copy of Matthew’s Gospel, said to have been brought by Bartholomew himself and treasured ever since.

Bartholomew, one of the apostles, had preached to them, and left with them the writing of Matthew in the Hebrew language, which they had preserved till that time.

— Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History 5.10, 3. Eusebius gives the whole as what “is reported” and “they say,” not as his own certain knowledge.

Jerome repeats it

Two centuries later Jerome, drawing on Eusebius in his life of Pantaenus, tells the same story — that in India Pantaenus found the Bartholomew tradition and carried the Hebrew Matthew back to Alexandria.

he found that Bartholomew, one of the twelve apostles, had preached the advent of the Lord Jesus according to the gospel of Matthew, and on his return to Alexandria he brought this with him written in Hebrew characters.

— Jerome, On Illustrious Men (De Viris Illustribus) 36.

Notes

  • What “India” meant. Ancient writers used “India” loosely for lands far to the south and east — Arabia Felix (South Arabia) and Ethiopia included — so Pantaenus’s “India” need not be the subcontinent. Where he actually went, and thus where Bartholomew’s mission lay, is weighed on the argument page.
  • A possible confusion of names. Some scholars suspect that Pantaenus, meeting the Thomas-Christians of the East, mistook their “Mar Thoma” (Saint Thomas) for “Bar Tolmai” (Bartholomew) — which would make this the mission of Thomas mis-remembered. The suggestion is discussed on the same argument page.
  • Why it still matters. Whatever its geography, this is the earliest datable tradition to attach a mission to Bartholomew at all, and it predates by centuries the Armenian tradition and the flaying legend. On how it ranks among the layered sources, see the apostles hub.
