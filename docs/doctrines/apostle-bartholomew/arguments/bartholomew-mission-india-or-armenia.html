Did Bartholomew preach in India or in Armenia?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — the earliest witness sends Bartholomew to “India,” but the word was elastic and the report may even confuse him with Thomas; the later and firmer tradition, still living in the Armenian church, makes Armenia the field of his mission and death. Each rests on real evidence of a different kind, and no clean route joins them. Genuinely disputed.

The claim

…Bartholomew, one of the apostles, had preached to them, and left with them the writing of Matthew in the Hebrew language, which they had preserved till that time.

— Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History 5.10, 3 (reporting Pantaenus). The question is where Bartholomew actually laboured: the “India” of the earliest notice, or the Armenia of the later tradition that also claims his death.1

The evidence for India

The India tradition is the older and better-pedigreed. Around 180 Pantaenus of Alexandria is reported to have reached “India” and found there Christians holding a Hebrew Matthew that Bartholomew had left — a notice Eusebius preserves and Jerome repeats. This is the earliest datable tradition of any Bartholomew mission, and it is sober in tone: no marvels, just a Gospel-book and a memory. Pseudo-Hippolytus and the apostle-lists keep “the Indians” as the field even as they add Armenia as the place of death. If the earliest evidence is to be weighted most heavily, the east — some India — has the first claim.2

The evidence for Armenia

The Armenia tradition is later but far more detailed and institutionally rooted. From Pseudo-Hippolytus onward the lists agree that Bartholomew died in Armenia — at Albanopolis (Allanum/Alban) in Armenia Major — and the apocryphal Martyrdom and the Golden Legend set his whole passion there, at the court of King Polemius and his brother Astyages. The Armenian Apostolic Church honours Bartholomew, with Thaddaeus, as a founding apostle of Armenia and keeps his cult at the monastery that bears his name. A living national tradition and a fixed burial-place are not nothing; they are the kind of local memory that often preserves a genuine core.3

Adversarial assessment

What “India” could mean. The India notice is early but geographically slippery. Ancient writers stretched “India” over Arabia Felix (South Arabia) and Ethiopia as well as the subcontinent, so Pantaenus’s destination is itself uncertain — his “India” may lie much nearer than the name suggests. Worse for the tradition, some scholars suspect a confusion of names at its root: meeting the Thomas-Christians of the East and their “Mar Thoma” (Saint Thomas), Pantaenus — or his source — may have heard “Bar Tolmai” (Bartholomew). If so, the earliest Bartholomew mission is really Thomas’s, and the whole India strand dissolves.

What Armenia cannot supply. The Armenia tradition, for all its detail, is late: it enters only with the mutually-dependent apostle-lists and the apocryphal Acts, centuries after the events, and its rich narrative is exactly the sort of legendary filling those sources specialize in. A national cult and a shown tomb prove veneration, not itinerary. The two traditions need not contradict — an apostle could in principle have worked in more than one region — but no source actually narrates a coherent career linking them; each simply asserts its own field. The honest position is that Bartholomew’s mission cannot be located with confidence: the earliest evidence points vaguely east and may be a misnomer, the fullest evidence points to Armenia and is late.4

Assessment

Contested. The India tradition has priority in date but suffers from the elasticity of the name and a plausible confusion with Thomas; the Armenia tradition has depth, a fixed site, and a living church behind it but arrives late and legend-laden. Neither drives the other from the field, and the sources give no route that reconciles them. Where Bartholomew truly preached is a genuine and unresolved question.
