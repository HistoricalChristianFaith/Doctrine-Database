Did Bartholomew preach in India or in Armenia?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — the earliest notice sends Bartholomew to “India,” and that notice is stronger than it first looks (a defensible case for the subcontinent, not merely a vague east); the later tradition, still living in the Armenian church and older than the Greek apostle-lists alone would suggest, makes Armenia the field of his mission and death. Each rests on real evidence of a different kind — India on chronological priority, Armenia on a national cult — and the two need not even be rivals: the apostle-lists hold both at once, a mission field and a death-place. No clean route joins them and no discipline’s evidence overrides the other’s. Genuinely disputed.

The claim

…Bartholomew, one of the apostles, had preached to them, and left with them the writing of Matthew in the Hebrew language, which they had preserved till that time.

— Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History 5.10, 3 (reporting Pantaenus). The question is where Bartholomew actually laboured: the “India” of the earliest notice, or the Armenia of the later tradition that also claims his death.1

The evidence for India

The India tradition is the older and better-pedigreed. Around 180 Pantaenus of Alexandria is reported to have reached “India” and found there Christians holding a Hebrew Matthew that Bartholomew had left — a notice Eusebius preserves and Jerome repeats. This is the earliest datable tradition of any Bartholomew mission, and it is sober in tone: no marvels, just a Gospel-book and a memory. Pseudo-Hippolytus and the apostle-lists keep “the Indians” as the field even as they add Armenia as the place of death.2

Its best form points not vaguely east but to the subcontinent itself. A Greek strand (Pseudo-Sophronius) has Bartholomew preach to “the Indians called Happy” — India Felix — and a line of scholarship (A. C. Perumalil, G. M. Moraes) reads this as the Konkan coast around Kalyān near Bombay, whose name itself carries the sense “happy, felix.” The circumstantial support is that Pantaenus’s own pupils and successors, Clement of Alexandria and Origen, write of India with real, differentiated knowledge — of its Brahmans and “gymnosophists” — not the hazy geography of writers who confused it with Arabia; plausibly they had it from Pantaenus himself. On this reading the earliest evidence names a genuine eastern mission, and Bartholomew has the first claim to a field in India.3

The evidence for Armenia

The Armenia tradition is later but far more detailed and institutionally rooted — and it is not merely the Greek apostle-lists. Armenia has an independent national memory: the fifth-century historian Movses Khorenatsi tells of Bartholomew’s mission and death on Armenian soil, and the Armenian Apostolic Church honours Bartholomew, with Thaddaeus, as a founding apostle — the tradition that makes Armenia the first kingdom to embrace Christianity as a state (301) and counts Bartholomew, by its reckoning, among its first patriarchs. From Pseudo-Hippolytus onward the Greek lists agree that he died in Armenia — at Albanopolis (Allanum/Alban) in Armenia Major — and the apocryphal Martyrdom and the Golden Legend set his whole passion there, at the court of King Polemius and his brother Astyages. The thirteenth-century Saint Bartholomew Monastery at Vaspurakan marks the traditional site. A living national church, a fixed burial-place, and a memory independent of the Greek catalogues are not nothing; they are the kind of local tradition that often preserves a genuine core.4

Adversarial assessment

What weakens the India case. The India notice is early but geographically slippery. Ancient writers stretched “India” over Arabia Felix (South Arabia) and Ethiopia as well as the subcontinent, and a long line of critics — Mosheim and Neander for Arabia, Harnack, Medlycott, and Mingana among the skeptics — hold that Pantaenus’s “India” lay much nearer than the name suggests; the Catholic Encyclopedia calls it “probably South Arabia.” Nor is there any surviving local Indian tradition of a Bartholomew mission to set beside the Thomas-Christians’ memory of Thomas. A further suspicion — that Pantaenus, meeting the Thomas-Christians and their “Mar Thoma” (Saint Thomas), or his source, heard “Bar Tolmai” (Bartholomew) — would, if true, hand the whole strand to Thomas. But this is a conjecture, not a datum: it has no textual warrant, and the genuine Indian knowledge of Clement and Origen tells against a mere muddle at the school’s own root. It lowers confidence in the India notice; it does not dissolve it.

What weakens the Armenia case. The Armenia tradition, for all its depth, is late in attestation: no source places him there within centuries of the events, and its rich martyrdom narrative is exactly the sort of legendary filling the apocryphal Acts specialize in. Its specifics do not survive scrutiny: there was no Armenian Arsacid king named “Polemius,” and the sources cannot agree who killed him — Polemius/Astyages in the Greek passion, Agrippa (Tigranes VI) or King Sanatruk in the Armenian — nor even how he died (crucified, flayed, or beheaded). A national cult and a shown tomb prove veneration, not itinerary.

Why the binary may be false. The two traditions need not contradict. No source narrates a coherent career linking them, but neither does any source treat them as alternatives: the apostle-lists calmly report both — “preached to the Indians… buried in… Armenia” — a mission field in one place and a death in another, which an apostle could in principle have had. Read that way the real questions are not “India or Armenia?” but whether the early India notice is reliable or a Thomas-confusion, and whether the Armenian death-tradition is historical memory or cult retrojection. On neither does the evidence close. The honest position is that Bartholomew’s mission cannot be located with confidence: the earliest evidence points east and has a defensible case for true India but may be a misnomer; the fullest and most rooted evidence points to Armenia but arrives late and legend-laden in its details.5

Assessment

Contested. The India tradition has priority in date and a genuine — not merely Arabian — case for the subcontinent, but it is dogged by the elasticity of the name and a possible confusion with Thomas; the Armenia tradition has institutional depth, a fixed site, and a living national church independent of the Greek lists, but it arrives late and its martyrdom particulars are demonstrably legendary. Neither drives the other from the field, and the sources give no route that reconciles them — indeed they may not need reconciling, if one names his mission and the other his death. Where Bartholomew truly preached is a genuine and unresolved question.
