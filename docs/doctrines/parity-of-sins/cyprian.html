Cyprian on the parity of sins

Dates: c. 252 (Letter to Antonianus) · Wikipedia
Summary: The first Christian writer surveyed here to name the parity thesis as a philosophers’ doctrine and set it outside the faith. Writing to a wavering African bishop about how to treat those who had lapsed under persecution, Cyprian identifies the refusal to distinguish degrees of guilt as Stoicism, not Christianity, and answers it with the physician’s question rather than the philosopher’s.

Claims & evidence

“All sins are equal” is the principle of the philosophers and Stoics, and Christians differ from them

The principle of the philosophers and stoics is different, dearest brother, who say that all sins are equal, and that a grave man ought not easily to be moved. But there is a wide difference between Christians and philosophers. And when the apostle says, “Beware, lest any man spoil you through philosophy and vain deceit,” we are to avoid those things which do not come from God’s clemency, but are begotten of the presumption of a too rigid philosophy.

— Letter 51.16 (numbered Letter 55 in the Oxford and Hartel editions), to Antonianus, source (also at New Advent). Antonianus had received letters from Novatian and was leaning to the rigorist party; Cyprian is arguing that the lapsed may be graded and received back according to the measure of their fall. He cites Colossians 2:8 against “philosophy and vain deceit” — making the parity thesis an instance of exactly the pagan wisdom Paul warns of.

The pastoral consequence: the church treats the wounded as wounded, not as dead

What medical skill can he exercise who says, “I cure the sound only, who have no need of a physician?” We ought to give our assistance, our healing art, to those who are wounded; neither let us think them dead, but rather let us regard them as lying half alive

— Letter 51.16, source. The medical image supplies the reason the grading matters: a doctrine that admits no degrees of injury can prescribe no degrees of treatment, and a penitential discipline is impossible without them.

Notes

  • Cyprian is the hinge between the exegetical witnesses and the polemical ones. Clement had read graded sin out of 1 John; Cyprian is the first here to identify a rival school holding the contrary, and the pairing “philosophers and Stoics” recurs almost verbatim in Ambrose, Jerome, Augustine and Bede.
  • He elsewhere states the graded doctrine positively from Scripture, collecting the texts on the sin that “shall not be forgiven” in his Testimonies — “if a man sin against God, who shall pray for him?”
    — Testimonies Against the Jews III, source.
  • Cyprian appears on this wiki also as a witness on purgatory.
