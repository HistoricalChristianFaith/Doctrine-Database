4 Maccabees on the parity of sins

Dates: c. AD 40 (composition generally placed between c. AD 20 and 70) · Wikipedia
Summary: A Greek Jewish martyrdom-homily whose author writes self-consciously in Stoic categories (“devout reason is sovereign over the emotions”). In the mouth of the aged priest Eleazar it states the parity thesis in a Torah key: a small transgression is as serious as a great one, because in either case the law itself is despised. It is the closest datable parallel to James 2:10, and it shows that when James wrote “guilty of all” the idea was already circulating in Greek-speaking Judaism rather than being imported from the Porch centuries later.

Claims & evidence

Small and great transgressions are of equal seriousness, because the law is equally despised in both

Therefore do not suppose that it would be a petty sin if we were to eat defiling food; to transgress the law in matters either small or great is of equal seriousness, for in either case the law is equally despised.

— 4 Maccabees 5:19–21 (NRSVue), source. Eleazar is being pressed to eat pork under Antiochus, and the argument is a defence of martyrdom over “reasonable” compromise: if the ceremonial and the moral commands stand or fall together, there is no such thing as a concession small enough to make. Note the ground given — not that the acts are of equal weight in themselves, but that the law is equally set at nought. That is the reasoning James 2:11 supplies when it appeals to the single Lawgiver who said both “do not commit adultery” and “do not kill.”

Notes

  • The book is a bridge, not a source in the strict sense: nobody on this timeline after it quotes 4 Maccabees on the point, and its usefulness is as evidence that the idiom of James 2:10 has a Hellenistic-Jewish home. That matters for the reading of James, because it means the verse can be construed as the commonplace about the law’s indivisibility without any commitment to Cicero’s stricter thesis that all sins are morally equal.
  • The author’s Stoicism is real but selective — he keeps the vocabulary of reason mastering passion while rejecting the Stoic ideal of extirpating the passions altogether. The same partial borrowing is visible here: the parity language is applied to transgression of the law, not to a general theory of vice.
  • 4 Maccabees is not in the canon received by any of the later witnesses on this timeline (it survives chiefly in Christian manuscripts of the Greek Bible), so the parallel is a matter of shared idiom, not of authority. On the canonical status of the Maccabean literature generally, see the Old Testament canon.
