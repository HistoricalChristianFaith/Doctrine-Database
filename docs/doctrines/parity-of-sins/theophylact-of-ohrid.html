Theophylact of Ohrid on the parity of sins

Dates: c. 1100 · Wikipedia
Summary: The mature Greek reading of James 2:10, transmitted through the catena tradition and stated here at its clearest. Theophylact reaches the same solution as Augustine — “guilty of all” concerns love, the head of the virtues — but arrives by a different road, and adds an argument Augustine does not use: if the verse meant that any single failure damns, “no one among men would survive.”

Claims & evidence

The verse is about love, and read otherwise it would damn everyone

Whoever sins in one thing becomes guilty of all, because he does not have perfect love, for love is the head of all that is good, and when the head is absent, the entire remaining body amounts to nothing. … If this were not so, no one among men would survive, because no one keeps all the commandments; but whoever preserved purity is sometimes overcome by anger, and whoever gives alms not infrequently has envy. Therefore he speaks not of there being no deficiency at all in the virtues, but of love — that it must not be practiced with deficiency, with partiality, but wholly.

— Commentary on James, ch. 2, source. The reductio is an exegetical argument, not a sentimental one: James himself says at 3:2 that “in many things we all offend,” so a reading of 2:10 on which one offence forfeits everything would put the epistle at war with itself. Note also the specific target: partiality in love — which returns the verse to its context, the respecting of persons in James 2:1–9.

“Do not commit adultery, do not murder” are chosen because they are the commandments of love

Note that they too are taken from the law relating to the perfection of love, for whoever loves his neighbor will neither commit adultery nor murder, because such deeds are characteristic of an enemy.

— Commentary on James, ch. 2, source. This is the load-bearing textual observation for the love-reading. James’s two examples in v. 11 are both drawn from the second table, and both are things one does to a neighbour — so, the argument runs, the appeal is to the love-command of v. 8 rather than to the bare authority of the Lawgiver. The same observation, more briefly, is made by Hilary of Arles: “Because they are the ones which deal most closely with loving and with hating one’s neighbors.”

Notes

  • Theophylact is not the originator. The same exposition, at times nearly word for word, stands in the commentary on the Catholic Epistles that circulates under the name of Oecumenius — “James said this concerning love … since it would not be said in this way, no one among men would be saved” — (source), and in briefer form in the Greek catena attributed to Andreas of Caesarea: “To fail in one point is to lack perfect love, for this is the source of all good deeds.” What is on this page is a tradition, not one man’s opinion.
  • That the Greek and Latin traditions converge on the love-reading independently is the strongest thing going for it; that they may both be reading Paul’s “love is the fulfilling of the law” (Romans 13:10) back into James is the strongest thing against. Both are weighed at is “guilty of all” explained by the love-command?
