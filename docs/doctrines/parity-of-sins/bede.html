Bede on the parity of sins

Dates: c. 716 (Commentary on the Catholic Epistles) · Wikipedia
Summary: Bede is the first witness surveyed here to handle both proof-texts in a single work, and the one who consolidates the whole patristic settlement. On James 2:10 he reproduces Augustine’s love-solution and Augustine’s answer to the parity objection; on 1 John 5:17 he names the Stoics and Jovinian together and repudiates them, while stating in his own words the distinction between the small sins “without which this life cannot be lived” and the sins that lead to eternal punishment. That sentence is as clear a statement of the venial/mortal distinction as the patristic period affords.

Claims & evidence

James 2:10 is explained by the love on which all the commandments hang — and this does not make sins equal

Or perhaps because the fullness of the law is love, by which God and neighbor are loved, upon which commandments of love the whole law and the prophets depend, he rightly becomes guilty of all who acts against that in which all things hang? No one sins except by acting against it. … Why then are not all sins said to be equal? Or perhaps because he acts more against charity who sins more gravely, and less who sins lightly? Yet even if he offends in one, he is guilty of all, because he acts against that in which all things hang.

— Commentary on the Catholic Epistles, on James 2:10, source. Both halves of Augustine’s Letter 167 are here, compressed: guilt of the whole from the object violated, gradation of guilt from the degree of violation. Bede keeps Augustine’s interrogative manner (“or perhaps because…?”) rather than asserting the solution — the reading is offered as the best available, not as demonstrated.

The Stoics’ parity is “inept” and against all human sense; Jovinian followed them

Such is the diversity of sins, he says, that everything which deviates from the rule of equity is counted among the sins, although small sins cannot in any way take away or diminish the merit of righteousness from the just, as long as they are those without which this life cannot be lived, and likewise there are certain sins so discordant with all justice, committed with such iniquity, that without any contradiction, unless they are corrected, they lead their perpetrator to eternal punishment. … Blessed John’s statement clearly repudiates the inept argument of the Stoics, who dared to say against all human sense and to affirm that all sins are equal, saying it makes no difference whether a man steals a human being, an ox, or a chicken, because it is not the animal but the intention that constitutes the crime. Jovinian the heretic followed them, asserting that there is no distinction between marriage and virginity

— Commentary on the Catholic Epistles, on 1 John 5:17, source. Three things are worth separating here. The first sentence is Bede’s own and is doctrinally the most important on this page: a two-class division of sins by their effect, one class survivable and one damning. The second reports the Stoic thesis with its reason (intention, not object, makes the crime) — the argument Cicero gives in Paradoxa III, and the stolen chicken is Cicero’s cock, arriving in Northumbria eight centuries later by way of Ambrose’s “common fowl.” The third is a report about Jovinian, downstream of Jerome, and it slides: the proposition Bede actually attributes to Jovinian is the one about marriage and virginity, not the parity of sins.

Grading sins is a rule of pastoral practice, not only of theory

He speaks, however, of daily and light sins, which as they are difficult to avoid, so also are easily cured. … If, therefore, by speaking, or thinking, or forgetfulness, or ignorance you have perhaps erred, go to your brother, confess to him, ask for intervention. … But these things are said about lighter sins. Moreover, if you have committed something more serious, bring in the elders of the Church, and at their examination, punish yourself.

— Commentary on the Catholic Epistles, on 1 John 5:16, source. Two grades of sin yield two remedies — mutual confession among brethren for the light, the elders of the church for the grave. The graded doctrine has become a rubric.

Notes

  • Bede is where the two strands of this timeline knit together. The 1 John line running from Clement and Tertullian supplies the two classes of sin; the James line running through Augustine supplies the explanation of why the graded doctrine survives a verse that appears to deny it. From Bede onward they are one argument.
  • His “small sins … without which this life cannot be lived” and “certain sins … unless they are corrected, they lead their perpetrator to eternal punishment” is, in substance, the venial/mortal distinction that Aquinas will formalise.
  • Bede appears on this wiki also as a witness on the nephilim.
