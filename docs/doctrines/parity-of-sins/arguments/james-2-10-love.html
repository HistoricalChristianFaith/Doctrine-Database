Is “guilty of all” explained by the love-command on which the whole law hangs?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: plausible — coherent, anchored in James’s own appeal to the royal law two verses earlier, and it reaches the right result, but it supplies a middle term James does not state · advanced by Augustine, and after him by Bede and Theophylact

Related arguments: “guilty of all” — see also the parity reading · the unity-of-the-law reading

The claim

Hence it is true that if a man shall keep the whole law, and yet offend in one point, he becomes guilty of all, because he does what is contrary to the love on which hangs the whole law. A man, therefore, becomes guilty of all by doing what is contrary to that on which all hang.

— Augustine, Letter 167.16, link. The claim in one sentence: the commandments are not a list but a structure resting on a single foundation, so any sin, whatever its object, damages the foundation and so implicates the whole — while the degree of damage still varies, which is why the verse does not make sins equal.

Who advances it

Augustine states it, in the letter he sent to Jerome in 415 and to which Jerome never replied. It becomes the Latin standard: Bede reproduces both halves of it on James 2:10, question-form and all. The Greek tradition arrives at a very similar reading by its own route — Theophylact (“love is the head of all that is good, and when the head is absent, the entire remaining body amounts to nothing”), the commentary circulating under the name of Oecumenius, the catena attributed to Andreas of Caesarea (“to fail in one point is to lack perfect love”), and Caesarius of Arles in the pulpit (“to have fallen from the precept of love and thereby to have offended in all the other commandments”).1 Whether the convergence is independent or reflects a shared exegetical inheritance is not settled by the texts themselves, but the two traditions state it in different vocabularies.

The evidence for

The context is the reading’s real strength, and it is often understated. James invokes the love-command by name two verses before the crux: “If ye fulfil the royal law according to the scripture, Thou shalt love thy neighbour as thyself, ye do well: but if ye have respect to persons, ye commit sin” (James 2:8–9). The sin that occasions “guilty of all” is thus already identified as a failure of neighbour-love; the reading does not import love into the passage but picks it up where James put it.2

Second, James’s two chosen examples both belong to the second table and both concern the neighbour — adultery and murder, not idolatry and sabbath-breaking. Theophylact makes this the load-bearing observation: “whoever loves his neighbor will neither commit adultery nor murder, because such deeds are characteristic of an enemy.” Hilary of Arles asks the same question and gives the same answer — they are chosen “because they are the ones which deal most closely with loving and with hating one’s neighbors.”3

Third, the reading is the only one on offer that explains both halves of what the verse has to do at once. It gives a reason why one offence implicates the whole (the foundation is common) and, in the same breath, a principle of gradation (some acts are further from love than others) — which is why every witness who adopts it uses it against the parity thesis rather than for it. Bede sets the two questions side by side in a single paragraph.

Fourth, it coheres with James’s ending. Having exposed a liability nobody escapes, James turns immediately to mercy — “he shall have judgment without mercy, that hath shewed no mercy; and mercy rejoiceth against judgment” (James 2:13). On the love-reading that is not a change of subject but the remedy fitted to the diagnosis, which is how Augustine takes it.

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The appeal to the royal law is in the text, the second-table examples are in the text, and the reading does the work a reading has to do: it saves the verse from the parity inference without emptying it. As an account of what James is doing in 2:1–13 as a whole — convicting partiality by the standard of neighbour-love — it is very strong.

What is supplied rather than stated. The middle term is not James’s. When James explains “guilty of all,” the reason he actually gives in v. 11 is the identity of the Lawgiver — “he that said, Do not commit adultery, said also, Do not kill” — not the love that all the commandments hang on. “All the law and the prophets hang” on love is Christ’s formula at Matthew 22:40, and “love is the fulfilling of the law” is Paul’s at Romans 13:10 — and it is noticeable that both Augustine and Bede quote the Pauline chain (“thou shalt not commit adultery … it is briefly comprehended in this saying”) at exactly the point where they need the premise. The reading is Pauline theology read into a Jacobean argument. It may be a true reading for all that, but the inference is theirs, not James’s.

A second gap. The love-reading explains the guilt but strains at the “of all.” If sinning against love makes one guilty of all, the guilt is guilt with respect to love — one thing, not all things. To get from there to liability under every commandment one needs the further premise that each commandment simply is love in a particular application, which the fathers assert rather than argue. Bonaventure’s later move — full pain of loss, graded pain of sense — is a tacit admission that the patristic answer needed reinforcing at this joint.

The rival. The reading that takes v. 11 at its word — the law is one because the Lawgiver is one, so breaking any of it is breaking it — is the more probable primary sense, and it needs no premise James does not supply. The two are not enemies: the unity-of-the-law reading tells you why the liability is total, and the love-reading tells you why the liability is nevertheless graded. Most of the force of the patristic answer survives on either.

Assessment

plausible — coherent, textually anchored, and not ruled out, but short of the most probable reading of what James argues in v. 11. Trust it as an account of the passage’s subject (partiality as a failure of neighbour-love, which James says outright) and as a theological explanation of why the guilt admits of degrees. Treat its distinctive claim — that the reason one offence implicates the whole law is that love is the thing all the commandments hang on — as an inference the fathers drew from Christ and Paul rather than one James states. It converges with the unity-of-the-law reading at the practical destination, and neither reading yields the parity of sins.
