Cicero on the parity of sins

Dates: c. 46 BC · Wikipedia
Summary: Cicero is not a Christian witness but the channel through which the Stoic thesis omnia peccata paria — “all sins are equal” — reached the Latin West. He reports it twice: once with open contempt, as a specimen of Stoic absurdity in a courtroom (Pro Murena, 63 BC), and once in a sophistical defence of a weakened form of it (Paradoxa Stoicorum III, c. 46 BC). Nearly every later Latin writer who refutes the parity thesis — Cyprian, Ambrose, Jerome, Augustine, Bede, Aquinas — is arguing against the doctrine in the shape Cicero gave it, and several borrow his stock example word for word.

Claims & evidence

The Stoic thesis, reported hostilely: killing a cock is no lesser crime than strangling one’s father

all offenses are equal; that every sin is an unpardonable crime; and that he does not commit a less crime who kills a cock if there was no need to do so, than the man who strangles his father

— Pro Murena 61, trans. C. D. Yonge, source. Cicero is defending Murena against a prosecution in which Cato the Younger, a famous Stoic, appeared; he sets out Zeno’s maxims (Latin omnia peccata esse paria) precisely in order to make a jury laugh at them. The rhetorical purpose matters for the reception: what the Latin tradition inherited was not a Stoic textbook but Cicero’s caricature of one, poultry and parricide included.

The same thesis defended, in a weakened form, in the Paradoxa

PARADOX III: THAT ALL MISDEEDS ARE IN THEMSELVES EQUAL, AND GOOD DEEDS THE SAME.

The matter it may be said is a trifle, but the crime is enormous; for crimes are not to be measured by the issue of events, but from the bad intentions of men.

— Paradoxa Stoicorum III, trans. Cyrus R. Edmonds, source. The argument runs on the intention rather than the outcome: a pilot who capsizes a ship has failed identically whether her cargo was gold or straw, because it is the lapse from right reason, not the size of the loss, that constitutes the fault. Cicero does not in fact carry the strict Stoic position — he retreats to the milder claim that offences of the same kind are equal, and concedes that penalties differ — but the heading is what was remembered and quoted.

Notes

  • Cicero’s cock-and-father illustration is the one that travels. Ambrose attacks the Novatianists for holding, “after the fashion of the Stoics,” that the man who “has stolen a common fowl, as they say” is no better off than one “who has smothered his father” — the Pro Murena pair, verbatim in substance. Jerome substitutes a stolen loaf for the fowl; Bede reduces it to a stolen chicken.
  • Aquinas is the one witness on this timeline who cites Cicero by name and by book — “The opinion of the Stoics, which Cicero adopts in the book on Paradoxes (Paradox. iii), was that all sins are equal” — and reads the whole later heresy of equal sins and equal punishments as descending from it.
  • The Stoic thesis reaches Jewish soil independently: 4 Maccabees, written in Greek by an author steeped in Stoic ethics, has Eleazar say that transgression “in matters either small or great is of equal seriousness.”
