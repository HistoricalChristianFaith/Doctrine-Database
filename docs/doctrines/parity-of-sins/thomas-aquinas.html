Thomas Aquinas on the parity of sins

Dates: c. 1270 (Summa Theologiae I-II) · Wikipedia
Summary: Aquinas gives the parity thesis a formal article of its own, diagnoses the exact philosophical mistake behind it, and settles the question for Latin theology. His diagnosis: the Stoics treated sin as a pure privation, like death or darkness, which admits of no more and less; but sin is the other kind of privation, like sickness, which leaves something of the opposite habit standing and therefore does admit of degrees.

Claims & evidence

The thesis, its source, and its Christian descendants

The opinion of the Stoics, which Cicero adopts in the book on Paradoxes (Paradox. iii), was that all sins are equal: from which opinion arose the error of certain heretics, who not only hold all sins to be equal, but also maintain that all the pains of hell are equal.

— Summa Theologiae I-II q. 73 a. 2, source. Aquinas traces the line of descent explicitly — Cicero to the Stoics, the heretics to Cicero — and notes that the heretics went further than their source, adding equal punishment to equal sin. That is precisely the shape of the position Jerome attributed to Jovinian.

The diagnosis: sin is not a pure privation

So far as can be gathered from the words of Cicero the Stoics arrived at their conclusion through looking at sin on the side of the privation only, in so far, to wit, as it is a departure from reason; wherefore considering simply that no privation admits of more or less, they held that all sins are equal. … There is, however, another privation which is not simple, but retains something of the opposite habit; it consists in “becoming” corrupted rather than in “being” corrupted, like sickness which is a privation of the due commensuration of the humors, yet so that something remains of that commensuration, else the animal would cease to live

— Summa Theologiae I-II q. 73 a. 2, source. The objection Aquinas is answering is the one Augustine had also faced, in a different dress: crossing a line is crossing a line, whether by an inch or a mile. Aquinas grants that some privations work that way and denies that sin is one of them, on the ground that a sin which destroyed the order of reason entirely would destroy the act along with it — “evil, if total, destroys itself.”

Gravity varies with the object, and highest against God

Wherefore a sin which is about the very substance of man, e.g. murder, is graver than a sin which is about external things, e.g. theft; and graver still is a sin committed directly against God, e.g. unbelief, blasphemy, and the like

— Summa Theologiae I-II q. 73 a. 3, source. This supplies what the patristic answers lacked: not merely that sins differ, but a principle by which they are ordered. It also answers Cicero on his own ground, since Cicero’s case for parity had run precisely on the claim that the object (the gold cargo or the straw) is irrelevant and only the lapse counts.

The proof-text against parity: “he that hath delivered me to thee hath the greater sin”

Our Lord said to Pilate (Jn. 19:11): “He that hath delivered me to thee, hath the greater sin,” and yet it is evident that Pilate was guilty of some sin. Therefore one sin is greater than another.

— Summa Theologiae I-II q. 73 a. 2, sed contra, source, citing John 19:11. The same verse anchors Jerome’s case in Against Jovinianus II.25, and it is the strongest single text against parity in the New Testament: Christ compares two guilty parties and rates one guiltier.

The graded doctrine applied: some heretics said all sins, punishments, merits and rewards are equal

Likewise it refutes certain heretics who said that all sins are equal, and all punishments, and all merits, and all rewards. Therefore, he excludes this, when he says, more tolerable…, because it will be worse for certain sinners.

— Commentary on Matthew, on Matthew 10:15, source. The “more tolerable… for the land of Sodom and Gomorrah” sayings (Matthew 10:15; 11:22) are the other main gospel evidence, since a comparative of tolerability presupposes a scale.

Notes

  • Aquinas does not name Jovinian in these places; he says “certain heretics.” The identification is available in his sources — Jerome and Bede both make it — and the description he gives (equal sins and equal punishments) matches what Jerome ascribes. Whether the ascription is sound is weighed at did Jovinian teach that all sins are equal?
  • The mortal/venial distinction that the earlier witnesses state descriptively — Bede’s “small sins … without which this life cannot be lived” against sins that “lead their perpetrator to eternal punishment” — Aquinas treats formally at Summa Theologiae I-II q. 88, defining venial sin as a disorder that leaves the last end intact and mortal sin as one that turns away from it (source). That is the terminus of the development this timeline traces.
