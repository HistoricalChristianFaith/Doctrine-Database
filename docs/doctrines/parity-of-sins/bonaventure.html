Bonaventure on the parity of sins

Dates: c. 1267 (the Collations on the Ten Commandments, delivered at Paris in Lent) · Wikipedia
Summary: Bonaventure answers James 2:10 with a scholastic distinction the fathers had not drawn: the man who offends in one point incurs the full pain of loss — exclusion from God — but not the full pain of sense. So “guilty of all” is true in one currency of punishment and false in the other, and the verse can be affirmed without conceding that sins are equal.

Claims & evidence

Full pain of loss, graded pain of sense

“Whoever shall keep the whole law, yet offend in one point, is become guilty of all”: not that for one transgression he is punished with as much punishment of sense as for the transgression of all; but he will be punished with as much punishment of loss, yet not as severely for one as for all. Therefore, if anyone wishes to attain to true life, it is necessary that he have regard to all the commandments of God in heart, mouth, and deed.

— Collationes de Decem Praeceptis, Collation 6, source. The medieval terms are poena damni (the loss of the vision of God, which does not come in degrees — one is either excluded or not) and poena sensus (positive suffering, which does). Applied to James, this yields exactly the shape Augustine had argued for — guilt universal in respect of one thing, graded in respect of another — but relocates the universality from the object violated to the penalty incurred.

The practical upshot: fear God and keep all the commandments

You must always fear God, because he who “keeps the whole law but offends in one point has become guilty of all.” And in Deuteronomy it is said: “And now, Israel, what does the Lord your God ask of you, except that you fear him and keep all his commandments and walk in his ways?”

— Collationes de Septem Donis, Collation 2, source. Bonaventure is preaching, and the verse does in the pulpit what it does in James: it forecloses the calculation that would trade a small commandment for a large one.

Notes

  • The poena damni / poena sensus move is a genuine advance on the patristic answers, because it locates the universality of the guilt in something measurable. It also raises a difficulty the fathers avoided: if even one sin incurs the whole pain of loss, the verse has been made to say something about mortal sin, and the question of which sins are mortal becomes urgent — which is where Aquinas takes it.
