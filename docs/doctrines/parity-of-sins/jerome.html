Jerome on the parity of sins

Dates: c. 393 (Against Jovinianus) · Wikipedia
Summary: Jerome wrote what antiquity regarded as the Christian refutation of the parity of sins — Book II of Against Jovinianus, a hundred pages of scriptural argument for graded merit, graded guilt, and graded reward. Two things complicate the picture. First, the thesis Jovinian is on record as holding was equal reward, not equal sin; the parity charge is Jerome’s inference and his label. Second, twenty-three years later, asked directly by Augustine to explain James 2:10, Jerome declined to answer.

Claims & evidence

Jovinian’s four propositions, as Jerome states them

He says that “virgins, widows, and married women, who have been once passed through the layer of Christ, if they are on a par in other respects, are of equal merit.”

He endeavours to show that “they who with full assurance of faith have been born again in baptism, cannot be overthrown by the devil.”

His third point is “that there is no difference between abstinence from food, and its reception with thanksgiving.”

The fourth and last is “that there is one reward in the kingdom of heaven for all who have kept their baptismal vow.”

— Against Jovinianus I.3, source (also at New Advent). This is Jerome’s own summary of the position he set out to destroy, given before the polemic begins — and none of the four is the parity of sins. The fourth is about recompense.

Jovinian, refuted in his own words, on equal punishment

He who says to his brother, ‘thou fool,’ and ‘raca,’ will be in danger of Gehenna. And the murderer and the adulterer will likewise be sent into Gehenna. In times of persecution some are burnt, some strangled, some beheaded, some flee, or die within the walls of a prison: the struggle varies in kind, but the victors’ crown is one.

— Jovinian, quoted at Against Jovinianus II.20, source (also at New Advent). The argument runs from Matthew 5:22 and the parable of the equal penny: not that the insult and the murder are equally wicked, but that both incur Gehenna. It is a claim about outcomes.

Jerome’s charge: this is Zeno, not Christ

In sexual intercourse and full feeding he is an Epicurean; in the distribution of rewards and punishments he all at once becomes a Stoic, He exchanges Jerusalem for Citium, Judaea for Cyprus, Christ for Zeno. If we may not depart a hair’s breadth from virtue, and all sins are equal, and a man who in a fit of hunger steals a piece of bread is no less guilty than he who slays a man: you must, in your turn, be held guilty of the greatest crimes.

— Against Jovinianus II.21, source. Citium in Cyprus was Zeno’s birthplace. Note the structure of the sentence: Jerome ascribes the Stoic turn to Jovinian’s doctrine of rewards and punishments, and then draws “all sins are equal” out of it as what that doctrine amounts to. Elsewhere he states the ascription flatly — “For you admit no difference between sins” (II.31) — and turns it against Jovinian by pointing out that on such terms a lie would damn him alongside parricides.

Against parity: Scripture everywhere grades guilt, reward, and rank

But what are we to think of your assertion, that because there is a division into good and bad, the good, or the bad it may be, are not distinguished one from another, and that it makes no difference whether one is a ram in the flock or a poor little sheep?

— Against Jovinianus II.22, source. The positive case fills chapters 18–34: the thirtyfold, sixtyfold and hundredfold of the sower; “one star differeth from another star in glory”; Ezekiel’s judgment between cattle and cattle; the many mansions; the talents; and Christ’s own word to Pilate that another had “the greater sin.” It is the fullest patristic collection of graded-recompense proof-texts, and Augustine and Bede both simply defer to it.

Twenty-three years later, he declines to expound James 2:10

But a most trying time has come upon us, in which I have found it better for me to hold my peace than to speak, so that our studies have ceased … For this reason I have not been able at the present time to give to those two books dedicated to my name — books of profound erudition, and brilliant with every charm of splendid eloquence — the answer which I would otherwise have given; not that I think anything said in them demands correction, but because I am mindful of the words of the blessed apostle in regard to the variety of men’s judgments, “Let every man be fully persuaded in his own mind.”

— Letter 134.1 (a.d. 416; numbered Letter 172 among Augustine’s), source. The “two books” are Augustine’s Letter 166 on the origin of the soul and Letter 167 on James 2:10, carried east together by the presbyter Orosius. Jerome praises them, declares he finds nothing in them needing correction, and answers neither. The reason he gives is the Pelagian crisis and the danger that any daylight between himself and Augustine would be exploited — a strategic silence, not a considered agreement.

Notes

  • Whether Jovinian in fact held the parity of sins is treated at length on did Jovinian teach that all sins are equal? The short of it: the equal-recompense thesis is verified in Jovinian’s own quoted words; the parity-of-gravity label is Jerome’s construction upon it, and every later report — Augustine’s, Bede’s, Aquinas’s — descends from Jerome.
  • The rhetorical excess of Against Jovinianus was notorious in Jerome’s own lifetime; the book’s attack on marriage embarrassed his Roman friends enough that he had to defend it in Letter 49. That does not touch the force of Book II’s scriptural argument against parity, which Augustine calls “that most delightful and noble dissertation” — but it is the reason to read the Jovinian-as-Stoic charge with care.
