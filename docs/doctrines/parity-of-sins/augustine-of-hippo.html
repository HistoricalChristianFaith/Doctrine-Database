Augustine of Hippo on the parity of sins

Dates: c. 415 (Letter 167) · Wikipedia
Summary: Augustine wrote the fullest treatment the patristic period produced: a treatise-length letter to Jerome devoted entirely to James 2:10. He takes the falsity of the parity thesis as settled (Jerome had proved it) and asks the harder question — what, then, does “guilty of all” actually mean? His answer: to break any commandment is to violate the love on which all the commandments hang, so every sinner is guilty with respect to the whole; but he violates it more who sins more gravely, so guilt is at once universal and graded.

Claims & evidence

The problem, stated without flinching

How, then, I beseech you, are we to understand the words: “Whosoever shall keep the whole law, and yet offend in one point, he is guilty of all”? Does this affirm that the person who shall have committed theft, nay, who even shall have said to the rich man, “Sit thou here” and to the poor man, “Stand thou there,” is guilty of homicide, and adultery, and sacrilege?

— Letter 167.3, source (also at New Advent). Augustine insists on reading the verse in its context — the rich man’s gold ring and the poor man’s vile raiment of James 2:1–9 — which makes the difficulty sharper, not milder: the offence in view is a seating arrangement.

The parity of sins is a Stoic tenet, and Jerome has already refuted it

The doctrine of the equality of sins, however, the Stoics alone dared to maintain in opposition to the unanimous sentiments of mankind: an absurd tenet, which in writing against Jovinianus (a Stoic in this opinion, but an Epicurean in following after and defending pleasure) you have most clearly refuted from the Holy Scriptures.

— Letter 167.4, source. Augustine adds no independent evidence about Jovinian; he is repeating Jerome’s characterisation back to Jerome, as a compliment. That is worth noting when the same ascription later turns up in Bede and Aquinas — it is one report, thrice echoed.

Even the unity of the virtues would not entail the parity of sins

Even though it were true that he who has one virtue has all virtues, and that he who lacks one virtue has none, this would not involve the consequence that all sins are equal; for although it is true that where there is no virtue there is nothing right, it by no means follows that among bad actions one cannot be worse than another, or that divergence from that which is right does not admit of degrees.

— Letter 167.14, source. This is the letter’s sharpest philosophical move. The Stoics’ strongest argument for parity ran through the inseparability of the virtues; Augustine grants the premise for the sake of argument and denies the inference, because vices — unlike virtues — are not connected, two of them standing against each virtue (imprudence and craftiness against prudence, cowardice and hardihood against courage). He illustrates with Catiline, whose endurance of “cold, thirst, hunger” looked like courage and was hardihood, because it kept no company with prudence or justice.

Virtue admits of degrees: the cavern, not the whirlpool

The way to wisdom is therefore not like that of a man rising from the water into the air, in which, in the moment of rising above the surface of the water, he suddenly breathes freely, but, like that of a man proceeding from darkness into light, on whom more light gradually shines as he advances.

— Letter 167.13, source. The Stoic image had been a drowning man who is equally drowned an inch or a mile below the surface — hence no degrees, and hence no partial wisdom. Augustine substitutes a man walking out of a cave: light and darkness in the same soul at once, in changing proportion. This is the metaphysical engine of the whole graded doctrine, and he summarises it in a sentence: “no one lives here wholly without sin — one sins more, another less, and the best is the man who sins least.”

The solution: love is what all the commandments hang on, so any sin makes one guilty of all

Hence it is true that if a man shall keep the whole law, and yet offend in one point, he becomes guilty of all, because he does what is contrary to the love on which hangs the whole law. A man, therefore, becomes guilty of all by doing what is contrary to that on which all hang.

Why, then, may not all sins be said to be equal? May not the reason be, that the transgression of the law of love is greater in him who commits a more grievous sin, and is less in him who commits a less grievous sin?

— Letter 167.16–17, source. The two halves belong together: universality of guilt from the object violated (the one thing all the commandments depend on), gradation of guilt from the degree of the violation. He then reads James’s closing “mercy rejoiceth over judgment” as the daily remedy for the daily sins the argument has just exposed — forgive, and be forgiven.

The sin unto death: hatred of the brotherhood, not a fixed catalogue

The “sin,” therefore, of a brother, “unto death,” I suppose to be when, after the acknowledging of God through the grace of our Lord Jesus Christ, one fights against the brotherhood, and is set on by the fire-brands of hatred against the very grace through which he was reconciled to God. But “a sin not unto death” is when a person, not having alienated his love from his brother, yet through some infirmity of mind may have failed to exhibit the due offices of brotherhood.

— Ten Homilies on the First Epistle of John 10 (c. 407), source. The same love-criterion that explains James 2:10 also sorts 1 John’s two classes of sin: the deadly sin is the one that turns love itself into hatred. Augustine later added a caution in his Retractations — the definition holds only “if he ends this life in a perversity of mind as wicked as this,” since “we must not despair of anyone, no matter how wicked he is, while he lives.” That qualification is what separates his reading from Tertullian’s irremissible class.

Notes

  • Letter 167 travelled east with Letter 166 on the origin of the soul, carried by Orosius in 415. Augustine got no answer to either: see Jerome’s reply of 416, and the Jerome–Augustine timeline for the correspondence as a whole.
  • Augustine’s reading of “guilty of all” became the standard patristic and medieval one — it is repeated almost verbatim by Bede and, in the Greek tradition, by Theophylact. Whether it is the sense James intended is weighed at is “guilty of all” explained by the love-command?, against the rival reading at the unity of the law and the one Lawgiver.
