Clement of Alexandria on the parity of sins

Dates: c. 200 · Wikipedia
Summary: The earliest Christian witness surveyed here to state, in so many words, that Scripture teaches “the differences of sins.” Clement — the father most willing of any to speak well of Greek philosophy — takes the graded reading directly from 1 John 5:16–17, without polemic and without naming the Stoics.

Claims & evidence

John “manifestly teaches the differences of sins”

John, too, manifestly teaches the differences of sins, in his larger Epistle, in these words: “If any man see his brother sin a sin that is not unto death, he shall ask, and he shall give him life: for these that sin not unto death,” he says. For “there is a sin unto death: I do not say that one is to pray for it. All unrighteousness is sin; and there is a sin not unto death.”

— Stromata II, source. The context is a discussion of sin, ignorance, and repentance, and Clement is establishing that some faults admit of an easy remedy and others do not. The argument is purely exegetical: John distinguishes a sin unto death from a sin not unto death, therefore sins differ — and if they differ in remedy, they differ in gravity.

Notes

  • Clement is the point at which the wiki’s two proof-texts part company. The parity debate later fastens onto James 2:10, a verse that at first glance supports parity and has to be explained; the graded doctrine is meanwhile carried by 1 John 5:16–17, which supports it directly and needs no explaining. Every subsequent witness here works one side or the other, and Bede is the first to argue both in one commentary.
  • That the earliest explicit Christian statement of graded sin comes from Alexandria, and not as a polemic against Stoicism, is worth noting: Clement elsewhere borrows Stoic moral vocabulary freely. He simply does not find the parity thesis in his Bible.
  • 1 John 5:16’s “sin unto death” is the seed of the later mortal/venial distinction, worked out by Augustine and codified by Aquinas. It also carries a disciplinary question — whether the church may absolve the gravest sins at all — taken up by Tertullian, Cyprian, and Ambrose.
