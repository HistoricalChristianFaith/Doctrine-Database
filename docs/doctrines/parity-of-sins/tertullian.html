Tertullian on the parity of sins

Dates: c. 210 · Wikipedia
Summary: Tertullian turns 1 John 5:16–17 into a hard two-class division of sins — remissible and irremissible — and uses it to deny that the church may forgive the graver class at all. He is thus a witness against parity who is also, in the same breath, the beginning of the rigorist line that Cyprian and Ambrose will find themselves fighting.

Claims & evidence

Sins divide into the remissible and the irremissible

Touching this difference, we have not only already premised certain antithetical passages of the Scriptures, on one hand retaining, on the other remitting, sins; but John, too, will teach us: “If any knoweth his brother to be sinning a sin not unto death, he shall request, and life shall be given to him;” because he is not “sinning unto death,” this will be remissible. “(There) is a sin unto death; not for this do I say that any is to request” — this will be irremissible.

— On Modesty, source. On Modesty belongs to Tertullian’s Montanist period and is written against a bishop’s edict granting reconciliation to the sexually lapsed. The exegesis of a graded scale of sins is doing disciplinary work: because John withholds even prayer from the sin unto death, Tertullian holds, the church may withhold peace.

The prohibition on prayer is the point of the passage, not an aside

For (in making these assertions) he was looking forward to the final clause of his letter, and for that (final clause) he was laying his preliminary bases; intending to say, in the end, more manifestly: “If any knoweth his brother to be sinning a sin not unto death, he shall make request, and the Lord shall give life to him who sinneth not unto death. For there is a sin unto death: not concerning that do I say that one should make request.” He, too, (as I have been), was mindful that Jeremiah had been prohibited by God to deprecate (Him) on behalf of a people which was committing mortal sins.

— On Modesty, source. The appeal to Jeremiah’s prohibited intercession (Jeremiah 7:16) shows how far Tertullian will push the reading: not merely that some sins are graver, but that some sinners pass beyond the reach of the church’s prayer.

Notes

  • The distinction between grading sins and despairing of the graver ones is the fault-line running through this whole timeline. Cyprian keeps Tertullian’s grading and rejects his despair; the Novatianists Ambrose answers do the opposite — they refuse reconciliation while (Ambrose charges) reasoning about sin like Stoics who admit no grades at all.
  • Augustine later reads “the sin unto death” as the settled hatred of the brotherhood, and explicitly retracts any implication that a living man should be despaired of — the exegetical move that defuses Tertullian’s use of the verse.
  • Tertullian also appears on this wiki as a witness on the origin of the soul and purgatory.
