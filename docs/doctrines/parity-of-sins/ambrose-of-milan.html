Ambrose of Milan on the parity of sins

Dates: c. 384 (Concerning Repentance; the date is the Benedictine editors’ estimate, with a possible range of c. 384–394) · Wikipedia
Summary: Ambrose charges the Novatianists with holding the Stoic parity of sins, and turns the charge into a reductio: if all sins really are equal, the Novatianists have no principled ground for singling out apostasy as the one unforgivable offence. He reproduces Cicero’s stock illustration — the stolen fowl set beside the smothered father — almost word for word.

Claims & evidence

The Novatianists reason “after the fashion of the Stoics,” and the position is self-defeating

For when the Lord forgave all sins, He made an exception of none. But since, as it were after the fashion of the Stoics, they think that all sins are equal in gravity, and assert that he who has stolen a common fowl, as they say, no less than he who has smothered his father, should be for ever excluded from the divine mysteries, how can they select those guilty of one special offence, since even they themselves cannot deny that it is most unjust that the penalty of one should extend to many?

— Concerning Repentance I.5, source (also at New Advent). The argument is not that parity is false — that Ambrose treats as obvious — but that it is inconsistent with the Novatianists’ own practice. They deny reconciliation to the lapsed in particular; a doctrine on which no sin is graver than any other cannot yield a shortlist.

Rigorism, not clemency, is the real contempt for God’s power

They affirm that they are showing great reverence for God, to Whom alone they reserve the power of forgiving sins. But in truth none do Him greater injury than they who choose to prune His commandments and reject the office entrusted to them.

— Concerning Repentance I.6, source. As with Cyprian, the parity thesis is being refuted for the sake of a penitential discipline: only if sins differ can the church have a graded remedy for them.

Notes

  • The fowl-and-father pairing is Cicero’s from Pro Murena 61 (“he does not commit a less crime who kills a cock if there was no need to do so, than the man who strangles his father”). Ambrose’s “as they say” marks it as a received tag rather than something he has heard a Novatian say; the Latin refutation of parity is being conducted, in part, in Cicero’s own words. Bede inherits the same illustration three centuries later.
  • Whether the Novatianists actually held the parity of sins, or whether Ambrose is labelling their rigorism with a handy philosophical tag, is the same question raised by Jerome’s parallel charge against Jovinian — see did Jovinian teach that all sins are equal?
