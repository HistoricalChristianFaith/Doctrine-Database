Josephus on Who Killed Goliath?

Dates: c. 93 (the Antiquities of the Jews) · Wikipedia
Summary: The earliest witness to feel the difficulty. Retelling 1 Samuel 17, Josephus keeps David as the slayer of the named Goliath; but when he reaches the Philistine-giant wars of 2 Samuel 21 — the chapter whose verse 19 credits Elhanan with killing "Goliath" — he never lets the name Goliath recur. He renders Elhanan as "Nephan," David's kinsman, and leaves the giant Nephan kills anonymous. Whether by deliberate harmonizing or from the text he used, Josephus already smooths the crux out of existence.

Claims & evidence

David — not Elhanan — kills the named Goliath (retelling 1 Samuel 17)

"But the youth met his antagonist, being accompanied with an invisible assistant, who was no other than God himself. And taking one of the stones that he had out of the brook, and had put into his shepherd's bag, and fitting it to his sling, he slang it against the Philistine. This stone fell upon his forehead, and sank into his brain, insomuch that Goliath was stunned, and fell upon his face… So David ran, and stood upon his adversary as he lay down, and cut off his head with his own sword… but David carried the head of Goliath into his own tent."

— Antiquities of the Jews 6.189–192, trans. William Whiston, source. Josephus follows the standard narrative: the shepherd David fells the champion of Gath, and Goliath is named throughout.

At the 2 Samuel 21:19 parallel, "Nephan his kinsman" slays an unnamed Philistine

"When the king heard that the Philistines were gathered together at the city Gazara, he sent an army against them, when Sibbechai the Hittite, one of David's most courageous men, behaved himself so as to deserve great commendation, for he slew many of those that bragged they were the posterity of the giants… After which defeat, the Philistines made war again; and when David had sent an army against them, Nephan his kinsman fought in a single combat with the stoutest of all the Philistines, and slew him, and put the rest to flight."

— Antiquities of the Jews 7.301–302, trans. William Whiston, source. This is the crux verse, 2 Samuel 21:19: Josephus renders Elhanan as "Nephan," calls him David's kinsman, and — the decisive move — makes his victim "the stoutest of all the Philistines," without a name. The word "Goliath" does not appear anywhere in Book 7. The six-cubit, six-fingered giant of 2 Samuel 21:20–21 he likewise gives to "Jonathan, the son of Shimea," again unnamed as a "Goliath."

Notes

  • The first harmonization. Because the name Goliath is reserved for David's victim in Book 6 and withheld from Nephan/Elhanan's victim in Book 7, Josephus's narrative contains no contradiction at all — there is only one Goliath, and David killed him. He is thus the earliest known witness to the smoothing tradition later made explicit by the Targum (which turns Elhanan into David) and by the Hebrew text history that inserts "the brother of" into 2 Samuel 21:19.
  • Deliberate, or a variant text? It cannot be settled from Josephus alone whether he consciously suppressed the second "Goliath" or simply followed a Greek/Hebrew copy in which the giant of 2 Sam 21:19 was unnamed. The Septuagint here reads a form close to the Masoretic Text, so a deliberate authorial harmonization is the likelier explanation — but Josephus offers no comment either way.
  • Names. "Nephan" (Elhanan) and "Sibbechai" correspond to the champions of 2 Samuel 21:18–19; Josephus preserves the sequence of giant-battles while stripping the one clash of the name that would collide with his own Book 6.
