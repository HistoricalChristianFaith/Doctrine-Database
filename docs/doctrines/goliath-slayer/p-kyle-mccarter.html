P. Kyle McCarter on Who Killed Goliath?

Dates: 1984 (II Samuel, Anchor Bible) · Wikipedia
Summary: The Anchor Bible commentary gives the strong modern-critical form of the case. Not only is 2 Samuel 21:19 the older text and 1 Chronicles 20:5 a harmonizing correction — the tradition itself, on McCarter's reading, ran the other way from the familiar story: the slaying of Goliath was originally Elhanan's deed, later transferred to the famous David, exactly as "deeds of obscure heroes tend to attach themselves to famous heroes."

Claims & evidence

1 Chronicles 20:5 is "a scribal error with the effect of harmonizing" Samuel with 1 Samuel 17

"I Chron 20:5 has ʾt lḥmy ʾḥy glyt, 'Laḥmi, the brother of Goliath,' a scribal error with the effect of harmonizing this notice with I Samuel 17. See Driver."

— P. Kyle McCarter, II Samuel (Anchor Bible 9; Garden City: Doubleday, 1984), p. 449, textual note on 21:19. McCarter translates the verse "Elhanan, a Jearite from Bethlehem, slew Goliath the Gittite, the shaft of whose spear was like a weavers' heddle rod," emending the garbled patronymic and deleting the intruded "oregim."

The Elhanan tradition is older than the David tradition

"This giant, the shaft of whose spear was 'like a weavers' heddle rod' (cf. I Sam 17:7), was identified in tradition with an anonymous Philistine slain by David (I Samuel 17)… Deeds of obscure heroes tend to attach themselves to famous heroes, and there is no doubt that the tradition attributing the slaying of Goliath to Elhanan is older than that which credits the deed to David. The contradiction in the tradition was eliminated in the targumic and midrashic literature by identifying David with Elhanan…"

— McCarter, II Samuel, p. 450, NOTE on "Goliath the Gittite," source. On his account the champion of 1 Samuel 17 was originally anonymous, and the name "Goliath" was drawn into that story from the older Elhanan notice here — so the two "harmonizations" the tradition attempted (the Chronicler's "brother of Goliath"; the Targum's "Elhanan = David") are both secondary repairs of a seam that opened when Goliath's death was credited to David.

Notes

  • Samuel-is-primary camp, in its boldest form. McCarter carries the line of Wellhausen, Driver, and H. P. Smith to its conclusion: not merely that Samuel's text is prior, but that the historical slayer of Goliath was Elhanan, with the David attribution a later growth. This is the "strong" reading the argument pages weigh — sound as textual criticism, more venturesome as history.
  • Against the harmonizers. Where Kennicott and Keil & Delitzsch emend Samuel to Chronicles to keep David the sole slayer, McCarter reads the Chronicler's "brother of Goliath" as the very move that betrays the harmonizing hand.
