S. R. Driver on Who Killed Goliath?

Dates: 1890 (Notes on the Hebrew Text… of the Books of Samuel) · Wikipedia
Summary: The Oxford Regius Professor's Notes gives the classic argument for the reverse of Kennicott's direction. Weighing which of the two texts is prior, Driver holds it "scarcely credible" that a scribe with 1 Samuel 17 in front of him could have manufactured the difficult statement that Elhanan slew Goliath — whereas the Chronicler's motive to remove that difficulty is "obvious." The Samuel reading is primary; 1 Chronicles 20:5 is the harmonizing correction.

Claims & evidence

The harder reading (Samuel) is original; Chronicles corrects it

"It is evident that [ōrĕgîm] has found its way into the text here by accident from the line below, though the error must be older than LXX. But what of the other variants?… When the character of the two alternative readings is considered, it is difficult to resist the conclusion that the former is the more probable. It is scarcely credible that a scribe having before him a text identical with that of Ch., even supposing that some letters in it had become obliterated or obscure, could, with the knowledge of I 17 that he must have possessed, have so altered or emended it as to make it state that 'Elhanan the son of Yaʿir the Beth-lehemite slew Goliath of Gath!'… On the other hand, a motive for the correction of the text of Samuel by the Chronicler—or even by a copyist of the Chronicles—is obvious."

— S. R. Driver, Notes on the Hebrew Text and the Topography of the Books of Samuel (Oxford: Clarendon, 1890), pp. 272–273, source. The change from Samuel to Chronicles is not a single lost word ("brother of") — which would be easy to imagine — but the substitution of eth for aḥi and, "still more remarkable," of "the Beth-lehemite" for "Lahmi": a chain of alterations that only makes sense as a deliberate harmonization, not as accidental damage. He lists Ewald, Thenius, Bertheau, Wellhausen, and Kuenen on the same side.

Notes

  • Samuel-is-primary camp. Driver crystallizes Wellhausen's suggestion into the standard text-critical argument, on the principle of the harder reading: the difficult "Elhanan slew Goliath" is the sort of text scribes smooth away, not invent, so it is the original, and 1 Chronicles 20:5 is the Chronicler's fix. This is the line followed by H. P. Smith and McCarter against Kennicott and Keil & Delitzsch.
  • "Former/latter." Driver's own phrasing calls the Chronicles-is-a-correction option "the former"; read in context his verdict is unambiguous — Samuel is prior, Chronicles secondary.
  • Older than the Septuagint. His observation that the intruded "oregim" "must be older than LXX" shows the corruption was already present in the Hebrew behind the Greek by the second century BC — bearing on the broader question of how early such variants entered the text.
