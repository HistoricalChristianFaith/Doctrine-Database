Michael S. Heiser on Who Killed Goliath?

Dates: 2014 ("Clash of the Manuscripts: Goliath & the Hebrew Text of the Old Testament," Bible Study Magazine) · Wikipedia
Summary: The evangelical Hebrew scholar presents the crux to a popular audience as a textbook case of scribal transmission, not contradiction: 2 Samuel 21:19 is a "defective reading," the honest by-product of a scribe struggling with a damaged manuscript, and 1 Chronicles 20:5 preserves the material needed to see what happened. His point is apologetic in the good sense — the "contradiction" dissolves once the mechanics of copying are understood — while conceding, unlike the harmonizers, that the Masoretic verse as it stands is in error.

Claims & evidence

A copying mistake, propagated by a later scribe's repair

"Taking these oddities together suggests very strongly that somewhere along the chain of copying the text of 1 Sam 17 one scribe made a mistake in 2 Sam 21:19 that caused a subsequent scribe some trouble."

— Michael S. Heiser, "Who Killed Goliath — David or Elhanan?" (reproducing the Bible Study Magazine essay), source.

2 Samuel 21:19 is a "defective reading"; 1 Chronicles 20:5 resolves it

"The solution to the contradiction between 2 Sam 21:19 and 1 Chr 20:5 is recognizing that 2 Sam 21:19 is a defective reading since it is the result of a scribe's sincere effort to cope with a problematic manuscript."

— Heiser, "Who Killed Goliath," source. The step-by-step reconstruction that accompanies the essay — the "oregim"/weavers dittography, the confusion of the object-marker ʾet with ʾaḥi ("brother of"), and "Bethlehemite" (bet ha-laḥmi) versus the name "Lahmi" — is the classic account from Gleason Archer's Encyclopedia of Bible Difficulties, which Heiser follows; the three-slip mechanism should be credited to Archer, the framing to Heiser.

Notes

  • Direction of correction: toward Chronicles. Heiser sides with the reading that uses 1 Chronicles 20:5 ("Elhanan slew Lahmi the brother of Goliath") to repair the Samuel verse — the camp of Kennicott and Keil & Delitzsch, though he frames it as scribal accident rather than deliberate emendation. This is the opposite of the McCarter line (Samuel primary, Elhanan the original slayer).
  • Concedes a real error. Unlike the ancient harmonizers, Heiser grants that the Masoretic 2 Samuel 21:19 is genuinely defective — his defense of Scripture runs through the doctrine of transmission, not through denying the fault. That places him within the modern text-critical consensus that one of the two texts is corrupt, disputing only the direction.
  • Overreach to flag: Chronicles treated as the pristine original. Heiser's conclusion that 1 Chronicles 20:5 simply "resolves" the defective Samuel verse presumes Chronicles is the untouched witness by which Samuel is to be corrected — the weak form of the reading. The mainstream text-critical judgment is the reverse: Chronicles' "Lahmi, the brother of Goliath" is itself a secondary, harmonizing reading (Driver, H. P. Smith, and the "obvious harmonization" consensus), so it cannot serve as the yardstick. The David-keeps-the-kill reading survives this, but only in its stronger form — Ozoliņš's, where both texts descend from a common original naming Goliath's unnamed brother and each branch garbled it — not in Heiser's "correct Samuel from Chronicles" form. Weighed in full on the argument page, Is 2 Samuel 21:19 a scribal corruption, its original naming Goliath's brother? (assessment: contested).
