Rashi on Who Killed Goliath?

Dates: c. 1090 · Wikipedia
Summary: The greatest medieval Jewish commentator receives the Targum's solution and states it flatly: the Elhanan of 2 Samuel 21:19 is David, and the "weaver" patronymic describes the Temple curtains, not a father named Jaare.

Claims & evidence

"Elhanan — David… consequently this Elhanan must be David"

"Elchonon. Dovid. We know that it was Dovid who killed Golias in Shmuel I, 17:51, consequently this Elchonon must be Dovid.

The son of Yari [whose family] were weavers. His family weaved the partition curtain of the Beis Hamikdosh that is [also] called a forest. The Beis Hamikdosh is called a forest because it was made with planks of acacia wood…

Like that of the weaver's rod. [Yonoson translates:] 'Like the weaver's rod,' that is called 'ensouple' in O.F. Let a weaver come and stand up against a weaver['s instrument]."

— Rashi on II Samuel 21:19, via Sefaria (the translation transliterates the names Ashkenazi-style: "Dovid," "Golias," "Elchonon"). Rashi reasons directly from 1 Samuel 17:51 — since Scripture already says David killed Goliath, the Elhanan of 2 Samuel 21:19 must be David — and glosses the patronymic through the Targum's "weaver of the sanctuary" image.

Notes

  • The mainstream medieval Jewish reading. Rashi fixes the Targumic identification as the standard Jewish harmonization: one Goliath, killed by David, with 2 Samuel 21:19 a poetic restatement. It is this reading that Radak reports a century later — while candidly admitting he cannot explain why David would be called "Elhanan."
  • Against the modern verdict. The identification treats the two names as one person; modern text critics (Driver, H. P. Smith, McCarter) instead take Elhanan to be a distinct hero and the David-Goliath link to be the secondary tradition.
