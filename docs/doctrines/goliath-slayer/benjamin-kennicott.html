Benjamin Kennicott on Who Killed Goliath?

Dates: 1753 (The State of the Printed Hebrew Text… Dissertation the First) · Wikipedia
Summary: The Oxford Hebraist whose vast collation of Hebrew manuscripts founded modern Old Testament textual criticism turns 2 Samuel 21:19 into a set-piece demonstration that the received Hebrew text is not inerrant. The verse, he argues, is a copyist's corruption of the reading preserved in 1 Chronicles 20:5 — "Elhanan slew Lahmi the brother of Goliath" — the two texts differing far less in Hebrew than in English.

Claims & evidence

The contradiction proves a transcriber's mistake

"Now every one knows, that Goliath the Gittite was slain by David, and therefore there must have been a Mistake of some Transcriber here; since Goliath could not be slain also by Elhanan the son of Jaare Oregim."

— The State of the Printed Hebrew Text of the Old Testament Considered: Dissertation the First (Oxford, 1753), pp. 78–79, source.

1 Chronicles 20:5 is "plain and consistent"; Samuel is the corruption of it

"We read then, 1 Chron. 20, 5 — And Elhanan, the son of Jaor, slew Lahmi the Brother of Goliath of Gath &c. Here all is plain and consistent, and these words have evidently been corrupted into the words now found in Samuel."

— Dissertation the First, p. 79, source. Kennicott then reconstructs the mechanism letter by letter: Jair corrupted to Jaare, the object-marker eth confused with beth, a he inserted at the head of "Lahmi" to make the local name "Bethlehemite" (Beth-hallahmi), and "Lahmi" thereby swallowed up — while the first "Oregim" ("weavers") "most probably was inserted from the Line immediately under" the text being copied. The differences that look enormous in English are, in the Hebrew consonants, a short chain of scribal slips.

Notes

  • Chronicles-is-original camp. Kennicott holds that 1 Chronicles 20:5 preserves the true text and 2 Samuel 21:19 is the damaged copy — the same verdict Keil & Delitzsch would reach in the next century. The opposite camp (Wellhausen, Driver, H. P. Smith, McCarter) reverses the direction — Samuel's "Elhanan slew Goliath" is primary, and the Chronicler harmonized it — but both camps agree with Kennicott's foundational point that the Masoretic text of Samuel here is corrupt.
  • Significance for the wider charge. That a leading eighteenth-century Christian Hebraist could argue so bluntly that the printed Hebrew text contained a scribal error made this verse a standard exhibit in debates over the integrity of the Masoretic Text — the terrain of the Septuagint-vs.-Masoretic question. Kennicott's own aim was conservative: locate the slip, recover the reading, not impugn Scripture.
  • Reference-tradition witness. His verdict is summarized in McClintock & Strong's Cyclopedia (s.v. "Elhanan"): "the differences between them being much smaller in the original than in English (see Kennicott, Dissertation, page 78)."
