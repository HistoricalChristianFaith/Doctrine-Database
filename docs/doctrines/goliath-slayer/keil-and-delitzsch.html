Keil & Delitzsch on Who Killed Goliath?

Dates: c. 1875 (the Biblical Commentary on the Books of Samuel) · Wikipedia
Summary: The standard conservative Lutheran commentary of the nineteenth century reaches the same conclusion as Kennicott by the opposite temperament: not to concede the fallibility of the Masoretic text but to restore it. The two verses, they observe, are nearly identical in Hebrew once the stray "weavers" is removed; the difference is a copyist's error, and the reading of 1 Chronicles 20:5 is the correct one, "since the assumption that there were two Goliaths is a very improbable one."

Claims & evidence

The two texts share one original; the difference is a copying error

"The words of our text are so similar to those of the Chronicles, if we only leave out the word ārgîm, which probably crept in from the next line through oversight on the part of a copyist, that they presuppose the same original text, so that the difference can only have arisen from an error in copying."

— Keil & Delitzsch, Biblical Commentary on the Books of Samuel, on 2 Samuel 21:18–19, via Bible Hub.

The Chronicles reading is the true one; "two Goliaths" is improbable

"The majority of the expositors (e.g., Piscator, Clericus, Michaelis, Movers, and Thenius) regard the text of the Chronicles as the true and original one, and the text before us as simply corrupt… Consequently we must regard the reading in the Chronicles as the correct one, and alter our text accordingly; since the assumption that there were two Goliaths is a very improbable one."

— Keil & Delitzsch, on 2 Samuel 21:19, via Bible Hub. They accept that the Masoretic verse must be emended to match Chronicles — "Elhanan… slew Lahmi the brother of Goliath" — so that David remains the only slayer of Goliath himself.

Notes

  • Chronicles-is-original camp. With Kennicott, Keil & Delitzsch hold that 1 Chronicles 20:5 preserves the original and Samuel is corrupt — the reading that keeps a single Goliath, killed by David. The rival camp (Wellhausen, Driver, H. P. Smith, McCarter) argues the reverse direction, that Chronicles is the harmonizing correction.
  • The agreed sub-point. Like every serious treatment, they grant that the first ōregim ("weavers") is an accidental repetition from the "weaver's beam" ending the verse.
  • Text note. The English is the standard Clark/Eerdmans translation as reproduced by Bible Hub, which carries no pagination; the substance is verified against the printed commentary.
