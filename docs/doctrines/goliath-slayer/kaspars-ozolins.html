Kaspars Ozoliņš on Who Killed Goliath?

Dates: 2022 ("Who killed Goliath? The puzzling text of 2 Samuel 21:19," Tyndale House) · Tyndale House
Summary: The Tyndale House linguist gives the most careful recent treatment, and resists the tidy resolutions of both camps. He lays out the three logical possibilities and judges the most plausible reconstruction to be the one the harmonizers half-saw: Elhanan the Bethlehemite really did kill a Philistine giant — the brother of Goliath — who was himself left unnamed, so that neither David's Goliath nor a second Goliath is in view. He also cautions that scribes "rarely made deliberate changes," tempering the malice-and-conspiracy framing.

Claims & evidence

Three options for the relation of Samuel and Chronicles

"(1) 'Lahmi' really was the name of the Philistine giant, and therefore 'Bethlehemite' has later crept into the text of Samuel, (2) Elhanan really was a Bethlehemite, and therefore we have a scribal error at Chronicles (i.e., 'Lahmi' wasn't the name of the Philistine giant), or (3) both were true: Elhanan was a Bethlehemite, and 'Lahmi' really was the name of the giant he slew."

— Kaspars Ozoliņš, "Who killed Goliath?" (Tyndale House, 14 April 2022), source.

His preferred reconstruction: Elhanan killed the (unnamed) brother of Goliath

"Elhanan the Bethlehemite actually killed the brother of Goliath, who himself was left unnamed."

— Ozoliņš, "Who killed Goliath?", source. He does not claim certainty — "we are still left with uncertainty as to the identity of Goliath's killer" — and he stresses that "scribes copying the Hebrew Bible rarely made deliberate changes to their text," so the divergence is better read as accident than as tendentious editing.

Notes

  • Neither pure camp. Ozoliņš does not simply crown Chronicles (against Kennicott) or Samuel (against McCarter). On his preferred reconstruction both texts descend from a shared original in which Elhanan the Bethlehemite killed Goliath's unnamed brother — Samuel losing the "brother of" (ʾaḥî misread as the object-marker ʾet), while Chronicles' "Lahmi" is not a genuine name but a scribal accident, the place-name "the Bethlehemite" (ha-laḥmî) mistaken for a proper noun (laḥmî). The corruption lies in how each branch garbled the shared source, and of the three options he lays out he sets aside the one on which "Lahmi" really was the giant's name.
  • Restraint on motive. His warning against assuming deliberate scribal change is a direct check on the stronger "the Jews altered the text" readings — the same caution the modern text-critical consensus applies to the wider Masoretic-vs.-Septuagint charge.
  • The latest witness. Ozoliņš closes the timeline as its most recent voice, and the most explicit that the honest verdict is a measured uncertainty rather than a slogan for either "David killed Goliath, full stop" or "Elhanan did."
