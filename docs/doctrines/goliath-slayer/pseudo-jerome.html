Pseudo-Jerome (Hebrew Questions) on Who Killed Goliath?

Dates: traditionally ascribed to Jerome (c. 400); the Quaestiones Hebraicae in libros Regum et Paralipomenon is now generally judged spurious (pseudo-Jerome), of uncertain, probably later date · Wikipedia
Summary: The Christian-Latin form of the Targum's solution. Read for centuries as Jerome's, this set of "Hebrew Questions" identifies the Elhanan of 2 Samuel 21:19 with David by translating "Elhanan" as Adeodatus ("God-given") and arguing from the Hebrew etymologies — and from the chapter's closing note that all the giants "fell by the hand of David" — that Adeodatus simply is David.

Claims & evidence

"Adeodatus ipse est David" — Elhanan is David

"…percussit Adeodatus filius saltus Polymitarius Bethlehemites Goliath Gethaeum… Adeodatus ipse est David. Idcirco dicitur Adeodatus, quia a Deo est electus in regnum. Filius saltus, quia de saltu ubi oves pascebat, est eductus… Bethlehemites, quia… idem locus, domus panis vocatus est. Quod vero Adeodatus ipse sit David, sequentia declarant, ubi ait: Hi quatuor nati sunt de Arespha in Geth, et ceciderunt in manus David, et servorum ejus."

— [Pseudo-]Jerome, Quaestiones Hebraicae in libros Regum et Paralipomenon, on 2 Regum (2 Samuel) 21:19; Migne, Patrologia Latina 23, col. 1361, via Latin Wikisource. Rendered: "…Adeodatus, the son of the wood, the weaver, the Bethlehemite, struck down Goliath the Gittite… Adeodatus is David himself. He is called Adeodatus ['God-given'] because he was chosen by God for the kingdom. 'Son of the wood,' because he was led out from the woodland where he pastured the sheep… 'Bethlehemite,' because that place was called the house of bread. That Adeodatus is David himself the following words declare, where it says: These four were born to the giant in Gath, and fell by the hand of David and of his servants." The parallel note on 1 Chronicles 20:5 (PL 23, col. 1385) reads the man as "Elhanan son of Jair" who "killed Goliath the Gittite, the brother of Goliath the Philistine whom David killed."

Notes

  • Authorship. These Quaestiones on Kings and Chronicles circulated under Jerome's name and were quoted as his (so McClintock & Strong, s.v. "Elhanan"), but they are absent from Jerome's genuine corpus and are now classed among the spurious/dubious works (auctor incertus). The identification is therefore best labeled "pseudo-Jerome"; the traditional c. 400 date is nominal, and its real date is uncertain.
  • Etymological, not text-critical. Like the Targum and Rashi, this reading keeps the consonants and turns "Jaare-oregim the Bethlehemite" into a bundle of epithets for David — the harmonizing solution the modern critics (Driver, McCarter) reject in favour of two distinct heroes.
  • Note the tension within the work. The Chronicles comment (PL 23:1385) accepts the "brother of Goliath" harmonization — David killed Goliath; Elhanan killed Goliath's brother — while the Samuel comment (PL 23:1361) instead identifies Elhanan with David; the two Christian solutions to the crux sit, unreconciled, in the same book.
  • Weighed as an argument. The "Adeodatus ipse est David" identification is the Christian-Latin form of the reading assessed on its own page, Is "Elhanan" in 2 Samuel 21:19 simply another name for David himself? (assessment: weak) — steelmanned via the biblical two-name pattern, but resting on an equation no text makes and told against by a distinct Bethlehemite Elhanan in David's Thirty (2 Samuel 23:24) and the Gob setting of David's later wars.
