The Taylor Prism — Sennacherib's Annals, the "caged bird" account — on the Historicity of Sennacherib's Siege of Jerusalem

Dates: the prism inscribed c. 691 BC (by its colophon); the campaign it records fought in 701 BC · Wikipedia
Summary: A six-sided baked-clay foundation prism from Nineveh whose account of Sennacherib's third campaign (701 BC) names Hezekiah of Judah, boasts of taking 46 fortified cities, claims to have shut Hezekiah up "like a caged bird" in Jerusalem, and lists a heavy tribute — but never claims Jerusalem itself was captured. It is the central external witness to the siege: an Assyrian record, independent of the Bible, that corroborates the campaign, the siege, and the tribute while saying nothing of the deliverance the Bible reports. It must be read as royal propaganda inscribed roughly a decade after the event, and its own date (691 BC) kept firmly distinct from the event's (701 BC) — the prism is not a 701 BC "eyewitness" object. What it attests is set out here; what it does and does not establish about the biblical account is weighed in the Arguments section, not asserted here.

Claims & evidence

What it is: a foundation prism of Sennacherib's annals, in three near-identical copies

The object is a hexagonal (six-sided) baked-clay foundation prism about 38 cm high, inscribed on all six faces with more than 500 lines of Neo-Assyrian cuneiform recording the military campaigns of Sennacherib (king of Assyria, 705–681 BC). Prisms of this kind were buried in the foundations of royal buildings as legitimation texts, not written for public reading. Three near-complete copies of this final edition of the annals survive: the Taylor Prism in the British Museum (registration 1855,1003.1, a.k.a. BM 91032; dated 691 BC); the Oriental Institute (Chicago) Prism (OIM A2793; dated 689 BC), now held by the Institute for the Study of Ancient Cultures at the University of Chicago; and the Jerusalem Prism in the Israel Museum (acquired 1970; also 691 BC). The three carry substantially the same text; it is the Chicago copy that D. D. Luckenbill edited and translated as the standard edition.1

Discovery and provenance: Nineveh, 1830; the British Museum, 1855

The Taylor Prism was not scientifically excavated. It was acquired at Nineveh in 1830 by Colonel Robert Taylor, British resident at Baghdad, decades before controlled excavation of the Assyrian mounds; it remained in his possession, and the British Museum purchased it from his widow in 1855. Its date is fixed not by the campaign it narrates but by its own colophon — the scribal note giving the eponym year — which places the writing of this copy in 691 BC, about a decade after the 701 BC campaign. That gap matters: the prism is a retrospective, edited royal record, not a dispatch from the field.2

The third-campaign passage: Hezekiah "like a caged bird"

The third campaign (701 BC) occupies a long section covering Phoenicia, Philistia, and Judah, and builds to the Hezekiah passage. In Luckenbill's standard translation:

As for Hezekiah, the Jew, who did not submit to my yoke, 46 of his strong cities, as well as the small cities in their neighborhood, which were without number—…I besieged and took (those cities). 200,150 people, great and small, male and female, horses, mules, asses, camels, cattle and sheep, without number, I brought away from them and counted as spoil. Himself, like a caged bird I shut up in Jerusalem, his royal city. Earthworks I threw up against him—the one coming out of the city-gate, I turned back to his misery.

…As for Hezekiah, the terrifying splendor of my majesty overcame him, and the Urbi (Arabs) and his mercenary(?) troops which he had brought in to strengthen Jerusalem, his royal city, deserted him. In addition to the 30 talents of gold and 800 talents of silver (there were), gems, … couches of ivory, house chairs of ivory, elephant hide, ivory …, all kinds of valuable (heavy) treasures, as well as his daughters, his harem, his male and female musicians, (which) he had (them) bring after me to Nineveh, my royal city. To pay tribute and to accept (lit. do) servitude, he dispatched his messenger(s).

— Sennacherib's Annals, third campaign, D. D. Luckenbill translation, The Annals of Sennacherib, Oriental Institute Publications 2 (Chicago: University of Chicago Press, 1924), 11–12. (Ellipses mark words elided for readability, not alteration; the bracketed queries are Luckenbill's own, flagging uncertain renderings.)3

A record of success — and a conspicuous silence about Jerusalem

The passage is boastful, not penitent: it records Judah devastated, 46 cities taken, a vast deportation counted as spoil, Hezekiah's own land carved up and handed to the kings of Ashdod, Ekron, and Gaza, and Hezekiah himself reduced to a tribute-paying vassal who sent gold, silver, and even his daughters and harem to Nineveh. This is the Assyrian version of a victory. Yet on the one point that would have crowned that victory the prism falls silent: it describes the siege-works thrown up around Jerusalem, but it never claims the city was taken — no capture, no sack, no deportation of Hezekiah, no Assyrian governor installed. For a text that trumpets the fall of forty-six other cities, the omission is loud, and it matches the Bible's own account of a city besieged but not captured (2 Kings 19:32–36). Luckenbill himself remarked that the unusually "full and detailed" description of the investment is "a sure sign that the victory claimed was not at all decisive." What that silence does and does not prove is a distinct question, weighed under Arguments, not settled here.4

The earliest recension: the Rassam Cylinder (c. 700 BC)

The prisms are not the first form of this account. The earliest surviving recension of the third-campaign narrative is the Rassam Cylinder, a barrel-shaped clay cylinder written c. 700 BC — only a year or so after the campaign — from which the later, fuller prism editions were expanded. Because the annals grew and were polished across successive editions, several historians (among them Mordechai Cogan) argue that a sober historical evaluation of the campaign should rest on this earliest recension rather than on the more elaborated prisms of 691 and 689 BC. The core Hezekiah data — the 46 cities, the shutting-up of Hezekiah, and the tribute — are already present in the Rassam Cylinder; the prisms carry the same substance in a later, more finished form.5

Notes

  • The prism's date is not the event's date. The Taylor Prism was inscribed c. 691 BC (the Chicago copy c. 689 BC), a decade after the 701 BC campaign; a common apologetic slip treats the prism as a 701 BC eyewitness document. It is nothing of the kind — it is a retrospective royal foundation text, and its evidential value has to be assessed as such.
  • It corroborates the frame, not the miracle. The prism independently confirms the campaign against Hezekiah, the siege of Jerusalem, and the tribute Judah paid — and, by its silence, the non-capture of the city. It says nothing of any Assyrian catastrophe, the 185,000 dead of 2 Kings 19:35, or the deliverance the Bible attributes to the angel of the LORD. The external record reaches the edge of the biblical story's climax and stops there.
  • It is royal propaganda, and several load-bearing claims turn on that. Whether the annals can be trusted as history rather than legitimation rhetoric; whether the prism's silence on Jerusalem's capture positively corroborates the biblical non-capture or is merely an argument from silence; whether the tribute figures agree or diverge from 2 Kings 18:14 (which gives 300 talents of silver, not 800); and what, if anything, external evidence can say about the cause of the Assyrian withdrawal — each is weighed on its own page under Arguments. This detail page is the attestation; the adjudication of what it establishes lives there.
