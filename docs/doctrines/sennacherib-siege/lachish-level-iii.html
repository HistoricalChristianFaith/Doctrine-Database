Tel Lachish, Level III — the destruction layer and the excavated Assyrian siege ramp — on the Historicity of Sennacherib's Siege of Jerusalem

Dates: destruction 701 BC (the physical stratum of Sennacherib's assault); excavated by the Wellcome–Marston expedition 1932–1938 and by David Ussishkin's renewed excavations 1973–1994 · Tel Lachish
Summary: Where the Assyrian palace art at Nineveh and the biblical narrative meet the dirt. Tel Lachish, Judah's second city, preserves the burnt Level III destruction of 701 BC and — against its south-west corner — the only Assyrian siege ramp ever excavated, together with the defenders' counter-ramp, over a thousand Assyrian arrowheads and slingstones, and the debris of the storming. It is a distinct body of evidence from the Lachish reliefs (Assyrian palace art carved at Nineveh): here is the ground those reliefs depict. The Bible places Sennacherib at Lachish while he pressed Jerusalem (2 Kings 18:14; 2 Chron 32:9). The stratum corroborates the campaign and the fall of Lachish specifically — not the deliverance of Jerusalem. What it attests is set out here; what it does and does not establish about the biblical account is weighed in the Arguments section, not asserted here.

Claims & evidence

The only excavated Assyrian siege ramp: the south-west corner of the mound

Piled against the steep south-west slope of the tell is a great wedge of stone and packed earth — the only Assyrian siege ramp ever discovered, the oldest siege ramp known anywhere. Masses of quarried boulders were heaped against the corner of the mound to raise a firm, even approach up which wheeled battering rams could be pushed against the city wall; the surface was faced with a layer of hard plaster to bear the machines. The scale is enormous: estimates run from roughly 13,000 to 19,000 tons of stone (some popular accounts put it far higher), spread up to about 75 m wide at the base, quarried from a nearby hillside and moved by hand. This is the physical correlate of the ramp carved on the Nineveh reliefs and of the biblical "Sennacherib…against Lachish": the carved ramp and the excavated ramp are the same event from two sides.1

The counter-ramp, the weapons, and the burnt city

The defenders answered the assault from inside. David Ussishkin's renewed excavations (1973–94) traced a counter-ramp the Judahites threw up within the wall, opposite the Assyrian ramp, to raise the defence as the enemy heightened his own mound — the Assyrians raised theirs in turn, and broke through. Near the top of the ramp Ussishkin's team recovered the material signature of the fighting: more than a thousand iron arrowheads, masses of sling-stones, a length of iron chain and a perforated stone — a pendulum the defenders swung to foul the battering rams — fragments of Assyrian armour scales, and a stratum of collapsed mudbrick burnt so hard it had partly vitrified. Beneath and within it lay the ash of a city stormed and fired. The whole of Level III is this destruction, sealed across the mound.2

The pit of bodies

Near the site the earlier Wellcome–Marston expedition cleared a rock-cut pit heaped with human remains — by the excavators' count on the order of 1,500 disarticulated skeletons, jumbled together with animal bone (including pig) and burnt debris, and evidently a mass reburial gathered up after the destruction rather than an orderly cemetery. Whatever its precise circumstances, it answers grimly to the scale of slaughter the reliefs picture — files of captives, the impaled, the deported — and to a city that fell by storm.3

Dating the destruction to 701 BC — a real interpretive achievement

That Level III is Sennacherib's 701 BC destruction was not obvious at first, and the dating is worth laying out because it was genuinely worked for. The 1930s Wellcome–Marston expedition under James Leslie Starkey (murdered in 1938 on the road to Jerusalem, the dig cut short) left the stratigraphy contested; his colleague Olga Tufnell, publishing the results over the following two decades, argued that the main destruction — Level III — belonged to Sennacherib in 701 BC, with the later Level II destruction being Nebuchadnezzar's in 586 BC. Others placed the great destruction at 586 and compressed the sequence. Ussishkin's renewed excavations settled the mainstream view decisively on Tufnell's side: the two burnt cities are distinct, Level III = 701 and Level II = 586, and the clinching evidence is the pottery and the LMLK storage jars. More lmlk-stamped handles have come from Lachish than from any other site, and all of them — stamped and unstamped royal jars alike — belong to the horizon sealed beneath the Level III destruction, i.e. to Hezekiah's reign, immediately before the Assyrian conquest. The stratum thus dates the jars and the jars date the stratum: a firm terminus at 701 BC.4

What the ground does — and does not — corroborate

The destruction layer is firm external evidence that the 701 BC campaign was real, that Lachish was besieged by a siege ramp and battering rams, stormed, burnt, and its people slaughtered or deported — the very event the Bible records when it stations Sennacherib "against Lachish" and has him send the Rabshakeh from there to Jerusalem (2 Kings 18:14, 18:17; 2 Chron 32:9). But the same asymmetry holds here as with the reliefs: this is the ground of a city Sennacherib did take. Jerusalem, which he did not take, has no such destruction layer to show — a besieged-but-unfallen city leaves no burnt stratum. The excavated ramp and the burnt bricks reach the campaign and the fall of Lachish, not any Assyrian catastrophe, not the 185,000 dead of 2 Kings 19:35, and not the lifting of the siege of Jerusalem. The external record here is powerful and pointed — and scoped.5

Notes

  • The ground, distinct from the palace art. The Lachish reliefs are Assyrian propaganda carved on a throne-room wall at Nineveh; the Level III stratum is the excavated tell in Judah. They corroborate each other — the carved ramp and the dug ramp are the same siege — but they are two different kinds of witness, and the tightness of their agreement is part of what makes the correlation strong.
  • The dating is an achievement, not an assumption. "Level III = 701" is the settled mainstream reading, but it was reached against a real alternative (a single great destruction at 586), and it rests on the stratigraphy and the LMLK jars, not on harmonizing with the Bible. That the jars sit sealed beneath the burnt layer is what fixes the date.
  • It corroborates Lachish, not Jerusalem. A city that fell leaves a destruction layer; a city that held does not. The stratum is the strongest kind of physical confirmation for the campaign and for the fall of Lachish, and by its nature says nothing about why the siege of Jerusalem lifted — which is weighed under Arguments.
