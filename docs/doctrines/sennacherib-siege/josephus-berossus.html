Josephus, Antiquities 10.1, citing Berossus — the Hellenistic and Jewish transmission — on the Historicity of Sennacherib's Siege of Jerusalem

Dates: the Antiquities of the Jews completed c. AD 93/94 (Flavius Josephus, c. AD 37–100); it quotes the Babyloniaca of Berossus, a priest of Bel-Marduk at Babylon, written c. 281 BC (and now lost); the campaign both transmit belongs to Sennacherib's western war of 701 BC · Antiquities 10.1 (Whiston)
Summary: The latest witness on this page, and a doubly-layered one: the Jewish historian Josephus, writing near the end of the first century AD, retells the campaign and the destruction of the Assyrian army, and does what no earlier source on this page does — he gathers his witnesses and weighs them, citing both Berossus the Babylonian and Herodotus the Greek, and correcting Herodotus for calling Sennacherib king "of the Arabians" rather than of the Assyrians. Its value is therefore not as an independent early record — Josephus stands seven centuries downstream of 701 BC and leans on the biblical account, on Berossus, and on Herodotus — but as a transmission datum: it shows the campaign remembered as history across the Babylonian, Greek, and Jewish traditions at once, and it is the earliest source to knit the extra-biblical notices to the biblical 185,000 and the pestilence. What the passage says, and how far its layers can be trusted, is set out here; the deliverance and the cause of the Assyrian withdrawal are weighed in the Arguments section, not asserted here.

Claims & evidence

The narrative: the pestilence, the 185,000, and the king's death

In the first chapter of Book 10 of the Antiquities, Josephus retells the events of 2 Kings 18–19 and Isaiah 36–37 in his own expansive Greek: Sennacherib invades in the fourteenth year of Hezekiah, takes the fortified cities, sends Rabshakeh to demand Jerusalem's surrender, and marches south only to be checked at the Egyptian frontier — Josephus keeps the biblical datum that he "heard that Tirhaka, King of the Ethiopians, was coming and bringing great forces to aid the Egyptians," and so "left Pelusium, and returned back without success." The destruction of the army Josephus then reports as a quotation (see below), in words that track the biblical account closely:

…God had sent a pestilential distemper upon his army: and on the very first night of the siege an hundred fourscore and five thousand, with their captains and generals, were destroyed. So the King was in a great dread, and in a terrible agony at this calamity; and being in great fear for his whole army, he fled with the rest of his forces to his own Kingdom, and to his city Nineveh. And when he had abode there a little while, he was treacherously assaulted, and died by the hands of his elder sons Adrammelech and Sarasar: and was slain in his own temple, which was called Araske. Now these sons of his were driven away on account of the murder of their father by the citizens, and went into Armenia. While Assarachoddas took the Kingdom of Sennacherib.

Every element here answers to 2 Kings: the pestilential stroke and the figure of 185,000 (2 Kings 19:35), the flight to Nineveh, and the murder by the sons Adrammelech and Sharezer, after which Esarhaddon (Josephus's "Assarachoddas") reigned (2 Kings 19:36–37).1

Josephus's two witnesses: Berossus of Babylon and Herodotus — and a correction

What sets this passage apart from every other on the page is that Josephus does not merely narrate; he marshals corroborating witnesses and adjudicates between them. He appeals first to Herodotus, then to Berossus, and openly faults the Greek where he thinks him wrong:

Now concerning this Sennacherib Herodotus also says, in the second book of his Histories, "How this King came against the Egyptian King, who was the Priest of Vulcan: and that as he was besieging Pelusium, he broke up the siege on the following occasion. This Egyptian Priest prayed to God, and God heard his prayer; and sent a judgment upon the Arabian King:" but in this Herodotus was mistaken, when he called this King not King of the Assyrians, but of the Arabians. For he saith, that "A multitude of mice gnawed to pieces in one night both the bows, and the rest of the armour of the Assyrians: and that it was on that account that the King, when he had no bows left, drew off his army from Pelusium." And Herodotus does indeed give us this history. Nay and Berosus, who wrote of the affairs of Chaldea, makes mention of this King Sennacherib; and that he ruled over the Assyrians, and that he made an expedition against all Asia and Egypt; and says thus.

This is source-criticism of a rudimentary but real kind: Josephus recognizes that Herodotus's "Sanacharib king of the Arabians" (see the Herodotus witness on this page) is the same Sennacherib, keeps what he takes to be sound in the Greek (the failed Egyptian campaign, the sudden reverse), and rejects what he takes to be error (the ethnic mislabelling). And it is the earliest place where the three streams — Babylonian (Berossus), Greek (Herodotus), and Jewish (Josephus's own biblical inheritance) — are drawn together and compared on the page of one author.2

What is securely Berossan, and what is the biblical account restated

Here the page must be careful, because Josephus's "and says thus" introduces the destruction-quotation above — so that in his presentation the pestilence and the 185,000 arrive under the name of Berossus. That layering will not bear the full weight readers sometimes place on it. The Babyloniaca is lost; it survives only as quoted, at several removes, by Josephus, Polyhistor, Eusebius, and Syncellus, and the Assyrian portions of that classical tradition are thought to have been contaminated by the unreliable Ctesias rather than drawn straight from cuneiform. What can be assigned to Berossus with confidence is the bare notice Josephus states in his own voice — that Sennacherib "ruled over the Assyrians, and that he made an expedition against all Asia and Egypt." The circumstantial destruction-narrative that follows — Rabshakeh, the pestilence, the exact figure of 185,000, the murder by the named sons — matches 2 Kings 19 point for point and is best judged Josephus's own biblical material worked into the citation, not an independent Babylonian record of a plague. So the pestilence strand is not here externally confirmed by Berossus; it is the biblical claim restated within a passage Josephus attributes to him.3

The one genuinely independent point of contact: Sennacherib's murder by his sons

One detail in the passage is corroborated outside the Bible, and it is worth marking as the strongest thing here. Sennacherib's death by assassination at the hands of a son — the biblical Adrammelech and Sharezer (2 Kings 19:37) — and the succession of Esarhaddon are independently attested in Babylonian records: the Babylonian Chronicle notes that on the 20th of Tebet Sennacherib was killed by his son in a rebellion, and Esarhaddon's own inscriptions describe seizing the throne after his brothers murdered their father in 681 BC. This is a real, non-biblical confirmation of a specific biblical claim — though it should be kept in proportion: it corroborates the king's violent end and the succession, events some twenty years after 701 BC, not the siege of Jerusalem or its deliverance.4

What it does — and does not — corroborate

Handled honestly, Josephus is a witness to the tradition's reach and handling, not an independent witness to the event. Writing c. AD 94, seven centuries after the campaign, he depends on the biblical narrative, on Berossus, and on Herodotus; his page is valuable because it shows that by the first century AD the campaign was remembered as real history in Babylonian, Greek, and Jewish letters alike, and because he himself compares his sources and corrects one of them. But its layered attributions must not be misread as independent confirmation of the miracle: the pestilence and the 185,000 come to us here as the biblical claim restated (dressed, in Whiston's text, in a Berossus citation whose secure core is only that Sennacherib ruled the Assyrians and campaigned against Asia and Egypt), and the cause of the Assyrian withdrawal is exactly what is disputed. The genuinely independent datum in the passage is Sennacherib's later assassination, which the Babylonian sources confirm. Like every witness on this page, Josephus reaches the frame of the story — a real king, a real campaign against Judah and Egypt, a real violent death — not its supernatural climax; and he reaches even the frame as a late compiler, not a contemporary. What weight the deliverance and the withdrawal can bear is weighed under Arguments.5

Notes

  • Late and dependent. The Antiquities was finished c. AD 93/94 — roughly 795 years after 701 BC. Josephus is a transmission witness: he retells 2 Kings/Isaiah and cites earlier historians, not a fresh source for the campaign. His worth is as evidence of the tradition's reach and of his own source-critical handling.
  • Two witnesses, one corrected. Josephus explicitly invokes both Herodotus (2.141) and Berossus, and faults Herodotus for calling Sennacherib "king of the Arabians" instead of "of the Assyrians" — an early instance of a historian weighing and emending his sources.
  • The "Berossus" plague quotation is source-critically fraught. Whiston's text folds the pestilence, the 185,000, and the king's death into the passage Josephus ascribes to Berossus ("and says thus"). Because every detail there tracks 2 Kings 19, the securely-Berossan content is only the bare notice that Sennacherib ruled the Assyrians and campaigned against Asia and Egypt; the destruction-narrative is best read as Josephus's biblical material. The Babyloniaca itself is lost and its Assyrian portions may be Ctesias-contaminated.
  • The one externally-confirmed detail is the assassination. Sennacherib's murder by a son and Esarhaddon's accession (681 BC) are corroborated by the Babylonian Chronicle and Esarhaddon's own inscriptions — a genuine, non-biblical point of contact, but one about the king's death, not the siege or the deliverance of Jerusalem.
