The Siloam Inscription — Hezekiah's Tunnel and the water-works before the siege — on the Historicity of Sennacherib's Siege of Jerusalem

Dates: inscription cut c. 701 BC (palaeographically late 8th century BC, in Hezekiah's reign, c. 715–687 BC); discovered 1880; Istanbul Archaeology Museum from 1890 · Siloam inscription
Summary: A six-line Paleo-Hebrew inscription found near the Pool of Siloam outlet of Hezekiah's Tunnel — the roughly 533 m (1,750 ft) rock-cut channel that carried Gihon Spring water inside Jerusalem's walls. The text commemorates the moment two digging teams, tunnelling from opposite ends, met in the middle. It is the material trace of the kind of siege-preparation the Bible credits to Hezekiah before the Assyrian threat (2 Kings 20:20; 2 Chron 32:2–4, 30). It is a Judahite witness — the defender's side — complementing the Assyrian prisms and reliefs. What it attests is set out here; what it does and does not establish about the biblical account is weighed in the Arguments section, not asserted here.

Claims & evidence

What it is: six lines cut where the diggers met

The Siloam inscription is a six-line text incised in Paleo-Hebrew script (a regional variant of the Phoenician alphabet, with words divided by dots) into the rock wall of a tunnel roughly six metres from the southern, Pool-of-Siloam outlet. The tunnel — Hezekiah's Tunnel, also called the Siloam Tunnel — is a serpentine, roughly 533 m (1,750 ft) rock-cut channel that carried the water of the Gihon Spring, which lay outside the city wall in the Kidron Valley, through the ridge of the City of David to the Pool of Siloam inside the walls. The inscription does not record who ordered the work or why; it commemorates the engineering feat itself — the dramatic meeting, deep in the rock, of two gangs of quarrymen who had begun at opposite ends and cut toward each other.1

The text: "the voice of a man calling to his fellow"

In W. F. Albright's standard rendering (as printed in Ancient Near Eastern Texts), with the damaged opening in brackets, the inscription reads:

… [when] (the tunnel) was driven through. And this was the way in which it was cut through: While … (were) still … axe(s), each man toward his fellow, and while there were still three cubits to be cut through, [there was heard] the voice of a man calling to his fellow, for there was an overlap in the rock on the right and on the left. And when the tunnel was driven through, the quarrymen hewed (the rock), each man toward his fellow, axe against axe; and the water flowed from the spring toward the reservoir for 1,200 cubits, and the height of the rock above the head(s) of the quarrymen was 100 cubits.

— the Siloam inscription, trans. W. F. Albright. The two figures the text supplies — a channel of 1,200 cubits and rock 100 cubits deep above the diggers — are the tunnel's own account of its scale.2

Discovery and provenance: 1880 in the tunnel; Istanbul since 1890

The inscription was found in 1880 by a teenage pupil of the architect Conrad Schick — later identified as Jacob Eliahu Spafford — who was wading through the tunnel and stumbled onto the text near the Siloam outlet. In 1890, under Ottoman rule, the inscribed slab was cut from the rock (it cracked into pieces in the removal), and it was taken to Constantinople; it is today in the Istanbul Archaeology Museum. Israel has repeatedly requested its return (in 1998, 2007, 2017, and 2022), and Turkey has consistently declined, treating it as Ottoman-era cultural property — so the object remains the subject of an open repatriation dispute.3

The biblical tie: Hezekiah's water-works against the Assyrian threat

The Bible twice credits Hezekiah with exactly such a project. 2 Kings 20:20 notes that Hezekiah "made a pool, and a conduit, and brought water into the city"; 2 Chron 32:30 says he "stopped the upper watercourse of Gihon, and brought it straight down to the west side of the city of David"; and 2 Chron 32:2–4 frames the work as siege preparation — when Hezekiah saw that Sennacherib "was come, and that he was purposed to fight against Jerusalem," he took counsel to "stop the waters of the fountains which were without the city… Why should the kings of Assyria come, and find much water?" Hezekiah's Tunnel is precisely a work that hides the spring outside the wall and delivers its water within — and the inscription is the tunnel's own contemporary label. It corroborates the biblical notice that Jerusalem's water supply was engineered inward under Hezekiah, i.e. the plausibility of a city bracing for siege.4

What it does — and does not — corroborate

The inscription is firm evidence that a major water tunnel of the right kind was cut through the City of David ridge in the late 8th century BC. But two limits must be stated plainly. First, the inscription names no king — not Hezekiah, not anyone. It is a workmen's commemoration of the breakthrough, not a royal foundation monument; its attachment to Hezekiah rests on the convergence of the Paleo-Hebrew script's date, the tunnel's function, and the biblical notices — a strong inference, but an inference, not a signature. It should not be presented as a signed Hezekiah inscription. Second, it bears on the siege only obliquely. It corroborates the water-works Scripture attributes to Hezekiah and the general picture of a city preparing for Assyrian attack — not the campaign's course, the siege of Jerusalem, or its outcome, and certainly not the deliverance the Bible reports. It is the defender's material context for 701 BC, complementing the Assyrian prisms and reliefs from the attacker's side, and what it can and cannot settle is weighed under Arguments.5

Notes

  • The inscription does not name Hezekiah or any king. Its date and function tie it to his reign inferentially (Paleo-Hebrew script + the tunnel's purpose + the biblical water-works notices), not by any named attribution. Treat it as a strong correlation, not a royal signature.
  • It corroborates the siege-preparation, not the siege's climax. The tunnel attests a city bringing its water inside the walls against a threat — the plausibility of the frame of the story — and says nothing of the 185,000 dead, the Assyrian withdrawal, or Jerusalem's deliverance.
  • Its own date is distinct from 701 BC. Palaeography places the letters in the late 8th century BC and so within Hezekiah's reign; the tunnel is a siege-preparation work rather than a record made during the siege itself.
