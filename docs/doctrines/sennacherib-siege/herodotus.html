Herodotus, Histories 2.141 — the field-mice and the Assyrian rout — on the Historicity of Sennacherib's Siege of Jerusalem

Dates: the Histories composed c. 440 BC (Herodotus of Halicarnassus, c. 484–425 BC); the events it garbles belong to Sennacherib's western campaign of 701 BC · Perseus (Godley, Loeb)
Summary: The earliest surviving literary echo of a sudden Assyrian reverse in Sennacherib's day — but a Greek one, gathered from Egyptian priests two-and-a-half centuries after the fact. Herodotus tells how the Egyptian priest-king Sethos, threatened by "Sanacharib, king of the Arabians and of the Assyrians," met the invasion at Pelusion on Egypt's north-east frontier, where a host of field-mice swarmed the Assyrian camp overnight, gnawed the soldiers' bowstrings, quivers, and shield-straps, and left them to be routed disarmed. It is regularly adduced as an independent, non-biblical trace of the disaster that ended Sennacherib's invasion (2 Kings 19:35) — but it is a weak witness, and the reasons must be stated with it: Herodotus sets the rout in Egypt, against Egyptians, not at Jerusalem against Judah, and the story is legendary in form. What the passage says is set out here; what evidential weight it can bear is weighed in the Arguments section, not asserted here.

Claims & evidence

The passage: Sethos, the mice at Pelusion, and the mouse-bearing statue

In the second book of the Histories (Euterpe), among the reigns the Egyptian priests recited to him, Herodotus reports that after the blind king came the priest of Hephaistos, Sethos, who had slighted the warrior caste — so that when the Assyrian came, the soldiers would not fight for him, and the god (in a dream) promised him helpers instead. In G. C. Macaulay's translation:

After this, Sanacharib king of the Arabians and of the Assyrians marched a great host against Egypt. Then the warriors of the Egyptians refused to come to the rescue, and the priest, being driven into a strait, entered into the sanctuary of the temple and bewailed to the image of the god the danger which was impending over him… Trusting in these things seen in sleep, he took with him… those of the Egyptians who were willing to follow him, and encamped in Pelusion, for by this way the invasion came: and not one of the warrior class followed him, but shop-keepers and artisans and men of the market. Then after they came, there swarmed by night upon their enemies mice of the fields, and ate up their quivers and their bows, and moreover the handles of their shields, so that on the next day they fled, and being without defence of arms great numbers fell. And at the present time this king stands in the temple of Hephaistos in stone, holding upon his hand a mouse, and by letters inscribed he says these words: "Let him who looks upon me learn to fear the gods."

The story is self-consciously a temple-legend: Herodotus flags it as what "the Egyptians and the priests … made the report," and the mouse-bearing statue with its pious motto is offered as the local proof. The kernel that draws readers to it is the shape of the disaster — a great Assyrian army, on the very edge of its western advance, destroyed overnight by no human hand and put to flight without a battle.1

The mouse as a plague-symbol — the bridge to 2 Kings 19:35

What makes the tale more than a curiosity is an ancient symbolic convention: the mouse was read as a token of pestilence. Apollo the plague-sender bears the cult-title Smintheus ("mouse-god") in the opening of the Iliad, where he looses a plague on the Greek camp with his arrows (Iliad 1.39); and in the Hebrew Bible the Philistines, struck by a plague while they hold the captured Ark, send it back with golden images of "your…mice that mar the land" alongside golden tumours — "for one plague was on you all" (1 Samuel 6:4–5). Read through that convention, Herodotus's gnawing mice are taken by many modern writers to encode a pestilence that swept the Assyrian camp — the same kind of sudden mortality the Bible ascribes to the angel of the LORD who "smote in the camp of the Assyrians a hundred fourscore and five thousand" (2 Kings 19:35). On that reading the two accounts would be independent memories of one event: a plague that broke Sennacherib's army, remembered in Judah as a divine deliverance and in Egypt as the miracle of the mice.2

Wrong country, wrong enemy — the governing caveat

That bridge, however, is built by the modern reader, not by Herodotus, and its joints are loose. Herodotus places the reverse in Egypt — at Pelusion, the north-eastern gateway of the Nile Delta — and makes its victim an Egyptian priest-king defending Egypt, not Hezekiah defending Jerusalem. Judah, Hezekiah, and Jerusalem do not appear; the deliverance is credited to Hephaistos (Ptah) in an Egyptian temple, not to the God of Israel. The mouse→plague→"this is the 185,000" chain is a scholarly reconstruction laid over the text: neither Herodotus nor the Bible mentions the other's detail — Herodotus names no plague and no 185,000, and the biblical and Assyrian accounts name no mice. What the two share is only the abstract shape "an Assyrian army suddenly destroyed near the Egyptian frontier," which is exactly the sort of memorable reversal a temple tradition would attach to a famous name.3

Legendary in form — and perhaps not even Sennacherib's

Historians treat the passage as an aetiological legend: a story told to explain a standing monument (the mouse-bearing statue) and to point a moral ("learn to fear the gods"), of the same folkloric kind as the divine dream, the cowardly soldiers, and the providential vermin. Some go further and doubt that the underlying event is Sennacherib's 701 BC campaign at all. Sennacherib never conquered Egypt; the deep Assyrian thrusts into Egypt and against the Delta belong to his successors Esarhaddon (who took Memphis in 671 BC) and Ashurbanipal — so an Egyptian tradition of an Assyrian invasion repelled at Pelusion may preserve a memory of the 670s, with the more famous name "Sanacharib" attached secondarily. Others suspect the mouse-motif migrated from the Greek cult of Apollo Smintheus in the Troad onto an Egyptian frame. On any of these readings the tale is a folk memory refracted through Egyptian priests and a Greek pen, not a documentary record; its very confusions — wrong king, wrong country, wrong enemy — are what make it evidentially thin.4

What it does — and does not — corroborate

Handled honestly, the passage is a genuine but slight datum. It shows that, within roughly two-and-a-half centuries of 701 BC, a tradition circulated in Egypt of a great Assyrian army under "Sanacharib" that came to sudden, non-military ruin on the edge of the Nile Delta — a tradition independent of the Hebrew scriptures, and one whose plague-coded imagery is at least consonant with the mass mortality 2 Kings reports. That is not nothing; an independent echo of a sudden Assyrian catastrophe is worth having. But it reaches no further. It does not confirm the siege of Jerusalem (which it never mentions), the figure of 185,000, the angel of the LORD, or even that the disaster befell Sennacherib rather than a later king. It is an indirect and garbled parallel, not clean corroboration — and it is precisely this witness whose evidential weight is weighed, alongside the biblical plague/angel language and the medical-epidemiological readings, under Arguments. Like every witness on this page it reaches, at most, the frame of the story, not its supernatural climax — and it reaches even the frame only obliquely, and in the wrong country.5

Notes

  • The date of the telling and the date of the event are far apart. Herodotus wrote c. 440 BC; the campaign he garbles was 701 BC (or, on the alternative reading, the Assyrian invasions of Egypt in the 670s–660s). The passage is a mid-fifth-century record of a temple tradition, not a contemporary report of either.
  • The mouse→plague reading is an inference, not a datum. Herodotus writes only of mice gnawing equipment; the identification of the mice as a cipher for pestilence rests on the ancient symbol-convention (Apollo Smintheus; the golden mice of 1 Samuel 6), and the further tie to the 185,000 of 2 Kings 19:35 is the modern reader's, made over silences on both sides.
  • Wrong country, wrong enemy. The rout is at Pelusion in Egypt, against Egyptians, delivered by an Egyptian god — not at Jerusalem, not against Judah, not by the God of Israel. This is the single most important caveat and the reason the witness is rated weak.
  • It may not even be Sennacherib. Because Sennacherib did not invade Egypt but Esarhaddon and Ashurbanipal did, some historians locate the kernel in the 670s–660s BC, with "Sanacharib" a secondary attachment. The page states the Sennacherib connection as the traditional reading, not as established fact.
