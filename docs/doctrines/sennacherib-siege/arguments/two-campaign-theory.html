Were there two Assyrian campaigns against Hezekiah, not one?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: plausible — the two-campaign reading is a serious minority position, held by real scholars on a genuine tension in the text: a submission-and-tribute block sits beside a defiance-and-deliverance block as though two invasions with opposite endings had been sewn together, and "Tirhakah king of Cush" (2 Kings 19:9) marches out against Sennacherib though Egyptian records put Taharqa's accession a decade later, at c. 690 BC. The theory is coherent and not refuted — no Assyrian record positively excludes a later action in the undated years after 689 BC. But it is outweighed: the annals attest only one western war against Hezekiah, a vassal rebellion against a peak-strength Assyria c. 688 BC is historically implausible, the tribute gap is trivial, and the Tirhakah datum is neutralized by a proleptic title without needing a second campaign at all. So the reading is coherent and seriously held, but short of the majority · a long-standing scholarly minority theory, not a popular apologetic claim

Related arguments: one or two campaigns — see also A single 701 BC campaign, and Tirhakah as a proleptic title

The crux

"… the Kushite ruler who came to the aid of the Judean forces was Tirhakah (=Taharqa), King of Kush, who ascended the throne of Egypt-and-Kush only eleven years later, in 690 BCE… Is the information that he was present in the events of 701 BCE an anachronism or is it historically reliable? … Did Sennacherib conduct two campaigns against the Levant, as some have suggested?"

— Dan'el Kahn, "Tirhakah, King of Kush and Sennacherib," Journal of Ancient Egyptian Interconnections 6 (2014). Two puzzles in the biblical narrative feed the theory. First, the account seems to report two opposite outcomes: 2 Kings 18:14–16 has Hezekiah submit at Lachish and buy Sennacherib off with the temple's gold — the surrender the Assyrian prism boasts of — while 2 Kings 18:17–19:37 has the Rabshakeh, Hezekiah's defiance, and a miraculous deliverance with no payment at all. Second, at 2 Kings 19:9 (and Isaiah 37:9) "Tirhakah king of Cush" marches out against Sennacherib — yet Egyptian records put Taharqa's accession at c. 690 BC, eleven years after 701.1

The case for two campaigns

The theory is old and has been held by serious scholars — George Rawlinson (1858), K. Fullerton, W. F. Albright (1953), S. H. Horn (1966), John Bright (whose A History of Israel defends it in an excursus), and, in the fullest recent form, William H. Shea ("Sennacherib's Second Palestinian Campaign," JBL 104 [1985]; "Jerusalem Under Siege," BAR 25:6 [1999]). Its case rests on the two puzzles above. The tribute: the prism lists 30 talents of gold and 800 talents of silver, and 2 Kings agrees on the gold (30) but gives only 300 talents of silver — and, more tellingly, the biblical surrender-and-tribute block sits uneasily beside the defiant-standoff-and-deliverance block, as though two campaigns with opposite endings had been sewn together. The chronology: if Tirhakah is genuinely "king" when he confronts Sennacherib, the confrontation must fall after his 690 BC accession — so the deliverance belongs to a hypothesized second campaign, c. 688–686 BC, for which no dated Assyrian inscription survives (the annals fall silent after 689 BC). On this reading the anachronism is not an error to be explained away but a chronological marker the harmonizers have missed.2

Adversarial assessment

Where it is outweighed. The mainstream single-campaign reading — adjudicated on its own page — is the stronger position, and it is stronger for a solid reason: the Assyrian evidence attests exactly one campaign against Hezekiah and no trace of a reconquest, and a vassal rebellion in 688 BC — against an Assyria then at its zenith, having just razed Babylon — is historically implausible; Mordechai Cogan calls the picture of "the mighty Assyrian army marching in defeat … with the soldiers' families and possessions in tow" "ludicrous." The two props do not carry the load they are asked to. The tribute figures are weak: the gold agrees exactly (30 talents), and a 300-vs-800 silver gap is the ordinary stuff of a boastful royal annal versus a defeated kingdom's own record (propaganda inflation, or Judahite versus Assyrian weight-standards), not the fingerprint of a separate war. And the Tirhakah datum — the theory's load-bearing prop — is defused without any second campaign: a proleptic royal title (calling the later king "king" at an earlier moment) is a genuine, well-attested narrative convention, so the confrontation need not fall after 690 BC at all. Once the anachronism is read as a convention rather than a marker, the case for splitting the account in two loses its sharpest edge.3

Why it is not refuted. The honesty of the matter lies in not overselling the rebuttal. The two-campaign theory is outweighed, not refuted: it is held by real scholars on a real tension in the text, and no Assyrian record positively excludes a later action in the undated years after 689 BC — the annals' silence there cuts both ways, since royal inscriptions routinely omit the king's setbacks. The theory does not depend on the Egyptian chronology being unsettled, but it is at least kept alive by the fact that the smoothest single-campaign harmonizations — the ones that station a battle-ready, twenty-year-old Taharqa at Eltekeh in 701 — rest on disputed synchronisms rather than fixed data (weighed on the single-campaign page). None of that rescues two campaigns as the better reading. It means only that the minority position is coherent and seriously argued, not a dead one.4

Assessment

Plausible — a serious minority reading, coherent but outweighed. Two invasions telescoped into one narrative would explain the account's double outcome and let "Tirhakah king of Cush" be a literal king, and the theory has been defended by real scholars from Rawlinson to Shea on a genuine tension in the text. But it is short of the majority for good reasons: the Assyrian annals know only one war against Hezekiah, a rebellion against a peak-strength Assyria c. 688 BC is implausible, the tribute discrepancy is trivial, and the Tirhakah "anachronism" — the theory's strongest prop — is neutralized by a proleptic title that needs no second campaign. What keeps the reading plausible rather than weak is that it is outweighed, not refuted: the post-689 BC years are undated, and Assyrian silence about a setback is no witness against one. Treat the single 701 campaign as the probable reading and the anachronism as harmless; treat the two-campaign theory as the coherent minority it is — and flag as overreach any claim that it has been positively disproved.
