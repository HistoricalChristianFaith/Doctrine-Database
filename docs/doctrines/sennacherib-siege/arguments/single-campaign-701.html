A single 701 BC campaign, and Tirhakah as a proleptic title

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: sound — the mainstream reading is a single 701 BC campaign told through several sources, and the account's historicity does not hinge on the Tirhakah crux: calling him "king" in 701 — a decade before his 690 BC accession — is a proleptic title, a normal narrative convention, and he was plausibly present as a young Kushite commander. The Assyrian record attests exactly one western war against Hezekiah and no reconquest, a vassal rebellion against a peak-strength Assyria c. 688 BC is implausible, and the tribute gap is trivial — so the account is historically coherent as one campaign, and the proleptic title defuses the anachronism whatever the Egyptian chronology proves. That core is well-grounded and near-consensus. Keep one caveat: the further harmonizations that station a battle-ready Taharqa at 701 (a securely-fixed birth-date, the Shabaka–Shebitku co-regency) rest on disputed synchronisms — they are the reconstruction, not the load-bearing defence, which is the proleptic title and needs none of that machinery · the mainstream scholarly reading, not a popular apologetic claim

Related arguments: one or two campaigns — see also Were there two Assyrian campaigns against Hezekiah, not one?

The crux

"… the Kushite ruler who came to the aid of the Judean forces was Tirhakah (=Taharqa), King of Kush, who ascended the throne of Egypt-and-Kush only eleven years later, in 690 BCE… Is the information that he was present in the events of 701 BCE an anachronism or is it historically reliable?"

— Dan'el Kahn, "Tirhakah, King of Kush and Sennacherib," Journal of Ancient Egyptian Interconnections 6 (2014). Two features of the biblical narrative drive the debate. First, the account seems to report two opposite outcomes: 2 Kings 18:14–16 has Hezekiah submit and pay tribute, while 2 Kings 18:17–19:37 has defiance and a miraculous deliverance with no payment. Second, at 2 Kings 19:9 (and Isaiah 37:9) "Tirhakah king of Cush" marches out against Sennacherib — yet Egyptian records put Taharqa's accession at c. 690 BC, eleven years after 701. The two-campaign theory reads these tensions as the seam between two invasions telescoped into one narrative; this reading takes a single 701 campaign told through several sources, with the royal title applied to Taharqa ahead of the fact.1

The case for one campaign

The majority of Assyriologists and Egyptologists — K. A. Kitchen, Frank Yurco, James Hoffmeier, and, decisively for the biblical side, Mordechai Cogan — read a single campaign in 701 BC, the three biblical units being diverse witnesses to one event rather than records of two. Two arguments carry it. First, the external record knows only one western campaign: Sennacherib's annals report the third campaign (701) and nothing like a later Judahite war, and by 689 BC Assyria was at the height of its power (it had just razed Babylon) — Cogan calls the picture of "the mighty Assyrian army marching in defeat … with the soldiers' families and possessions in tow" "ludicrous," and concludes it "is best to interpret the Biblical text as recounting a single campaign of Sennacherib waged in 701 B.C.E." Second, the Tirhakah title need not force a later date at all. Kitchen's point is that the whole two-campaign edifice rests on this one word, and that ancient (and modern) narrators routinely name a person by a title he acquired later — the proleptic title, calling the prince by the crown he would wear. And on the best-supported Egyptian chronology Taharqa really was present in 701: his own Kawa Stela V has him coming north as a "twenty-year-old recruit" summoned by his brother Shebitku, which (on a birth c. 722 BC) makes him old enough to command the Egyptian-Kushite force at Eltekeh. The Hebrew even fits: he is "king of Cush," a Kushite prince acting for Pharaoh Shebitku, not yet "king of Egypt." So the narrative is coherent as one campaign, and the anachronism dissolves into a convention.2

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The single-campaign reading is the mainstream position, and it is the stronger one for a solid reason: the Assyrian evidence attests exactly one campaign against Hezekiah and no trace of a reconquest, and a vassal rebellion in 688 BC — against an Assyria then at its zenith — is historically implausible. The tribute figures are a weak prop for the rival two-campaign reading: the gold agrees exactly (30 talents), and a 300-vs-800 silver gap is the ordinary stuff of a boastful royal annal versus a defeated kingdom's own record (propaganda inflation, or Judahite versus Assyrian weight-standards), not the fingerprint of a separate war. Most importantly, the "damaging anachronism" charge is itself weak: a proleptic royal title is a genuine and well-attested narrative convention, and it defuses the Tirhakah objection regardless of how the Egyptian chronology is settled — a narrator can call the later king "king" at an earlier moment without error. On its central claim — that the account is historically coherent as one 701 campaign — this reading is probable, and the mainstream.

Where it overreaches. The honesty of the case lies in not claiming more than the evidence has settled. The load-bearing defence is the proleptic title, which needs no Egyptological machinery at all. The specific mechanism that stations a battle-ready Taharqa at 701 is another matter, and it is genuinely contested. Macadam's older chronology dated his birth c. 710 BC, making him a boy of about nine in 701 — the very reading that first fed the anachronism charge; the revised c. 722 birth-date that yields "age twenty" is derived from a disputed synchronism (when to date his northward journey and Shebitku's accession), not an independent datum, and the Tang-i Var inscription (c. 706 BC, showing Shebitku already reigning) unsettles Kitchen's dates further. Yurco's Shabaka–Shebitku co-regency — the device that lets Taharqa march in 701 — has no direct inscriptional support and has been largely abandoned; critics fairly call it a modern fix for an ancient problem. Kahn's 2014 study frames the presence of Taharqa in 701 as an open question of anachronism-versus-reliability rather than a closed one. None of this weakens the single-campaign reading — because that reading does not depend on the machinery — but it does mean the smoothest harmonizations (a twenty-year-old general via a securely-fixed chronology) claim more than the Egyptian evidence has settled, and should be flagged as overreach.3

Assessment

Sound — the mainstream reading, load-bearing on the proleptic title, not the Egyptological machinery. A single 701 BC campaign told through several sources is the better-supported reading: the annals know only one war against Hezekiah, a 688 BC rebellion against a peak-strength Assyria is implausible, the tribute discrepancy is trivial, and the Tirhakah "anachronism" is not damaging — a proleptic royal title is a normal convention, and it neutralizes the objection whatever the Egyptian chronology turns out to be. On that much the account is historically coherent, and the two-campaign theory is the minority. The one caveat keeps the reading honest: the harmonizations that go beyond the proleptic title — a twenty-year-old Taharqa commanding at Eltekeh, the Shabaka–Shebitku co-regency — rest on disputed synchronisms and an evidentially unsupported co-regency, so they are a reconstruction, not settled fact. Trust the one-campaign reading as the probable one and the anachronism as harmless; flag as overreach any claim that the Egyptian chronology has been nailed down. The account's coherence does not depend on winning that Egyptological fight — which is precisely why the fight can stay open without damaging it.
