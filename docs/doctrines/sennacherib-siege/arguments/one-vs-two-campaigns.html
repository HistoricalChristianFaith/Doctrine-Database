One Assyrian campaign or two — and is Tirhakah a damaging anachronism?

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — the mainstream reading is a single 701 BC campaign, and the account's historicity does not hinge on the Tirhakah crux: calling him "king" in 701 — a decade before his 690 BC accession — is a proleptic title, a normal narrative convention, and he was plausibly present as a young Kushite commander. That much is well-grounded. But the question is not closed: the two-campaign theory survives as a serious minority reading of the tribute-then-defiance tension, and the Egyptian-chronology harmonizations that station a battle-ready Taharqa at 701 are themselves disputed — so the case is strong by the Assyrian records' lights and still snagged on Egyptian chronology · a long-standing scholarly debate, not a popular apologetic claim

The crux

"… the Kushite ruler who came to the aid of the Judean forces was Tirhakah (=Taharqa), King of Kush, who ascended the throne of Egypt-and-Kush only eleven years later, in 690 BCE… Is the information that he was present in the events of 701 BCE an anachronism or is it historically reliable? … Did Sennacherib conduct two campaigns against the Levant, as some have suggested?"

— Dan'el Kahn, "Tirhakah, King of Kush and Sennacherib," Journal of Ancient Egyptian Interconnections 6 (2014). Two puzzles in the biblical narrative generate the whole debate. First, the account seems to report two opposite outcomes: 2 Kings 18:14–16 has Hezekiah submit at Lachish and buy Sennacherib off with the temple's gold — the surrender the Assyrian prism boasts of — while 2 Kings 18:17–19:37 has the Rabshakeh, Hezekiah's defiance, and a miraculous deliverance with no payment at all. Second, at 2 Kings 19:9 (and Isaiah 37:9) "Tirhakah king of Cush" marches out against Sennacherib — yet Egyptian records put Taharqa's accession at c. 690 BC, eleven years after 701. One school reads these tensions as the seam between two distinct invasions telescoped into one narrative; the mainstream reads a single 701 campaign told through several sources, with the royal title applied to Taharqa ahead of the fact. This page weighs both.1

The two-campaign reading

The theory is old and has been held by serious scholars — George Rawlinson (1858), K. Fullerton, W. F. Albright (1953), S. H. Horn (1966), John Bright (whose A History of Israel defends it in an excursus), and, in the fullest recent form, William H. Shea ("Sennacherib's Second Palestinian Campaign," JBL 104 [1985]; "Jerusalem Under Siege," BAR 25:6 [1999]). Its case rests on the two puzzles above. The tribute: the prism lists 30 talents of gold and 800 talents of silver, and 2 Kings agrees on the gold (30) but gives only 300 talents of silver — and, more tellingly, the biblical surrender-and-tribute block sits uneasily beside the defiant-standoff-and-deliverance block, as though two campaigns with opposite endings had been sewn together. The chronology: if Tirhakah is genuinely "king" when he confronts Sennacherib, the confrontation must fall after his 690 BC accession — so the deliverance belongs to a hypothesized second campaign, c. 688–686 BC, for which no dated Assyrian inscription survives (the annals fall silent after 689 BC). On this reading the anachronism is not an error to be explained away but a chronological marker the harmonizers have missed.2

The one-campaign reading and the Tirhakah harmonization

The majority of Assyriologists and Egyptologists — K. A. Kitchen, Frank Yurco, James Hoffmeier, and, decisively for the biblical side, Mordechai Cogan — read a single campaign in 701 BC, the three biblical units being diverse witnesses to one event rather than records of two. Two arguments carry it. First, the external record knows only one western campaign: Sennacherib's annals report the third campaign (701) and nothing like a later Judahite war, and by 689 BC Assyria was at the height of its power (it had just razed Babylon) — Cogan calls the picture of "the mighty Assyrian army marching in defeat … with the soldiers' families and possessions in tow" "ludicrous," and concludes it "is best to interpret the Biblical text as recounting a single campaign of Sennacherib waged in 701 B.C.E." Second, the Tirhakah title need not force a later date at all. Kitchen's point is that the whole two-campaign edifice rests on this one word, and that ancient (and modern) narrators routinely name a person by a title he acquired later — the proleptic title, calling the prince by the crown he would wear. And on the best-supported Egyptian chronology Taharqa really was present in 701: his own Kawa Stela V has him coming north as a "twenty-year-old recruit" summoned by his brother Shebitku, which (on a birth c. 722 BC) makes him old enough to command the Egyptian-Kushite force at Eltekeh. The Hebrew even fits: he is "king of Cush," a Kushite prince acting for Pharaoh Shebitku, not yet "king of Egypt." So the narrative is coherent as one campaign, and the anachronism dissolves into a convention.3

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The one-campaign reading is the stronger and mainstream position, and it is stronger for a solid reason: the Assyrian evidence attests exactly one campaign against Hezekiah and no trace of a reconquest, and a vassal rebellion in 688 BC — against an Assyria then at its zenith — is historically implausible. The tribute figures are a weak prop for two campaigns: the gold agrees exactly (30 talents), and a 300-vs-800 silver gap is the ordinary stuff of a boastful royal annal versus a defeated kingdom's own record (propaganda inflation, or Judahite versus Assyrian weight-standards), not the fingerprint of a separate war. Most importantly, the "damaging anachronism" charge is itself weak: a proleptic royal title is a genuine and well-attested narrative convention, and it defuses the Tirhakah objection regardless of how the Egyptian chronology is settled — a narrator can call the later king "king" at an earlier moment without error. On its central claim — that the account is historically coherent as one 701 campaign — the mainstream reading is probable, and the two-campaign theory is the minority.

What is unresolved. The honesty of the case lies in not overselling it. The two-campaign theory is outweighed, not refuted: it is held by real scholars on a real tension in the text, and no Assyrian record positively excludes a later action in the undated years after 689 BC — the silence there cuts both ways. And the specific Egyptological mechanism that stations a battle-ready Taharqa at 701 is genuinely contested. Macadam's older chronology dated his birth c. 710 BC, making him a boy of about nine in 701 — the very reading that first fed the anachronism charge; the revised c. 722 birth-date that yields "age twenty" is derived from a disputed synchronism (when to date his northward journey and Shebitku's accession), not an independent datum, and the Tang-i Var inscription (c. 706 BC, showing Shebitku already reigning) unsettles Kitchen's dates further. Yurco's Shabaka–Shebitku co-regency — the device that lets Taharqa march in 701 — has no direct inscriptional support and has been largely abandoned; critics fairly call it a modern fix for an ancient problem. Kahn's 2014 study frames the presence of Taharqa in 701 as an open question of anachronism-versus-reliability rather than a closed one. None of this rescues the two-campaign theory as the better reading — but it does mean the smoothest harmonizations (a twenty-year-old general via a securely-fixed chronology) claim more than the Egyptian evidence has settled. The load-bearing defence is the proleptic title, which needs none of that machinery; the machinery, where it is invoked, is where the case is soft.4

Assessment

Contested — strong by the Assyrian records, still snagged on Egyptian chronology. The mainstream reading is a single 701 BC campaign, and it is the better-supported one: the annals know only one war against Hezekiah, a 688 BC rebellion against a peak-strength Assyria is implausible, the tribute discrepancy is trivial, and the Tirhakah "anachronism" is not damaging — a proleptic royal title is a normal convention, and it neutralizes the objection whatever the Egyptian chronology turns out to be. On that much the account is historically coherent, and the two-campaign theory is the minority. What keeps the question contested rather than closed is twofold: the two-campaign reading is outweighed but not refuted (serious scholars, a real textual tension, and no Assyrian record that excludes a later action in the undated post-689 years), and the harmonizations that go beyond the proleptic title — a twenty-year-old Taharqa commanding at Eltekeh, the Shabaka–Shebitku co-regency — rest on disputed synchronisms and an evidentially unsupported co-regency, so they are the majority reconstruction, not a settled fact. Trust the one-campaign reading as the probable one and the anachronism as harmless; flag as overreach any claim that the two-campaign theory is dead or that the Egyptian chronology has been nailed down. The account's coherence does not depend on winning that Egyptological fight — which is precisely why the fight can stay open without damaging it.
