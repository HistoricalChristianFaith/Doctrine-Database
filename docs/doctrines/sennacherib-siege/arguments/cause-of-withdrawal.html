The 185,000 and the cause of the Assyrian withdrawal — the angel, the plague, and Herodotus's mice

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — the fact that Sennacherib's siege broke off abruptly and left Jerusalem un-taken is well-grounded and not in dispute (it is weighed on the non-capture page); that the cause was a sudden mass mortality — a plague, which this wiki does not treat as excluding the biblical angel of the LORD, since a pestilence can be the means of the "angel" — is a plausible and long-standing reading. What is weak is the specific claim that the extra-biblical witnesses — Herodotus's field-mice and the Berossus/Josephus pestilence — independently corroborate the 185,000 and the deliverance: that chain is a modern reconstruction laid over silences on both sides, and possibility is not proof · advanced in popular apologetics (the outside sources as confirmation) and, in naturalized form, by historians who posit an epidemic (How & Wells; John Bright; Stephen W. Caesar)

Related arguments: the deliverance — see also Does the prism's silence on Jerusalem's capture corroborate the biblical non-capture?

The claim

Many scholars believe that Herodotus's account of Sennacherib's defeat in battle with Egypt at the city of Pelusium is in fact a reference to the sudden death of the Assyrian army during the siege of Jerusalem.

— Armstrong Institute of Biblical Archaeology, "Secular History of the Assyrians". The interpretation this page weighs runs in two directions from a single event. The Bible reports that "the angel of the LORD went out, and smote in the camp of the Assyrians a hundred fourscore and five thousand," so that Sennacherib "departed, and went and returned" (2 Kings 19:35–36; Isaiah 37:36; 2 Chron 32:21). The naturalizing reading identifies the agent as an epidemic and reads Herodotus's mouse-plague at Pelusion, and the pestilence-notices in Berossus and Josephus, as independent memories of the same catastrophe. The apologetic reading takes those same outside notices as external confirmation that the deliverance happened as described. The two share a premise — that a real, sudden mass death broke the Assyrian army — and this page grants the abrupt withdrawal while weighing how far the external record reaches toward the 185,000.1

Who advances it

The epidemic explanation is old and respectable, not an apologetic invention. The classic scholarly commentary on Herodotus, W. W. How and J. Wells's A Commentary on Herodotus (1912), read the field-mice of Histories 2.141 through the ancient convention that made the mouse a token of pestilence — Apollo the plague-sender bears the cult-title Smintheus ("mouse-god") at Iliad 1.39, and the plague-struck Philistines return the Ark with golden "mice that mar the land" (1 Samuel 6:4–5) — and so took the tale to encode an outbreak of disease. John Bright's standard A History of Israel concluded that "it was an epidemic of some kind that saved Jerusalem," and Stephen W. Caesar has argued specifically for septicemic plague, a rodent- and insect-borne infection with a near-total, same-day mortality that would fit a camp emptied overnight. Downstream of that scholarship, popular apologetics runs the inference the other way: from "a plague (or the mice) explains it" to "the outside sources thereby confirm the biblical miracle." The two camps differ on the agent — a natural epidemic or the angel of the LORD — but agree that something sudden and lethal, not a battlefield defeat, ended the siege.2

The evidence for

Three strands are marshalled for it. First, the shape of the disaster is unusually well-attested for an ancient event: a great army, on the edge of its advance, destroyed without a pitched battle and put suddenly to flight — the Bible's 185,000 dead "in the camp" overnight, and Herodotus's Assyrians routed at Pelusion after a night's calamity, both describe a non-military collapse. Second, the mouse is a recognized ancient cipher for pestilence, so Herodotus's gnawing field-mice are plausibly a folk-memory of an epidemic rather than a literal plague of vermin — and an epidemic is exactly the kind of sudden, mass, non-combat mortality the biblical text reports (the details of the Herodotus tale are set out on the Herodotus witness page). Third, the pestilence is named outright in the later transmission: Josephus reports that "God had sent a pestilential distemper upon his army," destroying "an hundred fourscore and five thousand… on the very first night," and folds a citation of the Babylonian priest Berossus into the account (the passage is quoted in full on the Josephus/Berossus witness page). Historically, plague really has broken sieges — Caesar adduces the Black Death lifting the siege of Tabriz in 1346 and bubonic plague forcing Napoleon off Acre in 1799 — so the mechanism is not far-fetched. On this reading the biblical, Greek, and Babylonian streams converge on one memory: a pestilence that annihilated Sennacherib's host and saved Jerusalem.3

Adversarial assessment

What holds. Two things stand. The fact of an abrupt Assyrian withdrawal that left Jerusalem un-captured is well-grounded, on the Assyrian record as much as the biblical one (weighed on the non-capture page). And a plague as its mechanism is a coherent, mainstream-respectable reading — and, importantly on this wiki, a naturalizing one does not by itself refute the biblical account. "It was only disease" is not a rebuttal, because a pestilence can be precisely the means by which the "angel of the LORD" acts; Scripture itself elsewhere pairs the destroying angel with plague. So the choice is not "miracle or epidemic": the epidemic reading, if correct, could be the how of the miracle, and the objection "no natural process kills 185,000 overnight" begs the question a miracle-claim openly concedes.

What is overstated or imprecise. The load-bearing move — that the outside sources corroborate the deliverance and the 185,000 — will not bear the weight placed on it, for four reasons. First, Herodotus is the wrong country and the wrong enemy. He sets the rout in Egypt, at Pelusion, against an Egyptian priest-king, delivered by an Egyptian god; Judah, Hezekiah, Jerusalem, the plague, the 185,000, and the God of Israel are all absent, and the story is legendary in form (a divine dream, cowardly soldiers, a monument-explaining mouse-statue). The mouse→plague→"this is the 185,000" chain is built by the modern reader over silences on both sides — neither the Bible nor the prism mentions mice, and Herodotus mentions neither plague nor Jerusalem — and some historians locate the tale's kernel in the later Assyrian invasions of Egypt under Esarhaddon and Ashurbanipal, with "Sanacharib" attached secondarily (see the Herodotus page). Second, the Berossus pestilence is not independent. In Whiston's Josephus the plague and the 185,000 arrive under Berossus's name, but every detail there tracks 2 Kings 19 point for point; the securely-Berossan core is only the bare notice that Sennacherib "ruled over the Assyrians, and … made an expedition against all Asia and Egypt," and the destruction-narrative is best judged the biblical claim restated within the citation, not a separate Babylonian record (see the Josephus/Berossus page). Third, the 185,000 is externally unverifiable and widely treated as a round or hyperbolic figure — Dan'el Kahn calls the number an exaggeration; one proposed emendation reads an original 5,180 (which others find far too low) — so the exact datum the corroboration is meant to confirm is itself uncertain. Fourth, the epidemiology is a hint, not a proof: a recent historico-medical review grants that "the only clue to a possible plague epidemic is the presence of rodents in large numbers, but the hint is too vague to be conclusive."4

Nor is the plague the only ancient explanation on offer: later Jewish tradition read the destruction as a fiery stroke — the Talmud has the angel burn the Assyrians' souls while their garments stayed intact (Sanhedrin 95b) — which is a reminder that the cause, unlike the fact of the withdrawal, was already being variously imagined in antiquity. And the decisive methodological point cuts against confirmation from either side: that God could have delivered Jerusalem does not establish that He did, and an Assyrian withdrawal is equally consistent with a negotiated departure after tribute, a strategic decision to move on, or trouble elsewhere in the empire. Consistency is not corroboration; the external record fixes that the siege ended without Jerusalem's fall, not why.5

Assessment

Contested — sound at the fact, plausible at the mechanism, weak at the corroboration. Three questions must be kept apart. Did the siege end abruptly with Jerusalem un-taken? Yes — that is well-grounded and undisputed. Was the cause a sudden mass mortality, plausibly a plague? That is a coherent and long-held reading, and this wiki does not set it against the biblical angel: a pestilence can be the very means of the deliverance, so the naturalizing account is not a refutation and "it was just disease" settles nothing. Do the extra-biblical witnesses independently corroborate the 185,000 and the deliverance? Here the case is weak. Herodotus's mice are in the wrong country against the wrong enemy and legendary in form; the "Berossus" pestilence in Josephus is the biblical account restated, not a separate record; the 185,000 is externally unverifiable and generally read as round; and the best epidemiological reading concedes the clue is "too vague to be conclusive." The whole is therefore contested: genuinely disputed, strong on the fact and the plausibility of an epidemic, weak on the claim that the outside sources confirm the miracle. Possibility is not proof — God could have acted, and the non-capture is consistent with it, but the external record does not establish the deliverance, which rests, if it rests anywhere, on the biblical witness itself. Trust the argument for the abrupt end of the siege and for the coherence of a plague-mechanism; flag as overreach any use of Herodotus or Berossus as confirmation of the 185,000.
