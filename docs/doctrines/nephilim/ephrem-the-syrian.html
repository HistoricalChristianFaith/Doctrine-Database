Ephrem the Syrian on the Nephilim

Dates: c. 373 · back to timeline Summary: Ephrem the Syrian (d. 373) holds a purely Sethite reading. The "sons of God" are the righteous descendants of Seth and the "daughters of men" are the adorned daughters of Cain who ensnared them. Ephrem further explains the giants' physical strength naturally: the Sethites were strong because they dwelt in the blessed land near paradise, while the cursed Cainites grew weak on land that withheld its strength.

Claims & evidence

The sons of God are the sons of Seth; the daughters of men are the daughters of Cain

He called the sons of Seth sons of God, those who, like the sons of Seth, had been called "the righteous people of God." The beautiful daughters of men whom they saw were the daughters of Cain who adorned themselves and became a snare to the eyes of the sons of Seth. Then Moses said, they took them wives of all which they chose.

— Commentary on Genesis, pages 134-135, source. Ephrem reads the union as intermarriage between the two human lines, not angelic descent.

The Sethites were strong from the blessed land; the Cainites weak from the cursed earth

The house of Cain, because the earth had been cursed so as not to give them its strength, produced small harvests, deprived of its strength, just as it is today that some seeds, fruits and grasses give strength and some do not. Because at that time they were cursed and sons of the cursed and were dwelling in the land of curses, they would gather and eat produce that lacked nutrition, and those who ate these were without strength just like the food that they ate. As for the Sethites, on the other hand, because they were the descendants of the blessed [Seth] and were dwelling in the land along the boundary of the fence of paradise, their produce was abundant and full of strength. So too were the bodies of those who ate that produce strong and powerful.

— COMMENTARY ON GENESIS 6.5.1 (printed translation; primary URL not yet located). Ephrem gives a wholly natural account of the giants' might, grounded in geography and diet rather than in any superhuman parentage.

Notes

  • Ephrem is a strong witness for the Sethite position, supplying a naturalistic explanation for the giants that removes any need for angelic begetters.
  • His emphasis on the Cainite women's adornment as a "snare" reads the union morally — a fall of the righteous line into the wicked — rather than as an incursion of fallen angels.
  • De Young casts Ephrem as a link in an "Africanus → Ephrem → Augustine" transmission of the Sethite reading out of Rabbinic Judaism; that pipeline is weighed — and found undocumented — on the did the Sethite reading originate as a Rabbinic reaction? crux (assessment: plausible).
