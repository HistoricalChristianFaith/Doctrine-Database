The angels' "habitation" (oikētērion) as a relinquished celestial body

Type: supporting argument / crux (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — oikētērion means "dwelling / abode," and Jude 6's Enochic context plus the unanimous patristic gloss make the angels' "habitation" their heavenly station, not a body they shed; the inference from Paul's one other use (2 Cor 5:2, where the word does denote the resurrection body) is an illegitimate cross-author word-transfer · back to timeline · advanced by Doug Wilson

An argument page weighs a single sub-claim adduced in support of a main interpretation and renders an adversarial assessment of the interpretation it serves. This one tests Wilson's literal-embodiment answer to the standard objection against the angelic reading — "spirits cannot interbreed with humans." His answer is a lexical mechanism: the angels who "left their own habitation" (Jude 6, oikētērion) relinquished their celestial bodies to marry human women, the warrant being that the word's only other New Testament occurrence (2 Cor 5:2) denotes the embodied resurrection body. It is the competitor to De Young's sacral-kingship ritual mechanism — both are accounts of "how spirits beget."

The claim

Paul is referring to his resurrection body as his "house," or "habitation." The angels abandoned their first estate, relinquishing their celestial bodies (or habitations) in order to marry human women.

— Doug Wilson, The Nephilim, Hades, and Other Oddments, dougwils.com.1 The asserted mechanism: the Greek οἰκητήριον ("habitation") that the angels "left" in Jude 6 is the celestial body they shed; the warrant is that the word occurs only twice in the New Testament — Jude 6 and 2 Corinthians 5:2 — and in the latter Paul applies it to the (embodied) resurrection body, so it is taken to denote "an embodied form" in both places.

Who advances it

The reading is Wilson's, in this 2021 expository sermon. It is a classic two-occurrence concordance move: a rare word appears twice in the NT, and the sense fixed in one passage is carried over to the other. The embodiment instinct it serves is not idiosyncratic — the angelic/Watcher tradition has always needed the celestial actors to be somehow embodied for a physical union, and older proponents supplied that by saying the angels assumed bodies or flesh. What is distinctive to Wilson is grounding the embodiment in the lexis of oikētērion specifically: not "angels took on bodies" but "the habitation they left was a body." This is the load-bearing move the assessment weighs.

The evidence for

One half of Wilson's premise is solid. οἰκητήριον does occur only twice in the New Testament, and in 2 Corinthians 5:2 — "we groan, earnestly desiring to be clothed upon with our house (oikētērion) which is from heaven"2 — it genuinely denotes an embodied form. The patristic database is unanimous on this: Chrysostom asks "What habitation? … The incorruptible body," and Theophylact likewise, "Into what dwelling? Into the incorruptible body"; Aquinas reads the heavenly dwelling as something "put on" and "inhering in him." So Paul's oikētērion = the resurrection body, conceived as a heavenly dwelling with which one is "clothed upon."3 Wilson is right that there the word denotes a body. And the angelic reading does require the Genesis 6 actors to be embodied somehow, so an embodiment account of the mechanism is not gratuitous.

Adversarial assessment

What holds. In 2 Corinthians 5:2 oikētērion really does denote an embodied form (the resurrection body), and the angelic reading does need the celestial actors embodied for a physical union. So far Wilson is on firm ground.

Where the move fails — and it fails at the load-bearing step, the transfer from 2 Corinthians to Jude:

  1. The word means "dwelling," not "body." οἰκητήριον (a cognate of oikos, "house") is glossed in the lexica simply as "dwelling, habitation, abode."4 The body-sense in 2 Corinthians 5:2 is not carried by the word; it is supplied by Paul's surrounding clothing-and-tabernacle metaphor — "our earthly house of this tabernacle," "a building of God, an house not made with hands," "clothed upon" (2 Cor 5:1–2). Strip that metaphor and the bare word still means only "dwelling."

  2. The cross-author transfer is illegitimate. A rare word's referent in Paul does not fix its referent in Jude — they are different authors, and meaning is set by each one's context, not by NT-wide concordance. Importing Paul's body-referent into Jude is the word-study fallacy James Barr named illegitimate totality transfer (and D. A. Carson catalogues among "exegetical fallacies"): treating the sense a word bears in one context as though it travels with the word into every other.5

  3. Jude's own context fixes the referent as heaven. Jude 6 is saturated with 1 Enoch, where the Watchers' sin is precisely abandoning their heavenly station to descend to earth — "you have left the high, holy, and eternal heaven" (1 Enoch 15:3; cf. 12:4; 15:7). The parallel clause confirms it: "kept not their first estate" renders ἀρχήν (position / domain / rule), a status-and-place word, so "left their own habitation" means they vacated their assigned heavenly post — not that they doffed a body.6 The participle Jude uses, ἀπολιπόντας ("having left / abandoned"), fits departing a place far more naturally than removing a garment.

  4. The unanimous patristic gloss reads Jude's "habitation" as the heavenly station, never a body. Bede: the fallen forsook "the seats of the heavenly kingdom which they would have received if they kept the faith"; Theophylact: they "cast off the heavenly manner of life given to them by grace." No commentator in the database reads Jude's oikētērion as a relinquished celestial body.6

One concession to the steelman. The interpretation the prop serves — that the angelic actors were embodied for the union — is the angelic reading's necessary supplement and has older, better footing in the tradition's "angels assumed bodies/flesh." But that footing is independent of, and is not improved by, the lexical claim weighed here. Wilson's premise about 2 Corinthians is correct; it is the transfer to Jude that does not hold.

Assessment

Weak. The reading does not hold on its best available case. οἰκητήριον means "dwelling / abode"; its body-sense in 2 Corinthians 5:2 is supplied by Paul's clothing-and-tabernacle metaphor, not by the word itself, and cannot be carried into Jude, a different author whose own context — steeped in 1 Enoch, with the parallel ἀρχήν ("first estate") and the verb "left/abandoned" — fixes the "habitation" as the angels' heavenly station, exactly as the entire patristic database reads it (Bede, Theophylact). Wilson's embodiment instinct points at something the angelic reading really does need, but the classic way to supply it is "angels assumed bodies," not "oikētērion = a celestial body they shed"; the lexical mechanism is a two-occurrence concordance overreach (illegitimate totality transfer). Trust his point that 2 Corinthians 5:2 calls the resurrection body a "habitation"; the step that should be flagged on Wilson's page is the inference from there to Jude 6 — Jude's "habitation" is heaven, not a body.
