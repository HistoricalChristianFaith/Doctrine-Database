Beowulf as a witness to the angelic reading of Genesis 6

Type: supporting argument / crux (off-timeline) · Assessment: weak — for the claim it is adduced for (that the "entire ancient world," Beowulf included, read Genesis 6 as a celestial–terrestrial union). The poem genealogizes its giants from Cain, a human ancestor — the medieval Augustinian/Sethite-adjacent harmonization, the very tradition the angelic reading was displaced by. It is a real witness to the persistence of the antediluvian-giant-and-Flood tradition, but not to the fallen-angel mechanism · back to timeline · advanced by Doug Wilson

An argument page weighs a single sub-claim adduced for a main interpretation and renders an adversarial assessment. Here the sub-claim is a passing one in Wilson's sermon — that the Beowulf tradition belongs in a roll-call of ancient witnesses (Josephus, 1 Enoch) for the angelic/celestial reading. The primary text cuts the other way: Beowulf descends Grendel and the gigantas from Cain, not from fallen angels — a human-lineage genealogy, which is what the medieval Latin West (after Augustine) made of the Genesis-6 giants.

The claim

In the expository sermon "The Nephilim, Hades, and Other Oddments," Doug Wilson appeals to a broad ancient consensus for reading the "sons of God" as celestial beings and the Nephilim as their giant offspring:

why on earth would such unions result in giants? And why would the entire ancient world concur with the reading that this was a perverse celestial/earthly connection, from Josephus to Beowulf to the Book of Enoch—from responsible histories to oddball books?

— The Nephilim, Hades, and Other Oddments, dougwils.com. Josephus and 1 Enoch are firm angelic witnesses and already sit on the timeline. The question this page weighs is the third name in the list: does the Old English epic Beowulf (c. 700–1000) read the antediluvian giants as the product of an angel–human union, such that it can be enlisted for the "celestial/earthly" side?

What Beowulf actually says

The poem twice locates its monsters and giants in salvation history, and both times the anchor is Cain, the fratricide of Genesis 4 — a man — not the fallen "sons of God" of Genesis 6. After Grendel is introduced, the poet traces his ancestry to Cain's exile and lists the broods that sprang from it:

Since the Creator had proscribed him
with the kin of Cain—that killing avenged
eternal God, for that he Abel slew! …
Of Cain awoke all that woful breed,
Etins and elves and evil-spirits,
as well as the giants that warred with God
weary while: but their wage was paid them!

— Beowulf, ll. 106b–114, trans. Francis B. Gummere, The Oldest English Epic (1909), elfinspell.com. The Old English makes Grendel one "in Caines cynne" ("in Cain's kin," l. 107), and says that þanon ("thence," i.e. from Cain) awoke "eotenas ond ylfe ond orcneas, / swylce gigantas, þa wið Gode wunnon" ("ettins and elves and evil-spirits, likewise giants, who strove with God").1 The giants are expressly a branch of Cain's human posterity.

The second passage, on the hilt of the giant-forged sword Beowulf recovers from Grendel's mere, depicts the Flood destroying that giant race:

where was etched the rise
of that far-off fight when the floods o'erwhelmed,
raging waves, the race of giants
(fearful their fate!), a folk estranged
from God Eternal: whence guerdon due
in that waste of waters the Wielder paid them.

— Beowulf, ll. 1688b–1693, trans. Gummere, elfinspell.com; the Old English "giganta cyn" ("the race of giants," l. 1690) "fremde þeod / ecean Dryhtne" ("a people estranged from the eternal Lord").1 So the poem knows the Genesis-6/Flood giants as a wicked race God drowned — but it has already routed them, with the rest of the monstrous broods, through Cain's line.

What the scholarship makes of it

The standard studies of the Beowulf-monsters read these lines exactly this way, and add the nuance that the poet has harmonized the Genesis-6 giant material into a Cainite genealogy — very possibly with help from Enochic/Noachic apocrypha that were available in Anglo-Saxon England.

  • R. E. Kaske, "Beowulf and the Book of Enoch," showed that the Enochic giant-tradition supplies the framework that lets the poet fuse Cain's progeny, the gigantas, and the Genesis-6 giants into one coherent monstrous descent — i.e. the angelic-Watcher material is absorbed, but re-attached to a human ancestor.2
  • Ruth Mellinkoff, in her two-part "Cain's Monstrous Progeny in Beowulf," takes the Cainite descent as given and treats the genuine puzzle it creates — how a line of Cain (Gen 4) survived the Flood that drowned all flesh but Noah's (Gen 6–7) — surveying the medieval traditions of post-diluvian survival that allowed it.3
  • Andy Orchard, in Pride and Prodigies ("The Kin of Cain"), gives the standard modern account of how the poet built his monsters out of the Cain genealogy, conflating biblical and apocryphal strands of patristic teratology.4

The background to all of this is the Latin West's reading of Genesis 6. Augustine (City of God XV.23) had detached the giants from any angelic origin: the "sons of God" are the righteous line of Seth, the "daughters of men" the line of Cain, the Nephilim mighty but wholly human offspring.5 The Vulgate an Anglo-Saxon poet knew literally called them gigantes (Gen 6:4), and read through the Augustinian frame those giants are human stock — descended, via the Cainite "daughters," from Cain. Beowulf's "gigantas … of Cain's kin" is that medieval equation in verse.

Adversarial assessment

The steelman. Wilson is not wrong that Beowulf is a genuine reception witness to part of the tradition. The poem independently attests (a) that there were antediluvian giants, (b) that they "warred with God" and were a "folk estranged from God Eternal," and (c) that the Flood was God's judgment that destroyed them — and Kaske shows the poet had absorbed Enochic giant-lore to say so. So the broad "giants + Flood + war against God" cluster that the angelic reading also carries does survive into the medieval Christian imagination, and Beowulf evidences its persistence. To that extent the poem is a real datum about how durable the giant-tradition was.

Where the appeal fails. But that is not the point in dispute. Wilson needs Beowulf for the specific reading that the giants issued from a "perverse celestial/earthly connection" — fallen "sons of God" begetting on women. On that exact question the poem testifies against him: it derives Grendel and the gigantas from Cain (Gen 4), a human murderer, not from angels. That is a Cainite, human-lineage genealogy — the Augustinian/Sethite-adjacent harmonization that replaced the older angelic reading in the Latin West, not a survival of it. Lumping Beowulf onto the celestial side therefore over-reads the roll-call: of Wilson's three names, two (Josephus, 1 Enoch) are squarely angelic, but the third sits with the camp he is arguing against. (The mention is a passing rhetorical flourish in a sermon, not a load-bearing argument; the overstatement is in the "entire ancient world … concur" framing.)

Assessment

Weak — as a witness for the interpretation it is adduced for. Beowulf attests the antediluvian giants, their war with God, and their destruction in the Flood, and even shows knowledge of the Enochic giant-tradition; that much is real and makes the poem a legitimate reception witness to the giant-and-Flood material. But on the one point Wilson enlists it for — that the giants sprang from a celestial–terrestrial union — the poem genealogizes them from Cain, a human ancestor, in line with the medieval Augustinian reading rather than the angelic one. So the "from Josephus to Beowulf to the Book of Enoch" lineup is double-edged: a sound claim about the persistence of the giant tradition, but not support for the fallen-angel mechanism, and on the genealogy Beowulf actually witnesses for the rival, Cainite/Sethite-adjacent camp. Flagged as a minor overstatement on Wilson's page; it does not touch the stronger Josephus/1 Enoch legs of his case.
