Og's cities Ashtaroth and Edrei as the seat of the Ugaritic Rapiu (KTU 1.108)

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: sound — the core identification is well-grounded, now-majority Ugaritology: the Ugaritic Rapiu libation-prayer KTU 1.108 (RS 24.252) hails Rapiu, King of Eternity — the divinized patron of the rpʾum (Rephaim) — as the god enthroned at ʿṯtrt and hdrʿy, read since B. Margulis (1970) as Ashtaroth and Edrei, the very two towns Deut 1:4 and Josh 12:4 assign to Og, "of the remnant of the Rephaim." That makes Og's own royal seat, in a pre-biblical Canaanite text, the cult-place of a deified Rephaim-king — independent grounding for the reading that Og is a surviving semidivine Rephaim and that "Rephaim" denotes the divinized royal dead, and it needs no bed and no performed rite. What keeps it short of airtight is philological, not interpretive: the h- of hdrʿy answers awkwardly to the aleph of אֶדְרֶעִי (the equation is "difficult to defend" on strict phonology — McAffee), and ʿṯtrt is spelled identically whether the town Ashtaroth or the goddess Athtart is meant, so the toponymic reading rests on the parallelism and the Og echo, not on orthography alone — a caveat inside a mainstream reading, not a defeater · the strongest unbuilt support for the supernaturalist Og reading advanced by Fr. Stephen De Young

Related arguments: the Rephaim — see also the dead-kings funerary text (KTU 1.161)

This page promotes a datum that sits buried as a parenthetical on the Og's-bed and dead-kings pages: that a Ugaritic prayer seats a deified Rephaim-king at Og's exact two cities. Of all the supports for reading Og as a supernatural Rephaim figure it may be the firmest — a pre-biblical, independent text, not a proof-text we strain — though it is one De Young himself does not press, leaning on the iron bed instead. The finding: the identification is the majority Ugaritological reading and genuinely load-bearing, but it turns on two contested philological joints that the page must keep honest.

The claim

Three times the Hebrew Bible plants Og — "the last of the Rephaim" — at a fixed pair of towns: "Og the king of Bashan, which dwelt at Ashtaroth and at Edrei" (Josh 12:4; cf. Deut 1:4; Josh 13:12). A Ugaritic liturgical text discovered at Ras Shamra — KTU 1.108 (RS 24.252), a libation-prayer inviting deities to a drinking-feast — opens by hailing a god under the name rpʾu, "Rapiu," and locates him at two places whose consonants match Og's towns:

May Rapiu, King of Eternity (rpʾu mlk ʿlm), drink wine; yea, may he drink, the mighty and noble god, the god who dwells (is enthroned) in Ashtaroth (ʿṯtrt), the god who rules (judges) in Edrei (hdrʿy) …

— KTU 1.108.1–3, in the translations of N. Wyatt and G. del Olmo Lete.1 On this reading the claim is not about a bed, a date, or a rite that may or may not have been performed. It is geographic and onomastic: the name Rapiu is the singular of rpʾum, the Ugaritic Rephaim — the divinized royal dead — so the text presents the patron-deity of the Rephaim enthroned precisely where Scripture later remembers the last Rephaim king reigning. Og's territory was, in a Canaanite source older than the biblical notices, the cult-seat of a deified Rephaim-king "of eternity." That is exactly the world the supernaturalist reading says stands behind Og.

Who advances it

The link is mainstream Ugaritology, not an apologetic construction. It was first proposed by B. Margulis (Baruch Margalit), "A Ugaritic Psalm (RŠ 24.252)," JBL 89 (1970), who identified the text's ʿṯtrt and hdrʿy with biblical Ashtaroth and Edrei and tied Og to the god Rapiu; S. B. Parker's "The Feast of Rāpiʾu" appeared the same year.2 It was carried into the standard reference works by Gregorio del Olmo Lete, who renders 1.108.2–3 "the god who dwells in Ashtaroth, the god who judges at Edrei" and calls it "the translation now accepted by most Ugaritologists," and by Mark S. Smith's "Rephaim" entry in the Anchor Bible Dictionary.2 On the side of the present wiki, the connection reaches the supernaturalist Og reading through Maria Lindquist, whose 2011 CBQ study adduces "the Ugaritic god Rapaʾu … a cognate of the Hebrew rěpāʾîm, also known as the King of Eternity," who "lived in the same region as Og, in Ashtaroth and Edrei," as evidence of "divine affiliations for King Og, the last of the Rephaim."2 Notably De Young himself does not press this text; he rests the supernatural Og case on the iron bed (Deut 3:11) and the funerary liturgy KTU 1.161. So this page weighs not a claim he makes but the strongest support his framework could claim — the firmer pillar he leaves unbuilt.

The evidence for

The identification is strong on three independent counts.

  • The name Rapiu is the Rephaim. Ugaritic rpʾu (singular) is the eponym and divine patron of the rpʾum / Rapauma — the noble, divinized dead — which is the same word as the biblical rěpāʾîm, "Rephaim," the race of whom Og is "the remnant." The text's epithet mlk ʿlm, "King of Eternity," carries an underworld register (Pardee reads it of the atemporality of the afterlife), and the figure is widely identified with Milku (the chthonic "eternal king," the Ugaritic form behind biblical Milcom/Molek), who in a separate Ugaritic text — the RS 24.244 snakebite incantation — is himself located "in ʿAṯtartu," Ashtaroth.3 So a second text independently seats an underworld-king at Ashtaroth — the toponym is not a one-off.
  • Both towns, in the right order, in one couplet. The match is not a single coincidental name but the pair Ashtaroth–Edrei in poetic parallelism — the same two places, joined, that the biblical formula joins. The geography is Bashan / the Transjordan, exactly Og's realm; del Olmo Lete builds from it a regional theology of Bashan as the Canaanite land of the dead, the ancestral home of the rpum.3
  • It needs neither the bed nor a performed rite. Unlike the Og's-bed argument — which must defend a Marduk allusion and a contested sacred-marriage rite — and unlike the funerary-liturgy argument, whose valence De Young inverts, this datum is inert to those disputes. It does not depend on what the Rephaim did or on any ritual's choreography, only on who was venerated where. As a grounding for "Og = a surviving semidivine Rephaim / Rephaim = divinized royal dead," it is the cleanest of the three.

The wider Ugaritic dossier reinforces the picture. The Rapiuma texts (KTU 1.20–22) — in the same scribal hand as the Aqhat epic, whose hero Dan'il bears the epithet mt rpi, "man of Rapiu" — show the rpum as divine/heroic figures summoned to a banquet, the long-dead "heroes of old" gathered at a feast; they belong to the same complex of the divinized dead from which Rapiu and the Rephaim draw.4

Adversarial assessment

What holds. That a Ugaritic prayer hails a deified Rephaim-patron, "King of Eternity," seated at a pair of towns whose consonants match Og's Ashtaroth and Edrei is real, first observed more than fifty years ago, and now the more common scholarly reading — to the point that the older attempt to dissolve the geography (see below) is itself now widely judged untenable. The rpʾu ↔ rpʾum ↔ Rephaim equation is not in dispute; the underworld register of "King of Eternity" and the Milku-in-Ashtaroth parallel are solid. For the supernaturalist Og reading this is genuinely load-bearing, and load-bearing in a way the bed is not.

Two philological joints keep it from airtight. Both are about the toponyms, and the page should not paper over them:

  1. Is hdrʿy really "Edrei"? Biblical Edrei is אֶדְרֶעִי, with an initial aleph; the Ugaritic is hdrʿy, with an initial heh. The heh↔aleph correspondence is, in Matthew McAffee's words, "difficult to defend" on strict phonology, so the equation requires positing a phonetic variant or scribal oddity (others speak of a "phonetic variant or scribal error for Edrei"). This does not sink the reading — such variants exist — but it is a real cost.5

  2. Town or goddess? ʿṯtrt is spelled identically whether one means the city Ashtaroth or the goddess ʿAṯtartu (Astarte), a major Ugaritic deity. An older rival reading took both names as divine names — the god enthroned "with Athtart," who judges "with Haddu the shepherd" (hdrʿy as a title of Hadad) — on which 1.108 names no towns at all, and Margulis's equation looks like circular reasoning (leaning on the Og story to license the very geography it sets out to find). The honest reply is twofold. First, the deity-reading has lost ground: treating hdrʿy / Hidraʿyu as a divine name is now widely judged grammatically impossible, which removes the rival for the second name and leaves the toponymic Ashtaroth–Edrei reading the standard one. Second, the case for "town" rests not on orthography (which cannot decide it) but on the couplet's parallelism and the striking convergence with Og — so it is strong but not self-evident.5

One caution about overreach. The maximal gloss — "the Canaanites worshipped a death-god ruling from Og's cities, and Israel knew it" — runs past the evidence in two directions. The text attests a deity Rapiu at these places; that the historical Og was understood by anyone as that deity, or as its incarnation, is an inference the reader supplies, not a datum the tablet states. And the broader assumption of a functioning cult of the beneficent dead behind these texts is itself contested — Brian Schmidt reads the Ugaritic death-texts as commemoration rather than an ongoing necromantic cult. The sober claim the evidence will bear is narrower and still substantial: in pre-Israelite Canaanite tradition, the patron-god of the Rephaim was venerated at the two towns Scripture later assigns to the last Rephaim king — a convergence of name, region, and divinized-dead motif that the supernaturalist reading rightly finds telling.5

Assessment

Sound. Trust the core: KTU 1.108 hails Rapiu, King of Eternity — the divine eponym of the rpʾum / Rephaim — as the god enthroned at ʿṯtrt and hdrʿy, read as Ashtaroth and Edrei (Margulis 1970; del Olmo Lete; Mark S. Smith; Lindquist), Og's own two cities (Deut 1:4; Josh 12:4). The identification is the majority Ugaritological reading, it rests on an undisputed name-link (rpʾu = the Rephaim) and an independent parallel (Milku seated at Ashtaroth in RS 24.244), and it grounds the reading that Og is a surviving semidivine Rephaim and that "Rephaim" denotes the divinized royal dead — without the "is it a bed / was the rite performed" wobble that dogs the Og's-bed and funerary-liturgy arguments. It rates sound on the "well-grounded and mainstream" standard, not as a closed case: the equation turns on a heh↔aleph correspondence for Edrei that is "difficult to defend" (McAffee), and on reading ʿṯtrt as the town rather than the identically-spelled goddess — caveats within a standard reading, not defeaters, and the chief rival (a divine-name reading of hdrʿy) is now widely held untenable. The temptation to flag is only the maximal gloss: the tablet attests a deity Rapiu at Og's cities, not that Og was that deity, and the cult-of-the-dead framework around it is itself disputed (Schmidt). Stated at that width — the patron-god of the Rephaim venerated at the towns Scripture remembers as the last Rephaim king's seat — it is the firmest of the Ugaritic supports for the supernaturalist Og reading, and the one its modern proponent has yet to build on.
