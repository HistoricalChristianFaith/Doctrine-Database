Are the unclean spirits the spirits of the dead Nephilim?

Type: supporting argument / crux (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — a genuine, well-attested early reading (explicit in 1 Enoch 15 / Jubilees 10 and stated by the ante-Nicene chorus — Justin, Athenagoras, Commodian, Lactantius — arguably the dominant 2nd–3rd-century demonology) that is nonetheless not asserted by the New Testament, is eclipsed in the mature tradition by the rival "demons = the apostate angels" identification, and is rejected outright by the Sethite camp; a serious interpretive option, not a settled doctrine · back to timeline · held by the Enochic tradition and the early Fathers; revived by Fr. Stephen De Young

An argument page weighs a single sub-claim adduced in support of a main interpretation and renders an adversarial assessment. We judge the interpretation on its strongest available case, not on any one proponent's wording of it. Here the sub-claim is a demonology: that the "unclean spirits" / demons of the Gospels are the surviving spirits of the dead Nephilim — the giant offspring of the "sons of God" and human women. Every factual claim is sourced; the witnesses' own words are quoted verbatim.

The interpretation

On this reading the demonic is not a single category. The "sons of God" (angelic beings) who transgressed in Genesis 6 are one order; their hybrid offspring, the Nephilim, are another; and when those giants died, their disembodied spirits — neither fully angelic nor ordinary human souls — remained on earth as the restless "unclean spirits" that afflict, possess, and are exorcised in the Gospels. The reading answers a real question (where do the Gospels' daimonia come from, and why are they bound to the earth and the tombs?) by importing the Second-Temple etiology of evil spirits. It is logically downstream of the angelic interpretation of Genesis 6: only if the giants were hybrids do they leave behind a distinct order of spirit to become the demons.

The strongest case for it

The scriptural anchor is explicit. The locus classicus is 1 Enoch 15, where God tells Enoch that the spirits which "have proceeded from the bodies" of the dead giants "shall be evil spirits upon the earth," afflicting, oppressing, and destroying — precisely the profile of the Gospel demons; Jubilees 10 adds the theodicy (Mastema's petition; one-tenth of the spirits left to roam under his command).1 This is not an inference read into the text; it is the text's own account of the origin of evil spirits.

The patristic chorus is broad and early. A line of Fathers — all on this timeline — states the identity outright:

  • Justin Martyr (c. 155): the angels "were captivated by love of women, and begat children who are those that are called demons."2

  • Athenagoras of Athens (c. 177), most explicitly: "the souls of the giants… are the demons who wander about the world."3

  • Commodian (c. 250): of the dead giants, "wandering they now subvert many bodies, and it is such as these especially that ye this day worship and pray to as gods."4

  • Lactantius (c. 310): the offspring "neither angels nor men, but bearing a kind of mixed nature," become the lower of the "two kinds of demons… the wicked spirits, the authors of all the evils which are done."5

  • The Clementine Homilies (Homily VIII, "The Law to the Giants or Demons") devote a whole chapter to it: "the souls of the deceased giants… as being a new race, were called also by a new name."6

Together these make the giant-spirits demonology not a fringe conjecture but, on the evidence surveyed here, the default demonology of the second and third centuries — coherent, textually grounded, and held by writers as central as Justin. As a reading of how the earliest Church understood the demonic, the case is strong.

The case against — why it stays contested

  1. The New Testament never states the etiology. The Gospels speak constantly of akáJarta pneúmata and daimónia, of Legion among the tombs and the swine driven to the abyss, but nowhere trace the demons' origin to the giants. The identification is a Second-Temple framework laid over the NT, not a claim the NT makes; it is as strong as the standing of the Enochic literature it rests on — early and widely current (so a real strand of second- and third-century thought), but short of settled canonical authority (the canonicity page rates the strong "received as Scripture across the early Church" claim weak, its narrower "early, widely used, held authoritative by some" core plausible).7

  2. A rival demonology competes — and wins the long run. The mainstream that consolidates with Augustine identifies the demons not as dead hybrids but as the apostate angels themselves — "the devil and his angels" (Matt 25:41; Rev 12:7–9) who fell at the beginning. On that account there is no separate class of giant-spirits at all, and the Gospel demons are simply fallen angels. This reading became the settled Western demonology, so the giant-spirits etiology is not the Church's final word but an earlier stream later eclipsed.8

  3. The whole reading presupposes the angelic interpretation, which the Sethite camp rejects. If the "sons of God" are merely the line of Seth and the Nephilim merely mighty men — the view of Chrysostom, Augustine, and the later majority — then the giants have ordinary human souls, and there is no order of hybrid spirit for the demons to be. Chrysostom even denies the premise the etiology needs: the souls of the dead do not become demons.9 So the reading is only available inside the angelic camp; it cannot claim the consensus of the tradition as a whole.

A note on the modern prop

Reviving the reading, Fr. Stephen De Young (Tucker Carlson Show, "Young giants," 2026) supports it by appeal to a Chrysostom homily — "it is people who live like demons who become demons."10 That particular prop is the weakest available support, and the steelman above does not rest on it: Chrysostom is the era's arch-Sethite, the homily is itself a refutation of the belief that the souls of the dead become demons, and it locates the change in the will ("not that their nature is changed"), so it speaks to a different idea (the living growing demonic by imitation) and from the wrong camp. The flag belongs on De Young's page; it does not weigh against the interpretation, which stands or falls with the Enochic tradition and the ante-Nicene Fathers above — not with Chrysostom.

Assessment

Contested. Judged as a reading of the early Church, the case is strong: the giant-spirits demonology is stated plainly in 1 Enoch 15 and Jubilees 10 and affirmed by a broad ante-Nicene chorus (Justin, Athenagoras, Commodian, Lactantius, the Clementine literature), and was arguably the period's default account of the demonic. What keeps it from "sound" is that it is genuinely disputed and never settled: the New Testament does not assert the etiology, the rival identification of the demons with the fallen angels competes and becomes the mature tradition's view, and the reading is unavailable to the entire Sethite half of the tradition because it presupposes the angelic interpretation of Genesis 6. So an argument in favor of the interpretation is valid and well-grounded — it establishes a real, coherent, widely-held ancient reading — but it does not defeat its ancient rival or bind the NT, and should be held as a strong interpretive option rather than a proven doctrine. (The modern proponent's Chrysostom prop is a separate and weaker matter, flagged on his page; the interpretation does not depend on it.)
