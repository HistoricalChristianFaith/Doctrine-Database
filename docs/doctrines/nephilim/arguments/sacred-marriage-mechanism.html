The sacred-marriage rite as the mechanism of the Nephilim

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: sound — a documented human-king bed-rite really does exist (the Sumerian hieros gamos), so the mechanism is anchored in a genuine institution; "to produce the next king" is sound as "perpetuate the divine kingship" (legitimation in this ideology is performative divine sonship), and outruns the evidence only as begetting the heir biologically through the bed; the "two-thirds divine" offspring rides a separate (apkallu / divine-birth) motif · advanced by Fr. Stephen De Young

Related arguments: the sacred-marriage (sacral-kingship) — see also Og's bed · the Khmer nāga-rite · the Daijōsai

An argument page weighs a single sub-claim adduced in support of a main interpretation and renders an adversarial assessment. This one tests De Young's answer to the standard objection against the angelic reading — "spirits cannot interbreed with humans." His answer is a mechanism: the Nephilim were begotten not by angels copulating with women but through a pagan sacral-kingship fertility ritual. The question here is narrow and factual: did such a rite exist, and does it do the work he asks of it? The companion page Og's bed and the bed of Marduk weighs the same complex from the other end (the dimensions of Og's bed); this page weighs the rite itself.

The claim

De Young repeatedly grounds the Nephilim's begetting in a sacral-kingship sex-rite rather than in literal angel–human breeding:

the king, as the high priest of the religion, would ritually embody the god whom they were worshiping … And then would go in and ritually have intercourse with a temple slave or a temple prostitute on this bed. … If a child was conceived to said king out of this ritual, that child would essentially have two fathers, the king who was regarded as divine himself [and] the god, and then one human mother, the temple slave who actually gave birth to him.

— Lord of Spirits, "Land of Giants." In the later interview he states the purpose flatly: the ritual bed "was used in these sexual rituals to produce the next king."1 The offspring, reckoned to have "two fathers" (the divinized human king and the god he embodies) and one human mother, is "two-thirds divine, one-third human" — as Gilgamesh is said to be. On this reading the Sethite objection ("demons can't interbreed") rejects a misunderstanding: the tradition never claimed a spirit's literal sperm, but a ritually-divinized human father and a god jointly "fathering" the child.

Who advances it

The mechanism is best understood as a synthesis by De Young and his Lord of Spirits co-host Fr. Andrew Stephen Damick — it is a joint thesis of the podcast and the book The Lord of Spirits, and some secondary write-ups attribute the hierogamy step to Damick specifically. It welds together three older strands, none of them original to either man:

  • The "two-thirds divine" hybrid — genuine Assyriology, decades older than De Young. The Mesopotamian apkallu (the seven antediluvian sages) become, after the flood, beings "of human descent"; the fourth, Lu-nanna, is expressly "two-thirds apkallu." Anne D. Kilmer (1987) first identified these post-flood hybrid sages with the biblical Nephilim, and Amar Annus ("On the Origin of Watchers," 2010) worked out the apkallu→Watchers derivation in full; Michael Heiser then popularized Annus. Gilgamesh's own "two-thirds god, one-third human" is ancient (the Epic, Tablet I).

  • The "sons of God = divinized kings" reading — older still. Meredith Kline, "Divine Kingship and Genesis 6:1–4" (1962), read the sons of God as antediluvian kings claiming divine titulary. But Kline is a non-supernaturalist: the kings are mere men, the sin is tyranny and polygamy, and there is no sex-rite and no actually-divine offspring.

  • The sacred-marriage rite (hieros gamos) itself — the king embodying Dumuzi, united with Inanna through a priestess — going back to Samuel Noah Kramer, The Sacred Marriage Rite (1969).

What is distinctive to De Young and Damick is the fusion: welding the apkallu "two-thirds" hybrid, the hieros gamos rite, and the supernaturalist/Watcher reading into a single ritual mechanism — a hierogamy rite producing a child of "two fathers," reckoned two-thirds divine, offered as the actual mechanism behind the Nephilim and as the answer to "spirits cannot interbreed." This is in fact a point where De Young departs from Heiser rather than borrowing from him: the two share the supernaturalist identity of the sons of God (spirits, not kings) and the apkallu background, but Heiser rejects royal / sacral-kingship framings of Genesis 6 as anachronistic, whereas De Young's mechanism turns precisely on importing a sacral-kingship rite. No source appears to state this ritual-mechanism reconstruction before the Lord of Spirits material (c. 2020): the building blocks are old and borrowed, but the particular fusion is the two priests' own.2

The evidence for

The crucial point — and the reason this is not mere invention — is that a documented human-king bed-rite genuinely exists. The companion Og's-bed page notes that the bed of the dimensioned cuneiform text belongs to the divine marriage of two gods (Marduk + Zarpanitu), so a human-king ceremony "to produce the next king" is "conjecture beyond the evidence" there. But that assessment was about the Babylonian rite. The earlier Sumerian sacred marriage is a different and better-attested institution:

  • It is a human-king rite. In the hymn Iddin-Dagan A (ETCSL 2.5.3.1) the historical king Iddin-Dagan of Isin, embodying Dumuzi (Ama-ušumgal-ana), unites with Inana on a bed expressly prepared for the rite: "Esparto grass is purified with cedar perfume and arranged on that bed for my lady, and a coverlet is smoothed out on the top of it," after which "the king goes to her holy thighs with head held high."3
  • It was performed by named, historical kings, not only gods. Rulers of the Third Dynasty of Ur and the dynasties of Isin and Larsa (21st–18th c. BC) declare in their own hymns that they "married" Inanna; Šulgi of Ur (Šulgi X) travels to Uruk to the goddess's residence for the union, and Iddin-Dagan's hymn describes the rite in detail. The bride is a priestess / hierodule embodying the goddess.2
  • There is a ritual bed, ceremonially prepared (bathing, anointing, the coverlet), and the union is consummated on it — exactly the shape of De Young's "this bed."3

So the form of De Young's mechanism — a divinized human king, embodying a god, uniting with a woman who embodies a goddess, on a prepared cultic bed, to secure the king's reign — is a real Sumerian institution, not an apologetic fabrication. On this, his steelman is stronger than the Og's-bed page alone implies.

Adversarial assessment

What holds. A documented human-king sacred-marriage bed-rite exists (the Sumerian hieros gamos; Iddin-Dagan A, Šulgi X). De Young is right that the rite turns on ritual identification — a king who "is" the god for the rite's purpose — which dissolves the crude objection that "spirits can't have sperm": the ancient logic is participatory, not biological. The general picture (king-as-god, goddess-as-priestess, cultic bed, kingship-legitimation) is sound.

Where the framing reaches past the evidence — three points, in rising order of importance:

  1. Literal sex is contested, not settled. The older consensus (Kramer, Jacobsen) took the rite as an actual physical union; but since Pirjo Lapinkivi, The Sumerian Sacred Marriage in the Light of Comparative Evidence (2004), a substantial body of scholarship reads it as a symbolic or mystical union (the soul's union with the divine, on the analogy of allegorized Song of Songs), and some hold it was primarily a literary–political metaphor. Because the rite survives only through allusion, the question is genuinely open. De Young's flat "sexual rituals" picks the older, contested reading.4

  2. "To produce the next king": the line between legitimation and "begetting" is partly one we draw — though a real residue remains. The attested goals of the hieros gamos are royal legitimation (the king secures the goddess's favor and a "favorable destiny" for his reign) and the fertility of the land (crops, herds, cosmic abundance); its texts (Iddin-Dagan A, Šulgi X) describe legitimation and abundance, not the begetting of an heir, and Philip Jones reads the rite as the king's mediation of divine power rather than a procreative act. So heir-production is not the documented telos of this rite. But the telos is not invented from nothing — and the tidy split we first drew is partly anachronistic. In sacral-kingship ideology legitimation is divine sonship, and it is conferred performatively — by decree and rite, not by biology. Kings are legitimated as "born of" or "suckled by" deities (Eannatum suckled by Ninhursag on the Stele of the Vultures; the deified Ur III kings — Ur-Namma "the son born of Ninsun … kingship descended from heaven for me," the period's "ideological shortcut"), and the Israelite cousin says it at the coronation itself: "You are my Son; today I have begotten you" (Ps 2:7) — begetting language that is plainly performative and adoptive, pronounced at enthronement, not a claim of biological generation. The Babylonian bed's own festival is built on the same father-god→divine-son theology (the akītu dramatizes Marduk and his son Nabû, the "son and avenger" who processes from Borsippa to his father; in Assyria the king is carried as "Ninurta, who has avenged his father"). So the divine-sonship payoff is not borrowed from a foreign strand: it is native to the world of the rite. De Young's slip is therefore not that he imports divine sonship at all, but the narrower routing he gives it. The phrase splits — but the seam is not where we first put it. Read as "constitute and perpetuate the divine kingship" — the king made the god's son, his house's "favorable destiny" secured — "to produce the next king" carries a genuine begetting-and-sonship sense and runs squarely with the grain (the rite's documented result is a favorable fate "for the king and his country," and the Neo-Assyrian sacred-marriage celebration was staged beside the throne room to intercede for the king's life and house — one courtier's prayer that Bēl and Nabû, "whose sacred marriage is performed in Šabatu, protect the life of the son of the king"). What still outruns the evidence is narrower and firmer than "begetting" as such: the biological routing of that begetting through this bed — a child literally conceived on the bed to "two fathers," who is then the heir. That specific procreative mechanism is unattested, and it sits in tension with the very parallels that vindicate the sonship idea: ANE divine sonship is conferred performatively (Ps 2:7's "begotten" is a decree; Ninsun-sonship an ideological claim), not generated by a documented procreative bed-rite. And the strict "conceived on the bed" reading runs against, not merely beyond, the current center of gravity: the recent majority trend reads the hieros gamos as a literary-ideological metaphor (Pongratz-Leisten's cosmogamy/hierogamy/theogamy typology; Nissinen; Cooper; Lapinkivi) more than a performed procreative rite. (The one rite actually read as producing "the next heir" is the Japanese Daijōsai on R. S. Ellwood's contested minority reading — not the Near-Eastern hieros gamos.) So the flag is precise: "to produce the next king" is sound as "perpetuate the divine kingship/sonship," and conjecture only as "beget the heir biologically through the rite."5

  3. The "two-thirds divine offspring" rides a separate motif — though a well-grounded one. De Young's clinching parallel — the child of "two fathers," "two-thirds divine" like Gilgamesh — does not come from the sacred-marriage rite at all. But the criticism should be stated precisely, because the "two-thirds" motif is not free-floating: its most apposite anchor is the post-flood apkallu hybrid named above — Lu-nanna, "two-thirds apkallu" — whom Kilmer (1987) identifies precisely with the Nephilim. So importing a "two-thirds divine" figure into a Nephilim discussion is itself sound; the slip is narrower — that anchor is the apkallu strand, not the bed-rite. The Gilgamesh form of the motif likewise sits elsewhere: he is "two-thirds god, one-third human" because his mother is the goddess Ninsun (his father the deified king Lugalbanda), a divine-birth claim (the king born of a goddess), distinct from the hieros gamos (king united with a goddess-priestess for fertility and legitimation). Indeed Gilgamesh is the one figure in whom the strands this analysis keeps apart genuinely converge — born of the goddess Ninsun (divine birth), "two-thirds god" (the hybrid fraction), a king (sacral kingship), and named among the Nephilim in the Book of Giants — so the separation is analytic, not a claim that the ancients filed these motifs in sealed boxes; even there, though, his "two-thirds" is generated by divine birth, not by a sacred-marriage bed. So the point is not that "two-thirds divine" is alien to the Nephilim — via the apkallu it is exactly on point — but that De Young routes it through the sacred-marriage rite, when the sources attach it to the divine-birth and apkallu strands instead.6

One further caution, shared with the Og's-bed page. The documented human-king rite is Sumerian (Uruk/Isin/Ur, 3rd–early 2nd millennium BC, the Dumuzi–Inanna pair). The bed De Young attaches to Og is the Babylonian one (the 9×4-cubit Esagil-tablet bed of Marduk and Zarpanitu) — a different deity-pair, place, and millennium, and there the marriage is of two gods. So the Sumerian rite vindicates the form of the mechanism but does not retroactively make the dimensioned Babylonian bed a human-king "produce the next king" object; the two should not be collapsed.6

Assessment

Sound. Trust the core: a real, documented human-king sacred-marriage bed-rite existed (the Sumerian hieros gamos — Iddin-Dagan A, Šulgi X), in which a divinized king embodying Dumuzi united with a priestess embodying Inanna on a prepared cultic bed. De Young's "mechanism" is therefore anchored in a genuine ANE institution, and his participatory logic answers the "spirits can't interbreed" objection on its own terms. This is a meaningful strengthening of his steelman over what the Og's-bed page alone allowed, which weighed only the divine Marduk–Zarpanitu rite. But three steps overreach, and they are exactly the steps the Nephilim claim needs: the rite's documented purpose is legitimation and the land's fertility. "To produce the next king" splits — but the seam is partly one we impose. In sacral-kingship ideology legitimation is divine sonship, conferred performatively (the Ur III kings "born of Ninsun"; the Israelite coronation "today I have begotten you," Ps 2:7; the akītu's own Marduk→Nabû father-son theology), so read as "perpetuate the divine kingship" the phrase carries a real begetting/sonship sense and runs with the grain, the divine-sonship payoff being native to the rite's world rather than borrowed. What still outruns the evidence is narrower and firmer: the biological routing of that begetting through the bed — a child conceived on it to "two fathers." That is in tension with the parallels themselves (ANE divine sonship is conferred performatively, not generated by a procreative bed-rite) and runs against the recent majority trend that reads the hieros gamos as ideological metaphor more than a performed rite; whether it involved literal sex is itself genuinely contested (Lapinkivi); and the "two-thirds divine offspring" is borrowed from separate strands — the apkallu hybrid Lu-nanna ("two-thirds apkallu," the genuine Nephilim parallel) and the divine birth of Gilgamesh from the goddess Ninsun — not from the sacred marriage itself (though in Gilgamesh those strands and kingship and the Nephilim-name all converge in one figure). Stated as "there was a sacral-kingship bed-rite in which a divinized king ritually embodied a god," the claim is sound; stated as "a ritual bed used in sexual rituals to produce a divinely-fathered next king," it fuses three things the sources keep distinct. The overstatement should be flagged on De Young's page — the documented purpose is legitimation/fertility, not heir-production — without denying the real institution underneath it.
