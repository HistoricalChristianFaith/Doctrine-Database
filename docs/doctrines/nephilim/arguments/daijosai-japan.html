The Japanese accession rite (Daijōsai) as a surviving sacral-kingship bed-rite

Type: supporting argument (off-timeline) · Assessment: contested — a real accession rite (the Daijōsai) in which the emperor is secluded overnight with a bed-like god-seat to commune with the sun goddess genuinely exists, and a credentialed scholar (R. S. Ellwood) reads it exactly as a sacred marriage producing "the next heir"; but that reading is a contested minority view, the "sexual element removed after WWII" misstates a pre-war interpretation as a rite component, and "Hirohito would have been the last one" is simply false — Akihito performed it in 1990 and Naruhito in 2019 · advanced by Fr. Stephen De Young

Related arguments: the sacred-marriage (sacral-kingship) — see also the mechanism · Og's bed · the Khmer nāga-rite

An argument page weighs a single sub-claim adduced for a main interpretation and renders an adversarial assessment. This one tests De Young's claim that his sacral-kingship sex-rite — the proposed mechanism of the Nephilim — survived "into living memory" in the Japanese emperor's accession ceremony. It is the living-memory bookend of a cluster: the Og's-bed page and the sacred-marriage mechanism page weigh the ancient Near Eastern form of the rite; this page asks whether the modern Japanese rite is the same thing, still performed. The questions are factual: does the rite exist and look as he says; is there an attested sexual element that was "removed after WWII"; and is it true that "Hirohito would have been the last one"?

The claim

In the Tucker Carlson interview De Young offers the Japanese accession rite as proof that the ancient sacral-kingship complex persisted to the present day:

[the sacral-kingship ritual persisted into living memory — the Japanese emperor, at his accession,] ritually and ceremonially spends the night with the sun goddess … [the sexual element was officially] removed after World War II … [so] Hirohito would have been the last one.

— Tucker Carlson Show, "Young giants" (2026); he ties it to "the Khmer parallel on the Lord of Spirits page."1 The function in his argument is clear: if a sacral-king "spends the night with" a goddess as a literal (originally sexual) rite, and this lasted into the 20th century, then the ancient Nephilim mechanism — a divinized king uniting with a goddess-figure on a cultic bed — is not a fanciful reconstruction but a documented institution with an unbroken life. So the page must be checked on three points: (a) the rite and its overnight "union" with the goddess; (b) the "sexual element removed after WWII"; (c) "Hirohito the last one."

The evidence for — the rite is real, and one scholar reads it his way

The core is not invented. The rite is the Daijōsai (大嘗祭, "Great Thanksgiving"), the most sacred and secret of the Japanese enthronement ceremonies, performed once per reign in a specially built and afterward dismantled compound (the daijōkyū). In it the new emperor, robed in white, is led by torchlight into a dark hall and spends the night alone (but for two attendant maidens) in the presence of the sun goddess Amaterasu, the imperial line's mythic ancestor, offering her sacred food and rice and then partaking himself — an act understood as his "first communion" with her and the moment he becomes the intermediary between the goddess and his people. "Spends the night with the sun goddess" is, as a bald description of the seclusion, fair journalism — it is exactly how the 2019 rite was reported.2

At the center of the hall stands the enigmatic object on which De Young's reading turns: the shinza (神座, "god-seat") — a couch-like structure "of strange and complex construction, with a well-defined pillow and coverlet," resembling a bed. Its original function is so thoroughly lost that during the rite the emperor never once touches it; what it is has been debated for a century — a throne, a resting-place for the visiting kami, an incubation/rebirth couch, or a marriage bed.3

And here is the genuine strength of De Young's steelman: the marriage-bed reading is not a fringe apologetic notion — it is the published thesis of a recognized scholar of Japanese religion. R. S. Ellwood, in The Feast of Kingship (1973), reads the shinza as a marriage bed and the Daijōsai as a hieros gamos — a sacred marriage between the heavenly sovereign and a female deity of the land, conferring fertility and legitimacy. Ellwood's own formulation is almost a verbatim match for what De Young needs: "The marriage is at once heaven with earth and the sovereign with his (womanly or spiritual) consort; the child is at once the harvest and the next heir to the throne." That is precisely the Og's-bed/"produce the next king" logic, asserted of the Japanese rite by a Japanologist, not a podcaster.4 On this much De Young is on real scholarly ground.

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The Daijōsai exists, is genuinely a sacral-kingship rite, places a bed-like object (the shinza) at its heart, and seclude the emperor overnight for a "union" (at minimum a communion) with the sun goddess. A real scholar (Ellwood) reads that union as a sacred marriage producing the heir. So De Young's general picture — an ancient royal "spends the night with a goddess" rite, read by a serious interpreter as a hierogamy, and surviving into the modern world — is anchored, not fabricated. Indeed the survival is, if anything, stronger than he says: the rite is not a dead relic but was performed in 1990 and 2019, within living memory.

Where the claim breaks down — three points, in rising order of seriousness:

  1. The sacred-marriage reading is a contested minority view, not the consensus. Ellwood's hierogamy is one of several competing interpretations, and not the strongest-supported. Carmen Blacker, surveying the field, judges "the argument by which he relates this [marriage] myth to the scenario of the daijosai … speculative and unconvincing." The rival readings dominate: that the rite's climax is the communion meal (D. C. Holtom; Okada Shoji, who rejects both the bedding and the sacred-marriage theories); that the shinza is mere hospitality, a bed where the visiting goddess may rest, never a marriage bed (the Japanese government's official position — the 1990 prime minister called it "a sacred seat for the imperial ancestor to rest"); or that it is an incubation / rebirth couch on which the emperor is symbolically reborn as Amaterasu's child (Orikuchi Shinobu). De Young presents the one reading that suits his thesis as if it were the meaning of the rite.5

  2. "The sexual element was removed after WWII" misstates an interpretation as a ritual act. There is no documented sexual or consummation component of the Daijōsai that was performed and then excised. What existed was an interpretation: the belief that the emperor had conjugal relations with the goddess was "a view put forth in pre–World War Two textbooks, an era when the emperor was considered divine." What changed after the war was not the choreography of the rite but the emperor's status — his 1946 renunciation of divinity, and the postwar constitutional separation of religion and state that made the rite legally fraught. De Young has reified a discredited pre-war reading of the secrecy into a concrete act that was edited out; the sources describe the conjugal idea as a persistent rumour the government and scholars dispute, not a former stage of the ceremony.6

  3. "Hirohito would have been the last one" is simply false. This is a checkable factual error. Hirohito (Emperor Shōwa) performed his own Daijōsai in 1928. Far from being the last, the rite has been performed twice more since the war: by his son Akihito on 22–23 November 1990, and by his grandson Naruhito on 14–15 November 2019 — the latter widely reported, in those very words, as the emperor "spending the night with a goddess." Whatever was or was not "removed after WWII," the rite itself plainly continued, which is the opposite of De Young's claim and, ironically, a better proof of his "survival into living memory" than the false version he gave.2

Assessment

Contested. The bones are real: the Daijōsai is a genuine sacral-kingship accession rite in which the emperor is secluded overnight with a bed-like god-seat (the shinza) to commune with the sun goddess Amaterasu, and a credentialed scholar — R. S. Ellwood — reads that union as a hieros gamos producing both the harvest and "the next heir to the throne," an arresting match to De Young's ancient-Near-Eastern mechanism. As a cross-cultural parallel for "a divinized king ritually unites with a goddess-figure," and as evidence that such rites can persist into the modern age, the example is sound — and the persistence is even firmer than De Young allows. But the specifics he hangs on it fail. The sacred-marriage reading is a contested minority position (Blacker calls it "unconvincing"; the dominant readings are communion, hospitality, and rebirth, and the government's is "a seat for the ancestor to rest"); the "sexual element removed after WWII" turns a discredited pre-war interpretation into a ritual act that was excised, which the record does not support; and "Hirohito would have been the last one" is flatly wrong — Akihito performed the Daijōsai in 1990 and Naruhito in 2019. Stated as "there is a Japanese accession rite in which the emperor spends the night communing with the sun goddess, which some scholars read as a surviving sacred marriage," the claim holds. Stated as "a sexual sacral-kingship rite that was edited after the war, with Hirohito the last to perform it," it is a contested reading wrapped around a factual error. Both overstatements — the certainty of the sex-marriage reading and, especially, the "Hirohito the last one" mistake — should be flagged on De Young's page.
