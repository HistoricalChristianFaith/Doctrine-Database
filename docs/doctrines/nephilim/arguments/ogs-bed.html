Og's bed and the bed of Marduk

Type: supporting argument / crux (off-timeline) · Assessment: plausible — the dimensional parallel is well-grounded and De Young's "Etemenanki summit" location is correct, and the old "basalt sarcophagus/tomb" rival is superseded (von Rad, Eslinger, Millard; no post-2011 defender). It stays short of sound because the load-bearing step — that the dimensions also allude to Marduk — is Lindquist's own (2011) addition layered on top of the literal "iron bed" reading she shares with Millard, Weinfeld, Tigay, and Block (so the live question is not bed-vs-allusion but whether the dimensions also invoke Marduk, which those commentators do not affirm); the allusion is peer-reviewed and unrefuted but not yet adopted as consensus, and Lindquist herself advances it only tentatively ("may have intended to allude"); and "to produce the next king" still overstates the attested rite · back to timeline · advanced by Fr. An argument page weighs a single sub-claim adduced in support of a main interpretation and renders an adversarial assessment. The finding below: the core parallel — that Og's bed dimensions echo the cultic bed of Marduk — is better-grounded than a casual reading would assume, resting on real scholarship and a cuneiform source, and De Young's placement of that bed at the summit of Etemenanki is correct, not a confusion of two beds. What keeps the reading short of settled is not the old tomb rival — that "basalt sarcophagus" reading is superseded, and against it the Marduk-bed view plainly wins — but that the load-bearing step, the deliberate allusion to Marduk, is an addition Lindquist layers on top of the mainstream "plain-sense iron bed" reading she shares with Millard, Weinfeld, Tigay, and Block — peer-reviewed and unrefuted, but not yet taken up as consensus; and that one feature of De Young's retelling — the "to produce the next king" telos — reaches past what the evidence makes probable.

The claim

Deuteronomy 3:11 records that Og of Bashan, "the last of the Rephaim," left an iron bed (Heb. ʿereś) "nine cubits long and four cubits wide." De Young reads those dimensions as a deliberate echo of a Babylonian cultic bed:

we found a ritual bed of the same measurements in the great ziggurat of [Etemenanki] in Babylon … that ritual bed was used in these sexual rituals to produce the next king.

— Tucker Carlson Show, "Young giants" (2026), YouTube. The dimensions, on this reading, are not a measure of Og's body but a tag identifying him with Babylonian sacral kingship and its fertility cult — the same complex De Young attaches to the Nephilim. He repeats the claim across the blog, the Lord of Spirits podcast, and this interview.

Who advances it

De Young is downstream of Michael Heiser, who popularized the Og-bed↔Marduk-bed link in The Unseen Realm: "its dimensions (9 x 4 cubits) are precisely those of the cultic bed in the ziggurat called Etemenanki."1 Behind Heiser stands a genuine piece of peer-reviewed scholarship: Maria Lindquist, "King Og's Iron Bed," Catholic Biblical Quarterly 73 (2011): 477–92, which argues — against the older "bed = basalt sarcophagus/tomb" reading — that the bed's size invokes the bed of the Babylonian god Marduk, whose dimensions underscored the deity's power and might.2 So the lineage is: Lindquist (academic) → Heiser (popular) → De Young. The claim thus has a real scholarly anchor in peer-reviewed Assyriology.

The evidence for

The dimensional match is real and textually grounded, not invented:

  • A cuneiform source actually gives a bed of these dimensions. The Esagil tablet (a Neo-Babylonian metrological text, copied 229 BC, now Louvre AO 6555), edited by the Assyriologist A. R. George in Babylonian Topographical Texts (1992), describes a bed of "nine cubits the length, four cubits the breadth," with "the throne in front of the bed."3 Og's bed is also 9×4 cubits — the same two numbers.
  • The bed belongs to a real cult. The bīt erši ("house of the bed") was the bedchamber of Marduk and his consort Zarpanitu, the setting of the annual sacred marriage (hieros gamos) rite at the New Year (akitu) festival, for cosmic renewal and the land's fertility.4

On these points De Young, Heiser, and Lindquist are on solid ground, and the numerical correspondence is a legitimate basis for reading Deut 3:11 as an anti-Babylonian literary signal rather than a giant's furniture dimensions.

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The 9×4-cubit correspondence is genuine and has a cuneiform source (George) and independent academic backing (Lindquist). The bīt erši and the Marduk–Zarpanitu sacred marriage are authentic Babylonian realities. And De Young's location is right: the dimensioned bīt erši belongs to the upper temple at the summit of Etemenanki, not the ground-level Esagila — the Esagil tablet describes Esagila first (lines 1–15), then passes to the ziggurat, whose summit temple houses the "house of the bed" with its bed and throne (and a second bed on the highest platform).4 So "a ritual bed … in the great ziggurat of Etemenanki" tracks the mainstream reconstruction rather than fusing two distinct beds. The core claim — that Og's bed dimensions deliberately echo Marduk's cultic bed — is defensible.

The tomb rival is superseded; the live question is the allusion, not the bed. It helps to separate two questions the older literature ran together. Is it a bed at all? — here De Young is on the winning side. The reading's traditional competitor, that Og's "iron bed" was really his basalt sarcophagus (basalt being the dark, iron-like dolerite of Bashan), began with J. D. Michaelis in the 18th century and was the historical-critical default through S. R. Driver — but it has been steadily abandoned: von Rad objected on size (the ~14-ft object is more than double the famous sarcophagus of Ahiram of Byblos), Eslinger on language (ʿereś always means "bed," barzel always "iron" — neither is naturally "coffin" or "stone"), and others on geography (there is no basalt at Rabbath-Ammon, where the relic stood). By the 1980s A. R. Millard had displaced it with a "plain-sense" reading — a real wooden bedstead plated or adorned with iron — which the major commentaries adopted (Weinfeld, Tigay, C. J. H. Wright), and both Veijola (2006) and Lindquist (2011) treat the tomb reading as "historically and linguistically dubious." No post-2011 scholarship defends the sarcophagus reading. So against the tomb reading the Marduk-bed view clearly wins — but that is a contest De Young's reading shares with the whole mainstream, not a point peculiar to it.2

What that does not settle. De Young's specific claim is not merely "a bed, not a tomb" — on that he and the mainstream agree — but that the dimensions deliberately allude to Marduk's cultic bed. That added step is Lindquist's own (2011) proposal, and the key point is that she layers it on top of the literal-bed reading rather than against it: she explicitly follows Millard that the object "can be understood in its literal sense as an important battle trophy," then argues its measurements were also chosen to evoke Marduk's. So the disagreement is narrow. Millard, Weinfeld, Tigay, and Wright read the same literal iron bed with no Marduk allusion, and Block takes the size-note as a plain rhetorical "check it yourself" flourish underscoring the giant's bulk; on that reading the 9×4 figures mean only "the defeated king was enormous." Lindquist does not deny that — she affirms the bed's "hugeness … attests to the great stature of King Og" — but adds that, by "setting Og's bed alongside the bed of a major deity, the author likens Og to a god," assimilating him to the semidivine Rephaim of Genesis 6. So if the allusion is granted, its payoff is stronger than "Og was big" and runs squarely with De Young: Og as a more-than-human figure tagged to a Babylonian deity. The dimensional correspondence with the Esagil-tablet bed is real and striking, and the allusion is peer-reviewed and — as far as the literature shows — unrefuted; but 9 and 4 are not exotic numbers, the size-only reading remains available, and the major commentators have not taken the allusion up, which is exactly why it stays a live minority proposal rather than the consensus.

Where the framing reaches past the evidence — two features of De Young's specific retelling. Neither is demonstrably false; each reaches past the likeliest reconstruction of the evidence:

  1. "We found a ritual bed." A wording-precision issue rather than a falsehood. Read as loose shorthand for "we have a source attesting a bed of these dimensions," the statement is true. Read as a claim about an excavated artifact, it is improbable: the 9×4 figure comes from a cuneiform text (a mathematical/topographical tablet), not a dug-up object. The phrasing is defensible as shorthand; it is just ambiguous in a way that invites the stronger, less-probable reading. (That said, the bed is more than an abstraction even apart from its dimensions: the cuneiform text describes an actual cultic object — a bed with "the throne in front of the bed," in a named room of the summit temple — not merely two numbers. Herodotus 1.181 may add a separate report of a physical summit couch "of unusual size, richly adorned," but his account is too unreliable to lean on, and the standard reconstruction treats it as independent of, not corroborating, the cuneiform evidence (see below) — so the weight rests on the tablet.5)

  2. "Used in these sexual rituals to produce the next king." A plausible extrapolation that outruns the documented evidence. The attested ritual at this Babylonian bed is the divine marriage of two gods (Marduk + Zarpanitu) for cosmic order and the land's fertility, not a documented human-king ceremony — and Herodotus's separate sacred-prostitution account (1.199) should not be conflated with it.5 A documented human-king bed-rite does exist, but it is the earlier Sumerian sacred marriage (the king as Dumuzi uniting with Inanna's priestess; Iddin-Dagan A, Šulgi X) — a different deity-pair, place, and millennium — and even there the purpose is legitimation and the land's fertility, not begetting an heir. That steelman is weighed on its own page, The sacred-marriage rite as the mechanism of the Nephilim (assessment: sound); the upshot is that the form of De Young's mechanism is real, but the specific "to produce the next king" telos at this Babylonian bed is conjecture beyond the evidence.

Two further cautions. Herodotus is shaky: even the summit-couch story is secondhand (he likely never visited Babylon), is explicitly disbelieved by Herodotus himself, and has "not a single scrap of Babylonian evidence" to corroborate it — the standard reconstruction even recommends discounting the couch as possibly a story borrowed from Egyptian ritual, so it cannot be treated as independent corroboration of the cuneiform bed (a tempting move, but one the sources do not license).5 And Heiser himself hedges far more than De Young: he allows that the dimensions "may have nothing to do with [Og's] actual size" and calls the Marduk/Amorite wordplay "speculative … we cannot of course know that with any certainty."1 The match also works only as a numerical/literary echo (the numbers 9 and 4), not as a claim that two physical beds were the same size — the Babylonian cubit (~0.5 m) and the Hebrew cubit (~0.45 m) differ, and Deut 3:11's own "cubit of a man" is ambiguous.2

Assessment

Plausible. Trust the core: Og's bed dimensions plausibly echo Marduk's cultic bed as an anti-Babylonian signal, and this rests on real scholarship (Lindquist, CBQ 2011) and a real cuneiform text (Esagil tablet, ed. George), not merely on Herodotus. And De Young's location is right — the dimensioned bīt erši belongs to the summit temple of Etemenanki, so "in the great ziggurat of Etemenanki" is accurate, not a confusion of two beds. The old objection — that Og's "bed" was really his basalt sarcophagus — is now superseded (von Rad on size, Eslinger on language, Millard's plain-sense reading; no post-2011 scholarship defends it), so against the tomb reading the Marduk-bed view plainly wins. What holds it inside plausible rather than sound is narrower than a rivalry over the object: the literal iron-bed reading is mainstream (Millard, Weinfeld, Tigay; Block), and Lindquist shares it — she does not oppose the plain-sense bed but builds on it. What the major commentators do not grant is the further, load-bearing step De Young needs: that the dimensions deliberately invoke Marduk's bed. That step is Lindquist's own (2011) proposal — elegant, peer-reviewed, and (as far as the literature shows) unrefuted, but not adopted as consensus, and advanced by Lindquist herself only tentatively ("may have intended to allude"). So: the bed-not-tomb point is mainstream and De Young is on it; the Marduk allusion he layers on it is a live but minority reading — plausible, not settled. Notably, where the allusion is granted its payoff runs De Young's way: Lindquist concludes it "likens Og to a god" and reads the Rephaim/Nephilim as semidivine offspring of "the union of gods and human women," even adducing the Ugaritic god Rapaʾu — "a cognate of the Hebrew rěpāʾîm," the "King of Eternity" — who dwells in Og's own region of Ashtaroth and Edrei (Deut 1:4). What should be flagged on De Young's page is narrower — an overstated certainty in two details, not factual error: the dimensions come from a text rather than an excavated "bed" (though Herodotus independently reports a physical summit couch), and the documented rite is a divine marriage of two gods rather than an attested ceremony "to produce the next king." Stated carefully, the argument supports the supernaturalist reading; stated as De Young states it, it claims a little more than the evidence will bear.
