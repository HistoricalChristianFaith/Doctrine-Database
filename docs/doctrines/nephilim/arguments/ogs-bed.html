An argument page weighs a single sub-claim adduced in support of a main interpretation and renders an adversarial assessment. The finding below: the core parallel — that Og's bed dimensions echo the cultic bed of Marduk — is better-grounded than a casual reading would assume, resting on real scholarship and a cuneiform source, and De Young's placement of that bed at the summit of Etemenanki is correct, not a confusion of two beds. The load-bearing step — the deliberate allusion to Marduk — is one Lindquist layers on top of the mainstream "plain-sense iron bed" reading she shares with Millard, Weinfeld, Tigay, and Block; it has stood unrefuted for fifteen years and has begun to draw scholarly uptake (Laura Quick adopts it; Ronald Hendel cites it favorably), so the interpretation holds up on the evidence even though a non-allusive "size-only" reading of the same bed remains available. What reaches past the evidence is not the interpretation but De Young's retelling: "to produce the next king" invokes a real Mesopotamian idea — the king as divinely-begotten, "two-thirds divine" heir (Šulgi "born of" Ninsun; Gilgamesh) — so the overreach is not the idea but the mechanism, routing that begetting through this particular Babylonian bed-rite.

The claim

Deuteronomy 3:11 records that Og of Bashan, "the last of the Rephaim," left an iron bed (Heb. ʿereś) "nine cubits long and four cubits wide." De Young reads those dimensions as a deliberate echo of a Babylonian cultic bed:

we found a ritual bed of the same measurements in the great ziggurat of [Etemenanki] in Babylon … that ritual bed was used in these sexual rituals to produce the next king.

— Tucker Carlson Show, "Young giants" (2026), YouTube. The dimensions, on this reading, are not a measure of Og's body but a tag identifying him with Babylonian sacral kingship and its fertility cult — the same complex De Young attaches to the Nephilim. He repeats the claim across the blog, the Lord of Spirits podcast, and this interview.

Who advances it

De Young is downstream of Michael Heiser, who popularized the Og-bed↔Marduk-bed link in The Unseen Realm: "its dimensions (9 x 4 cubits) are precisely those of the cultic bed in the ziggurat called Etemenanki."1 Behind Heiser stands a genuine piece of peer-reviewed scholarship: Maria Lindquist, "King Og's Iron Bed," Catholic Biblical Quarterly 73 (2011): 477–92, which argues — against the older "bed = basalt sarcophagus/tomb" reading — that the bed's size invokes the bed of the Babylonian god Marduk, whose dimensions underscored the deity's power and might.2 So the lineage is: Lindquist (academic) → Heiser (popular) → De Young. The claim thus has a real scholarly anchor in peer-reviewed Assyriology.

The evidence for

The dimensional match is real and textually grounded, not invented:

  • A cuneiform source actually gives a bed of these dimensions. The Esagil tablet (a Neo-Babylonian metrological text, copied 229 BC, now Louvre AO 6555), edited by the Assyriologist A. R. George in Babylonian Topographical Texts (1992), describes a bed of "nine cubits the length, four cubits the breadth," with "the throne in front of the bed."3 Og's bed is also 9×4 cubits — the same two numbers.
  • The bed belongs to a real cult. The bīt erši ("house of the bed") was the bedchamber of Marduk and his consort Zarpanitu, the setting of the annual sacred marriage (hieros gamos) rite at the New Year (akitu) festival, for cosmic renewal and the land's fertility.4

On these points De Young, Heiser, and Lindquist are on solid ground, and the numerical correspondence is a legitimate basis for reading Deut 3:11 as an anti-Babylonian literary signal rather than a giant's furniture dimensions.

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The 9×4-cubit correspondence is genuine and has a cuneiform source (George) and independent academic backing (Lindquist). The bīt erši and the Marduk–Zarpanitu sacred marriage are authentic Babylonian realities. And De Young's location is right: the dimensioned bīt erši belongs to the upper temple at the summit of Etemenanki, not the ground-level Esagila — the Esagil tablet describes Esagila first (lines 1–15), then passes to the ziggurat, whose summit temple houses the "house of the bed" with its bed and throne (and a second bed on the highest platform).4 So "a ritual bed … in the great ziggurat of Etemenanki" tracks the mainstream reconstruction rather than fusing two distinct beds. The core claim — that Og's bed dimensions deliberately echo Marduk's cultic bed — is defensible.

The tomb rival is superseded; the live question is the allusion, not the bed. It helps to separate two questions the older literature ran together. Is it a bed at all? — here De Young is on the winning side. The reading's traditional competitor, that Og's "iron bed" was really his basalt sarcophagus (basalt being the dark, iron-like dolerite of Bashan), began with J. D. Michaelis in the 18th century and was the historical-critical default through S. R. Driver — but it has been steadily abandoned: von Rad objected on size (the ~14-ft object is more than double the famous sarcophagus of Ahiram of Byblos), Eslinger on language (ʿereś always means "bed," barzel always "iron" — neither is naturally "coffin" or "stone"), and others on geography (there is no basalt at Rabbath-Ammon, where the relic stood). By the 1980s A. R. Millard had displaced it with a "plain-sense" reading — a real wooden bedstead plated or adorned with iron — which the major commentaries adopted (Weinfeld, Tigay, C. J. H. Wright), and both Veijola (2006) and Lindquist (2011) treat the tomb reading as "historically and linguistically dubious." No post-2011 scholarship defends the sarcophagus reading. So against the tomb reading the Marduk-bed view clearly wins — but that is a contest De Young's reading shares with the whole mainstream, not a point peculiar to it.2

What that does not settle. De Young's specific claim is not merely "a bed, not a tomb" — on that he and the mainstream agree — but that the dimensions deliberately allude to Marduk's cultic bed. That added step is Lindquist's own (2011) proposal, and the key point is that she layers it on top of the literal-bed reading rather than against it: she explicitly follows Millard that the object "can be understood in its literal sense as an important battle trophy," then argues its measurements were also chosen to evoke Marduk's. So the disagreement is narrow. Millard, Weinfeld, Tigay, and Wright read the same literal iron bed with no Marduk allusion, and Block takes the size-note as a plain rhetorical "check it yourself" flourish underscoring the giant's bulk; on that reading the 9×4 figures mean only "the defeated king was enormous." Lindquist does not deny that — she affirms the bed's "hugeness … attests to the great stature of King Og" — but adds that, by "setting Og's bed alongside the bed of a major deity, the author likens Og to a god," assimilating him to the semidivine Rephaim of Genesis 6. So if the allusion is granted, its payoff is stronger than "Og was big" and runs squarely with De Young: Og as a more-than-human figure tagged to a Babylonian deity. The dimensional correspondence with the Esagil-tablet bed is real and striking, and the allusion is peer-reviewed and — as far as the literature shows — unrefuted. Two cautions once kept it tentative: 9 and 4 are not exotic numbers, so a size-only reading remains available; and the major commentators had not taken the allusion up. The second has weakened. The standard commentaries that read the bed without any Marduk allusion — Weinfeld (AB, 1991), Tigay (JPS, 1996) — were written before Lindquist (2011), so their silence is chronological, not a considered rejection; only Block (2012) is genuinely contemporary, and he reads the size-note as rhetorical hyperbole rather than weighing and dismissing the allusion. And the allusion is no longer without takers: Laura Quick (Oxford) adopts Lindquist's Marduk-bed parallel and folds it into her own reading of Og, and Ronald Hendel (Berkeley) cites it favorably alongside Doak.2 The uptake is still modest — Quick's is a credentialed-author essay rather than a peer-reviewed monograph, and Hendel's is an approving citation rather than a developed endorsement — but the reading has moved from "unrefuted but unadopted" to "unrefuted and beginning to be adopted," with no contrary voice. So it is no longer held back for want of uptake; it is the best-supported and only argued reading of the dimensions, short only of majority adoption.

Where the framing reaches past the evidence — two features of De Young's specific retelling. Neither is demonstrably false; each reaches past the likeliest reconstruction of the evidence:

  1. "We found a ritual bed." A wording-precision issue rather than a falsehood. Read as loose shorthand for "we have a source attesting a bed of these dimensions," the statement is true. Read as a claim about an excavated artifact, it is improbable: the 9×4 figure comes from a cuneiform text (a mathematical/topographical tablet), not a dug-up object. The phrasing is defensible as shorthand; it is just ambiguous in a way that invites the stronger, less-probable reading. (That said, the bed is more than an abstraction even apart from its dimensions: the cuneiform text describes an actual cultic object — a bed with "the throne in front of the bed," in a named room of the summit temple — not merely two numbers. Herodotus 1.181 may add a separate report of a physical summit couch "of unusual size, richly adorned," but his account is too unreliable to lean on, and the standard reconstruction treats it as independent of, not corroborating, the cuneiform evidence (see below) — so the weight rests on the tablet.5)

  2. "Used in these sexual rituals to produce the next king." A plausible extrapolation that outruns the documented evidence. The ritual associated with this Babylonian bed is the divine marriage of two gods (Marduk + Zarpanitu) for cosmic order and the land's fertility, not a documented human-king ceremony — and whether even that marriage was regularly performed at this bed, rather than functioning as a cosmic/literary theme, is itself debated (see the festival-setting note below); Herodotus's separate sacred-prostitution account (1.199) should not be conflated with it.5 A documented human-king bed-rite does exist, but it is the earlier Sumerian sacred marriage (the king as Dumuzi uniting with Inanna's priestess; Iddin-Dagan A, Šulgi X) — a different deity-pair, place, and millennium — and even there the documented purpose is legitimation and the land's fertility, not begetting an heir. That steelman is weighed on its own page, The sacred-marriage rite as the mechanism of the Nephilim (assessment: sound). The key qualification is that what De Young's phrase invokes is firmer than the bare objection allows: the idea of a divinely-begotten, "two-thirds divine" royal heir is genuine Mesopotamian ideology — kings claim a deity as parent (Šulgi "born to" the goddess Ninsun, "good seed of Lugalbanda"; Gilgamesh "two-thirds god, one-third man"), and the king is reckoned to "partake of divinity." So "to produce the next king" splits three ways, not two: as "perpetuate the divine kingship" (the king's and his house's favorable destiny) it runs with the grain; as a claim that the heir was reckoned divinely begotten and "two-thirds divine" it states a real royal ideology, not a conjecture; what alone outruns the evidence is the mechanism — routing that begetting through this Babylonian sacred-marriage bed, when the divine-birth language belongs to royal hymnody (and the apkallu-hybrid strand — Lu-nanna, "two-thirds apkallu"), not to a documented procreative rite at the akītu.

The festival setting cuts both ways. One consideration tells for the dynastic reading of "to produce the next king." The dimensioned bīt erši does not sit in isolation: it belongs to the summit temple of Etemenanki within the akītu (Babylonian New Year) festival, whose central documented function was the annual re-legitimation of the king. The rite turns on the king — the high priest strips his royal insignia, slaps him, hears his "negative confession" that he has not failed Babylon, then reinvests him — and "only a legitimate king … could take the hand of [Marduk]," so the festival was not held when no king reigned. So kingship is structurally central to the bed's setting, and reading "to produce the next king" as "perpetuate the divine kingship" runs with the grain of where the bed sits, not against it.6 But the same inquiry tightens a caution the other way. Whether a sacred-marriage rite was actually performed at this bed during the akītu is itself contested: the surviving Nisannu ritual texts describe the king's legitimation, not a marriage; much of the "hieros gamos in the akītu" picture descends from S. H. Hooke's early-20th-century myth-and-ritual school; and the clearest evidence for a performed royal sacred marriage is the Assyrian Nabu–Tašmetu rite of the month Šabatu — a different deity-pair and season, known from a single letter, with the king's own participation uncertain. So the room's identity as Marduk and Zarpanitu's bedchamber (the "house of the bed") is solid, but a regularly performed marriage rite there is not a settled datum — which means the "rite at this bed" the argument is weighed against is itself less firm than a flat statement would imply.6

Two further cautions. Herodotus is shaky: even the summit-couch story is secondhand (he likely never visited Babylon), is explicitly disbelieved by Herodotus himself, and has "not a single scrap of Babylonian evidence" to corroborate it — the standard reconstruction even recommends discounting the couch as possibly a story borrowed from Egyptian ritual, so it cannot be treated as independent corroboration of the cuneiform bed (a tempting move, but one the sources do not license).5 And Heiser himself hedges far more than De Young: he allows that the dimensions "may have nothing to do with [Og's] actual size" and calls the Marduk/Amorite wordplay "speculative … we cannot of course know that with any certainty."1 The match also works only as a numerical/literary echo (the numbers 9 and 4), not as a claim that two physical beds were the same size — the Babylonian cubit (~0.5 m) and the Hebrew cubit (~0.45 m) differ, and Deut 3:11's own "cubit of a man" is ambiguous.2

Assessment

Sound. Trust the core: Og's bed dimensions echo Marduk's cultic bed as an anti-Babylonian signal, and this rests on real scholarship (Lindquist, CBQ 2011) and a real cuneiform text (Esagil tablet, ed. George), not merely on Herodotus. And De Young's location is right — the dimensioned bīt erši belongs to the summit temple of Etemenanki, so "in the great ziggurat of Etemenanki" is accurate, not a confusion of two beds. The old objection — that Og's "bed" was really his basalt sarcophagus — is now superseded (von Rad on size, Eslinger on language, Millard's plain-sense reading; no post-2011 scholarship defends it), so against the tomb reading the Marduk-bed view plainly wins. The load-bearing step De Young needs — that the dimensions deliberately invoke Marduk's bed — is Lindquist's own (2011) proposal, layered on the literal iron-bed reading she shares with the mainstream (Millard, Weinfeld, Tigay; Block), not set against it. It rates sound on the "well-grounded and unrefuted" standard rather than on consensus: it has a cuneiform anchor and a peer-reviewed argument, it has gone unrefuted for fifteen years, and it has begun to be adopted — Laura Quick (Oxford) takes it up in her reading of Og and Ronald Hendel (Berkeley) cites it favorably, while the older commentaries' silence is chronological (they predate 2011). It is short only of majority uptake, and a non-allusive "size-only" reading of the same bed remains available — but it is the only argued reading of the dimensions, with no contrary case in the literature. Notably, where the allusion is granted its payoff runs De Young's way: Lindquist concludes it "likens Og to a god" and reads the Rephaim/Nephilim as semidivine offspring of "the union of gods and human women," even adducing the Ugaritic god Rapaʾu — "a cognate of the Hebrew rěpāʾîm," the "King of Eternity" — who dwells in Og's own region of Ashtaroth and Edrei (Deut 1:4). What should be flagged on De Young's page is narrower, and it is in De Young's framing rather than the interpretation: the dimensions come from a text rather than an excavated "bed" (though Herodotus independently reports a physical summit couch), and "to produce the next king" splits. That last splits three ways: as "perpetuate the divine kingship" it runs with the grain (the akītu's documented core is the king's annual re-legitimation, so kingship is structurally central to the bed's setting); as a claim that the heir was reckoned divinely begotten and "two-thirds divine" it states a real royal ideology (kings "born of" deities — Šulgi of Ninsun, Gilgamesh "two-thirds god"), not a conjecture; what alone outruns the evidence is the mechanism — routing that begetting through this Babylonian sacred-marriage bed (whose regular performance there is itself debated), when the divine-birth language belongs to royal hymnody and the apkallu-hybrid strand, not to a documented procreative rite. Stated carefully, the argument supports the supernaturalist reading; the single thing to keep precise is the mechanism De Young attaches to it.
