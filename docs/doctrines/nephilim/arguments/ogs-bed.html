Og's bed and the bed of Marduk

Type: supporting argument / crux (off-timeline) · Assessment: plausible — the dimensional parallel is well-grounded, but De Young's framing of it overstates the evidence · back to timeline · advanced by Fr. Stephen De Young

An argument page weighs a single sub-claim adduced in support of a main interpretation and renders an adversarial assessment. The finding below: the core parallel — that Og's bed dimensions echo the cultic bed of Marduk — is better-grounded than a casual reading would assume, resting on real scholarship and a cuneiform source, while three specific features of De Young's retelling are plausible reconstructions that reach past what the evidence makes probable.

The claim

Deuteronomy 3:11 records that Og of Bashan, "the last of the Rephaim," left an iron bed (Heb. ʿereś) "nine cubits long and four cubits wide." De Young reads those dimensions as a deliberate echo of a Babylonian cultic bed:

we found a ritual bed of the same measurements in the great ziggurat of [Etemenanki] in Babylon … that ritual bed was used in these sexual rituals to produce the next king.

— Tucker Carlson Show, "Young giants" (2026), YouTube. The dimensions, on this reading, are not a measure of Og's body but a tag identifying him with Babylonian sacral kingship and its fertility cult — the same complex De Young attaches to the Nephilim. He repeats the claim across the blog, the Lord of Spirits podcast, and this interview.

Who advances it

De Young is downstream of Michael Heiser, who popularized the Og-bed↔Marduk-bed link in The Unseen Realm: "its dimensions (9 x 4 cubits) are precisely those of the cultic bed in the ziggurat called Etemenanki."1 Behind Heiser stands a genuine piece of peer-reviewed scholarship: Maria Lindquist, "King Og's Iron Bed," Catholic Biblical Quarterly 73 (2011): 477–92, which argues — against the older "bed = basalt sarcophagus/tomb" reading — that the bed's size invokes the bed of the Babylonian god Marduk, whose dimensions underscored the deity's power and might.2 So the lineage is: Lindquist (academic) → Heiser (popular) → De Young. The claim thus has a real scholarly anchor in peer-reviewed Assyriology.

The evidence for

The dimensional match is real and textually grounded, not invented:

  • A cuneiform source actually gives a bed of these dimensions. The Esagil tablet (a Neo-Babylonian metrological text, copied 229 BC, now Louvre AO 6555), edited by the Assyriologist A. R. George in Babylonian Topographical Texts (1992), describes a bed of "nine cubits the length, four cubits the breadth," with "the throne in front of the bed."3 Og's bed is also 9×4 cubits — the same two numbers.
  • The bed belongs to a real cult. The bīt erši ("house of the bed") was the bedchamber of Marduk and his consort Zarpanitu, the setting of the annual sacred marriage (hieros gamos) rite at the New Year (akitu) festival, for cosmic renewal and the land's fertility.4

On these points De Young, Heiser, and Lindquist are on solid ground, and the numerical correspondence is a legitimate basis for reading Deut 3:11 as an anti-Babylonian literary signal rather than a giant's furniture dimensions.

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The 9×4-cubit correspondence is genuine and has a cuneiform source (George) and independent academic backing (Lindquist). The bīt erši and the Marduk–Zarpanitu sacred marriage are authentic Babylonian realities. The core claim — that Og's bed dimensions deliberately echo Marduk's cultic bed — is defensible.

Where the framing is plausible but not probable — three features of De Young's specific retelling. None is demonstrably false; each is a coherent reading that reaches past the likeliest reconstruction of the evidence:

  1. "We found a ritual bed." A wording-precision issue rather than a falsehood. Read as loose shorthand for "we have a source attesting a bed of these dimensions," the statement is true. Read as a claim about an excavated artifact, it is improbable: the 9×4 figure comes from a cuneiform text (a mathematical/topographical tablet), not a dug-up object. The phrasing is defensible as shorthand; it is just ambiguous in a way that invites the stronger, less-probable reading.

  2. Location: "in the great ziggurat of Etemenanki." Plausible but not the likeliest reconstruction. It is possible that the dimensioned bed and the famous summit couch are one object — but the more probable reading keeps them distinct. The dimensioned bed (the bīt erši of Marduk and Zarpanitu) belongs to the Esagila temple at ground level, not the summit of the Etemenanki ziggurat.4 The image of a bed at the summit of the tower comes only from Herodotus 1.181 — a "couch of unusual size, richly adorned" — and Herodotus gives no dimensions at all.5 De Young (following Heiser) fuses the dimensioned Esagila bed onto the undimensioned Etemenanki summit couch: a coherent conjecture, not the likeliest one.

  3. "Used in these sexual rituals to produce the next king." A plausible extrapolation that outruns the documented evidence. The attested ritual at this Babylonian bed is the divine marriage of two gods (Marduk + Zarpanitu) for cosmic order and the land's fertility, not a documented human-king ceremony — and Herodotus's separate sacred-prostitution account (1.199) should not be conflated with it.5 A documented human-king bed-rite does exist, but it is the earlier Sumerian sacred marriage (the king as Dumuzi uniting with Inanna's priestess; Iddin-Dagan A, Šulgi X) — a different deity-pair, place, and millennium — and even there the purpose is legitimation and the land's fertility, not begetting an heir. That steelman is weighed on its own page, The sacred-marriage rite as the mechanism of the Nephilim (assessment: sound); the upshot is that the form of De Young's mechanism is real, but the specific "to produce the next king" telos at this Babylonian bed is conjecture beyond the evidence.

Two further cautions. Herodotus is shaky: even the summit-couch story is secondhand (he likely never visited Babylon), is explicitly disbelieved by Herodotus himself, and has "not a single scrap of Babylonian evidence" to corroborate it.5 And Heiser himself hedges far more than De Young: he allows that the dimensions "may have nothing to do with [Og's] actual size" and calls the Marduk/Amorite wordplay "speculative … we cannot of course know that with any certainty."1 The match also works only as a numerical/literary echo (the numbers 9 and 4), not as a claim that two physical beds were the same size — the Babylonian cubit (~0.5 m) and the Hebrew cubit (~0.45 m) differ, and Deut 3:11's own "cubit of a man" is ambiguous.2

Assessment

Plausible. Trust the core: Og's bed dimensions plausibly echo Marduk's cultic bed as an anti-Babylonian signal, and this rests on real scholarship (Lindquist, CBQ 2011) and a real cuneiform text (Esagil tablet, ed. George), not merely on Herodotus. What should be flagged on De Young's page is the overstated certainty, not factual error: the dimensions come from a text rather than an excavated "bed"; the dimensioned bed sits more probably in the Esagila temple than at the Etemenanki summit (that couch is Herodotus's, and undimensioned); and the documented rite is a divine marriage of two gods rather than an attested ceremony "to produce the next king." Each of these is a plausible reading, but each is the less-probable one, presented as settled. Stated carefully, the argument supports the supernaturalist reading; stated as De Young states it, it claims more than the evidence will bear.
