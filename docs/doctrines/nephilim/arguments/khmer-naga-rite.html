The Khmer nāga-tower rite (Zhou Daguan) as a sacral-kingship parallel

An argument page weighs a single sub-claim adduced for a main interpretation and renders an adversarial assessment. This one tests the cross-cultural parallel De Young leans on for his sacral-kingship mechanism of the Nephilim: that a king sleeping ritually with a spirit is not a fanciful reconstruction but a documented institution. It is a companion to the cluster — the Og's-bed and sacred-marriage pages weigh the ancient Near Eastern form, and the Daijōsai page weighs its modern survival in Japan; this page asks whether the Khmer rite reported by Zhou Daguan supplies a further, independent witness. The question is factual: does such a rite exist, attested where De Young implies, and does it mean what his mechanism needs it to mean?

The claim

Across the Lord of Spirits material and restated in the Tucker Carlson interview, De Young presents the sacral-kingship sex-rite — a divinized king ritually uniting with a goddess- or spirit-figure on a cultic bed — as a cross-cultural and long-lived institution, not a one-off ancient Near Eastern oddity. Alongside the Babylonian bed of Marduk and the Japanese Daijōsai he adduces a Khmer parallel: a king of Angkor who "spends the night" with a serpent-spirit. He ties the Japanese rite, on the Daijōsai page, to "the Khmer parallel on the Lord of Spirits page."1 The podcast episode making the Khmer point is not transcribed in our corpus, so rather than weigh a paraphrase this page goes to the primary source the parallel rests on — the thirteenth-century eyewitness report of Zhou Daguan — and asks whether it bears the weight: (a) does the rite exist as described; (b) what is its attested function; and (c) does it support the specific "ritual bed → produce the next king" mechanism.

The evidence for — a firsthand report, and it says almost exactly this

The parallel is not invented. Our only first-person account of daily life in the Khmer Empire is the Customs of Cambodia (真臘風土記, Zhenla Fengtu Ji; also translated A Record of Cambodia: The Land and Its People) by Zhou Daguan, a Yuan-dynasty Chinese envoy who stayed at Angkor in 1296–1297. In his chapter on the palace he reports, of the gold tower at the center of the royal compound (commonly identified with the temple Phimeanakas in Angkor Thom), in Peter Harris's translation:

Inside the palace there is a gold tower, at the summit of which the king sleeps at night. The local people all say that in the tower lives a nine-headed snake spirit which is the lord of the earth for the entire country. Every night it appears in the form of a woman, and the king first shares his bed with her and has sex with her.

— Zhou Daguan, A Record of Cambodia (trans. Peter Harris, 2007).2 The rite is nightly and binding both ways: Zhou adds that if for a single night the spirit fails to appear, it is the sign that the king's death is near; and if the king himself stays away for one night, disaster is sure to follow. Not even the queen dared enter, and only in the second watch did the king rejoin his own wives and concubines.3

For De Young's purpose this is, on its face, the cleanest of his parallels. It is a single primary text, not a reconstruction; the partner is unambiguously a spirit (a nine-headed nāga), not merely a priestess; the union is explicitly sexual and explicitly nightly; and the king's life and the realm's welfare are said to hang on it. The spirit's title — "lord of the earth for the entire country" — marks the meaning historians of Angkor give the rite: the nāga is the sovereign owner of the soil, and the king's nightly union with her is his sacral marriage to the land's own sovereignty, the mystical ground of his legitimacy.4

And the dynastic-descent dimension that De Young's larger mechanism wants — a royal line sprung from a union with a divine/serpent being — is independently present in the Khmer charter myth. The kings of Funan/Cambodia traced their line to Kaundinya (Preah Thong), a foreign brahmin, and the nāga princess Soma (Neang Neak), daughter of the nāga king — a union recorded by the third-century Chinese envoys Kang Tai and Zhu Ying and treated by George Coedès as the legitimating charter of an "Indianized" dynasty that claimed descent from both gods and nāgas. The nightly rite and the foundation myth are two faces of one idea: Khmer kingship is constituted by the king's bond with the serpent-power of the land.5

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The rite is genuinely attested, by a named eyewitness at the place and date implied, in words that match De Young's picture closely: a divinized king ritually and sexually unites, night after night, with a serpent-spirit that appears as a woman, the throne and the realm depending on it. As a documented instance of "a sacral king sleeps with a spirit" — and, with the Kaundinya–Soma myth, of a dynasty claiming descent from a union with a serpent-being — the Khmer material is solid, well-known scholarship, and an apt cross-cultural support for the form of his mechanism.

What is overstated or imprecise — two qualifications, neither fatal:

  1. The attested function is legitimacy and the realm's welfare, not "to produce the next king." Zhou Daguan ties the rite to the king's own life and the kingdom's safety, and the nāga's title fixes its sense as sovereignty over the soil; the text says nothing of begetting an heir. This is the same overlay flagged on the Og's-bed, sacred-marriage, and Daijōsai pages — De Young's recurring "to produce the next king" reads heir-production into rites whose documented purpose is legitimation. The mitigation here is real, though: the heir/lineage element is not absent from the Khmer complex, only relocated — it lives in the foundation myth (descent from the nāga princess Soma), not in the nightly tower-rite.

  2. It is reported belief about a secret rite, not an eyewitness account of the act. Zhou is careful: "the local people all say." He is transmitting what he was told happens in a tower no outsider (not even the queen) could enter, not something he saw. That is the ordinary status of evidence for a secret royal mystery and does not discredit it — the report is taken seriously by every historian of Angkor — but it means the Khmer case is a well-attested belief and institution, not a directly observed consummation, and should be cited as such rather than as harder than it is.

Assessment

Sound. Of De Young's "surviving sacral-kingship" parallels this is among the strongest: a single firsthand primary source (Zhou Daguan, 1296–97) attests, in nearly the terms his mechanism needs, a divinized king coupling nightly with a serpent-spirit that appears as a woman — the "lord of the earth for the entire country" — with his life and the realm's welfare resting on the union; and the Khmer dynasty independently claimed descent from a king's marriage to a nāga princess (Kaundinya and Soma). As a cross-cultural witness that "a divinized king ritually unites with a spirit-as-woman" is a documented, not fanciful, institution, the parallel holds firmly. Two cautions keep it honest rather than overturning it: the rite's attested function is the king's legitimacy and the land's welfare, not "to produce the next king" (his recurring overlay — though the heir/lineage motif does surface elsewhere in the Khmer complex, in the foundation myth); and Zhou reports it as what "the local people all say" of a secret tower, so it is an attested belief, not an eyewitnessed act. Stated as "a thirteenth-century Khmer king was reported to spend each night with a serpent-spirit appearing as a woman, the realm's sovereign 'owner of the soil,'" the claim is true and well-sourced; only the "to produce the next king" gloss should be flagged on De Young's page, consistent with the rest of the cluster.
