Does Matthew 22:30 ("they neither marry") foreclose the angelic reading?

Type: supporting argument / crux (off-timeline) · Assessment: sound — read in context the verse describes what the unfallen angels in heaven do not do, and grounds the non-marriage in deathlessness (no need to procreate), not in an incapacity to unite; so it does not rule out angels who abandoned heaven (Jude 6) having married. It clears one prop of the standard objection, but only one — the heavier "spirits are incorporeal" premise survives untouched and needs a separate embodiment mechanism · back to timeline · advanced by Doug Wilson

An argument page weighs a single sub-claim adduced in support of a main interpretation and renders an adversarial assessment of the interpretation it serves. This one tests Wilson's rebuttal of the classic objection that Matthew 22:30 rules out the angelic reading of Genesis 6 — the objection Chrysostom presses in Homily 22 on Genesis: angels are incorporeal, and since at the resurrection "they neither marry," it is "not for that kind of incorporeal creature to ever feel the onset of desire." Wilson answers that Jesus reports what the angels in heaven do, not what is metaphysically impossible — leaving room for the angels who "left their own habitation" (Jude 6) to have married. The crux: does Jesus' statement describe a contingent practice or an ontological incapacity?

The claim

Jesus does not say that this cannot be done. He says that it is not done by the angels in Heaven. The reason it is not done by them is that they were not the ones who left their assigned habitation.

— Doug Wilson, The Nephilim, Hades, and Other Oddments (expository sermon), dougwils.com.1 The asserted point is narrow and defensive: Matthew 22:30 is a description of the heavenly angels' state, not a denial that angels are able to marry — so it does not foreclose the reading that fallen angels did so in Genesis 6.

Who advances it

The reading is Wilson's, in this 2021 sermon, but the move is the standard reply of the angelic camp to the oldest patristic objection. The objection is Chrysostom's, who in Homily 22 on Genesis stacks three reasons against reading the "sons of God" as angels — angels are nowhere called "sons of God"; their incorporeal nature could not "be reduced to association with corporeal creatures"; and, clinching it, "do you not hear the words of Christ about the being of angels, 'At the resurrection they neither marry nor are given in marriage, but are like angels of God'? After all, it is not for that kind of incorporeal creature to ever feel the onset of desire."2 Wilson's rejoinder isolates the Matthew 22:30 prop of that bundle and argues it does not carry the weight Chrysostom puts on it.

The evidence for

On the narrow point Wilson is right, and on three independent grounds.

  1. The verse is scoped to the angels who are in heaven. Matthew has "as the angels of God in heaven," and Mark's parallel is sharper still — "as the angels which are in heaven" (Mark 12:25).3 The sentence makes a claim about the heavenly host as it now stands. Jude 6's actors are by definition the opposite case: angels who "kept not their first estate, but left their own habitation."4 A statement about angels in heaven simply does not reach angels who departed heaven, whatever else is true of them.

  2. Scripture itself grounds the non-marriage in deathlessness, not incapacity. Luke's version supplies the reason explicitly: the risen "neither marry, nor are given in marriage: neither can they die any more: for they are equal unto the angels" (Luke 20:35–36) — i.e., they do not marry because they no longer die, and marriage exists to replace what death removes.5 The cause is the absence of a procreative need, a contingent fact about the deathless state — not a built-in inability to unite.

  3. The patristic reading of the verse says the same. The Fathers expounding Matthew 22:30 in its own context tie the non-marriage to the cessation of death, not to an ontological bar. Chrysostom himself, on Matthew, insists the logic runs from state to practice: "not because they do not marry, therefore are they angels, but because they are as angels, therefore they do not marry." Pseudo-Chrysostom is blunter: "we marry to the end that that which death consumes, birth may replenish; therefore where the law of death is taken away, the cause of birth is taken away likewise."6 On that account the heavenly angels do not marry because, being deathless, they have no occasion to — precisely Wilson's "is not / cannot" distinction, drawn from the tradition's own gloss.

Adversarial assessment

What holds. The narrow exegetical claim is sound. Matthew 22:30 is a present-tense description of the unfallen heavenly angels, scoped by "in heaven" (Mark: "which are in heaven"), and both Luke's own clause and the patristic exposition root the non-marriage in deathlessness rather than incapacity. Nothing in the verse says angels cannot unite; it says the angels in heaven, like the deathless risen, have no place for marriage. Jude 6's fallen angels fall outside that scope by definition. So the verse does not, by itself, foreclose the angelic reading — Chrysostom over-reads it when he treats it as proof of an incapacity.

What the move does not accomplish — and where Wilson's framing can mislead if pressed too far:

  1. It clears only one prop of the objection, and not the load-bearing one. Chrysostom's case against the angelic reading does not rest on Matthew 22:30 alone; the verse is adduced to illustrate a prior premise — that the "incorporeal nature" of angels could not "be reduced to association with corporeal creatures."2 That metaphysical premise (spirits are bodiless and so cannot perform a bodily union) is untouched by showing that Matthew 22:30 describes a contingent practice. Defeating the Matthew prop leaves the real objection standing.

  2. The objection is met, if at all, by a separate embodiment mechanism — which is contested. The angelic reading still owes an account of how spirits beget. That account is supplied not by Matthew 22:30 but by the mechanisms weighed on their own pages: Wilson's relinquished-celestial-body reading of Jude 6 (assessment: weak) and De Young's sacral-kingship ritual (assessment: sound). Clearing Matthew 22:30 is necessary housekeeping for the angelic camp, not a positive argument for it.

  3. The rebuttal is defensive, not probative. "Matthew 22:30 does not rule it out" removes an obstacle; it does not move the reading from possible to probable. The positive weight for the angelic reading rests elsewhere (the Second Temple and ante-Nicene witnesses on the timeline, Jude/2 Peter), not on this verse.

One concession the objection's side should make in return: Wilson's distinction is not special pleading. The "angels in heaven do not, fallen angels might" structure is exactly the structure of Jude 6 and 2 Peter 2:4, which single out angels by their departure from their proper station. So reading Matthew 22:30 as silent about those angels is the natural, not a strained, reading.

Assessment

Sound. On its own terms Matthew 22:30 does not foreclose the angelic reading. The saying describes the unfallen angels "in heaven" (Mark: "which are in heaven"), and Scripture itself supplies the reason for the non-marriage as deathlessness — "neither can they die any more: for they are equal unto the angels" (Luke 20:36) — a contingent ground that the patristic exposition of the verse echoes (Chrysostom: "because they are as angels, therefore they do not marry"; Pseudo-Chrysostom: "where the law of death is taken away, the cause of birth is taken away likewise"). Angels who "left their own habitation" (Jude 6) lie outside a statement about angels in heaven, so Wilson's "is not / cannot" distinction holds, and Chrysostom over-reads the verse when he makes it prove an incapacity. But the win is strictly defensive and strictly partial: it removes the Matthew 22:30 prop from the objection without touching Chrysostom's deeper premise that angels are incorporeal and so cannot perform a bodily union — a premise answered, if at all, only by a separate embodiment mechanism (Wilson's oikētērion, assessment: weak; De Young's sacral-marriage rite, assessment: sound). Trust Wilson's reading of the verse; the thing to keep in view on his page is that clearing Matthew 22:30 is not, by itself, an argument for the angelic reading.
