Dr. Taylor Marshall on the Nephilim

Dates: 2018–2023 · "Angels having Relations with Humans within Jude and 2 Peter and 1 Enoch" (2018) · Podcast ep. 968 (2023) (YouTube)
Summary: Dr. Taylor Marshall (b. 1978), a Roman Catholic writer and podcaster of a Thomist cast, is a third modern popularizer of the angelic / Watcher reading, distinct in confession from Fr. Stephen De Young (Orthodox) and Doug Wilson (Reformed). In a 2018 essay he lays out the ancient supernatural reading sympathetically: 1 Enoch's Watchers copulate with human women and beget the Nephilim, Jude directly quotes the book, and 2 Peter borrows its word "Tartarus" — with the Wisdom of Solomon reading the Flood as a divine destruction of "the proud giants." He does not firmly commit, but presents the Sethite reading as "the accepted tradition of Catholicism" while treating the angelic reading as the older, near-unanimous pre-Augustinian one, and poses his framing question: would Augustine and Aquinas have revised their Sethite reading had they had access to 1 Enoch and the Dead Sea Scrolls? He hedges — calling the Sethite reading "plausible" and stressing that evil is not genetic — and rests his own case on the New Testament's use of the Enochic tradition rather than on the literal history behind it.

Claims & evidence

1 Enoch, the Watchers, and the begetting of the giants

Sometime around the 200s BC a Jewish document called "Book of Enoch" or 1 Enoch was produced.

These Watchers corrupt humanity by teaching them evil arts… These angels also seduce human women and copulate with them. The women give birth to nephilim or giants. God causes these giants to fight and die.

— Angels having Relations with Humans within Jude and 2 Peter and 1 Enoch, taylormarshall.com. Marshall summarizes the Enochic Watcher-legend — the angelic descent, the corrupting arts, the union with women, and the giant offspring — as the background the New Testament writers inherit.

Jude quotes 1 Enoch; 2 Peter borrows its "Tartarus"

It's not only this direct quotation of 1 Enoch by Jude, but Jude (and 2 Peter) allude to the fantastical events of 1 Enoch, namely the sexual encounters of fallen angels with human women, which gives birth to the nephilim or "giants." The birth of the giants, according to 1 Enoch, is the reason for the Noah's Flood.

— Angels having Relations with Humans…, taylormarshall.com. He notes that Jude 14–15 directly quotes 1 Enoch (1:9), and that 2 Peter 2:4 "explicitly uses Enoch's word 'Tartarus'" (the verb tartaróō) for the imprisonment of the fallen angels — the same two textual hooks De Young and Wilson press. (Marshall further holds that 2 Peter's "Tartarus" is a deliberate borrowing from the Greek Titanomachy — a distinct sub-claim weighed on the argument page 2 Peter's "Tartarus" as a Greek/Titanomachy borrowing (assessment: plausible), not on his witness here.)

The Wisdom of Solomon reads the Flood as a destruction of the giants

And from the beginning also when the proud giants (γιγάντων) perished, the hope of the world fleeing to a vessel, which was governed by thy hand, left to the world seed of generation. (Wisdom 14:6)

— Angels having Relations with Humans…, taylormarshall.com, quoting Wisdom 14:6. Marshall reads the deuterocanonical Wisdom of Solomon as itself presupposing the giant-tradition — "the proud giants" (τῶν γιγάντων) perishing in the Flood while Noah's vessel preserves the "seed of generation" — and adds that St. Paul, who quotes Wisdom "about seven times," likely shared the belief and "considered Wisdom as part of his Bible." (That canonicity inference — from a tally of Pauline allusions to Paul's holding Wisdom as Scripture — is a distinct claim, weighed on the argument page Does Paul's use of the Wisdom of Solomon (≈7 times) show he received it as Scripture? (assessment: weak); see the flag below. The same book anchors De Young's cannibal-cult argument, from Wisdom 12, a distinct strand.)

Sirach names the giants as beyond propitiation

He was not pacified toward the old giants, who fell away in the strength of their foolishness. (Ecclesiasticus 16:7)

In the 2023 podcast Marshall adduces Sirach 16:7 as a second deuterocanonical witness to the Genesis-6 giants, tying it to the 1 Enoch scene in which Enoch's plea for the antediluvian rebels is refused: the "old giants" toward whom God "was not pacified" are the canonical trace of that unforgiven judgment. It reads Genesis 6's gigantes as historical figures destroyed for their strength-trusting pride — the same premise as the Wisdom of Solomon passage above. (Weighed on its own Sirach page.)

Jude and 2 Peter use the legend as a teaching paradigm — whatever its literal status

Regardless of whether Jude and 2 Peter believed the Enochic "Watcher-Angel legend," we know for a fact that they applied it as a teaching paradigm for their contemporary Apostolic Church.

— Angels having Relations with Humans…, taylormarshall.com. Marshall's own conclusion is deliberately reserved: he holds it "a fact" that the two epistles wield the Watcher-legend rhetorically against the false teachers of their day, but leaves open whether they took it as literal history — a hermeneutical move that lets him commend the angelic reading's antiquity without staking his case on angel–human breeding. The exemplary reading itself is sound and mainstream; it is only his framing of it that overreaches (see the flag below). Weighed on the argument page The Watcher legend as a paraenetic exemplum against the false teachers (assessment: sound).

The framing question: would Augustine and Aquinas have revised the Sethite reading?

In the 2023 podcast Marshall surveys the Second Temple and patristic witnesses and, presenting the Sethite reading as "the accepted tradition of Catholicism," poses his central question — whether Augustine and Aquinas, the two Fathers who fixed that tradition, would have read Genesis 6 differently had they possessed 1 Enoch and the Dead Sea Scrolls (both recovered long after their day). He treats the angelic reading as the older and near-unanimous pre-Augustinian one, calls the Sethite reading "plausible" rather than certain, and cautions that evil is a matter of the will, not of genetics — so nothing in the supernatural reading makes wickedness heritable.

Saint Augustine and Saint Thomas Aquinas (both had not read 1 Enoch) interpretted the passages in Jude and 2 Peter in this way…

— Angels having Relations with Humans…, taylormarshall.com (the same premise underlies the 2023 podcast's framing question). (Flagged — overstatement; see the note below.)

Notes

  • This is the modern supernaturalist / angelic camp, which Marshall joins from a Roman-Catholic, Thomist vantage — alongside Fr. Stephen De Young (Orthodox) and Doug Wilson (Reformed). Unlike them he does not build out a mechanism for the begetting (De Young's sacral-kingship rite, Wilson's literal embodiment); his contribution is to press the New Testament's own use of the Enochic tradition and to ask whether the Sethite consensus would have survived contact with 1 Enoch and Qumran.
  • Flagged framing (the "teaching paradigm" fork and the "only makes sense" claim). Marshall's reading of how Jude and 2 Peter use the Watcher legend — as a judgment-paradigm against the false teachers — is itself sound and mainstream (Bauckham, Davids, Nickelsburg): Jude 5–7 is a triad of judgment-types turned on "these" intruders, 2 Peter 2:4–9 reworks it, and both inherit a Second Temple "stock list" pairing the giants/Watchers with Sodom (Sirach 16; 3 Maccabees 2; the Damascus Document). Two features of his framing overreach, however. First, the fork he presses — either the apostles held the Watcher event as "literal history" or it is "an ancient story we are using to make a contemporary application," so they used it "regardless of whether they believed it" — is a false either/or: the exemplary force presupposes the judgments were real (Jude ranks the Watchers beside the wilderness generation and Sodom, and quotes Enoch as a prophet who "prophesied," v. 14), so "regardless of belief" reads Jude against his own grain. Second, "the letters only make sense if their audience understood the background story" overstates: allusive exempla need only the shared tradition, not the text of 1 Enoch — and 2 Peter de-Enochizes Jude (dropping the direct 1 Enoch quotation, generalizing the "angels that sinned" notice) precisely so its argument does not depend on the book. Neither dents the interpretation, which is about rhetorical function; both are weighed on the argument page The Watcher legend as a paraenetic exemplum against the false teachers (assessment: sound).
  • Flagged overstatement (Augustine "had not read 1 Enoch"). Marshall's parenthesis that Augustine and Aquinas "both had not read 1 Enoch" is fair for Aquinas — the complete text survived only in Ge'ez and was lost to the Latin West until James Bruce recovered it in 1773 — but it misleads for Augustine, who in City of God XV.23 explicitly engages and sets aside "the fables of those scriptures which are called apocryphal… which contain these fables about the giants, saying that their fathers were not men." Augustine knew the Enochic Watcher-tradition's central claim and rejected the angelic reading on independent grounds — canon-history (non-reception by the custodians of the pre-Christian Hebrew canon) and an angelology denying that holy angels so fell — not from never having encountered it. So the Sethite reading of Augustine (and, following him, Aquinas) was a considered rejection of the Enochic tradition, which weakens Marshall's implied "they simply lacked the evidence." Whether Augustine's case actually holds is weighed on the argument page Does Augustine's case against the angelic reading hold? (assessment: sound).
  • Flagged misattribution (Barker's "symbolic" reading). In the 2023 podcast Marshall surveys other modern views and paraphrases Margaret Barker as reading the Nephilim symbolically — a metaphor for the wickedness and violence of the pre-flood world. This inverts her actual position. Barker reads the "sons of God" of Genesis 6 as real fallen angels — the "fuller version" of the creation-and-fall story of which Genesis preserves only a fragment — and never calls them a "symbol," "metaphor," or "figure"; she re-mythologizes rather than demythologizes ("The fallen angels were known as the sons of God. That is how they are described in Genesis 6"). The likely source of the confusion is that Barker reads Genesis 3 and Genesis 6 as two fragments of a single fallen-angel myth about corrupting knowledge, which a careless listener can collapse into "it's all a symbol of human sin" — the reverse of her point. So Barker is not a witness for a symbolic reading but, if anything, a scholarly witness for the angelic one; her position is set out on her own page.
  • Flagged overreach (the canonicity of Wisdom, and "Luther threw it out"). To argue that the giants-perished-in-the-Flood belief is canonical, Marshall claims that "St. Paul the Apostle quotes the Book of Wisdom about seven times, so clearly … he … considered Wisdom as part of his Bible," and that Wisdom is absent from Protestant Bibles because "Martin Luther threw it out, so you won't find it in a King James or a Lutheran Bible." Both overreach. The allusions are real and well-attested (Paul knew Wisdom closely — Romans 1 ≈ Wisdom 13–14; Bruce Metzger found "little doubt" he "made a close study" of it), but an unmarked allusion is not a citation as Scripture, and a tally of allusions is not a stronger one: Paul never introduces Wisdom with "it is written," and by the same logic his more direct quotation of the pagan poets Aratus, Epimenides, and Menander would make them part of his Bible. (Wisdom's real case for canonicity rests on its continuous reception, not the tally.) And the "Luther threw it out … not in a King James" framing is mistaken: Luther's 1534 Bible kept Wisdom in a separate "Apocrypha" section, and the 1611 King James included it among the fourteen books between the Testaments — the deuterocanon left common Protestant Bibles only in the nineteenth century. Weighed on the argument page Does Paul's use of the Wisdom of Solomon (≈7 times) show he received it as Scripture? (assessment: weak).
  • Flagged overreach (the Maccabean anti-Hellenistic allegory). Marshall's distinctive reading — that the Enochic Watcher-episode was written "in the Maccabean period" as a "judaized" retelling of the Greek Titanomachy allegorizing the Hellenistic invasion (Watchers = Hellenizers, Nephilim = Greek–Jewish mixed-marriage offspring, the Flood/war = the Maccabean purge) — overreaches on three counts. The dating is a manuscript-fact problem: the oldest Aramaic Qumran copies of the Book of the Watchers (Milik's 4QEna/4Q201, first half of the 2nd c. BC) pre-date the revolt and sit atop still-earlier layers, so the standard commentators fix the core in the 3rd century (Nickelsburg dates chs. 6–11 to c. the late 4th) — and the genuinely Maccabean Enochic booklet is the separate Animal Apocalypse (chs. 83–90), not the Watchers. Marshall's own 2018 essay in fact dates 1 Enoch to "around the 200s BC," in tension with the podcast's "Maccabean" claim. The tight allegory has no specialist support (the real anti-Hellenistic reading, Nickelsburg's, is a thematic mood about the Diadochi wars, not an event-keyed cipher), and the intermarriage referent is inverted — the marriage-polemic scholars do find (Suter) targets defiled Jewish priests, not Greeks marrying Jewish women. Weighed on the argument page The Watcher legend as a coded Maccabean anti-Hellenistic allegory (assessment: weak).
  • Flagged (partly sound, partly overstated) — 2 Peter's "Tartarus" as a Titanomachy borrowing. Marshall's other distinctive sub-claim — that 2 Peter 2:4's tartaróō ("cast into Tartarus") deliberately borrows the Greek Titanomachy, so the New Testament writers consciously map the Watchers onto the Titans — cuts across the timeline and is weighed on the argument page 2 Peter's "Tartarus" (tartaróō) as a Greek/Titanomachy borrowing (assessment: plausible). Its core is sound: tartaróō genuinely is a Greek Titan-word, and 2 Peter's choosing it where Jude 6 had only "everlasting chains under darkness" is a deliberate Hellenization. What overreaches is the conscious fusion: Greek 1 Enoch 20:2 had already stationed the fallen angels in Tartarus, so the specialists who grant the Greek coloring (Bauckham, Neyrey, Billings) route the word "through the mediation of Jewish apocalyptic" rather than a worked-out Watchers-are-Titans mythography.
