Margaret Barker on the Nephilim

Dates: c. 1988 (b. 1944) · margaretbarker.com
Summary: Margaret Barker (b. 1944), a British Methodist biblical scholar, past President of the Society for Old Testament Study (1998) and holder of a Lambeth Doctorate of Divinity (2008), is the founder of "Temple Theology." She takes 1 Enoch with unusual seriousness — as a survival of the older, pre-exilic temple religion — and reads the "sons of God" of Genesis 6 as real fallen angels, of whom Genesis preserves only a fragment: the "fuller version" of the creation-and-fall story. She further identifies those fallen angels with the apostate priesthood ("the priests were the angels in the temple"). Her reading is therefore re-mythologizing, not demythologizing — the opposite of the "the Nephilim are merely a symbol for human wickedness" position sometimes attributed to her (see Notes).

Claims & evidence

Genesis 6 preserves a fragment of a larger fallen-angel story

In Genesis we have one episode of a longer story. The human pair are tempted with knowledge, and the result is a corrupted world of dust, thorns, thistles and mortality. The fuller version is preserved in many other sources as the story of the fallen angels. Led by Azazel, 200 angels rebelled against the great Holy One and came to earth. They made a counter covenant, and revealed their angelic knowledge to the people on earth, having first seduced human women. The serpent tempting Eve is one element in the larger picture.

— Creation Theology (Wakefield, 2004), margaretbarker.com. Barker treats the descent of the Watchers as a real narrative — the pre-exilic myth of which the Eden story and Genesis 6 are surviving fragments — not as a figure of speech. Far from reducing the angels to a metaphor for human sin, she holds the biblical compilers truncated a fuller angelic account.

The "sons of God" of Genesis 6 are the fallen angels

The fallen angels were known as the sons of God. That is how they are described in Genesis 6, and the Book of Isaiah also begins with the sons of God: 'Sons have I reared and brought up, but they have rebelled against me.' These were the fallen angels.

— Creation Theology (2004), margaretbarker.com. This is a straightforwardly angelic identification of the sons of God — the same reading held by the earliest Fathers on the timeline — arrived at not by patristic authority but by her reconstruction of temple angelology and its Enochic sources.

The fallen angels are the apostate priesthood

The priests, as we have seen, were the angels in the temple. Their role was to maintain the eternal covenant by their teaching, and then, if it was damaged by human sin, to renew it with their prayers and ritual.

— Creation Theology (2004), margaretbarker.com. Barker's distinctive move is to map the "fall of the angels" onto the historical corruption of the temple establishment: reading 1 Enoch's history (chapter 93), she dates the "fall" to the 8th–7th centuries BC, when "the priests in the temple abandoned Wisdom and lost their spiritual sight," and calls the builders of the second temple the "impure apostates" of the Enoch tradition.1 On this reading the fallen "sons of God" and their corruption of the earth are historicized as apostate priests, not allegorized into generic human evil.

The sons of God "became the fathers of the giants" — ⚠ unverified

Barker's most explicit statement tying the fallen sons of God to the giants of Genesis 6 is reported from The Great Angel (1992): "This is the account of the sons of God who fell from heaven and became the fathers of the giants. A longer version of the tale appears in I Enoch…" — i.e. a narrative/historical-mythic reading, not a symbolic one. The primary has not yet been opened firsthand (the passage circulates secondhand), so it is marked ⚠ unverified pending a direct check of the book.2

Notes

  • She does not read the Nephilim as "merely a symbol for human wickedness." That paraphrase (as advanced by Taylor Marshall) inverts her actual position. Across her verified essays Barker never calls the fallen angels or their offspring a "symbol," "metaphor," or "figure"; she uses narrative-agentive language ("rebelled," "came to earth," "seduced human women") and re-mythologizes Genesis, insisting the angel-beings are real and were edited out of the canonical text. The one nuance that can be misheard is that she reads Genesis 3 and Genesis 6 as two fragments of a single fallen-angel myth about corrupting knowledge — which a careless listener might collapse into "it's all just a symbol of human sin," the reverse of her point. Her misreading is flagged on Marshall's page.
  • Barker belongs with the modern reception of the angelic / supernatural reading (alongside Heiser, De Young, and Wilson), but in a scholarly-mythic key and with her own distinctive thesis: the fallen "sons of God" encode the corruption of the priesthood (the "fallen angel, fallen priest" reading also associated with D. Suter and G. Nickelsburg's work on 1 Enoch).
  • Her wider "Temple Theology" reconstruction is influential but contested — welcomed in some quarters (and heavily in Latter-day Saint scholarship) and criticized by other biblical scholars as speculative. The point relevant here — that she reads the sons of God of Genesis 6 as real fallen angels, not a symbol — is secure from her own words.
