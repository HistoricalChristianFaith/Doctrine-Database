The Book of Giants on the Nephilim

Dates: c. 150 BC (composition 3rd–2nd c. BC, disputed; the Qumran copies are 1st c. BC – early 1st c. AD) Summary: The Book of Giants is a distinct Enochic work — a close cousin of 1 Enoch's "Book of the Watchers" — that survives in Aramaic among the Dead Sea Scrolls (as many as ten copies) and, centuries later, in a Manichaean recension. It takes up the story of Genesis 6 from the vantage of the giants themselves: the offspring of the fallen Watchers and human women, depicted as violent, devouring warriors who are warned by dream-visions of the coming Flood, petition Enoch through the giant Mahway, and are refused. Strikingly, it names the Mesopotamian heroes Gilgamesh and Humbaba among the giants — the clearest Second Temple bridge between the Nephilim of Genesis 6 and the gods-and-kings of Babylonian tradition.

Claims & evidence

A distinct Enochic book, attested in as many as ten Qumran manuscripts

Fragments of the Book of Giants turned up in caves 1, 2, 4, and 6 at Qumran (sigla 1Q23, 1Q24, 2Q26, 4Q203, 4Q530–532, 6Q8, and others). J. T. Milik, who first identified the work, counted six copies; Loren Stuckenbruck's standard critical edition reckons as many as ten — a manuscript spread that makes it one of the more widely copied Enochic writings at Qumran, comparable to the Book of the Watchers itself. Milik dated the best-preserved copy (4Q530) to the first half of the first century BC and 4Q203 to the late-first-century-BC Herodian hand; the composition is usually placed in the second century BC (Klaus Beyer argues for a late-third-century Aramaic original).1

The Watchers beget giants and monsters who devour the earth

They defiled [the earth]… they begot giants and monsters… the earth was corrupted… and they were seeking to devour many.

— Book of Giants, 4Q531 frag. 2, tr. Wise, Abegg & Cook, The Dead Sea Scrolls: A New Translation (1996), gnosis.org. The book shares the violent, cannibal characterization of the giants that its parent text, the Book of the Watchers, states at length — 1 Enoch 7: the Watchers "bare great giants… Who consumed all the acquisitions of men. And when men could no longer sustain them, the giants turned against them and devoured mankind. And they began to sin against birds, and beasts, and reptiles, and fish, and to devour one another's flesh, and drink the blood."2

Gilgamesh and Humbaba appear among the giants

The Qumranic edition of The Book of Giants, like its Manichaean counterpart, affiliates the names of the Sumerian hero Gilgamesh and the monster Humbaba with the Watchers and giants.

— summarizing the fragments (Gilgamesh, glgmš, in 4Q530–531; Hobabish/Humbaba, ḥwbbš, in 4Q203 and 4Q530).3 This is the point Fr. Stephen De Young presses: that the Book of Giants "identifies Gilgamesh as one of the Nephilim," reading Genesis 6 as a theological reframing of what was, to its first hearers, the received record of Babylonian gods and kings.

Dream-visions of the Flood, and the giants' refused plea

Let it be known to you… a great flood… will destroy all living things… loosen the bonds binding you to evil and pray.

— Book of Giants, 4Q530 frag. 2, tr. Wise, Abegg & Cook, gnosis.org. The giants Ohyah and Hahyah are troubled by dreams of a coming destruction and send the giant Mahway to the scribe Enoch to read the tablets of judgment; a fragmentary tablet-vision survives — "They drenched the tablet in the water… when it emerged, all but three names had been washed away" (2Q26) — and the verdict, once returned, is one the giants cannot avert. This is the same refused-propitiation motif that the canonical Sirach compresses ("He was not pacified toward the old giants"): the antediluvian rebels plead and are not spared.4

A long afterlife: the Manichaean recension

The book had a remarkable reach: Mani took it up in the third century AD and made his own Book of the Giants a canonical scripture of Manichaeism, copies of which surfaced among the Turfan fragments of Central Asia (where Ohyah and Hahyah are renamed Sām and Nariman). John C. Reeves's study of that recension argues "the fundamental structure of Manichaean cosmogony is ultimately indebted to Jewish exegetical expansions of Genesis 6:1–4" — independent testimony to how central this Watcher-and-giants material was across Second Temple and late-antique religion.5

Notes

  • The Book of Giants belongs to the same angelic / Watcher reading as 1 Enoch and Jubilees: the "sons of God" are fallen angels and the Nephilim their giant offspring. Its distinctive contribution is the naming of Gilgamesh and Humbaba as giants — the Babylonian apkallu/heroic background that De Young and Heiser build on.
  • Recovery. The Aramaic fragments came to light with the discovery of Qumran Cave 4 in 1952 and were edited by J. T. Milik in The Books of Enoch: Aramaic Fragments of Qumrân Cave 4 (Oxford, 1976); the full critical edition is Loren Stuckenbruck, The Book of Giants from Qumran (1997). Before the Aramaic Enoch material surfaced, scholars knew the giant-legend chiefly from the later Ethiopic 1 Enoch and the Manichaean fragments; the Qumran finds showed the tradition to be older and more textually developed than had been assumed.
  • The Gospel demons' plea "before the time" (Matt 8:29) and the 90%/10% schema of the dead giants' spirits belong to the neighboring Jubilees strand; the Book of Giants supplies the earlier scene — the giants themselves foreseeing and dreading the judgment.
