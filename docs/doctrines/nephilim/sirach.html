Sirach (Ecclesiasticus) on the Giants and the Refused Propitiation

Dates: c. 180 BC (Hebrew original by Jesus ben Sira of Jerusalem; Greek translation by his grandson c. 117 BC; deuterocanonical) Summary: Sirach (the Wisdom of Ben Sira, called Ecclesiasticus in the Latin/Vulgate tradition) supplies a second deuterocanonical witness — alongside the Wisdom of Solomon — that Second Temple Judaism read Genesis 6's gigantes as real, wicked "giants" who were destroyed. Reciting God's historic judgments, Ben Sira names "the old giants… who fell away in the strength of their foolishness" as a standing example of unforgiven pride (16:7), immediately before Sodom and Lot. Its distinctive note is the verb: God "was not pacified" — non exoraverunt, no propitiation was obtained — toward the giants, the compressed canonical echo of the Enochic scene in which the antediluvian rebels seek forgiveness and are refused. Dr. Taylor Marshall adduces the verse in this connection; see his page.

Claims & evidence

The "old giants" destroyed, and God not pacified toward them

In a catalogue of God's judgments on the proud (16:6–10 — the rebellious congregation, the giants, Sodom and Lot's neighbours, the hardened Egyptians), Ben Sira lists the antediluvian giants:

He was not pacified toward the old giants, who fell away in the strength of their foolishness. (Ecclesiasticus 16:7, KJV/Apocrypha)1

The Latin Vulgate, which numbers the verse 16:8, sharpens the "propitiation" note — the giants "did not obtain pardon for their sins" (non exoraverunt pro peccatis suis antiqui gigantes, qui destructi sunt confidentes suæ virtuti): they were destroyed "trusting to their own strength."2 Like Wisdom of Solomon 14:6 ("the proud giants perished") and the Septuagint's gígantes, this is a Second Temple Jewish text presupposing the giants of Genesis 6 as historical figures overthrown for their wickedness — here made a moral type of strength-trusting pride, set beside Sodom and the Egyptians.

"Not pacified" — the refused-intercession motif

The clause that no propitiation was made for the giants compresses a theme developed at length in 1 Enoch: after the Watchers and their giant offspring bring ruin on the earth, the fallen angels ask Enoch to write a petition on their behalf pleading "that they should have forgiveness," and God flatly refuses it —

Your petition will not be granted unto you throughout all the days of eternity, and that judgement has been finally passed upon you… (1 Enoch 14:4), and "Say to them therefore: 'You have no peace.'" (1 Enoch 16:4)3

Ben Sira's "he was not pacified toward the old giants" reads as the canonical, one-line distillation of that verdict: the antediluvian rebels were beyond propitiation. (The Enochic petition is spoken for the Watchers, the "fathers," while Sirach names the "giants," their offspring; the point held in common is that no forgiveness was granted the whole antediluvian rebellion.)

Notes

  • The primary text is Sirach / Ecclesiasticus 16:7 itself (directly quoted). Verse numbering varies: the Greek and KJV number it 16:7, the Vulgate and Douay-Rheims 16:8.
  • Sirach's value here is as a witness to the giant-tradition, not an adjudication of the "sons of God" question — it presupposes the giants of Genesis 6 and their destruction, without naming their origin.
