Michael S. Heiser on the Nephilim

Dates: c. 2015 (b. 1963 – d. 2023) · Wikipedia
Summary: Michael S. Heiser (1963–2023), an Old-Testament and Semitic-languages scholar (PhD, University of Wisconsin–Madison) and the popularizer of a "Divine Council" reading of the Bible, is the modern figure standing behind the recent revival of the angelic reading — the intellectual source most often tapped by Fr. Stephen De Young and Doug Wilson. In The Unseen Realm (2015) and Reversing Hermon (2017) he argues that the "sons of God" of Genesis 6 are divine beings (not the line of Seth), that the Nephilim are their giant offspring (the word itself meaning "giants," from Aramaic naphila), that the Babylonian apkallu sages are the background of 1 Enoch's Watchers, and that the later "giant clans" (Rephaim, Anakim, Og of Bashan) continue that bloodline, so the conquest of Canaan is in part a holy war to exterminate them. He is a supernaturalist like De Young — the sons of God are real spirit beings — but he rejects the sacral-kingship / divinized-human-ruler mechanism De Young builds on, reading it back into Genesis 6 as anachronistic.

Claims & evidence

The "sons of God" are divine beings, not the line of Seth

The Sethite view was incoherent before. Now it's become the position for ostriches.

— The Ancient Near Eastern Context for Genesis 6:1–4, drmsh.com. Heiser holds that the plural phrase bene elohim ("sons of God") denotes divine beings everywhere else in the Hebrew Bible (Job 1:6; 38:7; Ps 29:1; 89:6), so Genesis 6 is describing a transgression by spirit beings, not the intermarriage of two human lines; he judges the Sethite reading "deeply flawed" and unsupported by anything in the text (The Unseen Realm, Lexham 2015, chs. 12–13).

The Babylonian apkallu are the background of the Watchers

This article deals a death blow to any non-supernatural (or non-mythic as some prefer) interpretation … Annus' article is the most current study on the Mesopotamian apkallu.

— The Ancient Near Eastern Context for Genesis 6:1–4, drmsh.com. Following Amar Annus's 2010 study "On the Origin of Watchers," Heiser derives the Watchers of 1 Enoch from the divine apkallu sages of Mesopotamia — antediluvian bringers of civilization, some of them evil, whose post-flood offspring were reckoned part-human and "two-thirds apkallu" — making the giant-begetting sons of God of Genesis 6 the Israelite counterpart of that tradition. This apkallu→Watchers derivation is the shared scholarly floor beneath both De Young's and Heiser's readings; it is set out on the sacred-marriage mechanism argument page.

"Nephilim" means "giants" (Aramaic naphila), not "fallen ones"

I can make a workable morphological argument for nephilim being from Aramaic naphila and meaning giants … the pointing for that meaning should be nephulim or nophelim – the latter which occurs in Ezekiel but not in Gen 6.

— The Naked Bible: Nephilim, drmsh.com. This is one of Heiser's signature exegetical arguments: if nephilim derived from the Hebrew verb naphal ("to fall"), the expected form would be nophelim or nephulim; the actual spelling with the long i (as at Numbers 13:33) is best explained as a borrowing of the Aramaic noun naphila, "giant." The reading matches the Septuagint's gígantes and the Numbers 13:33 link between the Nephilim and the giant Anakim. (This etymology is elegant but genuinely debated — see Notes.)

The giant clans continue the bloodline; the conquest is a holy war against them

Heiser reads Numbers 13:32–33 (the giant Anakim called "descendants of the Nephilim") as evoking Genesis 6:1–4, and argues that the later giant peoples of the Transjordan — the Rephaim, the Anakim, the Emim, and above all Og of Bashan, "the last of the Rephaim" — carry the Nephilim pedigree forward past the Flood. On his reading, this is why certain conquest texts use ḥerem (kharam, "devote to destruction"): Israel is at war not merely with rival nations but with populations "spawned by rival divine beings," so coexistence was impossible and the ban fell on the giant-clan cities specifically (The Unseen Realm, chs. 23 "Giant Problems" and 25 "Holy War"). The Ugaritic grounding of that Rephaim = deified-royal-dead equation is weighed on the Ashtaroth-and-Edrei and Rephaim-as-dead-kings argument pages.

Og's iron bed as an echo of Marduk's cultic bed at Babylon

its dimensions (9 x 4 cubits) are precisely those of the cultic bed in the ziggurat called Etemenanki—which is the ziggurat most archaeologists identify as the Tower of Babel referred to in the Bible.

— The Unseen Realm (Lexham, 2015), ch. 23; cf. the companion note crediting Maria Lindquist's peer-reviewed argument, moreunseenrealm.com. Heiser popularized the link between Og's oversized "iron bed" (Deut 3:11) and the bed on which Marduk and his consort Zarpanitu were thought to meet at Babylon, tagging Og as an heir of Babylonian sacral kingship. He is careful to hedge it, though: the bed's dimensions "cannot be taken as a precise indication of Og's own dimensions," and the Marduk/Amorite name-wordplay he floats he flags as "only speculation."1 The parallel is weighed on the Og's-bed argument page (assessment: sound), where the lineage is traced as Lindquist (academic) → Heiser (popular) → De Young.

"Reversing Hermon": the New Testament uses 1 Enoch and reads Genesis 6 supernaturally

what cannot be coherently denied is that Peter and Jude have divine beings as the offenders, not mere humans … It is obvious to those who study all these texts, especially in Greek, that Peter and Jude knew 1 Enoch very well.

— Reversing Hermon Footnotes, drmsh.com. Heiser's second book argues that Jesus's ministry consciously reverses the sin of the Watchers at Mount Hermon, and that the New Testament writers (Jude quoting 1 Enoch, 2 Peter's verb tartaróō — "cast into Tartarus," the classical destination of the Titans) presuppose the Enochic Watcher-and-giant story, foreclosing an anti-supernatural reading of Genesis 6. The Titanomachy strand of this is weighed on the 2 Peter "Tartarus" argument page.

He rejects the sacral-kingship / divinized-king mechanism

Heiser and De Young agree that the sons of God are spirit beings and share the apkallu background — but they part on the mechanism. Where De Young explains the begetting by a pagan sacral-kingship fertility ritual (a divinized king uniting with a spirit-as-woman), Heiser holds that reading ancient kingship into Genesis 6 is anachronistic: divine-sonship language for a king is singular in the ancient Near East, whereas Genesis 6's plural "sons of God" refers to divine beings, so "it is flawed hermeneutics to read either ancient kingship or the glorification of believers back into Genesis 6" (The Unseen Realm, ch. 12, "Divinized Human Rulers"). For Heiser the offenders are simply rebel divine beings, not human ritual-kings. This is the point at which De Young departs from Heiser rather than borrows from him — noted on the sacred-marriage mechanism page.2

UFOs and the abduction phenomenon

Heiser's Genesis-6 work has a modern sequel that makes him the bridge from the Nephilim into the "UFOs and aliens as the demonic" timeline, where he is the most careful witness. In Aliens & Demons: Evidence of an Unseen Realm (2017, a book plus a 90-minute documentary) he engages the phenomenon at length — and, unusually for a supernaturalist, spends as much energy debunking the UFO-Nephilim sensationalists as he does affirming an unseen realm. The value of quoting him precisely is that he splits apart two questions the popular writers fuse: the open question of biological life elsewhere, and the far narrower one of the anomalous abduction experience.

Biological aliens would not overturn the Bible; the abduction experience is the real concern

I don't think the question of an ET reality is a problem for Christian theology.

what passes for contactee messaging and alien abductions is, in my view, quite sinister, and demonization is one (strong) possibility in my mind for explaining those phenomena.

— "Aliens and Demons: New 90-Minute Documentary," drmsh.com. This is the distinction that separates Heiser from the sensationalists: he does not read every sighting or the mere possibility of extraterrestrial life as demonic. Genuine biological life elsewhere would be part of creation and no threat to the faith; only the anomalous abduction/contactee subset — with its deceptive "messaging" — does he read as a doorway to the unseen realm of angels and demons.3

"Real space aliens cannot be demons" — but interdimensional ones could

if there are real space aliens (beings that meet the above criteria), then they cannot be demons, since demons do not conform to these criteria in biblical theology.

If aliens are interdimensional, though, then things change. The demon equation goes back on the table.

— "The Christian View of Aliens, Part 4," Michael S. Heiser Foundation. The whole demonic reading, on Heiser's terms, turns on what the entities are. If they are embodied biological creatures crossing physical space, the biblical category "demon" does not fit them. It is only if the intelligences are interdimensional — moving between the seen and unseen realms rather than merely across the galaxy — that they could be the fallen spirits of Scripture. This is the same "spirit or spacecraft?" fork that the ufo-demonic timeline builds its central arguments around.4

The bridge to Genesis 6: demons as the disembodied spirits of the Nephilim

What lets Heiser connect abduction accounts to Genesis 6 at all is the Second-Temple identification he defends in Reversing Hermon: the "demons" of the Gospels are the disembodied spirits of the dead Nephilim. The earliest source for it is 1 Enoch 15, where the giants, being born of both flesh and spirit, become "evil spirits upon the earth" at their death — a reading corroborated at Qumran (11Q11 = 11QApocryphal Psalms, col. V). So the very beings a supernaturalist would expect to be operating behind a deceptive contactee phenomenon are, on Heiser's framework, the surviving spirits of the Watchers' offspring — the same actors as the Genesis-6 story told forward into the present.5

Against the "ancient astronaut" theorists: von Däniken, Sitchin, and Ezekiel's "saucer"

The same care makes Heiser one of the sharpest critics of the ancient-astronaut writers — Erich von Däniken and especially Zecharia Sitchin — whose claim that the Mesopotamian Anunnaki were flesh-and-blood extraterrestrials he treats as paleobabble: pseudo-scholarship built on manipulated texts.

what Zecharia Sitchin has written about Nibiru, the Anunnaki, the book of Genesis, the Nephilim, and a host of other things has absolutely no basis in the real data of the ancient world … Because I don't like ancient texts manipulated to promote false claims.

— sitchiniswrong.com, the site Heiser built specifically to answer Sitchin from the cuneiform sources. He gives Ezekiel's "wheel within a wheel" the same treatment: not a spacecraft but a divine throne-theophany whose imagery a sixth-century exile would already know from Near-Eastern royal iconography. The ancient-astronaut books, he argues, ignore the actual vocabulary of Ezekiel 1 — the throne is never described as disk-shaped or metallic; that has to be imported — and read a modern machine back into a text that explains its own cherubim.6 Because he is a committed supernaturalist who nonetheless dismantles the UFO-Nephilim sensationalism, his voice guards the ufo-demonic reading against its wilder proponents.

Notes

  • The source behind the moderns. Heiser is the scholar most of the recent angelic-reading proponents draw on. Doug Wilson and De Young both stand downstream of his Unseen Realm framing (the Og-bed link, the apkallu background, the supernatural identity of the sons of God), even where they diverge from him on the mechanism (Wilson's literal-embodiment account; De Young's ritual one).
  • Caveat on the naphila etymology. Heiser's derivation of nephilim from Aramaic naphila ("giant") is a real and attractive proposal, but not a settled one: the standard lexica list the word's origin as uncertain, and many scholars still derive it from naphal in the sense "fallen ones" (those who fell, or who fell upon others) — the reading behind Jerome's and Bede's "fallen ones." His case rests on the vocalization and on the Numbers 13:33 giant link; it strengthens the "Nephilim = giants" conclusion but does not by itself decide the disputed sons-of-God identity.
  • He differs from De Young on the begetting mechanism (rejecting the sacral-kingship rite as anachronistic) and, unlike De Young, does not tie the tradition to a surviving cult; his emphasis falls on the textual and comparative-Semitic case for the supernatural reading.
  • "UFO religion." Heiser treated the modern belief in benevolent "space brothers" as a post-modern religion in its own right, not a science — a framing that converges with the religious-studies scholar Diana Walsh Pasulka's academic study of UFO belief as an emerging religion (American Cosmic: UFOs, Religion, and Technology, Oxford, 2019). His supernatural reading of the abduction subset and his debunking of the space-brother cult are two sides of the same caution.
