The Origin and Inspiration of the Septuagint

How did the Jewish Scriptures come to be in Greek? From the second century BC onward a story was told and retold: Ptolemy II Philadelphus, stocking his library at Alexandria, sent to the high priest in Jerusalem for the Law and for translators; seventy-two elders (six from each tribe) came, and rendered the Hebrew into Greek — the version "of the Seventy," the Septuagint (LXX). This page traces how that account grew across time, earliest first. The core was a simple royal commission (the Letter of Aristeas, Josephus); but in Christian hands it acquired a miracle — the translators, shut in separate cells and forbidden to confer, produced word-for-word identical versions, proof that the Holy Spirit had guided them (Irenaeus, the Cohortatio, Cyril of Jerusalem, Epiphanius, Augustine) — and a skeptical counter-tradition that called the cells a fable (Jerome, Hugh of Saint Victor). Around it gathered a late-antique offshoot: the legend of Simeon the Just, one of the Seventy, who balked at translating Isaiah 7:14's "virgin" and was kept alive three hundred years to see the Christ.

The doctrine matters beyond its own interest because it is the premise of a second one: if the LXX was a divinely-inspired, providentially-guarded translation, then the synagogue's later, divergent Hebrew and its rival Greek versions look like corruption — which is exactly the charge gathered on the Rabbinic Corruption of Scripture page. The two doctrines share the Septuagint-legend crux (is the legend history, pious fiction, or a memory of real translation choices?); it is weighed there.

Timeline

The witnesses are being added earliest first. The fullest forms of the legend — Philo, the Talmud, Justin, Irenaeus, Tertullian, Clement, the Cohortatio, Eusebius, Cyril of Jerusalem, Epiphanius, Augustine, Rufinus, the skeptic Jerome, and the long Syriac and Arabic chain down to the thirteenth century — are queued from the source dossier and not yet authored.

The Letter of Aristeas (c. 130 BC)

The fountainhead. The pseudepigraphal Letter of Aristeas, written in the name of a Greek courtier of Ptolemy II but composed by a Hellenistic Jew, tells the whole story in seed: Demetrius of Phalerum, keeper of the royal library, reports that the Jewish Law is missing and "must be translated," and the king writes to the high priest Eleazar for men to do it.1 Eleazar sends "six elders out of every tribe" — "seventy-two in all" — who complete the work "in seventy-two days, just as if this had been arranged of set purpose."2 Read out to the assembled Jews, the translation is acclaimed "so excellent and sacred and accurate" that they "pronounce a curse" on anyone who should alter it "by adding anything or changing… or making any omission" — the text fixed and guarded from the start.3 Notably, Aristeas has no separate-cells miracle: his translators confer and compare, reaching a text "by collation" — the inspiration-of-identical-independent-renderings is a later accretion onto this base.

Arguments & cruxes

The central sub-claim — whether the inspiration legend (the divinely-guided, independently-identical translation, and the "changes" the seventy-two are said to have made) is history, pious fiction, or a memory of real translation choices — cuts across this timeline and the Rabbinic Corruption timeline alike. It is weighed under that doctrine, where it is grouped in the "corruption charge" complex; the Simeon-the-Just Isaiah 7:14 strand is queued alongside it and not yet authored.

The Septuagint legend: were the seventy-two divinely moved to change the text? (assessment: plausible)

The Septuagint-legend crux weighs the tradition this whole timeline traces — that the seventy-two translators were divinely moved to change specific passages for Ptolemy (the Tractate Megillah list, paralleled in the Letter of Aristeas frame). The interpretation is plausible at its kernel, not as the inspired history it is told as: a real subset of the listed "changes" are genuine, identifiable Septuagint translation choices (Genesis 2:2's "sixth day"; the euphemism for the hare at Leviticus 11:6), but the inspiration-miracle frame (separate cells, divinely-prompted unanimity) is a demonstrable later accretion — absent from the earliest source, Aristeas, and grown across centuries, exactly the growth this timeline charts — and the list is a fluid midrashic tradition several of whose items appear in no extant Septuagint. The crux lives on the rabbinic-corruption side, where the inspired-LXX premise turns the synagogue's divergent Hebrew into apparent corruption.4
