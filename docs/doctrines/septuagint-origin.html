The Origin and Inspiration of the Septuagint

How did the Jewish Scriptures come to be in Greek? From the second century BC onward a story was told and retold: Ptolemy II Philadelphus, stocking his library at Alexandria, sent to the high priest in Jerusalem for the Law and for translators; seventy-two elders (six from each tribe) came, and rendered the Hebrew into Greek — the version "of the Seventy," the Septuagint (LXX). This page traces how that account grew across time, earliest first. The core was a simple royal commission (the Letter of Aristeas, Josephus); but in Christian hands it acquired a miracle — the translators, shut in separate cells and forbidden to confer, produced word-for-word identical versions, proof that the Holy Spirit had guided them (Irenaeus, the Cohortatio, Cyril of Jerusalem, Epiphanius, Augustine) — and a skeptical counter-tradition that called the cells a fable (Jerome, Hugh of Saint Victor). Around it gathered a late-antique offshoot: the legend of Simeon the Just, one of the Seventy, who balked at translating Isaiah 7:14's "virgin" and was kept alive three hundred years to see the Christ.

The doctrine matters beyond its own interest because it is the premise of a second one: if the LXX was a divinely-inspired, providentially-guarded translation, then the synagogue's later, divergent Hebrew and its rival Greek versions look like corruption — which is exactly the charge gathered on the Rabbinic Corruption of Scripture page. The two doctrines share the Septuagint-legend crux (is the legend history, pious fiction, or a memory of real translation choices?); it is weighed there.

Timeline

The witnesses are being added earliest first. The fuller forms of the legend — the Talmud, the skeptic Jerome, and the long Syriac and Arabic chain down to the thirteenth century — are queued and not yet authored.

Aristobulus of Alexandria (c. 150 BC)

The earliest datable mention. Aristobulus, a Hellenistic-Jewish philosopher who dedicated his commentary on the Law to Ptolemy VI Philometor, notes in passing — while arguing that Plato and Pythagoras borrowed from Moses — that "the entire translation of all the contents of our law was made in the time of the king surnamed Philadelphus… which was managed by Demetrius Phalereus," and that partial Greek renderings of the exodus and the Law had circulated even before Demetrius.1 His version is the barest of all: a royal commission and Demetrius, but no seventy-two elders, no separate cells, no inspiration miracle. By the usual dates he slightly precedes the Letter of Aristeas, though the two are close and scholars dispute which depends on which; either way the Letter is the fuller narrative the tradition inherits. Tellingly, a later writer (Anatolius, quoted by Eusebius) folds Aristobulus himself into the legend, calling him one "chosen among the seventy interpreters… by Ptolemy Philadelphus" — an anachronism of a century that shows the story reaching back to gather up its own sources.2

The Letter of Aristeas (c. 130 BC)

The literary fountainhead. The pseudepigraphal Letter of Aristeas, written in the name of a Greek courtier of Ptolemy II but composed by a Hellenistic Jew, tells the whole story in seed: Demetrius of Phalerum, keeper of the royal library, reports that the Jewish Law is missing and "must be translated," and the king writes to the high priest Eleazar for men to do it.3 Eleazar sends "six elders out of every tribe" — "seventy-two in all" — who complete the work "in seventy-two days, just as if this had been arranged of set purpose."4 Read out to the assembled Jews, the translation is acclaimed "so excellent and sacred and accurate" that they "pronounce a curse" on anyone who should alter it "by adding anything or changing… or making any omission" — the text fixed and guarded from the start.5 Notably, Aristeas has no separate-cells miracle: his translators confer and compare, reaching a text "by collation" — the inspiration-of-identical-independent-renderings is a later accretion onto this base.

Philo of Alexandria (c. AD 30)

The first inspiration miracle. Philo, the Hellenistic-Jewish philosopher of Alexandria, keeps the royal-commission frame — Ptolemy Philadelphus, admiring the Law, asks the high priest for translators, who "with great care selected the most respectable of the Hebrews"6 — but he is the first to make the translators' agreement a marvel: settled in seclusion on Pharos, they "like men inspired, prophesied, not one saying one thing and another another, but every one of them employed the self-same nouns and verbs, as if some unseen prompter had suggested all their language to them," though Greek is "rich in a variety of words."7 The Greek and the Hebrew are therefore to be revered "as sisters," and the seventy-two are "not mere interpreters but hierophants and prophets."8 Crucially, the inspiration is present but the separate cells are not: Philo's translators work together in "a secret place," not each sealed in his own chamber. The independent-and-identical proof — seventy-two men producing word-for-word identical Greek in separate cells — is added afterward, by Irenaeus and the Cohortatio; Philo supplies the miracle onto which that machinery is later grafted.

Josephus (c. AD 93)

The historian's retelling — a witness to the legend's plainer form. Josephus recounts the origin at length in Antiquities XII and briefly in Against Apion II: Ptolemy II Philadelphus, having "a great desire of knowing our laws," commits the work to Demetrius of Phalerum and the courtiers Andreas and Aristeas.9 The high priest sends "six elders out of every tribe" — seventy-two — who finish "in seventy-two days," the version read out to the assembled Jews and approved.10 He follows the Letter of Aristeas openly, naming "Aristeus" and referring the reader to "that book… which he wrote on this very occasion."10 Writing a generation after Philo, he reproduces only Aristeas's frame: no separate cells, no miracle of identical independent renderings — the translators work together "where the laws were translated." His value is that of an early, datable reader of Aristeas, and what he found there had no cells and no marvel.

Justin Martyr (c. AD 155)

The first Christian retelling — still bare. Justin, in the First Apology, gives the legend stripped to its bones: Ptolemy, stocking his library, "heard… of these prophets, and sent to Herod, who was at that time king of the Jews," for "the books of the prophets," received them in Hebrew, and had "men be commissioned to translate them into the Greek language," where they "remained… until now."11 There is no Demetrius, no Eleazar (whom he garbles into "Herod"), no seventy-two, no separate cells, and no inspiration miracle — the marvel of independently-identical renderings enters only with Irenaeus a generation later. Justin's two changes are instead of aim: he widens the scope from the Law to "the prophets," because his interest is the prophecies of Christ, now publicly on record in Greek for the pagan reader to check. (He knows the fuller form — in his later Dialogue with Trypho he leans on "your seventy elders that were with Ptolemy" — so the Apology's plainness is telling, not ignorant.)

Irenaeus (c. AD 175)

The hinge. Irenaeus is the first Christian writer to give the legend its full inspiration-miracle form. Keeping the royal-commission frame — "Ptolemy the son of Lagus… made request to the people of Jerusalem, that they should have their Scriptures translated" — he adds the decisive new element: Ptolemy, "fearing lest they might perchance, by taking counsel together, conceal the truth… separated them from each other, and commanded them all to write the same translation," and when the independent drafts proved identical "in the very same words and the very same names, from beginning to end," even "the Gentiles present perceived that the Scriptures had been interpreted by the inspiration of God."12 Philo had the marvel of unanimity, but his translators worked together; Irenaeus adds the isolation that turns unanimity into proof. He braces it with a second wonder — God once "inspired Esdras the priest… to recast all the words of the former prophets" after the Scriptures "had been corrupted" in the captivity13 — and he tells the whole story for a polemical end: to defend the Septuagint's "virgin" of Isaiah 7:14 against the Jewish proselytes Aquila and Theodotion, who rendered it "young woman." The physical "little cells" on Pharos are not yet here (they enter with the Cohortatio); the machinery that produces them is. Two details already drift: he counts "seventy" elders, not seventy-two, and assigns the work to Ptolemy I rather than Philadelphus.

Tertullian (c. AD 197)

The Latin West's first telling — and a step back to the plain form. Tertullian, in Apology 18, retells the origin to persuade pagan readers that the Christian Scriptures are ancient and publicly verifiable: Ptolemy Philadelphus, "at the suggestion of Demetrius Phalereus… applied to the Jews for their writings," and was given "seventy-two interpreters… The same account is given by Aristæus."14 After Irenaeus had built the inspiration-miracle, Tertullian drops it entirely — no separate cells, no marvel of identical renderings, just the royal-commission frame of Aristeas, whom he cites by name. He restores the precise seventy-two that Irenaeus had rounded to seventy, and corrects the king to Philadelphus (against Irenaeus's Ptolemy I). His distinctive note is evidential rather than miraculous: the proof is on public display — the libraries of Ptolemy "at the temple of Serapis," holding "the identical Hebrew originals," read aloud by the Jews "every Sabbath," so any pagan may go and check.15 The legend here serves antiquity-and-availability, the same apologetic use Justin made of it, not the corruption charge.

Clement of Alexandria (c. AD 200)

Inspiration kept, the cells dropped. Clement, in the Stromata, retells the origin to argue that the Greek philosophers borrowed from Moses, and reports the translation as itself inspired: the people of Jerusalem "selected… seventy elders, versed in the Scriptures, and skilled in the Greek dialect," who, "each having severally translated each prophetic book," found their drafts on being "compared together" to "agree both in meaning and expression" — for "it was not alien to the inspiration of God, who gave the prophecy, also to produce the translation, and make it as it were Greek prophecy."16 Like Irenaeus he affirms the inspiration and braces it with the same Esdras parallel — the Scriptures "having perished in the captivity of Nebuchadnezzar," Esdras, "inspired," "restored again the whole of the ancient Scriptures." But the machinery differs: where Irenaeus had sealed the elders apart so their unforeseeable agreement would prove the miracle, Clement's translators reach their agreement by comparison, working as Philo's had, together — no separate cells. Against Tertullian, who had just dropped the miracle entirely, Clement restores it; the inspiration line does not climb steadily. Two details track Irenaeus rather than Aristeas: he counts "seventy" elders, not seventy-two, and is openly unsure of the king, naming "Ptolemy the son of Lagos, or, according to others… Philadelphus." He widens the scope to "the law and… the prophets," and closes by citing Aristobulus by name.

The Cohortatio ad Graecos (c. AD 260)

The cells made physical — and the first eyewitness claim. The Cohortatio ad Graecos (Hortatory Address to the Greeks), an apology for the faith addressed to pagan readers and transmitted under Justin Martyr's name but written by a later, unknown hand, gives Irenaeus's separate-cells miracle its fullest concrete form: Ptolemy housed the seventy translators in "as many little cots as there were translators," seven stadia off on the island of Pharos, each man working alone and kept from "communication with one another, in order that the accuracy of the translation might be discernible even by their agreement"; when the drafts matched word for word, the king believed "the translation had been written by divine power."17 Where Irenaeus said only that the king "separated them" and Clement dropped the isolation entirely, here the separation becomes architecture — a row of individual cells, one per translator, guarded into silence. The author then does what no earlier teller had: he stakes the whole story on autopsy — "we ourselves having been in Alexandria, saw the remains of the little cots at the Pharos still preserved" — and names Philo and Josephus as his authorities,18 though neither of those named sources has the separate cells at all. The legend is here at its most vivid — no longer merely told but claimed to be "still preserved" and seen. (The work is usually cited as Pseudo-Justin; its date is disputed, ranging from the mid-third to the fourth century, and is given here by the conventional earlier reckoning.)

Eusebius of Caesarea (c. AD 313)

The compiler — the legend gathered, and given a providential frame. Eusebius, the first Church historian, mostly collects the story's sources and sets them in order rather than adding to it. In the Chronicon he notes in passing that the text "we use was translated by seventy Hebrew men… in complete harmony during the reign of Ptolemaeus Philadelphus, and placed… in the library at Alexandria."19 In the Praeparatio Evangelica he prefaces a full transcription of the Letter of Aristeas with a new theological reading: the translation was God's own doing — "God put it into the mind of King Ptolemy" to have the prophecies of Christ "accurately translated, and set up in public libraries" before He came, so the Jews could not afterward have "hidden away" the oracles "for their jealousy of us."20 And in the Church History he reproduces Irenaeus's separate-cells miracle verbatim — the translators "separated… from one another," their independent drafts agreeing "in the same words… from beginning to end," so "the heathen perceived that the Scriptures had been translated by the inspiration of God."21 He keeps Irenaeus's round "seventy," adds no new physical detail to the cells, and (through Anatolius) even repeats the anachronism that folds Aristobulus himself back "among the seventy interpreters." His own contribution is the frame: the inspiration miracle is now an act of divine providence for the Gentile mission — the very premise that, turned against the synagogue's later Hebrew, becomes the corruption charge he presses elsewhere on the chronology of Genesis.

Cyril of Jerusalem (c. AD 350)

The fullest classical form — and the Holy Ghost named. Cyril, bishop of Jerusalem, folds the origin story into the catechism itself: instructing baptismal candidates which Scriptures the Church receives, he tells them how the Greek came to be. He restores the precise Aristean frame the earlier Christian tellers had blurred — Ptolemy Philadelphus, Demetrius the librarian, "Eleazar … High Priest" (whom Justin had garbled into "Herod"), "six interpreters from each of the twelve tribes" finishing "in seventy-two days" — and keeps the physical separate chambers the Cohortatio had built, "assigning to each … his separate chamber in the island called Pharos," so that the drafts "agreed not only in the sense but even in words."22 What is new is the explicit pneumatology: where Philo had only an "unseen prompter" and Irenaeus "the inspiration of God," Cyril states it doctrinally — the work "was no word-craft, nor contrivance of human devices: but the translation of the Divine Scriptures, spoken by the Holy Ghost, was of the Holy Ghost accomplished." The miracle is here both physically detailed and fixed as catechesis, something a candidate for baptism is taught to believe. He also gives the king's isolation a motive — Ptolemy separates the elders "to make experiment whether the books were Divine or not" — but claims no eyewitness ruins, reporting the cells as received history rather than (with the Cohortatio) visible remains.

Epiphanius of Salamis (c. AD 392)

The cells re-paired, the seventy-two named, the divergences defended. Epiphanius, bishop of Salamis, gives the legend its most elaborate antique form in his Treatise on Weights and Measures. He keeps the full Aristean frame and the physical cells but rebuilds the architecture: where the Cohortatio and Cyril sealed each man alone with the whole Scripture, Epiphanius makes "thirty-six cells… formed… into pairs," shuts the translators "two by two," and gives "to every pair one book" — Genesis to one pair, Exodus to the next, and so on — yet keeps the proof intact, the copies agreeing so that the work was the gift "of the Holy Spirit."23 He is the first to explain the seventy-vs-seventy-two drift (after Moses's seventy elders, two added so six came evenly from each tribe) and the first to set down the whole roster by name, "as Aristeas has transmitted it." His most consequential move reverses the legend's logic: where the earlier tellers proved inspiration by the translators' identity with the Hebrew, Epiphanius openly concedes the Greek differs — they "added a word" and "made an omission" — and turns the difference itself into the miracle, for the additions mend a Hebrew that without them is "lame" (his demonstration from Psalm 140), "well added… for the advantage of the peoples about to be called to the faith."24 The inspired-Septuagint premise here built is the one the same treatise's notice on Aquila turns against the synagogue's rival Greek.

Rufinus of Aquileia (c. AD 401)

The legend made a weapon. Rufinus, the monk and translator of Aquileia, is the first on this timeline not to retell the origin but to argue from it. In his Apology against his estranged friend Jerome — who was then translating the Old Testament afresh from the Hebrew — he treats the inspired-cells miracle as settled fact and turns it into a legal title: "The seventy translators, each in their separate cells, produced a version couched in consonant and identical words, under the inspiration, as we cannot doubt, of the Holy Spirit; and this version must certainly be of more authority with us than a translation made by a single man," charging that Jerome had "dared to make havoc of the divine record handed down to the Churches by the Apostles."25 He keeps the cells in the plain Irenaean form — sealed isolation plus the Holy Spirit, no new physical detail — but for the first time the legend does work rather than being told. And he draws the inference outright: because the Septuagint is the Spirit-given text, Jerome's revision from the Hebrew is not recovery but corruption — "our law has been amended, or at least changed… things which undergo change at the hand of man cannot possibly be divine… Now therefore after four hundred years the truth of the law comes forth for us, it has been bought with money from the Synagogue."26 Where Jerome calls the separate cells a fable, Rufinus makes them the standing premise of the dispute — the exact point at which the origin legend fuses with the corruption charge.

Augustine of Hippo (c. AD 425)

The legend in the Latin West's authoritative voice — and pressed further. Augustine, bishop of Hippo, retells the origin in The City of God with the full Aristean frame restored — Ptolemy Philadelphus (distinguished from "the son of Lagus"), Eleazar the high priest, "seventy-two, out of each of the twelve tribes six men," the version "now by custom called the Septuagint" — and keeps the separate-cells proof in its plainer form: the elders sat "each one apart (for so it pleased Ptolemy to test their fidelity)" yet "differed from each other in no word… but, as if the translators had been one, so what all had translated was one, because in very deed the one Spirit had been in them all," the gift given "that the authority of these Scriptures might be commended not as human but divine… for the benefit of the nations."27 He adds no physical detail to the cells — no cots on Pharos, no paired chambers — and prefaces even the isolation with "it is reported," a soberer telling like Tertullian's. His decisive move comes in the chapter following: because "the same Spirit who was in the prophets… was also in the seventy men when they translated them," where the Greek differs from the Hebrew both readings are to be received as inspired — "thus showing that both were prophets."28 It is the same reversal Epiphanius had made in the East, now the Latin West's charter for the Septuagint's authority — and the premise that, turned against the synagogue's later Hebrew, becomes the corruption charge.

Cyril of Alexandria (c. AD 444)

The scope widened from the Law to the Prophets. Cyril, patriarch of Alexandria, refers to the origin of the Greek Scriptures in his apology Against Julian, and his contribution to the legend's growth is not a new miracle but a quiet expansion of what the Seventy are said to have done: he writes as though the seventy-two translated not only the Pentateuch — all the Letter of Aristeas claims for them — but "the holy prophets" as well, folding the whole Greek Old Testament into the one inspired translation.29 It is the scope-creep Justin ("the books of the prophets") and Clement ("the law and… the prophets") had already begun, now settled into the unspoken assumption that "the Seventy" produced the entire Greek Bible. Zacharias of Mitylene (c. AD 518)

The legend in Syriac — bare, and drawn straight from Eusebius. The Syriac Chronicle transmitted under the name of Zacharias of Mitylene reports the origin in one sentence that names its source — "as the Chronicle of Eusebius of Caesarea declares" — and reproduces a Eusebian date ("two hundred and eighty years and more before the birth of our Lord"): Ptolemy Philadelphus freed the Jewish captives, "sent offerings to Jerusalem," and "assembled seventy men learned in the law" to render the Holy Scriptures "from the Hebrew tongue into Greek."30 For the through-line this timeline traces, Zacharias carries neither the separate cells nor the marvel of identical independent renderings: he keeps Eusebius's round "seventy" (not the seventy-two of Aristeas), garbles the high priest into "Izra‘el" (as Justin had written "Herod"), and tells only the plain royal commission of Tertullian's and Josephus's form. What he adds is theological gloss, not narrative: he compresses Eusebius's providential frame — the translation prepared "for the calling of the nations" — and gives it a sixth-century conciliar edge, the king "moved by God… that they might be true worshippers of the glorious Trinity through the ministration of the Spirit." The inspiration is of the occasion (God moved Ptolemy), not of the translating; the wonder at the desk is absent. (The source is the late-antique Syriac compilation conventionally called Pseudo-Zachariah Rhetor — Book 12 is the compiler's own continuation, not Zacharias's lost history; dated here at his floruit, c. 518.)

Isidore of Seville (c. AD 636)

The legend folded into an encyclopedia. Isidore, bishop of Seville and the great compiler of late-antique Latin learning, gives the origin of the Greek Scriptures two terse paragraphs in his Etymologies, the most-copied schoolbook of the medieval West. Under "libraries" he sets Ptolemy Philadelphus emulating Pisistratus and gathering "seventy thousand books" at Alexandria; under "translators" he reports that the king, "requesting the Scriptures of the Old Testament from the high priest Eleazar, took care to have them translated… by seventy translators," each "separated in his own cell," who rendered everything "through the Holy Spirit" so that no copy "differed from the rest even in the order of words."31 Both the separate cells and the inspiration claim are present, but in the most reduced form on this timeline: the round "seventy" of Eusebius and Clement (not the seventy-two of Aristeas), one cell apiece (no Cohortatio cots on Pharos, no paired chambers), no eyewitness ruins, and — uniquely for this encyclopedist of numbers, who counts the books, the translators, and the cells alike at seventy — no count of days. He restores the high priest Eleazar by name (where the Syriac Zacharias had garbled him into "Izra‘el") and names Ptolemy correctly: in substance Augustine's form, reduced to seventy and fixed as a reference-shelf fact the Latin Middle Ages would copy for six centuries.

Eustathius of Tarihan (c. AD 750)

The developed form alive in the Syriac East. Eustathius of Tarihan, an East Syriac writer of the see of Tirhan on the Tigris, opens his biblical Commentaries with the legend in full: "the seventy elders whom Ptolemy, the king of Egypt sent for… that they might translate… the books of the prophets from Hebrew into Greek," housed in "small lodgings corresponding to the number of them, not in Alexandria, but at… seventy stadia, so that each one… should complete his translation by himself alone," with attendants set to "prevent them from talking with one another" — so that, the drafts agreeing "through the conformity of their words," the king "believed that the translation had been made by the power of God."32 Both the separate cells and the inspiration miracle are here in their strong Irenaean form — two centuries deeper into the East than Zacharias, whose bare Eusebian abridgment had dropped them. His distinctive notes are the widened scope ("the books of the prophets," continuing the drift of Justin and Cyril of Alexandria), a generalized "seventy stadia" echo of the Cohortatio's Pharos, and the round "seventy" (not the seventy-two of Aristeas), with neither Demetrius nor Eleazar named — the frame pared to king, cells, identical drafts, and divine power, set as the preface to a working commentary. ⚠ This block rests on the verbatim translation printed by the Wassersteins; the underlying Syriac is not yet verified against a direct edition, so the attribution is treated as unverified.

Ishodad of Merv (c. AD 850)

The legend pared back to a bare commission. Ishodad of Merv, bishop of Ḥedatta and the great East Syriac biblical commentator, opens his Commentaries with the origin of the Greek Old Testament in a single sentence: the version "was made under the patronage of Ptolemy Philadelphus… on the island of Pharos near Alexandria by seventy-two elders of the Jews, who were very well versed in the Hebrew and the Greek languages from their youth."33 For the through-line this timeline traces, Ishodad carries neither the separate cells nor the inspiration miracle: the marvel Eustathius had told whole a century before is simply gone, leaving the plain royal commission of Aristeas and Tertullian. His one descriptive note runs the opposite way from the inspiration tradition — he grounds the translators' reliability not in the Holy Spirit (as Cyril of Jerusalem had) but in their human competence, men "very well versed in the Hebrew and the Greek languages from their youth," the most naturalistic notice on this timeline and a fit one for the first East Syriac writer to cite the Septuagint and the rival Greek versions (Aquila, Symmachus, Theodotion) as a working text critic. He restores Aristeas's precise "seventy-two" against the round seventy his fellow Syriac Eustathius had kept, names Pharos and Philadelphus correctly, and widens the scope to the whole "Greek version of the Old Testament." ⚠ This block rests on the verbatim translation printed by the Wassersteins; the underlying Syriac is not yet verified against a direct edition, so the attribution is treated as unverified.

Moses bar Kepha (c. AD 903)

The cells restored, the Spirit left unspoken. Moses bar Kepha, bishop of the Syriac Orthodox Church, retells the origin in his Hexaemeron Commentary: Ptolemy Philadelphus, "who loved wisdom and collected many books," sends to the Hebrews for the Old Testament and "summoned seventy two men… who knew the Hebrew and the Greek languages very well," then "assigned each of them a separate cell to live in, in order to see whether they agreed with each other or not," and "all of them agreed… very exactly."34 For the through-line this timeline traces, bar Kepha carries the cells but not the explicit miracle: he restores the separate-cells test that his fellow East Syriac Ishodad had dropped a half-century before — the isolation in its original Irenaean function, sealing each man apart so the agreement would prove itself — and the test succeeds, yet where Eustathius had drawn the conclusion outright ("made by the power of God"), bar Kepha stops at the agreement and leaves the inference unspoken. Within the East Syriac chain the legend thus oscillates — full in Eustathius (c. 750), bare in Ishodad (c. 850), half here: cells without the named Spirit. He keeps Aristeas's precise seventy-two and names Philadelphus, but drops Demetrius, Eleazar, Pharos, and the count of days. The inspiration verdict he draws elsewhere — in a note appended to this same survey, the LXX's antiquity becomes the ground for the corruption charge against the later Jewish versions, so that bar Kepha is in the Syriac East what Rufinus was in the Latin West: the writer in whom origin legend and corruption charge meet in one hand. ⚠ This block rests on the verbatim translation printed by the Wassersteins; the underlying Syriac of the Hexaemeron is not yet verified against a direct edition, so the attribution is treated as unverified.

Hugh of Saint Victor (c. AD 1141)

The legend told whole, then disowned. Hugh of Saint Victor, the Paris schoolman whose De scripturis et scriptoribus sacris served as an introduction to the study of the Bible, gives the origin story in its full inspiration form — Ptolemy Philadelphus had the Old Testament rendered into Greek "by seventy translators, whom he received from Eleazar the High Priest," split into "individual cells" so that, "as some say," none could deceive the king, yet "they translated everything by the Holy Spirit in such a way that nothing was to be found in one codex which was not to be found in the others" — and then, in the same breath, withdraws it: "But Jerome says that no credence should be given to this matter."35 He is the first witness on this timeline who is himself a skeptic of the cells, and he is so by quoting Jerome, whose verdict the Latin West had by his day accepted; the strong Irenaean form Rufinus once threw at Jerome as a weapon is here recorded as a quarrel settled the other way. He is also unusually candid about the Septuagint's deficiency: the version was later found to lack things "the authority both of Christ himself and of the apostles" had announced were in it, so "others undertook to translate… from the Hebrew" — Jerome's logic, the divergence a defect to be corrected against the Hebrew rather than (with Augustine and Epiphanius) a second inspired witness. With Hugh the skeptical counter-tradition, long a minority report answered by the inspiration-defenders, becomes the medieval schoolroom's default, and the inspired-Septuagint premise on which the corruption charge rests is quietly let go.

Arguments & cruxes

The central sub-claim — whether the inspiration legend (the divinely-guided, independently-identical translation, and the "changes" the seventy-two are said to have made) is history, pious fiction, or a memory of real translation choices — cuts across this timeline and the Rabbinic Corruption timeline alike. It is weighed under that doctrine, where it is grouped in the "corruption charge" complex; the Simeon-the-Just Isaiah 7:14 strand is queued alongside it and not yet authored.

The Septuagint legend: were the seventy-two divinely moved to change the text? (assessment: plausible)

The Septuagint-legend crux weighs the tradition this whole timeline traces — that the seventy-two translators were divinely moved to change specific passages for Ptolemy (the Tractate Megillah list, paralleled in the Letter of Aristeas frame). The interpretation is plausible at its kernel, not as the inspired history it is told as: a real subset of the listed "changes" are genuine, identifiable Septuagint translation choices (Genesis 2:2's "sixth day"; the euphemism for the hare at Leviticus 11:6), but the inspiration-miracle frame (separate cells, divinely-prompted unanimity) is a demonstrable later accretion — absent from the earliest source, Aristeas, and grown across centuries, exactly the growth this timeline charts — and the list is a fluid midrashic tradition several of whose items appear in no extant Septuagint. The crux lives on the rabbinic-corruption side, where the inspired-LXX premise turns the synagogue's divergent Hebrew into apparent corruption.36
