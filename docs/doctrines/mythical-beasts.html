Dragons, Unicorns & Mythical Beasts

Scripture is populated with great and strange creatures — the sea-tannin and the dragon (Gen 1:21; Ps 74:13; Isa 27:1; Rev 12), leviathan and behemoth (Job 40–41; Ps 104:26), the re'em the King James Version rendered "unicorn" (Num 23:22; Job 39:9; Ps 22:21), the "fiery flying serpent" (Isa 14:29; 30:6), and the satyrs and night-creatures of the prophets' oracles (Isa 13:21; 34:14). Christian interpreters read these along several axes: as literal animals (natural, if rare or now-extinct kinds), as allegory — the dragon for the devil or for sin, leviathan for death — or as pieces of pagan legend to be sifted from fact. Around them grew a vast body of legend: dragon-slaying saints, cryptid and sea-serpent reports, the medieval bestiary. This page traces how the great beasts were understood across time, earliest witness first.

Timeline

The chronological witnesses are being added, earliest first.

Herodotus (c. 440 BC)

Herodotus, writing four centuries before Christ, is the earliest witness on this page and its only pre-Christian pagan one: he reports the winged serpents of Arabia not as a figure to be interpreted but as plain natural history. He claims to have gone to see their remains himself — "almost exactly opposite the city of Buto" he saw "the back-bones and ribs of serpents in such numbers as it is impossible to describe" — and relays that each spring "the winged snakes come flying from Arabia towards Egypt, but are met in this gorge by the birds called ibises, who forbid their entrance and destroy them all," the service for which the Egyptians revere the ibis. The creature is soberly described, "shaped like the water-snake," its "wings… not feathered, but resembl[ing] very closely those of the bat."1 In Arabia the same serpents, "small in size, and of varied colours," swarm and guard the frankincense trees, driven off only by the smoke of styrax; and Herodotus folds them into a providential order of nature that keeps the deadly kinds few.2 He interprets no Scripture, but supplies the classical natural-history tradition of the "flying serpent" — the backdrop against which the Bible's own "fiery flying serpent" (Isa 30:6) and its great beasts would later be read — and heads the stream that runs down through Ammianus Marcellinus to the timeline's literal-animal readers.

Cyril of Jerusalem (c. 350)

Cyril of Jerusalem, instructing candidates for baptism, reads Scripture's dragon as allegory pressed into a baptismal typology, and is the earliest Christian witness on this page. A great "dragon" lay in the waters — "the dragon that draweth up Jordan into his mouth" (Job 40:23 LXX) — and because "it was necessary to break the heads of the dragon in pieces" (Ps 74:14), Christ "went down and bound the strong one in the waters" at His own baptism, "that we might receive power to tread upon serpents and scorpions" (Luke 10:19). Cyril takes Job's picture of the beast at face value — "great and terrible," its tail too heavy for a fishing-boat — but bends the whole image toward salvation-history: "The Life encountered him, that the mouth of Death might henceforth be stopped," so that "the sting of death is drawn by Baptism."3 The typology completes itself in the candidate: as Christ went down to bind the dragon, so the baptized "goest down into the water, bearing thy sins," and the sealed soul is not "swallowed up by the terrible dragon."4 His "break the heads of the dragon" (Ps 74:14) anticipates Augustine's reading of the same verse's dragon-heads broken "upon the water" of Baptism, a generation and a half later.

Athanasius of Alexandria (c. 360)

Athanasius, in the widely-read Life of Antony, reads the great beasts of Job as pure allegory for the devil. Putting the discernment of spirits into the hermit's mouth, he has the demons appear "as the Lord revealed the devil to Job" — clothed in leviathan's fire and smoke, "From his mouth proceed burning lamps… his breath is coals and from his mouth issues flame" — the enemy wearing the mask of Job's monster to strike terror.5 The boast then collapses into Christ's victory: "like a dragon he was drawn with a hook by the Saviour, and… his nostrils were bound with a ring, and his lips bored with an armlet," leashed "as a sparrow, that we should mock him" — God's unanswerable questions to Job ("Canst thou draw out leviathan with an hook?… Canst thou put an hook into his nose?", Job 41:1–2) answered in the Saviour, who has hooked and tamed the devil.6 He is an early and pure representative of the dragon-as-devil strand — no interest here in whether leviathan is a real animal.

Basil of Caesarea (c. 368)

Basil the Great, preaching on Psalm 32 (LXX; = Ps 33) — "praise is comely for the upright" — reads Scripture's dragon as pure allegory, and turns it toward the moral life. The "crooked serpent" of Isaiah 27:1 is "the author of sin," against whom "the sword of God is drawn": the serpent is crooked, "which makes many twists and turns in its progress," so that "one who follows the serpent shows that his life is crooked, uneven and filled with contrarieties; but one who follows after the Lord makes his paths straight."7 Like Athanasius, Basil has no interest here in whether the dragon is a real animal; where Athanasius fixes on the enemy vanquished, Basil fixes on the enemy as tempter, the beast made a mirror of the twisting path the sinner walks.

Ephrem the Syrian (c. 373)

Ephrem the Syrian reads the two great beasts of Job 40–41 along both of the doctrine's axes, and is the earliest witness on this page to hold them together. On "Behold now behemoth, which I made with thee" (Job 40:15) he gives a flat, one-line zoological identification: "The Behemoth is a dragon, that is, a land animal, just as the Leviathan is an aquatic sea animal" — the pair told apart simply by habitat, as real creatures of land and sea.8 Yet on Leviathan's boast in Job 41 the same monsters turn to allegory: their "prodigious nature" describes "the devil… destined to be conquered by the power of Christ when the fullness of time will come."9 He thus anticipates Augustine's both/and by a generation — literal where the verse describes a creature, figurative where it describes the enemy — though tersely, and without Augustine's explicit statement of the genre principle that governs it.

Ammianus Marcellinus (c. 390)

Ammianus Marcellinus, the last great historian of classical Latin and a pagan writing amid the Christian generation of Ephrem and Augustine, carries Herodotus's flying serpent down into the fourth century AD. In the Egyptian excursus of his Res Gestae he records, as ordinary natural history, that the ibis is honoured for keeping the snakes down — it "sometimes encounter[s] flocks of winged snakes, which come laden with poison from the marshes of Arabia," which it "overcome[s] in the air, and then devour[s]."10 It is Herodotus's report — the Arabia-to-Egypt migration, the destroying ibis — restated some eight centuries on, and, like Herodotus, offered as fact by a writer with no stake in how Job's beasts should be read. He stands with the page's literal / natural-history strand, but as its one secular, pagan voice contemporary with the exegetes on either side of him.

Augustine of Hippo (c. 415)

Augustine reads Scripture's "dragons" along both of the doctrine's central axes, and does so without treating them as rivals. Expounding "Praise ye the Lord from the earth. Dragons and all abysses" (Ps 148:7), he takes the dragon as a literal animal — it lives about the water, comes out of caverns, flies through the air, and is "a huge kind of living creatures, greater there are not upon the earth."11 When the psalm bids such beasts "praise" God, he refuses to allegorize the creature away: dragons do not "form choirs," but move the beholder to praise their Maker — "then the dragons praise God by your voices."12 Yet on "Thou hast broken the heads of the dragons in the waters" (Ps 74:13–14) the same word is pure allegory: the "dragons' heads" are "demons' pride" broken in the water of Baptism, and the one "great dragon… whose head was broken" is "the devil himself," his broken head read through Genesis 3:15 as "the beginning of sin," which "is pride."13 He is the tradition's clearest early witness that the literal and figurative readings are governed by genre and context, not by a fixed verdict on what a dragon is.

Julian of Eclanum (c. 425)

Julian of Eclanum, in his Exposition on the Book of Job written during his exile, reads the great beasts of Job 40–41 along both axes but weights them toward the literal creature more decidedly than Ephrem or Augustine. On "Behold now behemoth, which I made with thee" (Job 40:15) he takes the beast as a real, terrifying animal and turns its very dreadfulness into a compact theodicy: "the power of the Creator did not only make those beasts that would have served human beings but also fashioned those who frighten them"; the "goodness of Providence" is seen because "it removed those beasts that would have been deadly from the midst [of humans] and placed them in the wilderness"; and there men "learn how severe he is against vices."14 He states his order of readings outright on "he is a king over all the children of pride" (Job 41:34): the literal is "the true description of the beast according to the faithful understanding of the story," its great size to be believed "because of the power of the Creator," and only then, without displacing the creature, does he grant a further sense "to a plane of higher intellect," so that "the strength of spiritual iniquity may be perceived more easily in the image of the bodies," the beast charged with "the sin of pride."15 The figure rides on the fact — a both/and, but with the literal creature affirmed first and the allegory added atop it — a pointed foil to Gregory the Great, who a century and a half later would read the very same beasts as pure allegory of the devil.

Sozomen (c. 450)

Sozomen, the church historian, breaks from the exegetes on either side of him: he reads the dragon not as a biblical figure to be interpreted at all, but as a literal beast encountered in living memory. Cataloguing holy bishops of the recent past in his Ecclesiastical History, he relays as sober local history that Donatus, bishop of Euroea in Epirus (fl. early fourth century), destroyed "a dragon of enormous size" that had stationed itself on the highway at Chamaegephyrae and "devoured sheep, goats, oxen, horses, and men." The bishop killed it unarmed — making the sign of the cross in the air and spitting into its mouth — and the carcass, "not inferior in size to the noted serpents of India," took "eight pair of oxen" to haul off for burning "that it might not… corrupt the air and generate disease."16 There is no allegory here: the beast is weighed, measured, and disposed of like any large dead animal, and the account is guaranteed by named local witnesses. Sozomen stands with the literal-animal readers — Augustine, Julian, and later John of Damascus — but supplies what none of them do: a contemporary, this-worldly sighting, and this page's earliest full specimen of the saint-versus-dragon motif that would later cluster around St George.

Gregory the Great (c. 595)

Gregory the Great, in the closing books of his vast Morals on the Book of Job, gives the ancient tradition's fullest sustained allegorical reading of Job's two great beasts: Behemoth (Job 40:15) and Leviathan (Job 41) are one figure — the devil, "the ancient enemy." On "Behold Behemoth, which I made with thee" he asks, "Whom does He suggest, under the name 'Behemoth,' except the ancient enemy? which being interpreted from the Hebrew word, means 'Animal' in the Latin tongue" — the beast's name a placeholder that "his malice… added below" fills in with a person, and, against the Manichees, a creature God "rightly fashioned by nature" who fell into being "the author… of wickedness."17 On God's question "Wilt thou be able to draw out Leviathan with a hook?" (Job 41:1) he supplies the developed form of the motif Athanasius and Cyril had sounded: "this Leviathan was caught with a hook, because… he seized… the bait of His Body, the sharp sting of His Godhead pierced him through" — Christ's flesh the bait, His concealed Godhead the barb that kills the devourer.18 He is the pure-allegory strand at full length, and a direct foil to Ephrem, who read the very same verse (Job 40:15) as bare zoology — "Behemoth is a dragon, that is, a land animal."

Bede (c. 705)

The Venerable Bede, in his early Commentary on Revelation, reads Scripture's dragon as pure allegory — not a creature at all, but one of the devil's names. On "he laid hold on the dragon, that old serpent, which is the Devil, and Satan" (Rev 20:2) he glosses the four titles the verse heaps on the enemy as a set of etymologies, each naming a vice: "He is called a dragon because of the malice of harming; a serpent because of the cunning of deceiving; the devil because of his fall from his status; and Satan because of his obstinacy in opposing the Lord."19 The "dragon" of Revelation is thus read straight through to the person of the devil, glossed alongside his other names, with no interest in whether a dragon is a real animal. Bede stands squarely in the line of Gregory the Great, whose Moralia he knew intimately — the pure-allegory strand compressed from Gregory's three books into a single glossarial sentence — and is a near-exact contemporary of, yet the polar opposite reader from, John of Damascus, who was defending the dragon as a real animal in the East at almost the same moment.

John of Damascus (c. 730)

John of Damascus, in a short treatise On Dragons and Ghosts, draws a firm line between real creatures and folkloric fiction: dragons exist, he grants, but they are ordinary animals — large serpents "borne of other serpents," growing "more than thirty cubits" and "as thick as a big log," a beast among the beasts Adam named.20 What he denies is the pagan and folk belief that dragons are intelligent beings who take human form, turn into serpents at will, and carry off women: God made only two rational natures, angels and men, so a scheming shapeshifting dragon "has not been true, and never will be" — and of the tale that thunder kills dragons, "When I heard this I laughed!"21 He describes a bearded, horned, golden-eyed kind (an agathodemon) and notes that dragons, alone among serpents, "do not emit poison," and he relays from Cassius Dio the giant serpent slain by the consul Regulus at the Bagradas, whose 120-foot hide was measured "by order of the senate" — cited as natural history, not legend.22 He is the tradition's clearest early witness for reading the dragon as a real animal rather than an apparition.

Marco Polo (c. 1298)

Marco Polo, the Venetian merchant, is the latest witness on this page and its one high-medieval traveler: in the account of his eastern journeys dictated c. 1298 he reports, as plain natural history, that the province of Carajan (Yunnan) breeds enormous clawed "serpents." Some "are ten paces in length… the bigger ones are about ten palms in girth. They have two forelegs near the head, but for foot nothing but a claw… The mouth is large enough to swallow a man whole, and is garnished with great [pointed] teeth."23 He adds the practical detail of a real quarry — the beasts lair in caverns by day and feed by night, and hunters kill them by burying iron-spiked stakes in the sand-tracks they take to the water, then prize the gall as medicine.24 Polo offers no allegory and reads no Scripture — he catalogues a beast; and his editor Henry Yule gives the standard identification: "It cannot be doubted that Marco's serpents here are crocodiles, in spite of his strange mistakes about their having only two feet and one claw on each."25 He stands with the page's literal-animal / legend strand — like Sozomen a this-worldly sighting reported as fact — and shows the medieval dragon-report's mechanism plainly: a real, alarming reptile, imperfectly seen and vividly retold into a "great serpent."

Arguments

Sub-claims marshaled for (or against) a reading above, which cut across the timeline rather than sitting at one date — each weighed adversarially on its own page, with an assessment of the interpretation it serves.

Does the broad classical attestation of the Bagradas serpent prove a real giant animal? (assessment: plausible)

The Bagradas serpent's many-author attestation — the report, relayed here by John of Damascus from Cassius Dio, that the consul Regulus fought and killed a colossal serpent at the Bagradas river during the First Punic War (256 BC), its 120-foot hide measured "by order of the senate" and sent to Rome; the same beast appears in Pliny, Aulus Gellius (from Tubero), Valerius Maximus, Silius Italicus, Florus, and Orosius, and that breadth of testimony is pressed as proof of a real animal of extraordinary size. The interpretation is plausible in its modest form and overstated in its headline. That a real, large snake — most plausibly an African rock python — lay behind the story is coherent and arguably probable: the report is early (Tubero and Livy, both within about two centuries of the event), it entered serious Roman history, and a very large constrictor in North Africa is zoologically ordinary. But the argument's two props fail: the "many independent authors" collapse to essentially one Livian source-tradition copied author-to-author (Valerius Maximus cites Livy by name; Florus, Orosius, Silius, and the epitomators all descend from it; only Cassius Dio's late Greek line is arguably separate), so the breadth is illusory; and the invariant "120 feet" is a copied, physically impossible figure (about six times any real python), the mark of legend accreting on a real kernel. Trust it as evidence of a real large serpent; distrust it as proof of a giant, or as many witnesses rather than one much-copied one.26
