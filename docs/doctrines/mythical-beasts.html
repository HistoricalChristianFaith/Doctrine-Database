Dragons, Unicorns & Mythical Beasts

Scripture is populated with great and strange creatures — the sea-tannin and the dragon (Gen 1:21; Ps 74:13; Isa 27:1; Rev 12), leviathan and behemoth (Job 40–41; Ps 104:26), the re'em the King James Version rendered "unicorn" (Num 23:22; Job 39:9; Ps 22:21), the "fiery flying serpent" (Isa 14:29; 30:6), and the satyrs and night-creatures of the prophets' oracles (Isa 13:21; 34:14). Christian interpreters read these along several axes: as literal animals (natural, if rare or now-extinct kinds), as allegory — the dragon for the devil or for sin, leviathan for death — or as pieces of pagan legend to be sifted from fact. Around them grew a vast body of legend: dragon-slaying saints, cryptid and sea-serpent reports, the medieval bestiary. This page traces how the great beasts were understood across time, earliest witness first.

John of Damascus (c. 730)

John of Damascus, in a short treatise On Dragons and Ghosts, draws a firm line between real creatures and folkloric fiction: dragons exist, he grants, but they are ordinary animals — large serpents "borne of other serpents," growing "more than thirty cubits" and "as thick as a big log," a beast among the beasts Adam named.1 What he denies is the pagan and folk belief that dragons are intelligent beings who take human form, turn into serpents at will, and carry off women: God made only two rational natures, angels and men, so a scheming shapeshifting dragon "has not been true, and never will be" — and of the tale that thunder kills dragons, "When I heard this I laughed!"2 He describes a bearded, horned, golden-eyed kind (an agathodemon) and notes that dragons, alone among serpents, "do not emit poison," and he relays from Cassius Dio the giant serpent slain by the consul Regulus at the Bagradas, whose 120-foot hide was measured "by order of the senate" — cited as natural history, not legend.3 He is the tradition's clearest early witness for reading the dragon as a real animal rather than an apparition.
