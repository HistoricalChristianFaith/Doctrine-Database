Dragons, Unicorns & Mythical Beasts

Scripture is populated with great and strange creatures — the sea-tannin and the dragon (Gen 1:21; Ps 74:13; Isa 27:1; Rev 12), leviathan and behemoth (Job 40–41; Ps 104:26), the re'em the King James Version rendered "unicorn" (Num 23:22; Job 39:9; Ps 22:21), the "fiery flying serpent" (Isa 14:29; 30:6), and the satyrs and night-creatures of the prophets' oracles (Isa 13:21; 34:14). Christian interpreters read these along several axes: as literal animals (natural, if rare or now-extinct kinds), as allegory — the dragon for the devil or for sin, leviathan for death — or as pieces of pagan legend to be sifted from fact. Around them grew a vast body of legend: dragon-slaying saints, cryptid and sea-serpent reports, the medieval bestiary. This page traces how the great beasts were understood across time, earliest witness first.

Timeline

The chronological witnesses are being added, earliest first.

Athanasius of Alexandria (c. 360)

Athanasius, in the widely-read Life of Antony, reads the great beasts of Job as pure allegory for the devil. Putting the discernment of spirits into the hermit's mouth, he has the demons appear "as the Lord revealed the devil to Job" — clothed in leviathan's fire and smoke, "From his mouth proceed burning lamps… his breath is coals and from his mouth issues flame" — the enemy wearing the mask of Job's monster to strike terror.1 The boast then collapses into Christ's victory: "like a dragon he was drawn with a hook by the Saviour, and… his nostrils were bound with a ring, and his lips bored with an armlet," leashed "as a sparrow, that we should mock him" — God's unanswerable questions to Job ("Canst thou draw out leviathan with an hook?… Canst thou put an hook into his nose?", Job 41:1–2) answered in the Saviour, who has hooked and tamed the devil.2 He is the tradition's earliest witness on this page, and a pure representative of the dragon-as-devil strand — no interest here in whether leviathan is a real animal.

Basil of Caesarea (c. 368)

Basil the Great, preaching on Psalm 32 (LXX; = Ps 33) — "praise is comely for the upright" — reads Scripture's dragon as pure allegory, and turns it toward the moral life. The "crooked serpent" of Isaiah 27:1 is "the author of sin," against whom "the sword of God is drawn": the serpent is crooked, "which makes many twists and turns in its progress," so that "one who follows the serpent shows that his life is crooked, uneven and filled with contrarieties; but one who follows after the Lord makes his paths straight."3 Like Athanasius, Basil has no interest here in whether the dragon is a real animal; where Athanasius fixes on the enemy vanquished, Basil fixes on the enemy as tempter, the beast made a mirror of the twisting path the sinner walks.

Ephrem the Syrian (c. 373)

Ephrem the Syrian reads the two great beasts of Job 40–41 along both of the doctrine's axes, and is the earliest witness on this page to hold them together. On "Behold now behemoth, which I made with thee" (Job 40:15) he gives a flat, one-line zoological identification: "The Behemoth is a dragon, that is, a land animal, just as the Leviathan is an aquatic sea animal" — the pair told apart simply by habitat, as real creatures of land and sea.4 Yet on Leviathan's boast in Job 41 the same monsters turn to allegory: their "prodigious nature" describes "the devil… destined to be conquered by the power of Christ when the fullness of time will come."5 He thus anticipates Augustine's both/and by a generation — literal where the verse describes a creature, figurative where it describes the enemy — though tersely, and without Augustine's explicit statement of the genre principle that governs it.

Augustine of Hippo (c. 415)

Augustine reads Scripture's "dragons" along both of the doctrine's central axes, and does so without treating them as rivals. Expounding "Praise ye the Lord from the earth. Dragons and all abysses" (Ps 148:7), he takes the dragon as a literal animal — it lives about the water, comes out of caverns, flies through the air, and is "a huge kind of living creatures, greater there are not upon the earth."6 When the psalm bids such beasts "praise" God, he refuses to allegorize the creature away: dragons do not "form choirs," but move the beholder to praise their Maker — "then the dragons praise God by your voices."7 Yet on "Thou hast broken the heads of the dragons in the waters" (Ps 74:13–14) the same word is pure allegory: the "dragons' heads" are "demons' pride" broken in the water of Baptism, and the one "great dragon… whose head was broken" is "the devil himself," his broken head read through Genesis 3:15 as "the beginning of sin," which "is pride."8 He is the tradition's clearest early witness that the literal and figurative readings are governed by genre and context, not by a fixed verdict on what a dragon is.

John of Damascus (c. 730)

John of Damascus, in a short treatise On Dragons and Ghosts, draws a firm line between real creatures and folkloric fiction: dragons exist, he grants, but they are ordinary animals — large serpents "borne of other serpents," growing "more than thirty cubits" and "as thick as a big log," a beast among the beasts Adam named.9 What he denies is the pagan and folk belief that dragons are intelligent beings who take human form, turn into serpents at will, and carry off women: God made only two rational natures, angels and men, so a scheming shapeshifting dragon "has not been true, and never will be" — and of the tale that thunder kills dragons, "When I heard this I laughed!"10 He describes a bearded, horned, golden-eyed kind (an agathodemon) and notes that dragons, alone among serpents, "do not emit poison," and he relays from Cassius Dio the giant serpent slain by the consul Regulus at the Bagradas, whose 120-foot hide was measured "by order of the senate" — cited as natural history, not legend.11 He is the tradition's clearest early witness for reading the dragon as a real animal rather than an apparition.
