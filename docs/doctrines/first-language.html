The First Language of Mankind

What language did Adam and Eve speak in Eden, and what single tongue did humanity share before the confusion of tongues at Babel (Gen 11:1–9)? Which surviving language, if any, preserved that primeval speech? The question turns on two texts — the confusion at Babel, and the genealogy in which Heber (Eber) fathers Peleg, "for in his days was the earth divided" (Gen 10:24–25) — and the interpreters divide into three camps. Camp A — Hebrew primacy: Hebrew was the original tongue, kept through Babel in the line of Heber, whence the very name "Hebrew" (Josephus, the Pseudo-Clementine Recognitions, Eusebius, Jerome, Augustine, Isidore, Jacob of Edessa, Bede). Camp B — Syriac/Aramaic primacy: Aramaic was first, and Hebrew a later "mixed" tongue Abraham took up after crossing the Euphrates (Theodore of Mopsuestia, Theodoret of Cyrus). Camp C — no divinely-given tongue: God gave the faculty of speech, but the words themselves are human convention (Gregory of Nyssa). Ephrem the Syrian sits athwart the divide: he affirms the shared premise of one primeval speech and one survivor at Babel, but never names that survivor, and the strong Syriac-primacy claim later hung on him is pseudo-Ephremic. The Hebrew-primacy reading is the earliest and, through Augustine and Isidore, the dominant one in the Latin West.

Timeline

The chronological witnesses are being added, earliest first. Alongside the Camp A (Hebrew-primacy) line it carries the Camp B (Syriac/Aramaic-primacy) witnesses, Theodore of Mopsuestia and Theodoret of Cyrus, and — from within the Syriac world — the Camp A counter-witness Jacob of Edessa; Ephrem the Syrian appears as a caveat who names no survivor. The Camp C witnesses are being added too.

Josephus (c. 93)

Josephus supplies the Jewish backdrop and both proof-texts of the Hebrew-primacy case without yet drawing the doctrine. He derives the people's name from the patriarch Heber — "his son was Heber, from whom they originally called the Jews Hebrews" — and reads Peleg's name as marking "the dispersion of the nations."1 And he gives the fullest early narrative of Babel, where mankind, "of one language" and understanding one another before, was scattered by a God-sent "confusion of that language" — the city thence called Babylon, "for the Hebrews mean by the word Babel, confusion."2

The Pseudo-Clementine Recognitions (c. 3rd–4th c.)

The Pseudo-Clementine Recognitions gives the earliest flat statement of the developed thesis: "Until that time the Hebrew language, which had been given by God to men, bore sole sway" — Hebrew the one tongue of the race, divinely given, until the generations of idolatry and dispersion.3 The text survives in Rufinus's Latin (c. 406) of an earlier Greek romance, so it witnesses a widely-held tradition of the period rather than a named author.

Eusebius of Caesarea (c. 313–322)

Eusebius affirms the narrow supports of the reading — the antiquity of the Hebrews and the derivation of their name and tongue from Heber, "the man that passes over" — noting that "the very name which is the appellation of the whole race has been derived from Heber," and that Moses came later than "those former true Hebrews, Heber and Abraham, from whom the derived name has been applied to the people."4 He preserves the Babel account of the one clear speech confused into many, but he does not develop the strong thesis that Hebrew survived Babel in Heber's line — that step is Augustine's, and should not be read back into him.5

Ephrem the Syrian (c. 363)

Ephrem the Syrian is a caveat, not a Camp B proponent. The greatest Syriac father holds that mankind once shared "a single language" and that at Babel every nation lost that original speech "with one exception" — but in his authentic works he never names whether the survivor was Hebrew or Syriac.6 The emphatic Syriac-primacy claim popularly hung on his name is pseudo-Ephremic — it comes from the Cave of Treasures (6th–7th c.), falsely ascribed to him — so Ephrem is best read as a witness to the framework both camps share, not a partisan of either.7

Jerome (c. 393)

Jerome, the Church's great Hebraist, makes Hebrew the "mother of all languages" (omnium linguarum matrix) — the hinge of his lifelong appeal to the Hebraica veritas. In the Hebrew Questions on Genesis he reads the name of the Hebrews from Heber and glosses Peleg's name as the division of tongues: "Eber, from whom the Hebrews are descended, gave his son Peleg the name which means 'division', because in his days the languages were divided in Babylon."8 Hebrew is the primeval tongue from which the rest descend — a conviction of a piece with his whole textual programme, and one that keeps Aramaic ("Chaldee") a later relative of Hebrew, not its parent.9

Theodore of Mopsuestia (c. 400)

Theodore of Mopsuestia is the fountainhead of the rival Camp B, in which Aramaic, not Hebrew, is the first tongue. His Greek commentary on Genesis survives only in fragments; his reported reading, quoted a century later in Ibn al-Nadīm's Fihrist, is that "God … addressed Adam in the Nabataean dialect, which was purer than the Syriac one" — the speech of Babel that alone went unchanged at the confusion, while the villagers' Aramaic decayed into "a broken Syriac."10 On this account Hebrew is no primeval mother-tongue but a latecomer. The attribution is well-attested in his East-Syrian reception — Isho’dad of Merv, Theodore bar Koni — though it cannot be confirmed from a surviving autograph, so the strongest wording is credited to him but ⚠ unverified at first hand.11

Augustine of Hippo (c. 420)

Augustine is the anchor witness, welding the earlier pieces into the full thesis. In City of God 16.11 he holds that Hebrew was "the primitive language of the race," nameless while it was the only tongue; at the confusion "there was still the house of Heber in which the primitive language of the race survived," and "because … his family preserved that language … it was on this account thenceforth named Hebrew."12 He argues it, not merely asserts it: the confusion of tongues was a punishment, so "it is fit to ascribe to the people of God an immunity from this punishment" — and he weighs and rejects the rival derivation of "Hebrew" from Abraham, fixing name and language on Heber.13

Theodoret of Cyrus (c. 453)

Theodoret, a native Syrian bishop who read Syriac, gives Camp B its most developed patristic form. In the Questions on the Octateuch he holds Syriac (Aramaic) to be more ancient than Hebrew — Hebrew the sacred tongue, but not the oldest (§§60–61; the wording of the modern critical translation is not freely extractable, so this is credited but ⚠ unverified at first hand)14 — and he rejects the derivation of "Hebrew" from the patriarch Heber, since "the Hebrews were not the only descendants of Eber, and, therefore, could not appropriate his name," deriving it instead from ʿebrā / περάτης, "one who crosses over," i.e. Abraham "the Hebrew" who crossed the Euphrates (Gen 14:13).15

Isidore of Seville (c. 636)

Isidore gives the most systematic statement in the Latin tradition. In the Etymologies he states that before Babel "there was one language for all nations, which is called Hebrew," that Adam named the animals in Hebrew — "the language of all peoples" before the Flood — and that "the Hebrew language is the mother of all languages and letters."16 He also rebuts the Syriac/Chaldean rival directly: if Hebrew were merely Chaldean, "how is it that the Hebrew children in the Book of Daniel … are ordered to be taught the Chaldean language, which they do not know?"17

Jacob of Edessa (c. 700)

Jacob of Edessa is Camp A's most striking witness — a Syriac bishop defending Hebrew as the Adamic tongue against the Syriac/Aramaic-primacy view that dominated his own tradition. Answering his correspondent John the Stylite, he holds Hebrew to be the original language of all, "not Syriac/Aramaic, as many people erroneously suppose, even some great and illustrious ones," and the tongue "beloved of God" — kept, after Babel, by the line of Eber, who "alone continued to speak the original Adamic language of Hebrew."18 The same premise drives his Hexaemeron, where the Syriac word for "heaven" is a loanword from Hebrew, "the first, Adamic tongue" of "the Hebrews" — Syriac the borrower, Hebrew the prior.19 Nearly every other Syriac writer took the opposite side, which makes his a deliberate, minority stance from inside Camp B's heartland.

Bede (c. 720)

Bede carries the Augustinian reading into the medieval West. On Genesis 11:1 he takes as given the one primeval speech — "the whole earth had one language and the same words" — broken at Babel as the punishment of pride, and sets Babel ("Confusion") against Jerusalem and Pentecost, where the Spirit reverses the scattering by gathering the many tongues into "one unvarying confession."20 He assumes the single original tongue rather than arguing it, standing avowedly in the line downstream of Augustine and Isidore.

Arguments

The sub-claims that cut across this timeline — chiefly whether the name and language "Hebrew" really descend from the patriarch Heber (the etymological hinge of the Hebrew-primacy case, set against the rival derivation from Aramaic), and whether the first tongue was a divine gift or a human convention — are being weighed on their own pages.
