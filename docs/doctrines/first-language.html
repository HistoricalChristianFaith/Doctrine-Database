The First Language of Mankind

What language did Adam and Eve speak in Eden, and what single tongue did humanity share before the confusion of tongues at Babel (Gen 11:1–9)? Which surviving language, if any, preserved that primeval speech? The question turns on two texts — the confusion at Babel, and the genealogy in which Heber (Eber) fathers Peleg, "for in his days was the earth divided" (Gen 10:24–25) — and the interpreters divide into three camps. Camp A — Hebrew primacy: Hebrew was the original tongue, kept through Babel in the line of Heber, whence the very name "Hebrew" (Josephus, the Pseudo-Clementine Recognitions, Eusebius, John Chrysostom, Jerome, Augustine, Isidore, Jacob of Edessa, Bede). Camp B — Syriac/Aramaic primacy: Aramaic was first, and Hebrew a later "mixed" tongue Abraham took up after crossing the Euphrates (Theodore of Mopsuestia, Theodoret of Cyrus). Camp C — no divinely-given tongue: God gave the faculty of speech, but the words themselves are human convention (Gregory of Nyssa). Two witnesses sit athwart the divide. Origen holds the Camp-A-adjacent premise of one "divine language" preserved through Babel by "the portion of the Lord … Jacob," yet never names it "Hebrew"; at the same time his name-realism — the Hebrew divine names have real, untranslatable power — is the sharpest foil to Camp C. Ephrem the Syrian affirms the shared premise of one primeval speech and one survivor at Babel, but never names that survivor, and the strong Syriac-primacy claim later hung on him is pseudo-Ephremic. Epiphanius of Salamis joins him as the clearest "agnostic" voice: he too takes mankind before Babel to be "of one speech and one language" yet declines to name the tongue, grounding true religion not in a sacred language but in a godliness older than the division of nations. The Hebrew-primacy reading is the earliest and, through Augustine and Isidore, the dominant one in the Latin West. The Syriac-primacy claim, for its part, had a long afterlife in the medieval Christian and Muslim East — spawning a counter-legend that inverted the Septuagint story and drawing a Karaite rebuttal in defence of Hebrew (see Reception, below).

Timeline

The chronological witnesses are being added, earliest first. Alongside the Camp A (Hebrew-primacy) line it carries the Camp B (Syriac/Aramaic-primacy) witnesses, Theodore of Mopsuestia and Theodoret of Cyrus, and — from within the Syriac world — the Camp A counter-witness Jacob of Edessa. John Chrysostom gives Camp A a second voice from inside Antioch, the Camp B heartland. Origen opens the line as a Camp-A-adjacent straddler (one "divine language," but unnamed) whose name-realism foils Camp C, and Ephrem the Syrian and Epiphanius of Salamis appear as the "agnostic" strand — both affirm one primeval speech but name no survivor. Gregory of Nyssa anchors Camp C, denying that any earthly tongue is a divine gift at all.

Josephus (c. 93)

Josephus supplies the Jewish backdrop and both proof-texts of the Hebrew-primacy case without yet drawing the doctrine. He derives the people's name from the patriarch Heber — "his son was Heber, from whom they originally called the Jews Hebrews" — and reads Peleg's name as marking "the dispersion of the nations."1 And he gives the fullest early narrative of Babel, where mankind, "of one language" and understanding one another before, was scattered by a God-sent "confusion of that language" — the city thence called Babylon, "for the Hebrews mean by the word Babel, confusion."2

Origen (c. 248)

Origen reads the primeval unity through Deuteronomy 32:8 (LXX): mankind once shared "one divine language," and "so long as they lived harmoniously together were preserved in the use of this divine language," until at Babel each nation was "handed over … to angels" who "imprinted on each his native language" and scattered them — while those who never left the east kept the first tongue and "alone became the portion of the Lord, and His people who were called Jacob."3 He does not name that surviving speech "Hebrew," but he sets it decisively apart, for he is also the sharpest patristic voice for name-realism: against Celsus he holds that the Hebrew divine names — "Sabaoth, and Adonai, and the other names treated with so much reverence among the Hebrews" — are "possessed of great power" and lose it in translation, "for it is not the things signified, but the qualities and peculiarities of words, which possess a certain power." Names are by nature, not mere convention — the direct foil to Camp C.4

The Pseudo-Clementine Recognitions (c. 3rd–4th c.)

The Pseudo-Clementine Recognitions gives the earliest flat statement of the developed thesis: "Until that time the Hebrew language, which had been given by God to men, bore sole sway" — Hebrew the one tongue of the race, divinely given, until the generations of idolatry and dispersion.5 The text survives in Rufinus's Latin (c. 406) of an earlier Greek romance, so it witnesses a widely-held tradition of the period rather than a named author.

Eusebius of Caesarea (c. 313–322)

Eusebius affirms the narrow supports of the reading — the antiquity of the Hebrews and the derivation of their name and tongue from Heber, "the man that passes over" — noting that "the very name which is the appellation of the whole race has been derived from Heber," and that Moses came later than "those former true Hebrews, Heber and Abraham, from whom the derived name has been applied to the people."6 He preserves the Babel account of the one clear speech confused into many, but he does not develop the strong thesis that Hebrew survived Babel in Heber's line — that step is Augustine's, and should not be read back into him.7

Ephrem the Syrian (c. 363)

Ephrem the Syrian is a caveat, not a Camp B proponent. The greatest Syriac father holds that mankind once shared "a single language" and that at Babel every nation lost that original speech "with one exception" — but in his authentic works he never names whether the survivor was Hebrew or Syriac.8 The emphatic Syriac-primacy claim popularly hung on his name is pseudo-Ephremic — it comes from the Cave of Treasures (6th–7th c.), falsely ascribed to him — so Ephrem is best read as a witness to the framework both camps share, not a partisan of either.9

Epiphanius of Salamis (c. 375)

Epiphanius of Salamis is the clearest agnostic witness — he affirms the premise both naming camps share, that before Babel mankind was "of one speech and one language," yet never names that primeval tongue. In the proem to his Panarion he frames the ages of the world as four "mother-sects" (Barbarism, Scythianism, Hellenism, Judaism) and holds that from Adam to the tower there was "one language, and one stock which had been planted on earth," a single humanity "of one speech and one language" with "no Judaism, no Hellenism, no other sect at all."10 His interest is that a proto-Christian "godliness" ran on "from the foundation of the world" — before the division of tongues and before Abraham, the first men being "neither Jew nor Greek" — so the primeval language is theologically incidental and stays unnamed. He knows the Camp A figure, calling Eber "the pious and godfearing," but draws no Hebrew-primacy claim from him.11

Gregory of Nyssa (c. 381)

Gregory of Nyssa anchors Camp C, which denies the premise the other two camps share — that any earthly tongue is the divinely-given first speech. Answering Eunomius (for whom names were divinely instituted, making Hebrew God's own language), Gregory holds that God gave only the faculty of speech, while "human language is the invention of the human mind or understanding"; even at Babel God is "not spoken of as creating men's languages, but as confounding the existing one."12 He drives the point home against Hebrew primacy directly: God is no Hebrew-speaker, and Moses "who lived many generations after the building of the tower, uses one of the subsequent languages" — Hebrew a later, Mosaic-era tongue, not the mother of all — while the many names for one thing (Hebrew Samaim, Roman coelum, and those of "the Syrian, the Mede …") show no language to be privileged.13

John Chrysostom (c. 388)

John Chrysostom holds the Camp A "Hebrew through Heber" line — and holds it from within Antioch, the very school that pressed the rival Syriac-primacy view. Preaching on Babel, he treats the one pre-Babel tongue as a privilege from God and its confusion as a measured chastisement of pride: the people "who had been dignified with similarity of language, used the privilege given them for evil purposes," so God "put a stop to the impulse of their wickedness through creating differences in language."14 He endorses the tradition that Heber alone kept his pre-confusion tongue, as "an enduring proof of the division of languages" — though this remark, in a passage whose modern translation is copyright-locked, is credited to his Babel homily but ⚠ unverified at first hand.15 (He does, in his homilies on Abraham, garble an etymology — taking "Abram" itself to mean "crossing," confusing the name with 'Eber — flagged on his detail page.)

Jerome (c. 393)

Jerome, the Church's great Hebraist, makes Hebrew the "mother of all languages" (omnium linguarum matrix) — the hinge of his lifelong appeal to the Hebraica veritas. In the Hebrew Questions on Genesis he reads the name of the Hebrews from Heber and glosses Peleg's name as the division of tongues: "Eber, from whom the Hebrews are descended, gave his son Peleg the name which means 'division', because in his days the languages were divided in Babylon."16 Hebrew is the primeval tongue from which the rest descend — a conviction of a piece with his whole textual programme, and one that keeps Aramaic ("Chaldee") a later relative of Hebrew, not its parent.17

Theodore of Mopsuestia (c. 400)

Theodore of Mopsuestia is the fountainhead of the rival Camp B, in which Aramaic, not Hebrew, is the first tongue. His Greek commentary on Genesis survives only in fragments; his reported reading, quoted a century later in Ibn al-Nadīm's Fihrist, is that "God … addressed Adam in the Nabataean dialect, which was purer than the Syriac one" — the speech of Babel that alone went unchanged at the confusion, while the villagers' Aramaic decayed into "a broken Syriac."18 On this account Hebrew is no primeval mother-tongue but a latecomer. The attribution is well-attested in his East-Syrian reception — Isho’dad of Merv, Theodore bar Koni — though it cannot be confirmed from a surviving autograph, so the strongest wording is credited to him but ⚠ unverified at first hand.19

Augustine of Hippo (c. 420)

Augustine is the anchor witness, welding the earlier pieces into the full thesis. In City of God 16.11 he holds that Hebrew was "the primitive language of the race," nameless while it was the only tongue; at the confusion "there was still the house of Heber in which the primitive language of the race survived," and "because … his family preserved that language … it was on this account thenceforth named Hebrew."20 He argues it, not merely asserts it: the confusion of tongues was a punishment, so "it is fit to ascribe to the people of God an immunity from this punishment" — and he weighs and rejects the rival derivation of "Hebrew" from Abraham, fixing name and language on Heber.21

Theodoret of Cyrus (c. 453)

Theodoret, a native Syrian bishop who read Syriac, gives Camp B its most developed patristic form. In the Questions on the Octateuch he holds Syriac (Aramaic) to be more ancient than Hebrew — Hebrew the sacred tongue, but not the oldest (§§60–61; the wording of the modern critical translation is not freely extractable, so this is credited but ⚠ unverified at first hand)22 — and he rejects the derivation of "Hebrew" from the patriarch Heber, since "the Hebrews were not the only descendants of Eber, and, therefore, could not appropriate his name," deriving it instead from ʿebrā / περάτης, "one who crosses over," i.e. Abraham "the Hebrew" who crossed the Euphrates (Gen 14:13).23

Isidore of Seville (c. 636)

Isidore gives the most systematic statement in the Latin tradition. In the Etymologies he states that before Babel "there was one language for all nations, which is called Hebrew," that Adam named the animals in Hebrew — "the language of all peoples" before the Flood — and that "the Hebrew language is the mother of all languages and letters."24 He also rebuts the Syriac/Chaldean rival directly: if Hebrew were merely Chaldean, "how is it that the Hebrew children in the Book of Daniel … are ordered to be taught the Chaldean language, which they do not know?"25

Jacob of Edessa (c. 700)

Jacob of Edessa is Camp A's most striking witness — a Syriac bishop defending Hebrew as the Adamic tongue against the Syriac/Aramaic-primacy view that dominated his own tradition. Answering his correspondent John the Stylite, he holds Hebrew to be the original language of all, "not Syriac/Aramaic, as many people erroneously suppose, even some great and illustrious ones," and the tongue "beloved of God" — kept, after Babel, by the line of Eber, who "alone continued to speak the original Adamic language of Hebrew."26 The same premise drives his Hexaemeron, where the Syriac word for "heaven" is a loanword from Hebrew, "the first, Adamic tongue" of "the Hebrews" — Syriac the borrower, Hebrew the prior.27 Nearly every other Syriac writer took the opposite side, which makes his a deliberate, minority stance from inside Camp B's heartland.

Bede (c. 720)

Bede carries the Augustinian reading into the medieval West. On Genesis 11:1 he takes as given the one primeval speech — "the whole earth had one language and the same words" — broken at Babel as the punishment of pride, and sets Babel ("Confusion") against Jerusalem and Pentecost, where the Spirit reverses the scattering by gathering the many tongues into "one unvarying confession."28 He assumes the single original tongue rather than arguing it, standing avowedly in the line downstream of Augustine and Isidore.

Reception — the Syriac-primacy claim in the medieval East

Camp B's Syriac/Aramaic-primacy thesis did not die with the Fathers. It had a vigorous afterlife in the Christian and Muslim East, where it hardened into popular legend, even inverted the Septuagint translation story, and finally drew a sharp philological rebuttal from a tenth-century Karaite scholar defending Hebrew. These are medieval receptions, not patristic witnesses, so they gather here rather than on the timeline above.

The Christian Syriac-primacy legends (the Fihrist)

Alongside its report of Theodore, the tenth-century Fihrist of Ibn al-Nadīm preserves a Christian legend of a heavenly origin for Syriac writing: "In one of the Gospels or some other Christian book, an angel called Saymūrus taught Adam the Syriac writing as it exists in the hands of the Christians of our own day."29 A script an angel teaches the first man to write can only be the first language — a vivid popular form of the Camp B claim, circulating among the Christians of Iraq in the very reception-stream that carried Theodore's primeval-Aramaic reading.

The Septuagint story inverted (Codex Scaliger 230)

A more polemical form turns the Septuagint legend on its head. A story copied in 1528 (Leiden, Codex Scaliger 230) and attributed to "Usbiyus" — apparently a garbled Eusebius — holds that the Torah "was originally sent down upon [Moses] in the Syriac tongue, the targum, and the seventy translated it into Hebrew in the tongue of His people and the language of His nation."30 Where the classic legend has seventy elders render the Hebrew Torah into Greek, this version makes Syriac the language of revelation and Hebrew a mere translation — denying Jewish primacy in both language and Scripture at a stroke. It is a Syriac-primacy variant of the translation legend weighed on the Septuagint's-origin and rabbinic-corruption pages; the wording is reported from the modern study of the legend, so it is credited but ⚠ unverified at first hand.

al-Mas‘ūdī reports both sides (c. 947)

The Muslim historian al-Mas‘ūdī, in his Murūj al-dhahab ("Meadows of Gold"), records the dispute without polemic: the Psalms were revealed to David in Hebrew, yet Syriac "is the original language, that of Adam, Noah, Abraham and other prophets, as is taught by the people of the books … It is said that the first to speak Hebrew was Abraham, the Friend of God, after he had left his village."31 That is Theodore of Mopsuestia's exact thesis — Hebrew as a post-Babel, post-"crossing" offshoot of Aramaic — surfacing in a tenth-century Arabic historian (credited from the modern study, ⚠ unverified at first hand).

The Karaite rebuttal — Ya‘qūb al-Qirqisānī (c. 937)

The claim drew a Jewish answer. The Karaite scholar Ya‘qūb al-Qirqisānī, in the introduction to the Pentateuch commentary of his Kitāb al-Anwār ("Book of Lights and Watchtowers"), reads the Christian Syriac-primacy claim as a face-saving move: "When it became clear to [the Christians] that alterations and changes had been introduced into the translations of our books, impudence led them to claim Syriac as the primeval language."32 He then argues at length, in a strikingly modern and philologically grounded way, for the primacy of Hebrew over Aramaic — a late, articulate Camp A voice answering Camp B on its own linguistic ground (credited from the modern study, ⚠ unverified at first hand).

Arguments

The sub-claims that cut across this timeline — chiefly whether the name and language "Hebrew" really descend from the patriarch Heber (the etymological hinge of the Hebrew-primacy case, set against the rival derivation from Aramaic), and whether the first tongue was a divine gift or a human convention — are weighed on their own pages.

  • The etymology of "Hebrew" — from the patriarch Heber, or from "crossing over"? — contested (the Heber derivation is grammatically possible and was the majority ancient reading, but was disputed already in antiquity and is doubted by modern philology, and either way it under-determines the primacy claim it is made to serve).
  • Was the primordial language a divine gift, or a human convention? — contested (the strong thesis that God authored a specific primeval tongue fails on the exegesis — Gen 11:7 has God confound an existing speech, not create one — which is Gregory of Nyssa's point; but the modest claim that at least the revealed divine names carry a God-given, non-arbitrary significance, which Origen presses, is unrefuted, so no consensus settles between them).
