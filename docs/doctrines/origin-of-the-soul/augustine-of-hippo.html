Augustine of Hippo on the origin of the soul

Dates: c. 415 · Wikipedia
Summary: Augustine is the great non-decider. He enumerated the options in his thirties, refused to choose between them at fifty-eight, sent Jerome a whole treatise begging to be told the answer at sixty-one, was still saying “I do not know” at sixty-five, and repeated it in his last years when he went back over his own books. What makes the indecision matter is that nobody wanted a decision more: the whole Pelagian controversy hung on how Adam's guilt reaches a newborn, and every account of the soul's origin he could think of made that either unexplained or unjust.

He stands seventh on this timeline, one entry after the man he asked and who never answered. His position is not a fourth option beside traducianism, creationism, and pre-existence. It is a set of constraints: whatever the answer is, it must leave the soul immaterial, must leave God guiltless of the sinner's sin, and must leave intact the church's practice of baptising infants in haste. He could not find a theory that met all three, and he would not pretend otherwise.1

The four opinions, and the decision not to decide

The list is Augustine's own, and he drew it up early. Writing On Free Will in the 390s he set out four ways a soul might come to be in a body, and then deliberately framed his argument so that it would stand whichever of them turned out to be true. When critics complained about the passage some twenty years later, he defended not his answer but his refusal to give one:

For whether all souls are derived by propagation from the first, or are in the case of each individual specially created, or being created apart from the body are sent into it, or introduce themselves into it of their own accord, without doubt this creature endowed with reason, namely, the human soul — appointed to occupy an inferior, that is, an earthly body — after the entrance of sin, does not govern its own body absolutely according to its free will.

— Letter 143 (to Marcellinus), §6, source. The care is visible in the sentence: he wrote “after the entrance of sin,” not “after he sinned,” precisely so that the clause would hold whether the sin in question was the man's own or the first parent's. He says so explicitly, and then names what forced the manoeuvre:

… the obscurity of this most mysterious subject, the origin of the soul, compels me to do as I have done; let them rather stretch out a friendly hand to me, confessing my ignorance, and desiring to know whatever is the truth on the subject…

— Letter 143, §7, source. The same letter contains the sharpest one-line statement of why the question is hard, and it is a statement about evidence rather than about metaphysics. Expounding Ecclesiastes 12:7 — the dust returns to the earth, the spirit to God who gave it — he notes that a traducianist can read the verse as an exact parallel, the spirit going back to its source as the body goes back to its source, and that the parallel breaks at just one point:

… for there is no difference of opinion as to the original stock of the body, but there is as to the original stock of the soul.

— Letter 143, §8, source. That is the whole difficulty in a clause. Everyone agrees the body descends; the verse's symmetry therefore proves nothing about the soul unless you have already decided the point at issue. Augustine notices this against his own convenience — the reading he is dismantling is the one that would have made the verse favour the theory he found most useful.2

A fifth possibility — that the soul is a piece of God's own substance — he left off the list on purpose, and explained why when Jerome's taxonomy included it. It is not a theory of how the soul gets into a body at all but a theory of what the soul is, and it was the position of the Manichees he was arguing against; he had answered it separately by showing that a fragment of an immutable God could not be corrupted. Setting it aside, the question he wanted settled was narrow and precise: “I desire to know which of the other four opinions we ought to choose.”3

The obstacle: an infant snatched away by death

Augustine's interest in this question is entirely downstream of another one. He believed — and against the Pelagians insisted — that an infant dying without baptism is not saved, and that what condemns it is guilt derived from Adam. Traducianism explains that instantly: the soul is Adam's soul, and inherits Adam's condition along with the flesh. Creationism does not, and it is the failure to explain it, not any positive argument, that kept him from adopting the view he plainly wished were true:

I ask where can the soul, even of an infant snatched away by death, have contracted the guilt which, unless the grace of Christ has come to the rescue by that sacrament of baptism which is administered even to infants, involves it in condemnation?

— Letter 166 (to Jerome), §6, source. He puts the difficulty twice more in the same letter, each time tightening it. If the newly made soul does not sin, “how is it compatible with the justice of the Creator, that, because of their being united to mortal members derived from another, they are so brought under the bond of the sin of that other, that unless they be rescued by the Church, perdition overtakes them”? And if it does sin, when? The dilemma is stated without any softening at the end:

For if new souls are made for men, individually, at their birth, I do not see, on the one hand, that they could have any sin while yet in infancy, nor do I believe, on the other hand, that God condemns any soul which He sees to have no sin.

— Letter 166, §21, source. Two things about this argument deserve marking. It is conditional: it does not show creationism false, only that creationism plus inherited condemnation plus divine justice cannot all three stand without some further account of how the guilt is contracted. And it is aimed at a doctrine of infant damnation that Augustine holds more firmly than almost anyone else in the tradition — so the difficulty is one he manufactures for himself, and cannot escape by loosening. The standard scholastic reply, that the soul contracts Adam's guilt by being joined to flesh descended from Adam, is exactly the answer he considers at §22 and rejects as an evasion he has “never heard or read anywhere.” Whether the reply works is weighed on its own page (assessment: contested).4

He is candid, too, that this is not the only place creationism strains. Infants suffer horribly in this life before they can have done anything; and some souls arrive with so little capacity that their owners “cannot learn even the letters of the alphabet.” If the soul is made to order for the body, someone chose these pairings. Augustine had once answered the suffering-of-infants version of the problem in On Free Will, by appeal to hidden recompense; in Letter 166 he quotes his own earlier answer at length and then withdraws it, because it cannot reach the infants who die unbaptised, for whom there is no recompense to come. Watching a man demolish his own published solution in front of the correspondent he is asking for help is the most attractive thing on the page.5

The consultation of 415

In 415 a young Spanish priest, Orosius, arrived at Hippo, having crossed from the far western coast to ask Augustine questions Augustine could not answer. Augustine sent him on to Bethlehem, and used him as a courier. What Orosius carried was not a note but a book: Letter 166, headed in the manuscripts A Treatise on the Origin of the Human Soul, Addressed to Jerome, together with a second long letter on the meaning of James 2:10. The tone of the opening is a bishop of fifty years putting himself to school with an older man:

Allow me, therefore, to bring before you a subject which I beseech you not to refuse to open up and discuss with me. Many are perplexed by questions concerning the soul, and I confess that I myself am of this number.

— Letter 166, §3, source. He knows what Jerome holds and says so, referring to the letter Jerome had sent Marcellinus with John 5:17 attached — the letter in which Jerome had told Marcellinus that Augustine, being conveniently in Africa, could explain Jerome's opinion viva voce. Augustine's comment on that referral is very dry: had he been able to do it, Marcellinus “would not have applied for instruction to you, who were so remote from him.” And then, with no hedging at all, he offers to be convinced:

Behold, I am willing that the opinion which you hold should be also mine; but I assure you that as yet I have not embraced it.

— Letter 166, §8, source. The offer is repeated at the close in a formula that is half concession and half ultimatum — and the second half is the more serious: “as to that opinion of yours concerning the creation of new souls, if it does not contradict this firmly grounded article of faith, let it be mine also; but if it does, let it be no longer yours.” He even lists in advance the proof-texts he does not want back — Zechariah 12:1, the psalm about hearts formed severally, Ecclesiastes 12:7 — explaining in each case why it fails to decide the question, “to prevent you from endeavouring to deliver me from my perplexities by quoting passages such as these.” Since those are precisely the verses Jerome had used, the letter is a request for help with the creationist's own artillery struck from the table.

Jerome answered neither letter on its merits. When he replied at all it was to say that the times were too hard for controversy and that he was resolved to defend Augustine's opinions as his own. The consultation, and the whole long correspondence it belongs to, is narrated on the Jerome–Augustine timeline; Jerome's side of this particular silence is on his page here. A generation later a Marseilles cataloguer, listing the notable Christian writers, still identified Orosius chiefly by this errand: he is “the Orosius who, sent by Augustine to Hieronymus to teach the nature of the soul,” came back with the relics of Stephen.6

Why he could not simply be a traducianist either

If creationism could not explain inherited guilt, why not take the theory that could? Because Augustine held the soul to be immaterial and thought Tertullian had bought his mechanism at that price. He states the commitment in the same letter, at length and with a worked example — the whole mind is present at every point of the body at once, which no extended thing can be — and takes trouble to head off a merely verbal dispute about the word:

That the soul is immaterial is a fact of which I avow myself to be fully persuaded, although men of slow understanding are hard to be convinced that it is so.

— Letter 166, §4, source. Anything that can be transmitted with the seed looks like something that has parts and place, and that is the one property he will not concede. So each option is blocked by a different commitment: creationism by justice, traducianism by immateriality, pre-existence by the doctrine of sin (he rejects it flatly — “I do not believe: I reject and protest against such an opinion” — on the ground that if a soul can sin before entering a body there is nothing to stop it sinning in Abraham's bosom after leaving one), and the fifth by the nature of God. The result is not scepticism but a set of walls.

Whether he ever found a way through is the one point on this page that cannot be settled from any freely available English text. He is reported to have floated, in a late letter to Optatus, a spiritual generationism — one soul kindled from another as one flame is kindled from another without the first being diminished — which would give traducianism's explanatory power without a material transmission. The report is a nineteenth-century scholar's summary of that letter, repeated in a standard history; it is offered here as a reported reading, not as something checked against the letter itself. It is at any rate not a settled position: the same summary concludes that Augustine “does not get over his indecision,” and the Retractations, written after the letter to Optatus, say the same in his own words.7

The terms of surrender (419–420)

Four years after the consultation a young enthusiast named Vincentius Victor published a confident creationist account and criticised Augustine's hesitation as ignorance unworthy of him. Augustine's two-book reply is where his position is stated most completely, and it begins by conceding the logic Victor had tried to escape. Victor wanted the soul to come neither from the parents nor from nothing, but somehow “from God”; Augustine closes the gap:

For it is one of two things: if the soul is not derived by natural descent from the parent, it comes out of nothing. To pretend that it is derived from God in such wise as to be a portion of His nature, is simply sacrilegious blasphemy. But we solicit and seek up to the present time some plain passages of Scripture bearing on the point, whether the soul does not come by parental descent…

— On the Soul and Its Origin I.24, source. The disjunction is exact and it is not friendly to either side: creationism means creation ex nihilo for every human being who has ever lived, and the only thing that could establish it is a text, which after thirty years of looking he has not found.8

Then comes the passage that is the best short statement of his whole stance. Victor had quoted a psalm at him — man being in honour understands not, and is compared to the senseless cattle. Augustine answers with the mother of the Maccabean martyrs, who told her sons she could not tell how they came into her womb:

Wherefore, I too, on my side, say concerning my soul, I have no certain knowledge how it came into my body; for it was not I who gave it to myself. He who gave it to me knows whether He imparted it to me from my father, or created it afresh for me, as He did for the first man. But even I shall know, when He Himself shall teach me, in His own good time. Now, however, I do not know; nor am I ashamed, like him, to confess my ignorance of what I know not.

— On the Soul and Its Origin I.25, source. The rhetorical move is worth noting on its own account: an accusation of brutish ignorance is answered by producing a woman whom Scripture honours and who confessed the same ignorance about the same thing.9

What he will not do is block the road. Late in the first book he states the terms on which the creationist case would be welcome, and they are terms about evidence and about who is making the case, not about the conclusion:

As for the opinion, that new souls are created by inbreathing without being propagated, we certainly do not in the least object to its maintenance — only let it be by persons who have succeeded in discovering some new evidence, either in the canonical Scriptures, in the shape of unambiguous testimony towards the solution of a most knotty question, or else in their own reasonings, such as shall not be opposed to catholic truth, but not by such persons as this man has shown himself to be.

— On the Soul and Its Origin I.33, source. The chapter that follows is the crux of the position, and is the nearest thing to a ruling Augustine ever issued on this question. He does not forbid creationism; he fences it, listing the four things a creationist may not say:

All, therefore, who wish to maintain that new souls are rightly said to be breathed into persons at their birth, and not derived from their parents, must by all means be cautious on each of the four points which I have already mentioned. That is to say, do not let them affirm that souls become sinful by another's original sin; do not let them affirm that infants who died unbaptized can possibly reach eternal life and the kingdom of heaven by the remission of original sin in any other way whatever; do not let them affirm that souls had sinned in some other place previous to their incarnation, and that on this account they were forcibly introduced into sinful flesh; nor let them affirm that the sins which were not actually found in them were, because they were foreknown, deservedly punished, although they were never permitted to reach that life where they could be committed.

— On the Soul and Its Origin I.34, source. Read that list against the difficulty of Letter 166 and the shape of his mind is plain. Each of the four bans closes an escape route from the infant problem: you may not say the fresh soul catches Adam's sin by contagion, you may not save the unbaptised, you may not fall back on pre-existent guilt, and you may not punish foreseen sins never committed. What remains for the creationist is to explain the condemnation of an innocent newly created soul without using any of the four available explanations. Augustine's closing sentence tells the reader what he expects to happen: if no decisive authority can be found, “it is, indeed, better for a man to confess his ignorance of what he knows nothing about.”10

The last word

He never took it back. Going through his own books near the end of his life, he came to On Free Will and the four opinions, and wrote of the soul that “as to its origin… I did not know then, and I still do not know.” The principle behind the confession he had stated years earlier, in the treatise he sent to that same Marcellinus who had written to Jerome: on an obscure subject where no clear scriptural proof can be had, “the presumption of man ought to restrain itself; nor should it attempt anything definite by leaning to either side.” And there is a corollary he draws which is the real reason he is content to stop — that if a point were necessary to salvation, Scripture would have made it plain.11

The irony of what followed is worth stating plainly. The Latin West settled on creationism within two generations — Cassian assuming it in passing by the 430s, Gennadius printing it as a canon by 470, a bishop of Rome writing against its rival in 498 — and it did so without ever supplying the thing Augustine had asked for. No new proof-text was found; the argument he could not answer was not answered but outlived. And the handbook that did most to fix the creationist rule circulated for centuries under Augustine's own name, so that the tradition could read a flat verdict and the most famous confession of ignorance in Latin theology as the work of one man.12

Notes

  • Augustine's indecision is often described as a failure of nerve or a gap in an otherwise complete system. It is better read as a consequence of how much else he had decided. A theologian with a looser doctrine of original sin, or a less absolute view of the fate of unbaptised infants, has no difficulty here at all; the problem is generated by his own commitments, and he declines to relieve it by weakening any of them. His treatment on the infant-baptism timeline is the other half of this page.
  • Open tension: he insists in Letter 166 §4 that the soul is immaterial, and in On the Soul and Its Origin I.24 that if it is not propagated it comes out of nothing. Put together, these should press him hard toward the creationist side — an immaterial thing is exactly what cannot be handed down with seed, which is Aquinas's argument eight centuries later. Augustine never runs the inference, because for him the metaphysical point does not outweigh the moral one about the infant.
  • The reported spiritual generationism of Letter 190 to Optatus — soul kindled from soul as flame from flame — would be the obvious way out, and is repeated in the handbooks as evidence that Augustine leaned traducian late. Nothing on this page rests on it; it is not something that can be checked here against a freely available English text of that letter, and even the summary that reports it concludes that he stayed undecided.
  • Augustine is one of the very few ancient witnesses on this timeline who states an argument against the view he wants to hold. Jerome states his own view three times and the rival only as a caricature; Tertullian presses his own case and does not raise the objection about incorporeality that would sink it. Compare Rufinus, who also refused to choose — but who refused because he had not made up his mind, where Augustine refused because he had, on three separate points, and they would not fit together.
  • Augustine appears across this wiki on many timelines, among them the Nephilim, the intermediate state, and infant baptism; his side of the correspondence with Jerome is on its own page. For the rest of the witnesses here see the summary timeline.
