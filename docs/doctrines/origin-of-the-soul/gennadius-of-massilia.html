Gennadius of Massilia on the origin of the soul

Dates: c. 470 · Wikipedia
Summary: Gennadius is where the question stops being open. His handbook of church dogmas devotes a chapter to the soul's origin, and it does not weigh opinions: it names Origen's pre-existence and the propagation of souls with the body as errors, in a single sentence, and states creationism as what “we say.” The soul is created and infused once the body is already formed. Much of the chapter's later authority was an accident of transmission — the book circulated for centuries under Augustine's name, so the Latin West could read a flat creationist canon and Augustine's permanent confession of ignorance as though both were the same man's.

He stands ninth on this timeline, and he is the hinge. Everything before him on the Latin side is an opinion advanced by a named writer — Tertullian arguing, Jerome asserting, Rufinus declining, Augustine begging for help, Cassian assuming. Gennadius writes it down as a rule with the alternatives labelled. Nothing had been decided in the interval; a genre had changed.1

The chapter

There is no published English translation of the De ecclesiasticis dogmatibus. The chapter is given here in Latin, with a rendering in the prose below that is our own from that text:

Animas hominum non esse ab initio inter caeteras intellectuales naturas, nec simul creatas, sicut Origenes fingit; neque cum corporibus per coitum seminatas, sicut Luciferiani, Cyrillus et aliqui Latinorum praesumptores affirmant, quasi naturae consequentiam servantes. Sed dicimus creationem animae solum Creatorem omnium nosse, et corpus tantum per conjugii copulam seminari, Dei vero judicio coagulari in vulva et compingi atque formari, ac formato jam corpore animam creari et infundi, ut vivat in utero homo ex anima constans et corpore, et egrediatur vivus ex utero plenus humana substantia.

— De ecclesiasticis dogmatibus, ch. xiv, Latin text. That the souls of men were not from the beginning among the other intellectual natures, nor created all together, as Origen imagines; nor sown with the bodies through intercourse, as the Luciferians, Cyril, and certain presumptuous Latins affirm, as though they were preserving the consistency of nature. But we say that the Creator of all alone knows the creation of the soul; that the body only is sown through the union of marriage, but by God's judgment is coagulated in the womb and compacted and formed; and that, the body being now formed, the soul is created and infused, so that in the womb a man consisting of soul and body lives, and comes forth from the womb alive, full of human substance.

Four things are being done at once, and it is worth separating them. The first is a condemnation of Origen by name. The second is a condemnation of traducianism, with a list of holders. The third is a concession that looks like modesty and functions as a wall: creationem animae solum Creatorem omnium nosse — only the Creator knows how a soul is created. The fourth is a positive chronology, and it is the part with the most content: body first, formed by God's judgment in the womb, and then the soul created and infused into it. That last clause is not a small thing. It abandons the simultaneity that both Tertullian and Gregory of Nyssa had insisted on from opposite ends of the argument — Gregory's whole objection to the Genesis literalists was that a man must not be “antecedent and posterior to himself” — and buys the creationist mechanism at the price of a formed body that is not yet a living man. Gennadius does not appear to notice the cost.2

Four chapters later he uses the doctrine as a premise, which is the surest sign of how settled he takes it to be. Arguing that the soul does not die with the body, he gives as his reason that it was not sown with the body:

Anima humana non cum carne moritur, quia non cum carne, ut superius diximus, seminatur; sed formato in ventre matris corpore, Dei judicio creatur et infunditur, ut vivat homo intus in utero, et sic procedat nativitate in mundum.

— De ecclesiasticis dogmatibus, ch. xviii, Latin text: the human soul does not die with the flesh, because it is not sown with the flesh, as we said above; but, the body having been formed in the mother's womb, by God's judgment it is created and infused, so that the man may live within in the womb and so proceed by birth into the world. This is Jerome's taunt turned into an inference. Jerome had said that a propagated soul would share the condition of the beasts, which perish with the body, and had not shown why; Gennadius simply treats non-propagation as the ground of immortality. The step is no better argued here than there — Tertullian's position had been precisely that a soul can be handed down and still be immortal — but it now appears in a list of things the church holds rather than in a polemic.3

The list of traducianists

“Luciferiani, Cyrillus et aliqui Latinorum praesumptores” is an odd company, and the attribution should be flagged rather than repeated. The Luciferians were the rigorist party descended from Lucifer of Cagliari, whose quarrel with the rest of the church was about the reception of former Arians and not about the soul at all. Which Cyril is meant is not stated, and neither of the two famous bearers of the name — of Jerusalem, of Alexandria — is elsewhere reported as a traducianist. The third item is a phrase, not a list.

Compared with the other ancient rosters this one is both more confident and less checkable. Rufinus had named “Tertullian or Lactantius among the Latins” — and on Lactantius's own text he was simply wrong. Jerome had named “Tertullian, Apollinaris, and the majority of the Western divines,” which the standard modern reference account judges exaggerated, since no other writer of prominence can be found advocating the position as certain. Gennadius names three parties, of which one is a sect known for something else, one is a bare name, and one is an insult. The pattern across all three lists is the same: everyone knows traducianism is out there, and nobody can produce more than Tertullian holding it. That is worth keeping in view when reading the chapter, because a canon which condemns an error that has no identifiable living advocates is doing something other than settling a live dispute — and a bishop of Rome writing to Gaul against traducians a generation later suggests there was something to condemn, just not anything whose spokesmen we can name.4

How a handbook became a rule

The treatise's influence had less to do with its author than with whose works it was bound among. It circulated widely as a pseudo-Augustinian piece, printed among Augustine's writings as well as under Gennadius's name, and is only in modern scholarship “now universally attributed to Gennadius.” The consequence for this question is peculiar and large. The Latin Middle Ages could open Augustine and find, in his own voice, the most sustained confession of ignorance about the soul's origin in patristic literature — and, a few volumes later, a flat statement that the church teaches the soul is created and infused into an already-formed body, with the rival named as an error. Nothing in the presentation told a reader that the second text was not the first author's.

It is cited as a standing authority by both of the men who closed the Latin question. Aquinas puts it in the sed contra of the article where he declares traducianism heretical, and it supplies the one clause of the chapter that exists in published English:

On the contrary, It is written in De Eccl. Dogmat. xiv that “the rational soul is not engendered by coition.”

— Thomas Aquinas, Summa Theologiae I q.118 a.2, source. A century earlier Peter Lombard had already settled the matter for the schools at the corresponding place in the Sentences — the chapter on Eve's soul, which he heads with the reason rather than the topic: “De anima mulieris, quae non ex anima viri, quia animae non sunt ex traduce” (on the woman's soul, which is not from the man's soul, because souls are not by propagation). That is the exact case Tertullian had made his strongest, and it is now the heading under which the schools teach its denial. From the Sentences the position passed into every medieval theology course.5

What the canon settles, and what it does not

Gennadius states creationism; he does not defend it. There is no proof-text in the chapter — not John 5:17, not Hebrews 12:9, not Ecclesiastes 12:7 — and no argument, only an assertion, a list of errors, and a chronology of the womb. Set beside the difficulty that had stopped Augustine, this is conspicuous. If the soul is created and infused into a body already formed, then it is created innocent and joined to flesh under condemnation; that is precisely the configuration Augustine said he could not reconcile with the justice of God and the church's practice of hastening to baptise, and precisely the configuration whose four available escape routes he had closed by name. Gennadius's chapter does not answer the objection. It does not mention it.

There is reason to think the omission is not accidental. He was a Marseilles priest of the Semipelagian persuasion, and the standard reference account of him finds the whole treatise “full of Semipelagianism, either open or implied,” with “original sin carefully evaded.” A writer who evades original sin does not feel the pressure that made this question urgent for Augustine, and can hold creationism cheaply. His own notice on Augustine, in the catalogue of Christian writers he is chiefly remembered for, praises the great books and then adds a clause about the fate of the unborn that is variously transmitted and reads, in the English version, like faint praise: on the resurrection of the dead, Augustine “wrote with equal sincerity, and left it to the less able to raise doubts respecting abortions.” However the clause is construed, the subject Gennadius chooses to qualify his praise with is the one on which Augustine had confessed himself beaten.6

What the chapter does achieve is real, and it is the reason it belongs on this timeline. After it, a Latin writer who wanted to hold the propagation of souls had to hold it against a text that said the church did not; before it, he had only to hold it against Jerome. That is not a demonstration and it is not a conciliar definition — no council ever defined the point, and the standard account concedes that “there are no strict definitions condemning Generationism as heretical.” It is the moment a disputed question was written down as a settled one.7

Notes

  • The chapter numbering used here is Migne's, which is what Aquinas and the standard citations follow. The critical edition — C. H. Turner, “The Liber Ecclesiasticorum Dogmatum,” Journal of Theological Studies 7 (1905–6), 78–99 — numbers the chapters differently, so a reference to “chapter xiv” in older and newer literature may not point to the same text.
  • Open question: how much of the treatise's authority in the Middle Ages was borrowed from the misattribution, and how much it would have had on its own. The two cannot be separated cleanly, since the writers who cite it are citing whatever their copies said. What can be said is that the misattribution made a particular confusion possible — a reader could take Augustine to have both refused to decide the question and decided it.
  • The date given here, c. 470, is the conventional placing of the treatise and is approximate. Gennadius was still writing in the early 490s (his catalogue of Christian writers mentions Pope Gelasius, who reigned 492–496) and died about 496; whether the De ecclesiasticis dogmatibus is early or late in his career is bound up with the unsettled question of which of his lost works it is a part of.
  • Gennadius supplies incidental testimony used elsewhere on this timeline: his catalogue identifies Orosius as the man “sent by Augustine to Hieronymus to teach the nature of the soul,” which is the 415 consultation seen from seventy years later, and it lists Cassian's conferences by subject, including the one that carries Cassian's creationism. He is a good witness to the fifth century even where he is a poor arbiter of it.
  • For the rest of the witnesses see the summary timeline; the two arguments that cut across it are the John 5:17 proof-text (assessment: weak) and the infant-damnation difficulty (assessment: contested).
