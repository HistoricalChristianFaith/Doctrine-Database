Didymus the Blind on the origin of the soul

Dates: c. 380 (Didymus the Blind, c. 313–398, blind from early childhood and for some half-century head of the catechetical school of Alexandria — a celebrated teacher who mastered Scripture by ear, and under whom Rufinus, Palladius, and, by his own report, Jerome sat) · Wikipedia
Summary: Didymus is the one major fourth-century witness who keeps Origen's third option — the pre-existence of souls — alive at Alexandria itself, a century and a half before it was anathematized. Reading God's question in the garden, “Adam, where are you?”, he takes the soul to have been “free from every place” in its incorporeal state and to have “come into a place” only when it attached itself to a body; and his pupil Jerome, turned prosecutor, reports a treatise in which Didymus held that the pre-existent souls “had not sinned much” and so were only lightly punished by being lodged in bodies. What he taught was Origen's version and not a cruder one: not a pre-existence of bare souls but of rational creatures who once wore subtler bodies and fell into these heavy, corruptible ones. He died in the Church's peace and honour; his teaching on the soul's origin was condemned only long after his death.

Claims & evidence

The soul was “free from every place” and “came into a place” only in the body

In the Commentary on Genesis — one of the works recovered in 1941 among the Tura papyri, and so a securely genuine text — Didymus glosses the Lord's question to the fallen Adam, “Where are you?” (Genesis 3:9), by turning it from a question about a body in a garden into a question about a soul in a condition. The soul that has fallen is now somewhere; before, being incorporeal, it was nowhere in particular:

You are in a place, though you were free from every place through incorporeality; since you did not preserve it and you have become attached to the body, you have come into a place.

— Didymus the Blind, Commentary on Genesis 91 (on Genesis 3:9), in Daria Szymańska-Kuta's translation of the Greek.1 The frame is Origen's exactly: a prior condition “beyond every place,” a lapse of watchfulness, and a descent into embodiment and so into location. One honest qualification belongs on the passage. Didymus introduces the sharpest form of the scheme — that the soul's “first substrate” was an incorporeal substance “beyond every place” — as the opinion of unnamed “some authors,” and does not there state in his own voice whether he holds it; the reported view and his own exegesis nonetheless move on the same pre-existence track, and elsewhere he makes the descent his own.

Jerome's report: the pre-existent souls “had not sinned much”

The plainest attestation that this was Didymus's own teaching, and not merely a view he reported, comes from a hostile but exceptionally well-informed witness: Jerome, who had studied under him and called him “my seer” before the Origenist controversy turned admiration to prosecution. In the Apology against Rufinus Jerome sorts Didymus with care — sound where Origen was unsound, unsound only where he trusted Origen:

As to Didymus, however, we extol both his powers of memory, and the purity of his faith in the Trinity, while on the other point in which he erred in trusting to Origen we withdraw from him.

And he names the very treatise, addressed to Rufinus, in which Didymus had set out the point at issue — why souls are lodged in bodies at all:

There exists a treatise of Didymus addressed to you, in which he meets this inquiry of yours, with the answer, that they had not sinned much, and therefore it was enough punishment for them just to have touched their bodily prisons.

— Jerome, Apology against Rufinus III.27–28.2 That is Origen's theodicy of the graded cosmos in miniature: the body is a penalty proportioned to a fault committed before birth, and a light fault earns a light one. Jerome is a partisan in a quarrel and the report should be weighed as such — but he had heard Didymus in person, names the book and its dedicatee, and his summary agrees with what the recovered commentaries show.

Origen's version, not a cruder one

What Didymus held is best stated with the precision modern scholarship has restored to it. He did not teach a pre-existence of bare, bodiless souls — the caricature the anathemas would later fix — but, like Origen, the prior existence of rational creatures already clothed in subtle, incorruptible bodies, which the fall coarsened into the heavy and mortal ones we now wear. He read the “garments of skin” God made for Adam and Eve (Genesis 3:21) as those present corruptible bodies, put on after the fall — the same exegesis, and the same distinction between two kinds of body, that Origen had drawn. Ilaria Ramelli, surveying the Tura commentaries, judges that Didymus “supported the so-called ‘preexistence of souls,’” yet “exactly like Origen, did not conceive of a preexistence of bare souls proper, but of rational creatures endowed with bodies that were not corruptible.”3 So the doctrine that put his name, three centuries on, in a list of anathematized errors is not the tale of naked souls falling out of heaven that the polemic supposed, but a carefully qualified reading of Genesis in the school Origen had built.

Notes

  • Between Origen and the anathema. On this timeline Didymus stands where the pre-existence view had almost no other fourth-century champion of standing: Origen proposed it as one option among three around 253; Gregory of Nyssa, his near-contemporary, rejected it as sliding into the transmigration tales; and Justinian anathematized it in 543. Didymus is the witness in between — the head of Origen's own school still teaching the master's third option at the height of the fourth century.
  • Died honoured; condemned only after death. Didymus was never tried or censured in his lifetime. Jerome, writing about 393, still lists his commentaries with admiration and notes that he “is still living, and has already passed his eighty-third year”; he died about 398 in the Church's communion. His name was joined to Origen's and Evagrius's only posthumously — reported by Cyril of Scythopolis among those the fathers of 553 condemned, then fixed by name in the definitions of Constantinople III (680–681) and Nicaea II (787), which anathematize “the mythical speculations of Origen, Evagrius and Didymus.” He is nonetheless still carried as “St. Didymus the Blind” on 18 October in the Orthodox Prologue of Ohrid.4 That an Alexandrian who held the very doctrine anathematized here died in honour, and remains a saint on one calendar, is the sharpest case on the wiki's comparison of who else held the condemned views and what became of them.
  • The recovered text. Almost nothing of Didymus's exegesis was known at first hand until 1941, when a cache of papyrus codices was found in a quarry at Tura, south of Cairo, preserving his commentaries on Genesis, Job, Zechariah, and the Psalms — the base on which his teaching can now be read rather than merely reported. That the whole ancient record of Origenist thought reaches us mostly through the excerpts of hostile compilers, and how far a condemned author can be judged fairly on such a transmission, is weighed on the transmission page.
  • Elsewhere on the wiki. Didymus's teaching on the other doctrine he was condemned for — the final restoration of all rational creatures — is set out on the apokatastasis timeline; and he appears as well as a witness to Mary's perpetual virginity and on the woman-taken-in-adultery pericope.
