Origen of Alexandria on the origin of the soul

Dates: c. 253 · Wikipedia
Summary: Origen supplies the third answer — that souls existed before their bodies — and he is the one witness on this question who says out loud that the church has not decided it. In De Principiis he notes that neither traducianism nor any rival is taught “with sufficient clearness” in the rule of faith, and into that acknowledged gap he puts a theory: rational minds were made equal, freedom made them unequal, and a mind that cooled from the divine warmth became a soul and was assigned a body proportioned to what it had become. He twice marks the whole discussion as exploratory. Neither the hedges nor the argument's motive — defending God's justice against the visible unfairness of human lots — survived into the condemnation three centuries later.

Two cautions belong at the top rather than in a footnote, because they govern how everything below should be read. First, the Greek of De Principiis is largely lost; what is quoted here is the Latin of Rufinus of Aquileia, who admitted to editing as he translated and whose fidelity Jerome attacked at length. Second, this is Origen's most speculative work, and he says so inside it. Both points are taken up in their own sections below. Origen stands second on this timeline, a generation after Tertullian, and the two are answering the same question from opposite ends.1

The church has not decided this

The preface to De Principiis is a survey of what the apostolic preaching has fixed and what it has left open, and the origin of the soul is placed firmly in the second category:

But with respect to the soul, whether it is derived from the seed by a process of traducianism, so that the reason or substance of it may be considered as placed in the seminal particles of the body themselves, or whether it has any other beginning; and this beginning, itself, whether it be by birth or not, or whether bestowed upon the body from without or no, is not distinguished with sufficient clearness in the teaching of the Church.

— De Principiis, Preface §5, source. Two things are already fixed in the same paragraph: the soul has “a substance and life of its own” and will be judged after death, and “every rational soul is possessed of free-will and volition.” What is not fixed is where it came from. The judgment is not a rhetorical concession; it is accurate for the year it was written, and it is what makes the whole later history intelligible. A question the rule of faith had not settled was open to speculation, and Origen speculated in it — as would Rufinus, who nearly two centuries later still refused to decide among the three options, and Augustine, who died undecided.2

Made equal, made unequal by their own freedom

The theory begins from a difficulty about God, not about souls. If God is good and just and creates without constraint, He had no reason to make some creatures better than others; yet the world is manifestly a place of unequal endowments and unequal lots. Origen's answer is that the inequality is not original but earned:

He created all whom He made equal and alike, because there was in Himself no reason for producing variety and diversity. But since those rational creatures themselves, as we have frequently shown, and will yet show in the proper place, were endowed with the power of free-will, this freedom of will incited each one either to progress by imitation of God, or reduced him to failure through negligence. And this, as we have already stated, is the cause of the diversity among rational creatures, deriving its origin not from the will or judgment of the Creator, but from the freedom of the individual will.

— De Principiis II.ix.6, source. The conclusion he draws is a theodicy: “the Creator will neither appear to be unjust in distributing… to every one according to his merits; nor will the happiness or unhappiness of each one's birth, or whatever be the condition that falls to his lot, be deemed accidental.” Everything in the doctrine of pre-existence is in the service of that sentence. It is worth stating plainly, because the later polemic against Origen treats pre-existence as a piece of pagan mythology smuggled into the church, and it is in fact an attempt to say why an innocent is born blind.3

Jacob in the womb: the proof-text

Origen does not pretend that Scripture teaches pre-existence, but he does think it presses the question. Paul's handling of Jacob and Esau — chosen before birth, “neither having done any good or evil” — raises the charge of injustice and then dismisses it without explaining how the injustice is avoided. Origen supplies the missing step:

As, therefore, when the Scriptures are carefully examined regarding Jacob and Esau, it is not found to be unrighteousness with God that it should be said, before they were born, or had done anything in this life, the elder shall serve the younger; and as it is found not to be unrighteousness that even in the womb Jacob supplanted his brother, if we feel that he was worthily beloved by God, according to the deserts of his previous life, so as to deserve to be preferred before his brother…

— De Principiis II.ix.7, source. The move is conditional (“if we feel that…”) and it is an inference to the best explanation, not an exegesis: the text says the choice preceded any deed, and Origen adds a prior life in which deeds could be done. That is precisely the weakness a critic will fasten on — Paul's own answer to “is there unrighteousness with God?” is that the purpose of election stands “not of works, but of Him who calls,” which is the opposite of a doctrine of pre-natal merit. The strength of the reading is that it takes the difficulty seriously; its weakness is that it resolves the difficulty by contradicting the clause Paul uses to resolve it.4

The soul as a mind grown cold

The mechanism by which a pre-existent mind becomes an embodied soul is carried by an etymology. Origen assembles the scriptural language in which God is a fire, angels a flame, and the saints fervent, against the language in which sinners' love “waxes cold” — and then proposes that the very word soul records a fall in temperature:

… we have to inquire whether perhaps the name soul, which in Greek is termed ψυχή, be so termed from growing cold out of a better and more divine condition, and be thence derived, because it seems to have cooled from that natural and divine warmth, and therefore has been placed in its present position, and called by its present name.

From all which this appears to be made out, that the understanding, falling away from its status and dignity, was made or named soul; and that, if repaired and corrected, it returns to the condition of the understanding.

— De Principiis II.viii.3, source. The etymology is not Origen's invention and is not good philology; it is the same folk-derivation of psychē from cooling that Tertullian had already ridiculed a generation earlier, when the Stoics used it to prove that the soul enters the newborn with its first breath of cold air. Tertullian's reply — that people born in the tropics have souls too — is a decent objection to the Stoic use of it; against Origen's use, where the cooling is moral and not meteorological, it does not bite. What can be said against Origen's version is simply that an etymology cannot do this much work: nothing follows about the history of souls from the sound of a Greek noun, and Origen himself does not treat it as more than a suggestion.5

What the theory buys is an explanation of an ordinary fact — that people are born with unequal minds. Since the falling-away “is not the same in all,” some understandings “retain something even of their former vigour, and others again either nothing or a very small amount”: hence “some are found from the very commencement of their lives to be of more active intellect, others again of a slower habit of mind, and some are born wholly obtuse, and altogether incapable of instruction.” The explanandum is real and the explanation is unfalsifiable, which is roughly where the argument stands today.6

“Not… in a dogmatic manner”

Origen marks the status of all this twice, and both marks are in the same book. In the middle of the discussion he hands the conclusion back to the reader:

Our statement, however, that the understanding is converted into a soul, or whatever else seems to have such a meaning, the reader must carefully consider and settle for himself…

— De Principiis II.viii.4, source — adding that these views are put forward not in a dogmatic manner but “simply as opinions, treated in the style of investigation and discussion.” And he closes the book on the same note:

We have brought forward as we best could these points regarding the rational soul, as topics of discussion for our readers, rather than as dogmatic and well-defined propositions.

— De Principiis II.viii, closing, source. This does not make the theory true, and it is fair to observe that a hedge attached to a thesis one has argued at length for two books is a weaker thing than a hedge attached to a passing remark. But it does bear on what Origen was condemned for. The anathemas of 543 do not condemn an investigation; they condemn an assertion, in the flat form “whoever says… let him be anathema.” Between Origen's quaestio and Justinian's anathema lie three centuries in which the school of his followers hardened what he had left open.7

What we are reading: Rufinus's Latin

The Greek De Principiis survives only in fragments and in quotations made by hostile witnesses. The continuous text is the Latin version made by Rufinus of Aquileia around 398, and Rufinus openly described what he had done to it:

I admit that I put something of my own into the work; as I stated in my Preface, I used my own discretion in cutting out not a few passages; but only those as to which I had come to suspect that the thing had not been so stated by Origen himself…

— Rufinus, Apology in Defence of Himself (to Pope Anastasius I) §7, source. Jerome, who had once admired Origen and now needed to be seen prosecuting him, made this the centre of his attack: the doctrine about the Father, Son and Spirit, he charged, “had been changed by the translator so as to give a more orthodox meaning,” and the selectivity was itself the offence — “if you mean to make a veracious and faithful translation, why do you change some things and leave others untouched?”8

For the material on this page the risk is real but limited, and it runs in a predictable direction. Rufinus's declared policy was to remove what he judged heretical interpolation, which would tend to make Origen look more orthodox, not less; so passages that still teach pre-existence after his editing are unlikely to have been inserted by him. The hedges are the more delicate case — a translator anxious to protect his author has an obvious motive to preserve or even to strengthen a disclaimer. Against that, the same disclaimers appear at the structurally natural places (the end of a discussion, the transition to a new topic), and Rufinus's own testimony to Anastasius describes Origen's position as an opinion among others he had read rather than as a dogma. The whole quarrel over the translation is followed on the Origenist controversy timeline.9

The condemnation

What was finally anathematized was the theory in its hardened form, stated as a chain of assertions with the hedges gone:

If anyone asserts the fabulous pre-existence of souls, and shall assert the monstrous restoration which follows from it: let him be anathema.

— The Anathemas Against Origen, i, transmitted with the Fifth Ecumenical Council (553), source. The earlier and better-attested text is Justinian's edict of 543, whose first anathema tracks Origen's own mechanism closely enough to be recognisable as a summary of De Principiis II.viii — souls that “had previously been spirits and holy powers, but that, satiated with the vision of God, they had turned to evil, and in this way the divine love in them had died out.” Read against Origen's text, the summary is fair on the content and silent on the modality: the thing condemned is the thesis, not the inquiry, and the reader of the anathema would never guess that its author had offered it “as topics of discussion for our readers.” Whether that silence is a distortion is a question about the anathema, not about whether pre-existence is true — after 543 the three-way question was a two-way one, and the West's long stalemate between traducianism and creationism was what remained.10

Notes

  • Origen and Tertullian are not simply opposites. Both deny that the soul arrives at birth from outside; both make the soul's condition a matter of its history rather than of raw divine decree. They divide on whether that history begins at conception or before it — and, revealingly, on the same Greek etymology, which Tertullian mocks and Origen exploits.
  • Gregory of Nyssa rejected pre-existence on the ground that it slides into the pagan transmigration tales; John of Damascus later fixed the Eastern formula against it by name. The Greek tradition thus closed off Origen's option without ever committing itself between the two Latin ones.
  • Open tension: Origen's theodicy and the later Western doctrine of original sin are rival explanations of the same data — why a newborn should be born into disadvantage. Augustine replaced a pre-natal fall of each soul with a single fall of the race, and then found he could not say how the guilt of that fall reaches a soul God has just made. That difficulty is weighed on its own page (assessment: contested).
  • Origen appears on this wiki also as a witness on the intermediate state, the Nephilim, and the Old Testament canon. For the rest of the witnesses on this question see the summary timeline.
