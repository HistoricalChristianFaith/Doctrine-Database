John of Damascus on the origin of the soul

Dates: c. 749 · Wikipedia
Summary: John of Damascus is where the Greek settlement becomes a formula. In the chapter “Concerning Man” of the handbook that served the East as its standard summary of doctrine, two sentences do all the work: the reasoning soul is God's gift “by His own inbreathing,” and body and soul were formed “at one and the same time, not first the one and then the other, as Origen so senselessly supposes.” That is Gregory of Nyssa compressed into a clause. What it settles is when the soul begins; what it leaves untouched is whence it comes — the question the Latin West had been fighting over for five hundred years, and which is not John's subject at all.

He stands twelfth on this timeline, and his entry has a different character from the ones around it. He is not intervening in an argument. He is codifying — writing a manual that states what the church holds, in order, without polemical apparatus, and the two sentences that concern us are the whole of what such a manual found it necessary to say.1

“By His own inbreathing”

The account of man's creation follows immediately on the making of the angels and of the sensible world, and its point is that man is the mixture of the two — the connecting link between the visible and invisible natures. The two halves are then assigned to their sources:

Now this being the case, He creates with His own hands man of a visible nature and an invisible, after His own image and likeness: on the one hand man's body He formed of earth, and on the other his reasoning and thinking soul He bestowed upon him by His own inbreathing, and this is what we mean by after His image. For the phrase after His image clearly refers to the side of his nature which consists of mind and free will, whereas after His likeness means likeness in virtue so far as that is possible.

— An Exact Exposition of the Orthodox Faith II.12, “Concerning Man”, source. This is an exposition of Genesis 2:7, and the sentence is about the first man. That has to be kept in view, because the phrase “by His own inbreathing” is easily read as a general doctrine of the soul's origin — each soul breathed in by God — which would make John a creationist. He does not say that. The clause tells us how Adam's soul came to be and identifies the image of God with the mind and the free will. It says nothing about the second man, or the millionth.2

“At one and the same time”

Then, in a single free-standing sentence, the one contested point the handbook thinks worth settling:

Further, body and soul were formed at one and the same time, not first the one and then the other, as Origen so senselessly supposes.

— An Exact Exposition of the Orthodox Faith II.12, source. Everything about this sentence — the doctrine, the shape of the denial, and the name attached to the error — comes from Gregory of Nyssa, who three and a half centuries earlier had set the two rival opinions against each other in On the Making of Man and rejected both: “as man is one, the being consisting of soul and body, we are to suppose that the beginning of his existence is one, common to both parts, so that he should not be found to be antecedent and posterior to himself.” John's clause is that argument's conclusion with the argument removed. Gregory reasoned from the unity of the man, from the impropriety of a maker who does the halves in turn, and from the wheat-grain that contains the whole plant potentially. A handbook keeps the result.3

The naming of Origen is likewise inherited, and slightly sharpened. Gregory had described the pre-existence doctrine without naming its author, gesturing only at “those … who have dealt with the question of principles.” By John's time there is no reason for the discretion: the doctrine had been under anathema since Justinian's edict of 543, and Origen's name had been attached to the condemnation. So what was an unnamed opinion in 379 is a named and dismissed error in the eighth century — “so senselessly supposes” — and it is the only error on this subject that the handbook mentions at all.4

What simultaneity does not decide

This is the point of the page, and it is easy to miss because the sentence sounds so decisive. Simultaneity of soul and body settles nothing between traducianism and creationism. Both can hold it, and historically both did.

The traducianist case for simultaneity is the older one. Tertullian argued it three centuries before Gregory, in a chapter whose heading states it as flatly as John's sentence does: “We indeed maintain that both are conceived, and formed, and perfectly simultaneously, as well as born together; and that not a moment's interval occurs in their conception, so that, a prior place can be assigned to either.” His reason was that death is the separation of soul and body, so life must be their conjunction, and the conjunction must therefore be as instantaneous as the separation. On that basis he concluded to traducianism: if the soul begins when the body begins, and the body comes from the parents, the soul comes the same way. Simultaneity was his premise for the propagation, not an alternative to it.5

The creationist can hold it too, though with more strain, and the Latin tradition mostly did not bother: Gennadius has the body sown first and the soul “created and infused” into a body already formed, and Aquinas would place the arrival of the intellectual soul at the end of human generation, after a succession of prior forms. But a creationist is not obliged to say that. God can as easily create a soul at the first instant of conception as at a later one, and Lactantius — a creationist — insisted on exactly that timing against the philosophers: the soul is not introduced after birth “but immediately after conception.” Timing and source are independent variables, and John fixes only the first of them.6

Nor does the condemnation of Origen narrow things further. Pre-existence is the one option ruled out; ruling it out leaves two, and John does not choose between them because he does not raise the question. There is no sentence in the chapter about whether a soul now beginning comes from its parents' souls or is made afresh, no proof-text adduced on either side, and no opponent named except the one already condemned. It is not that he takes a position and states it briefly. He does not take one.

The soul the handbook describes

What John does supply, at length, is a definition of what a soul is, and it is worth setting beside the silence about where souls come from:

The soul, accordingly, is a living essence, simple, incorporeal, invisible in its proper nature to bodily eyes, immortal, reasoning and intelligent, formless, making use of an organised body, and being the source of its powers of life, and growth, and sensation, and generation, mind being but its purest part and not in any wise alien to it … further it enjoys freedom and volition and energy, and is mutable, that is, it is given to change, because it is created.

— An Exact Exposition of the Orthodox Faith II.12, source. Set this against Tertullian's definition — a soul “immortal, possessing body, having form … evolved out of one (archetypal soul)” — and the divergence is total on exactly the property that Tertullian's traducianism required. John's soul is incorporeal and formless; Tertullian's has body and form, and has them because only such a thing can be carried in a seed. John immediately qualifies the incorporeality — angels, demons, and souls are incorporeal “by free gift, and in comparison with the denseness of matter,” while God alone is so by nature — but the qualification does not restore what Tertullian needed.

So the metaphysics in the handbook does tell against the Latin traducians, in the way that Aquinas would later make explicit: an incorporeal, subsistent soul is not the sort of thing a bodily process can produce. But John does not draw that inference, and the inference is not forced. A Latin creationist could quote this definition against Tertullian and a Latin traducianist could accept the definition and deny that it entails anything about generation. What the passage shows is that the Greek tradition had settled its doctrine of the soul's nature and its timing without ever needing to settle the question that consumed the West.7

Notes

  • The contrast that this page exists to draw: the East settled when and left whence comparatively undiscussed; the West fought over whence and never got a conciliar decision on it. Neither half of the church ended with a definition of the soul's origin — but the East had no dispute to define, and the West had one it could not close.
  • Be careful not to make John say more than he does. He is often cited as a creationist witness on the strength of “by His own inbreathing,” but that clause is an exposition of the making of Adam, and the sentence about simultaneity that follows is directed at pre-existence, not at traducianism. Neither states a doctrine of the origin of individual souls.
  • Open tension: the definition of the soul as incorporeal and formless sits badly with a soul transmitted in a seed, and John states the definition as settled church teaching. If that is right, the Latin quarrel had an answer lying in the East's own handbook the whole time — but nobody in the eighth-century East drew the conclusion, because nobody there was asking. Aquinas drew it five centuries later from a different metaphysics.
  • The half of Tertullian preserved here is the half his critics never attacked. His simultaneity survived, restated without the corporeal soul by Gregory of Nyssa and fixed as a formula here; his propagation was condemned in effect by Anastasius II and in words by Aquinas.
  • Neither of the arguments this timeline weighs — the John 5:17 proof-text (assessment: weak) or the infant-damnation difficulty (assessment: contested) — appears in John's chapter. Both are artefacts of the Latin controversy, the second of the Pelagian controversy in particular, and neither was a Greek problem.
  • For the rest of the witnesses see the summary timeline.
