John Cassian on the origin of the soul

Dates: c. 435 · Wikipedia
Summary: Cassian gives the creationist case the proof-text that outlived the debate. Hebrews 12:9 distinguishes “fathers of our flesh” from “the Father of spirits,” and on his reading the distinction is a doctrine of origins: men father bodies, God alone fathers souls, and He “makes them out of nothing whenever He pleases.” The mechanism is denied flatly — spirit does not beget spirit. What is as telling as the argument is where he makes it: not in a treatise on the soul but in passing, three sentences deep into a night-long conference about demons, as a premise nobody in the room is expected to dispute.

He stands eighth on this timeline, between the two men who turned creationism from an opinion into a rule. Jerome had stated it as the church's teaching and attached it to John 5:17; Gennadius, in the same city a generation later, would print it as a canon with the rival named and condemned. Cassian is the middle term: the point at which a Latin writer can use creationism as a premise rather than defend it as a thesis.1

The setting: a night conference on demons

The Conferences record conversations with the Egyptian fathers. The eighth is the second conference of Abbot Serenus, and its subject is announced in the title — On Principalities. Germanus and Cassian have asked where the hostile powers came from and why they differ in rank, and the discussion, which has run “nearly two whole nights,” works through the fall of the angels, the sons of God in Genesis 6, the antediluvian judgment, and finally the hardest of the questions put: what does the Lord mean by calling the devil a liar “and the father of it”? Is the devil's own father also a liar?

Serenus's answer requires a rule about what spirits can and cannot generate, and he has already laid one down earlier the same night. Asked whether angels could really have fathered children on the daughters of men, he had denied it on grounds of what would follow: “We cannot possibly believe that spiritual existences can have carnal intercourse with women. But if this could ever have literally happened how is it that it does not now also sometimes take place”? — and had reread Genesis 6 as the intermarriage of the line of Seth with the line of Cain. The reading is the one this wiki follows on the Nephilim timeline, where Cassian has his own page. It matters here because the doctrine of the soul arrives as its corollary.2

“Spirit does not beget spirit”

The rule, when it comes, is stated as something already agreed:

For as we said a little while ago spirit does not beget spirit just as soul cannot procreate soul, though we do not doubt that the compacting of flesh is formed from man's seed, as the Apostle clearly distinguishes in the case of both substances; viz., flesh and spirit, what should be ascribed to whom as its author, and says: Moreover we have had fathers of our flesh for instructors, and we reverenced them: shall we not much more be in subjection to the Father of spirits and live? What could show more clearly than this distinction, that he laid down that men were the fathers of our flesh, but always taught that God alone was the Father of souls.

— Conferences VIII.25 (the Second Conference of Abbot Serenus), source, citing Hebrews 12:9. Two claims are packed into this. One is negative and about capacity: a spirit is not the kind of thing that can generate another spirit, whatever it can do with flesh. The other is positive and exegetical: an apostle has sorted the two paternities, assigning flesh to men and spirit to God, and the sorting is the whole argument. Cassian is careful not to let even the body be simply the parents' work — in its formation men have “a ministerial office alone,” the chief part belonging to God, and he stacks up Job's milk and cheese, the psalm's “Your hands have made me and fashioned me,” and the word to Jeremiah before birth to say so. The concession is worth marking. It means his position is not that God does one thing and parents another, but that God does both and parents assist at one of them.3

Ecclesiastes, Adam's breath, and creation out of nothing

The second text is the one Jerome had used the same way, and Cassian presses it in the same direction — from where a thing goes back to, infer where it came from:

But what could be said with greater plainness than that he declares that the matter of the flesh which he styled dust, because it springs from the seed of man, and seems to be sown by his ministration, must, as it was taken from the earth, again return to the earth, while he points out that the spirit which is not begotten by intercourse between the sexes, but belongs to God alone in a special way, returns to its creator?

— Conferences VIII.25, source, on Ecclesiastes 12:7. He adds the breathing into Adam as confirmation, and then draws the conclusion in the form that would be quoted afterwards:

And so from these passages we clearly infer that no one can be called the Father of spirits but God alone, who makes them out of nothing whenever He pleases, while men can only be termed the fathers of our flesh.

— Conferences VIII.25, source. “Out of nothing whenever He pleases” is creationism in five words, and it is exactly the disjunction Augustine had insisted on a decade or so earlier — if the soul is not derived from the parent, it comes out of nothing. Cassian states the second limb without any sign of feeling the weight of what Augustine had been unable to get past: that a soul made new out of nothing is a soul made innocent, and the church nevertheless baptises infants in haste. He is a Massilian writing during the years when the Provençal monasteries were resisting Augustine on grace, so the silence may be deliberate; but it is silence, not an answer.4

How good is the proof-text?

It is a better one than John 5:17. “My Father worketh hitherto” comes from a sabbath controversy about a healing and says nothing about souls at all; a traducianist affirms God's ceaseless working as readily as a creationist does, so the verse selects between the two views not at all (it is weighed on its own page, assessment weak). Hebrews 12:9 is genuinely about the two fatherhoods, human and divine, and genuinely contrasts them. Cassian is reading a real distinction in the text and not manufacturing one.

Three things nonetheless limit what it can carry, and the third is the most serious.

The first is the passage's subject. Hebrews 12 is about discipline: we submitted to human fathers who chastened us for a few days after their own pleasure, so how much more should we submit to the Father of spirits, who chastens us for our profit. The contrast is drawn to license an argument from lesser to greater about obedience under suffering, not to explain where souls come from. A title used to ground an exhortation is not thereby a statement of origins.

The second is the range of the title itself. “Father of spirits” can name God's fatherhood of the whole spiritual order — the relation in which He stands to rational creatures as such — without specifying by what process any particular spirit came to be. A traducianist can say the same words: God is the Father of all spirits because He gave the first man the breath from which the rest descend, which is precisely the reply Augustine reports the traducians making to Ecclesiastes 12:7, and precisely Tertullian's whole account of the “entire outflow and redundance of men's souls” from the one.

The third is what Cassian himself needs the verse to prove. He is not arguing about human conception; he is arguing that the devil had no father but God. The “spirits” whose sole Father is God therefore have to include angels — who were never conceived at all, and about whom no one on any side of the traducian dispute thought otherwise. The verse is doing work in this conference that a traducianist could grant entire. Which leaves the conclusion about human souls resting on the phrase “fathers of our flesh” being read as exhaustive: that in saying men father flesh the apostle meant men father only flesh. That is a possible reading. It is not a compelled one, and nothing else in the passage forces it.5

What the aside shows

The historical value of the passage is largely independent of whether the exegesis holds. A generation earlier Jerome had to argue for creationism against named opponents, brand the rival as the doctrine of Tertullian and a heretic, and say it levelled man with the beasts; three decades before that Rufinus could set out three views and decline all of them without anyone supposing his orthodoxy was at stake on the point. Cassian neither argues nor declines. He uses the doctrine as a step in a proof about something else, in a work written for monks and destined to be read in every Latin monastery for a thousand years, and does not pause to defend it.

That is what a settled opinion looks like from the inside. It is also, on this question, how the settlement was in fact reached: not by anyone answering the objection Augustine had put to Jerome and never had answered, but by the objection ceasing to be raised. Within forty years Gennadius, writing in Cassian's own Marseilles and cataloguing his works with evident admiration — the eighth conference is listed there under its own title, On principalities — would set the same doctrine down as what the church teaches, with the alternatives named as errors.6

Notes

  • Hebrews 12:9 is the strongest scriptural card the creationist side ever held, and it is odd that the tradition leaned so heavily on John 5:17 instead. Part of the reason is accident of transmission: Jerome's authority attached the weaker verse to the doctrine early, and a bishop of Rome repeated it in 498. See Anastasius II and the John 5:17 argument page.
  • Open tension: Cassian's rule “spirit does not beget spirit” is stated as a general truth about spiritual natures, but he introduces it to settle a question about angels and applies it to human souls without remarking on the difference. An angel has no body to be generated with; a human soul does. Everything contested between traducians and creationists lives in that gap, and the conference passes straight over it.
  • Cassian offers no argument at all from original sin, in either direction — neither the traducian's explanation of it nor Augustine's objection to the creationist alternative. Given that he was writing at Marseilles in the years of the Provençal reaction against Augustine's doctrine of grace, the omission is at least suggestive; the difficulty itself is weighed on its own page (assessment: contested).
  • Cassian appears on this wiki also as a witness on the Nephilim timeline, where the same conference supplies his denial that angels could father children — the premise from which this page's doctrine is drawn as a corollary. See that page, and the summary timeline for the rest of the witnesses here.
