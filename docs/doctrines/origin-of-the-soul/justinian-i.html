Justinian I on the origin of the soul

Dates: 543 · Wikipedia
Summary: Justinian is the entry that removes an option. His tract against Origen, closing in a list of nine anathemas, begins with the pre-existence of souls and condemns not a slogan but a mechanism — souls that were once spirits, cooled from the vision of God, and were sent down into bodies as a punishment. That is Origen's account of the origin of the soul in Origen's own terms, including the etymology it rests on. After 543 the three-way question that had been open since the third century is, in both halves of the church, a two-way one.

He stands eleventh on this timeline, and he is the only witness on it who is not a churchman. The first list of anathemas on this subject was issued by an emperor, in a tract of his own composition, addressed to the patriarch of Constantinople and taken up by a local synod of the bishops under him. That is worth stating plainly at the outset, because it shapes everything about how the condemnation travelled.1

The first anathema

The list opens on the soul, and it opens on the precise doctrine Origen had put forward three centuries earlier:

Whoever says or thinks that human souls pre-existed, i.e., that they had previously been spirits and holy powers, but that, satiated with the vision of God, they had turned to evil, and in this way the divine love in them had died out (ἀπψυγείσας) and they had therefore become souls (ψυχάς) and had been condemned to punishment in bodies, shall be anathema.

— The Anathematisms of the Emperor Justinian Against Origen, i, source. Notice how much of Origen's actual argument is inside the sentence. This is not a summary condemnation of “the soul existed before the body.” It reproduces the whole derivation: the original state (spirits and holy powers), the cause of the fall (satiety with the vision of God), the mechanism (the divine love cooling), the result (they became souls), and the purpose of embodiment (punishment). Whoever drafted this had read the argument.2

The condemned etymology

The most striking feature is the two Greek words the text preserves. Origen's account had turned on a pun: a mind that grows cold — the verb is psychesthai — becomes a psychē, a soul. The name of the thing records its fall. The anathema keeps both terms in the same clause, so that the condemned proposition carries the derivation that generated it:

… the divine love in them had died out (ἀπψυγείσας) and they had therefore become souls (ψυχάς) …

— The Anathematisms of the Emperor Justinian Against Origen, i, source. This is unusual as anathemas go. Councils normally condemn a proposition; here the etymological engine of the proposition is itself written into the sentence of condemnation. What is being outlawed is De Principiis II.viii.3 — the chapter in which the cooling of the mind into a soul is set out and the word-play is the argument.3

There is an irony in this that the timeline should record. The same etymology had been in circulation long before Origen used it, and Tertullian had already dismissed it — in his version, the Stoic claim that the newborn receives its soul when cold air strikes it. He answered by geography: peoples born under a broiling sun have souls too. Origen took the same word-play in an entirely different direction and built a cosmology on it. The anathema condemns the cosmology and, with it, the derivation.

The rest of the list, and its target

The nine anathemas do not stay on the soul. They run through the pre-existence of Christ's soul, the doctrine that the Word became like each order of heavenly beings, spherical resurrection bodies, ensouled stars, and a future crucifixion for demons; and they close on the doctrine that made the whole system hang together:

If anyone says or thinks that the punishment of demons and of impious men is only temporary, and will one day have an end, and that a restoration (ἀποκατάστασις) will take place of demons and of impious men, let him be anathema.

— The Anathematisms of the Emperor Justinian Against Origen, ix, source. Pre-existence and universal restoration are the two ends of one arc: souls that fell from an original unity are souls that can be returned to it. Condemning the first without the second would have left the system standing on one leg. The list then names its man:

Anathema to Origen and to that Adamantius, who set forth these opinions together with his nefarious and execrable and wicked doctrine and to whomsoever there is who thinks thus, or defends these opinions, or in any way hereafter at any time shall presume to protect them.

— The Anathematisms of the Emperor Justinian Against Origen, conclusion, source. “Adamantius” was Origen's byname; the text as transmitted reads as though it names two people, and the NPNF editor flags the Greek and Latin as disagreeing at this point. The clause condemning anyone who “shall presume to protect them” is the operative one for what followed: it made defending Origen, not merely holding his opinions, an offence.4

The fifteen anathemas, and a question that is not settled

A second and longer list, of fifteen anathemas, is transmitted with the Fifth Ecumenical Council of 553. It opens with the same subject in a single line:

If anyone asserts the fabulous pre-existence of souls, and shall assert the monstrous restoration which follows from it: let him be anathema.

— The Anathemas Against Origen, i, source. The two doctrines are here bound together explicitly — the restoration “which follows from” the pre-existence — and the fourteen that follow work out the fallen-minds cosmology in far more detail than Justinian's nine: intelligences without bodies, differentiated into angelic orders and human and demonic bodies by the degree of their cooling; a final state in which “the end and the beginning shall be alike.”

Whether these fifteen belong to the acts of the council of 553 is genuinely disputed, and the wiki should not pretend otherwise in either direction. Three things can be said with confidence. First, the council's own capitula number fourteen and are entirely concerned with Theodore of Mopsuestia, Theodoret, and the letter of Ibas — the Three Chapters — not with Origen's cosmology. Second, the fifteen anathemas as we have them surfaced in a Vienna manuscript found by the librarian Peter Lambeck in the seventeenth century, headed as canons of the fifth synod, and a long line of scholars — Walch, Döllinger, Hefele among them — judged that heading untrustworthy and assigned the fifteen instead to a local synod at Constantinople. Third, a great deal of material later than the council does attribute a detailed examination and condemnation of Origen to it.5

The honest statement of where this leaves us was made by the editor of the standard English collection of the councils, and it has not been improved on as a statement of the epistemic position:

It would seem that with the evidence now in our possession it would be the height of rashness to give a dogmatic answer to this question. Scholars of the highest repute have taken, and do take to-day, the opposite sides of the case, and each defends his own side with marked learning and ability.

— Excursus on the XV. Anathemas Against Origen, NPNF² vol. 14, source. Even Hefele, the ablest advocate of the case against conciliar authorship, conceded the point: “We certainly possess no strong and decisive proof that the fifteen anathematisms belong to the Constantinopolitan synod of the year 543; but some probable grounds for the opinion may be adduced.” And the same editor adds the observation that limits how much the question matters: even if the fifteen were adopted before the ecumenical council opened, the council named Origen among the heretics in its eleventh capitulum, and so “practically ratified and made its own the action of that Synod.”6

For this timeline the dispute changes nothing. The edict of 543 is the earlier and better-attested text, its first anathema is squarely on the origin of the soul, and it is Justinian's under any reconstruction. Whatever happened at or around 553, the doctrine of pre-existence did not survive the sixth century as a live option in the church.7

What the condemnation caught, and what it missed

Origen had marked the discussion as exploratory. Twice in the same book of De Principiis he says that what he is offering is investigation and not dogma — “not to be regarded as advanced by us in a dogmatic manner, but simply as opinions, treated in the style of investigation and discussion” — and in the preface he had said explicitly that the church's rule of faith left the soul's origin undetermined, which is what gave him room to speculate in the first place. The anathema of 543 hits the doctrine in a hardened form, as a set of theses held and taught, three centuries after a writer proposed it as a possibility. That is not a defence of the doctrine, and it is not an argument that the condemnation was unjust — sixth-century Origenism was a real movement with real adherents, and its theses were being asserted, not floated. It is simply the difference between the position condemned and the way its author framed it.8

The other thing the condemnation does not do is decide anything between the two remaining options. Nothing in either list touches traducianism or creationism; the anathemas concern where souls were before they were embodied and where they will be afterwards, not whether a soul now beginning comes from its parents or from God. That question was, and remained, a Latin quarrel. The Greek settlement of it — Gregory of Nyssa's single beginning for soul and body, fixed as a formula two centuries later by John of Damascus — answers when without answering whence, and John's only named error is the one anathematized here.

Notes

  • This is the point at which the timeline narrows from three answers to two. That is the entry's significance, and it is worth noticing that the narrowing was accomplished by an imperial tract rather than by the exhaustion of an argument. Nobody refuted pre-existence in 543; it was condemned. The strongest theological arguments against it are earlier — Tertullian's, that a soul which began cannot have had a career before its body, and Gregory of Nyssa's, that a doctrine of descent by demerit cannot stop descending.
  • Open tension: an anathema tells us what was ruled out, not what was ruled in. After 543 the East had a prohibition and, in Gregory's formula, a doctrine of the soul's timing; it never developed a settled doctrine of the soul's source, because it never had the argument. The West, which had the argument, never got a conciliar decision. Both halves ended without a definition, by opposite routes.
  • The transmission question above — whether the fifteen anathemas belong to the acts of 553 — is a live scholarly dispute and is flagged as such wherever it appears on this wiki. Nothing on this timeline depends on resolving it, since the 543 text carries the point on its own.
  • Justinian appears here as a legislator against a doctrine he had also read. The tract to which these anathemas are the conclusion is a substantial piece of theological polemic, and the first anathema's reproduction of Origen's mechanism — cooling, the change of name, embodiment as penalty — shows that the drafting was done from the text, not from hearsay.
  • For Origen's own statement of the doctrine and his caveats about it see his page; for the rest of the witnesses, the summary timeline.
