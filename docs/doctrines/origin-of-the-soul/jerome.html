Jerome on the origin of the soul

Dates: c. 411 · Wikipedia
Summary: Jerome is the West's decided creationist, and the man who fastened the creationist case to a verse. God, he says, is daily making souls and sending them into bodies, on the authority of “my Father worketh hitherto”; the rival view he states in the form that makes it least attractive, as a doctrine on which the soul is bred like the soul of a beast. He held the position for at least fifteen years and stated it plainly three times. He also, when a pair of correspondents finally asked him to expound it, declined and sent them to Augustine instead — and then left Augustine's own long letter on the question unanswered for the rest of his life.

He stands sixth on this timeline. Two things distinguish his entry from those on either side of it: he is the first witness here to argue from Scripture rather than from inference, and he is the first to press the question polemically, as a test of somebody else's orthodoxy.1

“God is daily making souls” (c. 396)

Jerome's fullest statement is the earliest, and it comes out of a quarrel. About 396 he answered John of Jerusalem's profession of faith with a tract complaining that John had confessed everything except the thing he was asked about. John had said the soul is not part of God's essence and that angels do not become souls; Jerome wanted to know when the soul was made:

I now ask: When was that soul made, which is the work of God, which is distinguished by free will and reason, and is not of the essence of the Creator? Was it made at the same time that man was made out of the slime, and the breath of life was breathed into his face? Or, having previously existed, and having associated with reasonable and incorporeal creatures as well as lived, was it afterwards gifted with the inbreathing of God? Here you are silent.

— Against John of Jerusalem, §21, source. The next paragraph runs the question forward from Adam to everyone else — “whence had Eve her soul, seeing that God did not breathe into her face?”, and then “I ask whence Cain and Abel, who were the firstborn of our first parents, had their souls? And the whole human race downwards, what, are we to think, was the origin of their souls?” — and lays out the two live answers in a single sentence each. The first is traducianism, stated with its sting attached: “Did they come by propagation, like brute beasts? So that, as body springs from body, so soul from soul.” The second is Origen's. Then Jerome gives his own:

Surely (as the Church teaches in accordance with the Saviour's words, My Father works hitherto and I work; and the passage in Isaiah, Who makes the spirit of man in him; and in the Psalms, Who fashions one by one the hearts of them) God is daily making souls — He, with whom to will is to do, and who never ceases to be a Creator.

— Against John of Jerusalem, §22, source. Three things about this are worth marking. It is presented as the church's teaching, not as an opinion. It carries three proof-texts, of which the first would do all the later work. And the argument attached to the third clause is not exegetical at all but metaphysical — that a Creator does not stop creating, and that in God willing and doing are the same thing — which is a reason for thinking daily creation possible, not a reason for thinking it actual.2

He then meets the strongest objection to daily creation, and meets it well. If God makes each soul to order, what is He doing at a conception produced by adultery or incest — supplying a soul on demand to a crime? Jerome's answer is that the guilt attaches where the choice was:

Birth from adultery imputes no blame to the child, but to the father. As in the case of seeds, the earth which cherishes does not sin, nor the seed which is thrown into the furrows, nor the heat and moisture, under whose influence the grain bursts into bud, but some man, as for example, the thief and robber, who, by fraud and violence, plucks up the seed …

— Against John of Jerusalem, §22, source. The womb receives, nourishes, forms, and divides into members what it has received; “and among those secret recesses of the belly the hand of God is always working, and there is the same Creator of body and soul.” The reply is a real answer to a real difficulty, and it is the same move — distinguishing God's ordinary concurrence with nature from His endorsement of what men do with it — that later theology would use. It is also, characteristically, delivered inside an attack: the closing taunt of the section is “Suppose I am wrong, I openly say what I think. Do you, then, likewise either freely profess our opinions, or firmly maintain your own.”3

The commentaries: what returns to God must have come from God

The same doctrine surfaces twice in the commentaries, both times as an aside, and both times resting on a verse about the spirit's origin or destination. On the last chapter of Ecclesiastes he takes the return of the spirit as proof of where it came from:

From which there is enough to smile at in those who think that spirits are produced with bodies, not from God, but are made from the parent's body. For when the flesh is returned to the earth, and the spirit goes back to God, who gave it; it is obvious that God is the parent of all spirits, not man.

— Commentary on Ecclesiastes, on Ecclesiastes 12:7, source. The inference is from a symmetry: the flesh goes back where it came from, therefore so does the spirit, and the text says the spirit goes back to God. It is a better argument than John 5:17 — the verse is at least about the soul's origin, and the “who gave it” is in the text — though a traducianist can answer that God gave the spirit to Adam and gives every later one through him, which is exactly what Tertullian had said with the same verse-cluster available.4

On Zechariah the point is made in passing, expounding a verse that calls God the one who forms the spirit of man within him:

Therefore the Lord who stretched out the heavens like a skin, and solidified the earth with a high mass, and formed the spirit of man within it, is the same Creator of all souls, so that he might form one living being from two substances, the soul and the body.

— Commentary on Zechariah, on Zechariah 12:1, source. The clause “one living being from two substances” is the whole anthropology in six words, and it is notable that Jerome, unlike his opponents, does not need soul and body to begin at different moments in order to have them come from different sources. The prophet, on his reading, names God as the maker of the spirit in each man, in the present tense, which is the daily creation of the earlier tract.5

The taxonomy of 410

Then, some fifteen years after the tract against John, two correspondents in Africa — Marcellinus, the imperial commissioner who had presided over the conference with the Donatists, and his wife Anapsychia — wrote to Bethlehem asking where the soul comes from. Jerome's reply opens by restating the question in five branches:

I have not forgotten the brief query, or rather the very important theological question, which you propounded in regard to the origin of the soul — does it descend from heaven, as the philosopher Pythagoras and all the Platonists and Origen think? Or is it part of the essence of the Deity, as the Stoics, Manichæus, and the Priscillianists of Spain imagine? Or are souls kept in a divine treasure house wherein they were stored of old as some ecclesiastics, foolishly misled, believe? Or are they daily created by God and sent into bodies, according to what is written in the gospel, My Father works hitherto, and I work? or are souls really produced, as Tertullian, Apollinaris, and the majority of the Western divines conjecture, by propagation, so that as the body is the offspring of body, the soul is the offspring of soul, and exists on conditions similar to those regulating the existence of the inferior animals.

— Letter 126 (to Marcellinus and Anapsychia), §1, source. Jerome does not say which branch is his, and does not have to. The fourth is the only one that gets a proof-text rather than a list of partisans, and the fifth is the only one that gets an insult. The pattern is the same as in 396: the doctrine arrives attached to John 5:17, and the rival arrives attached to the animals. Set beside Rufinus's three-way division of ten years earlier, this list is longer and less useful: two of its branches (the soul as a piece of God, the treasury of stored souls) are positions Jerome is naming in order to dismiss, and the third and fifth are, on any careful reading, the same Origenist option split in two.6

The question he would not expound

Having stated the question, he declined to answer it. He referred his correspondents to his published exchange with Rufinus — “I know that I have published my opinion on this question in my brief writings against Ruffinus” — and then handed them on:

… you have in Africa that holy man and learned bishop Augustine, who will be able to teach you on this subject viva voce, as the saying is, and expound to you his opinion, or, I should rather say, my own opinion stated in his words.

— Letter 126, §1, source. Both halves of the referral are questionable. The Apology against Rufinus contains Jerome's position mostly in the form of questions put to somebody else, and its positive content on the soul is a single Christological either/or; a reader sent there for Jerome's doctrine would find chiefly Jerome's contempt for Rufinus's refusal to state one. And the man commended as holding “my own opinion stated in his words” held no settled opinion at all: Augustine had been unable to decide this question for twenty years when the letter was written, and would still be unable to decide it when he died.

The rest of the letter explains, without meaning to, why the answer never came. Jerome's working life had collapsed with the empire's western provinces:

… at the time when I had just begun to dictate the proposed exposition, my mind was so much agitated by the devastation of the western provinces of the empire, and especially by the sack of Rome itself by the barbarians, that, to use a common proverbial phrase, I scarcely knew my own name; and for a long while I was silent, knowing that it was a time for tears.

— Letter 126, §2, source. Marcellinus took the referral. Five years later Augustine sent Jerome a whole treatise in the form of a letter — Letter 166, On the Origin of the Human Soul, carried east by the priest Orosius — confessing his own perplexity and pressing the one difficulty he could not get past: if God makes each soul new and sinless, where did an infant snatched away before baptism contract the guilt for which it is condemned? Jerome never answered it. When he replied at all, it was to say that the times were too hard for controversy and that he was resolved to defend Augustine's opinions as his own. The question the West most needed the West's best Latinist to answer was put to him twice and dropped twice.7

“The majority of the Western divines”

One claim in the taxonomy is a historical assertion rather than a theological one, and it does not hold up. Jerome says traducianism is the view of Tertullian, Apollinaris, and maxima pars occidentalium — most Westerners. The standard modern reference account cites this very sentence and calls the testimony exaggerated, on the ground that no other writer of prominence can be found advocating the position as certain. That is the right verdict on the evidence available: Tertullian is the theory's author and its only sustained ancient defender, Apollinaris is a name without a surviving argument, and the one Latin whom Rufinus had added to the list — Lactantius — turns out on inspection to deny it.

Two qualifications keep this from being a simple error. The first is that absence of surviving advocacy is not absence of opinion; Jerome had lived in the Latin West and in the Greek East and was in a position to know what ordinary Latin churchmen assumed, and a century later a pope thought it worth writing to the bishops of Gaul against people there who held exactly this. The second is textual: the reference account renders the phrase “the majority of Oriental writers,” where the Latin has occidentalium and the English translation used here has “Western.” Its judgment of exaggeration is unaffected either way, but the slip is a reminder that the ancient lists have been quoted more often than they have been checked.8

What the case actually rests on

Strip away the polemic and Jerome's position has two supports.

The first is John 5:17, “my Father worketh hitherto, and I work.” It is the verse he attaches to the doctrine in 396 and again in 410, and it is the verse the creationist side would lean on for the next thousand years — a bishop of Rome used it against the Gallic traducians in 498. It does not carry the weight. Its own context is a sabbath controversy about a healing, and the work Jesus claims to share is the Father's unceasing upholding of everything; a traducianist affirms that too. The verse is compatible with daily creation and selects it over nothing, and the case is weighed on its own page (assessment: weak). Jerome's supporting texts from Isaiah and the Psalms are stronger in one respect — they are at least about the forming of the human spirit and heart — and weaker in another, since neither says when or from what.

The second is the sting: that traducianism makes the human soul the offspring of a soul as a beast's is the offspring of a beast. He puts it that way in the letter of 410, and had put it more sharply against Rufinus in 402 — “If it arose by traduction, then human souls, which we believe to be eternal, are subject to the same condition as those of the brutes, which perish with the body.” As a rhetorical weapon it worked; as an argument it assumes what the traducians denied, namely that whatever is propagated must be mortal. Tertullian's whole position was that a soul could be handed down and still be immortal, and Jerome nowhere shows that it cannot. The demonstration that the two are incompatible is Aquinas's, eight and a half centuries later, and it needs a metaphysics of subsistence that Jerome does not have.9

What Jerome did supply, and what mattered most, was not a proof but a settled voice. The most learned man in the Latin church stated creationism three times as the church's own teaching, over fifteen years, in works that were read everywhere; and the alternative he named he named as the doctrine of a condemned author and a heretic. By the 430s Cassian could assume the creationist answer in passing, and by 470 Gennadius could print it as a canon. That is how the West settled the question — not by winning the argument, but by having stopped hearing the other side.10

The Origenist controversy is where he demanded an answer on the soul from Rufinus and got none — see his page there — and the correspondence with Augustine is where an answer was demanded of him and he gave none; see that page. The symmetry is not flattering.
  • Open tension: Jerome told Marcellinus that his published opinion was in the books against Rufinus and that Augustine held “my own opinion stated in his words.” Neither statement survives inspection — the books against Rufinus argue chiefly that Rufinus should have had an opinion, and Augustine's opinion was that he had none. Whether this is a busy man's shorthand or an evasion dressed as a referral cannot be settled from the letter itself; the sack of Rome and the state of the monastery, described in the same letter, are a real explanation for a scholar who has stopped answering his mail.
  • Jerome's creationism sits uneasily beside the doctrine of inherited guilt he defended against the Pelagians in the same years. If each soul is made new and clean, the guilt an infant is baptised to remove has to reach it some other way. Augustine put that difficulty to him directly and never got a reply; it is weighed on its own page (assessment: contested).
  • The stated ground of the doctrine — John 5:17 — is weighed on its own page (assessment: weak). Nothing there depends on the doctrine being false; the verse simply does not decide between the two live views, and both Jerome and, later, Anastasius II used it as if it did.
  • Jerome's position on the East is worth noting for the shape of the timeline: the Greeks he had lived among had settled the timing question with Gregory of Nyssa's formula and had never had the Latin argument about the mechanism at all. Jerome imports the Greek horror of pre-existence into a Latin dispute where the real rival was Tertullian, and so fights two opponents with one weapon.
