Rufinus of Aquileia on the origin of the soul

Dates: c. 400 · Wikipedia
Summary: Rufinus of Aquileia contributes to this question the cleanest statement anyone in antiquity made of it — three opinions, named, with their partisans — and then refuses to choose between them. Writing to Pope Anastasius I under suspicion of Origenism, he declares that after reading each view he cannot hold any of them as certain, and that the only thing the church requires him to believe is that God makes souls as He makes bodies. That refusal is his position, and it is the datum: at the year 400 a man clearing himself of heresy could treat the origin of the soul as still undecided. Jerome answered that the refusal was the evasion.

He stands fifth on this timeline, and unlike every other witness on it he is not arguing for an answer. He appears here because of what his non-answer shows about the state of the question — and because his three-way taxonomy is the frame in which everyone afterwards, down to the modern histories, has set the debate.1

Why the question was put to him at all

Rufinus had spent some six years at Alexandria under Didymus the Blind and nearly two decades on the Mount of Olives, and had come home to Italy an admirer of Origen. Pressed by the monk Macarius, he put Origen's Peri Archon (On First Principles) into Latin, smoothing as he went what he took to be heretical interpolations, and prefaced it by pointing at Jerome as the translator who had done the same before him. That preface set off the quarrel that consumed the rest of both men's lives. When Pope Siricius died in 399 and Anastasius I succeeded him, Rufinus's accusers pressed their case at Rome, and Rufinus — pleading that he could not come in person — sent a written profession of faith instead.2

The origin of the soul was on the charge sheet because it was one of the doctrines Peri Archon is notorious for. Origen's souls are minds that cooled, fell, and were assigned bodies to match what they had become; a man who had just published that book in Latin was expected to say whether he believed it. The relevant paragraph of the Apology is his answer, and it is the sixth of eight.

The three-way division

I am next informed that some stir has been made on the question of the nature of the soul. Whether complaints on a matter of this kind ought to be entertained instead of being put aside, you must yourself decide. If, however, you desire to know my opinion on the subject, I will state it frankly. I have read a great many writers on this question, and I find that they express various opinions. Some of those whom I have read hold that the soul is infused together with the material body through the channel of the human seed; and of this they give such proofs as they can. I think that this was the opinion of Tertullian or Lactantius among the Latins, perhaps also of a few others. Others assert that God is every day making new souls, and infusing them into the bodies which have been framed in the womb; while others again believe that the souls were all made long ago, when God made all things of nothing, and that all that he now does is to plant out each soul in its body as it seems good to him. This is the opinion of Origen, and of some others of the Greeks.

— Apology in Defence of Himself (to Pope Anastasius I), §6, source. Set beside the other ancient lists, the merits of this one are obvious. It is exhaustive without padding: exactly three answers, distinguished by where the soul comes from rather than by who taught it, and each attached to the people who actually taught it. Jerome's rival taxonomy eleven years later runs to five branches, two of which are heresies nobody in the room held, and the fifth of which he states in a form designed to make it ridiculous. Gennadius's later list has two errors and one truth, which is a creed, not a survey. Rufinus's is the one a modern account can still use unaltered, and in substance the one it does.3

The refusal

Then he declines all three:

For myself, I declare in the presence of God that, after reading each of these opinions, I am up to the present moment unable to hold any of them as certain and absolute; the determination of the truth in this question I leave to God and to any to whom it shall please him to reveal it. My profession on this point is therefore, first, that these several opinions are those which I have found in books, but, secondly, that I as yet remain in ignorance on the subject, except so far as this, that the Church delivers it as an article of faith that God is the creator of souls as well as of bodies.

— Apology in Defence of Himself, §6, source. The last clause is doing real work and is easy to skim past. Rufinus is not saying the church has no teaching about the soul; he is drawing the line where he thinks the church has drawn it. That God is the maker of souls is de fide; by what route He makes this one is not. On that reading his agnosticism is not a shrug but a claim about the extent of the deposit — and it is the same claim Origen had made in the preface to the very book Rufinus had translated, where the soul's origin is listed among the things the church's preaching has not defined.4

The rest of the document is consistent with that line-drawing. On the resurrection he is specific to the point of pedantry — “this very flesh in which we now live … without the loss of a single member” — and on the devil's punishment he is emphatic enough to curse anyone who denies it. Where he thought the church had spoken he speaks; where he thought it had not, he does not.5

Jerome's answer: a question with a name attached

Two years later Jerome worked through the Apology line by line in Book II of his own Apology against Rufinus, and the paragraph on the soul got a chapter to itself. His tactic was to convert the abstract question into a Christological one that no orthodox man could decline — not “where do souls come from?” but “where did that soul come from?”:

That soul which Jesus took upon him, did it exist before it was born of Mary? Was it created together with the body in that original Virgin nature which was begotten by the Holy Spirit? Or, when the body was already formed within the womb, was it made all at once, and sent down from heaven? I wish to know which one of these you choose as your opinion. If it existed before it was born from Mary, then it was not yet the soul of Jesus; and it was employed in some way, and, for a reward of its virtues, it was made his soul. If it arose by traduction, then human souls, which we believe to be eternal, are subject to the same condition as those of the brutes, which perish with the body. But if it is created and sent into the body after the body has been formed, tell us so simply, and free us from anxiety.

— Jerome, Apology Against Rufinus II.4, source. The three limbs of the question are Rufinus's own three opinions, applied to Christ; and the first limb is the one with teeth, because on Origen's account the soul of Jesus is precisely a pre-existent soul that did not fall, and Jerome elsewhere lists that as one of Origen's flat heresies. Rufinus, having declined to rule out pre-existence in general, has by Jerome's arithmetic declined to rule it out here. His verdict on the whole reply: “None of these answers will you give us.”6

The sustained attack comes later in the book, and it is directed less at the ignorance than at the choice to publish it:

If he is ignorant on the subject for which ill-reports are current against him, what need was there for him to send an Apology, which contains no defense of himself, but only a confession of his ignorance? This course is calculated to sow a crop of suspicions, not to calm them.

— Jerome, Apology Against Rufinus II.10, source. He goes on to compare Rufinus to the sceptical Academics, Arcesilaus and Carneades, and to say he outdoes them, since they at least invented a doctrine of probability to temper their agnosticism. Then comes the accusation that gives the chapter its edge — that the ignorance is selective:

Take care! You may find people saying that the reason for your confession of your ignorance of the three is that you do not wish to be compelled to condemn one. You spare Tertullian and Lactantius so as not to condemn Origen with them.

— Jerome, Apology Against Rufinus II.10, source. And a theological objection underneath the personal one: “are you astonished if your brethren are scandalized when you swear that you know nothing of a thing which the churches of Christ profess to know?” That premise — that the churches do profess to know — is exactly what Rufinus had denied, and it is the real point at issue between them.7

Weighing the refusal

Both men have a case, and they are not answering the same question.

Rufinus's position is defensible on its merits and was shortly to be held by a better theologian than either of them. Augustine spent forty years unable to decide this question, said so repeatedly in print, asked Jerome for help and never got it, and wrote in his last years that he did not know. If professed ignorance about the origin of the soul is a symptom of heresy, the West's greatest doctor had it worse and longer. Nor was Rufinus's line about the deposit eccentric: no council ever defined the point, the modern reference account concedes that “there are no strict definitions condemning Generationism as heretical,” and the question was still being argued in the schools eight centuries later.8

Jerome's complaint, however, is not really that ignorance is a heresy. It is that this particular document is not a treatise but a plea — a defence filed against a specific charge, before a judge, by a man asking to be cleared — and that in such a document “I have not made up my mind” on the charged article does not do the work a defence has to do. On the resurrection and the devil Rufinus gives Anastasius definite propositions; on the one article where he was actually suspected he gives a reading list. That asymmetry is a fair thing to notice, and Jerome noticed it. It does not follow that Rufinus was lying about his uncertainty, or that he secretly held Origen's view — Jerome's imputation of motive is inference, not evidence, and its force depends on already believing him an Origenist. But the observation that the reply does not answer the accusation stands independently of the motive alleged for it.

The honest summary is that Rufinus was probably right about the state of the doctrine and probably wrong about what his own document needed to do. Anastasius, at any rate, was unpersuaded: he condemned the translation and disowned the translator, writing to John of Jerusalem that he neither knew nor wished to know what Rufinus was doing or where he was living.9

The slip about Lactantius

One detail of the taxonomy does not survive checking. Rufinus assigns the seed-borne soul to “Tertullian or Lactantius among the Latins,” and on Tertullian he is exactly right; on Lactantius he is wrong, and wrong about the one chapter in which Lactantius treats the question, where souls are said to come from neither parent nor from both. The error is hedged (“I think that this was the opinion”) and made from memory under pressure, and it is best read as a slip rather than as evidence about Lactantius; the full account is on his page. It is worth recording here only because Jerome, for all his malice, caught it — “I am not aware of having read that Lactantius spoke of the soul as planted at the same time as the body. But, as you say that you have read it, please to tell me in what book it is to be found” — and because a taxonomy this influential deserves to have its one bad attribution flagged wherever it is quoted.10

Notes

  • Rufinus's other appearance on this wiki is as one of the two principals in the quarrel that produced this document: see the Origenist controversy timeline and his page there, where the translation of Peri Archon, the interpolation thesis behind it, and the war with Jerome are treated at length. The Apology to Anastasius is discussed there as a moment in that quarrel; here it matters for the one paragraph that has nothing to do with Jerome.
  • Open tension: Rufinus says the church requires belief that God is “the creator of souls as well as of bodies” and treats that as compatible with all three opinions, including the traducian one. A traducianist can indeed say it — God made the one soul from which the rest descend, as He made the one body — but the sentence sits oddly next to a mechanism in which no soul after Adam's is directly made by anyone. Rufinus does not work out how much his “article of faith” excludes, which is part of why Jerome found the paragraph slippery.
  • The modern reference account lists Rufinus, with Gregory of Nyssa, Macarius, and Nemesius, among those who “seem to prefer” the traducian side while conceding that their views “are not always clear.” On the strength of this paragraph that reading is hard to sustain for Rufinus in particular: he states the traducian view first and attributes it to others, and declines it along with the rest.
  • Jerome later told Marcellinus and Anapsychia that his own published opinion on the soul's origin was to be found “in my brief writings against Ruffinus” — that is, in this exchange. The claim is thinner than it sounds: what Book II mostly contains is questions put to Rufinus, and the positive creationist doctrine has to be gathered from elsewhere in Jerome. See his page.
  • Neither the John 5:17 proof-text, weighed on its own page (assessment: weak), nor the infant difficulty, weighed on its own page (assessment: contested), appears in Rufinus's paragraph — he reports the daily-creation opinion without the verse that would later be attached to it.
