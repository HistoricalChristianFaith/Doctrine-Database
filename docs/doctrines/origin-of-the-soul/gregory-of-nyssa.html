Gregory of Nyssa on the origin of the soul

Dates: c. 395 · Wikipedia
Summary: Gregory of Nyssa is the Greek East's answer to the question of when the soul begins. In On the Making of Man he sets the two current opinions against each other — souls living in a society of their own before they fall into bodies, and souls formed after the clay figure was moulded — rejects both, and puts in their place a single beginning for soul and body together, on the model of a seed that already contains the whole plant. What he does not do is settle where the soul comes from. The formula he minted decided the timing and left the mechanism open, and that is how the East kept it.

He stands fourth on this timeline, and he is the first witness on it writing in Greek for a Greek church that had already had the argument once, in Origen's terms. The relevant chapters are xxviii and xxix of a treatise Gregory wrote to complete his brother Basil's unfinished work on the six days of creation.1

The question as the churches were putting it

Gregory opens by naming the dispute as a live one and stating both sides without caricature. The first is pre-existence, and he describes it in the language of a settled society with its own moral order:

Some of those before our time who have dealt with the question of principles think it right to say that souls have a previous existence as a people in a society of their own, and that among them also there are standards of vice and of virtue, and that the soul there, which abides in goodness, remains without experience of conjunction with the body; but if it does depart from its communion with good, it falls down to this lower life, and so comes to be in a body.

— On the Making of Man, xxviii.1, source. Nobody is named. But “those … who have dealt with the question of principles” is as close to a citation as the chapter comes: it points at the treatise On First Principles, and the doctrine described — equality before the fall, descent by cooling from the good, embodiment as the consequence — is Origen's, in Origen's own order.2

The second opinion is the literalist reading of Genesis 2:7 — God made the clay figure first and breathed into it afterwards — and Gregory presses it to its conclusion in order to show what it costs:

Others, on the contrary, marking the order of the making of man as stated by Moses, say, that the soul is second to the body in order of time, since God first took dust from the earth and formed man, and then animated the being thus formed by His breath: and by this argument they prove that the flesh is more noble than the soul; that which was previously formed than that which was afterwards infused into it: for they say that the soul was made for the body, that the thing formed might not be without breath and motion.

— On the Making of Man, xxviii.1, source. The argument he is reporting is a good one on its own premises: what exists for the sake of something else is the lesser thing, as raiment is less than the body it clothes. Gregory does not answer the inference; he refuses the premise that gives it purchase, which is that either part came first at all.3

Against pre-existence: the slide into transmigration

His case against the first opinion is not that it is impious but that it cannot stop. Once a soul can fall from a higher state into a body by wickedness, the same logic keeps working: the embodied life offers more occasions of sin, so the next descent is lower still, into the brute, then the insensible, then the inanimate, and finally into nothing — while the return journey the doctrine also requires would have to make the life of a bush better than the incorporeal state it fell from. Along the way he pauses to say what he thinks of the pagan metempsychosis-tales the doctrine keeps company with:

They tell us that one of their sages said that he, being one and the same person, was born a man, and afterwards assumed the form of a woman, and flew about with the birds, and grew as a bush, and obtained the life of an aquatic creature — and he who said these things of himself did not, so far as I can judge, go far from the truth: for such doctrines as this of saying that one soul passed through so many changes are really fitting for the chatter of frogs or jackdaws, or the stupidity of fishes, or the insensibility of trees.

— On the Making of Man, xxviii.3, source. The joke is that Pythagoras's alleged autobiography is a fair description of the argument. It is also, read strictly, an argument by association: Origen's fall of minds is not the transmigration of Empedocles, and Gregory himself has to work to derive the one from the other through several steps. The derivation is the substance; the jibe is the ornament.4

Neither first: one man, one beginning

Having disposed of both, he states the middle position in the sentence that would outlive everything else in the chapter:

The doctrine of both, then, is equally to be rejected; but I think that we ought to direct our own doctrine in the way of truth between these theories.

— On the Making of Man, xxviii.8, source. The positive doctrine follows in the next chapter, and its argument is from the unity of the man rather than from any verse. A human being is one thing made of two; if the two have different starting-dates then the one thing is older than itself:

Nor again are we in our doctrine to begin by making up man like a clay figure, and to say that the soul came into being for the sake of this; for surely in that case the intellectual nature would be shown to be less precious than the clay figure. But as man is one, the being consisting of soul and body, we are to suppose that the beginning of his existence is one, common to both parts, so that he should not be found to be antecedent and posterior to himself, if the bodily element were first in point of time, and the other were a later addition.

— On the Making of Man, xxix.1, source. A second argument is added from the maker's side: a God who had to do the halves in turn would be “in some way imperfect, as not being completely sufficient for the whole task at once.”5

The wheat-grain

The image that carries the doctrine is agricultural, and it does two jobs at once — it explains how the soul can be present from the first without being visible, and it rules out any later arrival:

But just as we say that in wheat, or in any other grain, the whole form of the plant is potentially included — the leaves, the stalk, the joints, the grain, the beard — and do not say in our account of its nature that any of these things has pre-existence, or comes into being before the others, but that the power abiding in the seed is manifested in a certain natural order, not by any means that another nature is infused into it — in the same way we suppose the human germ to possess the potentiality of its nature, sown with it at the first start of its existence, and that it is unfolded and manifested by a natural sequence as it proceeds to its perfect state … so that it is not true to say either that the soul exists before the body, or that the body exists without the soul, but that there is one beginning of both.

— On the Making of Man, xxix.3, source. The clause worth pausing on is “not by any means that another nature is infused into it.” In context it answers the body-first opinion; but a reader coming to it from the later Latin argument will notice that it also excludes, in terms, the picture of a soul made elsewhere and put into a formed body. Gregory develops the point in the same chapter: the powers of the soul are there from the beginning and grow with the instrument, nutrition first, then sensibility, then reason “like a fruit” — not because a new thing has arrived, but because the recipient has grown large enough to show what was in it.6

Nothing dead comes out of a living body

Then comes the argument that most resembles the Latin traducians', and it is the reason Gregory is sometimes filed with them:

For since it is not from a dead body that the potentiality for conception is secreted, but from one which is animate and alive, we hence affirm that it is reasonable that we should not suppose that what is sent forth from a living body to be the occasion of life is itself dead and inanimate; for in the flesh that which is inanimate is surely dead; and the condition of death arises by the withdrawal of the soul. Would not one therefore in this case be asserting that withdrawal is antecedent to possession — if, that is, he should maintain that the inanimate state which is the condition of death is antecedent to the soul?

— On the Making of Man, xxix.5, source. He backs it with an empirical test — what is warm and in motion is alive, what is chill and motionless is dead, and the thing in question is warm and operative — and with a second horticultural case: kernels and torn-off roots put into the ground are “not deprived by death of the vital power which naturally resides in them,” and the earth does not implant that power from outside but only feeds it, “for surely then even dead wood would proceed to growth.”7

What Gregory settled, and what he left open

The structure of that last argument is Tertullian's. Both men reason from a living seed to a living product, both refuse to let soul and body come apart in time, and both make simultaneity at conception the load-bearing claim. Two differences matter, and they are the whole difference between a formula the East could keep and a theory the West would condemn.

The first is that Gregory draws no conclusion about the soul's source. Tertullian's living seed carries a shoot of Adam's soul, so that the race is one stock; Gregory's living seed carries a potentiality that unfolds, and he never says whose soul it is a portion of, or that it is a portion of anyone's. His own framing of where the whole thing comes from is not a doctrine of transmission at all but of the divine foreknowledge in which “all the fullness of human nature had pre-existence” — a claim about the race as God foresees it, not about what passes from parent to child. So the question the Latins were arguing about is not answered here; it is not asked.8

The second is that nothing in Gregory requires a bodily soul. Tertullian bought his transmission at the price of a soul with body, form, and dimension, because only such a thing can be carried in a seed. Gregory's seed does not carry a soul; it carries a power that is manifested in order, and the analogy that governs the whole account is between an unformed embryo and an unformed plant, both of which contain their futures without containing them as parts laid out in advance. That is why the simultaneity survived the theory that first produced it: it could be detached from the materialism.

The detachment was not obvious to everyone. The standard modern reference account lists Gregory — with Macarius, Rufinus, and Nemesius — among those who “seem to prefer” the generationist side, while conceding that their views “are not always clear.” The concession is the right one. What can be said firmly is that Gregory's premises point away from a soul infused into an already-formed body, and that he supplies no premise at all for a soul propagated from a parent's soul. A reader who wants him for either camp has to supply something he does not say.9

Three and a half centuries later John of Damascus compressed the chapter into a clause of the East's standard handbook — body and soul formed “at one and the same time, not first the one and then the other, as Origen so senselessly supposes” — keeping Gregory's timing and Gregory's named opponent, and, like Gregory, saying nothing about where souls come from.10

Notes

  • Gregory's is the only entry on this timeline that answers a question the Latin debate barely asked, and declines the one it was actually asking. Read as a contribution to the traducianism-versus-creationism dispute he looks evasive; read on his own terms he is answering the dispute that was live in front of him, in which the rival to Origen was not Tertullian but a literalism about the order of Genesis 2:7.
  • Open tension: xxix.3 says that in the human germ no other nature “is infused into it,” and xxix.1 grounds the whole race's pre-existence in God's foreknowledge rather than in any generative transmission. Taken together these are hard to combine with a soul individually created and joined to a formed body — but Gregory never confronts creationism as a stated position, and it is not safe to treat a rejection he did not make as one he would have made.
  • The chapters are also an argument about embryology, and their force depends on an ancient view of generation in which the seed is a portion secreted from a living body. The theological conclusion — one beginning for the whole man — does not stand or fall with that biology, but the argument from the living seed does.
  • Gregory's target here is Origen, whose pre-existence doctrine Justinian would put under anathema in 543. The half of Tertullian that Gregory preserves — simultaneity — is the half that John of Damascus made standard; the half he does not touch is the one Aquinas would call heretical.
  • Neither the John 5:17 proof-text for daily creation, weighed on its own page (assessment: weak), nor the difficulty about unbaptised infants, weighed on its own page (assessment: contested), plays any part in Gregory's argument — the first because he needs no verse, the second because the Pelagian controversy that made it urgent lay ahead of him in the West.
  • For the rest of the witnesses see the summary timeline.
