Pope Anastasius II on the origin of the soul

Dates: 498 · Wikipedia
Summary: Anastasius II is the first bishop of Rome to write against traducianism. His letter Bonum atque iucundum of 23 August 498, addressed to the bishops of Gaul, describes people there who reason that parents give their children souls as they give them bodies, calls the reasoning carnal, and answers it from the verse the Latin creationists had been using for a century — the Father who “works up to this time.” What the letter is not is a definition. It is a pope's charge to a regional episcopate, it survives in the standard collection only in heavily elided excerpt, and its evidential value is at least as much historical as doctrinal: by 498 traducianism in Gaul was something Rome would write to suppress.

He stands tenth on this timeline and is the first entry on it who is not arguing as a theologian but ruling as a bishop. Everything before him — Tertullian's vine-shoot, Lactantius's denial, Jerome's proof-text, Augustine's forty years of not knowing — is argument. This is correspondence from the Roman see telling a province what to stop saying.1

The heretics in Gaul

The letter opens the subject by describing the position it means to condemn, and the description is worth reading closely, because it is the only picture we have of what fifth-century Gallic traducianism actually claimed:

… Certain heretics in Gaul think that by a rational assertion they are persuaded of this, that just as the parents transmit bodies to the human race from material dregs, so also they bestow the vital principle of the living souls. …

— Bonum atque iucundum, as excerpted in Denzinger, source. Two features of the sentence are significant. The first is “by a rational assertion”: the pope concedes that his opponents are reasoning, not quoting. That is exactly right as a description of the tradition running from Tertullian — whose case rested on family likeness of character and on the symmetry of conception and death, not on any verse. The second is the parallelism “just as … so also.” The traducianist argument in Gaul was the argument from a single generative act: whatever accounts for the body must account for the soul, since one act produces one child.2

What the letter does not do is name anyone. There is no Gallic traducianist we can put a face to here — no bishop, no book, no council. The nearest contemporary witness to the same milieu is Gennadius of Massilia, writing in Gaul a generation earlier, who lists the error alongside Origen's and attributes it to “the Luciferians, Cyril, and certain presumptuous Latins.” Between Gennadius's handbook and this letter, the picture is of a live southern-Gallic dispute in which the creationist side had the ecclesiastical initiative.3

The argument: a work that has not ceased

The refutation is short, and it is the same one Jerome had made:

… How (therefore) do they, contrary to God's will, with a very carnal mind think that the soul made to the image of God is diffused and insinuated by the mixture of human beings, when that very action by Him, who did this in the beginning, has not ceased even today, just as He Himself said: My Father works up to this time, and I work? …

— Bonum atque iucundum, as excerpted in Denzinger, source. Two moves are packed into one sentence. The first is an appeal to the image of God: a soul made to that image is not the sort of thing that gets “diffused and insinuated” by a bodily mixture — the objection is to the indignity of the mechanism, and it anticipates in rhetorical form the metaphysical objection Aquinas would make eight centuries later with the vocabulary of subsistence. The second is the proof-text, John 5:17, deployed exactly as Jerome deployed it: the Father's work did not stop at the end of the six days, therefore He is still making something, therefore He is still making souls.4

The letter then anticipates the obvious objection to its own use of the verse — that Genesis has God resting on the seventh day — and answers it with a distinction that is more careful than the polemic around it. God established every creature “by single species” potentially and causally in the simultaneous work of creation, from which He rested; He operates visibly now in the work that belongs to the passage of time. The conclusion follows in the form of a positive doctrine:

… let the sound doctrines then rest, namely, that He, who calls those, which are not, just as those that are, imparts souls.

— Bonum atque iucundum, as excerpted in Denzinger, source. The clause borrowed at the end is Romans 4:17, God “who calleth those things which be not as though they were” — that is, the God whose characteristic act is creation out of nothing. That is the creationist thesis in its strict sense: not that God arranges or supervises the arrival of a soul, but that He originates it.5

Guilt without soul-stock

The collection files the excerpt under the heading “The Origin of Souls and Original Sin,” and the second half shows why. The traducianists of Gaul were evidently arguing what Augustine had felt the force of: that souls must come from the parents because sin does. Anastasius grants the premise about sin and denies the inference:

… they ought to be separated from them by this wise sundering, because nothing else can be transmitted by them than what has been brought to pass by their own evil presumption, that is, guilt and the punishment of sin …

— Bonum atque iucundum, as excerpted in Denzinger, source. The “wise sundering” is the whole answer in two words: what parents hand on is guilt and its penalty, not substance. Whatever descends by the vine-branch — the Latin image the letter keeps using, and the same image that gives traducianism its name — descends as inherited liability, so that men “are born vicious and distorted,” while God has “no communion” in the wrong.

This is the shape the answer would keep. It is not a demonstration; it is a refusal to let the transmission of guilt carry the transmission of substance with it, and it leaves entirely open how a newly created soul comes to owe a debt it did not incur. That is the gap Augustine had been staring into for decades and that the infant-damnation argument weighs. Anastasius does not close it. He asserts that it can be closed — which, in a disciplinary letter, is all he is trying to do.6

What the letter is, and what it is not

Three limits should be kept in view, and none of them is a reason to discount the letter.

It is a letter, not a definition. Bonum atque iucundum is addressed to the bishops of a province and deals with an error current there. No council took it up; no creed embodies it. The standard reference account of the whole question, which cites this very letter as evidence that Rome opposed the doctrine, is also the account that concedes the absence of any conciliar sentence: “although there are no strict definitions condemning Generationism as heretical, it is certainly opposed to the doctrine of the Church, and could not be held without temerity.” That is a fair summary of what a document like this establishes — a settled Roman attitude, not a defined dogma.7

It survives in excerpt. The text quoted above is not the letter; it is what a dogmatic collection preserved of it, and the ellipses in the passages above are the collection's own. We are reading the sentences a later compiler thought doctrinally load-bearing, arranged for that purpose. The critical edition of the Latin is Thiel's, from the manuscript tradition of the papal letters; the connective tissue between the excerpted sentences — who exactly was teaching this, and what the bishops of Gaul were being told to do about it — is not in the excerpt.8

Its argument is borrowed, and the borrowed argument is weak. The single verse the letter leans on does not do the work asked of it. Its own setting is a sabbath controversy about a healing, and the “work” Jesus claims to share is the Father's unceasing providential upholding of everything — which a traducianist affirms as readily as a creationist, since on his view God upholds the process by which souls propagate. The verse is compatible with the pope's conclusion and selects it over nothing. That is weighed on its own page; the conclusion may be true regardless, and nothing here turns on the verse being the only thing the tradition had.9

A tag that is not his

One correction belongs on this page because the confusion is common. The Latin tag usually quoted in this connection — quotidie Deus fabricatur animas, “God fashions souls daily” — is not Anastasius's. It is Jerome's, from the tract to Pammachius against John of Jerusalem, written about a century before this letter, where it appears in precisely the same argumentative slot:

Surely (as the Church teaches in accordance with the Saviour's words, My Father works hitherto and I work; and the passage in Isaiah, Who makes the spirit of man in him; and in the Psalms, Who fashions one by one the hearts of them) God is daily making souls— He, with whom to will is to do, and who never ceases to be a Creator.

— Jerome, To Pammachius Against John of Jerusalem 22, source. The resemblance to the papal letter is not a coincidence and is not plagiarism either: it is one argument, with one proof-text, in general Latin circulation. But the sentence belongs to Jerome, and attributing it to the pope credits a private controversialist's formula to the Roman see.10

The pope's own reputation

A curiosity, unrelated to the soul but worth flagging because it colours how this letter has been read. Anastasius II spent his twenty-three months in office trying to end the Acacian schism, was willing to recognise the sacraments conferred by the excommunicated patriarch of Constantinople, and was reported to have given communion to one of Acacius's associates. This outraged a large part of the Roman clergy; when he died suddenly in 498, his opponents read it as a judgement, and a medieval tradition grew up in which he was a heretic and a traitor — a tradition that reaches its most famous form in Dante, who puts a Pope Anastasius in the sixth circle of hell among the heresiarchs. Modern commentators regard that verdict as a confusion, most likely of the pope with the emperor of the same name; and he remains, with Liberius, one of only two of the first fifty bishops of Rome not canonised.11

None of this bears on the letter. The traducianist question had nothing to do with the Acacian quarrel, and no ancient or medieval critic of Anastasius attacked him over the origin of the soul. But it is a useful reminder that the first papal intervention in this debate came from a pontificate that the next several centuries remembered for something else entirely.

Notes

  • The evidential weight of this entry runs in two directions at once. As doctrine it is thin — a regional letter, preserved in fragments, resting on a verse that does not carry the conclusion. As history it is solid: the letter is unambiguous evidence that in 498 there were people in Gaul teaching that parents give souls, and that Rome regarded the teaching as an error requiring correspondence. The doctrinal thinness and the historical firmness are independent, and it is a mistake to let either one govern the reading of the other.
  • Open tension: the letter concedes that guilt and punishment pass from parents to children while denying that substance does. That is a coherent position, but the letter does not say how a soul that God has just created out of nothing comes to stand under an inherited liability. The scholastic answer — that the soul contracts Adam's guilt through its union with flesh descended from Adam — is Aquinas's, seven and a half centuries later; see the argument page (assessment: contested).
  • What the letter tells us about the state of the question in the West is more interesting than its argument. Jerome in 411 had reported traducianism as the view of “the majority of the Western divines”; Gennadius around 470 wrote it out of the church's teaching; by 498 a pope is telling Gaul to stop. Whatever the merits of the arguments, the direction of institutional travel over that century is not in doubt.
  • The doctrine condemned here is Tertullian's, but Tertullian is not named — neither in this letter nor, so far as the excerpt goes, anywhere in its reasoning. The Latin West condemned the theory without much engaging its author's actual case, which was an argument from inherited character and from the simultaneity of conception rather than from any of the premises the letter attacks.
  • The rival option that Anastasius does not mention is pre-existence. Forty-five years later Justinian would put that under anathema, after which the question in both halves of the church is a two-way one. For the rest of the witnesses see the summary timeline.
