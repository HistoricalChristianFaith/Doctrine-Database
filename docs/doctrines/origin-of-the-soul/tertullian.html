Tertullian on the origin of the soul

Dates: c. 220 · Wikipedia
Summary: Tertullian is the author of traducianism and its first sustained defender. In A Treatise on the Soul he argues that soul and body are conceived at the same instant, out of a single generative act, so that every human soul is a shoot from the one soul God breathed into Adam. His evidence is not a proof-text but an observation — children resemble their parents in character, not only in feature — and the price he pays for it is a soul that has body, form, and dimension: exactly the consequence his critics, down to Aquinas, would use against the theory.

He stands first on this timeline because he is where the Latin question begins. Nobody before him had put the alternatives in this form, and the treatise that does so is written against two opponents at once: the philosophers, who make the soul unborn and eternal, and those who have the soul arrive from outside at the moment of birth.1

Against Plato: the soul had a beginning

Tertullian's starting point is not the mechanism but the fact of a beginning. If the soul comes from God's breath, then it is a thing that started, and a thing that started is a creature, not a fragment of divinity:

After settling the origin of the soul, its condition or state comes up next. For when we acknowledge that the soul originates in the breath of God, it follows that we attribute a beginning to it. This Plato, indeed, refuses to assign to it, for he will have the soul to be unborn and unmade. We, however, from the very fact of its having had a beginning, as well as from the nature thereof, teach that it had both birth and creation.

— A Treatise on the Soul, ch. 4, source. The polemical target matters for reading everything that follows. Tertullian is arguing against the soul's pre-existence before he is arguing for its propagation, and the two moves are connected: a soul that is born cannot have had a career before its body. He spends two chapters on Plato's recollection-doctrine, objecting that a soul which can forget a previous life is not the divine, impassible thing Plato needs it to be, and setting his own estimate against it — the soul is far below God, “the breath (of God), though not His Spirit.”2

We, however, who allow no appendage to God (in the sense of equality), by this very fact reckon the soul as very far below God: for we suppose it to be born, and hereby to possess something of a diluted divinity and an attenuated felicity, as the breath (of God), though not His spirit…

— A Treatise on the Soul, ch. 24, source. Three centuries later an emperor would anathematize the doctrine Tertullian is here refuting; the position Origen built on it was not yet on the table when Tertullian wrote.3

Not a moment's interval: soul and body conceived together

The other rival is the view that the soul is inhaled at birth — the Stoic position, with Plato's assent, that the infant receives its soul with its first breath of cold air. Tertullian states it fairly and then buries it:

… such of them as begin by maintaining that the soul is not conceived in the womb, nor is formed and produced at the time that the flesh is moulded, but is impressed from without upon the infant before his complete vitality, but after the process of parturition.

— A Treatise on the Soul, ch. 25, source. His refutation is empirical and, for a second-century writer, strikingly plain: he calls mothers as witnesses. The child kicks, turns, is quieted and disturbed, shares its mother's cravings and even her bruises; stillbirths happen, and nothing can die that has not lived. He also notices that the whole theory hangs on a pun — that the Greek psychē was thought to name a cooling, so that the soul is what the cold air imparts — and answers it by geography: peoples born under a torrid sun have souls too, and nobody's wits are sharper for being born in Scythia.4 (The same etymology, taken in an entirely different direction, is the hinge of Origen's account.)

The positive thesis follows in the chapter whose heading states it flatly — “Soul and Body Conceived, Formed and Perfected in Element Simultaneously”:

How, then, is a living being conceived? Is the substance of both body and soul formed together at one and the same time? Or does one of them precede the other in natural formation? We indeed maintain that both are conceived, and formed, and perfectly simultaneously, as well as born together; and that not a moment's interval occurs in their conception, so that, a prior place can be assigned to either.

— A Treatise on the Soul, ch. 27, source. The argument for it is symmetry with death. If death is by definition the separation of soul and body, then life is their conjunction; and since the separation is simultaneous, so must the conjunction be. He grants that there are two seeds, but refuses to let them come apart in time: “although we shall allow that there are two kinds of seed — that of the body and that of the soul — we still declare that they are inseparable, and therefore contemporaneous and simultaneous in origin.”5 This half of Tertullian — the simultaneity — outlived the other half by centuries: it is what Gregory of Nyssa and, after him, John of Damascus kept while dropping the propagation.

A sprout from Adam's root

Given simultaneity, the mechanism follows. If the soul begins when the body begins, and the body comes from the parents, the soul comes the same way — and the whole race is one growth from one stock:

Even the infancy of a log, then, may have an intellect (suitable to it): how much more may that of a human being, whose soul (which may be compared with the nascent sprout of a tree) has been derived from Adam as its root, and has been propagated among his posterity by means of woman, to whom it has been entrusted for transmission, and thus has sprouted into life with all its natural apparatus, both of intellect and of sense!

— A Treatise on the Soul, ch. 19, source. The context is an argument that infants are not mindless: trees have their proper vitality while still twigs, the young vine knows to reach for a prop, and so the newborn's cry, its recognition of its mother, its refusal of a stranger's breast are evidence of an intellect present from the start rather than acquired later. The botanical image is not decoration. The Latin word for the doctrine, tradux, is the vine-shoot layered into the ground to raise a new plant from the old — and it is the vine, not a syllogism, that carries the argument.6

Tertullian then grounds the picture in Genesis. The two ingredients of the first man — the clay and the breath — are, he says, the archetypes of the two seeds, and the propagation of the race is simply their continued joint flow:

Most true are the examples of the first creation. Adam's flesh was formed of clay. Now what is clay but an excellent moisture, whence should spring the generating fluid? From the breath of God first came the soul. But what else is the breath of God than the vapour of the spirit, whence should spring that which we breathe out through the generative fluid? Forasmuch, therefore, as these two different and separate substances, the clay and the breath, combined at the first creation in forming the individual man, they then both amalgamated and mixed their proper seminal rudiments in one, and ever afterwards communicated to the human race the normal mode of its propagation…

Accordingly from the one (primeval) man comes the entire outflow and redundance of men's souls — nature proving herself true to the commandment of God, Be fruitful, and multiply.

— A Treatise on the Soul, ch. 27, source. Two things are worth marking here. First, this is an exegesis of Genesis 2:7 that has to be extended by analogy: the text says what God did once, and Tertullian reads the once-for-all act as the pattern of an ongoing natural process. His opponents read the same verse as the pattern of a repeated divine act. Neither reading is compelled by the text, which is why the debate ran for a millennium without a decisive verse on either side. Second, he is candid that the argument requires him to talk about the mechanics of generation, and says so: “Nature should be to us an object of reverence, not of blushes.”7

The evidence: children inherit character, not only features

The one piece of positive evidence Tertullian offers, and the one his successors kept repeating, is family likeness of the wrong kind — likeness in temper and disposition, which is a likeness of soul:

Pray, whence comes it that from similarity of soul we resemble our parents in disposition, according to the testimony of Cleanthes, if we are not produced from this seed of the soul?

— A Treatise on the Soul, ch. 25, source. The observation is sound and the inference is not obviously wrong; something is being handed down, and it is not only bone-structure. But the source Tertullian cites shows what the argument was originally for. Cleanthes the Stoic used family likeness of character to prove that the soul is a body, since only bodies can resemble and differ from one another — and Tertullian reproduces that inference approvingly, in the very chapter where he enlists the Stoics against Plato:

Cleanthes, too, will have it that family likeness passes from parents to their children not merely in bodily features, but in characteristics of the soul; as if it were out of a mirror of (a man's) manners, and faculties, and affections, that bodily likeness and unlikeness are caught and reflected by the soul also. It is therefore as being corporeal that it is susceptible of likeness and unlikeness.

— A Treatise on the Soul, ch. 5, source. The likeness argument and the corporeality thesis are, in Tertullian's hands, one argument. That is the honest statement of what his traducianism costs.8

The cost: a soul with body, form, and dimension

Tertullian does not hide the consequence; he asserts it. His formal definition of the soul places “possessing body” and “having form” among its properties, alongside immortality and freedom, and he ascribes to it the three dimensions by which philosophers measure any body:

The soul, then, we define to be sprung from the breath of God, immortal, possessing body, having form, simple in its substance, intelligent in its own nature, developing its power in various ways, free in its determinations, subject to be changes of accident, in its faculties mutable, rational, supreme, endued with an instinct of presentiment, evolved out of one (archetypal soul).

— A Treatise on the Soul, ch. 22, source. “Evolved out of one” is the traducianism; “possessing body, having form” is what pays for it. The standard modern account of the doctrine makes the linkage explicit — the theory's author is Tertullian, and “with Tertullian this theory was connected with a materializing view of the soul.”9

That linkage is what eventually killed the theory in the Latin West, and it was killed with Tertullian's own logic run backwards. Aquinas argued that a subsistent immaterial soul cannot be produced by any power acting in matter, and drew the conclusion Tertullian had accepted from the other end: to hold that the soul is caused by the begetter “is nothing else than to hold the soul to be non-subsistent and consequently to perish with the body. It is therefore heretical to say that the intellectual soul is transmitted with the semen.” Tertullian would have agreed with the first clause of the reasoning and denied the premise: he thought a soul could be bodily and immortal at once.10

Eve's soul from Adam — and Ambrose's denial

The most exposed application of the theory is Eve. If souls pass with the flesh, and Eve's flesh came out of Adam, then Eve's soul came out of Adam too — and God's separate in-breathing was not needed for her:

So that her flesh was for a long time without specific form (such as she afterwards assumed when taken out of Adam's side); but she was even then herself a living being, because I should regard her at that time in soul as even a portion of Adam. Besides, God's afflatus would have animated her too, if there had not been in the woman a transmission from Adam of his soul also as well as of his flesh.

— A Treatise on the Soul, ch. 36, source. The reasoning is an argument from silence — Genesis records no second in-breathing — and it is the tightest form of the claim that the race is one soul-stock. It is also the point at which the tradition turned against him first. Ambrose of Milan, expounding the same verses, draws exactly the opposite conclusion from the same silence: what was taken from Adam was a rib and not a portion of his soul.

Considera quia ex corpore costam sumpsit, non ex anima portionem; hoc est, non anima ex anima, sed os de ossibus meis et caro de carne mea haec vocabitur mulier.

— Ambrose, De paradiso xi.50, Latin text: “Consider that he took a rib from the body, not a portion from the soul; that is, not soul from soul, but bone from my bones and flesh from my flesh — she shall be called woman.” Neither writer has more of Genesis than the other; each is reading the narrative's silence in the direction his doctrine of the soul already runs. What the pair shows is how early the Latin tradition had a ready answer to Tertullian's strongest single case.11

Notes

  • Tertullian never argues the point that later made traducianism attractive — the transmission of original sin — as a reason for the theory. That leverage is applied afterwards, above all by Augustine, who could not accept creationism precisely because he could not see how a freshly made soul contracts Adam's guilt. The difficulty is weighed on its own page (assessment: contested).
  • Nothing in Tertullian's case is a proof-text. The creationist side eventually acquired one — John 5:17, “my Father worketh hitherto” — which is weighed on its own page (assessment: weak). The asymmetry is apparent rather than real: both sides argued from inference, and only one of them dressed the inference in a verse.
  • The half of Tertullian that survived is the simultaneity of soul and body at conception, which Gregory of Nyssa restated without the material soul and John of Damascus fixed as an Eastern formula. The half that did not survive is the propagation, condemned in effect by Anastasius II and in words by Aquinas.
  • Open tension: Tertullian's corporeal soul is not obviously the same thesis as a soul made of the stuff of the body. He insists the soul's body is “of a quality and kind peculiar to itself,” which may be nearer to a doctrine of a fine spiritual substance than to materialism in the modern sense — and if so, Aquinas's objection lands on the letter of his definition without quite reaching his intent. He nowhere works the distinction out. Whether the corporealism is therefore crude materialism or a defensible substance-realism against Platonic and Gnostic immaterialism is weighed on its own argument page (assessment: contested), under the timeline on whether Tertullian was a heretic.
  • Tertullian appears on this wiki also as a witness on the Nephilim timeline, the intermediate state, and infant baptism — where his argument for delaying baptism sits oddly beside the doctrine of inherited soul-stock developed here. See the summary timeline for the rest of the witnesses.
