Thomas Aquinas on the origin of the soul

Dates: c. 1270 · Wikipedia
Summary: Aquinas closes the Latin question, and closes it with a metaphysical argument rather than a proof-text. Because the intellectual soul has an operation the body takes no part in, it is subsistent; a subsistent immaterial thing cannot be produced by any power working in matter; therefore it “cannot be caused through generation, but only through creation by God,” and to say otherwise “is nothing else than to hold the soul to be non-subsistent and consequently to perish with the body.” Hence his verdict: “It is therefore heretical to say that the intellectual soul is transmitted with the semen.” The nuance usually lost is that the article immediately before this one holds the sensitive soul is transmitted with the semen. The split is deliberate, and it is where the whole argument lives.

He stands last on this timeline, a thousand years after Tertullian opened it. Question 118 of the Prima Pars, “The production of man from man as to the soul,” puts the whole matter in three articles: is the sensitive soul transmitted with the semen; is the intellectual soul thus transmitted; were all souls created at the same time. The three answers, taken together, dispose of traducianism, of pre-existence, and of the loose sense in which people had been saying that souls come from parents — and they do it without appealing to a single verse as evidence.1

Article 1: the sensitive soul is transmitted

The first article grants the traducianists something substantial, and the concession is what makes the second article's denial precise rather than merely dogmatic. Some, Aquinas notes, have held that the sensitive souls of animals are created by God. That would follow if the sensitive soul were subsistent — but it is not:

But this principle is false—namely, that being and operation are proper to the sensitive soul, as has been made clear above: for it would not cease to exist when the body perishes. Since, therefore, it is not a subsistent form, its relation to existence is that of the corporeal forms, to which existence does not belong as proper to them, but which are said to exist forasmuch as the subsistent composites exist through them.

— Summa Theologiae I q.118 a.1, source. Non-subsistent forms are made when their composites are made, and composites are made by generation. So the sensitive soul is produced by the ordinary generative power, and the semen carries a force derived from the parent's soul as an instrument carries motion from the hand that swings it:

… the semen of the animal or plant derives a certain active force from the soul of the generator, just as the instrument derives a certain motive power from the principal agent. And as it matters not whether we say that something is moved by the instrument or by the principal agent, so neither does it matter whether we say that the soul of the generated is caused by the soul of the generator, or by some seminal power derived therefrom.

— Summa Theologiae I q.118 a.1, source. Read on its own, that is traducianism — soul from soul, by way of the seed. Aquinas is careful that it is not the soul itself that travels: the active force in the semen “is not the soul or a part of the soul, save virtually,” as the form of a bed is not in the saw. But within its own order the mechanism is exactly the one Tertullian described.2

Article 2: the intellectual soul is not

The second article withholds from the intellectual soul precisely what the first granted, and the reason is the one property the sensitive soul was found to lack. The respondeo gives three arguments in sequence. The first is about causal proportion:

It is impossible for an active power existing in matter to extend its action to the production of an immaterial effect. Now it is manifest that the intellectual principle in man transcends matter; for it has an operation in which the body takes no part whatever. It is therefore impossible for the seminal power to produce the intellectual principle.

— Summa Theologiae I q.118 a.2, source. The second observes that the seminal power acts by virtue of the parent's soul precisely as that soul is the act of a body using a body — and the intellect is the one operation in which the body does nothing. So the power of the intellectual principle, as intellectual, cannot reach the semen at all; whence the tag Aquinas takes from Aristotle's De Generatione Animalium: “It follows that the intellect alone comes from without.” The third argument is the one that carries the verdict:

Again, since the intellectual soul has an operation independent of the body, it is subsistent, as proved above: therefore to be and to be made are proper to it. Moreover, since it is an immaterial substance it cannot be caused through generation, but only through creation by God. Therefore to hold that the intellectual soul is caused by the begetter, is nothing else than to hold the soul to be non-subsistent and consequently to perish with the body. It is therefore heretical to say that the intellectual soul is transmitted with the semen.

— Summa Theologiae I q.118 a.2, source. The structure of the charge deserves attention. Traducianism is not condemned for contradicting a defined doctrine of the soul's origin — there is none. It is condemned as entailing the mortality of the soul, which is a defined doctrine. The heresy is downstream: hold the transmission and you have made the soul the sort of thing that perishes with what carried it. That is a real entailment on Aquinas's premises, and it is why he can reach a censure without a canon.3

The conclusion of the article's long reply to the second objection puts the positive doctrine in a form that also answers John of Damascus's simultaneity without meaning to: through a succession of generations and corruptions the embryo passes from nutritive to sensitive form, and then “the intellectual soul is created by God at the end of human generation, and this soul is at the same time sensitive and nutritive, the pre-existing forms being corrupted.” Aquinas's soul is not present from the first instant. It arrives, and what it replaces was produced by the parents.4

Tertullian's inference, run backwards

The relation between the first and last entries on this timeline is worth stating exactly, because they share an inference and differ over which end of it to hold.

Tertullian wanted the transmission — the race as one soul-stock descending from Adam — and saw that to be carried in a seed the soul must be the sort of thing a seed can carry. So he accepted the consequence and wrote it into his definition: the soul is “immortal, possessing body, having form … evolved out of one (archetypal soul).” The corporeality was the price, and he paid it openly.

Aquinas holds the same conditional and denies the consequent. An immaterial, subsistent soul cannot be produced by a bodily power; therefore it is not transmitted. He is not refuting Tertullian's logic; he is running it in the other direction, having settled first what a soul is. The two men would have agreed that traducianism requires a quasi-bodily soul. They disagreed about whether a soul can be quasi-bodily and immortal at once — and it is that disagreement, not any exegetical one, that ends the Latin debate.5

The authority in the sed contra

The authority Aquinas cites against traducianism is a single sentence:

On the contrary, It is written in De Eccl. Dogmat. xiv that “the rational soul is not engendered by coition.”

— Summa Theologiae I q.118 a.2, source. The work is the De ecclesiasticis dogmatibus, the fifth-century handbook of Gennadius of Massilia — which circulated for centuries in the Augustine volume of the Latin fathers and was widely read as Augustine's. Aquinas cites it again in the very next article, for the proposition that “the soul is created together with the body.” It is his only patristic authority in either place.

The irony is worth registering plainly and without exaggeration. Augustine is the one Latin father who cared most about this question and the one who refused to settle it — confessing his perplexity to Jerome in 415, still confessing it against Vincentius Victor, and writing at the end of his life that as to the soul's origin “I did not know then, and I still do not know.” The Latin settlement nonetheless leaned on a flat creationist canon that stood under his name in the manuscripts. It would be too much to say that the settlement was caused by the misattribution; Aquinas's argument does not depend on the authority, and he plainly regards the metaphysics as decisive. But the pattern — Augustine's permanent hesitation and a canon he never wrote circulating together as his — is a real feature of how the West read its own tradition, and Peter Lombard had already cited the work from the Augustine volume when settling the question in the Sentences.6

How precise is “heretical”?

Aquinas's word is haereticum, and it is his own theological censure, not the citation of a conciliar sentence. No council ever defined the origin of the soul. The standard reference account of the question, which gathers everything the Roman see ever said on the subject — Anastasius II's letter to the bishops of Gaul, the profession Leo IX required of Peter of Antioch, the errors Benedict XII listed for the Armenians, the nineteenth-century condemnations of Froschammer and Ubaghs — ends with the concession that none of it amounts to a definition: “although there are no strict definitions condemning Generationism as heretical, it is certainly opposed to the doctrine of the Church, and could not be held without temerity.”7

That same account draws the distinction Aquinas's argument actually turns on. Traducianism in the strict sense is the materialist doctrine that the soul is transmitted by the organic process of generation; generationism is the doctrine that the offspring's soul originates from the parents' souls in some manner not reducible to that process. Corporeal traducianism “is heretical because it goes directly against the spirituality of the soul” — which is exactly Aquinas's reasoning — while generationism is “certainly opposed to the general attitude of the Church,” which is a weaker claim. Aquinas's article addresses the soul “transmitted with the semen,” the strict form; a generationist who denies that the soul travels in matter is not straightforwardly hit by the argument from the impotence of material powers, though he still owes an account of how an immaterial thing divides.

Nor was the scholastic settlement quite unanimous. Creationism was “held as certain by the Scholastics, with the exception of Hugh of Saint Victor and Alexander of Hales, who propose it merely as more probable.” Two of the more considerable figures of the century before Aquinas thought the question was one of probability, not certainty — which is a fair marker of how much of the West's confidence here is argument and how much is consolidated custom.8

Original sin without an inherited soul

The reason traducianism had survived so long in the West was not its evidence but its usefulness: it explained how Adam's guilt reaches a newborn. Deny the inherited soul and the difficulty that stopped Augustine returns — a soul freshly created by God, innocent by construction, condemned for a sin it did not commit. Aquinas answers it, and the answer is the piece the Latin creationist case had been missing for eight centuries.

He first clears away the two standard replies. The theory that the rational soul is transmitted so that “an infected soul would seem to produce other infected souls” he has already ruled out; the theory that a bodily corruption in the semen carries a defect into the soul, as a leper begets a leper, he rejects as insufficient — because a defect one contracts by origin looks like precisely the sort of thing that cannot be guilt, guilt being essentially voluntary. He is blunt that this objection would sink traducianism too:

Wherefore granted that the rational soul were transmitted, from the very fact that the stain on the child's soul is not in its will, it would cease to be a guilty stain binding its subject to punishment; for, as the Philosopher says, “no one reproaches a man born blind; one rather takes pity on him.”

— Summa Theologiae I-II q.81 a.1, source. The positive answer relocates the voluntariness. All men born of Adam are considered as one man by their common nature, as the members of a community are reckoned one body; the act of a hand is voluntary not by the hand's will but by the soul's; so the disorder in a man born of Adam “is voluntary, not by his will, but by the will of his first parent, who, by the movement of generation, moves all who originate from him.” The role of the semen then follows without any soul travelling in it:

Although the soul is not transmitted, because the power in the semen is not able to cause the rational soul, nevertheless the motion of the semen is a disposition to the transmission of the rational soul: so that the semen by its own power transmits the human nature from parent to child, and with that nature, the stain which infects it …

— Summa Theologiae I-II q.81 a.1 ad 2, source. What descends is nature, and guilt attaches to the nature; the individually created soul contracts it by being made the form of flesh descended from Adam. That is a coherent construction, and it became the settled scholastic answer. Whether it dissolves Augustine's difficulty or relocates it — and it does depend on a doctrine of inherited guilt that is itself disputed — is weighed on its own page (assessment: contested).9

Notes

  • The single most important thing on this page is the split between articles 1 and 2. Aquinas is not denying that parents generate a soul; he is denying that they generate this soul, the intellectual one, on the ground that it alone is subsistent. Quoting the heresy-charge without the concession makes him sound more sweeping than he is, and makes the argument look like an assertion rather than an inference from a doctrine of subsistence.
  • Open tension: the whole case rests on the claim that the intellect has an operation “in which the body takes no part whatever.” That is an Aristotelian thesis defended earlier in the Summa, not a datum of revelation, and it is doing all the work. A reader who does not grant it does not get the conclusion — and does not get the heresy-charge either, since the charge is that traducianism entails a mortal soul, an entailment that runs only through the same premise.
  • Notice what is absent. There is no proof-text for creationism in the respondeo; the one verse the Latin tradition had leaned on since Jerome — “my Father worketh hitherto” — appears in this question only in article 3, and there it appears in a reply to an objection, to explain how God can be said to rest and still be working. It is not adduced as evidence for anything. The argument that closed the question is philosophical, and the verse weighed on its own page (assessment: weak) had by then become ornamental.
  • Article 3 disposes of the third option on this timeline for the same reasons a Greek would have given, though without citing them: if it is natural to the soul to be united to a body, a soul made without one is deprived of its natural perfection, and God does not begin with the imperfect. Aquinas notes that embodiment as penalty “is in keeping with the opinion of Origen,” and concludes that souls “are created at the same time as they are infused into them” — which is where Justinian's anathema had left the matter seven centuries earlier.
  • For the rest of the witnesses see the summary timeline.
