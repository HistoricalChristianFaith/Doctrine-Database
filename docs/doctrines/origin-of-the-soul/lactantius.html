Lactantius on the origin of the soul

Dates: c. 325 · Wikipedia
Summary: Lactantius is the first Latin writer to state creationism explicitly and to argue for it. In On the Workmanship of God he holds that what the soul is may never be settled, but that where it comes from can be: not from the father, not from the mother, not from both, since nothing can be detached from a simple and imperceptible thing. Souls are therefore made by God alone. He agrees with Tertullian that the soul is present from conception and against the philosophers who have it inhaled at birth — an agreement on timing that later helped get him misfiled, by Rufinus, among the traducians.

The treatise is an argument from design: chapter after chapter on the structure of the eye, the hand, the voice, the viscera, each turned into evidence of a maker. The soul comes last, and it comes as the hardest case — the one part of man whose workmanship cannot be inspected. Lactantius stands third on this timeline, roughly a century after Tertullian, and he is answering him without naming him.1

What the soul is may never be known

Lactantius opens the subject by conceding the whole ground on which the philosophers had built:

It remains to speak of the soul, although its system and nature cannot be perceived… But what the soul is, is not yet agreed upon by philosophers, and perhaps will never be agreed upon. For some have said that it is blood, others that it is fire, others wind, from which it has received its name of anima, or animus, because in Greek the wind is called anemos—and yet none of these appears to have spoken anything.

— On the Workmanship of God, ch. 17, source. The three candidate materials are dispatched one by one, and the method is worth marking because it is the method he will use on the origin question too. He does not claim to know what the soul is; he claims to know what it is not, and to know that from features of the case that any observer can check. The soul is not blood, because a lamp is not oil: “they are plainly different, but the one is the nourishment of the other.” It is not fire, because fire is visible, insensate, and burns to the touch, and the soul is none of the three. It is not breath, because “the soul is produced much before air can be conceived in the mouth.”2

The last of those three refutations is the one that carries doctrinal weight, and it is a claim about when:

For it is not introduced into the body after birth, as it appears to some philosophers, but immediately after conception, when the divine necessity has formed the offspring in the womb; for it so lives within the bowels of its mother, that it is increased in growth, and delights to bound with repeated beatings. In short, there must be a miscarriage if the living young within shall die.

— On the Workmanship of God, ch. 17, source. The evidence is exactly Tertullian's: the child moves in the womb, and a child that dies there was alive there. So is the target — Varro's definition of the soul as “air conceived in the mouth, warmed in the lungs, heated in the heart, diffused into the body,” and behind it the Stoic doctrine of ensoulment at the first breath. On the timing of ensoulment the first traducian and the first Latin creationist are on the same side, and against the same opponents. That is a fact worth holding on to, because it is where the confusion about Lactantius's position starts.3

Souls come from neither parent, nor from both

On the origin itself Lactantius is not agnostic at all. He poses the question in exactly the form the later debate would take, and answers it by eliminating all three parental options at once:

A question also may arise respecting this, whether the soul is produced from the father, or rather from the mother, or indeed from both. But I think that this judgment is to be formed as though in a doubtful matter. For nothing is true of these three opinions, because souls are produced neither from both nor from either. For a body may be produced from a body, since something is contributed from both; but a soul cannot be produced from souls, because nothing can depart from a slight and incomprehensible subject. Therefore the manner of the production of souls belongs entirely to God alone.

— On the Workmanship of God, ch. 19, source. The load-bearing clause is the middle one, and it is an argument from the soul's simplicity: generation works by something being detached and contributed, and there is nothing to detach from a thing that has no parts and no bulk. Bodies can shed seed because bodies are quantities; a soul cannot, because it is not. Notice how much this concedes and how much it gains. It concedes that the mechanism of bodily inheritance is perfectly intelligible; it denies only that the soul is the sort of thing that mechanism can operate on. That is, in embryo, the argument Aquinas would make with the technical vocabulary of subsistence nine and a half centuries later — an immaterial subsistent thing cannot be produced by a power working in matter, so it can only be created. Lactantius has the intuition without the metaphysics, and he has it before Tertullian's theory had acquired any Latin defenders worth naming.4

The premise the argument needs is the soul's simplicity, and that premise is precisely what Tertullian denies: his soul possesses body, form, and the three dimensions, which is exactly what makes it the kind of thing a seed can carry. Neither writer engages the other. The disagreement about where souls come from is downstream of a disagreement about what kind of thing a soul is, and that is why the debate could not be settled by finding a better verse.

The parents' whole contribution

Lactantius then states the positive doctrine and draws its boundary around what human parenthood actually accomplishes:

From this it is evident that souls are not given by parents, but by one and the same God and Father of all, who alone has the law and method of their birth, since He alone produces them. For the part of the earthly parent is nothing more than with a sense of pleasure to emit the moisture of the body, in which is the material of birth, or to receive it; and to this work man's power is limited, nor has he any further power. Therefore men wish for the birth of sons, because they do not themselves bring it about. Everything beyond this is the work of God—namely, the conception itself, and the moulding of the body, and the breathing in of life, and the bringing forth in safety…

— On the Workmanship of God, ch. 19, source. Two supporting arguments are attached. The first is from ignorance as evidence: a father cannot be said to transmit what he does not know he is transmitting — he “in no way perceives that he has transmitted or breathed a soul from his own; nor, if he perceives it, comprehends in his mind when or in what manner that effect is produced.” The second is from the observed failure of inheritance in the very faculty at issue: God “gives to man wisdom, which no earthly father can by any means give; and therefore it often happens that foolish sons are born from wise parents, and wise sons from foolish parents.” Both are aimed at the traducian's best evidence. Tertullian's whole positive case was that children resemble their parents in disposition, so that something of the soul must be handed down; Lactantius answers with the opposite observation — that in the highest capacities of the soul, children conspicuously do not resemble their parents at all. Neither observation is decisive, and the two are compatible: temperament may run in families while wisdom does not. But it is the only direct collision between the two positions in this century, and Lactantius does not duck it.5

He seals the passage with a line of Lucretius, quoted approvingly against its own author's materialism — “we are all sprung from a heavenly seed, all have that same Father” — and with the gloss that “nothing but what is mortal can be generated from mortals.” The rhetorical instinct is characteristic: an apologist trained in rhetoric, writing for educated pagans, wins the point with a poet they already revere.6

Misfiled among the traducians

Around the year 400, defending himself before Pope Anastasius I, Rufinus of Aquileia set out the three views on the soul's origin in what is still the cleanest ancient statement of the division — and put Lactantius on the wrong side of it:

Some of those whom I have read hold that the soul is infused together with the material body through the channel of the human seed; and of this they give such proofs as they can. I think that this was the opinion of Tertullian or Lactantius among the Latins, perhaps also of a few others.

— Rufinus, Apology in Defence of Himself (to Pope Anastasius I) §6, source. On Tertullian the attribution is exactly right. On Lactantius it is wrong, and wrong about the one chapter in which he addresses the question: he denies in terms that souls come from the parents, and gives an argument for the denial. Three things should be said in Rufinus's favour before the finding is stated. He hedges — “I think that this was the opinion” — and he is writing from memory of “a great many writers,” under pressure, in a defence against a charge of heresy, not in a treatise on the soul. He is also right that Lactantius holds the soul to be present from conception, which is the visible half of Tertullian's thesis and easy to mistake for the whole of it. And Rufinus's grouping is by the timing of ensoulment as much as by its source: his first category is the soul “infused together with the material body,” and Lactantius does say the soul is there from conception. But the category is defined by the words that follow — “through the channel of the human seed” — and that is what Lactantius denies. The misattribution is best read as a scholar's slip under polemical pressure rather than as evidence about Lactantius; but it is a slip, and it has been repeated since.7

The episode is a useful caution about the ancient taxonomies generally. Rufinus, Jerome, and Gennadius all set out the options in three or five branches and attach names to each; the lists are the backbone of the modern account of this controversy, and at least one of the attributions in the earliest of them does not survive a check against the text it cites.8

Notes

  • Lactantius argues for creationism without a proof-text. The verse that later carried the creationist case — John 5:17, “my Father worketh hitherto” — enters the debate with Jerome, a century later, and is weighed on its own page (assessment: weak). Lactantius's own case rests on the soul's simplicity and on what parents demonstrably cannot do, which is a stronger foundation than the verse ever was.
  • What is entirely absent here is the consideration that would later make creationism agonising: Augustine's question about how a freshly created, innocent soul incurs the guilt for which an unbaptised infant is condemned. Lactantius writes before the Pelagian controversy has made original sin the axis of the question, and so states the creationist position in its uncomplicated form. The difficulty is weighed on its own page (assessment: contested).
  • Open tension: Lactantius calls the soul “slight and incomprehensible” and denies it can shed anything, yet also says of it that it is “nourished by the moisture of the blood, as light is by oil.” The image is meant to distinguish the soul from blood, not to make it material; but a soul that can be nourished is not obviously the simple, partless thing his argument against traduction requires. He does not resolve the strain, and the metaphysical apparatus that would resolve it is not available to him.
  • Lactantius appears on this wiki also as a witness on the Old Testament canon, the Nephilim, and purgatory. For the rest of the witnesses on this question, see the summary timeline.
