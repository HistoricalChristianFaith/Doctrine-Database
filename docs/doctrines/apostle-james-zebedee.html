James, son of Zebedee

James the son of Zebedee — “James the Great,” to distinguish him from the other apostle of that name — was a fisherman of Galilee, the elder brother of the apostle John, and, with Peter and John, one of the three disciples Jesus kept nearest him. He is the one apostle whose death the New Testament actually records: Herod Agrippa I “killed James the brother of John with the sword,” making him the first of the Twelve to be martyred. That single hard fact is the bedrock; almost everything else the church came to tell of him — a mission to Spain, a body carried by sea to Galicia, the great shrine at Santiago de Compostela — grew up long afterward. This page is not so much a life of James as a history of what was remembered and claimed about him, set out earliest source first, so that the firm Gospel and Acts notices, the early story of his accuser's conversion, and the late Spanish legend can each be weighed on its own date. This is a biographical tradition-timeline: its “witnesses” are the datable sources that report on James, and the contested questions — did his accuser really turn and die beside him? did he ever set foot in Spain? do his bones lie at Compostela? — are weighed on their own argument pages below rather than settled by assertion. For the shared ladder of sources and how far to trust each rung, see the apostles hub.

Timeline

The New Testament (c. 30–90)

The New Testament gives James a fuller portrait than most of the Twelve. He is a fisherman of Galilee, the son of Zebedee and the elder brother of John, called with his brother from mending their nets; Jesus surnames the pair Boanerges, “sons of thunder.” With Peter and John he forms the inner three admitted to the raising of Jairus's daughter, the Transfiguration, and the agony in Gethsemane. He and John ask for the seats at Jesus's right and left, and are told they will “drink of the cup” he drinks — a saying the tradition read as a prophecy of his martyrdom. That martyrdom the New Testament alone, of all the apostles', records: about the year 44 Herod Agrippa I “killed James the brother of John with the sword,” the first of the Twelve to die for the faith.1

Clement of Alexandria, preserved by Eusebius (c. 200)

Clement of Alexandria, in the lost Hypotyposes and quoted a century later by Eusebius, supplies the earliest tradition beyond the bare Acts notice: the man who had brought James to the judgment-seat was so moved by his testimony that he confessed himself a Christian, and the two were led off to be beheaded together — James, asked for forgiveness on the way, answering “Peace be with thee,” and kissing him. It is an early and not implausible story, told on the authority of “those who were before him,” but it survives only in this one quotation and cannot be checked against any other source.2

The apostle-lists and the Breviarium Apostolorum (c. 600–700)

No early source sends James on any mission — killed about 44, he had little time for one — and the sober tradition of Origen and Eusebius, which parcels out the mission fields of the other apostles, gives James none. The claim that he preached in Spain first surfaces only in the late Latin apostle-lists, above all the Breviarium Apostolorum (c. 600–700), which assigns each apostle a region and a resting-place. These catalogues are of uncertain authorship, depend on one another, and are critically judged unreliable as history; earlier Latin notice (Isidore of Seville) is ambiguous or against a Spanish mission. The Spain tradition is thus late, thin, and burdened by the chronology of Acts 12.3

Santiago de Compostela: the tomb and the pilgrimage (9th c. onward)

Around 820–830, in the reign of Alfonso II of Asturias, a tomb in a field in Galicia was proclaimed to be that of the apostle James, and a shrine was raised over it. From this grew Santiago de Compostela, which by the twelfth century (the Codex Calixtinus) had become, with Rome and Jerusalem, one of the three great pilgrimages of Christendom, and James — Santiago Matamoros, “Moor-slayer” — the patron of the Reconquista. A companion legend explained how his body came to be there: after his beheading in Jerusalem his disciples bore it by sea to Spain. The cult is one of the great facts of medieval Europe; as history of the first-century apostle it rests on a ninth-century identification and a still later translatio, with no ancient witness behind either.4

The Golden Legend (c. 1260)

Jacobus de Voragine's Golden Legend gives the fullest and most colourful life — James preaching in Spain, his return to Judea, his dispute with the magician Hermogenes, the conversion of his accuser Josias on the way to execution, and the miraculous voyage of his corpse to Galicia. It is cited here as late medieval hagiography, more than a millennium after the events, gathering the martyrdom, the accuser's conversion, and the Spanish legend into a single story.5

Arguments

Was James's accuser converted and beheaded with him? (assessment: plausible)

The accuser's conversion is the earliest tradition about James beyond Acts, carried by Clement of Alexandria on the authority of those before him. It is early, internally coherent, and in character with the age of the martyrs — but it hangs on a single quotation, is uncorroborated, and has the shapely feel of edifying martyr-story. Plausible — coherent and not ruled out, well short of the security of the bare fact of the martyrdom itself.6

Did James preach in Spain, and do his relics lie at Compostela? (assessment: weak)

The Spanish mission and the Compostela relics must be kept apart from the fact of the martyrdom, which is solid. No source before the late apostle-lists sends James to Spain; the chronology of Acts 12 (dead by c. 44, in Jerusalem) leaves scant room for it; and the tomb at Compostela rests on a ninth-century identification and a later legend of the body's voyage. Weak — a great medieval cult grafted onto the best-attested of the apostolic deaths, but poor history of the first-century apostle.7
