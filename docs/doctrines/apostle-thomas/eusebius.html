Origen and Eusebius: Parthia and Edessa

Dates: c. 230 (Origen’s notice) · c. 325 (Eusebius’s History) · Eusebius, Church History 3.1
Summary: The earliest datable traditions of where Thomas went come not from the colourful Acts but from two sober notices Eusebius preserves. Origen, in a lost commentary on Genesis, assigns Thomas Parthia — the Persian East, not yet India. And the Edessene Abgar legend, which Eusebius gives from a Syriac document, makes “Judas, who was also called Thomas” the apostle who sent Thaddaeus to King Abgar — the first thread tying Thomas to the Syriac world that would become the centre of his cult.

Claims & evidence

Origen: Parthia was allotted to Thomas

Writing about 230, Origen set out the apostles’ “division of the world” in his commentary on Genesis. The work is lost, but Eusebius quotes the passage in the third book of his Ecclesiastical History. It is a bare geographical allotment, without narrative or miracle — the plainest and earliest form of the tradition.

…the holy apostles and disciples of our Saviour were dispersed throughout the world. Parthia, according to tradition, was allotted to Thomas as his field of labor, Scythia to Andrew, and Asia to John, who, after he had lived some time there, died at Ephesus.

— Origen, quoted in Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History 3.1. “Parthia” is the Arsacid Persian empire east of the Euphrates — a real and reachable mission field, and notably not India. The later tradition would push Thomas further east; this earliest notice stops at Persia.

Eusebius: “Judas, who was also called Thomas” and the Edessa legend

Eusebius also preserves, from what he says was a Syriac document in the archives of Edessa, the famous legend of King Abgar — the ailing king who wrote to Jesus and received a promise that a disciple would be sent. In Eusebius’s telling it is Thomas who dispatches that disciple after the resurrection.

After the ascension of Jesus, Judas, who was also called Thomas, sent to him Thaddeus, an apostle, one of the Seventy.

— Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History 1.13. Two things are worth marking. First, the double name “Judas, who was also called Thomas” is the Syriac form — Judas Thomas — that the eastern church regularly used and that the Acts of Thomas would build on. Second, the notice binds Thomas to Edessa: even though he does not go himself but sends Thaddeus, the legend places the origin of the Edessene church under his authority, and Edessa is exactly where, a century later, Thomas’s relic and cult are firmly attested.

Notes

  • Parthia versus India. Origen’s “Parthia” and the later India tradition are not simply contradictory: Parthia and the Indo-Parthian kingdoms shaded into one another, and a mission that began in the Persian East could be remembered as reaching “India,” a name antiquity stretched very far. But the earliest witness names Persia, and the leap to the subcontinent is later — a point weighed on the India argument page.
  • The Abgar legend’s status. The correspondence between Jesus and Abgar is universally judged spurious, and the Decretum Gelasianum condemned it early. Its value here is not as history of a royal conversion but as evidence that, by Eusebius’s day, Thomas was already the apostle the Syriac church looked back to as the source of its mission.
  • Both notices are pre-Acts. Origen (c. 230) and the Edessa document Eusebius used are independent of, and in Origen’s case likely earlier than, the Acts of Thomas. They matter because they show an eastern-mission tradition for Thomas that does not depend on the romance — the foundation on which the Acts then built.
