Ephrem the Syrian and the shrine at Edessa

Dates: c. 306–373 (Ephrem) · c. 384 (Egeria’s visit) · Catholic Encyclopedia, “The St. Thomas Christians”
Summary: By the fourth century Thomas had a fixed tomb-cult in the Syriac world. Ephrem the Syrian, who spent his last decade at Edessa, sings of the apostle martyred in India whose bones a merchant carried home to Edessa; an early Syriac calendar keeps his feast on 3 July; and about 384 the Spanish pilgrim Egeria prayed at his church there, “where his body is laid entire.” This is the firmest early evidence in the whole Thomas tradition — not of the India mission itself, but of a relic and a shrine already central to Edessene devotion a century before Egeria.

Claims & evidence

Ephrem: the apostle of India whose bones lie at Edessa

Ephrem the Syrian — deacon, hymn-writer, and the great voice of Syriac Christianity — moved to Edessa about 363 and lived there until his death in 373. In his Carmina Nisibena and in hymns dedicated to Thomas, he takes it as settled that the apostle was martyred in India and that his remains were afterward translated to Edessa, brought by a merchant. In Ephrem’s poetic imagination the very demons are dismayed to find the apostle they thought dead in India now honoured and working wonders at their doorstep in Edessa.

In the forty-two of his “Carmina Nisibina” he tells us the Apostle was put to death in India, and that his remains were subsequently buried in Edessa, brought there by a merchant.

— Catholic Encyclopedia, “The St. Thomas Christians,” summarizing Ephrem’s hymn. For Ephrem, writing on the spot, the India martyrdom and the Edessa relic were not a thesis to be defended but a fact of local devotion.

The Syriac calendar: the feast of 3 July

An early Syriac ecclesiastical calendar preserves the memory in its liturgical form, naming the day, the manner of death, the resting-place, and even the merchant who is said to have carried the bones.

3 July, St. Thomas who was pierced with a lance in India. His body is at Urhai [Edessa], having been brought there by the merchant Khabin. A great festival.

— the Syriac calendar quoted in the Catholic Encyclopedia, “The St. Thomas Christians.” “Urhai” is the old Syriac name of Edessa; 3 July remains the feast of Saint Thomas in the Syriac churches and the Dukrana of the Saint Thomas Christians of India.

Egeria: praying at the memorial of Thomas

The strongest single witness is the pilgrim Egeria (Etheria), a woman of the Latin West who kept a diary of her journey through the Holy Land and Mesopotamia about 384. She went out of her way to Edessa expressly to pray at the shrine of Thomas.

Departing thence, we arrived at Edessa in the Name of Christ our God, and, on our arrival, we straightway repaired to the church and memorial of saint Thomas.

— Itinerarium Egeriae, tr. M. L. McClure and C. L. Feltoe, The Pilgrimage of Etheria; she came, she says, “for the sake of prayer at the memorial of S. Thomas the Apostle, where his body is laid entire. This is at Edessa.” A large basilica of Thomas stood at Edessa by her day.

Notes

  • What this evidence does and does not prove. Ephrem, the calendar, and Egeria are firm, early, and independent testimony to a cult — a relic, a shrine, and a feast of Thomas at Edessa by the mid-fourth century. That is not the same as proof of the India mission: a shrine attests devotion, and the claim that the bones came from India is itself part of the tradition under examination. But the cult is old enough that it cannot be dismissed as late invention, and it is the reason the India question is weighed as genuinely contested rather than dismissed.
  • The merchant who brought the bones. The recurring figure of a merchant carrying Thomas’s remains west — named Khabin in the Syriac calendar — picks up the thread the Acts of Thomas had left, where the apostle’s bones are taken “to the West” after his martyrdom. Whether relics were ever literally translated, the story of the translation is old.
  • A later chapter. In 1258 relics venerated as Thomas’s were carried from the East to Ortona in Italy, where they remain — a medieval sequel to the ancient Edessa tradition, and further evidence of how tenacious the cult of Thomas has been.
