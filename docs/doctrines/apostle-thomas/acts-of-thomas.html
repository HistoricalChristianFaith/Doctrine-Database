The Acts of Thomas

Dates: c. 220–240 (Syriac, Edessa) · Early Christian Writings
Summary: The Acts of Thomas is the longest and best-preserved of the apocryphal Acts, and the source of nearly everything the tradition says about Thomas in India: the sale of the apostle as a slave-carpenter, the palace built in heaven for King Gundaphar, and the martyrdom by four spears under King Misdaeus. It is romance-hagiography with encratite tendencies, of little worth as biography — yet its King Gundaphar is a genuine Indo-Parthian monarch recovered from coins, which sets it apart from every other apocryphal Acts and forces the India question to be taken seriously.

Claims & evidence

India falls to Thomas by lot; Christ sells him to a merchant

The Acts opens with the apostles at Jerusalem dividing the regions of the world among themselves by lot. India falls to Thomas, who protests that a Hebrew cannot go and preach among the Indians. The risen Christ settles the matter by selling him, as a skilled carpenter, to a merchant named Abban (Habban) who has come from the Indian king Gundaphar to buy an architect — and so Thomas is carried to India against his will (Acts of Thomas 1–3, tr. M. R. James, The Apocryphal New Testament).

The palace built in heaven

King Gundaphar gives Thomas money and a commission to build him a royal palace. Thomas draws the plans, takes the money, and distributes it all to the poor and afflicted, telling the king the work is proceeding. When the king comes to inspect the finished building and finds nothing, he is enraged — but Thomas answers that the palace stands in heaven, invisible to the king until he leaves this life. The truth of it is confirmed when the king’s own dead brother, Gad, is permitted to return and reports that he has seen the shining palace built for Gundaphar in heaven. King and brother are baptized.

Thou canst not see it now, but when thou departest this life, then thou shalt see it.

— Acts of Thomas 21 (Thomas to the king). This palace-in-heaven parable is the most famous episode of the Acts and the reason Thomas is patron of builders and architects, his emblem the carpenter’s square.

The martyrdom under King Misdaeus

The apostle’s later travels bring him to the court of another king, Misdaeus (Syriac Mazdai). Thomas converts the queen Tertia, the king’s son Vazan, and Mygdonia, the wife of the king’s kinsman — and their consecrated chastity so enrages Misdaeus that he condemns the apostle. Led out of the city to a hill, Thomas is run through with spears by four soldiers — and, the Acts notes, he calls it fitting that four should slay him, for of four elements he was made (Acts of Thomas, Martyrdom, §§164–168). Afterward his bones are carried away “to the West” — the seed of the Edessa relic-tradition — and Misdaeus himself is at last converted when dust from the apostle’s grave heals his son. (M. R. James translation.)

Notes

  • What kind of text this is. The Acts of Thomas is Syriac romance-hagiography of the early third century, marked by encratite (and in places gnostic) tendencies — the exaltation of celibacy is its engine, and it embeds the celebrated “Hymn of the Pearl.” By the standard applied across these pages it is of low biographical value and high value as a witness to how Thomas was venerated. It is not laundered into fact here.
  • Why this one Acts is different. Alone among the apocryphal Acts, the Acts of Thomas names a ruler — Gundaphar — who has since proved historical: an Indo-Parthian king of the first half of the first century, unknown from any surviving literary source and recovered only in the nineteenth century from his coins and the Takht-i-Bahi inscription. A romance that happens to know a real and obscure Indian king of the right era cannot simply be dismissed. Whether that anchor carries the India mission is weighed on the argument page.
  • The bones move west. The Acts’ closing note that Thomas’s remains were taken “to the West” is the narrative hinge to the Edessa cult that Ephrem and Egeria attest — the tradition that a merchant brought the apostle’s bones from India to Edessa.
