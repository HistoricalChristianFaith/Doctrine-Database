The New Testament on Thomas

Dates: c. 30–90 (the events, and the writing of the Gospels and Acts) · Wikipedia
Summary: Thomas stands in all four lists of the Twelve, but it is John’s Gospel that makes him a person rather than a name. Three scenes give him a voice — the brave “let us also go, that we may die with him,” the puzzled “how can we know the way?” and the doubt that ends in “My Lord and my God” — and three times John glosses his name as Didymus, “the Twin.” After Pentecost the New Testament falls silent; everything else belongs to the later tradition.

Claims & evidence

Named in all four lists of the Twelve

Thomas appears in every list of the apostles. In Matthew and Luke he is paired with Matthew the publican; in Mark he stands beside Bartholomew; in Acts, listing the Eleven after the resurrection, he comes between Philip and Bartholomew. The lists give his name and nothing more — it is John who fills it in.

Philip, and Bartholomew; Thomas, and Matthew the publican; James the son of Alphaeus, and Lebbaeus, whose surname was Thaddaeus…

— Matthew 10:3 (cf. Mark 3:18; Luke 6:15; Acts 1:13).

“Thomas” and “Didymus” both mean “the Twin”

“Thomas” is the Aramaic te’oma, “twin”; John three times adds the Greek gloss Didymus (didymos), which means the same. Like “Bartholomew” (a patronymic), it is a by-name rather than a personal name, and it leaves open the question the Syriac church would later press — a twin of whom? The Syriac tradition supplied a personal name, calling him Judas Thomas; some strands went further and made him the twin of Jesus. Those developments are weighed on the argument page; the Gospels themselves say only that he was “the Twin.”

“Let us also go, that we may die with him”

When Jesus announces he will return to Bethany in Judea — where the authorities had lately tried to stone him — to raise Lazarus, it is Thomas who answers for the frightened disciples, not with a refusal but with a grim loyalty.

Then said Thomas, which is called Didymus, unto his fellowdisciples, Let us also go, that we may die with him.

— John 11:16.

“How can we know the way?”

At the Last Supper, when Jesus says he goes to prepare a place and that they know the way, Thomas voices the plain confusion the others feel — and his question draws out one of the Gospel’s great sayings.

Thomas saith unto him, Lord, we know not whither thou goest; and how can we know the way?

— John 14:5; Jesus answers, “I am the way, the truth, and the life” (John 14:6).

The doubt, and “My Lord and my God”

Thomas is the disciple who was absent when the risen Jesus first appeared, and who refuses the others’ testimony until he can touch the wounds for himself. Eight days later Jesus returns and offers him precisely that — and Thomas’s doubt breaks into the highest confession of divinity anywhere in the Gospels.

Except I shall see in his hands the print of the nails, and put my finger into the print of the nails, and thrust my hand into his side, I will not believe.… And Thomas answered and said unto him, My Lord and my God.

— John 20:24–28. Jesus replies, “Thomas, because thou hast seen me, thou hast believed: blessed are they that have not seen, and yet have believed” (John 20:29).

Among the fishing disciples

Thomas appears once more, in the epilogue of John’s Gospel, among the apostles to whom the risen Jesus shows himself at the Sea of Tiberias.

There were together Simon Peter, and Thomas called Didymus, and Nathanael of Cana in Galilee, and the sons of Zebedee…

— John 21:2.

Notes

  • The most vivid of the silent apostles. Of the apostles whose later lives are wholly traditional, Thomas is the one the Gospels most nearly let us know — loyal, literal-minded, unwilling to say he understands when he does not. That characterization colours every later story told of him.
  • “My Lord and my God.” Thomas’s confession is cited across the tradition as the Gospel’s plainest ascription of full deity to the risen Christ; it belongs to the doctrine pages on Christ’s divinity as much as to Thomas’s biography, and is noted here only as the New Testament’s last and greatest word about the man.
  • Silence after Pentecost. Thomas is named at the upper room in Acts 1 and then vanishes from the New Testament. Everything on his mission and death traced on the summary timeline — Parthia, India, the martyrdom — belongs to the later tradition.
