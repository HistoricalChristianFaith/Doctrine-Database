The Golden Legend: the medieval Thomas

Dates: c. 1260 (Jacobus de Voragine, Legenda Aurea) · the Golden Legend, tr. Caxton
Summary: The form in which the Latin West received Thomas: the palace built in heaven for King Gundaphorus, the dead brother who saw it and came back to tell of it, and, in a second Indian kingdom, the idol cast down and the apostle run through by pagan priests. Jacobus draws it from the old Acts of Thomas and the apostle-lists and adds the medieval colour. It is hagiography a thousand years after the events, of no biographical worth — but it fixed Thomas’s emblem, the carpenter’s square, and his standing as patron of builders.

Claims & evidence

The palace built in heaven for King Gundaphorus

The Legend retells the central episode of the old Acts. The risen Christ makes Thomas over to a servant named Abban and sends him to serve Gundaphorus, king of India, who wants a splendid palace built. Thomas takes the king’s gold and, while the king is away, gives it all to the poor and the sick. When the king returns and demands to see his palace, Thomas answers that it is finished — in heaven, not on earth, and that the king will enter it when he leaves this life. The furious king casts Thomas into prison; but the king’s brother, newly dead, is shown in a vision the shining palace that Thomas’s charity had built in heaven, and returns to life to beg it for himself. King and brother are baptized, and Thomas goes free.

The second kingdom, the idol, and the martyrdom

The Legend carries Thomas on to the realm of another Indian king — Mesdeus (the Misdaeus of the Acts) — where the apostle’s preaching wins the queen and other notable converts to lives of chastity. Brought before an idol in a pagan temple, Thomas commands the demon within to depart and the image is destroyed. At this the priests of the temple fall on him in fury, and Thomas is run through and killed — smitten, in Jacobus’s telling, with the priests’ spears. The Golden Legend, like the older sources, knows more than one account of the death and rehearses the variants, but the spearing is the version it carries.

— Jacobus de Voragine, the Golden Legend, life of St Thomas the Apostle.

Notes

  • Late legend, not early witness. The Golden Legend is c. 1260 — more than a thousand years after Thomas — and gathers the Acts of Thomas and the apostle-lists into a single edifying narrative. It is cited here as hagiography, following the house treatment set out on the apostles hub.
  • The carpenter’s square. Because the palace-in-heaven story casts Thomas as a builder, medieval and later art gave him the builder’s set-square as his emblem, and made him patron of architects, masons, and carpenters — iconography that descends directly from this legend.
  • Continuity with the Acts. Every load-bearing element here — Gundaphorus, the heavenly palace, King Misdaeus, the spearing — is already in the third-century Acts of Thomas. The Legend transmits, embellishes, and popularizes; it adds no independent historical weight, and the India question is weighed not on it but on the argument page.
