Simon the Zealot

Simon the Zealot is, with James the son of Alphaeus, among the most silent of the Twelve. He stands eleventh in every list — next to last, just before Judas Iscariot — and the New Testament tells us nothing of him but a single word of description: Matthew and Mark call him “the Cananaean,” Luke and Acts “the Zealot.” The two are the same epithet, not two facts: “Cananaean” is not “of Cana” nor “Canaanite” but the Aramaic qan’ana, “zealous,” which Luke simply renders into Greek as zēlōtēs. What that zeal was — membership in the revolutionary party that would later fight Rome, or a plain devotion to the Law — is the one real question the name raises, and it is weighed below. Everything else about Simon is later tradition, and the traditions do not agree: the Greek menologies send him to Egypt, North Africa, the Black Sea, even Britain; the Latin Passio and the Golden Legend send him to Persia to die with Jude the apostle; the Georgians keep his tomb in the Caucasus; and the apostle-lists cannot even keep him distinct from Simeon of Jerusalem, the Lord’s kinsman. This page is less a life of Simon than a history of what was remembered and claimed about him, set out earliest source first: the bare Gospel epithet, the mutually-dependent apostle-lists that first scattered his mission and confused his person, the Latin passion and the Golden Legend that fixed the Persian martyrdom and gave Western art its saw, and the modern scholarship that weighs the epithet and grades the travels. This is a biographical tradition-timeline: its “witnesses” are the datable sources that report on him, and the contested questions — what did “the Zealot” mean? where did he go and die? — are weighed on their own argument pages below rather than settled by assertion. Timeline

The New Testament (c. 30–90)

The New Testament names Simon four times, always in the same place — eleventh in the third group of four, immediately before Judas Iscariot — and never otherwise. It gives him no saying, no deed, no mission, no death: only a surname that marks him off from that other Simon, Simon Peter. Matthew and Mark write “Simon the Cananaean” (ho Kananaios); Luke and Acts, in the same slot, write “Simon called Zelotes.” These are not two men nor two facts but one epithet in two languages: “Cananaean” transliterates the Aramaic qan’ana (“zealous”), and Luke translates it for Greek readers as zēlōtēs. That the word does not mean “from Cana” or “Canaanite” is settled; what the “zeal” denotes — a former insurgent of the revolutionary party, or simply a man zealous for the Law — is the one question the New Testament leaves open, and is weighed below.1

The apostle-lists (uncertain date, mutually dependent)

Where the Gospels give a name only, the late apostle-lists — Pseudo-Hippolytus On the Twelve Apostles, Pseudo-Dorotheus of Tyre, and their Byzantine and Latin kin — supply a mission and a death, and cannot agree on either. One strand sends Simon far into the north and west — the Black Sea coast, Egypt and North Africa, and even Britain; another keeps him in the East, in Persia or the Caucasus. Worse, the lists cannot even keep his person distinct: Pseudo-Hippolytus writes that “Simon the Zealot, the son of Clopas, who is also called Jude, became bishop of Jerusalem after James the Just, and fell asleep and was buried there at the age of 120 years” — which is not the apostle Simon at all but Simeon son of Clopas, the second bishop of Jerusalem, whom Hegesippus reports martyred under Trajan at 120. That the catalogues fuse the apostle with the bishop, and scatter his travels across three continents, is the tell: his “biography” is being assembled after the fact from names that happened to match.2

The Latin Passio and the Golden Legend (c. 1260)

The Western Church settled on one of these strands and made it the standard story. The Latin Passio Simonis et Iudae, retold in Jacobus de Voragine’s Golden Legend (c. 1260), pairs Simon with Jude/Thaddaeus, sends the two together into Persia — Simon “preached in Egypt,” Jude “in Mesopotamia,” “and from thence came they into Persia” — to confront the sorcerers Zaroes and Arphaxat and to die together in a pagan temple at Suanir, where the priests “ran upon the apostles and hewed them to death.” From this joint martyrdom the West keeps their common feast on 28 October, and Western art gave Simon a saw as the emblem of his death — from the parallel tradition, reported as far back as the Greek and Latin passions, that he was sawn asunder. The Golden Legend itself hedges even here, noting that “it is founden in divers places, of S. Simon, that he was nailed to the cross,” while “some say verily that it was not this Simon that suffered the martyrdom of the cross, but it was another.” It is late legend — a millennium and more after the events — and church memory, not history.3

Modern scholarship

Critical study leaves Simon nearly as blank as the Gospel lists found him, and turns the two live questions over to the tools that grade them. On the epithet, most now doubt that “the Zealot” can mean a card-carrying member of the revolutionary party: Josephus dates the organized Zealots to the war of 66–70, a full generation after Jesus called Simon, so the surname more likely means “zealous for the Law” — though Martin Hengel’s study of a longer-running “freedom movement” reaching back to Herod keeps the political reading alive, and the question stays genuinely open. On the travels, the verdict is harder: the mission-traditions are late, mutually contradictory, and geographically impossible to reconcile — Persia, Britain, Egypt, and the Caucasus cannot all be true — and Sean McDowell’s survey of the martyrdom accounts rates the case for any particular death of Simon among the weakest of the Twelve. What can safely be said is only this: a Simon called “the Zealot” was numbered among the apostles; everything more is either silence or a tradition the evidence cannot carry.4

Arguments

Was Simon “the Zealot” a member of the revolutionary party? (assessment: contested)

The political reading takes Simon’s surname to mark him a former insurgent of the Zealot party — the nationalist movement that sought to throw off Rome — so that Jesus’ circle included a reformed revolutionary beside a tax collector who had served Rome. Against it stands a chronological objection: Josephus dates the organized “Zealots” as a named faction to the war of 66–70, decades too late, so that the epithet on the lips of Jesus can only mean “zealous” for the Law. For it stands Hengel’s case that a broader Jewish freedom movement long predated the war-years party. Strong by one discipline’s lights and weak by another’s, with no clear winner. Contested.5

Where did Simon the Zealot preach and die — Persia, Britain, or elsewhere? (assessment: weak)

The mission-traditions place Simon in mutually exclusive fields — Persia (with Jude, martyred at Suanir), Britain, Egypt and North Africa, the Black Sea, the Caucasus — and cannot be harmonized: no early or independent witness fixes any one of them, the sources that carry them are the same late catalogues that confuse Simon with Simeon of Jerusalem, and the Persian story is a paired romance built to match Jude’s. That some mission is likely is not in doubt; that we can know which is what the evidence cannot deliver. Weak.6
