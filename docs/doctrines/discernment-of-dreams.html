The discernment of dreams (God, demons, or the body — and how to tell?)

Scripture is full of dreams that carry God's word: Joseph reads Pharaoh's, Daniel reads Nebuchadnezzar's, an angel warns Joseph the carpenter three times, and the Lord tells Aaron and Miriam plainly, "If there be a prophet among you, I… will speak unto him in a dream" (Numbers 12:6). Yet the same Scripture is just as full of warnings: "the prophets that prophesy… dreams" are the mark of the false prophet (Jeremiah 23:25–32), Israel is forbidden to "observe dreams" (Leviticus 19:26), and the sage says flatly, "Dreams have deceived many" (Sirach 34:7). Above all, Deuteronomy 13:1–5 lays down the hard rule that a dreamer's sign may come to pass and he still be a deceiver to be rejected — so a dream's coming true is not by itself proof it came from God.

Out of this tension the Fathers built a settled doctrine: a dream may arise from more than one source — classically three, from God (revelation), from demons (illusion, often mixing truth into a lie to win credit), and from the body or the mind itself (the stomach, the day's cares, the soul's own natural ranging in sleep) — and the burden falls on the dreamer to discern which he has received. The question is not whether God can speak in a dream (all agree He does) but how the God-sent is told from the demonic and the merely bodily, when the counterfeit can be calm, plausible, and even accurate. This timeline traces that taxonomy and its test from Tertullian's fourfold division (c. 210) to Aquinas's scholastic synthesis (c. 1271). Its near neighbour is the question of dream-apparitions of the dead (when the figure in the dream is a departed person, whose act is it?), and it shades into the wider suspicion of divination by the heavens.

Timeline

Tertullian (c. 210)

Tertullian, in A Treatise on the Soul (De Anima) 45–49, gives the earliest sustained Christian anatomy of dreams and sorts them by source into a fourfold division: most are "inflicted on us mainly by demons, although they sometimes turn out true"; others come "from God," who pours out His Spirit so "His servants and His handmaids should see visions"; a third class "the soul itself apparently creates for itself"; and a residue belongs to the "purely and simply… ecstatic state." Because demonic dreams "sometimes turn out true," truth is no guarantee of source — the dreamer must still interpret them "in another sense."1

Athanasius of Alexandria (c. 318)

Athanasius, in Against the Heathen (Contra Gentes) 31–33, grounds the third, natural source: the dreaming soul's own activity. "When the body is quiet, and at rest and asleep, man moves inwardly, and beholds what is outside himself, travelling to other countries, walking about, meeting his acquaintances" — proof, he argues, that the rational soul is distinct from the body and outlives it. Not every dream is a message from beyond, then; much of it is simply the immortal soul ranging on its own.2

John Chrysostom (c. 390)

Chrysostom, in the Homilies on Matthew, holds the God-sent dream firmly — but notes that God fits the mode of revelation to the recipient. Joseph the carpenter is warned only "in a dream," not by an open vision like the shepherds or Zacharias, because "the man was exceedingly full of faith, and needed not this vision"; the Virgin, with greater tidings, "needed also a marvellous vision," and the shepherds got an angel "as being by disposition rather dull and clownish." The dream is a real vehicle of God's word, calibrated to the faith of the one who receives it.3

Augustine of Hippo (c. 415)

Augustine, in The Literal Meaning of Genesis (De Genesi ad litteram) XII, supplies the classic framework: three kinds of vision — corporeal (with the bodily eyes), spiritual (images seen in the spirit, as in dreams and ecstasy, when the senses are at rest), and intellectual (truths with no bodily image). The dream lives in the middle, spiritual kind — and there Augustine states the discernment problem at its sharpest: such images may be impressed by "some other spirit, whether evil or good," and the discernment is "exceedingly difficult" (difficillima), because a malign spirit can act calmly, "even say true things and foretell useful things," transfiguring itself "as an angel of light" precisely so that, once believed in these manifest goods, it may seduce the soul.4

John Cassian (c. 426)

Cassian, in the Conferences, carries the taxonomy into the monastery. Abbot Moses teaches "three origins of our thoughts… from God, from the devil, and from ourselves," and calls for the discretion of a "good money-changer" who tests each coin — for the devil, "transforming himself into an angel of light," counterfeits the true king's image. The cautionary tale is the monk Heron, who "received with the utmost reverence an angel of Satan as an angel of light" and cast himself down a well on its promise; the safeguard is never trusting one's own judgment but submitting every thought to the elders.5

Gregory the Great (c. 593)

Gregory the Dialogist, in Dialogues IV.48, gives the most-cited classification of all — six kinds of dream: from a full stomach, from an empty stomach, from illusion, from thought together with illusion, from revelation, and from thought together with revelation. His decisive move is that the evil source mixes truth into falsehood: the wicked spirit "sometime useth to foretell many true things, that, in the end, he may by some falsehood ensnare our soul." Because dreams "grow from such divers roots," they are the harder to trust; only holy men, "by a certain inward spiritual taste," discern true revelation from illusion — and Gregory backs the caution with "Dreams have deceived many" (Sirach 34:7).6

Thomas Aquinas (c. 1271)

Aquinas, in the Summa Theologiae II-II, q. 95, a. 6, hardens the tradition into a decision procedure. Dreams have an inward cause (the soul's waking thoughts recurring, or "the inward disposition of the body") and an outward cause, itself "corporal and spiritual" — corporal from "the surrounding air, or… a heavenly body," spiritual either "referable to God, Who reveals… by the ministry of the angels" or "to the action of the demons." The rule follows directly: reading the future from dreams is lawful "so long as those dreams are due to divine revelation, or to some natural cause," but "unlawful and superstitious… if it be caused by a revelation of the demons."7

Arguments

One inference cuts across the whole timeline and is worth weighing on its own — the folk reassurance that a dream's coming true settles that it was from God.

  • Does a dream's coming true prove it was from God? — weak as a universal rule (Deuteronomy 13 stages a fulfilled dream-sign that must still be rejected; fulfilment corroborates but never decides), though veridical dreams are real.
