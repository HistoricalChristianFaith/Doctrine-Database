The Condemnation of Origen

Origen of Alexandria (c. 185–c. 253) died in communion with the Church. He had been tortured under Decius and survived it, honoured as a confessor; he was the most productive and the most quoted Christian teacher of his age, and for a century afterwards the man whose commentaries every serious exegete in East and West worked from. Three hundred years later his name stands in a list of arch-heretics between Eutyches and the anathematized dead. This page asks how that happened, and whether it was fair.

The subject here is not Origen's theology as such but the verdicts passed on it: a timeline of judgements, earliest first — who first called him heretical, on what charges, on whose authority, and how the charge-sheet changed between Demetrius of Alexandria in the 230s and the emperor Justinian in the 540s, and after. Its “witnesses” are therefore dated acts and documents — a bishop's synod, a rival's dialogue, a martyr's defence from prison, a heresy-catalogue, a papal letter, an imperial edict, a conciliar anathema, a modern monograph — each footnoted to its source. Two features of the story recur and are worth watching for: the charges are remarkably stable from about 300 onwards, and they are almost always drawn from hostile excerpts of a single book, Peri Archōn (On First Principles), whose Greek original had by then largely ceased to circulate.

The bitter Latin quarrel this controversy produced between Jerome and Rufinus in 393–411 is told on its own timeline, and its Eastern half — Theophilus of Alexandria's destruction of John Chrysostom — on another; this page gives those years only the space their place in the whole arc requires. The doctrine that drew the heaviest fire, the pre-existence of souls, is traced as a belief in its own right on the origin of the soul. Origen appears on this wiki also as a witness on some sixteen belief-timelines, from the canon and purgatory to astrology and the Nephilim — which is itself part of the point: a condemned man whom the tradition never stopped reading.

Timeline

Origen marks out the open questions himself (Peri Archōn, c. 229)

Nearly everything later condemned comes from one work, written at Alexandria before Origen left it, and its preface is the first thing any fair account of the condemnation has to reckon with. Origen there divides Christian teaching in two. On some points “the holy apostles, in preaching the faith of Christ, delivered themselves with the utmost clearness”; on others they stated only that a thing is so, “leaving, however, the grounds of their statements to be examined into by those who should deserve the excellent gifts of the Spirit.” Into the second class he explicitly puts the origin of the soul — whether it “is derived from the seed by a process of traducianism… or whether it has any other beginning” is, he says, “not distinguished with sufficient clearness in the teaching of the Church” — and the nature of the devil and his angels, of whom “the teaching of the Church has laid down that these beings exist indeed; but what they are, or how they exist, it has not explained with sufficient clearness.”1 The propositions later anathematized are, on their author's own account, offered as investigations of questions he took the Church to have left open — a framing his accusers uniformly declined to accept, and one that later readers have had to weigh against the fact that he sometimes argues those investigations hard.

Demetrius of Alexandria drives him out (c. 231–233)

The first condemnation was not doctrinal. Sent to Greece on church business, Origen passed through Palestine and was ordained presbyter at Caesarea by the bishops of that country — without the consent of his own bishop, Demetrius of Alexandria. Eusebius, writing a century later and no neutral, will not say what followed: “The matters that were agitated concerning him on this account, and the decisions on these matters by those who presided over the churches… demand a separate treatise.”2 Jerome, writing in 392 and still an admirer, is blunter: Origen “offended Demetrius, who was so wildly enraged at him that he wrote everywhere to injure his reputation.”3 Origen left Egypt for Caesarea; Demetrius died not long after, and the school Origen had built passed to his own pupil Heraclas, who succeeded Demetrius in the see.4 The precedent set here shaped everything after it: a sentence against Origen issued from Alexandria, on grounds his defenders read as jurisdictional and personal, and circulated by letter to other churches.

Palestine declines to receive the sentence (from 231)

The Alexandrian verdict was not universally received — the first and most important fact about it. Alexander of Jerusalem and Theoctistus of Caesarea, the bishops who had ordained him, “attended on him constantly, as their only teacher, and allowed him to expound the Divine Scriptures, and to perform the other duties pertaining to ecclesiastical discourse”; Firmilian of Caesarea in Cappadocia summoned him into his own province “for the benefit of the churches” and afterwards travelled to Judea to sit under him.5 For the remaining two decades of his life Origen taught, preached, and wrote as a presbyter of the Palestinian church, and the bishops of three provinces treated the Egyptian sentence as having no force over them.

Tortured under Decius; dies in communion (250 – c. 253)

In the Decian persecution Origen was arrested and tortured — “bonds and bodily tortures and torments under the iron collar and in the dungeon; and how for many days with his feet stretched four spaces in the stocks he bore patiently the threats of fire” — under a judge who “strove eagerly with all his might not to end his life.” He did not recant.6 He survived, broken, and died a few years later at Tyre, where he was buried, in his sixty-ninth year.7 No church had excommunicated him for his teaching; he died a confessor of the faith, and the next generation of Greek theologians — Dionysius of Alexandria, Gregory Thaumaturgus, Pamphilus, Eusebius — were his pupils or his pupils' pupils.

Methodius attacks the doctrine, not the man (c. 290 – 311)

The first sustained theological assault comes from Methodius of Olympus, in a dialogue on the resurrection (the Aglaophon) written shortly before his martyrdom. Its target is the Origenist account of the risen body: Methodius argues “that the body is not the fetter of the soul, as Origen thought, nor are souls called by the prophet Jeremiah ‘fettered’ on account of their being within bodies”, and that Origen “said that the body was given to the soul as a fetter after the fall, and that previously it lived without a body.”8 Two things are worth noticing. Methodius disputes a doctrine, in the ordinary form of a philosophical dialogue; he does not ask a synod to condemn a dead presbyter. And his work survives chiefly because Epiphanius later quarried most of it into his own heresy-catalogue — the route by which a third-century theological disagreement became fourth-century evidence of heresy.

Pamphilus and Eusebius answer the charges from prison (c. 307–310)

Pamphilus of Caesarea, arrested in November 307 and beheaded in 310, spent his imprisonment composing, with Eusebius, an Apology for Origen in five books; Eusebius added a sixth after his death. Only the first survives, in Rufinus's Latin of 397. It is the earliest surviving inventory of the charge-sheet, and its list is close to final already: the standing accusations it answers concern the Trinity and the status of the Son, the Incarnation, whether Origen dissolved the biblical narratives into allegory, the resurrection of the dead, the punishment of the wicked, the nature of the soul, and metempsychosis.9 That an apology was needed at all shows the accusations were circulating a full generation before Epiphanius; that a martyr wrote it shows how far from settled the question then was.

Epiphanius makes Origen a heresy in his own right (c. 375–377)

In the Panarion, his catalogue of eighty heresies, Epiphanius of Salamis gives Origen an entry of his own — heresy 64 — alongside the Gnostics and the Arians. This is the decisive formal step: a churchman who died in communion is now listed as a heresiarch under his own name, and the bulk of the chapter is Methodius's dialogue, quoted at length.10 Epiphanius pressed the same case in a letter to John of Jerusalem which Jerome translated into Latin, and which is why it survives: “The Son, he tells us, cannot see the Father, and the Holy Spirit cannot see the Son”; souls “were once angels in heaven, and… having sinned… have been cast down into this, and have been confined in bodies”; and Origen “denies the resurrection of this flesh, as he most clearly does in his book of explanations of the first psalm.”11

The synods of 400 and Rome's sentence (399–402)

In 399 Theophilus of Alexandria, who had begun on the Origenist side of the dispute then dividing the Egyptian monasteries, changed sides; in 400 he convened a council at Alexandria and circulated its synodical letter, which Jerome translated: “The books of Origen have been read before a council of bishops and unanimously condemned.” The letter enumerates the errors — among them that “the Son compared with us is truth, but compared with the Father he is falsehood” and that “Christ's kingdom will one day come to an end.”12 At Rome the same year, with Rufinus's Latin Peri Archōn in hand, Pope Anastasius I held a synod and wrote to Simplicianus of Milan: “everything written in days gone by Origen that is contrary to our faith is even by us rejected and condemned… If Origen has put forth any other writings, you are to know that they and their author are alike condemned by me.”13 Writing to John of Jerusalem the following year he added that Origen's “object was to disintegrate our faith, which is that of the Apostles”, and noted the imperial decree “by which every one who serves God is warned against the reading of Origen.”14 Within three years the same campaign had expelled the Origenist monks from Egypt and deposed the bishop of Constantinople — told on the Theophilus–Chrysostom timeline — and had broken Jerome's friendship with Rufinus, told on the Origenist controversy. What was condemned in 400 was, in every case, doctrine drawn from Peri Archōn as excerpted by hostile readers; no numbered anathema-list yet existed.

Vincent of Lérins: the great trial of the Church (434)

By the fifth century the Latin verdict is settled enough to be used as a teaching example. Vincent of Lérins heads a chapter of his Commonitorium “The Error of Origen a great Trial to the Church” and makes him the type of the learned teacher whose gifts magnify his error: “this Origen, such and so great as he was, wantonly abusing the grace of God, rashly following the bent of his own genius…” — with the result that “very many were led astray from the integrity of the faith, not by any human excellencies of this so great man, this so great doctor, this so great prophet, but, as the event showed, by the too perilous trial which he proved to be.”15 Vincent also records the mitigating suggestion, already current, that the offending texts had been tampered with — the thesis Rufinus had pressed a generation earlier, weighed on its own page.

Justinian's edict to Menas: the charges become anathemas (543)

The sixth-century revival of the quarrel was monastic and Palestinian, and its occasion was a fight between rival parties of Origenist monks rather than anything Origen wrote. In 543 the emperor Justinian issued an edict addressed to Menas, patriarch of Constantinople, quoting Peri Archōn at length and appending nine anathemas — the first time the charges take the numbered, canonical form in which they would be handed down. The first condemns anyone who “says or holds that the souls of human beings pre-exist, as previously minds and holy powers, but that they reached satiety with divine contemplation and turned to what is worse… and were made to descend into bodies as a punishment”; the ninth, anyone who holds “that the punishment of demons and impious human beings is temporary and that it will have an end at some time.”16 The document is often described as ratified by a synod, but its authority was in the first instance imperial: the canons were sent to the patriarchs, in Richard Price's summary, “not for their confirmation but for their circulation.”17

The fifteen anathemas and the council of 553

A second and fuller list of fifteen anathemas — the text most often quoted as “the condemnation of Origen” — belongs to the same decade. Its first article anathematizes anyone who “asserts the fabulous pre-existence of souls, and shall assert the monstrous restoration which follows from it”; its last, anyone who says “that the life of the spirits shall be like to the life which was in the beginning… so that the end and the beginning shall be alike, and that the end shall be the true measure of the beginning.”18 Whether the Second Council of Constantinople itself passed them is the single most contested question in this whole history. The nineteenth-century editors of the conciliar acts in English conceded that “after all is said the matter remains involved in the greatest obscurity”;19 the prevailing modern view, following Franz Diekamp's study of 1899, is that the fifteen were issued by the bishops already gathered at Constantinople in the weeks before the council formally opened on 5 May 553, and that their doctrinal target was the sixth-century Palestinian Origenists rather than the third-century Alexandrian himself. Norman Tanner's standard edition of the councils accordingly prints the acts of 553 without them, noting that “recent studies have shown that these anathemas cannot be attributed to this council.”20 What the council's own canons undoubtedly do contain is Origen's name, in the eleventh: “If anyone does not anathematize Arius, Eunomius, Macedonius, Apollinarius, Nestorius, Eutyches and Origen, as well as their heretical books… let him be anathema.”21 Whether that name stood there from the first, or was added later, is itself disputed — the acts of 553 survive only in Latin — but nothing in the later tradition doubted it.

The name in the later councils and the Byzantine lists (680–787 and after)

Two subsequent ecumenical councils treat the matter as closed and name Origen in company that was to become fixed. Constantinople III (680–681) refers to “the fifth holy synod, the latest of them, which was gathered here against Theodore of Mopsuestia, Origen, Didymus and Evagrius”;22 Nicaea II (787) anathematizes “the mythical speculations of Origen, Evagrius and Didymus, as did the fifth synod, that assembled at Constantinople.”23 Both are ratifications by reference rather than fresh trials; but they establish that from the seventh century onward the Church read 553 as having condemned Origen personally, and they harden the Origen–Didymus–Evagrius triad into a formula. It is that formula, not the third-century books, which the medieval East and West inherited.

The modern reassessment (20th century onwards)

The twentieth century reopened the question along a distinction the sixth century had no reason to draw: between Origen and Origenism. The doctrines anathematized in 543 and 553 — a pre-existent equality of all minds, the risen body as a sphere, the eventual equality of every spirit with Christ — belong demonstrably to a sixth-century Palestinian monastic system indebted to Evagrius of Pontus, and correspond only loosely, and in places not at all, to what stands in Peri Archōn. Elizabeth Clark's study of the first controversy argued further that already around 400 “Origenism” had become a construct, each of its opponents building a different version of it and using the charge to prosecute quarrels that were not principally doctrinal.24 The mid-century ressourcement theologians — Henri de Lubac, Hans Urs von Balthasar — had already recovered Origen as a spiritual exegete rather than a Platonizing speculator, and Henri Crouzel's Origène (1985) remains the mature scholarly statement of the case that the condemned propositions are not his.25 None of this vacates the anathemas, which stand; what it changes is the question of whom they hit. That question, and the several particular charges, are weighed separately as the arguments below are added.
