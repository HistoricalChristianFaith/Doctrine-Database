The Condemnation of Origen

Origen of Alexandria (c. 185–c. 253) died in communion with the Church. He had been tortured under Decius and survived it, honoured as a confessor; he was the most productive and the most quoted Christian teacher of his age, and for a century afterwards the man whose commentaries every serious exegete in East and West worked from. Three hundred years later his name stands in a list of arch-heretics between Eutyches and the anathematized dead. This page asks how that happened, and whether it was fair.

The subject here is not Origen's theology as such but the verdicts passed on it: a timeline of judgements, earliest first — who first called him heretical, on what charges, on whose authority, and how the charge-sheet changed between Demetrius of Alexandria in the 230s and the emperor Justinian in the 540s, and after. Its “witnesses” are therefore dated acts and documents — a bishop's synod, a rival's dialogue, a martyr's defence from prison, a heresy-catalogue, a papal letter, an imperial edict, a conciliar anathema, a chanted liturgical list, a schoolman's textbook, a humanist's thesis, a printed edition, a modern monograph — each footnoted to its source. Two features of the story recur and are worth watching for: the charges are remarkably stable from about 300 onwards, and they are almost always drawn from hostile excerpts of a single book, Peri Archōn (On First Principles), whose Greek original had by then largely ceased to circulate.

The bitter Latin quarrel this controversy produced between Jerome and Rufinus in 393–411 is told on its own timeline, and its Eastern half — Theophilus of Alexandria's destruction of John Chrysostom — on another; this page gives those years only the space their place in the whole arc requires. The doctrine that drew the heaviest fire, the pre-existence of souls, is traced as a belief in its own right on the origin of the soul. Origen appears on this wiki also as a witness on some sixteen belief-timelines, from the canon and purgatory to astrology and the Nephilim — which is itself part of the point: a condemned man whom the tradition never stopped reading.

Timeline

Origen marks out the open questions himself (Peri Archōn, c. 229)

Nearly everything later condemned comes from one work, written at Alexandria before Origen left it, and its preface is the first thing any fair account of the condemnation has to reckon with. Origen there divides Christian teaching in two. On some points “the holy apostles, in preaching the faith of Christ, delivered themselves with the utmost clearness”; on others they stated only that a thing is so, “leaving, however, the grounds of their statements to be examined into by those who should deserve the excellent gifts of the Spirit.” Into the second class he explicitly puts the origin of the soul — whether it “is derived from the seed by a process of traducianism… or whether it has any other beginning” is, he says, “not distinguished with sufficient clearness in the teaching of the Church” — and the nature of the devil and his angels, of whom “the teaching of the Church has laid down that these beings exist indeed; but what they are, or how they exist, it has not explained with sufficient clearness.”1 The propositions later anathematized are, on their author's own account, offered as investigations of questions he took the Church to have left open — a framing his accusers uniformly declined to accept, and one that later readers have had to weigh against the fact that he sometimes argues those investigations hard.

Demetrius of Alexandria deposes him (c. 231–233)

The first sentence passed on Origen was not a verdict on his teaching. Sent to Greece “on account of a pressing necessity in connection with ecclesiastical affairs”, he travelled through Palestine “and was ordained as presbyter in Caesarea by the bishops of that country” — without the consent of his own bishop, Demetrius of Alexandria. Eusebius, who had read the papers and wrote a defence of Origen in six books, then declines to narrate what followed: “The matters that were agitated concerning him on this account, and the decisions on these matters by those who presided over the churches… demand a separate treatise.”2

Behind the ordination lay a grievance fifteen years old. On an earlier stay in Palestine, Origen — then a layman — had been asked by the bishops there to preach; Demetrius objected, in a letter they answered, “that such a thing was never heard of before, neither has hitherto taken place, that laymen should preach in the presence of bishops.” Alexander of Jerusalem and Theoctistus of Caesarea replied with precedents: “I know not how he comes to say what is plainly untrue. For whenever persons able to instruct the brethren are found, they are exhorted by the holy bishops to preach to the people.” Demetrius recalled him to Alexandria by letter, and he went back.3 The ordination of 231 settled over his bishop's head the question of standing that had been open since.

The proceedings themselves survive only through Photius's ninth-century summary of Pamphilus's lost defence, and they were two sittings, not one: the ordination was “contrary to the rule of the Church”, the bishop's “love… changed to hate”, and “a synod of bishops and some presbyters was summoned to condemn Origen. According to Pamphilus, it was decided that he must not remain in Alexandria or teach there, but that he should be allowed to retain his priesthood. But Demetrius and some Egyptian bishops, with the assent of those who had formerly supported him, also deprived him of his sacred office.”4 Expulsion first, then deposition; the stated ground in both is his standing, not his teaching.

The one personal charge that surfaces is the self-castration Origen had performed on himself as a young catechist. Demetrius had at the time “admired greatly the daring nature of the act” and urged him on in his teaching; now, “overcome by human weakness, [he] wrote of his deed as most foolish to the bishops throughout the world” — having, Eusebius adds, “nothing else that he could say against him, save this deed of his boyhood.”5 Jerome, writing in 392 and still an admirer, puts it as pure temper: Origen “offended Demetrius, who was so wildly enraged at him that he wrote everywhere to injure his reputation.”6 Origen left Egypt for Caesarea; Demetrius died within about two years, after forty-three in the see, and the school Origen had built passed to his own pupil Heraclas, who succeeded him in it.7 The precedent set here shaped everything after it: a sentence against Origen issued from Alexandria and circulated by letter to other churches. Whether the circulated letters also charged him with false teaching — and how far a record written entirely by his defenders can settle that — is weighed below.

Palestine declines to receive the sentence (from 231)

The Alexandrian verdict was not universally received — the first and most important fact about it. Alexander of Jerusalem and Theoctistus of Caesarea, the bishops who had ordained him, “attended on him constantly, as their only teacher, and allowed him to expound the Divine Scriptures, and to perform the other duties pertaining to ecclesiastical discourse”; Firmilian of Caesarea in Cappadocia summoned him into his own province “for the benefit of the churches” and afterwards travelled to Judea to sit under him.8 For the remaining two decades of his life Origen taught, preached, and wrote as a presbyter of the Palestinian church, and the bishops of three provinces treated the Egyptian sentence as having no force over them. Jerome, counting the provinces a century and a half later, reckons that Origen “stands condemned by his bishop, Demetrius, only the bishops of Palestine, Arabia, Phenicia, and Achaia dissenting”, adding that “imperial Rome consents to his condemnation, and even convenes a senate to censure him, not… because of the novelty or heterodoxy of his doctrines, but because men could not tolerate the incomparable eloquence and knowledge which, when once he opened his lips, made others seem dumb.”9 That is partisan — and the Roman synod Jerome describes is attested nowhere else — but the geography is not in dispute: the sentence held in Egypt and lapsed everywhere Origen was personally known.

Tortured under Decius; dies in communion (250 – c. 253)

In the Decian persecution Origen was arrested and tortured — “bonds and bodily tortures and torments under the iron collar and in the dungeon; and how for many days with his feet stretched four spaces in the stocks he bore patiently the threats of fire” — under a judge who “strove eagerly with all his might not to end his life.” He did not recant.10 He survived, broken, and died a few years later at Tyre, where he was buried, in his sixty-ninth year.11 No church had excommunicated him for his teaching; he died a confessor of the faith, and the next generation of Greek theologians — Dionysius of Alexandria, Gregory Thaumaturgus, Pamphilus, Eusebius — were his pupils or his pupils' pupils.

Methodius attacks the doctrine, not the man (c. 290 – 311)

The first sustained theological assault comes from Methodius, bishop of Olympus in Lycia, martyred at the end of the last persecution. Jerome's catalogue lists three anti-Origen works among his six titles — “an excellent work On the resurrection, against Origen and On the Pythonissa and On free will, also against Origen” — and this is the first point in the story at which an Origenist position, rather than Origen's person or his standing, is what is under attack.12 The chief work, a dialogue on the resurrection called the Aglaophon, goes after the pre-existence of souls — Origen “said that the body was given to the soul as a fetter after the fall, and that previously it lived without a body”, and held that “the soul alone is man, as did Plato” — and, above all, after Origen's account of the risen body: that what rises is the persisting form of the flesh “stamped upon another spiritual body”, the material substrate being in flux and so no part of what is raised. Methodius answers that a form has no existence apart from the matter it shapes and perishes with it, as the shape of a melted statue “has no longer a substantial existence.”13 A companion treatise, On Things Created, attacks the argument that God cannot have become a Creator — “since there is no workman without some work… so neither is there an Almighty without an object of His power” — and so that the world is co-eternal.14

Three things are worth noticing. Methodius disputes doctrines, in the ordinary form of a philosophical dialogue; he does not call Origen a heretic, invokes no ecclesiastical authority, asks no synod to condemn a dead presbyter, and in the middle of the attack cites him approvingly on a point of etymology.15 Origen's own school nevertheless answered in kind: in the sixth book of the Apology for Origen, Eusebius “makes the same complaint against Methodius the bishop and martyr” that Jerome would later face — not that he had misdescribed the doctrine, but that he had no standing to object, having drawn on Origen before.16 And his work survives chiefly because Epiphanius later quarried most of it into his own heresy-catalogue — the route by which a third-century theological disagreement became fourth-century evidence of heresy.

Pamphilus and Eusebius answer the charges from prison (c. 307–310)

Pamphilus of Caesarea, presbyter and keeper of the library in which Origen's books had been collected, was arrested in November 307 and beheaded on 16 February 310; he spent the intervening months composing, with his pupil Eusebius, an Apology for Origen in five books, to which Eusebius added a sixth after his death. Only the first survives, in Rufinus's Latin of 397, and it is the earliest surviving inventory of the charge-sheet. Pamphilus enumerates nine accusations before answering them: that Origen holds the Son unbegotten; that the Son was emitted, as Valentinus taught; that Christ is a mere man, as Paul of Samosata taught; that Christ's acts were done “only in appearance, and by allegory”; that he preaches two Christs; that he dissolves the historical narratives of Scripture; that he denies the punishment of sinners and the resurrection of the dead; that his account of the soul is erroneous; and that he teaches transmigration into animals.17 His first observation is that they cannot all be true together — the first four contradict one another, “for malice is blind” — and his method throughout is to answer not in his own words but by quoting the accused at length, chiefly out of the very book the accusations come from. Two things follow for this timeline. The accusations were circulating a full generation before Epiphanius, and a martyr thought them worth a book, which shows how far from settled the question then was. And the list is not the one that was finally enforced: the four pre-Nicene Christological charges disappear, the doctrines the sixth century anathematized — the fall of souls from a heavenly state, the restoration of the devil, the end identical with the beginning — are absent here, and of the nine only transmigration survives intact into Justinian's edict. The indictment grew, and it grew in the fourth century.

Epiphanius makes Origen a heresy in his own right (c. 375–377)

In the Panarion, his catalogue of eighty heresies, Epiphanius of Salamis gives Origen an entry of his own — heresy 64 — alongside the Gnostics and the Arians. This is the decisive formal step: a churchman who died in communion is now listed as a heresiarch under his own name, in the reference work every later heresy-hunter would consult. The catalogue's own index states the charges in one sentence that nearly every later list varies: the Origenists “reject the resurrection of the dead, represent Christ and the Holy Spirit as creatures, allegorize Paradise, the heavens and all the rest, and foolishly say that Christ's kingdom will come to an end.” The chapter itself is unusual for the genre in printing what it attacks — Origen's now-lost commentary on the first psalm, quoted “word for word, so that no one may call my attack on him vexatious,” followed by some fifty chapters of Methodius's dialogue, which survives in Greek only because Epiphanius copied it here. It is also where a hostile life of Origen enters the record: a forced offering of incense under persecution, an expulsion, and a silent weeping sermon at Jerusalem — a story Eusebius knows nothing of, and one Epiphanius prefaces with the admission that “though I have no faith in the exaggerated stories about him, I have not neglected to report what is being said.”18 Epiphanius pressed the same case in a letter to John of Jerusalem which Jerome translated into Latin, and which is why it survives: “The Son, he tells us, cannot see the Father, and the Holy Spirit cannot see the Son”; souls “were once angels in heaven, and… having sinned… have been cast down into this, and have been confined in bodies”; and Origen “denies the resurrection of this flesh, as he most clearly does in his book of explanations of the first psalm.”19

Theophilus of Alexandria: the first synodical condemnation (399–403)

Theophilus of Alexandria had preached against the monks who held that God has a human body — the view Origen's books were quarried to refute — and in 399 a crowd of them came to Alexandria and threatened his life over it. He bought his way out by giving them what they demanded: “I myself also disapprove of Origen's works, and consider those who countenance them deserving of censure.”20 He then expelled the Origenist monks from the monasteries of Nitria, and in 400 convened a council at Alexandria whose synodical letter Jerome translated: “The books of Origen have been read before a council of bishops and unanimously condemned.” Eight errors are enumerated, said to be drawn mainly from Peri Archōn — among them that “the Son compared with us is truth, but compared with the Father he is falsehood”, that “Christ's kingdom will one day come to an end”, and that Christ, having suffered once for men, “will suffer again for the demons.”21 He asked Epiphanius of Salamis for a parallel synod on Cyprus, and got one; Epiphanius “assembled the bishops of Cyprus together, and prohibited the examination of the books of Origen.”22

This is the first time Origen is condemned by standing episcopal synods rather than by an individual polemicist, and the form of the sentence is worth stating exactly, because the later tradition stopped distinguishing it. What the decrees condemned was the books. Socrates, who saw the copy carried to Constantinople in 403, records that in it “they did not excommunicate Origen himself but condemned his books” — though Theophilus had asked for more than that, writing to Epiphanius that a synodical letter should go round “that by universal consent Origen himself may be expressly condemned and also the infamous heresy of which he was the author.”23 No acts of either synod survive; what survives is the correspondence. A Gallic traveller who reached Alexandria in the middle of it reports the bishops pressing for the wider sentence — that Origen's writings, “along with the author himself, and even his whole works, be condemned” — the Origenist monks answering with the thesis that the offending passages had been interpolated by heretics, and the governor of the city called in to disperse them.24 Nor was the decree received everywhere. When Epiphanius read it to the bishops at Constantinople, Theotimus of Scythia refused to subscribe: “I neither choose, Epiphanius, to insult the memory of one who ended his life piously long ago; nor dare I be guilty of so impious an act, as that of condemning what our predecessors did not reject.”25

At Rome in 400, with Rufinus's Latin Peri Archōn in hand, Pope Anastasius I held a synod at Theophilus's prompting and wrote to Simplicianus of Milan: “everything written in days gone by Origen that is contrary to our faith is even by us rejected and condemned… If Origen has put forth any other writings, you are to know that they and their author are alike condemned by me.” Writing to John of Jerusalem the following year he added that Origen's “object was to disintegrate our faith, which is that of the Apostles”, and noted the imperial decree “by which every one who serves God is warned against the reading of Origen.”26 Whether the Roman sentence reached Origen's person or only his books — the pope who passed it wrote in the same letter that he neither knew nor wished to know who Origen was — is weighed on its own page. Within three years the same campaign had deposed the bishop of Constantinople — told on the Theophilus–Chrysostom timeline — and had broken Jerome's friendship with Rufinus, told on the Origenist controversy. What was condemned in 400 was, in every case, doctrine drawn from Peri Archōn as excerpted by hostile readers; no numbered anathema-list yet existed. And within months of the campaign's end Theophilus was reading Origen again: asked why he countenanced what he had publicly condemned, he answered that “Origen's books are like a meadow enameled with flowers of every kind. If, therefore, I chance to find a beautiful one among them, I cull it: but whatever appears to me to be thorny, I step over, as that which would prick.”27

Augustine narrows the Latin charge-sheet (414–428)

The question reached Augustine from Spain, not from Palestine. In 414 the priest Paulus Orosius came to Hippo to report that Priscillianism had been answered in his province with books of Origen's, and that the readers had swallowed the cure and the poison together; he set out six articles of what was being taught, among them that the world was made last of all so that souls which had sinned beforehand might be purged in it, and that the eternal fire is neither real fire nor eternal. Augustine's reply refuses the simple verdict. He grants that the province got “no small good from those books, in the very matter where the error is mortal” — the Trinity, where Priscillian was worst — and then forbids exactly one thing, and forbids it as a trespass rather than as a refuted opinion: “do not dare to think anything about the correction of the devil and his angels and their restoration to their former state… because we ought to add nothing of our own presumption to the final sentence of the highest and most truthful Judge.”28

The City of God states the case twice, and both statements passed into the Latin inheritance. In XI.23 he names the pre-existent fall — souls which “sinned by abandoning God” and “merited different degrees of debasement from heaven to earth, and diverse bodies as prison-houses” — and, unusually among Origen's accusers, cites his source: “in the books which he entitles peri archōn… this is his sentiment, this his utterance.” The objections are arguments, not authorities: Genesis gives creation one cause, that a good God should make good creatures; and if bodies were graded penalties the devils would have the grossest bodies of all, whereas “the wickedest devil possesses an ethereal body, while man… received even before his sin a body of clay.”29 In XXI.17, arguing amicably with the tender-hearted who expect the damned to be released, he adds the sentence this timeline turns on: Origen went further than they, expecting the devil himself to be freed, “but the Church, not without reason, condemned him for this and other errors, especially for his theory of the ceaseless alternation of happiness and misery” — a doctrine in which, Augustine says, Origen “lost even the credit of being merciful”, since a blessedness that can be lost again is no blessedness.30 Written in the 420s, more than a century before any council touched the question, that is a Latin bishop treating Origen's condemnation as an accomplished fact of church discipline — on the strength of the synods and papal letters of 400–401, which are the only proceedings there were.

His catalogue of heresies, compiled about 428, shows him sorting the inherited charge-sheet rather than adding to it. Entry 43 opens by quoting Epiphanius verbatim — the resurrection denied, Christ and the Spirit made creatures, paradise and the heavens allegorized — and then steps back from three of the four: “those who defend him say that he taught the Father, the Son and the Holy Spirit to be of one and the same substance, and did not reject the resurrection of the dead; though those who have read more of him try to convict him on these points too.” What he owns without qualification is the purgation and release of all the damned including the devil, followed after immense ages by a relapse of the freed into the same evils, “these alternating turns of blessedness and misery of the rational creation” running on for ever.31 The Latin West's standing entry on Origen thus came to rest on a single doctrine, and on a cyclical version of it that Peri Archōn raises as a difficulty rather than asserts. On the question the whole controversy turned on — where any soul comes from — Augustine refused all his life to decide, as that timeline records.

Vincent of Lérins: the great trial of the Church (434)

By the fifth century the Latin verdict is settled enough to be used as a teaching example. Vincent of Lérins heads a chapter of his Commonitorium “The Error of Origen a great Trial to the Church” and makes him the type of the learned teacher whose gifts magnify his error: “this Origen, such and so great as he was, wantonly abusing the grace of God, rashly following the bent of his own genius…” — with the result that “very many were led astray from the integrity of the faith, not by any human excellencies of this so great man, this so great doctor, this so great prophet, but, as the event showed, by the too perilous trial which he proved to be.”32 Vincent also records the mitigating suggestion, already current, that the offending texts had been tampered with — the thesis Rufinus had pressed a generation earlier, weighed on its own page.

The Latin book-lists: Gennadius and the Decretum Gelasianum (c. 480 – c. 550)

In the West the verdict next appears not as a trial but as an entry in a handbook, which is how it reached the Middle Ages. Gennadius, presbyter of Marseilles, turned the charges into flat dogmatic canons in his Book of Church Doctrines: the risen body will not be exchanged for another, “not according to Origen”, but the same corruptible flesh will rise incorruptible; after the resurrection and the judgement we are not to believe in a restoration to come, “which Origen raves about”, whereby demons or wicked men, purged as it were by torments, return to the angelic dignity in which they were created; and the souls of men are not from the beginning among the other intellectual natures nor created all at once, “as Origen imagines.”33 The three propositions are Augustine's apokatastasis, Methodius's resurrection charge, and the pre-existence of souls; there is no argument, only a canon. The treatise circulated for centuries under Augustine's own name, which multiplied its authority — the same accident that gave the West a flat creationist ruling on the soul in Augustine's voice, told on his page there. In the same years his catalogue of writers preserves the milder Egyptian ruling in the form it took after the dust settled: a synod at Alexandria had decreed “that Origen is to be read, though cautiously, by those who are wise, for the good that is in him, and is to be rejected by the less able on account of the evil.”34

The other Latin list is the so-called Decretum Gelasianum, a register of books to be received and not received which travelled under the name of Pope Gelasius I and was for centuries read as a papal decree. It is neither: von Dobschütz's edition established that it is an anonymous private compilation of the first half of the sixth century, most probably Italian, and F. C. Burkitt summarized the case — the section ascribed to Damasus quotes Augustine, who was writing about 416, so the ascription “is of no historical value”, and “had it been an official decree of Gelasius it would have been known and used by Dionysius Exiguus and Cassiodorus. Thus these famous Lists represent no Papal ordinance, but are the production of an anonymous scholar of the sixth century.”35 Its ruling on Origen is worth quoting because of where it sends the reader for the criterion: “likewise some works of Origen, which the blessed man Jerome does not reject, we receive to be read, but we say that the rest with their author must be refused.” Eusebius survives the same list only in part, having “wrote one book in praise and to excuse Origen the schismatic”; and a work called the Repentance of Origen is listed among the apocrypha.36 Two features of the Latin settlement are visible in that one clause. The sentence now falls on the author as well as the books — “the rest with their author” — and the standard by which a Latin reader is to sort them is not a synod, a list of propositions, or the text of Peri Archōn, but Jerome's approval.

Justinian's edict to Menas: the charges become anathemas (543)

The sixth-century revival of the quarrel was monastic and Palestinian, and its occasion was a fight between rival parties of monks rather than anything Origen wrote. An Origenist current owing more to Evagrius of Pontus than to Origen had settled in the Judaean lauras; Sabas of the Great Laura went to Constantinople in 531 to ask for imperial help against it and got nothing; his successor Gelasius expelled the Origenists from the Great Laura in 537 and they regrouped at the New Laura, while two of their party, Domitian and Theodore Askidas, were made bishops and stayed at court. The anti-Origenist monks drew up a memorandum, the papal representative at Constantinople carried it to the emperor, and in 543 Justinian issued an edict addressed to Menas, patriarch of Constantinople, quoting Peri Archōn at length and appending nine anathemas — the first time the charges take the numbered, canonical form in which they would be handed down.37 The first condemns anyone who “says or holds that the souls of human beings pre-exist, as previously minds and holy powers, but that they reached satiety with divine contemplation and turned to what is worse… and were made to descend into bodies as a punishment”; the ninth, anyone who holds “that the punishment of demons and impious human beings is temporary and that it will have an end at some time.”38

The document is often described as ratified by a synod, and the bishops resident in the capital did subscribe it; but its authority was in the first instance imperial, the canons being sent to the patriarchs, in Richard Price's summary, “not for their confirmation but for their circulation.”39 Everyone signed, Theodore Askidas included, and he remained the most influential churchman at court for another fifteen years. Two things about the list are worth carrying forward. Only some of it tracks Peri Archōn: the first and ninth anathemas reach doctrines Origen really canvassed, but the spherical resurrection body, the ensouled stars, and the soul of Christ united to the Word before the incarnation belong to the sixth-century monastic system rather than to anything he wrote. And because the edict quotes his Greek at length, it became, in Paul Koetschau's edition of 1913 and the English translation made from it, a principal source for the text of Peri Archōn itself — the accused's book reconstructed out of the prosecutor's brief, an editorial decision since reversed.40

The council of 553: the fifteen anathemas and the eleventh canon (5 May – 2 June 553)

“Condemned by the Fifth Ecumenical Council” is the one thing most people know about Origen, and three separate things travel under it. The council itself was summoned for other business entirely: its subject was the Three Chapters — Theodore of Mopsuestia, Theodoret of Cyrrhus, Ibas of Edessa — which Justinian had already condemned by edict and now wanted endorsed, and which Pope Vigilius, brought to Constantinople under guard in 546, would not endorse. Eight sessions ran from 5 May to 2 June 553 under Eutychius of Constantinople, with the pope absent and his name struck from the diptychs; the bishops issued fourteen anathemas against the Three Chapters, and Vigilius capitulated the following December.41 Origen is not in the summons, and not in any of the correspondence surrounding it.42

The first of the three things is a set of fifteen anathemas against Origen and the Origenists — fuller and far more explicitly Christological than Justinian's nine of 543, and the text most often quoted as the condemnation. Its first article anathematizes anyone who “asserts the fabulous pre-existence of souls, and shall assert the monstrous restoration which follows from it”; its last, anyone who says “that the life of the spirits shall be like to the life which was in the beginning… so that the end and the beginning shall be alike, and that the end shall be the true measure of the beginning.”43 Between those two the fifteen describe a whole system — undifferentiated minds, a fall by degrees into graded bodies, ensouled stars, a spherical resurrection body, an end in which every spirit is equal to Christ — which is the system of the sixth-century Palestinian monks called Isochristoi and is nowhere set out in that form in Peri Archōn. Origen's own name occurs in none of them. Whether the council in session passed them is the most contested question in this whole history: the text survives under a seventeenth-century Vienna manuscript's caption ascribing it to “the 165 holy Fathers of the holy fifth Synod”, the nineteenth-century editors of the acts in English conceded that “after all is said the matter remains involved in the greatest obscurity”,44 and the prevailing modern view, following Franz Diekamp's study of 1899, assigns them to the bishops already gathered at Constantinople in the weeks before the council opened. Norman Tanner's standard edition accordingly prints the acts of 553 without them, noting that “recent studies have shown that these anathemas cannot be attributed to this council.”45 Against that stands the earliest narrative witness, Cyril of Scythopolis, writing within five years and reporting that the assembled fathers anathematized Origen together with Theodore, Evagrius and Didymus — a Judaean monk of the party that had petitioned for exactly that outcome.46

The second is the council's eleventh canon, which does contain Origen's name: “If anyone does not anathematize Arius, Eunomius, Macedonius, Apollinarius, Nestorius, Eutyches and Origen, as well as their heretical books… let him be anathema.”47 That is not a trial but a loyalty formula requiring assent to condemnations already made, and Origen stands in it as the last name on a list — last, though he is by three centuries the earliest of the seven. Whether the name stood there in 553 is disputed: the Greek acts are lost and the council is known from a Latin version; the parallel roll in Justinian's Homonoia omits Origen, and Vigilius's own documents never name him, which Ilaria Ramelli takes as evidence of later insertion.48 The third is the reception, which drew none of these distinctions and is treated in the next block.

Maximus the Confessor rebuilds the system from inside (c. 628)

The most serious answer Origen ever received came from a man who never named him. Writing about 628, some seventy-five years after the anathemas, Maximus the Confessor took up a phrase of Gregory Nazianzen — that we are “a portion of God” who have “flowed down from above” — and found it being read in the condemned sense. He states that reading fairly and at length before touching it: “According to the opinion of these people, there once existed a unity of rational beings, by virtue of which we were connatural with God, in whom we had our remaining and abode. In addition to this they speak of a ‘movement’ that came about, as a result of which the rational beings were variously dispersed, prompting God to look toward the creation of this corporeal world, so that He could bind them in bodies as a punishment for their former sins.”49 His answer is not a citation of the council. It is an argument, and it works by reversing the order of the terms: coming-into-being precedes motion, so motion cannot be a fall away from a prior static unity but is the created nature tending toward an end it has not yet reached. “If, then, coming into being must necessarily be posited before beings can begin to move, it follows that motion is subsequent to the manifestation of being”; and therefore “it cannot be maintained that a movement of rational beings previously at rest in a primordial unity subsequently brought corporeal bodies into being.”50

Two further moves make the block worth its place here. Against the fall-through-satiety on which the whole scheme depends, Maximus argues that the Good cannot cloy: “satiety by definition is the quenching of appetite, and this happens either because appetite desired things that were trivial, or because it was repulsed and nauseated by things that were base and repugnant… It is obvious that neither of these can apply to God, who by nature is infinite and infinitely attractive, and who rather increases the appetites of those who enjoy Him owing to their participation in that which has no limit.” And having demolished the pre-existence of minds, he keeps a pre-existence: not of souls but of the logoi, the divine intentions of each creature, “steadfastly fixed” in God from eternity while the creatures themselves are made in time. That lets Gregory's phrase stand: “We are, then, and are called ‘portions of God’ because of the logoi of our being that exist eternally in God. Moreover, we are said to have ‘flowed down from above’ because we have failed to move in a manner consistent with the logos according to which we were created and which preexists in God.”51 Maximus is sharp about the misuse of Gregory — “their behavior is unethical and irreligious” — but throughout the discussion his opponents are only “some people” and “certain individuals”; Origen's name does not occur. Nicholas Constas, who edited and translated the work, judges that the Origenism refuted here comes not from a live seventh-century party but “from the conciliar documents of the preceding century, especially the edicts of Justinian”, and that because those documents “simply condemned Origenism without addressing the deeper conceptual issues, Maximos was impelled to dive down into the wreckage of Origen's system, in order to reconstruct it on sound philosophical and theological principles” — “overcoming the weaknesses of Origen's theology from within and preserving its essential truths for the Christian tradition.”52 That is what a condemnation looks like when it is taken seriously rather than merely enforced: the anathema settles which conclusions are shut, and leaves the question they were answers to still open.

The name in the later councils and the Byzantine lists (680–787 and after)

Two subsequent ecumenical councils treat the matter as closed and name Origen in company that was to become fixed. Constantinople III (680–681) refers to “the fifth holy synod, the latest of them, which was gathered here against Theodore of Mopsuestia, Origen, Didymus and Evagrius”;53 Nicaea II (787) anathematizes “the mythical speculations of Origen, Evagrius and Didymus, as did the fifth synod, that assembled at Constantinople.”54 Both are ratifications by reference rather than fresh trials; but they establish that from the seventh century onward the Church read 553 as having condemned Origen personally, and they harden the Origen–Didymus–Evagrius triad into a formula. It is that formula, not the third-century books, which the medieval East and West inherited.

Photius reads the whole dossier (c. 845)

Three centuries after the anathemas, the most learned reader in Constantinople was still working through the condemned books and the defences of them. Photius — later patriarch, then a layman compiling reading notes for his brother — devotes three notices of his Bibliotheca to the Origen file, and the mixture in them is the point. On Peri Archōn itself he is hostile and brief, giving a book-by-book table of contents with the objections inserted as they arise: “Read Origen's four books On First Principles… In this his statements are often blasphemous; thus, he asserts that the Son was created by the Father, the Holy Ghost by the Son… He also makes other strange and impious statements, indulging in frivolous talk about the migration of souls, the stars being alive, and the like.”55 An anonymous five-book apology for Origen gets a fuller notice, and with it a fresh list of fifteen counts on which the apologist says Origen was falsely accused — that prayer should not be offered to the Son, that rational natures enter the bodies of irrational beings, that there is neither eternal punishment nor resurrection of the flesh, that the soul of the Saviour was the same as the soul of Adam, and the rest. Photius reports the apologist's two lines of defence — that the offending passages were “only intended as a rhetorical exercise, or that they were foisted into his writings by certain heterodox persons” — without adopting either, and closes the notice by rejecting the whole plea: “But, my dear sir, if any one is shown to be not altogether impious, this is no reason why he should escape punishment for obvious blasphemies.”56

Set against that, the notice on Pamphilus and Eusebius's Apology — the source from which this page's account of Demetrius's synods is drawn — contains no denunciation at all. It is plain biography, and source-critical: Photius sets the tradition that Origen was martyred under Decius beside the tradition that he died at Tyre in his sixty-ninth year and adjudicates between them (“This is the truer account, unless the letters supposed to have been written by him after the Decian persecution are spurious”), and narrates the Alexandrian quarrel with Demetrius, not Origen, as the man whose “love… changed to hate.”57 Elsewhere in the same collection he transcribes Stephen Gobar's sixth-century pro-and-con dossier on Origen, which records that Athanasius and Titus of Bostra approved him on many points, that Gregory Nazianzen called him “friend of beauty and goodness” and Gregory of Nyssa remembered him with praise, and that “Theophilus and Epiphanius detest Origen” — and Photius lets the dossier stand without repudiating any father in it.58 The verdict is settled; the reading is not over.

The doctrines anathematized again, without the name (1082)

The text that actually told a Byzantine congregation who the church's enemies were was not the acts of a council but the Synodikon of Orthodoxy, chanted every first Sunday of Lent from 843 onward. It names them at length — Arius “the first fighter against God and leader of the heresies”, Nestorius, Valentinus, Paul of Samosata, Sabellius, Eutyches, Dioscorus, Severus, Sergius, Pyrrhus, “and simply all heretics”. In the received text Origen is not among them, and neither are Didymus or Evagrius.59 What happened instead is more interesting than a name on a list. In March 1082 the philosopher John Italos was tried at Constantinople, and eleven chapters against him were added to the Synodikon — the first major accretion to it since 843. They anathematize those who “receive the Platonic ideas as true”; those who say that at the last and common resurrection human beings “will rise and be judged with other bodies, and not with those in which they lived the present life”; and, in the tenth chapter, those “who receive and hand on the vain and Greek sayings: that there is a pre-existence of souls, and that all things were not made and brought forth out of nothing, and that there is an end to punishment or a restoration again of the creation and of human affairs.”60 That is the substance of 543 and 553 — the pre-existence of souls, the changed resurrection body, the end of punishment, the restoration of all things — re-enacted five centuries later against a living Constantinopolitan philosopher, with Origen's name nowhere in it. In the East the doctrines outlived the man's name in the anathema; the propositions were what the church was guarding against, and it did not need him to state them.

The Latin schools: condemned in the handbook, quoted on every other page (12th century)

The West inherited the opposite arrangement. There the name was formally condemned and the books were read continuously, because Rufinus and Jerome had put most of the homilies and commentaries into Latin before the quarrel closed — so that, as the standard English edition of Rufinus puts it, “a considerable part of the works of the great Alexandrian have floated down across the ocean of the Dark Ages, and, while lost in their native Greek, have in their Latin garb come to enrich the later civilization of the West.”61 The clearest single specimen of the resulting arrangement is Peter Abelard's Sic et Non, the twelfth century's central textbook of theological method. Its prologue prints the Decretum Gelasianum's ruling verbatim under the heading “From the decrees of Pope Gelasius on authentic books” — “certain small works of Origen, which the most blessed man Jerome does not reject, we receive to be read; but all the rest, with their author, we say must be refused” — and lists the Repentance of Origen among the apocrypha, exactly as the sixth-century compiler had. In the same prologue Abelard defends the practice of quoting him, by way of Jerome: “If this is a crime, let the confessor Hilary be accused, who translated the interpretation of the psalms and the homilies on Job out of his books” — and cites Origen's own disclaimer, as Jerome reports it, that the offending speculations “are not, in our judgement, dogmas, but questions only, and put forward, so that they should not seem entirely untouched.”62

Then, having reprinted the sentence, Abelard cites Origen as an authority fourteen times in the body of the work — including on whether Christ in descending to hell freed all, and on whether God punishes the same sin both here and hereafter, which are the questions he was condemned for. And in the Historia calamitatum, defending his own castration, he reaches for Origen's as the precedent and calls him “the greatest of Christian philosophers” (maximum illum Christianorum philosophorum, summum Christianorum philosophorum) — the phrase a twelfth-century master could still apply to a man his own textbook lists among books to be refused.63 The limit of that rehabilitation is worth recording too: Dante, who found room in the Commedia for pagans, heretics and Muslims alike, never names Origen at all, and the one attempt by later commentators to identify him with an unnamed figure in Paradiso X has not been accepted.64

“Is Origen saved?”: Pico, Merlin, and the printed Origen (1486–1574)

In December 1486 Giovanni Pico della Mirandola published nine hundred theses for public disputation at Rome. The last of the twenty-nine he offered on his own theological opinion reads: “It is more reasonable to believe that Origen is saved than to believe that he is damned.”65 A commission appointed by Innocent VIII in February 1487 indicted thirteen of the nine hundred, and this was among them; Pico reports the censure in his own words — the masters said the conclusion was “rash and reprehensible, and savouring of heresy, and contrary to the determination of the universal Church” — and answered it with a treatise, the Disputatio de salute Origenis, laid out in seven articles of which the fifth is the question itself. His argument is anachronism plus non-obstinacy: on the point that mattered, the origin of the soul, “Origen was not bound in his own time to believe explicitly all that we are now bound to believe about the soul, because it was neither set down plainly in Scripture nor yet determined by the Church as it was afterwards”; and no one can produce evidence that he repented, because no one can produce evidence that he thought he had erred. The pope condemned all nine hundred theses in a bull of 4 August 1487 — which does not name Origen — and Alexander VI absolved Pico in 1493.66

The printing press then did what no synod had contemplated. In 1512 Jacques Merlin brought out at Paris the first collected Latin Origen, in four folio tomes, and prefaced the third with an Apologia for him; Noël Béda, the syndic of the Paris faculty of theology, attacked it and wrote a refutation, though the faculty itself seems never to have pronounced on the matter.67 Erasmus finished a far better edition for Froben at Basel; it appeared in September 1536, two months after his death, and his short preface to the reader ends: “Nowhere is Origen not aflame, but nowhere is he more aflame than where he handles the words and deeds of Christ.” His private estimate was blunter still, in a letter of 1518 to Johann Eck: “A single page of Origen teaches me more Christian philosophy than ten of Augustine” — a sentence he quotes as a saying of his own, and immediately qualifies with the news that he intends to edit Augustine too. His serious point about the condemnation is methodological, made in the preface to a fragment of Origen on Matthew in 1527: “We weigh Origen by the manners, rites and dogmas of these times. If you want to judge how absurd that is, reverse the scene” — and imagine the schoolmen judged by the third century.68 The Reformation did not follow him. Luther, who had already written that Jerome and “his Origen” “filled the world with these trifles”, told his table in 1532 that “in the whole of Origen there is not one word about Christ”, and elsewhere that “Origen I have already excommunicated.”69 The Catholic side went the other way: Gilbert Génébrard's Paris edition of 1574 announces on its title page that its author is “the great and ancient interpreter of the Scriptures and the second master of the Church after the apostles.”70

Defended, censured again, and finally edited (1648–1759)

The seventeenth century argued the case out in print, and it did not divide along the line one would expect. The fullest defence was a Jesuit's: Pierre Halloix's Origenes defensus (Liège, 1648) — which Rome then put on the Index donec corrigatur, the decree communicated to its author in 1650 and published in 1655. The roster of Origen's modern defenders drawn up later in the century by the Dominican Noël Alexandre — Pico, Merlin, Génébrard, Étienne Binet, Halloix — is a list of Catholics, and the sharpest early-modern hostility came from Protestants.71 The exception is England, where a group around the Cambridge Platonists revived the condemned doctrines themselves. An anonymous Letter of Resolution concerning Origen and the Chief of his Opinions (London, 1661), traditionally ascribed to George Rust, defends the pre-existence of souls and concludes that “the punishment of the damned, as it implies the sense of pain, shall not be eternal in the highest sense of the word”, and argues from the Church of England's own silence — the Thirty-Nine Articles dropped the Edwardine article condemning the restorers of that opinion. Henry More, who publicly denied writing it, called Origen “that Miracle of the Christian World” and disposed of the Fifth Council in a sentence that concedes the authority while voiding the verdict: the particulars of his condemnation were removed from the records by the same power that occasioned it, “for it was their Wrong, not their Fault, that they were misinformed.”72

The scholarship of the same period was more careful than either party. Pierre-Daniel Huet's edition of the Greek exegetical remains (Rouen, 1668) carried a three-part study of Origen's life, doctrine and writings, the Origeniana, which Huet undertook precisely because the thing had been done “sparingly by some, carelessly by others, and fully, with a mind free of either love or hatred, by no one at all”. His verdict is a qualified defence and not a vindication: Origen held the orthodox doctrine of the Trinity, and “besmirched it with Platonic ravings.”73 The definitive edition was Benedictine. Charles Delarue of the Congregation of Saint-Maur brought out the first two volumes of the Opera omnia at Paris in 1733, dedicated to Clement XII, the third in 1740, and the fourth — begun by him and finished after his death by his nephew Charles-Vincent — in 1759, with Huet's Origeniana reprinted in it. That edition, reprinted by Migne in 1857 as volumes 11–17 of the Greek Patrologia, is the text in which almost everyone has read Origen ever since.74 A man condemned by an ecumenical council was, by the middle of the eighteenth century, a papally dedicated monastic edition in four folio volumes.

The modern reassessment (20th century onwards)

The twentieth century reopened the question along a distinction the sixth century had no reason to draw: between Origen and Origenism. The doctrines anathematized in 543 and 553 — a pre-existent equality of all minds, the risen body as a sphere, the eventual equality of every spirit with Christ — belong demonstrably to a sixth-century Palestinian monastic system indebted to Evagrius of Pontus, and correspond only loosely, and in places not at all, to what stands in Peri Archōn. Elizabeth Clark's study of the first controversy argued further that already around 400 “Origenism” had become a construct, each of its opponents building a different version of it and using the charge to prosecute quarrels that were not principally doctrinal.75 The mid-century ressourcement theologians — Henri de Lubac, Hans Urs von Balthasar — had already recovered Origen as a spiritual exegete rather than a Platonizing speculator, and Henri Crouzel's Origène (1985) remains the mature scholarly statement of the case that the condemned propositions are not his.76 None of this vacates the anathemas, which stand; what it changes is the question of whom they hit. That question, and the several particular charges, are weighed separately below.

Arguments

Off-timeline questions this history raises, weighed on their evidence:

  • Was the first condemnation of Origen a disciplinary sentence rather than a verdict on his doctrine? (Demetrius's Alexandrian synods, c. 231–232) — sound (the stated ground in every surviving report is the irregular ordination, with the castration used in support; the stronger claim that doctrine played no part at all in the quarrel goes beyond what the evidence will carry)
  • Did Rome in 400–401 condemn Origen's books only, and not the man? — weak (the Egyptian and Cypriot synods drew that line, but Anastasius I's own letter condemns the writings “and their author” alike; what survives is the narrower point that no canonical anathema issued, and that the pope had never read him)
