The Historicity of Noah's Flood

Genesis 6–9 tells of a Flood that destroyed all humanity except Noah, his family, and the creatures in the ark. The New Testament treats it as real history (2 Pet 2:5; 3:5–6; 1 Pet 3:20; Heb 11:7) and as a type — of judgment and of baptism — and Christ sets "the days of Noah" beside his own coming (Matt 24:37–39; Luke 17:26–27). Christian interpreters divide along several axes: whether the Flood is history at all (against myth or pure allegory); whether it was anthropologically universal (all men outside the ark perished) or also geographically global (water over the whole planet); and how the inherited reading of a real Flood relates to the modern "young-earth" and "flood-geology" movements. The earliest and overwhelmingly dominant reading is that the Flood was a real event in real time, with figurative senses (ark = Church / Christ's cross) layered on the history, not substituted for it.

Arguments & cruxes

Sub-claims marshaled for (or against) a reading, which cut across the timeline rather than sitting at one date — each weighed adversarially on its own page, with an assessment of the interpretation it serves.

Was reading the Flood as real history the universal Christian position until the 19th century? (assessment: sound)

The historic-consensus thesis — the Reddit-thread challenge that Noah, the Ark, and a humanity-destroying Flood were treated as historical fact by every Father, Doctor, and scholastic, East and West, so biblical "literalism" on the Flood is the inherited Christian position and not a nineteenth-century American-fundamentalist invention. The historical spine is sound: the pre-modern interpreters (the allegorists included) read the Flood as a real event and built figurative senses atop it; the older Catholic scholarship affirms the unanimity outright (the Catholic Encyclopedia quoting Zorell — the Flood "has never been explained or understood in any but a truly historical sense by any Catholic writer," the dissent dated only to the 1890s); and the first serious denial is early-modern (La Peyrère's local flood, 1655), not patristic. What overstates the case is the word literalism: it imports the modern young-earth, flood-geology package — itself a twentieth-century construction (Price 1923; Whitcomb & Morris 1961) — and flattens two distinctions the orthodox kept open, that anthropological universality (all men died) does not entail geographic globality (Augustine, Cajetan, and Tostatus all limit the Flood's extent), and that "historical" never meant "young-earth."1

Was patristic allegory of the Flood a denial of its history — and did any named ancient writer deny it? (assessment: sound)

Allegory was layered on history, not a denial of it — the sub-question behind "who first denied the Flood?": Origen, Clement, Ambrose, and Augustine are filed as "allegorists," but they read the Flood on multiple levels, affirming the event and building the figure (ark = Church / Christ's cross) atop it. The reading is sound: Augustine sets the both/and down as a rule and rejects both "those who receive the bare history but reject the allegorical interpretation" and "those who maintain the figurative and not the historical meaning" (City of God 15.26–27); even Philo and Origen, the heaviest allegorists, keep the literal event as the floor of their figures; and no named ancient Christian writer can be shown to have denied the Flood's historicity. The one caveat the same passage supplies: Augustine's refutation shows a figure-only reading existed as a category — so the exact finding is "no named denier," not "no one ever entertained it."2
