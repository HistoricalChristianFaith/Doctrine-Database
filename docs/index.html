Doctrines

  • The Sons of God and the Nephilim (Genesis 6) — fallen angels and giant offspring vs. the line of Seth and merely mighty men; 33 people.
  • The Intermediate State (the soul between death and resurrection) — conscious survival vs. soul-sleep, and a structured Hades (Abraham's bosom / torment / Tartarus) vs. two destinies only; 39 witnesses.
  • Purgatory and prayer for the dead — the ancient, near-universal practice of prayer and alms for the dead vs. the distinctively Latin theory of a satisfactory cleansing fire before judgment, denied root and branch by the Reformation; 27 witnesses.
  • Prayer to the Saints (invocation of the saints) — may the living address the saints in heaven, "Holy Mary, pray for us"? Everything turns on a distinction both sides press: praying to the saints (invocation — direct second-person address) is not the same act as believing the saints intercede (that the blessed pray for us). The intercession-belief is early and near-uncontested; the dating of invocation proper is the live question — a fourth-century development (Gavin Ortlund: Origen's ambivalence, Augustine's strictures, the mildness of the earliest examples), or an older practice the 1st–3rd-c. apologists simply left unrecorded (the catacomb petitions, the Sub Tuum, Hippolytus)? Part of the intermediate-state / purgatory family; timeline in progress, 4 arguments.
  • The Historicity of Noah's Flood — the near-universal pre-modern reading of Genesis 6–9 as real history (with figurative senses layered on) vs. the modern questions its "literalist" slogan conflates: anthropological vs. geographic universality, and ancient historicity vs. 20th-c. flood-geology; 6 witnesses so far, timeline in progress.
  • The Rabbinic Corruption of Scripture (Septuagint vs. Masoretic Text) — the patristic-to-medieval charge that post-Second-Temple rabbinic Judaism deliberately altered the Hebrew text (messianic proof-texts and the Genesis chronology) while the older Septuagint preserved the true reading, vs. the modern text-critical finding (Tov; the Dead Sea Scrolls) that the LXX renders a different Hebrew Vorlage — divergent traditions, not corruption; 17 witnesses so far, timeline in progress.
  • The Origin and Inspiration of the Septuagint — how the account of the LXX's making grew across time: from a simple royal commission (the Letter of Aristeas, Josephus) to the Christian miracle of the seventy-two translators producing word-for-word identical versions in separate cells "by the Holy Spirit" (Irenaeus, Cyril of Jerusalem, Epiphanius, Augustine), the skeptics who called the cells a fable (Jerome, Hugh of Saint Victor), and the Simeon-the-Just Isaiah 7:14 offshoot; the inspiration premise underwriting the rabbinic-corruption charge; 27 witnesses so far, timeline in progress.
  • The Biblical Canon — how the Church came to recognize which books are Scripture: the gradual, regional reception of the Old and New Testaments (the Muratorian fragment, Eusebius's "acknowledged/disputed/spurious," Athanasius's 367 list, Hippo and Carthage) vs. the popular legend that the canon was fixed in one stroke at the Council of Nicaea (325) — Constantine's commissioned-and-burned "new Bible" (Dan Brown), even sorted by a miracle (the Synodicon Vetus altar-ordeal); 11 witnesses so far (Justin Martyr, c. 155; the Muratorian fragment, c. 170; Irenaeus, c. 180; Origen, c. 248; Eusebius, c. 324; Cyril of Jerusalem, c. 350; Athanasius, 367; the Council of Rome under Damasus, 382, on the contested Damasine list; the African councils of Hippo and Carthage, 393–419; Innocent I, c. 405; the rejection-lists, c. 500–810), 10 arguments.
  • The Old Testament Canon (Is the Deuterocanon Scripture?) — is Tobit (with Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch, 1–2 Maccabees — the deuterocanon/Apocrypha) part of the Christian OT? YES/NO verdicts across time: the Septuagint included them, the Hebrew-minded Fathers and the Reformation excluded them, the African councils, Rome, Florence, Trent, and the Synod of Jerusalem affirmed them — settled by reception, not date. Ties into septuagint-origin and rabbinic-corruption; 57 witnesses so far (the Septuagint; the "Council of Jamnia"; Clement of Rome; the Didache; Polycarp of Smyrna; Melito of Sardis; the Muratorian Fragment; Athenagoras of Athens; Irenaeus of Lyons; Clement of Alexandria; the Babylonian Talmud; Hippolytus of Rome; Tertullian; Origen of Alexandria; Cyprian of Carthage; Dionysius of Alexandria; Methodius of Olympus; Lactantius; Eusebius of Caesarea; Alexander of Alexandria; the Council of Nicaea; Aphrahat the Persian Sage; Cyril of Jerusalem; Hilary of Poitiers; the Cheltenham List; Athanasius of Alexandria; Basil of Caesarea; Gregory of Nyssa; Amphilochius of Iconium; the Apostolic Canons; the Council of Rome; Epiphanius of Salamis; Ambrose of Milan; Gregory of Nazianzus; Jerome; the African councils of Hippo and Carthage; John Chrysostom; Augustine of Hippo; Sulpicius Severus; Rufinus of Aquileia; Innocent I; the Catalogue of Codex Claromontanus; John Cassian; Vincent of Lérins; the Synopsis of Sacred Scripture; Gregory the Great; Isidore of Seville; Codex Amiatinus; John of Damascus; the Stichometry of Nicephorus; the Masoretic Text; the Council of Florence; Martin Luther; the Council of Trent; the Thirty-Nine Articles; the Westminster Confession of Faith; the Synod of Jerusalem), timeline in progress; 26 arguments.

Arguments

The Sons of God and the Nephilim (Genesis 6)

Related arguments — the sacred-marriage (sacral-kingship)

  • The sacred-marriage rite as the mechanism of the Nephilim — assessment: sound (De Young's answer to "spirits can't interbreed": a sacral-kingship sex-rite begetting a "two-thirds divine" child. The form is real — a documented human-king bed-rite exists, the Sumerian hieros gamos (king as Dumuzi + Inanna's priestess; Iddin-Dagan A, Šulgi X) — correcting the impression that no human-king rite is attested. But the documented purpose is legitimation + land-fertility, not "to produce the next king"; literal sex is contested (Lapinkivi); and the "two-thirds divine offspring" rides a separate divine-birth motif (Gilgamesh born of the goddess Ninsun), not the sacred marriage).
  • Og's bed and the bed of Marduk — assessment: sound (core parallel well-grounded — Lindquist/George; the old tomb/sarcophagus rival is superseded; the load-bearing Marduk allusion Lindquist layers on the mainstream literal "iron bed" reading has stood unrefuted for fifteen years and is beginning to be adopted (Quick, Hendel) — sound on the "well-grounded and unrefuted" standard, short only of majority uptake. What stays overstated is De Young's "excavated bed … to produce a king" framing, not the interpretation: "to produce the next king" splits — the divinely-begotten, "two-thirds divine" heir it invokes is real royal ideology (Šulgi, Gilgamesh), so only the mechanism overreaches).
  • The Khmer nāga-tower rite (Zhou Daguan) as a sacral-kingship parallel — assessment: sound (a firsthand thirteenth-century report — Zhou Daguan at Angkor, 1296–97 — that the Khmer king coupled nightly, in a gold tower at Phimeanakas, with a nine-headed nāga serpent-spirit, "the lord of the earth for the entire country," appearing as a woman, his life and the realm resting on the union; with the dynasty's separate claim of descent from a king's marriage to the nāga princess Soma (Kaundinya and Soma). A solid documented witness that "a divinized king ritually unites with a spirit-as-woman" is a real institution — among De Young's strongest parallels, the partner being explicitly a spirit. The only overlay to flag is his recurring "to produce the next king": the attested function is the king's legitimacy and the land's welfare, and Zhou reports it as what "the local people all say" of a secret tower).
  • The Japanese accession rite (Daijōsai) as a surviving sacral-kingship bed-rite — assessment: contested (De Young's claim that his sex-rite survived "into living memory" in the Japanese emperor's accession, where he "spends the night with the sun goddess," element "removed after WWII," "Hirohito the last one." The core is real — the Daijōsai secludes the emperor overnight with a bed-like god-seat (the shinza) to commune with Amaterasu, and R. S. Ellwood (The Feast of Kingship, 1973) reads it as a hieros gamos producing "the next heir to the throne." But the sacred-marriage reading is a contested minority view (Blacker: "unconvincing"; cf. the communion/hospitality/rebirth readings); the "sexual element removed after WWII" turns a pre-war interpretation into an excised act; and "Hirohito the last one" is false — Akihito performed it in 1990, Naruhito in 2019).

Related arguments — the angelic embodiment mechanism

  • The angels' "habitation" (oikētērion) as a relinquished celestial body — assessment: weak (Doug Wilson's competing answer to "how spirits beget": the angels who "left their own habitation" (Jude 6, oikētērion) shed their celestial bodies, the warrant being that the word's only other NT use (2 Cor 5:2) denotes the embodied resurrection body. The premise holds at one end — in 2 Cor 5:2 oikētērion does denote a body (Chrysostom/Theophylact/Aquinas) — but the transfer to Jude fails: the word means "dwelling / abode," its 2 Cor body-sense is supplied by Paul's clothing/tabernacle metaphor, and carrying it into Jude (a different author, Enochic context, parallel ἀρχήν "first estate") is illegitimate totality transfer; the whole patristic database reads Jude's "habitation" as the heavenly station, never a shed body (Bede, Theophylact)).
  • Does Matthew 22:30 ("they neither marry") foreclose the angelic reading? — assessment: sound (Doug Wilson's rebuttal of Chrysostom's objection (Hom. 22 on Genesis: angels are incorporeal and "neither marry"). The verse describes the angels in heaven (Mark: "which are in heaven") and grounds the non-marriage in deathlessness ("neither can they die any more," Luke 20:36), not incapacity — and the patristic exposition of the verse agrees (Chrysostom on Matthew; Pseudo-Chrysostom) — so angels who "left their own habitation" (Jude 6) fall outside it. But the win is defensive and partial: it clears only the Matthew 22:30 prop, leaving the deeper "spirits are incorporeal" premise to the embodiment-mechanism pages).

Related arguments — the Sethite reading

  • Did the Sethite reading originate as a Rabbinic reaction against the Enochic reading? — assessment: plausible (the spine holds — the angelic reading is oldest (Alexander), its earliest rejection is Jewish and 2nd-c. (R. Simeon b. Yohai's curse, Gen. Rabbah 26:5), the Enochic tradition genuinely diverged (Reed), and the "too-Christian" motive has an ancient witness (Tertullian) — but De Young overstates and mislabels: the rabbinic reading was "sons of judges/nobles," not "Seth"; the Sethite reading proper is first attested in a Christian, Julius Africanus; the "Africanus → Ephrem → Augustine" pipeline is undocumented; and "reaction against Christianity" flattens a multi-causal ("two powers"/Metatron — Segal), never-total process).
  • Does Augustine's case against the angelic reading hold? — assessment: sound (the structural case largely holds — his rejection of 1 Enoch rests on independent canon-history, not a circle, and his Sethite reading is drawn from the canonical text itself, so De Young's "circular" charge overstates it — but it is not decisive: it leans on one unargued premise (holy angels cannot so fall) that the opposing camp denies, and Augustine himself could not settle 1 Peter 3:19).
  • Is Chrysostom's Sethite reading genuinely his, or a later editor's hand? — assessment: weak (the doubt does not hold: De Young's "412–413" date appears in no study of the homilies and postdates Chrysostom's death in 407 — the Sethite Homily 22 is securely Antiochene, c. 388 — and his Photius prop misattributes an authorship doubt; Photius questioned the homilies' count, 61 vs. 67, explained as lost folios, not their authenticity. The genuine two-series puzzle leaves both series authentically Chrysostom's).

Related arguments — the Rephaim

  • Og's cities Ashtaroth and Edrei as the seat of the Ugaritic Rapiu (KTU 1.108) — assessment: sound (the strongest unbuilt support for the supernatural Og reading: the Ugaritic libation-prayer KTU 1.108 hails Rapiu, King of Eternity — divine eponym of the rpʾum (Rephaim) — enthroned at ʿṯtrt and hdrʿy, read since B. Margulis (1970) as Ashtaroth and Edrei, Og's own two towns (Deut 1:4; Josh 12:4). So Og's royal seat was, in a pre-biblical Canaanite text, the cult-place of a deified Rephaim-king — grounding "Og = surviving semidivine Rephaim / Rephaim = divinized royal dead" with no bed and no performed rite. The majority Ugaritological reading (del Olmo Lete: "accepted by most Ugaritologists"; M. S. Smith, ABD; adduced for Og by Lindquist), on an undisputed name-link (rpʾu = the Rephaim) + the Milku-at-Ashtaroth parallel (RS 24.244). Short of airtight only on philology: hdrʿy's heh answers awkwardly to "Edrei"'s aleph (McAffee: "difficult to defend"), and ʿṯtrt is spelled identically for the town and the goddess Athtart — caveats inside a standard reading, the rival divine-name reading now widely judged untenable. De Young himself leans on the bed and leaves this firmer pillar unbuilt).
  • The Rephaim as the spirits of dead kings (KTU 1.161) — assessment: sound (Rephaim = deified dead kings is near-consensus — Levine–de Tarragon/Lewis/Pardee — and fits Isa 14/Ps 88; but the Ugaritic rite summons and blesses the royal dead, so De Young's "ward off the Rephaim so the king could pass by safely" inverts it, and the leap to the giant Rephaim is separable).
  • 1 Peter 3:19 — the "spirits in prison" — assessment: contested (patristic database = human dead; modern criticism = the Watchers).
  • The Sumerian King List ≅ Enoch (the seventh antediluvian) — assessment: sound (Enoch ↔ Enmeduranki seventh-king parallel is mainstream — VanderKam/Kvanvig; De Young's "seventh king creates the solar calendar" conflates Enoch's calendar role with Enmeduranki's divination, and the base-60 reading of the ages is unsettled).
  • The canonicity of 1 Enoch — was it received as Scripture? — assessment: weak (real early esteem — Jude quotes it as prophecy, Tertullian defends it as inspired Scripture — but "authoritative Scripture across the early Church" overstates it: contested in its own day (Origen: Enoch does "not at all circulate in the Churches as divine") and excluded by Athanasius/Jerome/Augustine, canonical only in the Ethiopian tradition).
  • Are the unclean spirits the spirits of the dead Nephilim? — assessment: contested (a strong, well-attested early reading — explicit in 1 Enoch 15 / Jubilees 10 and stated by Justin, Athenagoras, Commodian, Lactantius, arguably the default 2nd–3rd-c. demonology — but never settled: the NT doesn't assert the etiology, the rival "demons = the apostate angels" view (Matt 25:41 / Augustine) competes and becomes mainstream, and the reading presupposes the angelic interpretation, so the Sethite camp rejects it. De Young's modern Chrysostom prop for it is wrong-camp and demoted to a note).
  • Does the Wisdom of Solomon attest the giant-cult's human sacrifice and cannibalism? — assessment: sound (De Young rests the giant-clans' "ongoing ritual life" on the Wisdom of Solomon, and the proof-text holds, unusually directly: Wisdom 12:3–6 names the Canaanites' "wicked sacrifices," "merciless murderers of children" (child-sacrifice), and "devourers of man's flesh, and the feasts of blood" (cannibalism) in one breath, with the idolatry→child-sacrifice spine restated at 14:23/14:27. The one qualification is a citation precision: the cannibalism clause is specifically Wisdom 12:5, not the 14:23 list, so that link rests on chapter 12. A Second Temple polemical witness, distinct from the 1 Enoch 7 strand).
  • Does Beowulf witness the angelic reading of Genesis 6? — assessment: weak (Doug Wilson lists "the Beowulf tradition" with Josephus and 1 Enoch as the "entire ancient world" reading Gen 6 as a "celestial/earthly connection." But the poem genealogizes Grendel and the gigantas from Cain (Gen 4, "in Caines cynne"), a human ancestor, and shows the Flood drowning that "race of giants estranged from God." It is a genuine reception witness to the antediluvian-giant-and-Flood tradition (the poet absorbed Enochic giant-lore — Kaske), but routes the giants through Cain's human line — the Augustinian/Sethite-adjacent harmonization (Mellinkoff, Orchard) that displaced the angelic reading — so on the disputed celestial-union point it witnesses for the rival camp, not Wilson's; Josephus and 1 Enoch carry his case, Beowulf does not).

The Intermediate State (the soul between death and resurrection)

Related arguments — the descent

  • Did the descended Christ free the OT righteous and "lead captivity captive"? — assessment: plausible (whether the crucified Christ went down to the realm of the human dead and freed the OT righteous from a holding-place — "Abraham's bosom" / the limbus patrum — "leading captivity captive" (Eph 4:8) and emptying it; distinct from the 1 Peter 3:19 argument of the imprisoned angels. The bare descensus — Christ's soul went to the dead — is sound (creedal, affirmed East and West, grounded in Acts 2:27/31); but the liberation-of-a-holding-place mechanism, though the pre-Reformation mainstream (Irenaeus, Clement, Origen, Cyril of Jerusalem, Jerome, John of Damascus, Aquinas's emptied limbus), leans on the genuinely ambiguous Eph 4:9 ("the lower parts of the earth" — Hades, or the incarnation? — Aquinas allows both), is partly inferred from resurrection-texts, and is denied by the Reformed who reframe the descent and recognize no third place (Calvin; Westminster)).
  • Were the saints raised at the cross OT righteous freed from Sheol, a firstfruits with the risen Christ? — assessment: contested (whether the "many bodies of the saints which slept" who "arose, and came out of the graves after his resurrection" (Matt 27:52–53) are OT righteous released from Sheol by the descent, a firstfruits raised with Christ — the gospel's own evidence for the descent to the dead. The meaning is the patristic mainstream — a firstfruits and sign of the general resurrection (Chrysostom, Gregory, Aquinas) and a witness to the harrowing's freeing of the dead (Ignatius, Hilary, Jerome, Theophylact) — but the verse times the saints' emergence to Christ's own resurrection and makes him "the firstborn from the dead" (Jerome), so the Holy-Saturday release of OT saints from Sheol is an inference the text does not state, and Matthew's bare "saints which slept" is over-specified as the OT patriarchs. And the historicity is genuinely disputed: uniquely Matthean, absent from the other gospels, steeped in OT apocalyptic resurrection imagery (Ezek 37; Dan 12:2), read as a theological/apocalyptic sign by interpreters ancient (Origen) and modern (Brown, Licona) and as literal history by the patristic mainstream and conservative defenders).
  • "He descended into hell" — is the creed clause original, and should it be retained? — assessment: contested (the creed words, not the doctrine. Text-history is firm: the clause is absent from the oldest form of the creed (Old Roman Symbol, Marcellus c. 340), first surfaces in a creed at the Arian-leaning "Dated Creed" of Sirmium (359), is reported by Rufinus (c. 404) as missing from the Roman and Eastern creeds and meaning only "buried," and enters the received Western text only in the eighth century (Pirminius) — so the words are a late, non-original addition, as Grudem presses (JETS 1991). But retention is genuinely divided: Scaer (JETS 1992) and the evangelical retrieval grant the history yet keep the article because the descent doctrine it summarizes is older than the clause and itself sound (descent-to-the-dead), and the Arian provenance of the first attestation does not taint a non-Arian line. Strong by philology's lights for "late addition," contested on retention — most of the tradition converging on "he descended to the dead").
  • Did Paradise relocate from Hades to heaven after the Ascension? — assessment: contested (whether the abode of the righteous dead was transferred from a compartment of Hades up to the third heaven at the harrowing/ascension, so believers now go up — "present with the Lord" — not down to Abraham's bosom. Distinct from the descent-to-the-dead argument (the freeing of the OT righteous): this is the relocation thesis. The conclusion — the believing dead now consciously with Christ above — is broadly shared (2 Cor 5:8; Phil 1:23; the Latin emptying of the limbus patrum into the beatific vision, Aquinas and Benedict XII; the Reformed "highest heavens," Westminster). But the specific relocation of a place is denied by the earliest Fathers, who — writing after the ascension — keep the ordinary faithful in an intermediate Hades and name this very thesis to reject it (Tertullian De Anima 55; Irenaeus AH V.31; Hippolytus), and its pillar (Luke 23:43 paradise = 2 Cor 12:4 "third heaven") is undercut by John 20:17 / Acts 2:27, which put Christ's Good-Friday soul in Hades, not heaven. Strong by Latin-scholastic and much-of-the-NT lights, weak by early-patristic and Eastern ones).
  • What did Christ proclaim to the spirits in prison — victory, or salvation? — assessment: contested (the content of the proclamation of 1 Pet 3:19 — a herald of condemnation/victory over the imprisoned spirits, or an offer of salvation, the postmortem "second chance"? Distinct from the identity argument (who the spirits are) and the descent-to-the-dead argument (the freeing of the OT righteous). The content tracks the identity: condemnation if the spirits are the Watchers — the modern-critical "proclamation of triumph" (Dalton, Bauckham, Schreiner), and unobjectionable, since no Father evangelizes the fallen angels and 1 Enoch keeps them bound till judgment, while Gregory the Great and Augustine refuse a universal second chance — but salvation if the human dead. Across the descent tradition the claim collides with the patristic mainstream, which made the proclamation a salvific preaching of the gospel to the human dead (Clement, even floating their repentance; Irenaeus; Cyril of Alexandria; Hippolytus; Bede on 1 Pet 4:6's euangelizō), and the lexical prop that kēryssō excludes the gospel is imprecise (Mark 16:15). Doug Wilson is the popular proponent ("not the word for preaching the gospel … the final victor")).
  • The "excised" descent-prophecy — authentic Scripture cut by the Jews, or an apocryphal logion? — assessment: weak (a one-line prophecy of the descent — "The Lord God remembered His dead people of Israel who lay in the graves; and He descended to preach to them His own salvation" — that Justin (Dial. 72) and Irenaeus quote as Scripture and Justin says the Jews deliberately cut from Jeremiah. It would be the OT's plainest descent oracle were it authentic, but it survives in no Hebrew or Greek text of either prophet; Irenaeus fathers it on Isaiah (AH III.20.4), Jeremiah (IV.22.1) and no prophet at all across five citations — the signature of a free-floating testimonium, not a scriptural text; and Justin's "the Jews excised it" is a formula he also applies to Jer 11:19, which never left the canon. An early, sincere witness to how scriptural the descent already felt by AD 160, but not the excised prophecy it was taken for — and the descent doctrine it served never rested on it).
  • Did John the Baptist go as Christ's forerunner into Hades? — assessment: weak (whether the Baptist, beheaded by Herod before the crucifixion, went down to the realm of the dead ahead of Christ and there preached as his forerunner, announcing the coming descent. Hippolytus alone asserts it (On Christ and Antichrist §45: "He also first preached to those in Hades, becoming a forerunner there … that the Saviour would descend to ransom the souls of the saints"). The pieces are individually sound — John's earthly forerunner office is firm Scripture (Mal 3:1; Luke 1:76; John 1:23), his death before Christ is history (Matt 14:10), and the descent he heralds is broadly held — but their combination into a postmortem heralding in Hades is a single Father's conceit, unrepeated in the tradition, with no scriptural warrant (Scripture says nothing of John after death), and resting on the already-contested peopled Hades and postmortem-preaching readings. A coherent homiletical flourish on the descent, dispensable even by the descent's own logic).
  • Is a two-compartment Hades (Tartarus vs. Paradise) taught by Scripture? — assessment: contested (a single underworld divided into Tartarus, the angelic prison of 2 Pet 2:4, and Paradise/Abraham's Bosom, the righteous dead of Luke 16. The structured intermediate Hades is the early-patristic and Eastern mainstream — 1 Enoch 22, Hippolytus, Tertullian — and fits the surface of Luke 16; but reading that parable as cosmography is disputed, the "chasm between Tartarus and Paradise" fuses Luke 16's human gulf with 2 Peter's angelic Tartarus, the present-tense version is undercut by the descent emptying the limbus patrum (Aquinas), and the Reformed confessions deny any third place (Calvin; Westminster: "besides these two places… the Scripture acknowledgeth none")).
  • Is soul-sleep (psychopannychism) taught? — assessment: weak (whether Scripture's "sleep" language for death — Dan 12:2; Eccl 9:5,10; John 11:11–14; 1 Thess 4:13–15; 1 Cor 15:51 — teaches genuine unconsciousness until the resurrection, or is a euphemism for the body's death. The idiom is real and the doctrine rightly centres the bodily resurrection (Aphrahat; modern conditionalism), but on its best case unconsciousness does not hold: "sleep" most naturally describes the body in the dust, the Ecclesiastes texts speak "under the sun," and the conscious-state texts require awareness — the martyrs "under the altar" cry out and are answered (Rev 6:9–11), Paul counts it "far better" "to depart, and to be with Christ" (Phil 1:23). Even Aphrahat keeps a conscious "spirit … with our Lord," and the tradition closes ranks (Calvin's Psychopannychia; Westminster: souls "neither die nor sleep")).
  • Are Gehenna and the lake of fire one final state, distinct from intermediate Hades? — assessment: sound (whether Gehenna = the lake of fire = the second death names the post-judgment eternal state ("hell" proper), distinct from the intermediate Hades/Sheol that presently holds the dead. The distinction is the patristic and scholarly mainstream and follows directly from Rev 20:14, where Hades is emptied and then itself "cast into the lake of fire" — so the two cannot be the same place. Hippolytus keeps the "lake of unquenchable fire … prepared against the day determined by God" unoccupied while Hades already detains souls (Against Plato); Irenaeus equates the lake of fire, the second death, and Gehenna outright (AH V.35.2). The one inferential step — that the Synoptics' Gehenna and the Apocalypse's lake of fire name the same thing — is well grounded though not verbatim in any single verse, and English "hell" (rendering both Hades and Gehenna) hides the distinction. The separable question of the final state's nature — eternal torment vs. annihilation — is contested in its own right and does not touch this argument).

Purgatory and prayer for the dead

Related arguments — the temporal nature of purgatory

  • Does the "final sanctification" syllogism establish purgatory? — assessment: plausible (a piece of reasoning, not a proof-text, advanced by James Akin on deliberately Protestant premises: nothing unclean enters heaven (Rev 21:27; Heb 12:14); we are not yet perfectly holy at death (1 John 1:8); ∴ a purification must come between death and glory, which is purgatory "by definition." The syllogism is valid and its conclusion — a final perfecting of the soul — is near-consensus, affirmed even by the Reformed as instantaneous glorification ("being then made perfect in holiness," Westminster). But it establishes only the genus (some final purification), not the species — the historic purgatory of penal satisfaction for the temporal debt of sin, over time, on which indulgences and the Masses of the living operate; that content is carried in by the name and by the separate cleansing-fire case, not by the premises, and Akin's own concession that it "may take no time" collapses the conclusion into the glorification Protestants already hold).
  • Is purgatory instantaneous or durational? — assessment: contested (against the popular picture of a sentence measured in days and years, the modern magisterium presses that purgatory is not a span of earthly time at all. The duration is genuinely left open — CCC 1030–1032 fixes neither place nor time — and the non-temporal reading is held at the summit (Ratzinger: the transforming encounter "cannot be quantified by the measurements of earthly time"), while the popular "days and years" rest on a misreading: they were equivalences to ancient canonical penance, not time deducted from purgatory, abolished as numbers by Paul VI in 1967. But the durational picture carries the longer tradition — Gregory the Great's souls released after an appointed term, Aquinas's punishment of severity and duration — and the logic of suffrages (Masses offered for the dead across our time) presupposes the soul persists in purgatory through some duration. No clear winner, and the dispute is partly verbal: even the non-temporal party keeps an ordered "transition," denying only that it runs on a physical clock).

Related arguments — indulgences

  • Did the crusade indulgence seed the abuses that led to the Reformation? — assessment: sound (a historical-development thesis from the crusade historian Thomas Madden: indulgences — the practical face of purgatory, remitting the temporal punishment of forgiven sin — grew out of the crusade indulgence. Urban II's full remission for the First Crusade (1095), extended to the Spanish Reconquista by the First Lateran Council (1123); then the hinge — Innocent III made the crusade vow redeemable for cash and let paying non-combatants buy a share of the indulgence (Quia maior 1213; Fourth Lateran 1215), the moment the reward became purchasable; Gregory IX made vow-redemption routine; and the line runs forward to the St. Peter's indulgence and Luther's protest of 1517 — "the seed that in three centuries' time would grow into the weed of abuses." The developmental spine holds and is mainstream scholarship. The needed refinement: the crusade indulgence is the principal tributary that became the main channel, not the sole headwater — indulgences have pre-1095 roots (penitential redemptions; eleventh-century episcopal grants; Alexander II's 1063 Spanish precedent), and their defining theology (the treasury of merits; application to purgatory and the dead) was worked out a century to three-and-a-half centuries after the practice).
  • Why doesn't the pope free every soul from purgatory? — assessment: weak (an internal-coherence objection put as a dilemma: if a plenary indulgence remits the whole temporal debt, can be applied to a soul by another's act, and the pope can grant it at will, then charity joined to that power would seem to demand he empty purgatory at a stroke; since he does not, either his power is secretly limited or he withholds charity. A false dilemma. The Church has always taught she has no jurisdiction over the dead: an indulgence reaches a soul in purgatory only per modum suffragii — by way of suffrage, an offering of the treasury's satisfaction that God accepts in His own measure, with no guarantee and no compulsion (Bellarmine: indulgences "avail not after the fashion of a juridical absolution"; Aquinas: they "cannot avail [the dead] directly"). So the "limited power" horn is conceded outright and is the express teaching, not a hidden flaw; and charity is not impugned, since the pope is the souls' intercessor, not their judge, and cannot empty purgatory by fiat. The sharper form — an inexhaustible treasury offered as maximal suffrage should free all — only relocates the limit to God's economy of justice and the soul's disposition, and bites only on the older satisfaction model, fading as the magisterium moves toward sanctification. A sincere, answerable question; not a refutation).

Related arguments — the Scriptural proof-texts

  • Is a purgatorial cleansing fire before judgment taught? — assessment: contested (the practice of prayer and alms for the dead is a near-consensus of the ancient Church, East and West — 2 Macc 12; Tertullian; Cyprian; Cyril of Jerusalem; Augustine; and the East to this day — and is carried by the purgatory timeline itself. But a distinct post-mortem cleansing fire that satisfies for sin is weaker: its proof-text "saved; yet so as by fire" (1 Cor 3:13–15) is read by the Greek exegetes (Chrysostom, Theophylact, Theodoret) as the fire of judgment, not an intermediate purgation — Chrysostom turning it against the purgative sense — its Matt 12:32 support is an inference from an idiom, its 2 Macc 12 warrant is canonicity-dependent, and the developed satisfactory fire was never received in the East (rejected at Florence) and is denied by the Reformed (Calvin "a deadly device of Satan"; Westminster). Augustine's own "may be left doubtful" and Gregory's "very light sins … if he deserved it in life" are far more guarded than the doctrine later built on them).
  • Does "prison until the last penny" (Matt 5:25–26 // Luke 12:58–59) teach a temporary purgation? — assessment: weak (Jesus' warning to settle with one's accuser "lest … thou be cast into prison" from which "thou shalt by no means come out … till thou hast paid the uttermost farthing." Of purgatory's proof-texts this has the most congenial shape — a prison, a debt, a payment to the last small coin, release implied — which is why the earliest Latins, Tertullian ("the prison" = Hades, the farthing recompensed "before the resurrection") and Cyprian, read it of a post-mortem reckoning. But its plain sense is prudential counsel to be reconciled in this life (Chrysostom, Jerome, Clement, Theophylact); where read of the next life the chief interpreters take "till thou hast paid" as the idiom of permanence and make the prison Gehenna with no exit — Augustine ("he will never come out … the everlasting punishment of his earthly sins"), Bede, Cyril — the contrary of a temporary purgation; and the debtor's-prison image signals full exaction, not easy release. The one frankly purgative reading is Tertullian's idiosyncratic universal-Hades scheme, not the developed doctrine; Calvin notes it even undercuts the suffrages it is cited to serve — "Christ … expressly demands from each person the payment of what he owes" — and the magisterium's developed case (CCC 1030–1032) dropped the text).
  • Does "baptized for the dead" (1 Cor 15:29) support aid for the dead? — assessment: weak (a proof-text adduced beside 2 Macc 12 and 1 Cor 3:15 because, on its surface, it shows the living doing something sacramental for the dead ("baptized for the dead … if the dead rise not"). But Paul's argument is about the resurrection of the body, not post-mortem intercession, and the verse is read that way across the tradition — Tertullian ("baptized for the [dead] body"), Chrysostom, Theophylact, and Cosmas (the baptismal creed, "I believe in the resurrection of the dead"), Ambrosiaster, Epiphanius — always for the candidate's own rising. The "baptism for the dead" it names is taken either as a heretical vicarious rite the Church ridiculed and forbade (Marcion, Cerinthus; condemned at Carthage in 419) or as deathbed catechumen baptism — neither of which is prayer for the dead. Decisively, Aquinas — the one sympathetic reader — answers his own question ("if one's prayers profit another, why not his baptism?") by ruling that baptism works "from the intention of Christ," not ours, and so does not transfer, whereas "prayer can help those in purgatory": the doctrine's own systematizer severs this verse from the practice it is cited to support. And the clause is a notorious crux interpretum — dozens of competing readings — the thinnest possible foundation).
  • Was prayer for the dead uncontested in the ancient Church? — assessment: sound (the cleansing-fire argument's strong leg is a historical thesis: prayer, alms, and the Eucharist for the dead were the near-universal practice of the ancient Church, controverted only by isolated figures. It holds, and the sole ancient counter-witness confirms it — Aerius (c. 355) is the one named denier, and Epiphanius and Augustine catalogue his rejection among the heresies, appealing to a "world-wide agreement before Aerius": the exception that marks the rule. Refinements: Aerius's objection is principled (vicarious post-mortem help makes personal holiness dispensable — the moral-hazard worry the Reformers later press), so the practice was near-universal as practice without being immune to coherent dissent; and the claim that the De Fide makes Arius a second denier is not borne out — it affirms the practice, the Arian tie being to Aerius's own theology, and Augustine merely epitomizes Epiphanius).

Prayer to the Saints (invocation of the saints)

Related arguments — invocation of the saints

  • Praying to the saints vs. praying with them — is invocation the same as believing the saints intercede? — assessment: sound (the definitional lens both the Reformed critic (Gavin Ortlund) and a veteran Catholic theologian press: the living addressing a saint in the second person ("O Saint N., pray for me") is a distinct act from the belief that the saints in glory pray for us. The distinction is real and load-bearing — the intercession-belief is attested early, widely, and almost uncontested, while direct invocation in the strict sense is sparser and clearer only from the later 4th century, and the sharpest ancients keep the two senses apart in one breath (Origen affirms the saints "pray with" us yet reserves prayer "in its full meaning" to God; Augustine honours the martyrs yet bars their invocation "by the sacrificing priest"). What it does not do is settle the dating: a genuine subset of 3rd–early-4th-c. witnesses (catacomb petitions; the Sub Tuum) already are second-person address, so the line between the senses is not simply early-vs-late, and the lens must not be weaponised into a dating rule).
  • Is invocation a fourth-century development, or an older practice the apologists didn't record? — assessment: contested (Gavin Ortlund's thesis that invocation "genuinely looks like it's forming, coming to be, in the mid to late patristic era," against a "Protestant inquiring into the Ancient Church" who reads the apologists' silence as non-treatment of an existing practice and finds a hundred-year jump "from pagan to basically universal" implausible. Strong by one discipline's lights, weak by another's. By the history of the cult of saints the development reading holds — the public, liturgical practice crystallises in the later 4th century (Peter Brown: the cult "took rise" then; the Cappadocians supply the first unambiguous invocations), Origen reserves worship-prayer to God, Augustine is a brake (and doubts the dead perceive us), and even Newman concedes the lateness he then justifies as legitimate development. But the continuity reply has real evidence — the catacomb petitions to Peter and Paul ("Paule et Petre petite pro nobis," mid-3rd–early-4th c.), the disputed Sub Tuum, Hippolytus's address to the three youths c. 204 — and a forceful argument from silence (an anti-pagan apologetic genre with no occasion to describe internal devotion; a once-pagan-seeming practice does not go uncontested-universal in a century without older roots). Each side overstates its flagship: "viewed as pagan" imports a hostility the apologists never voiced, the 3rd-c. pillars are softer and more date-disputed than claimed (Roberts; Förster), and "Origen's ambivalence" understates that he actually permits request to the saints. The honest verdict is a real 4th-century flowering of a practice with genuine but thin earlier roots, the apostolic-era record neither attesting nor excluding it).
  • Do the San Sebastiano catacomb graffiti attest third-century invocation of the saints? — assessment: sound (the continuity side's most concrete evidence, weighed on its own. Beneath the triclia at the apostles' memorial on the Appian Way — the Memoria Apostolorum, now under San Sebastiano — some five-to-six-hundred petitions were scratched into the plaster c. 250/260 – c. 320, the majority addressed to Peter and Paul: "Paule et Petre petite pro Victore" ("Paul and Peter, intercede for Victor"), "in mente habete nos" ("keep us in mind"), even "keep [us] … in your prayers." These are second-person invocations — not mere statements that the saints pray for us — and the standard editors (ICUR/Ferrua, Carletti, Felle) read them so. The revisionist "it's only the funeral banquet / continuous with the pagan cult of the dead" reading (Snyder, MacMullen) sharpens the cultural context but does not dissolve the address: even the refrigerium-graffiti petition the apostles. Sound evidence that invocation in the strict sense was practised by ordinary Roman Christians a century before the Cappadocian homilies — which refutes "invented in the 380s." Its limits feed the dating argument, not this one: it is the cult of the two apostle-martyrs at their relic-shrine, beginning c. 250, so it settles that invocation is older than the Cappadocians without settling that it is apostolic or universal).
  • Does the Council of Constantinople's letter to Theodosius (381) prove conciliar invocation of the saints? — assessment: weak for the strong reading; sound as a witness to intercession-belief (the continuity side's heaviest datum — an ecumenical council, not a private devotion. The 381 synod's letter to Emperor Theodosius closes "May God by the prayers of the Saints, show favour to the world …," which a "Protestant inquiring into the Ancient Church" reads as the 150 bishops praying "a common refrain that is similar to what we say today," making invocation conciliar and official by 381. The provenance checks out — authentic text, secure date, ecumenical council — but the construction petitions God to act "by the prayers of the Saints" (instrumental dative, εὐχαῖς τῶν ἁγίων), addressing no saint in the second person. It voices the intercession-belief, not invocation; on the to-vs-with distinction it sits on the "with/before God" side. So as proof of conciliar invocation it is weak — though as a conciliar witness that belief in the saints' intercession was official and uncontroversial by 381 it is sound).

The Historicity of Noah's Flood

Related arguments — who first denied the Flood

  • Was patristic allegory of the Flood a denial of its history — and did any named ancient writer deny it? — assessment: sound (the sub-question behind "who first denied the Flood?": Origen, Clement, Ambrose, and Augustine are filed as "allegorists," but read the Flood on multiple levels — affirming the event, building the figure (ark = Church / Christ's cross) atop it. Augustine sets the both/and down as a rule and rejects both bare-history-without-allegory and figure-without-history (City of God 15.26–27); even Philo and Origen, the heaviest allegorists, keep the literal event as the floor of their figures; and no named ancient Christian writer can be shown to have denied the Flood's historicity. The one caveat the same Augustine passage supplies: a figure-only reading existed as a category worth refuting — so the exact finding is "no named denier," not "no one ever entertained it").
  • Who first denied the historical Flood — and is the first real denial early-modern, not patristic? — assessment: sound (the positive half of "who first denied the Flood?": no Father, Doctor, or scholastic denied it, and the first systematic, influential break with the universal reading is Isaac La Peyrère's local flood (Prae-Adamitae, 1655) — required by his pre-Adamite Gentiles, the book burned at Paris 1656, the author recanting to Alexander VII — with Isaac Vossius's independent local flood (1659), Lyell's geology, and higher criticism mainstreaming the local and then the non-historical readings after. The hinge falls in the 17th c. and after, exactly where the argument places it, not in 19th-c. American fundamentalism reacting to nothing. Two precisions: "first" means first systematic and influential (La Peyrère caps a Renaissance run-up — Allen, The Legend of Noah — and Vossius reaches a local flood independently in 1659), and La Peyrère denied the Flood's universality, not its reality — outright denial of historicity is later still; both push the denial further from antiquity, not nearer).
  • Was reading the Flood as real history the universal Christian position until the 19th century? — assessment: sound (the thesis that Noah, the Ark, and a humanity-destroying Flood were historical fact for every Father, Doctor, and scholastic, so "literalism" is the historic position, not a 19th-c. fundamentalist invention. The historical spine holds — the pre-modern interpreters (allegorists included) read the Flood as a real event, the older Catholic scholarship affirms the unanimity (the Catholic Encyclopedia quoting Zorell: never understood "in any but a truly historical sense by any Catholic writer," dissent dated only to the 1890s), and the first serious denial is early-modern (La Peyrère, 1655), not patristic. But the word literalism overstates: it imports the modern young-earth flood-geology package — itself 20th-c. (Price 1923; Whitcomb & Morris 1961) — and flattens two distinctions the orthodox kept open: anthropological universality (all men died) ≠ geographic globality (Augustine, Cajetan, Tostatus all limit the extent), and "historical" never meant "young-earth").
  • Did the tradition require a geographically global Flood, or only an anthropologically universal one? — assessment: contested (the refinement behind the slogan "global flood": anthropological universality — all men outside the ark died — is distinct from geographic globality — water over the whole planet. Strong by one discipline's lights, weak by another's. As a claim about doctrinal status it is sound: anthropological universality was the firm consensus, and named Doctors who limited the Flood's extent (Augustine reporting the Olympus exemption, Cajetan, Tostatus) show strict globality was never fixed de fide, so one can affirm the historical Flood without the modern global-geology picture. As a claim about the history of exegesis it is overstated: the dominant patristic reading was geographically universal — Theophilus argues for it against Plato's partial flood, and Gen 7:19–20's "all the high hills … under the whole heaven" was read as worldwide. The genuinely modern import is the flood-geology package, not the worldwide waters).
  • Was the Flood's historicity ever "essential to the faith" — a defined dogma, or universally assumed but never defined? — assessment: contested (a related strand of the debate: one may "peer with suspicion" at the defense that denying the historical Flood is acceptable because it "wasn't seen as essential to the faith." The two disciplines that bear on it pull opposite ways. By strict dogmatic theology the hedge holds — the Flood's historicity is in no creed, defined by no council, and the one modern decree binding the historical sense of a Genesis narrative (the 1909 Pontifical Biblical Commission) covers chapters 1–3, not the Flood of chapters 6–9; geographic globality is expressly free. But "not essential to the faith" overshoots: between defined dogma and matters of indifference sits the ordinary universal magisterium, the unanimous consent of the Fathers (binding in faith and morals per Trent and Vatican I), and the New Testament's own use of the Flood as real event and type (baptism, 1 Pet 3:20–21; judgment, 2 Pet 3:5–6; the parousia, Matt 24:37–39) — by which the Catholic Encyclopedia places "the typical character of both ark and Flood" within "matters of faith and morals." The honest verdict is the ordinary situation for a narrative-historical claim woven deep into Scripture: universally assumed and taught, extent left open, the bare event nowhere solemnly defined).

The Rabbinic Corruption of Scripture (Septuagint vs. Masoretic Text)

Related arguments — the corruption charge

  • Did the Jews deliberately corrupt the Hebrew Old Testament text? — assessment: weak (the master charge the whole timeline bears: that post-Second-Temple rabbinic Judaism maliciously altered the Hebrew text — the messianic proof-texts and the Genesis chronology — to deny Christ, while the older Septuagint preserved the true reading. What holds is the observation: the Septuagint and the rabbinic Hebrew really do diverge, systematically and on the disputed passages. What fails is the inference. The Dead Sea Scrolls — Hebrew manuscripts sealed before AD 70 — already show the divergent text the Septuagint translates (4QSamuela siding with the Greek against the Masoretic Text; Jeremiah in a short and a long Hebrew edition), so the difference cannot be a post-Resurrection plot, and the mainstream account (Emanuel Tov) is that the Septuagint faithfully renders a different Hebrew Vorlage — divergent traditions, not corruption. On the flagship example the direction reverses (Ps 96:10's "from the wood" is a Christian addition to the Greek, not a Jewish deletion from the Hebrew); the Isaiah 7:14 example confuses a translation choice ('almah → parthenos) with a textual one; the "excised" messianic logia were never canonical. Only the Genesis 5/11 chronology rests on a genuinely open text-critical problem — and even there the evidence points more to Septuagint inflation than Hebrew deletion, the motive pure conjecture).
  • The Septuagint legend: were the seventy-two divinely moved to change the text? — assessment: plausible (the premise the charge depends on, weighed from the other side: the Jewish tradition (the Tractate Megillah list, paralleled in the Letter of Aristeas frame) that the seventy-two translators were divinely moved to change some fifteen passages for Ptolemy. Plausible at its kernel, not as the inspired history it is told as. A real subset of the listed "changes" are genuine, identifiable Septuagint translation choices that diverge from the proto-Masoretic Hebrew (Genesis 2:2's "sixth day"; the euphemism for the hare at Leviticus 11:6; the anti-anthropomorphic touches), so the tradition preserves a true memory that the divergence is the translators' deliberate work, not corruption of the Hebrew. But it is not sober history: the inspiration-miracle frame (separate cells, divinely-prompted unanimity) is a demonstrable later accretion, absent from the earliest source (Aristeas) and grown over centuries; the list is a fluid midrashic tradition whose count and contents drift across the rabbinic parallels; and several items (Genesis 1:1, 1:26, 11:7) describe an apologetic change the extant Greek never received. Tellingly, none of the changes is a christological proof-text — so the legend cuts against the corruption charge. Shared with the Origin and Inspiration of the Septuagint; anchors Tov (JSJ 1984), Veltri, Wasserstein).
  • Aquila's revision: anti-Christian distortion, or literalizing toward the Hebrew? — assessment: weak (the master charge in its narrowest, best-documented form: not anonymous "teachers" mutilating the Hebrew, but a single named translator, Aquila (c. 130), whose Greek revision the synagogue preferred to the Septuagint. Epiphanius charges he "distorted certain of the words" to make the things "testified to about Christ" be "fulfilled in some other way." Weak. What holds is the kernel — Aquila's revision did move the Greek toward the proto-Masoretic Hebrew, in the anti-Christian milieu of Rabbi Akiba (whose disciple he was remembered to be), and his readings served the synagogue's side of the proof-text dispute. What fails is the leap to "distortion": his divergences are the uniform output of a thoroughgoing literalizing method (particle for particle — the untranslatable 'et by syn — etymology for etymology) applied to every book of the Greek Bible alike; the method is older than Aquila and broader than the polemic (Barthélemy's first-century kaige revision toward the Hebrew under the Palestinian rabbinate); his most-cited "anti-Christian" reading, neanis ("young woman") at Isaiah 7:14, is the more accurate rendering of the unchanged Hebrew 'almah the Septuagint glossed parthenos; and the Church's own scholars (Origen, Jerome, even Epiphanius) used Aquila for his fidelity to the Hebrew. A defensible literal version, made in a hostile milieu, preferred for readings that cut against the Christian proof — a use, not the genesis, of the version).
  • The reverse charge: did the Jews accuse the Christians of corrupting Scripture? — assessment: weak (the master charge held up to its mirror: if the Christians accused the Jews of mutilating the text, did the Jews return the charge against the Christians? As a symmetric accusation of deliberate textual tampering it is weak — the tampering charge ran overwhelmingly one way (Christian against Jew, with its own fixed vocabulary of excision), and the Jewish side answered the diverging texts in two other registers: by the Septuagint legend (the divergences are the translators' own sanctioned work, not anyone's malice) and, far more substantially, by a charge of Christian misinterpretation and mistranslation — voiced already by Trypho over Isaiah 7:14's "young woman," systematized in the medieval Nizzaḥon Vetus — not mutilation of the shared Hebrew. Where antiquity's Jews branded Christian books as falsehood, the target was the gospels (the Tannaitic gilyonim ruling), not the Old Testament; a genuine counter-charge of Christian textual falsification surfaces only late, in the high-medieval reversal Irven Resnick documents. The honest twist: at its one clearest textual point the reverse charge is right — Psalm 96:10's "from the wood," the Christians' flagship example of a Jewish deletion, is in fact a Christian addition, so the rarer, quieter charge is better grounded than the loud one it answers).

Related arguments — the Genesis chronology

  • Were the Genesis 5/11 patriarchal lifespans shortened (MT) or inflated (LXX) — and was it tampering? — assessment: contested (the one strand of the master charge resting on a real, still-open text-critical problem: Eusebius, Julian, and Agapius held the Jews deliberately shortened the antediluvian begetting-ages of Genesis 5 and 11 — 586 fewer years from Adam to the flood — to push the Messiah's time into the future. The divergence is real and too patterned to be accidental (the three witnesses differ by a clean 100 years apiece; Adam-to-Abraham totals ~2,008 MT / ~2,249 Samaritan / ~3,394 Septuagint), so deliberate editing by someone is likely, the direction is genuinely unsettled after 17 centuries, and a serious modern camp (Henry B. Smith Jr.; Jeremy Sexton) revives the Hebrew-deflation reading the Fathers intuited. But the deflation thesis is a strong minority view, not the established one: the text-critical mainstream (Hendel; Tov; Wevers) leans Septuagint inflation; the Samaritan Pentateuch's pre-Christian short figures owe nothing to anti-Christian malice; the "second Cainan" named as a deletion is more likely a Greek-side addition (absent even from Eusebius's chronicle); the divergence predates the polemic (Demetrius, 3rd c. BC), killing Agapius's post-Resurrection framing; and the malicious motive is conjecture even the deflation camp declines to lean on. Cross-links the Flood antediluvian genealogy).
  • The "second Cainan" (Genesis 11 LXX / Luke 3:36) — original reading the Jews deleted, or a secondary plus? — assessment: weak (the one name the chronology charge specifies: Agapius's claim that the Jews excised the extra Kainan inserted between Arpachshad and Shelah in Genesis 11 (and standing at Luke 3:36). As a prop for deliberate corruption it is weak, though the bare text-critical question is contested. On the underlying question Kainan's originality has a real modern defense (Henry B. Smith Jr.; Jonathan Sarfati) — he stands in nearly all Septuagint manuscripts and in the pre-Christian Demetrius (c. 220 BC) and Jubilees (c. 160 BC), so no Christian invention — but the standard judgment (Andrew Steinmann; Marshall Johnson; Cassuto) makes him a secondary harmonizing plus, absent from the Masoretic Text, the Samaritan Pentateuch, 1 Chronicles (twice), Josephus, Philo, the Vulgate, and the earliest Luke papyrus (𝔓75), his Gen-11 figures merely duplicating Shelah's — so the "deletion" is more likely a Greek-side addition. And the corruption reading fails on its own terms: even Kainan's defenders posit an accidental loss centuries before the rabbis; the omission is shared by the very pro-Septuagint Fathers (Africanus, Eusebius, Jerome) who rejected him as an interpolation; Agapius's clinching appeal to the Samaritan Torah is mistaken (it omits Kainan too); and even Smith grants the longer chronology "is not dependent on Kainan." Intersects the genealogy of Luke 3:36).

The Origin and Inspiration of the Septuagint

  • The Simeon-the-Just legend: did one of the Seventy balk at Isaiah 7:14's "virgin"? — assessment: weak (a late-antique offshoot of the origin legend, told whole by al-Makīn (c. 1273) and in seed by Ibn al-Rāhib, attested first by Eutychius of Alexandria (c. 938) and Agapius (c. 942): that one of the seventy-two translators, Simeon the Just — identified with the Simeon of Luke 2 — grieved over the messianic prophecies he rendered, and at Isaiah 7:14's "virgin" (in the sharpest, Coptic-Synaxarium form) was tempted to write "girl," for which he was kept alive 350 years until he held the Christ in the Temple. As an argument that the Septuagint preserved an authentic "virgin" against a Jewish softening, weak: the hagiographic mirror of the Aquila charge, and a genuine witness to how deeply the Isaiah 7:14 anxiety had saturated the Christian East — but as evidence a late (10th-c. and after), demonstrably legendary conflation of three Simeons bridged by an impossible 350-year lifespan, adding nothing to the philological question of 'almah, and locating the temptation to soften "virgin" inside the translators' own work — conceding that Isaiah 7:14 is a question of how to render the unchanged Hebrew, not of tampering with the Hebrew text. Cultural memory, not proof. Master collection: Wasserstein, The Legend of the Septuagint (2006)).

The Biblical Canon

Related arguments — the Nicene-canon legend

  • Did the Council of Nicaea (325) fix the biblical canon? — the popular legend — assessment: unsupported (the claim, bestselling in Dan Brown's The Da Vinci Code (2003) but Enlightenment-rationalist in origin (Paine, Voltaire), that the Bible's contents were decided at Nicaea: a canon voted, a "new Bible" commissioned and financed by Constantine "which omitted those gospels that spoke of Christ's human traits," the rest "outlawed, gathered up and burned." Unsupported on every part — the council's twenty surviving canons and acts say nothing of Scripture; Eusebius, present at Nicaea, records the canon as still disputed in his own day (Church History 3.25); Athanasius, also present, in 367 still frames listing the books as his own undertaking of "necessity," the canon "handed down" not decreed (whence Breen infers no Nicene catalogue was promulgated); and Constantine's one real Bible-project, the fifty copies from Eusebius (Life of Constantine 4.36), was transcription of an existing collection, not selection, with no suppression or burning. The legend distorts a real but smaller history — an emperor who convened a doctrine-and-discipline council and later paid for Bibles. A far narrower scholarly question — whether Nicaea used or recognized particular disputed books in passing — is separate, and even its defenders deny the council drew up an official list).
  • The altar-ordeal miracle — did the inspired books leap onto the altar at Nicaea while the spurious fell beneath? — assessment: unsupported (the mechanism strand of the same legend: the disputed writings laid by the altar, the council at prayer, the inspired books found on top and the spurious beneath. It rests on a single source — §35 of the anonymous Synodicon Vetus, compiled c. 900, nearly six centuries after the council — a work crowded with details found nowhere else and recounting synods scholars doubt ever met; no contemporary witness, and none of the council's own acts or twenty canons, knows of it; and its result, a canon closed by divine sign in 325, is contradicted by Eusebius and Athanasius, both present at Nicaea, who go on treating the canon as open. The tale entered Western letters only when its Greek manuscript was carried west by Andreas Darmasius and printed in 1601 by Johannes Pappus — hence later citations "Pappus, in his Synodicon" — and was relayed by Voltaire (Philosophical Dictionary, "Councils," 1764), the source of the modern "even sorted by a miracle" embellishment).
  • Were the infancy gospels suppressed — banned, voted out, gathered up and burned? — assessment: unsupported (the same legend in New-Testament-apocrypha dress: that the gospels left out of the New Testament, the infancy gospels above all — the Infancy Gospel of Thomas, the Protoevangelium of James — were not merely passed over but actively suppressed, "outlawed, gathered up and burned" (Dan Brown), "banned from the Bible." The documented history is gradual non-reception: from the second century on the proto-orthodox never widely received these texts as apostolic (Irenaeus already files the boy-Jesus alphabet tale among forged apocrypha c. 180; Eusebius brackets the apostolic-pseudonym gospels among writings "no one … deemed worthy of mention"), and the late Western rejection-lists speak the language of books "not to be received" — Innocent I (405) "rejected and condemned," the Decretum Gelasianum (c. 500) literally titled On books to be received and not to be received — not destroyed. The one real decree-and-bonfire near Nicaea burned a heretic's doctrinal treatises — Constantine's order to torch Arius's writings on pain of death — not the apocryphal gospels, and even that failed; meanwhile the excluded gospels circulated freely for a thousand years, the Protoevangelium surviving in 140-odd Greek manuscripts and a dozen languages — the footprint of free circulation, not eradication. The milder true claim survives: they were never received as Scripture).

Related arguments — the scholarly case for a Nicene canon-action

  • Jerome's Preface to Judith — did the Council of Nicaea count Judith among the Scriptures? — assessment: weak (the one early text ever adduced for a real Nicene action on the canon, apart from the popular legend, and Cornely's strongest prop: Jerome (c. 405) reports that Judith "is found by the Nicene Council to have been counted among the number of the Sacred Scriptures," which the Jesuit scholar Rudolph Cornely reads as proof the council of 325 enumerated the sacred books in a decree, deuterocanon included. But Jerome's hedged "is found to have been counted" attests at most a viva voce use of Judith in the deliberations — a book quoted as Scripture, as Ephesus later leaned on Hebrews without decreeing a NT canon — not a promulgated list; he cites no Nicene catalogue, the council's twenty canons say nothing of Scripture, and Jerome himself in the same prefaces rules Judith out of the canon, which he could not do had Nicaea decreed it in. Athanasius (present at Nicaea) excludes Judith in 367 likewise, and Hefele treats a Nicene canon-decree as a later misattribution. The modest reading — Nicaea cited Judith in argument — is plausible; the decree-reading is weak. The popular form of the same claim is weaker still: unsupported).
  • The Council of Hippo's canon 36 under a "Nicaea" heading — does the Vallicellian codex prove the council drew its canon from Nicaea? — assessment: unsupported (Cornely's third prop: a single eighth/ninth-century codex (Vallicelliana B.58, fol. 77) prefaces the canon-list of the Council of Hippo (393) — "that besides the canonical Scriptures nothing be read in church" — with the rubric "Chapter XXIV of the Council of Nicaea," then runs out a catalogue intermixing the proto- and deuterocanonical books; Cornely concludes the Fathers of Hippo drew their list from the decrees of Nicaea, carried to Africa by Caecilian of Carthage, so that Nicaea decreed the wider canon. But the list beneath the rubric is the securely-dated African canon of Hippo — reaffirmed at Carthage in 397 and 419 — not a Nicene one; the "of Nicaea" heading survives in this lone late manuscript and is a textbook case of the mislabelling Hefele documents, later conciliar canons drifting under Nicaea's name because the genuine Nicene canons always headed the early corpus (the Rome copy welding Nicaea and Sardica sub uno titulo; Pope Zosimus quoting a Sardican canon as Nicene). The very Nicene exemplar Cornely invokes — Caecilian's copy at Carthage — is on record as having held no such canon when the African bishops searched it in the Apiarius affair, sending instead to Alexandria and Constantinople for authentic texts. Breen grants the prop "what force it may have" yet still refuses to "refer the Council of Nice among the proofs of the Canon," noting it "exists but in one sole codex"; the council's twenty canons are silent).
  • Cassiodorus's Institutiones I.14 — does listing Nicaea among the dividers of Scripture show the council took up the canon? — assessment: unsupported (Cornely's weakest prop: Cassiodorus (c. 543) names "the Councils of Nicaea and Chalcedon," with Hilary, Rufinus, and Epiphanius, among those who "by their divisions, fitted the sacred books to the appropriate mysteries," and Cornely infers the Nicene Fathers drew up a division of the canon. But the chapter is about how the already-received books are divided and numbered — reconciling Jerome's, Augustine's, and the Septuagint's schemes — not about which books are Scripture; the sentence yokes Nicaea to Chalcedon, which decreed no canon, so the inference proves too much; and as sixth-century, second-hand testimony to 325 it is best read as an instance of the later drift of canon-material under Nicaea's name — the very drift Hefele documents, the source from which Cornely (Breen notes) borrowed the citation. Breen's verdict — "obscure words" that "form no forcible proof" — stands, and the council's own twenty canons are silent).

Related arguments — the shape of the canon's formation

  • Was the New Testament canon settled gradually, contested at its edges — not delivered whole? — assessment: sound (the doctrine's positive backbone, and the corrective to the Nicaea-decreed-it legend: the timeline's own witnesses show a boundary that formed by gradual reception and stayed fuzzy at its edges for centuries. Books once received as Scripture or near it were later dropped — Irenaeus's 1 Clement and Shepherd of Hermas, the Muratorian list's Apocalypse of Peter and Wisdom of Solomon, Barnabas and the Shepherd bound into Codex Sinaiticus and the Clementine epistles into Alexandrinus — while seven that made it in were long disputed: Eusebius's antilegomena (James, Jude, 2 Peter, 2–3 John) plus Hebrews (doubted in the West) and the Apocalypse (doubted longest in the East — questioned by Dionysius of Alexandria, omitted by Cyril, absent from the twenty-two-book Syriac Peshitta). The early lists are partial and regional, the first exact twenty-seven coming only with Athanasius (367). Sound — mainstream canon history, attested from within by Eusebius's threefold sorting and articulated by Metzger and Bruce — with one load-bearing qualification: the fuzziness belongs to the edges. The fourfold-Gospel-and-Pauline core was fixed by c. 180 (Irenaeus's closed Tetramorph), so the thesis underwrites neither the "decreed whole at Nicaea" legend nor its mirror image, the "late and arbitrary" debunking).
  • Was the fourfold Gospel already closed by c. 180? — Irenaeus's Tetramorph — assessment: sound (the early-fixed-core complement to the soft-edges thesis above. By c. 180 Irenaeus defends the Gospels as exactly four, "neither more nor fewer," matched to the four zones of the world, the four winds, and the four cherubic faces — the "Tetramorph" (Against Heresies 3.11.8). Two claims must be kept apart. The argument for the number four — the cosmic symbolism — is an a priori fitness-argument, weak as proof of why there must be four (Skeat showed Irenaeus borrowed even the living-creatures scheme from an earlier source). But the historical datum it presses — that the fourfold Gospel was a fixed, closed collection by the late second century, a century before the rest of the New Testament settled — is sound: mainstream canon scholarship (Stanton, Hengel, Hill, Kruger), resting not on the symbolism but on independent attestation — Justin's "memoirs … called Gospels" (c. 155), Tatian's harmony of the four (the Diatessaron, c. 170), the Epistula Apostolorum (c. 140s), the early four-Gospel codices. Irenaeus is the witness to a settled fourfold Gospel, not its inventor; his weak argument for the number no more unsettles the fact than a poor proof unsettles a true theorem. The firm center against which the soft edges are measured).
  • The Council of Rome (382) and the "Damasine list" — did Damasus promulgate the canon? — assessment: weak (the documentary basis of the popular claim that "the canon was fixed at the Council of Rome in 382 under Damasus." A real synod met at Rome in 382, and a canon-list matching the present twenty-seven books and the longer Septuagint Old Testament — the "Damasine list" — is ascribed to it; a respectable minority (Arévalo, who first proposed the attribution in 1794; Turner; Jurgens) holds the canon-chapters authentically Damasan. But the list survives in no fourth-century document — only as a chapter of the Decretum Gelasianum, which von Dobschütz showed to be a single sixth-century pseudonymous compilation (its opening chapter quotes Augustine's work of 416), dated c. 500 on this site; the 382 attribution is itself an eighteenth-century conjecture unsupported by any contemporary witness; and Jerome — present at Rome, the age's foremost authority on the canon, and the very writer the list depends on — nowhere mentions a Roman conciliar canon, ranking Judith, Tobit, and the Maccabees below the canon in his own prefaces. What the evidence bears is the modest, plausible claim that Damasus's Rome received the canon the West then held, a decade before Hippo put it into conciliar form; the "fixed at Rome in 382" framing does not hold — a settled boundary read backward onto a council that left no such act, the close cousin of the Nicaea-counted-the-canon error).
  • Which "Gospel of Thomas" did the Fathers condemn — the sayings gospel or the Infancy Gospel? — assessment: weak (one title, "Gospel of Thomas," names two unrelated works — the Gnostic sayings gospel (114 logia, Nag Hammadi) and the Infancy Gospel of Thomas (the miracle-working boy Jesus) — so the early condemnations of "the Gospel of Thomas" (Hippolytus, Origen, Eusebius, Cyril of Jerusalem, the Gelasian Decree) are routinely read as the rejection of the infancy text. That equation is weak: where the target is identifiable it is the sayings gospel or a Manichaean book of that name — Hippolytus quotes its saying 4 and ties it to the Gnostic Naassenes; Cyril names a "Gospel of Thomas" written by "one of the three wicked disciples of Manes"; Eusebius brackets it among the heretics' apostolic-pseudonym forgeries (Peter, Thomas, Matthias). Only Origen's bare list and Eusebius's catalogue-name are loose enough to admit the infancy text, and an undescribed title identifies nothing. The witness who does attest the infancy material — Irenaeus, on the boy-Jesus alphabet tale (Against Heresies 1.20.1) — never calls it "Thomas" or a "gospel"; and the Gelasian list itself registers the Manichaean "Gospel of Thomas" and "the book on the infancy of the saviour" as separate entries. The infancy gospel was excluded too — but on its own, partly different evidence; the famous "Gospel of Thomas" rejections should not do double duty. See also were the infancy gospels suppressed?).

The Old Testament Canon (Is the Deuterocanon Scripture?)

Related arguments — the primitive church's witness

  • Does the New Testament's never quoting the deuterocanon as Scripture prove it is not canonical? — assessment: weak (as a decisive disqualifier. The stock Protestant argument from silence — scholarly form Roger Beckwith, The Old Testament Canon of the New Testament Church (1985): the NT cites most of the protocanon as Scripture with formulae like "it is written," but never a deuterocanonical book that way, so the apostles did not reckon them inspired. The observation is sound — the citation asymmetry is real — but the inference to exclusion does not hold. It proves too much: the NT likewise never quotes Esther, Ecclesiastes, the Song of Songs, Ezra–Nehemiah, Obadiah, or Nahum, which no one drops. It is false to the data: the NT alludes to the deuterocanon repeatedly — Hebrews 11:35 ("tortured, not accepting deliverance … a better resurrection") to the martyrs of 2 Maccabees 7, Romans 1 to Wisdom 13–14, Romans 9:21 to Wisdom 15:7, Matthew 27:43 to Wisdom 2, James 1:19 to Sirach 5:11 — allusions the Nestle–Aland critical Greek New Testament itself registers, and Gallagher & Meade (2017) catalogue. And "quotation as Scripture" is a fuzzy test applied inconsistently, the NT quoting 1 Enoch as prophecy (Jude 14–15) and pagan poets (Acts 17:28; Titus 1:12; 1 Cor 15:33) without canonizing them. What survives is the narrower, true point that the apostles cite the protocanon as Scripture and the deuterocanon only by allusion — evidence for the graded standing the Hebrew-minded Fathers (Origen, Jerome) assigned these books, not the flat exclusion the syllogism wants).
  • Did the early church reject the deuterocanon as Scripture, making its later inclusion an accretion? — assessment: contested (the Protestant removal argument from the patristic canon-lists — Luther in instinct, Roger Beckwith in print: the earliest Christian lists (Melito, Origen, Cyril, Athanasius, Epiphanius, Jerome, Rufinus) are Hebrew-sized, so the primitive church did not hold the disputed books Scripture, and their later reception is an accretion. The early witness is genuinely divided, and the verdict turns on the form of the claim. The narrow, graded thesis is strong — a learned, Hebrew-canon-conscious stream did rank these books below a canon measured by the Hebrew table, the backbone of the Reformation's case. But the flat, universal claim does not hold: the church's working Bible was the Septuagint, which carried the books; the great codices interleave them with the protocanon; a parallel, equally early Western stream (the African councils, Augustine, Innocent I) received them among the Scriptures; and even the "excluders" read and cited them, tiering rather than rejecting (Origen's "copies in use in our Churches," Rufinus's "Ecclesiastical" books). So the deuterocanon is not a late addition but a long-disputed inheritance — tiered by a minority, used by the majority, as the Synod of Jerusalem itself conceded ("not always have all of these been considered on the same level as the rest"). No clear winner).

Related arguments — Abel to Zechariah

  • Does "from the blood of Abel to the blood of Zechariah" delimit the Hebrew OT canon, excluding the Apocrypha? — assessment: weak (as a demarcation of the canon. The popular Protestant "A-to-Z" / "bookends" argument — scholarly form Roger Beckwith, popularly F. F. Bruce and Doug Wilson: because Chronicles stands last in the Hebrew order and records a Zechariah's murder, Jesus' "from the blood of Abel … to the blood of Zechariah" (Luke 11:51 // Matt 23:35) traces the limits of the canon, Genesis to Chronicles, excluding Maccabees and the rest of the Apocrypha. That Jesus names the first and last martyrs of Old-Testament history, summing up the generation's guilt in a beginning-to-end merism, is sound and is how the whole church read the saying. But the canon-demarcation inference fails on three independent counts. Reception: no interpreter on record read it as a canon-boundary until Eichhorn in 1780 — the unbroken tradition (Tertullian, Origen, Chrysostom, Jerome, Calvin, Lightfoot) read it of the first-to-last martyr, and Jerome, the great Hebrew-canon champion, never enlisted it for the canon. Book-order: Genesis-to-Chronicles is not securely the order Jesus knew — across the first-millennium canon-lists no Chronicles-last sequence is dominant (it standardizes only after 1207), and its one early Talmudic witness (Bava Batra 14b) sits beside material (Bava Kamma) that counts Sirach among the Writings. Identity: Matthew's "Zechariah son of Berechiah" does not fit the Chronicles figure (son of Jehoiada, 2 Chron 24:20–22) without positing a scribal error or a name-conflation — a difficulty even proponents (Wilson, citing Gleason Archer) concede. What survives is the modest, universally-held martyr-merism, carrying at most a faint, order-dependent presumption toward a Hebrew-shaped canon — far short of the claim that the Apocrypha is here excluded; the Hebrew-canon case stands on stronger grounds elsewhere, weighed by Edmon Gallagher (NTS 60, 2014; Tyndale Bulletin 65.2, 2014)).
  • Which Zechariah does Jesus name in "from the blood of Abel to the blood of Zechariah" — and is he the Chronicles figure the "bookends" argument needs? — assessment: contested (the load-bearing sub-claim beneath the bookends argument: that the Zechariah Jesus names is Zechariah son of Jehoiada (2 Chron 24:20–22), the priest stoned in the temple court and so the "last martyr in the last book." The identification is the most defensible reading and probably right: his is the only temple-court martyrdom in inspired Scripture, it matches "between the sanctuary and the altar" exactly, and the most exacting interpreters — Jerome, Calvin, Lightfoot, Michaelis, and the weight of modern commentary — settle there. But it is genuinely contested. Matthew's "son of Berechiah" (Matt 23:35) fits the priest (son of Jehoiada) only by an unattested second name, a textual emendation against every critical edition, an over-loose "descendant," or a name-conflation device whose use in Jesus' day is unproven; and a strong patristic stream (Origen, Basil, Gregory of Nyssa, Cyril of Alexandria) identified the figure instead with the father of John the Baptist, with the prophet of Zechariah 1:1 and Josephus' son of Baruch as further live candidates. For the bookends argument the upshot is twofold: the sub-claim it rests on is probable but contested, so it cannot be assumed; and even granted in full, identifying the Chronicles Zechariah shows only that Jesus named a martyr who stands in the last book of one Hebrew order — not that he was tracing the canon's edge. The most likely identification, established, still does not carry the canon-demarcation inference it was enlisted to support).
  • Did the Hebrew canon end with Chronicles in Jesus' day — was a Genesis-to-Chronicles order established and dominant before the first century? — assessment: weak (for the form the bookends argument needs. The historical premise the argument rests on — Roger Beckwith in print, leaning on the Talmud; Doug Wilson popularly, "the order … Jesus would have been familiar with": that the Jews of Jesus' day arranged their Bible Genesis-to-Chronicles, Chronicles standing last, so "Abel … to Zechariah" spans first book to last. Surveyed against the record, the strong form does not hold. Across the first-millennium canon-lists and codices no Chronicles-last order is dominant; the great majority close on Daniel, Ezra, Esther, or Maccabees (Melito, Origen, Cyril, Athanasius, Epiphanius, Jerome, Augustine, and the great codices among them), and the authoritative Tiberian Hebrew manuscripts — the Aleppo and Leningrad codices — put Chronicles first in the Writings, not last. The Chronicles-last arrangement becomes common only after the Adath Deborim (1207) and standard with print — and the Adath Deborim itself reports that Chronicles-first is the "right and true" order and that "the Babylonians have altered this order," moving Chronicles to the end. The one clearly early Jewish witness to a Chronicles-last order, the baraita at Bava Batra 14b, is genuine and is the steelman's proper foundation — but it stands nearly alone for a millennium and sits in a context (Bava Kamma citing Sirach as Writings; Epiphanius reporting Jews still disputing Sirach and Wisdom) that does not yield the clean Apocrypha-excluding canon the argument needs; and the other early Chronicles-last list, Innocent I's (405), includes the deuterocanon. To carry the premise one must hold that Bava Batra preserves a pre-Christian order Jesus knew, lost to history for a thousand years and recovered only after 1207 — possible, but unevidenced, and against the grain of the data. What survives is the modest point that a Chronicles-last order existed in some rabbinic stream; what fails is the load-bearing premise that it was the familiar shape of the Bible in Jesus' day. Weighed by Edmon Gallagher, Tyndale Bulletin 65.2 (2014)).

Related arguments — New-Testament quotations with no Old-Testament source

  • Does the New Testament quote, as Scripture, words found in no Old Testament book? (Ephesians 5:14) — assessment: contested (the centerpiece of the cluster, and the companion to the deuterocanon-non-quotation argument run the other way: not why the apostles never quote the disputed Old-Testament books, but what to make of the places where they cite, with a Scripture-formula, words found in no Old-Testament book at all — Ephesians 5:14 ("Awake thou that sleepest…," prefaced by dió légei) the centerpiece, with siblings at 1 Corinthians 2:9, John 7:38, and Luke 11:49. The bare phenomenon is sound and undisputed — Jerome himself, scouring "all the editions of the ancient Scriptures and the texts of the Hebrews themselves," could not find Ephesians 5:14 — but the interpretation it is pressed to serve, that the apostles thereby quoted a non-canonical writing as Scripture so their Bible had no closed Old-Testament edge, splits. Strong by one reading: apostolic "Scripture" demonstrably had soft outer edges (the same NT cites 1 Enoch and follows the Septuagint), which tells against any cleanly-closed-Hebrew-canon premise. Weak by another: for the centerpiece the better explanation is an early Christian baptismal hymn, not an apocryphon or lost prophet; the one verse a Father pins to the Apocalypse of Elijah (1 Cor 2:9, via Origen and Ambrosiaster) is contested by Jerome, who tied it to Isaiah 64:4, and rests on a source that postdates the New Testament and lacks the words, so the borrowing may run the other way; Epiphanius's primary text says only that the words stand "in Elijah," not naming the Apocalypse, with Holl proposing to emend even that to "Isaiah"; and a citation formula marks a quotation, not a canon-verdict — Jude quotes 1 Enoch as prophecy without canonizing it. What survives is the modest, true point that apostolic "Scripture" had soft outer edges, not the wider claim that the apostles canonized an apocryphon. No clear winner).
  • "Eye hath not seen…" (1 Corinthians 2:9): does Paul quote, as Scripture, an apocryphon outside the Old Testament canon? — assessment: weak (the firmest sibling for the wider-canon reading, since here a named Father pins the words to a specific apocryphal book: Origen (and Ambrosiaster after him) traces Paul's "Eye hath not seen, nor ear heard…," introduced "as it is written," to the lost Apocalypse (Secrets) of Elijah. Yet it is weak on its best case. Jerome, reading the Hebrew, dismissed the apocryphal ascription as "the ravings of the apocryphal books" and located the words in a free paraphrase of Isaiah 64:4 — a canonical source, on the exact paraphrastic practice he documents across a dozen apostolic citations. The apocryphal anchor is itself divided (Ambrosiaster couples it with Isaiah; Jerome denies it), and the surviving Apocalypse of Elijah is a late Coptic work that postdates Paul and lacks the phrase, so any borrowing runs the other way. A canonical source is available and probably right; a citation formula does not canonize what it cites — the verse shows the looseness of apostolic quotation, not a wider canon).
  • "Out of his belly shall flow rivers of living water" (John 7:38): does Jesus cite, as Scripture, words found in no Old Testament book? — assessment: weak (the weakest sibling: "as the scripture hath said" introduces words with no verbatim Old-Testament locus, but unlike 1 Corinthians 2:9 no Father or modern scholar proposes a non-canonical writing here at all. The candidates are all canonical "living-water" texts — Jerome to Proverbs, others to Zechariah 14:8 (fitting John's Tabernacles setting), Isaiah 58:11, Ezekiel 47, the wilderness rock — best read as a free, composite, fulfillment-shaped allusion to "the Scripture" as a whole; and Chrysostom dissolves even that by repunctuating, attaching "as the scripture hath said" to believing rather than to a quotation ("where does the Scripture say this? No where"). On every serious reading the reference stays inside the canon or is no quotation; as evidence for an open canon it barely registers).
  • "Therefore also said the wisdom of God" (Luke 11:49): does Jesus quote a written source outside the Old Testament canon? — assessment: weak (the sibling whose source is least recoverable, and so where the puzzle is most genuine: a saying ("I will send them prophets and apostles…") is ascribed to "the wisdom of God" with no Old-Testament locus. But the inference to a quoted non-canonical writing is still weak, because the leading readings need no book. The patristic reading (Ambrose, Bede) takes "the wisdom of God" to be Christ speaking in his own person — confirmed by the parallel at Matthew 23:34, where Jesus says "I send" — and the dominant modern reading takes it as personified Wisdom (Sophia) speaking, redacted onto Jesus' lips by Matthew. Whether a lost wisdom-text or a free sapiential composition lies behind the words is conceded uncertain, but both leading options identify a speaker, not a quoted writing. The hard-to-place source leaves a soft-boundary point standing, not the wider-canon claim).

Related arguments — Augustine's canon witness

  • Should Augustine's canon witness be discounted because he was "no biblical expert" and could not read Hebrew, unlike Jerome? — assessment: weak (CARM's comparative-competence move, with James White's sharper "Augustine could not read Hebrew": the councils that carried the longer canon "were influenced heavily by Augustine, who was no Biblical expert, compared to the scholar Jerome," and "were not universal Church councils," so where the two contemporaries disagree the Hebraist's "No" should outrank the non-Hebraist's "Yes," and the African councils' verdict should be discounted as his product and merely regional. The factual scaffolding is sound — Jerome was the superior philologist, Augustine read no Hebrew, and he did shape the African synods — but the discounting inference is weak. It impeaches a competence Augustine's canon-argument never invoked: his test was breadth of churchly reception (On Christian Doctrine II.8.12), not Hebrew enumeration, for which reading Hebrew is not decisive — a genetic move. "No biblical expert" holds only by redefining the phrase as "Hebraist," begging the very question at issue; Augustine — whose On Christian Doctrine is itself a manual of biblical interpretation — was no such thing. The longer canon was no one man's product: it stood in Western use before and beyond him — Cyprian, the Cheltenham List, his own bishop Ambrose, the Roman Damasine list, Innocent I, and the Septuagint behind them all — so Hippo and Carthage ratified a reception already in hand, even deferring it to Rome for confirmation in 419. And "not universal" is true of the regional African synods but misfires on the ecumenical Florence (1442) CARM lumps with them, whose Cantate Domino enumerates the full canon. What survives is the real fault line this timeline traces — reception against Jerome's Hebraica veritas — a dispute over the criterion, which the men's relative command of Hebrew does not settle. Sibling of the "he thought they were in the Hebrew canon" argument).
  • Did Augustine accept the deuterocanon only because he mistakenly thought it stood in the Hebrew canon? — assessment: weak (James White's bid to recruit Augustine for the Protestant side by a counterfactual about his state of mind: "though he accepted the Apocryphal books, he did so because he thought they were part of the Hebrew canon. If he had known what the Hebrew canon was, he would have taken a different position." The true core survives — Augustine read no Hebrew and held the Septuagint inspired (City of God XVIII.43), so his canon does follow the Greek Bible — but the conclusion built on it does not hold, because Augustine contradicts its factual premise in his own late words. City of God XVII.20 (c. 425) flags Wisdom and Sirach as "not written in the canon of the Jews," and XVIII.36 grants the Maccabees are "held as canonical, not by the Jews, but by the Church": he knew the disputed books stood outside the Hebrew table and kept them on churchly reception, the criterion of On Christian Doctrine II.8.12, which never measured the canon by the Hebrew enumeration in the first place — so "had he known the Hebrew canon" changes nothing his test runs on. He had urged Jerome not to translate "out of the Hebrew" precisely because the two canons diverge, and cited the deuterocanon as Scripture to the end (On Rebuke and Grace, c. 426) — no late drift toward Jerome. What is left is not a recruited Protestant witness but the genuine Augustine-versus-Jerome dispute over the criterion — churchly reception against the Hebrew measure — in which Augustine's longer canon is a considered verdict, not a mistake).
  • Did Augustine retract his view and rank the deuterocanon below the canonical Scriptures? — assessment: weak (Archibald Alexander's bid (The Canon of the Old and New Testaments Ascertained, 1826) to reclaim Augustine for the shorter canon: that he said Ecclesiasticus "deserves to be received among the prophetical books" "but this opinion he retracted afterwards"; that he reports a custom by which the apocrypha were "read in some of the churches" but "not as Holy Scripture … from a lower place"; and so "did not put them on a level with the genuine canonical books." Its props fail. The retraction is real but misread: Augustine's Retractations II.4 corrects only the authorship of the Wisdom of Solomon — "I made a mistake as to the authorship" — not its canonical standing, which the same review leaves intact (it touches the forty-four-book sentence only to qualify the phrase "Old Testament"). The "lower place / not as Holy Scripture" custom is not Augustine's at all but the canonical-versus-"ecclesiastical" distinction of Jerome and Rufinus (books read "for edification," "not appealed to for the confirmation of doctrine") — the very line Augustine declines, using "canonical" in the wider sense and listing the disputed books inside his canon (On Christian Doctrine II.8.12–13). And Augustine cites Wisdom, Sirach, and 2 Maccabees as "Scripture" into his final years (On Rebuke and Grace, c. 426). What survives is a smaller, sound point — his reception-criterion grades authority by breadth, so books received by fewer churches rank lower on that scale — but that is a gradation within the canon, not the categorical demotion (in vs. out) Alexander needs. Flag the overread retraction and the misattributed custom. Third of the Augustine-witness siblings).

Related arguments — the Trent claim

  • Did the Council of Trent add the deuterocanonical books to the Bible in 1546? — assessment: weak (for the bare "addition" framing. The headline popular-Protestant trope — Mark Driscoll ("voted to insert new books into the Bible"), Mike Winger ("'OK — pop — now it's in the Bible'"), Todd Friel, Bible.org, downstream of the Reformation's Hebrew-canon instinct: that Trent added — "inserted," "voted into the Bible" — the seven deuterocanonical books in 1546, so before then they were no part of Scripture. As a description of what happened the slogan does not hold on its best case: it confuses defining a book's status with adding the book, and a dispute over rank ("on the same level") with one over presence ("now it's in the Bible"). The books were inside the Septuagint the church inherited, the great uncial codices (Vaticanus, Sinaiticus, Alexandrinus), the Latin Vulgate of the West, the African and Roman conciliar lists, and the ecumenical profession of the Council of Florence (Cantate Domino, 1442) for a millennium and more before Trent, and were cited as Scripture by a long line of Fathers; Trent's own decree describes itself as "following the examples of the orthodox Fathers" in receiving books "used to be read in the Catholic Church" — a definition of a reception, not the creation of new Scripture — and even the careful proponents (Allen Parr) concede "these books were in the Bible" before 1546. What survives, and is sound, is the narrower, different claim that Trent's dogmatic close under anathema was an innovation in force (the first binding ecumenical definition of the canon's edge), taken up by the companion Trent-novelty argument. The slide from "not on the same level / disputed" (true) to "added / inserted new books" (false) is the overstatement to flag).
  • Did Trent add the deuterocanon in order to manufacture support for purgatory and prayer for the dead? — assessment: weak (for the motive/causation claim. The sharpened, motive form of the "added at Trent" trope — Todd Friel ("added those to the canon in order to support their doctrines that had actually no biblical support"), Mike Winger, Bible.org ("29 years after Luther"): not just that Trent added the books in 1546 but that it added them deliberately to supply proof-texts for purgatory, prayer for the dead, and almsgiving-merit. It does not hold, and for a reason the bare trope shares but this version sharpens — it is anachronistic. The books it says were inserted to rescue the doctrine had stood in the Septuagint, the uncial codices, the Vulgate, the African and Roman lists, and the ecumenical profession of Florence (1442) for more than a thousand years before purgatory was a Reformation controversy, and 2 Maccabees 12 was read as the warrant for prayer for the dead from the patristic era (Tertullian, Cyprian, Cyril of Jerusalem, Augustine — the practice traced on the purgatory timeline), so the doctrine grew up alongside a book already received rather than the book being inserted to save it. Motive and merit stay separate: a polemically-timed definition is not thereby false — the genetic move would equally discredit the Reformers' own sixteenth-century removal — and a canonical book does not by itself prove the doctrine, the proof-text strength weighed on the 2 Maccabees and Tobit pages. What is granted is the narrower point this overshoots: Trent defined the canon in deliberate answer to the Reformation, so the definition's timing and emphasis were polemically shaped — but a definition's timing is not the books' origin).
  • Was the deuterocanon a late novelty — held below Scripture for a thousand years until Trent canonized it in 1546? — assessment: weak (for the novelty-as-disqualifier inference.
