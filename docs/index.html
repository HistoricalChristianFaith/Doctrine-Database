Scripture’s text & canon

The Biblical Canon

how the Church came to recognize which books are Scripture: the gradual, regional reception of the Old and New Testaments vs. the popular legend that the canon was fixed in one stroke at the Council of Nicaea (325).

the Nicene-canon legend

  • Did the Council of Nicaea (325) fix the biblical canon? — the popular legend — unsupported
  • The altar-ordeal miracle — did the inspired books leap onto the altar at Nicaea while the spurious fell beneath? — unsupported
  • Were the infancy gospels suppressed — banned, voted out, gathered up and burned? — unsupported

the scholarly case for a Nicene canon-action

  • Jerome's Preface to Judith — did the Council of Nicaea count Judith among the Scriptures? — weak
  • The Council of Hippo's canon 36 under a "Nicaea" heading — does the Vallicellian codex prove the council drew its canon from Nicaea? — unsupported
  • Cassiodorus's Institutiones I.14 — does listing Nicaea among the dividers of Scripture show the council took up the canon? — unsupported

the shape of the canon's formation

  • Was the New Testament canon settled gradually, contested at its edges — not delivered whole? — sound
  • Was the fourfold Gospel already closed by c. 180? — Irenaeus's Tetramorph — sound
  • The Council of Rome (382) and the "Damasine list" — did Damasus promulgate the canon? — weak
  • Which "Gospel of Thomas" did the Fathers condemn — the sayings gospel or the Infancy Gospel? — weak
  • Did Martin Luther remove four New Testament books — Hebrews, James, Jude, and Revelation? — weak

The Old Testament Canon (Is the Deuterocanon Scripture?)

is Tobit — with Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch, 1–2 Maccabees, the deuterocanon/Apocrypha — part of the Christian Old Testament? YES/NO verdicts across time: the Septuagint carried them, the Hebrew-minded Fathers and the Reformation excluded them, the African councils, Rome, Florence, and Trent affirmed them — settled by reception, not date.

the primitive church's witness

  • Does the New Testament's never quoting the deuterocanon as Scripture prove it is not canonical? — weak
  • Does the absence of a recorded canon dispute between Jesus and the Jews prove a settled, shared Hebrew canon? — weak
  • Did the early church reject the deuterocanon as Scripture, making its later inclusion an accretion? — contested

Abel to Zechariah

  • Does "from the blood of Abel to the blood of Zechariah" delimit the Hebrew OT canon, excluding the Apocrypha? — weak
  • Which Zechariah does Jesus name in "from the blood of Abel to the blood of Zechariah" — and is he the Chronicles figure the "bookends" argument needs? — contested
  • Did the Hebrew canon end with Chronicles in Jesus' day — was a Genesis-to-Chronicles order established and dominant before the first century? — weak

New-Testament quotations with no Old-Testament source

  • Does the New Testament quote, as Scripture, words found in no Old Testament book? (Ephesians 5:14) — contested
  • "Eye hath not seen…" (1 Corinthians 2:9): does Paul quote, as Scripture, an apocryphon outside the Old Testament canon? — weak
  • "Out of his belly shall flow rivers of living water" (John 7:38): does Jesus cite, as Scripture, words found in no Old Testament book? — weak
  • "Therefore also said the wisdom of God" (Luke 11:49): does Jesus quote a written source outside the Old Testament canon? — weak

Augustine's canon witness

  • Should Augustine's canon witness be discounted because he was "no biblical expert" and could not read Hebrew, unlike Jerome? — weak
  • Did Augustine accept the deuterocanon only because he mistakenly thought it stood in the Hebrew canon? — weak
  • Did Augustine retract his view and rank the deuterocanon below the canonical Scriptures? — weak

the Trent claim

  • Did the Council of Trent add the deuterocanonical books to the Bible in 1546? — weak
  • Did Trent add the deuterocanon in order to manufacture support for purgatory and prayer for the dead? — weak
  • Was the deuterocanon a late novelty — held below Scripture for a thousand years until Trent canonized it in 1546? — weak
  • Was the Council of Trent the first universal (ecumenical), binding affirmation of the deuterocanon? — contested
  • Does the deuterocanon dispute undermine sola scriptura — can Scripture settle its own table of contents? — contested
  • Does Tobit 12:9 ("alms… shall purge away all sin") teach a works-righteousness that bars it from the canon? — weak
  • Does 2 Maccabees 12:43–45 teach prayer and atoning offering for the dead? — contested
  • Does the deuterocanon's thousand years in the Vulgate put the burden of proof on those who would remove it? — weak
  • Do the deuterocanonical books disclaim their own inspiration, excluding themselves from the canon? — weak
  • Were the Jews "entrusted with the oracles of God" (Romans 3:2), so the church should follow the Hebrew canon? — contested
  • Did the Jews reject the deuterocanon — was the first-century Jewish canon closed and Hebrew-only? — weak
  • Does Josephus's twenty-two-book count (Against Apion 1.8) prove a closed, deuterocanon-excluding Hebrew canon? — contested
  • Did prophecy cease after Malachi — does that disqualify the deuterocanon from the canon? — weak
  • Were there "400 silent years" — no prophet at all between Malachi and Christ — closing the canon? — weak

  • Do the deuterocanon's historical errors (the book of Judith) disqualify it from the canon? — weak

  • Was the apostles' Bible the Greek Septuagint — and does that make the deuterocanon canonical? — contested

The Origin and Inspiration of the Septuagint

how the account of the LXX’s making grew across time — from a royal commission (the Letter of Aristeas, Josephus) to the miracle of the seventy-two translators producing word-for-word identical versions in separate cells “by the Holy Spirit” (Irenaeus, Cyril, Epiphanius, Augustine), and the skeptics who called the cells a fable (Jerome).

  • The Simeon-the-Just legend: did one of the Seventy balk at Isaiah 7:14's "virgin"? — weak

The Rabbinic Corruption of Scripture (Septuagint vs. Masoretic Text)

the patristic-to-medieval charge that rabbinic Judaism deliberately altered the Hebrew text — the messianic proof-texts and the Genesis chronology — while the older Septuagint preserved the true reading, vs. the modern text-critical finding (Tov; the Dead Sea Scrolls) that the LXX renders a different Hebrew Vorlage: divergent traditions, not corruption.

the corruption charge

  • Did the Jews deliberately corrupt the Hebrew Old Testament text? — weak
  • The Septuagint legend: were the seventy-two divinely moved to change the text? — plausible
  • Aquila's revision: anti-Christian distortion, or literalizing toward the Hebrew? — weak
  • The reverse charge: did the Jews accuse the Christians of corrupting Scripture? — weak

the Genesis chronology

  • Were the Genesis 5/11 patriarchal lifespans shortened (MT) or inflated (LXX) — and was it tampering? — contested
  • The "second Cainan" (Genesis 11 LXX / Luke 3:36) — original reading the Jews deleted, or a secondary plus? — weak

Prayer to the Saints (invocation of the saints)

may the living address the saints in heaven, “Holy Mary, pray for us”? Praying to the saints (invocation — direct second-person address) is a distinct act from believing the saints intercede. The intercession-belief is early and near-uncontested; the dating of invocation proper is the live question — a fourth-century development, or an older practice the apologists simply left unrecorded?

invocation of the saints

  • Praying to the saints vs. praying with them — is invocation the same as believing the saints intercede? — sound
  • Is invocation a fourth-century development, or an older practice the apologists didn't record? — contested
  • Do the San Sebastiano catacomb graffiti attest third-century invocation of the saints? — sound
  • Does the Council of Constantinople's letter to Theodosius (381) prove conciliar invocation of the saints? — weak for the strong reading; sound as a witness to intercession-belief

  • Was reading the Flood as real history the universal Christian position until the 19th century? — sound
  • Did the tradition require a geographically global Flood, or only an anthropologically universal one? — contested
  • Was the Flood's historicity ever "essential to the faith" — a defined dogma, or universally assumed but never defined? — contested
