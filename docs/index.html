Scripture’s text & canon

The Biblical Canon

how the Church came to recognize which books are Scripture — the gradual, regional reception of the Old and New Testaments vs. the popular legend that the canon was fixed in one stroke at the Council of Nicaea (325). Alleged contradictions in Scripture

Who Killed Goliath? (2 Samuel 21:19)

the textbook "Bible contradiction": David kills Goliath in 1 Samuel 17, yet 2 Samuel 21:19 (Masoretic Hebrew) says Elhanan the Bethlehemite "struck down Goliath the Gittite," while 1 Chronicles 20:5 has Elhanan kill "Lahmi the brother of Goliath." Nearly everyone agrees the Hebrew of 2 Samuel 21:19 was damaged in copying; the live dispute is the direction of the error — a concrete instance of the same Septuagint-vs.-Masoretic textual divergence traced on the rabbinic-corruption and Septuagint-origin pages. Traced from Josephus (c. 93), who smooths the crux by leaving the second giant unnamed, through the harmonizers who make Elhanan a byname of David (the Targum, Rashi, and a pseudo-Jerome etymology, "Adeodatus is David himself"), to Kennicott (1753) reopening it as a plain scribal corruption, the 19th-century split — Keil & Delitzsch holding Chronicles original, Wellhausen, Driver, and H. P. Smith holding Samuel primary and Chronicles a harmonization — McCarter's strong form (1984, Elhanan the original slayer), and Ozoliņš's measured recent verdict (2022) that Elhanan killed Goliath's unnamed brother.

the direction of the corruption

  • Is 2 Samuel 21:19 a scribal corruption, its original naming Goliath's brother (so David keeps the kill)? — contested
  • Was Elhanan the original slayer of Goliath, the deed later transferred to David? — contested
  • Is 2 Samuel 21:19's "Elhanan" simply another name for David himself? — weak

How Tall Was Goliath? (six or four cubits)

not an internal contradiction but a manuscript variant: the Masoretic Hebrew of 1 Samuel 17:4 makes Goliath "six cubits and a span" (~9′9″), while the older witnesses — the Dead Sea Samuel scroll 4QSama, the Septuagint, and Josephus — read "four cubits and a span" (~6′9″), as the ESV footnotes. A concrete, high-recognition case of the same Septuagint-vs.-Masoretic divergence traced on the rabbinic-corruption and Septuagint-origin pages. Traced from the "four" of the older Greek and Qumran line (the Septuagint, c. 250 BC; 4QSama, mid-1st c. BC; Josephus, c. 93), through the "six" that fills the Latin, rabbinic, and Masoretic tradition (Jerome's Vulgate, c. 405; the Talmud, c. 500; Bede, c. 716; the Aleppo and Leningrad codices, c. 930–1010), to the modern text-critics — J. Daniel Hays (2005), with McCarter, Tov, and Ulrich — who argue "four" is original and the Masoretic "six" a later inflation, against Billington and Barrick's defense of the "six."

The human soul

The Origin of the Soul (traducianism, creationism, and pre-existence)

where an individual soul comes from: propagated from the parents' souls as the body is from their bodies (traducianism), created fresh by God for each conception (creationism), or made long ago and joined to a body later (pre-existence). A question with almost no explicit scriptural warrant that nevertheless decided how original sin reaches a newborn, what to say of infants who die unbaptised, and whether the inequality of human lots is the residue of choices made before birth. Traced from Tertullian's soul sprouting from Adam as its root (c. 220) and Origen's souls that “cooled” from the vision of God (c. 253), through Gregory of Nyssa's one beginning for soul and body (c. 395), Jerome's decided creationism (c. 411) and Augustine's forty years of refusing to decide (c. 415), to Justinian's anathema on pre-existence (543) and Aquinas's verdict that traducianism is heretical (c. 1270).

Sin and its gravity

Are All Sins Equal? (the gravity of sins)

the Stoics held that a needless killing of a chicken is no smaller wrong than strangling one's father, since a departure from right reason is a departure. Christianity rejected the thesis all but unanimously — while holding a verse that appears to teach it, “whosoever shall keep the whole law, and yet offend in one point, he is guilty of all” (James 2:10). Traced from Cicero's transmission of omnia peccata paria to the Latin West (c. 46 BC) and 4 Maccabees' Jewish form of the same principle (c. AD 40), through Clement's “differences of sins” from 1 John (c. 200), Cyprian's and Ambrose's use of the graded doctrine against Novatianist rigour (252, c. 384), Jerome against Jovinian (393) and Augustine's treatise-length letter on James 2:10 (415), to Bede's two classes of sin (c. 716) and Aquinas's diagnosis that the Stoics mistook sin for a pure privation (c. 1270).

Dream-apparitions of the dead (do the departed themselves appear to the living?)

when a dead relative appears in a dream or a waking vision and reveals something true, is it the actual soul — conscious and acting — or only a likeness, worked by angels (or counterfeited by a demon) while the departed lie unaware? The fault line the Fathers drew runs between the ordinary dead (sealed away, not naturally attending us) and the glorified saints (granted awareness — the ground of prayer to the saints). Universal restoration (apokatastasis)

will God at the last restore all rational creatures — every soul, even the fallen angels — so that evil is undone and God becomes “all in all” (1 Cor 15:28)? Traced from Clement’s corrective punishment (c. 200) and Origen’s “recall all His creatures to one end” (c. 229), through Gregory of Nyssa’s uncondemned “complete annihilation of evil” (c. 380), the counter-arguments of Basil and Augustine and the anathemas of 543–553, to its survival in Maximus’s “honourable silence” and Isaac of Nineveh’s “scourge of love” (7th c.), its last patristic form in Eriugena (c. 860), and its contested modern recovery (Ramelli vs. McClymond). The verdicts on its first teacher — including whether it was condemnable in his day — are weighed under the condemnation of Origen.

Marriage, celibacy, and the single life

Did the early church permit remarriage? (the deprecation of digamy)

may a Christian whose spouse has died marry again? The early church answered almost with one voice in a single divided breath — yes, but the single life is better: remarriage after a spouse's death ("digamy") was held lawful and no sin, yet everywhere deprecated as the lesser state, the twice-married barred from the clergy and, in the East, penanced before readmission to communion. Traced earliest-first from Paul's two-sided rule (1 Corinthians 7; 1 Timothy 3, 5), through its mainstream restatement in Hermas, Clement, and Origen, its hard pole in Athenagoras (c. 177) and its lone outright prohibition in Tertullian ("a species of fornication"), the canon law of Neocaesarea, Laodicea, and Basil, and the settled Latin verdict of Ambrose, Jerome, and Augustine ("not to condemn any marriages… a better good"), to the East's crystallization at the Council in Trullo (692). The one figure who broke the consensus is Tertullian — whose ban is weighed on the remarriage-as-fornication argument (assessment contested), the central count on which Augustine hung his heresy.

  • Could a staged "alien disclosure" be the end-times "strong delusion" of 2 Thessalonians 2:11? — weak

Babel and the tongues

The First Language of Mankind

what tongue Adam spoke and what single language humanity shared before the confusion at Babel (Gen 11) — with three camps: Hebrew primacy (Hebrew the original speech, kept through Heber's line, whence "Hebrew"), Syriac/Aramaic primacy, and no divinely-given tongue (speech as human convention). The Hebrew-primacy reading runs from Josephus (c. 93) and the Pseudo-Clementine Recognitions through Eusebius and Jerome to Augustine (c. 420), who welds the etymology (Heber → "Hebrew") and the Babel account into the full thesis, and on to Isidore (c. 636) and Bede (c. 720) in the Latin West.

the origin of language

  • Was the primordial language a divine gift? (divine tongue / name-realism) — plausible
  • Was the first language a human convention, not a divine gift? (Gregory of Nyssa) — sound
  • The etymology of "Hebrew" — from the patriarch Heber, or from "crossing over"? — contested (the Heber derivation was the majority ancient reading but was disputed in antiquity and is doubted by modern philology; and either way it under-determines the primacy claim)

Biblical history & archaeology

The Historicity of the Conquest of Canaan

the external, non-biblical evidence adduced for Israel's entry into Canaan (Joshua) — Egyptian inscriptions and Levantine archaeology — with the proposed synchronisms weighed honestly. Four data points anchor the timeline, earliest first: the Soleb “Shasu of Yhw” inscription (~1400 BC), the Amarna Letters and their Habiru (~1350 BC), the Merneptah Stele’s “Israel” (~1208 BC), and the Iron I highland-settlement horizon (~1200 BC). The tightest correlations (Habiru = Hebrews, Shechem) rest on a contested early-date (~1400 BC) chronology that is not the mainstream position; the arguments adjudicate what the evidence does and does not establish.

the conquest date

  • Does the conquest date to c. 1400 BC (the early date)? — contested (biblical chronology read at face value — the 480 years of 1 Kings 6:1 and Jephthah's 300 years in Judges 11:26 — favours a fifteenth-century entry, and the plain sense of those numbers does not dissolve into symbolism; but it is a minority position, its one archaeological prop (Wood's re-dated Jericho) is weak, and it stands against the Egyptology behind the late date. The linchpin the Amarna synchronisms depend on)
  • Does the conquest date to c. 1250 BC (the late date)? — contested (the store-city name “Rameses” (Exodus 1:11) is a nineteenth-dynasty toponym pointing the oppression to the thirteenth century, and this is the majority position among scholars who grant a historical Exodus — a real point in its favour; but its field-archaeology backbone — the thirteenth-century destruction “cluster” — largely dissolves site by site, the “Rameses” reading has a live rebuttal, and it must set aside the plain biblical numbers that favour the early date)

the Amarna synchronisms

  • Are the Habiru of the Amarna Letters the biblical Hebrews? — weak as proof of the invasion (the strong equation rests on a contested etymology and treats a trans-regional social term as an ethnonym, and holds only on the early date; the defensible softer reading — Israel emerging from the ʿapiru milieu — points toward emergence within Canaan, not an invasion)
  • Was Shechem handed over without a battle — Lab'ayu (EA 289) and Joshua 8 & 24? — contested (Joshua never fights for Shechem yet holds a covenant assembly there, and Amarna independently shows its king siding with the ʿApiru — so the letters plausibly illuminate the silence; but the strong claim that EA 289 records the Joshua-24 assembly reads a rival's hostile accusation as a covenant transfer, and stacks the Habiru and early-date premises)
  • Who destroyed Canaanite Hazor — an Israelite conquest, or an internal revolt? — contested (the deliberate burning and the beheaded, hand-severed Canaanite and Egyptian statues fit an external Israelite conquest and dovetail with the one city Joshua burns; Ben-Tor reaches “Israel” by elimination, but his co-director Sharon Zuckerman reads the same selective, battle-debris-free destruction as an internal “termination ritual,” not an invasion — and the ground can narrow the suspects but cannot itself name them “Israel”: that comes from Joshua 11 alone)
  • Do the Iron I highland villages bear a material “signature” of early Israel? — contested (the trait-cluster — pig-avoidance, four-room houses, collared-rim jars, no temples — is sound as evidence a distinct highland group, plausibly early Israel, existed by c. 1200 BC, but weak as evidence of an invasion: the pottery's continuity with Late Bronze Canaan and the non-exclusivity of every trait are the mainstay of the emergence-within-Canaan models)
  • Is the Iron I structure on Mount Ebal the covenant altar of Joshua 8? — contested (splits at two joints: that Zertal's ramp-approached, unhewn-stone installation, filled with the burnt bones of young sacrificial animals, is a genuine early Iron I cult place is near-consensus — Mazar and reportedly Finkelstein grant it, and the watchtower/farmstead readings do not survive the bone deposit — and, unlike Jericho and Ai, it needs no early date; but that it is specifically Joshua's altar is read in from the text, the northern-slope location sits awkwardly against the Deuteronomy 27 ceremony, and serious scholars grant the cult place while denying the altar. The separate “curse tablet” from the excavation dump is rated weak and is no part of the case)
  • Does Soleb's “Shasu of Yhw” attest the divine name — and early Israel? — contested (the name-attestation — the oldest extra-biblical YHWH, c. 1400 BC — is plausible, arguably sound; but the leap to Israel/the conquest is weak: the ring names Shasu bedouin of the Edom–Midian fringe, not Israel, and the same datum grounds the rival Midianite/Kenite origin of Yahwism)
  • Does the Berlin pedestal relief (ÄM 21687) name “Israel” before Merneptah? — weak (two intact name-rings on a broken Egyptian statue-pedestal read Ashkelon and Canaan — two of the three Levantine names on the Merneptah Stele — and Görg/van der Veen/Theis reconstruct the damaged third ring as an archaic “Israel,” dated two centuries before Merneptah. The hook is real, but the reading fails on its best case: it needs a reconstructed sign in the broken ring, unique even when restored; its sibilant is š where Merneptah's Israel has ś (Hoffmeier's objection); the object is a 1913 dealer purchase with no find-spot, so its dating is uncontrolled; and Ritner's 2020 refutation reads the name as Assyria. A live minority reading, not a falsehood — but it cannot bear the weight of “earliest Israel,” which remains the Merneptah Stele)
  • Does the Deir ‘Alla inscription's “Balaam son of Beor” corroborate the conquest narrative? — weak as evidence for the conquest, though it splits at its joint (a plaster wall-text from Tell Deir ‘Alla, c. 880–770 BC, opens with “the account of Balaam son of Beor, the man who was a seer of the gods” — the same name, patronymic, calling, and Transjordan region as the seer of Numbers 22–24, and it shares the divine title Shadday(in) with Balaam's oracles. As attestation of the figure and a regional Balaam tradition behind the episode it is plausible-to-sound; but as proof of the conquest it is weak — the text postdates any conquest by ~500 years, recounts decidedly different events, and portrays a pagan seer of the gods. Bryant Wood's leap from a real Balaam to “Balak, Moses, Joshua… and the events described authentic” is a non-sequitur the scholarship rejects: the literary text bars adding Balaam to the “biblical people confirmed by archaeology” and shows a shared Transjordanian storytelling tradition, nothing more)
  • Was Jericho's “City IV” destroyed c. 1400 BC (Wood) or c. 1550 BC (Kenyon)? — contested, leaning against 1400 (everyone agrees the fortified City IV was violently burned; the fight is purely when. Wood's revival of Garstang's c. 1400 date rests on a minority pottery reading, and three of his four props are weak or withdrawn — the scarabs date a cemetery not the city, the stratigraphy restates the pottery, his 1410 BC radiocarbon figure collapsed on a lab calibration error — while high-precision radiocarbon of short-lived charred grain from the burn layer returned c. 1562 ± 38 BC; the flagship instance of the chronology dispute)
  • Is Joshua's Ai to be identified with Khirbet el-Maqatir rather than et-Tell? — weak (here the starting datum is a problem, not evidence: et-Tell had no Middle or Late Bronze city for Joshua 7–8 to destroy — its own excavators found only a ruined Early Bronze town, which the dominant reading takes to make the account an etiological legend for a heap named “the ruin.” The conservative answer relocates Ai to nearby Khirbet el-Maqatir, a small Late Bronze I fortress that was burned; its correspondences are real hooks — small site, smaller than Gibeon, north gate, LB I burn — but the identification stands only by importing the contested early date and an ad hoc migrating-toponym harmonization for Genesis 12:8, names a border fortress not a royal city, and has lost supporters like Joel Kramer; the companion site-correlation case to the Jericho-dating argument)
  • Do the Late Bronze destruction layers cluster into a single Joshua campaign? (Lachish, Bethel, Debir) — weak for the strong claim of one synchronized campaign (the empirical backbone of Albright's 1930s 13th-century model — Tell Beit Mirsim = Debir, Beitin = Bethel, Lachish, and Yadin's Hazor. Corrected, the cluster comes apart: Debir is misidentified and drops out — biblical Debir is Khirbet Rabûd, untouched by these waves; Bethel/Beitin is disputed and its burn early-12th-century, plausibly Philistine; Lachish fell ~1130 BC as a still-Egyptian city its excavator credits to the Sea Peoples; the destructions scatter across three waves and a century-plus with mostly non-Israelite causes. The early-date camp concedes the cluster and relocates the conquest rather than rescuing the synchronism; the general multi-site test the Jericho/Hazor/Ai cases feed)
  • Do the patristic defenses answer the moral problem of the ḥerem? — contested (a different axis: not whether the conquest happened, but how the Church answered the moral difficulty of the God-commanded ḥerem — Deuteronomy 20:16–17; Joshua 6:21. The objection runs from Marcion and the Manichaean Faustus to modern critics; the two classic replies are Origen's allegory, which transposes the slaughter into the believer's interior warfare and so declines to affirm a literal extermination, and Augustine's divine-command / just war — Contra Faustum 22, "not cruelty but righteous retribution," the fountainhead of Western just-war theory. Both serious and coherent, and the wiki does not count the divine-command defense out a priori; but the two answers pull against each other, the allegorical route sidesteps the literal history, and the killing of non-combatants on the authority of the command is where critics still press)

The Historicity of Sennacherib's Siege of Jerusalem

the external, non-biblical evidence adduced for Sennacherib's 701 BC invasion of Judah and the siege of Jerusalem under Hezekiah (2 Kings 18–19; Isaiah 36–37; 2 Chronicles 32) — the Assyrian royal annals (the Taylor Prism and its siblings), the Lachish palace reliefs, Hezekiah's siege-preparation archaeology (the Siloam inscription, the LMLK jars), and later Greek and Hellenistic historians. The external record corroborates the frame of the story — the campaign, the 46 fortified cities, the siege and tribute, and (conspicuously) that the prism never claims Jerusalem was captured — but the deliverance miracle, the 185,000 dead, and the cause of the Assyrian withdrawal are attested by no external source; the arguments weigh what the evidence does and does not establish.

the deliverance

  • Does the prism's silence on Jerusalem's capture corroborate the biblical non-capture? — sound at its core (the narrow reading is mainstream — the annals claim no capture of Jerusalem, and the Assyrian and biblical records agree the city did not fall; but the popular leap from that silence to confirmation of the deliverance miracle — the 185,000 — is weak, since silence fixes the fact of non-capture, not its cause)
  • What ended the siege — the angel, the plague, and Herodotus's mice — and do the outside sources corroborate the 185,000? — contested (that the siege ended abruptly with Jerusalem un-taken is sound; that the cause was a plague is plausible and long-held — How & Wells, Bright, Caesar — and this wiki does not set it against the biblical angel of the LORD, since a pestilence can be the means of the "angel" and "it was just disease" is no rebuttal; but the claim that Herodotus's mice and the Berossus/Josephus pestilence corroborate the 185,000 is weak — Herodotus is in the wrong country against the wrong enemy and legendary, the "Berossus" plague restates 2 Kings, and the figure is externally unverifiable and generally read as round; possibility is not proof)

one or two campaigns

  • Were there two Assyrian campaigns against Hezekiah, not one? — plausible (a serious minority reading held by real scholars — Rawlinson, Horn, Bright, Shea — on a genuine tension in the text: a submission-and-tribute block beside a defiance-and-deliverance block, and a "Tirhakah king of Cush" who acceded only c. 690 BC, read as the seam between two invasions telescoped into one; coherent and not refuted, since the post-689 BC years are undated and Assyrian silence about a setback is no witness against one — but outweighed, the annals knowing only one western war against Hezekiah, a rebellion against a peak-strength Assyria c. 688 BC implausible, the tribute gap trivial, and the Tirhakah datum neutralized by a proleptic title needing no second campaign)
  • A single 701 BC campaign, and Tirhakah as a proleptic title — sound (the mainstream near-consensus reading — Cogan, Kitchen, Yurco, Hoffmeier: one 701 BC campaign told through three biblical witnesses, with "Tirhakah king of Cush" a proleptic title, a decade before Taharqa's 690 BC accession, that defuses the anachronism whatever the Egyptian chronology proves; the load-bearing defence is that title, which needs no Egyptological machinery. One caveat keeps it honest: the further harmonizations that station a battle-ready twenty-year-old Taharqa at Eltekeh — a securely-fixed birth-date, the Shabaka–Shebitku co-regency — rest on disputed synchronisms, a reconstruction not settled fact, so flag as overreach any claim the Egyptian chronology has been nailed down)
  • Do the prism's tribute figures confirm the biblical tribute — and how to weigh the silver gap? — plausible (the corroboration is real but partial: the Assyrian annals and 2 Kings independently agree Hezekiah paid a heavy tribute after submitting, matching to the talent on the gold — 30 in both — a pointed agreement, not coincidence; but the silver diverges, the prism's 800 talents against 2 Kings 18:14's 300, and no reconciliation — weight-standards, a combined weight, Assyrian inflation, a scribal variant, or two tribute moments — is more than a possible reading)
  • What kind of witness is the Taylor Prism — and how far do the annals confirm the Bible? — sound, for the modest claim (the methodological hinge under the others: a Neo-Assyrian prism is a royal foundation inscription — ideological legitimation, not a neutral chronicle — so historians trust its skeleton of events, cross-checked against the Babylonian Chronicle, and discount its outcomes and boasts; "the prism confirms the Bible" is true once scoped to the frame — campaign, 46 cities, siege, tribute, non-capture — but the annals omit defeats, so their silence cannot confirm the deliverance, and inflate success, so their figures carry least weight)
  • How did the Fathers read the deliverance? — sound, as reception (a coda on a different axis from the evidence: from Athanasius c. 356 to the seventh-century Syriac writers, in Greek, Latin, and Syriac, the Fathers received the deliverance as plain history and preached one lesson — God, not human arms, saved Jerusalem; the angel a real agent, faith and prayer Hezekiah's weapon, the event a vindication of Isaiah's prophecy and a type of the devil's rout, Hezekiah's later pride a caution — the mainstream Christian reading, essentially uncontested; but as belief downstream of and dependent on the text it attests continuous reception, not the 185,000 — even the devout retellings vary the figure)

Dragons, unicorns & mythical beasts

Dragons, Unicorns & Mythical Beasts

how Christians across history read the great and strange creatures of Scripture — leviathan and behemoth (Job 40–41), the dragon/tannin, the re'em the KJV called "unicorn," the fiery flying serpent — as literal animals, as allegory for the devil or sin, or as pagan legend to be sifted from fact, and the legends (dragon-slayings, sea-serpent and cryptid reports) that grew around them. Opens with the pagan natural-history tradition of the "flying serpent" (Herodotus, c. 440 BC; Ammianus Marcellinus, c. 390) and runs through the Fathers' allegory (dragon-as-devil) and literal-animal readings down to John of Damascus (c. 730), whose On Dragons and Ghosts reads the dragon as a real animal — a large serpent, not a shapeshifting apparition.

dragons as real creatures

  • Does the broad classical attestation of the Bagradas serpent (Regulus, 256 BC) prove a real giant animal? — plausible
  • Did the early Christians read dragons, leviathan, and behemoth as real animals? (and does it warrant "dragons = dinosaurs"?) — contested (as a claim about the Fathers; the "dinosaur"/young-earth leap: weak)
  • Is the "fiery flying serpent" of Isaiah 30:6 a real winged reptile? — weak (as a literal winged reptile; the real-snake-and-emblem core: plausible)

the re'em / unicorn

  • The KJV "unicorn" (Hebrew re'em, 9× from Num 23:22 to Isa 34:7) — a real one-horned animal? — contested (a real animal, and the English word a transmission artifact; but the animal's identity — two-horned aurochs vs. the one-horned-in-profile oryx of the biblical deserts — and so the single horn, genuinely unsettled; the Septuagint's reading not to be presumed inferior to the Hebrew)
  • Is the Bible's "unicorn" the fabulous one-horned horse of legend? — weak (as a claim about what Scripture denotes — the word names a real beast at every link, "unicorn" in English since the 13th century, not coined in 1611; the fabulous horse a separate legend fused onto it; but the myth genuinely shaped the reading and the word)

the identity of Job's two beasts

  • Are Behemoth and Leviathan (Job 40–41) real known animals — the hippopotamus and the crocodile? — contested (the scholarly default, matched by a cognate patristic instinct, and close on the plain detail; but the poet amplifies the animal past any Nile creature and the same name elsewhere is the mythic chaos-dragon — no clean winner. The devil-allegory is folded as a second sense, sound as reception; the young-earth "dinosaur" variant folded as weak)
  • Are Behemoth and Leviathan the mythic chaos-monster of Near-Eastern combat myth? — plausible (the name is the Ugaritic Lôtān, the same Leviathan is unmistakably mythic in Ps 74, Isa 27, and Rev 12, and Job 41's fire-and-smoke excess overshoots any animal — Theodore of Mopsuestia, John Day; but Job 40 plainly describes a real river-beast, so the mythic dragon is best read as amplifying color, not the whole creature)
  • St George and the dragon (Golden Legend, c. 1260): is the dragon-slaying a historical event? — weak (as history; the martyr himself: plausible)

The apostles

The Apostles (their lives and the traditions about them)

who the Twelve were, and how the church remembered them. The New Testament names the apostles in four lists (Matthew 10, Mark 3, Luke 6, Acts 1) that agree on the core Twelve but differ at the edges — “Thaddaeus” in Matthew and Mark stands where Luke and Acts have “Judas son of James,” and Simon is called both “the Cananaean” and “the Zealot”; after Judas Iscariot's fall the eleven chose Matthias to replace him, and the risen Christ later called Paul, “one born out of due time.” This overview introduces the group; from it, a timeline for each apostle traces — earliest source first — how the memory of his origin, mission field, and death grew across the centuries.

Simon Peter

the fisherman of Bethsaida who stands first in every list of the Twelve, and whose life and death are the best-attested of any apostle. A timeline of the sources — from the New Testament's portrait of his origin, name, marriage, and leadership, through Clement of Rome, the apocryphal Acts, Tertullian, Gaius, Origen, Eusebius, and Jerome — tracing how the memory of his mission to Rome and his martyrdom under Nero took shape.

  • Did Peter lead and die at Rome? — sound
  • Was Peter crucified head-downward? — plausible

Andrew

Simon Peter's elder brother, the fisherman of Bethsaida whom John's Gospel calls the “first-called” — and about whom the New Testament then falls silent. A timeline of the sources traces how the tradition of his mission grew: from Origen's bare notice of Scythia on the Black Sea, through the apocryphal Acts' story of his crucifixion at Patras and Jerome's mention of Achaia, to the Byzantine claim that he founded the church of Constantinople and the late-medieval saltire that became his cross.

  • Was Andrew martyred by crucifixion at Patras? — plausible
  • The X-shaped “St Andrew's cross” — weak
  • Did Andrew found the church of Byzantium? — weak

James, son of Zebedee

James the Great — a Galilean fisherman, elder brother of John, and with Peter and John one of the inner three — is the one apostle whose death the New Testament records: Herod Agrippa I “killed James the brother of John with the sword” about the year 44, the first of the Twelve to be martyred. A timeline of the sources traces how the tradition grew from that firm anchor: Clement of Alexandria's early story of the accuser converted and beheaded beside him, then the late Latin apostle-lists' claim of a mission to Spain, and the medieval shrine of Santiago de Compostela.

  • Was James's accuser converted and beheaded with him? — plausible
  • Did James preach in Spain, and do his relics lie at Compostela? — weak

John, son of Zebedee

the younger brother of James and, with Peter, one of the inner three — remembered as “the disciple whom Jesus loved,” entrusted with the mother of Jesus at the cross, the exile of Patmos, and the last of the Twelve to die, an old man at Ephesus. A timeline of the sources traces how the tradition grew — from the New Testament portrait, through Papias's tangled “two Johns” and George Hamartolus's report of an early martyrdom, Irenaeus and Clement and Polycrates on his old age at Ephesus, Tertullian's boiling oil and the apocryphal Acts' wonders, down to Jerome and the Golden Legend.

the end of John

  • Did John die of old age at Ephesus? — sound
  • Was John martyred early, with his brother James? — weak
  • Was Papias's “John the elder” a second John? — contested

Philip

the disciple of Bethsaida who fetched Nathanael and voiced the question “Lord, shew us the Father” — and whose later tradition is tangled by a second man of the same name, Philip the deacon and evangelist of Acts, whose four prophesying daughters look like the daughters the Asian church remembered. A timeline of the sources traces how the memory grew: from the Gospels, through Papias and Polycrates placing Philip and his daughters at Hierapolis, to the apocryphal Acts' head-downward crucifixion and the martyrium excavated at Hierapolis in 2011.

  • Which Philip dwelt and died at Hierapolis — the apostle or the evangelist? — contested
  • Was Philip crucified head-downward at Hierapolis? — weak

Bartholomew

the most shadowy of the Twelve — a name that is only a patronymic (“son of Tolmai”), listed beside Philip and never described, and traditionally identified with the Nathanael of John's Gospel. A timeline of the sources traces how a biography grew around the silence: from the Gospel lists, through Pantaenus's early report of a Hebrew Matthew left in “India,” to the apostle-lists that fixed Armenia and a martyr's death, and the Golden Legend's scene of the apostle flayed alive.

  • Was Bartholomew the Nathanael of John's Gospel? — plausible
  • Did Bartholomew preach in India or in Armenia? — contested
  • Was Bartholomew flayed alive in Armenia? — weak

Thomas

the apostle whom the Gospels let us hear speak — the loyal “let us also go, that we may die with him,” the puzzled “how can we know the way?” and the doubt that ends in “My Lord and my God” — and whose name, like “Didymus,” means “the Twin.” His is the best-developed missionary tradition after Peter and Paul. A timeline of the sources traces it: from the Gospels, through Origen's bare allotment of Parthia and the Acts of Thomas' romance of a palace built in heaven for a king (Gundaphar) whom coins have since proved real, to Ephrem and the Edessa relic-cult, the Kerala Saint Thomas Christians, and the Golden Legend.

  • Did Thomas evangelize India? — plausible
  • Was Thomas the twin brother of Jesus? — weak

Matthew

the tax collector called from the toll-booth — the Levi of Mark and Luke, the church remembered — and the apostle credited from the second century with the first Gospel, “composed in the Hebrew tongue.” A timeline of the sources traces how his tradition grew: from the Gospel call-scene, through Papias on the Hebrew oracles and Clement's hint that Matthew was not martyred, to Jerome's Latin summary, the wandering mission-traditions, and the Golden Legend's tale of martyrdom in Ethiopia.

  • Was Matthew the tax collector the same man as Levi? — plausible
  • Was Matthew's Gospel first written in Hebrew? — contested
  • Was Matthew martyred in Ethiopia? — weak

James, son of Alphaeus

the most shadowy of the Twelve — a name and a father's name in every apostle-list, and nothing else. A timeline of the sources traces how his tradition was almost entirely borrowed: from the bare Gospel notices, through the apostle-lists that first grafted onto him the Jerusalem martyrdom of James the Just, to Jerome's fusion of the two Jameses and the Golden Legend's finished identification — which modern scholarship, distinguishing the three first-century Jameses, pulls apart again.

  • Was James son of Alphaeus the same man as James the brother of the Lord? — weak

Thaddaeus / Jude

the apostle of many names — “Thaddaeus” (or “Lebbaeus”) in Matthew and Mark, “Judas of James” in Luke and Acts, “Judas, not Iscariot” in John — whom the church harmonized into one man and then identified with Jude the author of the Epistle. A timeline of the sources traces how the tradition grew around a near-total silence: from the tangle of names in the New Testament, through Eusebius and the apocryphal letters of King Abgar (whose healer he makes “one of the seventy,” not one of the Twelve), to Jerome’s harmonizing of the names, the apostle-lists and the Golden Legend’s martyrdom with Simon the Zealot in Persia, and the modern scholarship that pulls the names apart again.

the name and identity of Jude

  • Are Thaddaeus, Lebbaeus, and Judas of James one and the same apostle? — plausible
  • Did the apostle Jude write the Epistle of Jude? — weak
  • Did the apostle Thaddaeus found the church of Edessa? (the Abgar legend) — weak

Simon the Zealot

the apostle Matthew and Mark call “the Cananaean” and Luke and Acts “the Zealot” — one Aramaic zeal-word in two languages, not “of Cana” — and about whom the New Testament says nothing else at all. A timeline of the sources traces how a mission and a martyrdom were supplied after the fact: from the bare Gospel epithet, through the mutually-dependent apostle-lists that scatter his labours across Persia, Britain, Egypt, and the Caucasus and even confuse him with Simeon of Jerusalem, to the Latin passion and the Golden Legend’s Persian martyrdom with Jude that gave Western art his saw.

  • Was Simon “the Zealot” a member of the revolutionary party? — contested
  • Where did Simon the Zealot preach and die — Persia, Britain, or elsewhere? — weak
  • Was Simon the Zealot the same man as Simeon son of Clopas, the second bishop of Jerusalem? — contested

Judas Iscariot

the one apostle everyone remembers for a single act — the betrayal — and the only one whose death the New Testament records twice, in accounts that do not read alike. A timeline of the sources traces how the memory grew: from the New Testament’s double-ended record (the “man of Kerioth,” the thirty pieces, and the two deaths — Matthew’s hanging, Acts’ falling headlong), through Papias’s grislier swelling and the 2nd-century Gnostic Gospel of Judas that made the traitor a hero, to the medieval Oedipus-legend of the Golden Legend and Dante’s Satan-chewed Judas.

the two accounts of Judas’s end

  • Matthew’s hanging and Acts’ bursting: one death, told twice — plausible
  • Matthew’s priests and Acts’ Judas: one purchase, one name — plausible
  • Why does Matthew credit the thirty-pieces oracle to “Jeremy the prophet”? — sound

Matthias

the one apostle added after the Ascension — chosen by lot to replace the fallen Judas, required to be an eyewitness “from the baptism of John” to the Ascension, and then never named again in the New Testament. A timeline of the sources traces how a life was supplied for a man known from a single verse: from the full Gospel account of his election, through the second-century Traditions of Matthias Clement quoted and the “Gospel of Matthias” Eusebius rejected, the cannibal-city romance that keeps confusing him with Matthew, and the late lists that carry him to Colchis and give him three incompatible deaths, to the relic-cult at Trier.

  • Was Matthias’s election legitimate, or was Paul the “true” twelfth apostle? — weak
  • Was Matthias the same man as Zacchaeus, or as Barnabas? — weak

Paul

the apostle to the Gentiles, called last of all on the Damascus road — and the one apostle whose life the New Testament documents in full but whose death it never records, leaving Paul under house-arrest at Rome. A timeline of the sources traces the one great gap the tradition fills, what happened after Acts 28: from the New Testament’s Saul of Tarsus and his hoped-for mission to Spain, through Clement of Rome’s early word that Paul reached “the extreme limit of the west” and was martyred, the Acts of Paul’s famous portrait (“small in size, bald-headed, bandy-legged”) and Thecla romance, Tertullian, Eusebius, and Jerome on the beheading at Rome under Nero, to the tomb on the Ostian way.

  • Did Paul reach Spain? — contested
  • Was Paul beheaded at Rome under Nero? — sound

Controversies among the Fathers

The Condemnation of Origen

Origen died in communion with the Church, tortured and unbroken in the Decian persecution and honoured as a confessor; three centuries later his name stands in an ecumenical council's list of arch-heretics. A footnoted timeline of the verdicts passed on him, earliest first — who first called him heretical, on what charges, on whose authority, and how the charge-sheet changed from Demetrius of Alexandria's synods in the 230s, through Methodius, Pamphilus's defence from prison, Epiphanius's heresy-catalogue and the synods of 400, to Justinian's edict of 543, the fifteen anathemas and the council of 553, and the modern reassessment that distinguishes Origen from Origenism.

the fall and restoration of the rational minds

  • Was the pre-existence of souls an open question when Origen taught it, so that the anathema of 543 judges him by a later rule? — contested (no rule of faith settled the soul's origin in his day, and Rufinus, Synesius and Augustine left it open long after; but the doctrine was refuted from Methodius on, and 543 condemned the pre-cosmic fall, not bare pre-existence)
  • Did Origen teach an endless succession of worlds, and does it entail a second fall? — contested (he ridiculed the Stoic wheel of identical recurrence by name and aimed his succession of ages at a real terminus where God is “all in all”; but “no second fall” overstates, since his end-like-beginning principle and inalienable free will leave the final rest insecure — the point Augustine pressed and 553 condemned)
  • Did Origen teach universal restoration (apokatastasis) as a doctrine, and was it condemnable in his day? — contested (no defined error when he taught it, and Gregory of Nyssa taught the same, uncondemned; but Origen argued it more firmly than “hope” allows, it was refuted on scriptural grounds before 543, and the mainstream-universalism claim is disputed)

the devil's salvation

  • Did the devil's-salvation charge misrepresent Origen? — contested (he denied it explicitly and his book destroys the hostile will not the being; but the system does entail the fallen being's reconciliation, and the charge misstates his words while catching his drift)
  • Does Origen's system require the devil's final salvation? — plausible (the restoration reaches “even His enemies” and III.6.5 keeps the substance while destroying only its enmity; but “requires” overstates, since Origen leaves it an open question and never secures any restoration against a fresh fall)

the verdict on the man

  • Did Rome in 400–401 condemn Origen's books only, and not the man? — weak (the Egyptian synods drew that line; Anastasius I's own letter condemns the writings “and their author” alike)
  • Was Origen himself anathematized by an ecumenical council? — contested (the canonical form of the fairness question: that the fifteen anathemas cannot be attributed to the council’s recorded sessions is strong and mainstream — Diekamp’s home synod, Tanner prints the acts without them — and that Origen’s name in the eleventh canon is a later insertion is arguable; but “no ecumenical council ever condemned him” overstates, since the same assembled bishops issued the fifteen as a synod, the earliest witness has the fathers condemning him by name, and two later councils (680–681, 787) name him and refer his condemnation to the fifth synod)
  • Is it more reasonable to believe Origen saved than damned — that a teacher condemned in his doctrine was never personally tried and died in the Church's communion? — contested (Pico’s censured thesis of 1486, and the capstone the timeline builds toward: the question turns on what no evidence can settle, a soul’s final state, so what is weighed is which belief is more reasonable — and in Pico’s modest comparative form it is strong, since Origen died in communion, a confessor never tried in life, his errors offered in still-open questions and without the obstinacy heresy requires; but the Church did determine the propositions errors and did name the man, so the defensible reading is Pico’s comparison, not the certainty of salvation his enemies attacked)
  • Was Origen condemned by a standard that did not exist in his lifetime? — contested (the umbrella fairness question: the rule of faith of his day had defined nothing on the soul's origin, the risen body, the fate of the damned or the Trinity's inner relations, and the Church kept Irenaeus, Papias, Tertullian and Gregory of Nyssa in honour for comparable errors; but “not yet defined” bears on culpability, not truth, some charges did meet a contemporary standard, and the inconsistency convicts the judges, not the accused)
  • Were Origen's condemned opinions offered as inquiry rather than assertion, so that the anathemas condemn a genre error? — contested (the genre form of the fairness question: Peri Archōn is genuinely written as inquiry — Origen flags his hardest speculations as “investigation and discussion… rather than fixed and certain decision” and even hostile Jerome preserved the disclaimer; but the hedges are uneven, the restoration and the ages are argued as conclusions, and a broadcast hypothesis is still a proposition the Church can judge, so the plea mitigates his culpability, not the propositions' truth)
  • Did the fifteen anathemas of 553 condemn sixth-century Evagrian Origenism rather than Origen? — contested (the philological form of the fairness question, and the load-bearing modern thesis: since Guillaumont recovered the unexpurgated Syriac Kephalaia Gnostica, the fifteen anathemas are known to track the Evagrian system of the sixth-century Palestinian monks — the spherical body, the ensouled stars, the nous-Christ, the final equality of all with Christ — and to quote Peri Archōn nowhere; but the list is framed by the pre-existence and the restoration, both genuinely his, and 543 quotes his own Greek, so it misnames the author of the system without missing him)
  • Can Origen be fairly judged at all, given how his text reaches us? — contested (the epistemic form of the fairness question: Peri Archōn survives whole only in Rufinus's softening Latin and its Greek chiefly in its prosecutors' briefs — Koetschau's 1913 edition even spliced the two — so the precise wording of the condemned propositions cannot be checked against his page; but “no verdict is recoverable” overstates, since the surviving Greek makes Rufinus's divergence measurable and the vast authenticated corpus gives independent access to his mind, so the caution is sound about the letter of the condemned system, weak about the substance)
  • Was Origen's condemnation driven by ecclesiastical politics rather than by his doctrine? — contested (the political, or motive, form of the fairness question: every institutional condemnation sits inside a non-doctrinal quarrel — Demetrius's jurisdiction, the campaigns against Chrysostom and the Tall Brothers, Jerome's reputation, the monastic war and the Three Chapters intrigue behind 543 and 553 — and contemporaries said so; but “doctrine was the instrument, not the cause” overstates, since motive is not merit, the charge-sheet stayed stable from Methodius to Justinian, and disinterested critics pressed the same doctrines with no stake in the fights)
  • Was the first condemnation of Origen a disciplinary sentence rather than a verdict on his doctrine? (Demetrius's Alexandrian synods, c. 231–232) — sound (the stated ground everywhere is the irregular ordination; the stronger claim that doctrine played no part at all goes beyond the evidence)
  • Was Origen's subordination of the Son a pre-Nicene idiom, so that reading him as an Arian is anachronistic? — contested (he wrote before Nicaea in the shared ante-Nicene idiom and is a source of the eternal generation Athanasius quoted against the Arians, so “really an Arian” is a retrojection; but his subordination is ontological, dating does not dissolve it, and it was never the charge the councils condemned him on)
  • Did Origen teach that the two Seraphim of Isaiah 6 are the Son and the Holy Spirit — and does the identification rank them among the creatures? — contested (the sharpest instance of the subordination charge; that he taught it is beyond dispute — Peri Archōn I.3.4 credits it to “my Hebrew master” and the first Homily on Isaiah preaches it — but read as proof of subordinationism it overreaches, since he uses it to exhibit an undivided Trinity and warns against dividing it; yet the seraphim are created servants, the one safeguarding clause survives only in Jerome's Latin, the reading coheres with a real gradient of the persons, and the young Jerome rejected it because John says the prophet saw Christ enthroned, not a seraph)
  • Did Origen teach that God and all rational creatures are “of one and the same essence” — a creaturely consubstantiality dissolving the line between Creator and creature? — weak (Jerome's climactic charge in Letter 124 is a real quotation put to a false use: Origen does write that beings sharing the “intellectual light” are “of one nature and of one essence,” but the sentence is a proof of the soul's immortality whose engine is participation — the relation that presupposes the participant is not what it shares in — hedged with “in some sort” and flatly qualified where he says creatures are “not holy in their essence” but only “by participating”; the mirror of the subordination charge — there the persons too far apart, here God and creatures too close)
  • Did Origen deny the resurrection of the flesh, and did he teach that risen bodies are spherical? — contested (the flat “denied the resurrection” is false — he taught a real, transformed body from 1 Corinthians 15, “the same body… its substance certainly remains” — and the spherical body of the 553 anathema is a sixth-century accretion; but he did deny the return of the material flesh, lodging identity in a persisting form, so the charge misnames the mechanism while catching a real distance)
  • Did Origen teach that hellfire is the fire of a guilty conscience, not a material, external fire? — sound (like the ensouled stars, a charge readable straight from his own words — Peri Archōn II.10 makes the “everlasting fire” a fire each sinner “kindles for himself,” the conscience witnessing against itself, and Against Celsus V a “purificatory fire… it burns indeed, but does not consume”; only the implication overshoots, since he does not abolish punishment but interiorizes it)
  • Was Origen's pre-existent soul of Christ a theological achievement — the full human soul Apollinaris denied and Chalcedon vindicated? — contested (strong as history of doctrine — Origen is among the first to insist Christ took a complete rational human soul, which Apollinaris denied and Chalcedon confessed as “a reasonable soul and body,” and the fiercest anathemas of 553 strike an Evagrian nous-Christ absent from his text; but the mediating soul presupposes the pre-existence scheme that fails independently, its union is by merit not hypostasis, and the eighth anathema catches a reciprocal predication his text does in part supply)
  • Did Origen teach that the sun, moon, and stars are living, ensouled rational beings? — sound (the mirror of the spherical-body charge: unlike the Evagrian accretions, this one is demonstrably his — Peri Archōn I.7 and Against Celsus V — though the anathema hardens into dogma what he floated as inquiry)
  • Did Origen teach that “astronomy is the cause of events” — that the stars govern what happens? — weak (the charge ascribes to Origen the astral fatalism he in fact refuted more fully than any Father before him — Philocalia 23, the stars signify but do not cause — so the earliest reader to weigh it filed it among the slanders)
  • Did Origen teach reincarnation (the transmigration of souls)? — sound
  • Did Origen teach that “magic is not an evil” — that certain names have a real, natural power? — contested (the doctrine of the natural power of names is genuinely his — Against Celsus I.24–25, V.45 — and he there defends “the so-called magic” as a real system, not empty superstition; but the charge that magic is not an evil misnames a claim about the reality of names as a claim about their goodness, against Origen's own condemnation of sorcery as demonic and forbidden)
  • Did Origen's allegory dissolve the literal and historical sense of Scripture? — contested (the threefold sense makes the historical sense the body Scripture is built on, and he keeps the letter in the great majority of cases; but he did dehistoricize Eden by a stated rule of divinely-planted “impossibilities,” on a criterion the Antiochenes rose to resist — strong by Alexandrian lights, weak by Antiochene)
  • Did Origen teach that the “coats of skins” (Genesis 3:21) are the mortal human body? — contested (the origin-of-the-body charge, distinct from the pre-existence of the soul: its root is genuinely his — Against Celsus IV.40 makes the garments of skins a “secret and mystical doctrine… of the soul… borne downwards to earth,” guarded as a mystery not for the multitude, and Methodius was refuting Origenists who held the skins to be bodies by c. 300; but the accusers harden it into the flat “skins = flesh” with its boneless paradise, a corollary supplied by hostile witnesses and disputed among modern scholars)
  • Did Origen teach that the image and likeness of God was lost, no longer remaining in man after the fall? — weak (the charge inverts his teaching: Origen holds that man keeps the image, given at creation, and must acquire the likeness, reserved for the end — Peri Archōn III.6.1, Against Celsus IV.30 — and calls it “impious” to think the image corrupted out of man; even Epiphanius refutes the charge with Origen's own position, and it survives only by hardening the real theme of an image obscured and to be renewed into an image lost)

Was Tertullian a Heretic?

Tertullian of Carthage gave the Latin West its word for the Trinity and wrote its fiercest defence of the rule of faith against heretics — yet he went over to the ecstatic New Prophecy (Montanism), was never canonized, and had his books listed among those “not received.” Unlike Origen he was never anathematized by any council: his “condemnation” is reputational, not conciliar. A footnoted timeline of the judgements and reports that made the verdict, earliest first — from Tertullian's own Montanist works and the telling silence of Cyprian's Carthage, through Jerome's biographical notice and Augustine's heresy-catalogue entry on the Tertullianistae, to the medieval book-lists and the modern reassessment.

the fairness of the verdict

  • Did Tertullian ever formally leave the catholic church? — contested (the load-bearing question of the timeline: his own works record no synod, excommunication, or exit, place the New Prophecy inside the Sunday assembly, and argue as an insider, so the mainstream since Barnes and Powell reads the African movement as an ecclesiola in ecclesia; but On Modesty 21 sets the church of the Spirit against the church of bishops, the Psychici polemic is separatist in tone, and Augustine reports a real Tertullianist basilica in Carthage)
  • Was the New Prophecy heretical in doctrine, or only in discipline and church order? — contested (that Tertullian was condemned for no error about God or Christ is close to consensus — he confessed the catholic Trinity, wrote the West's great anti-modalist treatise as a Montanist, and Augustine expressly declined to found the heresy on his doctrine; but the broad claim that the verdict merely mistakes a disciplinary quarrel for a doctrinal one overreaches, since new revelation binding on the whole church touches the closure of the apostolic deposit, the ecstatic manner was condemned as false prophecy, and a later Montanist wing did fall into real modalism)
  • Was his denial of church forgiveness for adultery and apostasy a defensible reading of early discipline, or a Montanist novelty? — contested (the historical frame holds — pre-Nicene penance was severe, the post-baptismal “second repentance” was once-only, and a real rigorist current from the Shepherd of Hermas to the Novatianists feared the capital sins beyond the church's reach, so judging him by the later repeatable penance is anachronistic; but the claim of “no novelty” overreaches, since his own earlier On Repentance had offered the grave sinner that penance, he had to reject the Shepherd to hold his line, and the church settled the same question against Novatian)
  • Was his ban on second marriage a severe reading of Paul, or a false doctrine? — contested (the count on which Augustine actually hung the heresy)
  • Was Tertullian right that flight in persecution is forbidden? — contested (his rule that a Christian may neither flee persecution nor buy immunity)

his theology

  • Was Tertullian's subordination of the Son one of order and origin, not of nature? — contested (the man who gave the West trinitas ranks the Son “in degree” and calls him “a derivation and portion of the whole”; the defence reads this as subordination of origin within one undivided substance — “God of God… in position, not in nature” — the germ of the homoousion and the reverse of Arius, so branding him an Arian is anachronistic; but the portio language grades the Godhead and “there was a time… nor the Son” ties his generation to creation where Nicaea made it eternal)
  • Was Tertullian's corporeal soul crude materialism, or a defensible anti-Platonism? — contested (the one doctrinal charge anyone actually laid: his corpus reads as substance-realism against Platonic and Gnostic immaterialism, so not the crude materialism the charge implies, and on the core point — the soul a creature, not a fragment of God — he is orthodox; but he ascribes the soul figure, dimension, and colour on Stoic premises and extends “body” to God, and even the sympathetic Augustine judged it an error)

the biography from Jerome

  • Was Tertullian a presbyter, a lawyer, and a convert in middle life? — contested (the familiar life descends almost entirely from one paragraph Jerome wrote 172 years after Tertullian died; his own works twice class him among the laity, and the specialists since Barnes have abandoned the identification with the Digest-jurist, so “Tertullian the lapsed presbyter” may misdescribe a layman who led a rigorist party inside his own church; but the sweeping claim that little survives overreaches — an educated African convert with real forensic training remains, attested by Eusebius before Jerome, and the case rests at bottom on the silence of a man who volunteered almost no autobiography, which proves the biography doubtful, not false)
  • Did the envy of the Roman clergy drive Tertullian to Montanism? — sound (that the motive is unsupported: Jerome's “envy and abuse of the clergy of the Roman church” is a single-witness claim about an interior motive, written 172 years late by a man freshly quarrelling with the Roman clergy himself — Mohrmann reads Jerome's own resentment reflected in it — confirmed by no other source and contradicted by the reasons Tertullian gives for the turn, which are the Paraclete and discipline throughout; the one Rome-grievance his works record, a bishop's reversal on the Phrygian prophecies in Against Praxeas 1, is doctrinal, not the personal slight alleged; his real animus against the “bishop of bishops” keeps the finding to “undocumented,” not “he felt none”)
  • Did Tertullian teach fideism — “I believe because it is absurd”? — sound (the most-quoted line ever hung on him is one he never wrote: credo quia absurdum is a fabrication, and the real sentence — “certain, because it is impossible,” On the Flesh of Christ 5 — is the pivot of a reasoned reductio against Marcion, turning on the Aristotelian topos of the credible-because-improbable; the fideist Tertullian is an Enlightenment invention, finished by Voltaire, laid over an advocate steeped in Stoic logic and Roman law — though Prescription 7 and the later appeal to the Paraclete’s oracles keep the acquittal exact)
  • Is it coherent to keep his theology while withholding his name? — contested (the umbrella question: the Church took his arguments and gave him neither sainthood nor a formal condemnation. The teaching-vs-teacher principle is sound and its own — Tertullian's rule “prove the faith by the persons, or the persons by the faith?”, Cyprian's daily reading of “the master,” Jerome's “I have praised the commentator but not the theologian” — and the double refusal tracks the case, no doctrinal heresy proven so no anathema, a turn to the New Prophecy and a schism so no sainthood; but “precise judgement” overstates a verdict no court ever pronounced, and the comparanda — Hippolytus sainted for his martyrdom, Cyprian's error “cleared away by the pruning-hook of martyrdom,” Origen anathematized in a partly political quarrel — show the outcome turning on martyrdom, reconciliation, and the fortunes of a name as much as on his faults)
